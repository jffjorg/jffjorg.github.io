REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 22BBCV00702    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: A

MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSE

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22BBCV00702

 

MP:  

Maria Baires, Gloribel Baires, individually and as successor-in-interest to and on behalf of Maria Baires, Mirna Vanegas, and Samuel Baires (Plaintiffs)

RP:  

ICC Convalescent Corp. (Defendant)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is required and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

 

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Gloribel Baires, individually and as successor-in-interest to and on behalf of Maria Baires (Decedent), Mirna Vanegas and Samuel Baires (collectively Plaintiffs) bring this action against ICC Convalescent Corp. (ICC). Plaintiffs allege that Decedent received substandard care as a resident at ICC’s facilities and that such substandard care resulted in Decedent’s injury and death.

 

Plaintiffs move to compel ICC’s further responses to their Special Interrogatories (SPROG) and Request for Production of Documents (RFPD). ICC opposes the motions and Plaintiffs reply.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories

 

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete, (2)¿an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response, or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. §¿2030.300(a).) The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections thereto. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-21.)

  

Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFPD

 

A motion to compel further responses to RFPD may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See C.C.P. § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

II.                 MERITS

 

RFPD Timeline

On October 3, 2024, ICC served their First Supplemental Responses to SPROG and RFPD. (Farwell Decl. Exh. C.) Plaintiffs allege no documents accompanied the supplemental responses to RFPD (Farwell Decl. ¶ 4).  On October 18, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel reached out via email to meet and confer regarding the responses. (Farwell Decl. Exh. D.) On November 4, 2024, ICC’s counsel responded with a meet and confer email attempting to address the perceived shortcomings of their initial and First Supplemental Responses. (Farwell Decl. Exh. E.)

 

On November 5, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel requested a two-week extension for the deadline to file the instant motion. (Farwell Decl. ¶ 7.) The Court notes that although this request is stated to be attached as Exhibit F to the Farwell Declaration, no such attachment is present. Plaintiffs’ counsel states that additional time was required to review ICC’s meet and confer letter and reply. (Farwell Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs state that this request received no response. (Farwell Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

On November 6, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel requested that ICC provide the documents referenced in Defendant’s First Supplemental Responses as to RFPD Nos. 4, 5, 9 and 15. (Farwell Decl. ¶ 8.) It appears these documents were produced as they are attached to Exhibit C of Plaintiffs’ motion.

 

On November 8, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed the instant motion. After the motion was filed, it is apparent that further supplemental production took place. The Court notes that none of this supplemental production is attached to ICC’s oppositions, nor attested to in the declaration of their counsel. In fact, the declaration submitted in opposition to the motion to compel further RFPD responses contains facts which apparently speak to an entirely unrelated discovery dispute. (See Alvarado Decl. ¶ 2 [“On August 15, 2023, Plaintiffs served Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, to Defendant that contained 143 separate requests.”].) Regardless, these supplemental responses are attached to Plaintiffs’ reply briefings.

 

On November 18, 2024, ICC served its Second Supplemental Responses to the RFPD, which were unverified. (Farwell Reply Decl. ¶ 2.) These responses are not attached to Plaintiffs’ reply briefing. On December 27, 2024, ICC served Plaintiffs with its Third Supplemental Responses to the RFPD, which were also unverified. (Farwell Reply Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. A.)  

 

Timeline for SPROG

 

On June 25, 2024, Plaintiffs served ICC with their SPROG (Farwell Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) On September 26, 2024, ICC served its responses. (Farwell Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. B.)

 

On October 18, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel reaches out via email to meet and confer regarding the responses. (Farwell Decl. Exh. C.) On November 4, 2024, ICC’s counsel responded with a meet and confer email attempting to address the perceived shortcomings of their initial responses. (Farwell Decl. Exh. E.)

 

On November 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion. It does not appear any further responses were rendered to SPROG.

 

General Discussion

 

Plaintiffs’ meet and confer efforts do not appear sufficient. A motion to compel further responses to a request for production must include a meet and confer declaration consistent with C.C.P. § 2016.040. (C.C.P. § 2031.310 (b)(2).) In turn, Section 2016.040 requires that the moving party have made “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (C.C.P. § 2016.040.) C.C.P. § 2030.300(b)(1) contains an identical provision as regards motions to compel further responses to interrogatories.

 

“A single letter, followed by a response which refuses concessions, might in some instances be an adequate attempt at informal resolution, especially when a legitimate discovery objective is demonstrated. The time available before the motion filing deadline, and the extent to which the responding party was complicit in the lapse of available time, can also be relevant. An evaluation of whether, from the perspective of a reasonable person in the position of the discovering party, additional effort appeared likely to bear fruit, should also be considered” (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 432.)

 

For purposes of determining whether a motion to compel discovery was preceded by a reasonable and good faith effort to meet and confer, such a reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with opposing counsel; rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate. (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294)

 

Plaintiffs’ October 18, 2024 meet and confer letter totals 90 pages. Upon review it appears that the letter is more of a separate statement than meet and confer correspondence. In fact, the content of Plaintiffs’ meet and confer letter largely mirrors the content of their separate statements submitted in support of these motions. While some overlap is certainly expected between these two documents, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ lengthy recitation of authority to be a sufficient attempt at informal compromise. This letter certainly initiated the meet and confer process, but it is not indicative of a comprehensive effort to resolve the issues with opposing counsel prior to bringing this motion.

 

Further, ICC’s November 5 response appears to indicate that further informal efforts would have, at least in part, produced additional documents sought by Plaintiffs’ motions. This is especially true because it appears from ICC’s response that Plaintiffs’ meet and confer efforts did not address ICC’s First Supplemental Responses. (Farwell Decl. Exh. %, p. 293-294 [“However, as to Demands Nos. 4, 5 and 9, on October 3, 2024, Defendant served its Further Responses and provided documents responsive to these demands. It is hereby requested that Plaintiffs confirm their receipt of same. Additionally, Defendant will also be serving its Further Amended Responses to these Demands with additional documents shortly.].)

 

The Court understands that meet and confer efforts were time limited by Plaintiffs’ 45-day deadline to file this motion, however Plaintiffs failed to attach their request for extension. While the request, and ICC’s failure to respond, is attested to in the Farwell declaration, the Court is unaware of the context of that request. For example, was the request attached to other correspondence that would have made it easy to miss? Or was it contained in its own email such that overlooking it would be impossible? It seems unlikely to the Court that ICC’s counsel would purposefully not respond to Plaintiffs’ request and then proceed to issue two more sets of supplemental response shortly thereafter.

 

The result of this truncated meet and confer period is substantial confusion in what discovery demands remain the proper subject of these motions, especially as concerns the RFPD. As a result of the three rounds of supplemental responses, at least some portion of Plaintiffs’ filed motion have been rendered moot. Plaintiffs’ separate statement, the tool intended to assist the Court in parsing out complicated discovery issues such as this one, has been rendered ineffective by the supplemental responses and production.

 

Plaintiffs’ reply papers do little to clear this confusion. Although Plaintiffs reference some of the supplemental responses, their argument seems to be that these supplemental responses cannot be considered because they were unverified. As will be explained below, the Court is unable to ascertain which supplemental responses were verified and which were not. While Plaintiffs maintain in their Reply that RFPD Nos. 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 15, 65, and 67 still require further production, their discussion of these RFPD are lumped together in a way which makes adjudication of this dispute unwieldy.

 

Further, the Court is entirely uncertain as to whether any of the responses issued by ICC have been verified. Of course, ICC should know that responses must be verified, and the failure to do so is a discovery violation; nonetheless, this can often be cured with a genuine meet and confer effort.  Each of the Exhibits containing ICC’s responses to RFPD either lack a verification entirely or state that verifications will follow. Neither the declaration of Farwell or Alvarado state whether verifications were ultimately provided. This poses a substantial challenge to the Court in ruling on these motions, as it is unclear which of ICC’s three rounds of supplemental RFPD responses are verified and which are not.

 

Nor is it clear which objections remain and to which RFPD they apply. ICC has objected to RFPD Nos. 31-36, 42, 46-48, 51-52, 54, 56-57, 63-64, and 68-72 on grounds that these RFPD request protected confidential, financial, and proprietary information. Among the statutes cited as grounds for this objection in ICC’s opposition are Evidence Code § 1157, Bus & Prof. Code § 805, and 42 U.S.C. § 1395i-3(B)(1)(B). Yet none of these statutes are cited in the ICC’s separate statement in opposition, leaving the Court to guess as to which statutes apply to which request. It cannot be that Bus & Prof. Code § 805 prohibits discovery of every document in ICC’s possession which is potentially responsive to any of these 20 plus RFPD. 

 

It appears that even Plaintiffs are unclear what has and has not been produced. By way of example, the Court notes Plaintiffs’ argument in their Reply that ICC, “…falsely asserts in its Opposition that it produced “Nursing Staff and Assignment Sheets and Daily Census Reports”, CDPH 530 forms, CDPH 612 forms, Roster/Sample Matrix “CMS-802” (Reply. p. 3.) Indeed, ICC’s separate statement claims that on January 6, 2025, “…[d]efense Counsel provided additional documents responsive to this request, i.e., Nursing Staff and Assignment Sheets and Daily Census Reports, as “Further RDP #4/#5/#9.”  (Opp. SS. No. 2.) Plaintiffs’ Reply, filed January 10, 2025, makes no mention of this apparent Fourth Supplemental Response.

 

In short, the Court finds that the failure of the parties to meet and confer has caused substantial difficulty in properly adjudicating these motions. Despite the hundreds of pages of briefing and separate statements, the Court remains unaware as to what requests and documents remain in dispute. Where Plaintiffs have propounded such voluminous discovery requests, it is incumbent upon them to properly meet and confer to whittle down the issues prior to filing these motions. Plaintiffs cannot move to compel further responses to a vast amount of discovery requests and then request piecemeal adjudication in their Reply papers after four rounds of supplemental production.

 

Accordingly, the Court orders Plaintiffs’ motions continued to February 14, 2025. The parties are ordered to further meet and confer regarding what production remains outstanding and which requests have been mooted by supplemental production. To the extent that any of the supplemental production by ICC remains unverified, ICC is ordered to serve verifications. Plaintiffs are ordered to submit a revised separate statement and memorandum no later than January 31, 2025. ICC is ordered to submit a revised separate statement and memorandum in opposition no later than February 7, 2024. Should ICC maintain its objections on the grounds of privilege, it must submit a log specifically identifying the documents it refuses to produce on those grounds.

 

In the event these informal efforts resolve the outstanding issues, counsel is to contact the Court to request the motions be removed from the calendar.

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action […], without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, […] the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (C.C.P. § 2025.450(a).)

Timeline

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition for November 14, 2024 at 9:00 a.m. (Wilson Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. A.) The Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently demonstrated they are entitled to relief under C.C.P. § 2025.450(a). Plaintiff has demonstrated that (1) Defendant was properly served with notice of the deposition, (2) Defendant failed to object to the notice, (3) Defendant failed to appear at the noticed deposition, and (4) Plaintiff’s counsel properly inquired as to the nonappearance in satisfaction of their meet and confer requirements. Here, the Court finds sanctions are not appropriate.  Albeit it was the day before the deposition, Defense counsel requested to reschedule for the following month and did not oppose this motion.  While Plaintiff was justifiably frustrated that Defense counsel did not timely request a continuance nor provide an explanation, nevertheless the conduct is such that Defendant appeared to be making an effort to pick an alternative date.  Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions.

NOTICE:

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Victoria Abdo (Abdo) brings this action against Mark Towhidlow (Towhidlow). Abdo seeks specific performance of a June 9, 2024 agreement for the sale of the real property commonly known as 1751 Camino De Villas, Burbank, CA 91501 for $1,150,000. (Compl. ¶ 7, 8-9.) Abdo alleges that Towhidlow refused to sell despite Plaintiff depositing the funds into escrow.    (Compl. ¶¶ 8-9.) Abdo further alleges that Towhidlow thereafter sent cancellation of escrow instructions. (Compl. ¶ 10.)

 

On September 13, 2023, Towhidlow filed a Cross-Complaint against Abdo, Dona T. Abdo, Ramona Nazarimasihi, and High Vision Realty. The Cross-Complaint states a cause of action for (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (as against High Vision Reality and Ramona Nazarimasihi) and (2) Fraud (as against all Cross-Defendants).

 

On July 24, 2024, the Court sustained Abdo’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint with leave to amend. (See Jul. 24 Minute Order.) It appears that Towhidlow never filed an amended Cross-Complaint in accordance with his grant of leave. As such, on September 5, 2024, the Court granted Abdo’s ex parte application to have the Cross-Complaint dismissed and scheduled an Order to Show Cause (OSC) re: Dismissal for failure to serve for November 11, 2024. (See Sep. 5 Minute Order).

 

On September 20, 2024, the Court granted the motion to be relieved filed by Towhidlow’s counsel. Towhidlow had represented himself in this action since that date.  On November 11, 2025, the Court held a hearing on the OSC re Dismissal. Towhidlow appeared at this hearing in pro per. The Court advised Towhidlow that he must reserve the Cross-Complaint and continued the OSC to be heard concurrently with this motion.

 

Before the Court is Abdo’s motion for terminating sanctions, seeking that Towhidlow’s Answer be stricken and default be entered against him. This motion is based upon Towhidlow’s failure to adhere to the Court’s July 24, 2024 order that he provide responses to Abdo’s initial discovery demands. Abdo states that Towhidlow has produced no discovery responses and has not paid the monetary sanctions assessed in the order. Towhidlow has filed no opposition to the motion. Pursuant to C.R.C. Rule 8.54(c), a failure to oppose a motion may be deemed consent to its being granted.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Where a party fails to obey an order compelling answers to discovery, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction.” (C.C.P. §§ 2030.290(c), 2023.010(c); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal. App. 4th 486, 495.) Terminating sanctions are appropriate when a party persists in disobeying the court’s orders. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 795-796.)

 

A terminating sanction is a “drastic measure which should be employed with caution.” (Id. at 793.) “A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 262, 279-280.)

 

While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Deyo supra, 84 Cal. App. 3d at 793.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

The Court finds that it lacks the information to determine whether terminating sanctions are warranted. The Court acknowledges that this lack of information is not the result of any action by Abdo. Rather, events have transpired between the filing of this motion and the instant hearing which give the Court pause in issuing terminating sanctions.

 

Specifically, at the November 5, 2024 OSC hearing, the Court ordered that Abdo re-serve all discovery on Towhidlow. This order was made in light of the fact that Towhidlow now represented himself in pro per and had stated to the Court that his previous attorney of record did not provide him with the discovery demands. In the time since making this order, no proofs of service or declarations have been submitted from which the Court could determine that Towhidlow was served with the demands.

 

In the context of discovery compliance, a lack of diligence may be deemed willful in the sense that the party understood his obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply. (Deyo supra, at 787.) To properly determine whether Towhidlow’s willingly refused to comply with the Court’s order, the Court must confirm that he understands his obligations under those discovery demands. It follows that the Court must be certain that Towhidlow received those demands and understood the obligations which flowed from them. The Court simply lacks the resources to make such a determination at this juncture. Accordingly, the Motion for Terminating Sanctions is DENIED without prejudice.

ALLEGATIONS:

 

Marisol Sanchez, Maria Jose Sanchez, & Guadalupe Selene Sanchez, as individuals and successors in interest to Jose Sanchez (collectively Plaintiffs) bring this action against Pacifica of the Valley Corporation, Pacifica Hospital of the Valley (collectively Pacifica), Woody Herman Jackson, M.D., Mehboob Ali Makhani, M.D., Edmond Melikterminas, M.D., and Farsheed Nikbakht, M.D. (collectively Defendants). Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to assess and address the medical condition and needs of Jose Sanchez (Decedent) when he was admitted to Pacifica on July 21, 2022, resulting in his death. 

 

On January 5, 2024, the Court sustained Pacifica’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ cause of action for Elder Abuse with leave to amend. Plaintiffs proceeded to file their First Amended Complaint (FAC) and Pacifica once again demurred to the Elder Abuse cause of action. On April 26, 2024, the Court sustained Pacifica’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ cause of action for Elder Abuse with leave to amend. Plaintiff thereafter filed their Second Amended Complaint (SAC).

 

Pacifica now demurs to same cause of action in Plaintiffs’ SAC, arguing that the amendments have not cured the insufficient factual allegations. Plaintiffs oppose and Pacifica replies.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARDS

 

The grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (C.C.P. § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal. 3d 311, 318.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)   A demurrer assumes the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading. (Blank v. Kirwan, supra, 39 Cal. 3d at p. 318.) No matter how unlikely or improbable, the plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.  App. 3d 593, 604.) But this does not include contentions; deductions; conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.  (Blank, supra, 39 Cal. 3d at 318.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Meet and Confer

 

C.C.P. §§ 430.41(a) requires that the moving party meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer. Upon review the Court finds the meet and confer requirements were met. (Carter Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. A.)

 

Facts Carrying Over from Prior Pleadings

 

Plaintiffs’ SAC retains basic facts that were present in the Complaint and FAC. For the purpose of completeness, the Court identifies these facts and their location in the SAC below.

 

On July 17, 2022, Decedent was admitted to Harbor UCLA Medical Center after being placed on a 72-hour § 5150 hold. (SAC ¶ 19.) On July 21, 2022, Decedent was transferred to Pacifica for continuing psychiatric observations as well as for COVID-19 positive viral infection, depression, and confusion. (SAC ¶ 20.) Decedent was admitted to Pacifica’s medical psychiatric department. (SAC ¶ 20.)

 

Upon admission to Pacifica, Woody Herman Jackson ordered that Pacifica place Decedent on a one-to-one psychiatric protocol, otherwise known as a “sitter” protocol, for an indefinite period of time. (SAC ¶ 21.) The sitter protocol was shortly thereafter acknowledged by a Pacifica employee or agent named Carlo Pascual. (SAC ¶ 21.) Plaintiffs allege that slightest deviation from the strict requirements of a sitter protocol can lead to a patient committing acts which can lead to or cause death or serious injuries. (SAC ¶ 22.) Plaintiffs allege that sitters are required to be within arm’s distance of a patient on a 24/7 basis. (SAC ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs allege that from the time the initial protocol was ordered on July 21, 2022 at 10:53 p.m. until Decedent’s death on July 24, 2022 at 9:57 a.m., Pacifica failed to follow these requirements. (FAC ¶ 24.)

 

Sometime in the early morning hours between 1:00 a.m. and 2:15 a.m. on July 24, 2022, Decedent was found on the floor after having fallen and hitting his head on the concrete floor. (SAC ¶ 28.) Decedent was then sent to get a brain CT scan. (SAC ¶ 29.) At 8:51 a.m., Decedent was found naked and sitting on his roommate’s bed, appearing confused and in distress. (SAC ¶ 29.) Decedent began showing respiratory distress, gasping for air and turned unresponsive. (SAC ¶ 29.) Decedent was pronounced dead at 9:57 a.m. (SAC ¶ 26.)

 

Facts Added in the 3AC

 

Plaintiffs now additionally allege that Defendants were on notice of Decedent’s suicidal tendencies and the risk it posed him if left unattended. (SAC ¶ 20.) Specifically, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants were aware that Decedent, “…had attempted suicide by throwing himself onto sharp metal fencing rods in his backyard to meet his recently deceased wife in heaven.” (SAC ¶ 20.)

 

Plaintiffs also now allege that, based on discovery responses, Pacifica does not utilize its nursing staff in carrying out sitter protocols. (SAC ¶ 23.) Instead, these protocols are allegedly carried out by Certified Nursing Assistants (CNA). (SAC ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs allege that Pacifica’s nursing staff conducts separate hourly checks, but they are not assigned to conduct any 1:1 sitter observation. (SAC ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs allege these separate hourly checks last no more than five minutes and do not serve the same self-harm prevention purposes as the sitter protocols. (SAC ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs allege that Decedent never received the 1:1 attention required by the sitter protocol. (SAC ¶¶ 23, 25.) Plaintiffs allege that instead, Pacifica determined the sitter protocol would be adhered to on an “as needed basis” (SAC ¶ 25.) Plaintiffs allege that after Decedent’s fall, Pacifica nurse Aivie Janeo purportedly “commenced” and “executed” the 1:1 sitter protocol for the first time. (SAC ¶ 29.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that the discovery responses by Pacifica also reveal that they maintained an inadequate number of CNA to carry out the sitter protocols on the day of Decedent’s incident. (SAC ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs allege that to properly carry out the sitter protocols for the nine patients who required it, Pacifica would require 36 CNAs. (SAC ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs therefore allege that Pacifica’s maintaining only six CNA was inadequate. (SAC ¶ 26.)

 

Perhaps the greatest change from the previous pleadings are the facts concerning Decedent’s fall. Previously, Plaintiffs allegations were unclear as to the cause of Decedent’s fall. The Complaint and FAC contained a variety of allegations indicating the fall was caused by Pacifica’s apparent failure to institute proper fall protocols needed by Decedent. (See FAC ¶¶ 25, 27.) Plaintiffs now explicitly allege that Decedent’s fall was the result of his plan to commit suicide. Plaintiffs allege that, “Based on information obtained by Pacifica, the fall was intentional with the intent to sustain a fatal head injury. This information is derived from comments by Decedent Sanchez expressing his intent to die and to do it by self-inflicted means.” (SAC ¶ 28.)

 

Discussion

 

The Court finds the additional allegations are insufficient to cure the deficiency in Plaintiffs’ cause of action for Elder Abuse. As will be explained below, the Court does not view these new allegations as speaking to the routine denial of basic care as contemplated by the Elder Abuse Act.

 

To plead elder or dependent adult abuse, the plaintiff must allege “facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care [citations]; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs [citations]; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult’s basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the¿plaintiff¿alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness) [citations].” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC¿(2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-07.)

 

Neglect as contemplated by Welf. and Inst. Code § 15610.57 is a somewhat amorphous concept. In Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, the California Supreme Court acknowledged that the statute provided a non-exhaustive list of examples such as failure to assist in personal hygiene, failure to provide food, clothing, or shelter, failure to protect from health and safety hazards, and failure to prevent malnutrition or dehydration. (Winn supra 63 Cal.4th 148, 156 citing Welf. and Inst. Code §§ 15610.57(b)(1)-(6).) From this, the Winn court reasoned that neglect meant the failure to carry out an assumed responsibility, “…for attending to one or more of an elder's basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.” (Id. at 158.)

 

Other case law has made clear that neglect within the meaning of the statute is intended to cover an area of misconduct distinct from professional negligence.¿ (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 783.)¿ The problem thus arises that the lines between failure to provide for basic needs and medical negligence are often blurry. The Covenant Care court addressed this issue in stating, “As used in the Act, neglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.”¿ (Id. [citation omitted])¿¿¿ 

 

Under the lens of Covenant Care, the Court finds Plaintiffs’ allegations concerning the sitter protocol still do not sufficiently allege neglect under the Elder Abuse Act. Plaintiffs notably do not allege that Pacifica outright refused to adhere to the sitter protocol, instead alleging that Pacifica only carried out the protocol on an “as needed” basis. This is an allegation of professional negligence, not neglect. The same can be said of Plaintiffs’ allegation that Pacifica did not have sufficient CNA to carry out its sitter protocols. The fact that six CNA are insufficient to provide the required 1:1 supervision again speaks to Pacifica’s failure to adequately provide care, not the outright failure to provide it in the first place. Here too Plaintiffs allege that the protocol was not followed sufficiently such that Decedent was found in distress some hours later.

 

In other words, Plaintiffs’ additional allegations in the SAC all speak to Pacifica’s failure to adequately administer the sitter protocol, rather than a complete refusal to provide those services, or to provide them to such a minimal degree that it is tantamount to no services at all. Each of these allegations are sufficient to establish in a Complaint that Pacifica was negligent in rendering medical services, but they are not sufficient to allege that Pacifica failed to provide those services in the first place.

 

Plaintiffs’ attempt to distinguish Pacifica’s hourly checks from the checks required by the sitter protocol are unpersuasive. Plaintiff argues that Pacifica’s use of hourly checks, rather than a CNA, constitutes neglect for purposes of Elder Abuse. The Court finds this argument unpersuasive. Both checks serve the purpose of supervising mental health patients, although they require different levels of supervision. Pacifica’s decision to monitor Decedent via hourly checks rather than the 1:1 supervision by a CNA speaks to Pacifica’s failure to adequately provide supervision for Decedent, not their outright refusal to supervise him. Pacifica’s election to forgo the 1:1 supervision may certainly be an act of professional negligence, but it is not the outright denial of basic needs needed to sustain a cause of action for Elder Abuse.

 

In short, the Court finds the allegations of the SAC remain insufficient to state a cause of action for Elder Abuse. Plaintiffs’ allegations all reflect that Pacifica was negligent in carrying out the sitter protocol, not that Pacifica failed to carry it out full stop.

 

Accordingly, Pacifica’s demurrer to the first cause of action for Elder Abuse is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. This is Plaintiff’s third attempt to state a cause of action and has been unable to do so.  Further, it is apparent that significant discovery has already occurred such that any additional facts supporting the Elder Abuse cause were already within Plaintiffs’ knowledge when they filed their SAC.

ALLEGATIONS:

 

Petitioners’ seeks a “Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award” pursuant to C.C.P. § 1285 as it relates to an uninsured motorist arbitration. Respondent opposes the petition, arguing that no arbitration award was ever made. Petitioners reply.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARDS

 

Any party to an arbitration award may petition the court to confirm, correct, or vacate the award. (C.C.P. § 1285.) “If a petition or response under this chapter is duly served and filed, the court shall confirm the award as made, whether rendered in this state or another state, unless in accordance with this chapter it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award or dismisses the proceeding.” (C.C.P. § 1286.)

 

 

II.                 ANALYSIS

 

Facts

 

The California Insurance Code requires that claims involving uninsured/underinsured motorists must be arbitrated before any cause of action under that claim begins to accrue. (See Insurance Code § 11580.2(i)(1)(c) [“No cause of action shall accrue to the insured under any policy or endorsement provision issued pursuant to this section unless…[t]he insured has formally instituted arbitration proceedings by notifying the insurer in writing sent by certified mail, return receipt requested… within two years from the date of the accident.”].)

 

On June 14, 2022, Petitioners sent Respondent a letter formally demanding arbitration pursuant in accordance with their obligation under Insurance Code § 11580.2(i)(1)(c). (Kim Decl. ¶ 4.) On November 22, 2022, Respondent sent Petitioners a letter formally acknowledging the arbitration requirement. (Kim Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. C.)

 

On April 19, 2024, the parties selected Judge Bryan F. Foster (Ret.) as the arbitrator and set arbitration proceedings for December 5-6, 2024. (Kim Decl. ¶ 11.)

 

On December 2, 2024, the parties reached a settlement prior to entering arbitration. (Kim Decl. ¶ 15, Exh. 1; Long Decl. Exh. 2.) No release was requested or signed. (Kim Decl. ¶ 15.) Petitioners’ counsel then reached out to the arbitrator and cancelled the pending arbitration. (Long Exh. 3.)

 

On December 11, 2024, Petitioners filed the instant petition. On December 17, 2024, Petitioners requested the arbitrator, Judge Foster to make a post settlement arbitration determination that Respondent be ordered to pay costs and prejudgment interest. (Kim Decl. ¶ 26, Exh. K.) On December 19, 2024, the arbitrator responded and deferred to the discretion of this Court to declare the appropriate forum for the recovery of costs. (Id.)

 

Discussion

 

C.C.P. § 1285.4 sets forth the requirements for a petition to confirm a binding arbitration award. By statute, the petition must do all of the following:

 

(1)    set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement;

 

(2)    set forth the name(s) of the arbitrator(s); and

 

(3)    set forth or have attached a copy of the award and written opinion of the arbitrator.

 

(C.C.P. § 1285.4(a)-(c).)

 

Here, arbitration is mandated by law, but Petitioners cannot comply with the requirements of C.C.P. § 1285.4(c) because no arbitration ever occurred. Both parties agree that the matter settled prior to arbitration. It is undisputable that no arbitration took place, and no arbitration award was ever made. As no arbitration award was ever made, there is no award to confirm.  The financial obligations between the parties is dictated by the settlement agreement entered into prior to and in lieu of arbitration.  Accordingly, the petition is defective and must be DENIED.

 

 

To clarify the prior petition filed with the Court. That previous petition granted jurisdiction pursuant to Insurance Code § 11580.2(f)(1) only for the purpose of discovery disputes. Once a dispute is submitted to arbitration, it is the province of the arbitrator to make all findings and rulings necessary for complete resolution of the dispute. (See Briggs v. Resolution Remedies (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1400-1401.) As such, any dispute here regarding costs and prejudgment interest is properly resolved by the arbitrator.

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Clarissa Esther Avila-Garduno (Clarissa) and Karla Nancy Garduno Hernandez (Karla) (collectively Plaintiffs) bring this action against Hai Van Le (Defendant) in connection with a January 13, 2023 motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs allege that Defendant negligently operated his motor vehicle such that he struck both Plaintiff Karla and her child, Clarissa, while they were crossing the street at Thienes Ave. in South El Monte, California.

 

Before the Court are the following six motions, two of which were continued from their original hearing date of January 10, 2025. All motions are bought by Defendant and are as follows:

 

·         Motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories as to Garduno

·         Motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories as to Garduno (Continued from January 10, 2025)

·         Motion to compel further responses to RFPD as to Garduno (Continued from January 10, 2025)

·         Motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories as to Hernandez

·         Motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories as to Hernandez

·         Motion to compel further responses to RFPD as to Hernandez

 

Plaintiff Clarissa appears to have filed oppositions to the motions regarding Special Interrogatories and RFPD, but has not filed an opposition for the motion regarding Form Interrogatories.  Plaintiff Karla has filed no opposition to any motion. Pursuant to C.R.C. Rule 8.54(c), a failure to oppose a motion may be deemed consent to its being granted.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories

 

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete, (2)¿an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response, or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. §¿2030.300(a).) The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections thereto. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-21.)

  

Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFPD

 

A motion to compel further responses to RFPD may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See C.C.P. § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Timeline

 

On June 3, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiffs’ counsel with their Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and RFPD. (Young Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) On July 23, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel correspondence reminding them that responses were due July 5, 2024. (Young Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. B.)  Defendant’s counsel provided until July 29, 2024 to provide answers without objections, lest he move forward with the instant motions. (Id.)

 

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiffs served their responses consisting of objections and unverified answers. (Young Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendant states that no verifications have been received to date. (Id.)

 

Discussion

 

The Court finds that under the circumstances described above, Defendant’s motions are more akin to a motion to compel initial responses than a motion to compel further responses. This is because none of the responses received by Defendant were verified by Plaintiffs. C.C.P. § 2030.250(a) provides that, “The party to whom the interrogatories are directed shall sign the response under oath unless the response contains only objections.” As Plaintiffs failed to verify their responses, Defendant has functionally never received answers to his discovery demands. (See Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636 [“Unverified responses are tantamount to no responses at all.”].)

 

As mentioned above, the only exception to this verification requirement is where the responses consist of nothing but objections. A review of the responses rendered here reveal that this is not the case. While Plaintiffs’ responses to each set of demands are laden with objections, they also contain factual assertions which require verification. Plaintiffs’ failure to verify their responses has resulted in an effective failure to respond.  As such, the Court sees little reason to engage in the analysis required by a motion to compel further responses.

 

The opposition papers do not refute the responses issued were both untimely and unverified. Plaintiffs’ counsel states that they immediately began to prepare responses upon receipt but their efforts at verification were thwarted when Plaintiffs refused to communicate. Plaintiffs’ counsel states that their office has experienced complete radio silence from Plaintiffs despite numerous attempts to reach them. (Myers Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.) The Court also notes that the oppositions advance no argument in support of the objections contained in those responses.

 

In short, the Court finds Plaintiffs should be compelled to respond to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and RFPD. As Plaintiffs failed to render responses prior to the statutory deadline (July 5, 2024), they have waived their objections. (See C.C.P. §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a).)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motions are GRANTED. Plaintiffs are ordered to serve verified and objection free responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and RFPD no later than February 14, 2024.

 

Sanctions

 

The Court has discretion to impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct. (C.C.P. § 2023.030(a).) Sanctions are only mandatory as against a party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories or RFPD, unless the Court finds they acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (C.C.P. §§ 2030.290(c) & 2031.300(c).) Regardless, it is customary to grant sanctions where a party has filed a motion to compel, and the other party fails to file an opposition. (C.R.C. Rule 3.1348(a).) 

 

Here, four of Defendant’s six motions remain unopposed. As concerns the two opposition that were rendered, the Court finds they were without substantial justification. While the Court understands the duty of Plaintiffs’ counsel to protect their client’s interests, the fact remains that Plaintiffs have no justification for their failure to communicate with their counsel or provide verified discovery responses. Plaintiffs cannot seek to avoid the imposition of discovery sanctions when their failure to respond has resulted from nothing other than their own neglect of the case.

 

Accordingly, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $1,321.02 as against Plaintiffs only. This amount reflects six hours of attorney work in preparing the motions at the stated $160.17 hourly rate, plus the $60 filing fee per motion. (Young Decl. ¶ 7.)

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Demurrer

 

The grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (C.C.P. § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal. 3d 311, 318.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Id.)

 

A demurrer assumes the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading. (Blank v. Kirwan, supra, 39 Cal. 3d at p. 318.) No matter how unlikely or improbable, the plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.  App. 3d 593, 604.) But this does not include contentions; deductions; conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.  (Blank, supra, 39 Cal. 3d at 318.)

 

Pursuant to C.C.P. §§ 430.10(e) and (f), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may demur to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, or that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous and/or unintelligible. It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal. 4th 1074, 1082.)

The Court notes that a party requesting judicial notice of material under Evidence Code section 452 or 453 must provide the court and each party with a copy of the material. (CRC Rule 5.115.) The only exception to this rule is where the matter to be noticed is already a part of the Court’s record, in which case the requesting party must specify the portion of the record to be noticed. (Id.) Here, Orsatti’s request is unaccompanied by the records to be noticed. Nor are the records to be noticed a part of the Court’s record. Accordingly, Orsatti’s request is DENIED.

 

Demurrer for Lack of Standing – Overruled

 

The Court first addresses Orsatti’s demurrer to the entire Complaint on grounds that Plaintiff does not have standing to sue. (C.C.P. § 430.10(b).) Orsatti argues:

 

Plaintiff is not the originator of the promissory note and deed of trust at issue, has not produced an assignment of the deed of trust, has not alleged possession of a promissory note that contains an allonge or is endorsed in blank, and therefore has not sufficiently alleged it has an equitable right to payments, debt or claims against Amherst Drive or standing to sue for Equitable Assignment of Lien.

 

(Dem. p. 7.)

 

The Court finds this argument unpersuasive. "In general terms, in order to have standing, the plaintiff must be able to allege injury - that is, some invasion of the plaintiff's legally protected interests.” (Angelucci v. Century Supper Club 41 Cal.4th 160, 175 [internal quotation marks omitted].) A demurrer lies for lack of standing when the defect appears on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (Qualified Patients Assn. v. City of Anaheim (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 734, 752.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has explicitly alleged that it purchased the Loan and all interests in the Note and Deed from Indymac. (Compl. ¶ 10.) Plaintiff further alleges that it, “…is the current holder and in possession of the original Note, the current payee under the Note, and is duly authorized to enforce the terms of the Note and Deed of Trust.” (Id.) As such, the Court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently pled its standing to state its claim. The Complaint clearly asserts that Plaintiff is the owner of the Note and seeks a declaration that the Deed was assigned to it by virtue of purchase from Indymac. Further, Plaintiff has adequately pled injury in the form of the gap in the chain of title for the Subjects Property.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer on these grounds is OVERRULED.

 

First COA – Equitable Assignment of Lien – Sustained with Leave to Amend

 

As concerns Plaintiff’s first cause of action is for “Equitable Assignment of Lien”, the Court agrees with Orsatti that the cause of action is uncertain as currently pled. To begin, the Court is unaware of a legally cognizable cause of action for “Equitable Assignment of Lien”. While the concepts of “Equitable Lien” and “Equitable Assignment” have basis in case law, no authority supports Plaintiff’s specific cause of action here. Nor do the factual allegations in the Complaint make clear whether a cause of action for Equitable Lien or Equitable Assignment are being stated.

 

Equitable Assignment is a legal concept which has developed through case law to address situations where the existence of an assignment is implied from the conduct of the parties rather than established by express words of formal agreement. (First Nat. Bank of Stockton v. Pomona Tile Mfg. Co. (1947) 82 Cal.App.2d 592, 606 [citation omitted].) Though not specifically provided for by statute, courts have founds a variety of situations warrant finding that an assignment was created in equity. (Id.) Despite this seemingly broad applicability of equitable assignments, the Court is unpersuaded that such an assignment is at issue here.

 

In Recorded Picture Co. v. Nelson Entertainment, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 350 (Nelson), the Second District Court of Appeal observed that, “The doctrine of equitable assignments is typically used to enforce an attempted assignment of rights that is technically defective or to create a right of subrogation.”

 

Neither case cited by Nelson concern situations where an actual assignment existed but was not properly recorded. Kelly v. Kelly (1938) 11 Cal.2d 356 concerned the creation of an equitable assignment where an interest in property had not yet shifted to an intended beneficiary under trust. (Id.) The Kelly court found that, “[E]quity will uphold assignments, not valid at law…which are vested, but relate to property to come into existence in the future, and those which rest only in possibility…” (Id. at 364.) In the Court’s estimation, Kelly did not contemplate the situation alleged here.

 

The second case cited in Nelson, Fidelity National Title Ins. Co. v. Miller (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1163, 1174 (Fidelity), is similarly inapposite. Fidelity described equitable assignment as, “…a creature of equity and applies to all cases where one party involuntarily pays a debt for which another is primarily liable and which in equity and good conscience should have been paid by the latter.” (Fidelity supra, 215 Cal.App.3d at 1174.) In other words, Fidelity observed that the concept of equitable assignment was essentially akin to what is modernly referred to as a right of subrogation. Fidelity simply does not provide guidance in the instant case.

 

In short, none of the existing authority discussing Equitable Assignments indicate that such a cause of action is applicable here. The same can be said of a cause of action for Equitable Lien.

 

An Equitable Lien, “…is a right to subject property not in the possession of the lienor to the payment of a debt as a charge against that property…It may arise from a contract which reveals an intent to charge particular property with a debt or out of general considerations of right and justice as applied to the relations of the parties and the circumstances of their dealings.” (County of Los Angeles v. Construction Laborers Trust Funds for Southern California Admin. Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 410, 415.)

 

“It has been judicially observed that equitable liens arising by contract are as various as the contracts parties may make, and the question whether a lien has been created under particular circumstances depends upon the facts of the case…For example, where a lessor contracted to purchase insurance on behalf of both the lessor and the lessee but purchased protection for itself only, and a loss ensued for which the lessor was reimbursed, the lessee is entitled to an equitable lien on the insurance proceeds.” (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 454, citing Gordon v. J. C. Penney Co. (1970) 7 Cal. App. 3d 280, 285.)

 

Here, the Court does not view Plaintiff’s allegations as pled support a cause of action for Equitable Lien as against Orsatti. The cases discussing equitable liens all concern situations where the party seeking to enforce the lien and the party against whom it was to be enforced were in direct contract with another. This is important because the intent of the parties to encumber the property at issue is a crucial determination in whether to create an equitable lien. (See In re Cedar Funding, Inc. (Bankr. N.D. Cal. 2009) 408 B.R. 299 [discussing California law].) Conversely, the assignment at issue in this case has nothing to do with the intent of the initially contracting parties to encumber the Subject Property. The dispute here is not whether the Subject Property is encumbered under the original lien. Rather, the dispute is whether Plaintiff is now the lawful holder of said lien. In the Court’s view, these allegations are simply not the subject of an action for equitable lien.

 

Upon thorough search, the Court is unable to locate any case where an equitable assignment or equitable lien was granted in favor of an assignee as the result of a previous lienholder’s failure to record an assignment. This is not to say that such causes of action are unavailable to Plaintiff, only that the first cause of action is stated so ambiguously that determining the sufficiency of its factual allegations is impossible. Further, by apparently combining a cause of action for Equitable Assignment with that of an Equitable Lien, Plaintiff has presented significant difficulty for Orsatti in responding. 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days’ leave to amend. Leave to amend is granted in so far as the Court finds its possible Plaintiff could amend the Complaint to state a more cognizable cause of action for either Equitable Assignment or Equitable Lien.

 

Second COA - Declaratory Relief – Overruled

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

 

Orsatti argues that Plaintiff has not sufficiently stated a cause of action for Declaratory Relief because they have not alleged an actual controversy. Orsatti states the following:

 

Given that Plaintiff is not the originator of the promissory note and deed of trust at issue, has not produced an assignment of the deed of trust, and has not alleged possession of a promissory note that contains an allonge or is endorsed in blank, Plaintiff has failed to show that there is an actual, present controversy that would warrant declaratory relief.

 

(Dem. p. 8.)

 

The Court finds Orsatti’s argument to be unpersuasive for reasons that mirror its analysis as to Plaintiff’s standing. As stated above, Plaintiff has specifically alleged that it is the holder of the Note and entitled to collect upon it. Whether Plaintiff can produce the Note and subsequently show they are entitled to a declaration proclaiming them to be the legal owner is a question beyond the purview of a demurrer. On the face of the pleadings, Plaintiff has demonstrated sufficient controversy as to the chain of title for the Subject Property.
