Case Number: 18STCV04752    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 49

Kurt Knutsson, et al. v. Judith Salkow Shapiro, et al.


DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE THE THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT
 

MOVING PARTIES: Defendants Judith Salkow Shapiro, A.P.C., and San Pasqual Fiduciary Trust Company

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc. bring this suit against the law corporation of their deceased former lawyer, Judith Salkow Shapiro, for legal malpractice and fraud in the handling of an underlying employment and right of publicity action against KTLA, Local TV, and other television stations.  The operative Third Amended Complaint asserts causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Professional Negligence/Legal Malpractice, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty.  

Defendants now move to strike portions of the Third Amended Complaint. Plaintiffs opposed.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendants’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED. The portions of the TAC identified in the notice of motion (see Notice p. 2, lines 6-13) are hereby ordered stricken.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.

DISCUSSION:

Motion to Strike 

I. Legal Standard

A motion to strike lies either (1) to strike any irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) to strike any pleading or part thereof not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule or order of court.  (CCP § 436.)
II. Meet and Confer

The declaration of attorney Adam A. Ainslie reflects the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

III. Analysis

Defendants move to strike portions of the Third Amended Complaint. Plaintiffs initially named the beneficiaries of the Shapiro Family Trust as Defendants in this action, but dismissed them without prejudice pursuant to a tolling agreement. Plaintiffs then moved this court to vacate the dismissals. At the February 15, 2024 hearing on that motion, all parties orally stipulated to bring the beneficiaries back into the action. The court gave Plaintiffs leave to file a Third Amended Complaint to do so. 

Defendants now contend, however, that Plaintiffs “went well beyond what was agreed and permitted, and filed an amended complaint with numerous additional allegations against Shapiro.” (Mtn. 3: 9-10.) 

The court agrees that Plaintiffs filed an amended complaint with allegations that exceed the scope of leave granted by this court and understood by the parties. The purpose of the amendments was to add the beneficiaries back into the action, as well as allegations showing that the statute of limitations would not be an issue. As stated at the hearing on the record, a simple reference to the tolling agreement would be sufficient to accomplish this. Nothing more, nothing less.

Plaintiffs were not given leave, however, to supplement the pleading with allegations to bolster their claims against Shapiro. Plaintiffs feigned ignorance or dispute of this point in their opposition based on the lack of a transcript with the moving papers is unavailing and disingenuous. 

If Plaintiffs wish to file an amended pleading containing additional allegations against Shapiro, they must file a noticed motion for leave to file an amended complaint. This court is well aware of the policy favoring liberal amendments (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428), and that absent prejudice to the adverse party, the court may permit amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761 [internal quotes omitted].) These are considerations to be made if and when a proper motion for leave appears before this court.

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED. The portions of the TAC identified in the notice of motion (see Notice p. 2, lines 6-13) are hereby ordered stricken.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Dated:   May 13, 2024 ___________________________________
Randolph M. Hammock
Judge of the Superior Court

DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO THE THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT
 

MOVING PARTIES: Defendants, Susan Salkow Shapiro Moss, Kenneth Salkow-Shapiro, and Ben Shapiro

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiffs Kurt Knutsson and Woojivas, Inc. bring this suit against the law corporation of their deceased former lawyer, Judith Salkow Shapiro, for legal malpractice and fraud in the handling of an underlying employment and right of publicity action against KTLA, Local TV, and other television stations.  The operative Third Amended Complaint asserts causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Professional Negligence/Legal Malpractice, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, and (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty.  

Defendant Beneficiaries now demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint. Plaintiffs opposed.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendants’ Demurrer as to all Causes of Action asserted against Kenneth and Barbara as purported Trustees of the Trusts is SUSTAINED. Generally speaking, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Plaintiffs are given limited leave to substitute the appropriate trustee(s), if applicable.

Defendants’ Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the remaining Defendants.

Defendants are to file an Answer to the 3AC within 21 days.

DISCUSSION:

Demurrer

I. Meet and Confer

The Declaration of Alexandra L. Letzel, Counsel for Defendants, reflects that the parties met and conferred.

II. Judicial Notice

Pursuant to Defendants’ request, the court takes judicial notice of Exhibits 1 and 2. (See Paragraph (IV)(A), infra, for further discussion.)

III. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.  (CCP § 430.30(a).)  A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (Id.)  The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.  (Hahn, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)  

IV. Analysis

A. Demurrer as to Trustees

First, the Beneficiaries argue that each cause of action against Kenneth, as Trustee of the Shapiro Family Trust FBO Kenneth Salkow-Shapiro (“Kenneth’s Trust”), and Barbara Zipkin (“Barbara”), as Trustee of the Shapiro Family Trust FBO Ben Shapiro (“Ben’s Trust”), fail because (1) Kenneth is not and never has been the Trustee of Kenneth’s Trust and (2) Barbara resigned as Trustee of Ben’s Trust after the tolling agreement was executed. 

In support of these contentions, the Beneficiaries ask the court to take judicial notice of the Tolling Agreement that Plaintiffs’ signed and attached to their counsel’s declaration in support of their Motion to Vacate, which reflects that Kenneth’s Trust has a corporate trustee. (See RJN, Exh. 1.) They also ask the court to take judicial notice of a copy of Ms. Zipkin’s fully executed and notarized resignation, which reflects she is no longer a Trustee. (RJN, Exh. 2.) 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue only that the Beneficiaries’ requests for judicial notice is improper because the facts are reasonably in dispute. 

Defendants’ Exhibit 1 is the tolling agreement submitted by Plaintiffs in support of their previous motion to vacate. It lists a corporate entity as Trustee. But Plaintiffs argue this “may be nothing more than a typing error.” (Opp. 15: 16-17.) 

Absent more, Plaintiffs’ have failed to demonstrate that the Exhibit is reasonably subject to dispute. Merely claiming the potential of a typing error is insufficient. Exhibit 1 therefore establishes that Kenneth is not Trustee of the Shapiro Family Trust FBO Kenneth Salkow-Shapiro. Accordingly, any claims against him as Trustee fail.

Defendants’ Exhibit 2 is a notarized document indicating Barbara Zipkin’s resignation as co-Trustee of the Shapiro Family GST Exempt Trust FBO Ben Shapiro. (See RJN, Exh. 2.) Plaintiffs argue this “declaration” is not a proper subject of judicial notice. However, the Exhibit is not a declaration, but a notarized document showing Zipkin’s resignation. Again, Plaintiffs have failed to show that this document is reasonably subject to dispute. Moreover, courts may take judicial notice of “facts that can be deduced, and/or clearly derived from, [a document’s] legal effect, such as the names and dates contained in the document, and the legal consequences of the document. [Citations]. This is different from taking judicial notice of the truth of specific factual representations within a document.” (Julian Volunteer Fire Co. Assn. v. Julian-Cuyamaca Fire Prot. Dist. (2021) 62 Cal. App. 5th 583, 600.)

Therefore, Exhibit 2 establishes that Barbara is no longer Trustee of the Shapiro Family Trust FBO Ben Shapiro. 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer as to all Causes of Action asserted against Kenneth and Barbara as purported Trustees of the Trusts is SUSTAINED. Generally speaking, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Plaintiffs are given limited leave to substitute the appropriate trustee(s), if applicable.

B. Demurrer Based on Probate Code

Next, the Beneficiaries argue the claims against them fail because Plaintiffs have failed to plead facts supporting liability against them. 

Probate Code Section 19400 states:

Subject to Section 366.2 of the Code of Civil Procedure, if there is no proceeding to administer the probate estate of the deceased settlor, and if the trustee does not file a proposed notice to creditors pursuant to Section 19003 and does not publish notice to creditors pursuant to Chapter 3 (commencing with Section 19040), then a beneficiary of the trust to whom payment, delivery, or transfer of the deceased settlor’s property is made pursuant to the terms of the trust is personally liable, to the extent provided in Section 19402, for the unsecured claims of the creditors of the deceased settlor’s probate estate.”

Section 19402 provides, in pertinent part, that “personal liability under this chapter is applicable only to the extent the claim of the creditor cannot be satisfied out of the trust estate of the deceased settlor and is limited to a pro rata portion of the claim of the creditor, based on the proportion that the value of the property distributed to the person out of the trust estate…” (Cal. Prob. Code. § 19402(b) [emphasis added].) 

The crux of the Beneficiaries argument is that Probate Code section 19400 & 19402 relief cannot be invoked here because there are no allegations demonstrating that Shapiro’s revocable trust is inadequate to satisfy the claim. The beneficiaries argue the mere allegation based on information and belief that that the trust is inadequate is insufficient. (See TAC ¶ 11 [“Plaintiffs are further informed and believe and on that basis allege that the assets of the Shapiro Family Trust are not sufficient to satisfy a judgment in the amount that Plaintiffs’ damages expert has estimated will be owing to Plaintiffs in this action, which totals between $8 million and upwards of $10 million”].)

Under California’s “fact-pleading” rule, allegations need only provide fair notice of the claim and must be liberally construed. (Medical Marijuana, Inc. v. ProjectCBD.com (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 869, 886.) The plaintiff satisfies this burden by alleging ultimate, as opposed to evidentiary facts that “ ‘as a whole apprise[ ] the adversary of the factual basis of the claim. [Citations.]’ ” (Id.) The essential elements to be pleaded “are determined by the substantive law that defines the cause of action.” (Foster v. Sexton (2021) 61 Cal. App. 5th 998, 1018.) In addition, “[l]ess specificity is required when ‘it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy.’” (Comm. On Children's Television, Inc. v. Gen. Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal. 3d 197, 217.)

Here, it appears from the allegations that the relevant facts rest within the knowledge of the Defendants. For pleadings purposes, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged that the assets of the Shapiro Family Trust are not sufficient to satisfy the potential judgment. Nothing more is required at the pleading stage. 

Next, the Beneficiaries assert that claims against them are improper so long as the underlying claims are pending. As the court understands that argument, the Beneficiaries suggest that any relief against them can only be sought after a judgment has been entered against the Law Corporation and Trust. 

While the court agrees that the Probate Code and cases interpreting it appear to contemplate this two-step scenario, the court is unaware of any authority requiring that the claims proceed separately or in two separate actions. Notably, section 19402 refers to “claims,” not “judgments.” The Probate Code defines claims as “a demand for payment…whether due, not due, accrued or not accrued, or contingent, and whether liquidated or unliquidated.” (§ 19000(a). Thus, Plaintiffs’ may pursue their pending “claims” against the beneficiaries, even though it is not certain at this time that the assets of the Shapiro Family Trust will be insufficient to satisfy the potential judgment.  [FN 1]

Be that as it may, and contrary to Plaintiffs’ contention, this court is equally unconvinced the law requires that the claims must proceed together in this manner. This is all to say that Plaintiffs’ course of action appears permissible, but not mandatory.

Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint on this ground is OVERRULED.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Dated:   May 13, 2024 ___________________________________
Randolph M. Hammock
Judge of the Superior Court

FN 1 - Of course, these Code sections and cases interpreting them may dictate the procedure by which Plaintiffs can ultimately attempt to collect on any potential judgment in this matter. The court takes no position on that procedure at this time. It concludes only that the matter may proceed as pled.

Case Number: 23STCV12356    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 49

Melani Cargle M.D., M.B.A. v. The County of Los Angeles, et al.


DEFENDANT THE REGENTS OF THE UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA’S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT
 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant The Regents of the University of California (“Regents”)

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Melani Cargle

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

Plaintiff Melani Cargle obtained her medical degree from UCLA. Plaintiff alleges she is “African American, female, and queer,” and suffers from ADHD. While attending medical school, Plaintiff alleges a supervising physician, Dr. Louis Kwong, M.D., carried a concealed firearm in the hospital and frequently made racist, sexist, and homophobic remarks. Plaintiff complained to medical school leadership, but the issues were never resolved. Plaintiff alleges that in November or December of 2019, she published a detailed complaint of Dr. Kwong in the Orthopedic-Match Document, which resulted in an investigation of Dr. Kwong. Plaintiff alleges she was later passed for a residency at UCLA based on her complaints. 

In March 2020, Plaintiff started her residency at USC/Keck. As part of her residency, Plaintiff was required to obtain employment with the County. Plaintiff alleges she faced abuse and racism at the hands of supervising physicians, and that the program failed to offer her accommodations for her ADHD. On May 19, 2022, the County provided Plaintiff with a Notice of Intent to Discharge. The County cited alleged “discourteous, unprofessional, and unethical conduct towards co-workers and patients” and “failure to perform duties as required” as reasons for Plaintiff’s intended discharge.

After her discharge, Plaintiff made posts on Twitter about the program and started a GoFundMe. In or about June of 2022, Plaintiff alleges the County sent out a county-wide email, also posted on twitter, personally attacking Plaintiff by name.

Plaintiff now brings causes of action for (1) discrimination on the basis of race, sex, or sexual orientation, (2) harassment on the basis of race, sex, or sexual orientation, (3) discrimination on the basis of disability, (4) retaliation, (5) Violation of Health and Safety Code section 1278.5, (6) wrongful termination, (7) failure to reasonably accommodate, (8) failure to engage in the interactive process, (9) aiding and abetting, and (10) defamation.

Defendant Regents now demurrers to the ninth cause of action in the FAC for aiding and abetting. Plaintiff opposed.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Ninth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED. Generally speaking, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Because it appears here that no reasonable possibility of successful amendment exists, no leave to amend is given.

Defendant’s Demurrer to punitive damages is OVERRULED.

Defendant Regents are ordered to file an Answer to the FAC within 21 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice, unless waived.

DISCUSSION:

Demurrer

I. Meet and Confer

The declaration of attorney Adrienne T. Subido reflects that Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to defense counsel’s meet and confer attempts, which demonstrates the lack of any substantive meet and confer. 

This court exercises its discretion to consider the motion absent a sufficient meet and confer. The parties are admonished to comply with all meet and confer obligations in good faith going forward.

II. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.  (CCP § 430.30(a).)  A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (Id.)  The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.  (Hahn, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

III. Analysis

A. Demurrer to Ninth Cause of Action

Defendant Regents demurrers to Plaintiff’s Ninth Cause of Action for aiding and abetting. Defendant argues the claim fails because it is a public entity that is immune from common law torts like aiding and abetting.

“Except as otherwise provided by statute,” a public entity “is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person.” (Govt. Code § 815(a).) “As that language indicates, the intent of the Tort Claims Act is to confine potential governmental liability, not expand it.” (Eastburn v. Reg'l Fire Prot. Auth. (2003) 31 Cal. 4th 1175, 1179.) “If the Legislature has not created a statutory basis for it, there is no government tort liability.” (State ex rel. Dep't of California Highway Patrol v. Superior Ct. (2015) 60 Cal. 4th 1002, 1009.)

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Regents “aided and abetted Defendants County; LACDHS; LAC + USC; USC; USC KSOM; and DOES in their various tortious activities,” which includes “discrimination and harassment on the basis of race, sex, and/or sexual
Orientation,” among other things. (FAC ¶ 176.) 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that because her aiding and abetting claim is “derived from” the statutory claims asserted against the other Defendants, Defendant is not immune from the claim. Plaintiff notes that a claim for aiding and abetting is not a “standalone” claim, as it is generally dependent on the existence of an underlying tort. (See Nasrawi v. Buck Consultants LLC (2014) 231 Cal. App. 4th 328, 344 [“there can be no aiding and abetting liability absent the commission of an underlying tort”].)

While the point is a fair one, Plaintiff has provided no authority clearly recognizing liability against the government for aiding and abetting. Just as a claim against the government must be established by statute, this court also suspects that a claim for aiding and abetting must also be provided by statute. There is no clear indication here that the legislature has done so for any of the claims here. (See, e.g., Vernon v. State of California (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 114, 13 [holding that “liability cannot be imposed upon the State under the aiding and abetting provisions of the FEHA”].) 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the Ninth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED. Generally speaking, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Because it appears here that no possibility of successful amendment exists, no leave to amend is given.

B. Demurrer to Punitive Damages

Defendant also demurrers to Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. It is well settled that the appropriate vehicle to attack a request for punitive damages is a motion to strike, not a demurrer. A general demurrer challenges only the sufficiency of the cause of action pleaded and must be overruled if any valid cause of action is pleaded; a demand for improper relief does not vitiate an otherwise valid cause of action. (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal. App. 4th 1547, 1561-1562; see also Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal. App. 3d 159, 163 [“Since a demurrer does not lie to a part of a cause of action[,] …punitive damage allegations [are] not subject to real parties' demurrers”].)

Accordingly, on that procedural basis alone, Defendant’s Demurrer to punitive damages is OVERRULED.  Of course, The Regents are certainly free to attack the punitive damages request against it by means of a motion for summary adjudication, if warranted under the applicable law and facts. See, C.C.P. § 437c (f)(1).

IT IS SO ORDERED.
