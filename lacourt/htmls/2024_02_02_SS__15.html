DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV36914    Hearing Date: February 2, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Plaintiffs Randy Bronson, Ricky Bronson, Robby Bronson, and Joy Bronson filed this action alleging that decedent Ronald Bronson developed mesothelioma from exposure to Defendant Sherwin-Williams Company’s products.  On November 30, 2021, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgement arguing Plaintiffs cannot prove exposure to asbestos in Defendant’s products.  Plaintiffs did not oppose the motion.

A defendant seeking summary judgment must “conclusively negate[] a necessary element of the plaintiff’s case, or . . . demonstrate[] that under no hypothesis is there a material issue of fact that requires the process of trial.”  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 334.)  To show that a plaintiff cannot establish an element of a cause of action, a defendant must make the initial showing “that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)  “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence – as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.”  (Id. at p. 855.)  A plaintiff’s deposition testimony that the plaintiff has no knowledge of any exposure to the defendant’s products may be sufficient to shift the burden to the plaintiff to demonstrate the existence of triable issues of fact.  (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104.)  The plaintiff’s deposition testimony that he did not recall ever working with a product manufactured by the defendant may not be sufficient to shift the burden if the plaintiff is able to prove his case by another means.  (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1439.)  “‘If plaintiffs respond to comprehensive interrogatories seeking all known facts with boilerplate answers that restate their allegations, or simply provide laundry lists of people and/or documents, the burden of production will almost certainly be shifted to them once defendants move for summary judgment and properly present plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses.’” (Id. at p. 1440.)

Defendant argues Plaintiffs cannot establish that Ronald Bronson was exposed to asbestos in Defendant’s paint.  (Motion at p. 3.)  Bronson’s son testified Bronson used Defendant’s Block-Tek paint between the mid-1960s up to 1980.  (Undisputed Material Fact “UMF”) 3-5.)  He recalled seeing his father sand the paint only in 1980.  (UMF 7.)  Bronson’s other sons did not see Bronson work with Defendant’s products.  (UMF 11, 12.)  Defendant’s witness testified that Bloc-Tek contained a small amount of asbestos up to about 1973.  (UMF 21.)  Defendant’s expert testified that asbestos in Bloc-Tek paint is encapsulated and therefore does not become airborne during normal use, and no industrial hygiene studies show asbestos fiber release or exposure to asbestos from sanding paint.  (UMF 23, 24-26.)

Defendant established that by 1980 when Bronson sanded Defendant’s paint, it did not contain asbestos.  And even if Bronson sanded Defendant’s paint when he used it pre-1973 (although there is no evidence of that), Defendant established such sanding did not expose him to asbestos.  Defendant’s evidence of lack of exposure to asbestos shifted the burden to Plaintiffs.  Because Plaintiffs did not file an opposition, they did not show any disputed issue of material fact.

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Defendant is to file a proposed judgment within five day.

The moving party is to give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV01373    Hearing Date: February 2, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT (PLASTIC SALES SOUTHERN)

On January 20, 2023, Plaintiffs Roberto E. Sotomayor and Patricia Bilgin filed this action alleging Sotomayor developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure to asbestos-containing products from Defendant Plastic Sales Southern, Inc.  On November 15, 2023, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment, and alternatively a motion for summary adjudication of the fraud cause of action and request for punitive damages. 

Plaintiffs’ Objection Nos. 1-6:  The court did not rely on this evidence.

A defendant seeking summary judgment must “conclusively negate[] a necessary element of the plaintiff’s case, or . . . demonstrate[] that under no hypothesis is there a material issue of fact that requires the process of trial.”  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 334.)  To show that a plaintiff cannot establish an element of a cause of action, a defendant must make the initial showing “that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)  “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence – as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.”  (Id. at p. 855.)  A plaintiff’s deposition testimony that the plaintiff has no knowledge of any exposure to the defendant’s products may be sufficient to shift the burden to the plaintiff to demonstrate the existence of triable issues of fact. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104.)  The plaintiff’s deposition testimony that he did not recall ever working with a product manufactured by the defendant may not be sufficient to shift the burden if the plaintiff is able to prove his case by another means.  (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1439.)  “ ‘If plaintiffs respond to comprehensive interrogatories seeking all known facts with boilerplate answers that restate their allegations, or simply provide laundry lists of people and/or documents, the burden of production will almost certainly be shifted to them once defendants move for summary judgment and properly present plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses.’”  (Id. at p. 1440.)

Defendant contends Plaintiffs have no evidence Sotomayor was exposed to asbestos-containing products from Defendant, citing Plaintiffs’ interrogatory responses.  (Motion at p. 13; Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) 3, 4.)  In response to interrogatories asking for all facts supporting Plaintiffs’ contention that Defendant is liable, Plaintiffs respond vaguely and without any specific evidence that Sotomayor was exposed to asbestos from Defendant’s machining plastic parts, including plastic molding compounds, phenolic molding compounds, phenolic resin materials, and Micarta.  (Defendant’s Index, Ex. B at pp. 1-3.)  Plaintiffs identified Sotomayor as a person with knowledge of facts of his exposure, along with unnamed people associated with Defendant.  (Id. at p. 5.)  In response to requests for production of all documents supporting exposure to asbestos from products supplied by Defendant, Plaintiffs generally identified Sotomayor’s interrogatory responses, deposition testimony, and his employment and medical records.  (Defendant’s Index, Ex. D at pp. 1-2.) 

Defendant also cites Sotomayor’s deposition testimony that his company, S&K Precision Engineering, worked with a lot of material from Plastic Sales, purchased Micarta from Plastic Sales, placed orders from Plastic Sales, and had an account with Plastic Sales.  (Defendant’s Index, Ex. I at pp. 655-656, 657, 690, 691, 698.)  He does not know the ingredients of the parts he purchased from Plastic Sales or the brand of the materials he bought from Plastic Sales and has no documents about the sales with Plastic Sales.  (Id. at pp. 695, 698.) 

None of these discovery responses or testimony contained evidence that Sotomayor was exposed to asbestos from Defendant’s products.  Thus Defendant shifted the burden.

In opposition, Plaintiffs emphasize that Sotomayor bought Micarta, which they say was manufactured by Westinghouse, from Defendant.  (Opposition at p. 12.)  However, Plaintiffs submit no evidence that Micarta contained asbestos.

Plaintiffs contend in their opposition that they need a continuance of the hearing because of outstanding discovery.  (Motion at p. 13.)  Plaintiffs say they noticed the deposition of Defendant.  (Ibid.)  However, that deposition took place on January 22, 2024, eleven days ago.  (Steward Decl. at p. 2.)  Therefore there is no need for a continuance.  Further, Plaintiffs filed this lawsuit against Defendant on January 20, 2023.  Thus, Plaintiffs had many months to depose Defendant.  Instead, Plaintiffs waited until December 29, 2023, six weeks after being served with Defendant’s motion on November 15, 2023, to serve the deposition notice.  If Plaintiffs had served the deposition notice immediately after November 15, 2023, the deposition could have taken place many weeks ago.  Plaintiffs should no good cause for their lack of diligence.

The motion is GRANTED.  Defendant is to file a proposed judgment within five days.

The moving party is to give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV16061    Hearing Date: February 2, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTIONS TO QUASH (IMI FABI)

            Plaintiff Maria Lozano filed this action alleging she developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure to asbestos in products from Mary Kay, Revlon, Max Factor, and Johnson & Johnson.  (Opposition at pp. 1-2.)  Defendants IMI Fabi (USA), Inc., IMI Fabi, LLC, and IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC filed motions to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction.  Plaintiff asked for jurisdictional discovery, so the court continued the hearing date.  The parties filed supplemental briefs.

A defendant may move to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)  The court may dismiss without prejudice the complaint in whole, or as to that defendant, when dismissal is made pursuant to Section 418.10.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 581, subd. (h).) 

“A court of this state may exercise jurisdiction on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.)  “The Due Process Clause protects an individual’s liberty interest in not being subject to the binding judgments of a forum with which he has established no meaningful ‘contacts, ties, or relations.’”  (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 471-472.)  A state court may not exercise personal jurisdiction over a party under circumstances that would offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.”  (Asahi Metal Industry Co., Ltd., v. Superior Court of California, Solano County (1987) 480 U.S. 102, 113.) 

When a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.  (Jayone Foods, Inc. v. Aekyung Industrial Co. Ltd. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 543, 553.)  Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, the defendant has the burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.  (Ibid.)  “The plaintiff must provide specific evidentiary facts, through affidavits and other authenticated documents, sufficient to allow the court to independently conclude whether jurisdiction is appropriate.  [Citation.]  The plaintiff cannot rely on allegations in an unverified complaint or vague and conclusory assertions of ultimate facts.  [Citation.]”  (Strasner v. Touchstone Wireless Repair & Logistics, LP (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 215, 222.)

A defendant is subject to a state’s general jurisdiction if its contacts “are so continuance and systematic as to render [it] essentially at home in the forum State.”  (Saimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 127.)  A nonresident defendant may be subject to the specific jurisdiction of the forum “if the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits [citation], and the ‘controversy is related to or “arises out of” a defendant’s contacts with the forum.’  [Citations.]”  (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446.)  This test does not require a “causal relationship between the defendant’s in-state activity and the litigation.”  (Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial District Court (2021) 141 S.Ct. 1017, 1026.)  The “arise out” of standard “asks about causation,” but “relate to” does not.  (Ibid.)  “[W]hen a corporation has ‘continuously and deliberately exploited [a State’s] market, it must reasonably anticipate being haled into [that State’s] court[s]’ to defend actions ‘based on’ products causing injury there.”  (Id. at p. 1027.) 

IMI Fabi (USA), Inc. states it is the holding company of IMI Fabi, LLC and never did any business in connection with California.  (See Zuppini Decl. generally.)  IMI Fabi, LLC states it is a West Virginia company, and before 2005 it sold only industrial grade talc for non-cosmetic applications and never did any business in connection with California.  (See Woods Decl. generally.)  IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC states it is a North Carolia company with its headquarters in New York until it closed in 2004 and had no connections to California.  (See Brown Decl. generally.)

Plaintiff does not contest that there is no general jurisdiction over Defendants.  Instead, Plaintiff argues without evidence that IMI Fabi LLC, IMI Fabi (USA) Inc., and IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC “supplied asbestos-containing talc” to manufacturers of products” that she used.  (Oppositions at p. 1.)  Plaintiff cites no evidence for these assertions and does not identify the talcum powder and cosmetic products for which Defendants allegedly provided the talc.  Instead, Plaintiff points to the allegations of her complaint as sufficient to defeat this motion.  (Opposition at p. 4.)  That is contrary to the law; a plaintiff must submit evidence, not allegations.  (Strasner, supra, 5 Cal.App.5th at p. 222.)

            Plaintiff then points to evidence about Avon products.  (Opposition at p. 4.)  But Plaintiff does not state that she used Avon products.  She states she used Mary Kay, Revlon, Max Factor, and Johnson & Johnson products (Opposition at p. 1), and Avon is not a defendant in this case.  Therefore sales from Defendant to Avon are irrelevant to this case. 

            In her supplemental opposition, Plaintiff contends IMI Fabi Diana sold Talc 141 to Revlon.  (Supp. Opposition at pp. 1-2.)  Plaintiff says there was a distribution agreement between IMI Fabi (Plaintiff does not identify which of the three entities) and Cosmetic Specialty for Cosmetic Specialty to distribute IMI Fabi’s talc, including in California.  (Supp. Opposition at p. 3.)  However, the existence of the distribution agreement and sales to Revlon do not establish that Plaintiff’s claim arises out of or relates to Defendants IMI Fabi (USA), Inc.’s, IMI Fabi, LLC’s, or IMI Fabi (Diana)’s contacts with California.

            Plaintiff states she needs more discovery because IMI Fabi (USA), Inc., IMI Fabi, LLC, and IMI Fabi (Diana) have not yet produced documents requested by Plaintiff.

The court continues the hearing to April 25, 2024 at 9 a.m.  If Plaintiff is going to file a motion to compel jurisdictional discovery, it must file the motion forthwith.  The parties may file supplemental briefs according to the statutory schedule.

            The moving parties are to give notice.
