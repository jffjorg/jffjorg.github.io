DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV37567    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

May 31, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV37567

MOTION

Motion to Stay Pending Appeal

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Dale Prokupek, M.D.; Dale Prokupek, M.D. Inc.; and Millennium Medical Associates, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Pericles Chamis

 

BACKGROUND

 

On or about November 4, 2020, Plaintiff Pericles Chamis (“Plaintiff”) underwent an upper endoscopy procedure, during which “Plaintiff began vomiting and convulsing on the operating table.”  (Fifth Amended Complaint [“5AC”] ¶¶ 20, 26.)  A subsequent MRI revealed that Plaintiff suffered a stroke, causing central visual impairment, dysphagia, and cognitive and behavioral issues.  (5AC ¶ 32.) 

 

Plaintiff brought suit against the surgeon, Defendant Dale Prokupek, M.D. and Defendant Dale Prokupek, M.D., Inc. (“Prokupek”); Dr. Prokupek’s company, Defendant Millennium Medical Associates, Inc. (“Millennium”); Dr. Prokupek’s employee Defendant Andrew Gelis (“Gelis”); anesthesiologist Defendant Michelle Dinh, M.D. (“Dinh”), Defendant Soma Surgery Center, Inc. (“Soma”), where the endoscopy was performed; Defendant Sean Ravaei, owner and operator of Soma (“Ravaei”); nurse practitioner Defendant Jeanne Hershey-Weber (“Hershey-Weber”); and nurse Defendant Melanie Kerr (“Kerr”); alleging three causes of action: (1) professional negligence – medical malpractice; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; and (3) intentional misrepresentation.

 

On February 27, 2024, the Court granted Millennium’s Motion for Summary Judgment, and Judgment was entered on March 14, 2024.  On May 13, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of the Court’s order on Millennium’s Motion for Summary Judgment.  The same day, on May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Appeal of the judgment entered in Millennium’s favor.

 

Similarly, on April 5, 2024, the Court granted Kerr’s Motion for Summary Judgment, and Judgment was entered on May 3, 2024.   On May 15, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of the Court’s Order on Kerr’s Motion for Summary Judgment.  Plaintiff has not yet appealed the judgment in favor of Kerr.

 

Defendants Prokupek and Prokupek, M.D. (“Moving Defendants”) have moved to stay the proceedings, including the trial, pending the resolution of Plaintiff’s appeal of the Millennium judgment and anticipated appeal of the Kerr judgment.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

In general, “the perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 916, subd. (a).)  Furthermore, “When there is a stay of proceedings other than the enforcement of the judgment, the trial court shall have jurisdiction of proceedings related to the enforcement of the judgment as well as any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order appealed from.”  (Id. at subd. (b).) 

 

            The purpose of the automatic stay provision “is to protect the appellate court’s jurisdiction by preserving the status quo until the appeal is decided.  The [automatic stay] prevents the trial court from rendering an appeal futile by altering the appealed judgment or order by conducting other proceedings that may affect it.”  (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 189 (hereafter Varian).)  The provision “stays all further trial court proceedings ‘upon the matters embraced’ in or ‘affected’ by the appeal.”  (Ibid.)  “In determining whether a proceeding is embraced in or affected by the appeal, we must consider the appeal and its possible outcomes in relation to the proceeding and its possible results.”  (Ibid.)  Whether a proceeding is “embraced” or “affected” by the appeal turns on whether the proceedings would affect the “effectiveness” of the appeal.  (Ibid.)  Proceedings affecting the effectiveness of the appeal are stayed; other proceedings are not.  (Ibid.) 

 

            That a “proceeding may render the appeal moot is not, by itself, enough to establish that the proceeding affects the effectiveness of the appeal” sufficient to warrant a stay.  (Varian, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 189.)  “Rather, something more is needed.  For example, the trial court proceeding must directly or indirectly seek to enforce, vacate or modify [the] appealed judgment or order.”  (Ibid.)  Alternatively, “the proceeding must substantially interfere with the appellate court’s ability to conduct the appeal.  (Id. at p. 190.) 

 

“A trial court proceeding also affects the effectiveness of an appeal if the possible outcomes on appeal and the actual or possible results of the proceeding are irreconcilable.”  (Varian, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 190.)  For example, terminating jurisdiction over spousal support is irreconcilable with possible outcomes on appeal of the denial to vacate the spousal support order.  (Ibid.)  Similarly, “an appeal from a judgment on the pleadings precludes a trial court from granting leave to amend the complaint because affirmance of the judgment is irreconcilable with an order granting leave to amend.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“Finally, a proceeding affects the effectiveness of the appeal if the very purpose of the appeal is to avoid the need for that proceeding.”  (Varian, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 190.)  For example, “an appeal from the denial of a motion to compel arbitration automatically stays all further court proceedings on the merits” because the trial court proceedings themselves are “inherently inconsistent with a possible outcome on appeal.”  (Ibid.)  Similarly, because the purpose of the anti-SLAPP statute is to protect litigants from being “dragged through the courts because [they] exercised [their] constitutional rights […] an appeal from the denial of an anti-SLAPP motion is no different than an appeal from the denial of a motion to compel arbitration.”  (Id. at p. 193.)

 

But “an appeal does not stay proceedings on ancillary or collateral matters which do not affect the judgment [or order] on appeal even though the proceedings may render the appeal moot.”  (Varian, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 191.)  “[C]ertain proceedings, by law, are collateral to the merits of an appeal despite their potential effect on the appeal.”  (Ibid.)  For example, a motion for a new trial is legislatively ancillary or collateral and may proceed notwithstanding an appeal from the judgment.  (Ibid.)  Similarly, a proceeding to expunge a lis pendens is collateral to an appeal from the judgment.  (Ibid.)

 

A “[p]roceeding is also ancillary or collateral to the appeal despite its potential effect on the appeal, if the proceeding could or would have occurred regardless of the outcome of the appeal.”  (Varian, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 191.)  For example, where a litigant could achieve the same result by posting a bond to stay the spousal support provisions of a judgment, the Court was not prevented from modifying its spousal support provisions in the interlocutory judgment.  (In re Marriage of Horrowitz (1984) 159 Cal.App.3d 377, 382.)  Similarly, “an appeal from the denial of a preliminary injunction does not stay further trial court proceedings on the merits” because “the affirmance or reversal of its denial does not and cannot eliminate the need for additional proceedings on the merits” as “the injunction amounts to a mere preliminary or interlocutory order to keep the subject of the litigation in status quo pending the determination of the action on its merits.”  (Varian, 35 Cal.4th at p. 191.)  “Likewise, an appeal from an order denying a motion to disqualify counsel does not automatically stay further trial court proceedings on the merits because such proceedings would occur regardless of whether the reviewing court affirms or reverses the order.”  (Ibid.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

            Moving Defendants argue a stay is warranted under Sction 916 because (1) “Having a trial go forward with the remaining defendants carries with the it the risk that a second, separate trial would go forward solely against MILLENNIUM”; (2) it would prejudice the remaining defendants to not have potentially two other defendants not placed on any jury verdict; and (3) it would protect the appellate court’s jurisdiction by preserving the status quo until the appeal is decided.

 

            With respect to Moving Defendants’ first point, the risk that a second, separate trial may go forward against Millennium (and/or Kerr) if the appellate court overturns the judgment does not impact the appellate court’s jurisdiction, nor is such a scenario “irreconcilable” with a potential outcome of the appeal.  Nor does proceeding with the trial against the remaining defendants impact the very purpose of the appeal.  Regardless of whether the judgment in Millennium’s (and/or Kerr’s) favor is overturned on appeal, the remaining Defendants must still proceed with the remainder of the case and trial. 

 

Moving Defendants’ argument in this regard does not support the request for a stay under section 916.  Rather, this argument appeals to the Court’s inherent power to stay proceedings to promote judicial efficiency and ensure the orderly administration of justice.  (See, e.g., Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489; Cottle v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1376-1379.)  As discussed below, because the defendants in this case are largely separately situated, the Court declines to grant the requested stay under its inherent power.

 

            As for the second argument, Moving Defendants have provided no legal or factual basis supporting their contention that separate trials against Millennium and/or Kerr would prejudice the remaining defendants.  Notably, most of the defendants are uniquely situated from one another, and are alleged to have participated in different aspects of the procedure giving rise to Plaintiff’s injuries.  Specifically, the theory of liability against Millennium rests solely on an alter ego theory, and will necessarily involve alter ego evidence and arguments not relevant to the other Defendants.  Similarly, Kerr was the nurse during Plaintiff’s procedure, and the wrongdoing alleged against Kerr is separate from the wrongdoing alleged against the nurse who Plaintiff saw in the office prior to the procedure, the office worker alleged to have altered medical records, the anesthesiologist who sedated Plaintiff during the procedure, etc.  That is why the Court granted summary judgment in favor of some Defendants but not others. 

 

            As for the third point, the Court is not required to “preserve the status quo” as to matters “not affected by the judgment or order.”  Resolution of the claims against the remaining defendants has no bearing on the status quo of Plaintiff’s claims against Kerr and Millennium, which resulted in judgments in favor of Kerr and Millennium, and therefore will not be tried or otherwise disturbed during the pendency of the appeal.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court denies Moving Defendants’ motion to stay the trial court proceedings pending the appeal of the Millennium Judgment. 

 

            Moving Defendants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.
