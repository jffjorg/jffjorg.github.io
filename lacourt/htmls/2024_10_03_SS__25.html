Case Number: 23STLC06975    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Thursday, October 3, 2024

Case Name:                             KAREN KARAKHANYAN vs MNATSAKAN ZOHRABYAN

Case No.:                                23STLC06975

Motion:                                   1) Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions;

 

2) Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

Moving Party:                         Defendant Mnatsakan Zohrabyan

Responding Party:                   None

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED

 

                                                Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, is GRANTED

 

Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified discovery responses to Defendant’s Discovery within thirty (30) days from the date of  Notice of this Court’s  Ruling. 

 

 Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s Counsel of record in the amount of $600.00, payable to Defendant within thirty (30) days from date of Notice of this Court’s Ruling.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff Karen Karakhanyan (“Plaintiff”) filed a personal injury complaint against Defendant Mnatsakan Zohrabyan (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10 inclusive for 1) Motor Vehicle Negligence for a vehicle collision that occurred on October 28, 2021.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            On September 5, 2024, Defendant filed the following motions: 1) Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions; and 2) Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Initial Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanctions.

 

Defendant argues that they served Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff via electronic service on January 4, 2024. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. A; Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. A.) Defendant had allowed an extension to March 4, 2024. (Mot. RPD, Exh. B; Mot. FROGS, Exh. B.) Plaintiff did not request an additional extension and failed to provide responses. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶4-6; Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶4-6.)

 

On March 19, 2024, Defendant sent meet and confer correspondence to Plaintiff requesting responses to the Requests for Production of Documents and Form Interrogatories without objections by March 29, 2024, which included a 10-day extension. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶5; Exh. B; Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶5; Exh. B.) As the date of filing the instant Motions, Defendant has not received Plaintiff’s responses. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶6; Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶6.)

 

OPPOSITION

 

            No Opposition was filed.  

 

           

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Legal Standard & Discussion

A. Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents

A party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories or requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories or production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)   

Here, Defendant served Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, on Plaintiff via electronic service on January 4, 2024. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. A.) As of the date this Motion was filed, Defendant had not received any responses. (Mot. RPD, Trostler Decl., ¶6.) Plaintiff did not oppose this motion. Thus, Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff’s response to the Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, without objections.

B. Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

Here, Defendant served Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff via electronic service on January 4, 2024. (Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. A.) As of the date this Motion was filed, Defendant had not received any responses. (Mot. FROGS, Trostler Decl., ¶6.) Plaintiff did not oppose this motion. Thus, Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff’s responses to the Form Interrogatories, Set One, without objections.

 

C. Request for Sanctions – Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents and Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)

 

Here, the Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One, a misuse of the discovery process. Plaintiff has not opposed this motion and has provided no substantial justification or other circumstances to make the imposition of a sanction unjust. Thus, the Court imposes a monetary sanction on Plaintiff for their failure to respond to Defendant’s discovery requests.

 

The courts awards sanctions in the reasonable amount of $600.  

 

II.        Conclusion

           

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Requests for Production, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified discovery responses to Defendant’s Discovery within thirty (30) days from the date of  Notice of this Court’s  Ruling. 

 

 

Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED against Plaintiff  and Plaintiff’s Counsel of record in the amount of $600.00, payable to the Defendant within  thirty (30) days from date of Notice of this Court’s Ruling.

BACKGROUND

 

            On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff Adriana Maria Moreno (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants United Car Company, Inc. (“UCC”), First Credit Finance (“FCF”), Philadelphia Indemnity Insurance Company (“PII”), and Does 1 to 40. Plaintiff purchased a used vehicle from UCC and alleges UCC made false representations and omissions regarding the vehicle’s history, safety, and condition. Plaintiff signed a Retail Installment Contract (“Contract”) with UCC, which was assigned to FCF as the financer. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Consumers Legal Remedies Act, Civil Code § 1750 et seq., (2) Violation of California Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq., Unlawful Acts or Practices, (3) Violation of Civil Code §1632, (4) Claim Against Surety, and (5) Violation of Code of Civil Procedure §§1281.97, 1281.99.

 

            On September 9, 2024, Defendant FCF filed an answer. Defendant UCC das not filed an answer.

 

            On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel arbitration, request to stay the proceeding, request for an order requiring FCF to pay all costs and fees related to arbitration.

 

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Plaintiffs contends that on September 25, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a CRLA Demand Letter to Defendants UCC and FCF (“Defendants”), which included a formal Demand for Arbitration. (Sadr. Decl., Exh. 2.) On March 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed for arbitration via American Arbitration Association (“AAA”) with UCC and FCF as parties. (Mot. p.6, lines 3-18.) On March 29, 2024, AAA sent Plaintiff and Defendants a letter informing them that AAA declined to administer the arbitration with UCC because UCC failed to comply with AAA’s policies regarding consumer claims prior to this arbitration. (Sadr Decl., Exh. 4.) AAA further noted that if UCC indicated its intent to comply with AAA’s polices in the future, AAA would consider accepting newly filed cases going forward. (Ibid.) On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed for arbitration with AAA against only FCF. On June 7, 2024, AAA sent a letter to all parties stating that FCF had failed to pay the required fees by the June 6, 2024, deadline and that the case was closed. (Sadr Decl., Exh. 6.)  

 

            Plaintiff elects to arbitrate via the AAA pursuant to the Contract, or in the alternative, via JAMS. Plaintiff asserts that both Defendants are required to be parties to the arbitration pursuant to the contract.

 

Plaintiff argues the right to compel arbitration has not been waived because Plaintiff attempted to initiate arbitration, but Defendants failed to comply. Plaintiff argues FCF is in material breach of the agreement because it failed to pay initial arbitration fees as required by CCP §1281.97. As a result, Plaintiff has the right to “[c]ompel arbitration in which the drafting party shall pay reasonable attorney’s fees and costs related to the arbitration.” (Cal. Civ. Proc. §1281.97, subd. (b)(2).) Plaintiff acknowledges both Defendants have responsibility to pay under the arbitration provision, although Plaintiff only seeks FCF to pay.

           

 

OPPOSITION

 

            FCF agrees to arbitrate via AAA and objects to arbitration via JAMS. FCF notes that when Plaintiff filed this motion, it was in the process of posting fees in the AAA claim.

 

            UCC has not filed an opposition as of September 26, 2024.

             

 

REPLY

 

            No reply was filed as of September 26, 2024.

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

I. Legal Standard

            Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (CCP § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿

 

¿           “If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (CCP § 1281.4.)¿¿ 

 

II. Discussion

 

A.    Motion to Compel

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.) 

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218, 105 Cal.Rptr.2d 597 (Condee¿); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.) 

 

Plaintiff has incorporated the arbitration provision in verbatim in the motion and has provided a copy of the Contract. (Mot. p.6, Lines 15-23, Sadr Decl., Exh. 2.) The arbitration provision provides the following:

 

“Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court decision… You may choose the American Arbitration Association, 1633 Broadway, Floor 10, New York, NY 10019 (www.adr.org); or any other organization to conduct the arbitration subject to our approval.”

 

(Id.)

 

            UCC and Plaintiff signed the agreement. (Id.) While FCF did not sign the Contract, FCF is bound as the Contract holder pursuant its terms. The pertinent provision states the following:

 

ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES OBTAINED PURUSANT HERETO OR WITH THE PROCESS HEREOF.

 

(Id.; see also Opp., Exh. A. (assigning the contract to First Credit Finance below this provision.))

           

FCF has also agreed to arbitrate the Contract in its opposition to this motion.

           

            The Court thus finds the existence of an arbitration agreement in the Contract.

 

Validity of Agreement

 

            FCF has not contested the validity of the agreement. UCC has not filed an opposition.

 

As neither Defendant has challenged the validity and enforceability of the arbitration agreement, the Court finds the arbitration provision is valid and enforceable.

 

            Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the motion to compel arbitration and the request to stay the action.

 

The Court notes that AAA declined to administer this arbitration with UCC because UCC failed to comply with AAA’s policies regarding consumer claims in the past. (Sadr Decl., Exh. 4.) If UCC indicates its intent to comply with AAA’s polices in the future, AAA would consider accepting newly filed cases going forward. (Ibid.)

 

B.     Request for Attorney’s Fees and Costs Related to the Arbitration

 

Plaintiff requests the Court to order FCF to pay expenses related to the arbitration up to $5000, in accordance with the arbitration provision and CCP §1281.97. The Court finds both Defendants have responsibility to pay, although Plaintiff only requests that FCF pays.

 

CCP §1281.97, subdivision (a)(1) states that “[i]n an employment or consumer arbitration that requires, either expressly or through application of state or federal law or the rules of the arbitration provider, the drafting party to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, if the fees or costs to initiate an arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days after the due date the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement, is in default of the arbitration, and waives its right to compel arbitration under Section 1281.2.”

 

CCP §1281.99 states that a court “shall impose a monetary sanction against a drafting party that materially breaches an arbitration agreement pursuant to subdivision (a) of Section 1281.97. . .by ordering the drafting party to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees and costs, incurred by the employee or consumer as a result of the material breach.” CCP §1280, subdivision (e) defines “Drafting party” as “the company or business that included a predispute arbitration provision in a contract with a consumer or employee. . .[including] any third party relying upon, or otherwise subject to the arbitration provision, other than the employee or consumer.” The Court finds both Defendants are “drafting parties” for the purposes of CCP §1281.99.

 

The Court acknowledges that the arbitration provision is governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA). (Sadr Decl., Exh. 1 (“Any arbitration under this Arbitration Provision shall be governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. §1 et seq.) and not by any state law concerning arbitration.”).) Nevertheless, the Court is able to award reasonable expenses related to the arbitration because the FAA does not preempt CCP §§ 1281.97, 1281.99. (See Gallo v. Wood Ranch USA, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 621, 642-43 (“sections 1281.97 and 1281.99 facilitate arbitration by preventing parties from insisting that a dispute be resolved through arbitration and then sabotaging that arbitration by refusing to pay the fees necessary to move forward in arbitration.”).)

 

The arbitration provision states the following in regard to fees: “We will pay your filing, administration, service, or case management fee and you arbitrator or hearing fee all up to a maximum of $5000, unless the law or the rules of the chosen arbitration organization require us to pay more.” (Sadr Decl., Exh. 2.)

 

Plaintiff attempted to initiate arbitration via a demand letter to Defendants, which indicated their responsibility to pay pursuant to the arbitration provision. (Sadr Decl., Exh. 2.) Plaintiffs then filed for arbitration with AAA, including both Defendants. (Mot. p.4, Lines 3-18.). AAA refused to arbitrate with UCC because of its failure to comply with AAA polices in the past. (Ibid.) Plaintiff then filed for Arbitration solely against FCF. (Ibid.) AAA eventually cancelled the case because FCF failed to pay the fees pursuant to CCP §1281.97 by the June 6, 2024, due date. (Sadr Decl., Exh. 7.)

 

Both parties thus are in violation of CCP §1281.97 and the arbitration provision. FCF failed to make the required payment for arbitration to AAA by June 6, 2024, due date and UCC was prevented from participating in the arbitration. (Sadr Decl., Exhs. 4, 7.)

 

The Court finds Defendants must pay for Plaintiff’s reasonable expenses related to the arbitration up to $5000, as required by the arbitration provision.

 

III. Conclusion

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay the Action.

 

Civil Proceedings are stayed pending completion of Arbitration.

                                               

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for expenses.  The Court finds Defendants must pay for Plaintiff’s reasonable expenses related to the arbitration up to $5000, as required by the arbitration provision.
