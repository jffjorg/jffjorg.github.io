DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02815    Hearing Date: December 24, 2024    Dept: W

ZAVEN KOUYOUMDJIAN vs SEDRAK FODOLYAN, et al.

 

defendants sedrak fodolyan, sedrak fodolyan dba prestige home healthcare inc., and eprem fodolyan’s demurrer to plaintiff’s first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        December 24, 2024                     Trial Date:     None set

Department:              W                                                   Case No.:      23VECV02815

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Sedrak Fodolyan, Sedrak Fodolyan dba Prestige Home Healthcare Inc., and Eprem Fodolyan  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Zaven Kouyoumdjian  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Bagdasaryan Decl. ¶¶3-11.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff Zaven Kouyoumdjian filed a complaint against Defendants Sedrak Fodolyan, Sedrak Fodolyan dba Prestige Home Healthcare Inc., and Eprem Fodolyan. On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint against Defendants for conversion, fraud, misappropriation of funds, breach of covenant of good faith and breach of oral contract.

 

Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff and Defendant Sedrak entered into an oral agreement whereby Plaintiff loaned Defendant $154,079.80 to assist Defendant Sedrak in his personal needs as well as for the startup of Defendant Prestige Home Healthcare Inc. Defendant Sedrak started making periodic payments but Plaintiff alleges there is a balance remaining and while Plaintiff continued to make demands through the years, on July 1, 2021, Defendant Sedrak informed Plaintiff he would not be making further payments. Plaintiff further alleges sometime during the years 2013 through 2020, Defendant Eprem approached Plaintiff and asked to borrow $60,000.00. Through the years of 2015 to 2021, Defendant Eprem made periodic payments. However, Plaintiff further alleges while he continued to demand Defendant Eprem to repay the balance and Defendant continued to promise he would, on August 5, 2021, Defendant Eprem informed Plaintiff he would no longer be making payments.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Sedrak Fodolyan, Sedrak Fodolyan dba Prestige Home Healthcare Inc., and Eprem Fodolyan’s Demur to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Sedrak Fodolyan, Sedrak Fodolyan dba Prestige Home Healthcare Inc., and Eprem Fodolyan demur to the First Amended Complaint brought by Plaintiff Zaven Kouyoumdjian on the grounds the complaint and each cause of action therein fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim against Defendants and are uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible.

 

Breach of Contract

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) Breach of oral contract claims are subject to a two-year statute of limitations. (See CCP § 339(1).) 

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for breach of oral contract on the grounds the precise terms of the contract, including the execution date as well as the specific alleged breaches are unclear given the misappropriation of funds allegation that follows. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges the loan was to assist Defendant Sedrak yet simultaneously alleged Defendants received money that was intended to be used for the benefit of Plaintiff. Defendants further argues there is inconsistencies in the original complaint and FAC and moreover, the cause of action is barred by the statute of frauds and statute of limitations.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff acknowledges that there are deficiencies in the operative complaint and has offered to amend the complaint to clarify the allegation, however, Defendants have chosen to demur to the FAC instead. Plaintiff briefly addresses Defendants’ argument regarding inconsistent allegations, statute of limitations and statute of frauds.

 

First, Plaintiff claims there are several ways to interpret the allegations and therefore, could be seen as consistent with complaint and in any case, Plaintiff does wish to clarify the allegations. Moreover, the alleged oral contract had very simple terms as it was an agreement between family members/friends. The fact there is no explicitly defined repayment schedule does not justify the demur. Plaintiff further claims that the exception to the statute of fraud applies as such as partial performance or promissory estoppel. (See Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Association, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1396; Sutton v. Warner (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 415, 422.) 

 

The court agrees the allegations as alleged are insufficient. However, as noted by Plaintiff, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Plaintiff has sufficiently shown they can amend their breach of contract claim to clarify any inconsistent terms and the cause of action is not automatically barred by the statute of frauds or statute of limitations. The current complaint includes partial performance and moreover, the face of the complaint is not barred by the statute of limitations.

 

If Plaintiff wishes to add allegations of financial elder abuse and promissory estoppel, Plaintiff will need to file a motion for leave to amend.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

The covenant of good faith and fair dealing is implied by law in every contract, and it acts “as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract.” (Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031-1032.) 

 

Defendants demur to this cause of action on the grounds Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged a breach of contract claim and moreover, Plaintiff has failed to allege any conduct by Defendants beyond the purported contractual breach to support a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant.

 

As the demurrer to the breach of contract cause of action has been sustained, the court sustains the demurrer to the fourth cause of action for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing. The court notes Plaintiff must allege more than conclusory terms that Defendants unfairly interfered with Plaintiff’s rights to receive the benefits of the oral contract.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fraud

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) In California, fraud, including negligent misrepresentation, must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)  

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to give rise to a fraud cause of action. Specifically, Defendants contend the complaint allege vague and ambiguous language and moreover, Plaintiff’s allegations inadvertently establish there could not have been fraud as Plaintiff alleges Defendants also made partial payments throughout the years. Lastly, Defendants contend Plaintiff’s allegations against the corporate defendant do not meet the required specificity.

 

The court agrees. The fraud cause of action is bare bones and fails to allege the fraud cause of action with the required specificity against Defendants, including the corporate Defendant Prestige. At the hearing, Plaintiff should address whether they wish to include this cause of action in their amended complaint and if so, how they can correct these issues.

 

Prestige Home Healthcare Inc.

 

Defendants demur to the FAC on the grounds there are no factual allegations against the corporate defendant. At the hearing, Plaintiff is to explain whether they seek to include allegations against Defendant Prestige Home Health in the amended complaint and if so, how to correct the issues noted by Defendants in the demurrer. 

 

Conversion

 

To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.) “[U]nder the economic loss rule, tort recovery for breach of a contract duty is generally barred  [citation] unless two conditions are satisfied. A plaintiff must first demonstrate the defendant's injury-causing conduct violated a duty that is independent of the duties and rights assumed by the parties when they entered the contract. Second, the defendant's conduct must have caused injury to persons or property that was not reasonably contemplated by the parties when the contract was formed. A review of the cases in which the doctrine has evolved provides context.” (Rattagan v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2024) 17 Cal.5th 1, 20–21.)

 

Defendants demur to the conversion cause of action on the grounds that the claim is barred by the economic loss rule and even if the economic loss rule is inapplicable, Plaintiff’s simple recitation of the elements of conversion do not satisfy the pleading requirements.

 

The court agrees, as alleged, the harm directly flows from the alleged breach of contract. As such, at the hearing, Plaintiff is to explain whether they seek to include allegations and  how such claim would not be barred by the economic loss rule.

 

Misappropriation of Funds

 

Lastly, Defendants demur to the misappropriation of funds cause of action on the grounds that it is uncertain and vague given that there is no civil cause of action for misappropriation of funds, if Plaintiff is claiming embezzlement that claim is also barred by the economic loss rule and lastly, the allegations contradict Plaintiff’s breach of contract cause of action.

 

The court agrees there is no misappropriation of funds cause of action. At the hearing, Plaintiff needs to explain whether they are seeking an embezzlement claim and whether that can be claimed.

 

Because there is no cause of action for misappropriation of funds, the court SUSTAINS the demurrer WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV03540    Hearing Date: December 24, 2024    Dept: W

ARTINEH NAVASARTIAN vs ANAHIT KHACHOYAN, et al.

 

defendants anahit khachoyan and arutyun martirosyan’s motion to compel deposition of plaintiff and request for sanctions

 

Date of Hearing:        December 24, 2024              Trial Date:       April 14, 2025  

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV03540

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Anahit Khachoyan and Arutyun Martirosyan  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Artineh Navasartian

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Mahlstedt Decl. ¶6.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 14, 2023, Plaintiff Artineh Navasartian filed a complaint against Defendants Anahit Khachoyan and Arutyun Martirosyan for negligence and motor vehicle negligence.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Anahit Khachoyan and Arutyun Martirosyan’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff is GRANTED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits objections to Defendants’ separate statement on the grounds of attorney-client privilege. As discussed further below, the objections do not stand and therefore, are overruled.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Anahit Khachoyan and Arutyun Martirosyan move this court for an order compelling Plaintiff Navasartian to testify in Volume II of her deposition. Defendants also seek sanctions against Plaintiff and/or Plaintiff’s record of attorney for failure to answer certain questions at her deposition on October 14, 2024 per her attorney’s instructions.

 

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information,¿or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (CCP §2025.480(a); see Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015 n. 3 [the only proper basis to instruct a deponent to not answer a question is an objection based upon a privilege or manifestly irrelevant questions or questions designed only to harass.]) “If the court determines that the answer or production sought is subject to discovery, it shall order that the answer be given or the production be made on the resumption of the deposition.” (CCP § 2025.480(i).)¿Failure to obey an order may be considered contempt of court and could subject a party to the action to issue, evidentiary, or terminating sanctions as well as monetary sanctions. (CCP § 2025.480(k).) “This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.”¿(CCP § 2025.480(b).) 

 

Defendants move to compel Plaintiff to provide an answer to their questions regarding how they found PMIR and if anyone, other than their attorney, referred them to PMIR. Defendants contend the questions directed at how Plaintiff found their doctor and whether it was attorney-referred is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and not barred by attorney-client privilege (see Qaadir v. Figueroa (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 790).

 

In opposition, Plaintiff opposes the motion on the grounds while relevant, it is still protected by attorney-client privilege. The court disagrees. Plaintiff misses the point of Qaadir v. Figueroa. In Qaadir, the court agreed “[r]eferral evidence [i.e., evidence that a party was referred by his attorney to lien-physicians] … [is also] relevant to the question of the reasonable value of the lien-physicians’ medical care because it may show bias or financial incentives on the part of the lien-physicians.” (Qaadir v. Figueroa (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 790, 808.) Although the court did not explicitly go on to state that therefore, the information was not barred by the attorney-client privilege, it is clear that the court found evidence of a witness being referred to by Plaintiff's counsel is relevant to show bias and to challenge credibility. Moreover, Plaintiff has not shown that such a referral constitutes attorney-client communications. Defendant may only ask who referred plaintiff to the physician.  If plaintiff indicates that the referral came from his attorney, no further questions can be asked about communications between plaintiff and his attorney on that topic. If the referral came from someone else, follow up questions may be asked. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Anahit Khachoyan and Arutyun Martirosyan’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendants request monetary sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010. Defendants request $810.00 against Plaintiff and/or their counsel. The court GRANTS Defendants’ request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $310.00. Defendants did not file a Reply.
