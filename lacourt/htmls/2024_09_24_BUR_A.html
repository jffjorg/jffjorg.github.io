DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify "all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue."  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is requested.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 22BBCV01288    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: A

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH CENTRAL DISTRICT - BURBANK

DEPARTMENT A

 

TENTATIVE RULING

SEPTEMBER 24, 2024

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22BBCV01288

 

MP:  

Burbank Community YMCA (Defendant)

RP:  

W. Roe (Plaintiff)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is requesting oral argument on this matter.  Parties are requested to address the issue of “foreseeability” and the application Youth Soccer, infra and DCFS, infra to this summary judgment.  Given the request for argument and that the Court is unexpectedly handling two civil courtroom calendars, the matter will be heard at 10:00 AM.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

W. Roe (Plaintiff) brings this action against Burbank Community YMCA (YMCA). Plaintiff alleges that he regularly attended the YMCA in 1987 to swim after school. Plaintiff alleges that during this time he became acquainted with a lifeguard at the YMCA identified here by the name “Steve”. Plaintiff claims that one day in 1987 Steve sexually assaulted Plaintiff.

 

Before the Court is motion for summary judgment brough by YMCA. Plaintiff opposes the motion and YMCA replies.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) C.C.P.¿§ 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)¿ 

¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (C.C.P.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ 

¿ 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v.¿Paetkau¿(1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿ 

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Plaintiff states three causes of action for (1) Negligence, (2) Negligent Hiring, (3) Negligent Supervision.

 

In order to prevail in his negligence action against YMCA Plaintiff “…must show the defendant owed a legal duty to her, the defendant breached that duty, and the breach proximately caused injury to the plaintiff. A defendant does not owe a legal duty to protect against third party conduct unless there exists a special relationship between the defendant and the plaintiff. In that circumstance, in addition to the special relationship there must also be evidence showing facts from which the trier of fact could reasonably infer that the defendant had prior actual knowledge, and thus must have known, of the offender's assaultive propensities. In short, the third party's misconduct must be foreseeable to the defendant.” (Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 682.) "In cases involving minors, courts generally have recognized a special relationship where adults and organizations acted as 'quasi-parents' by assuming responsibility for the safety of [minors] whose parents were not present." Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (2021) 70 Cal. App. 5th 657, 671 (quoting United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal. App. 5th 1118, 1130).

 

Likewise, to prevail in his causes of action for negligent hiring and negligent supervision, Plaintiff must show “(1) the employer’s hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee; (2) the employee was incompetent or unfit; (3) the employer had reason to believe undue risk of harm would exist because of the employment; and (4) harm occurs.” (Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 828, 836-837.)

 

YMCA brings this motion for summary judgment, arguing that no triable issue of fact exists as to the foreseeability of Plaintiff’s abuse at the hands of Steve. Indeed, foreseeability is a required factor to be shown in each of Plaintiff’s causes of action. YMCA argues that no evidence exists as to whether Steve’s abuse of Plaintiff was “foreseeable” as established by Rowland. (See Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 113 [A departure from this fundamental principle involves the balancing of a number of considerations; the major ones are the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff…].)

 

The foreseeability determination under Rowland has been clarified by the more recent Supreme Court ruling in Regents of the University of California v. Superior Court (Regents). There the court held that “in examining foreseeability the court's task is not to decide whether a particular plaintiff's injury was reasonably foreseeable in light of a particular defendant's conduct, but rather to evaluate more generally whether the category of negligent conduct at issue is sufficiently likely to result in the kind of harm experienced that liability may appropriately be imposed.” (Regents of University of California v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 607, 629 [internal citations and quotation marks omitted].) Thus, the question this motion presents is not whether the evidence shows YMCA was aware of Steve’s propensity to sexually abuse children, it is instead whether the evidence shows that YMCA was aware that the abuse of children in their care was reasonably foreseeable in general.

 

In looking at the Rowland factors, courts start by determining whether it was foreseeable that an employee would sexually abuse another. An affirmative duty to take action to control the wrongful acts of an employee is imposed only in instances where the conduct can be reasonably anticipated. In other words, the reasonableness standard test is predicated upon the degree of foreseeability.  If the foreseeability is high enough, then courts will impose on a defendant the duty to act on it. “Courts use a ‘sliding-scale balancing formula’ under which ‘imposition of a high burden requires heightened foreseeability, but a minimal burden may be imposed upon a showing of a lesser degree of foreseeability.’ (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 243.) Heightened foreseeability can be shown by evidence of prior similar criminal incidents or “other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults … .” (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1131.)

 

In the context of child sexual abuse, California Courts have issued a wide variety of opinions discussing foreseeability. The case primarily relied upon by YMCA is Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (DCFS). The plaintiff in DCFS alleged that she had been assaulted by members of the foster family with whom she had been placed by the non-profit agency Children’s Institute. (DCFS supra, 37 Cal.App.5th at 679.) The court first acknowledged that in order for Children’s Institute to have a duty for purposes of a negligence action, it was necessary to establish that a special relationship existed between themselves and plaintiff. (Id. at 682.) Having established that the plaintiff and Children’s Institute had such a relationship, the court turned to considerations of whether evidence of foreseeability had been shown. (Id. at 683.) 

 

Children’s Institute argued that the evidence of the case pointed to the opposite of foreseeability, as the plaintiff had told DCFS directly that this placement was “the best one she had ever had” (Id. at 684.) Plaintiff maintained that despite the lack of actual knowledge by Children’s Institute, the harm to her was still foreseeable by virtue of Children’s Institute failing to institute safeguards to protect from child sexual abuse in general. (Id.) In making this argument, plaintiff relied upon the holding in Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (Youth Soccer). As Youth Soccer is a case relied upon by Plaintiff here, the Court finds a summary would be helpful.

 

Youth Soccer concerned a plaintiff asserting a cause of action for negligence against her soccer organization after she was sexually abused by one of her instructors. (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1122.) The defendant did not deny that a special relationship existed between themselves and the plaintiff, but they did argue that the harm was not foreseeable under Rowland. (Id. at 1131.) The court observed that “California courts have frequently recognized special relationships between children and their adult caregivers that give rise to a duty to prevent harms caused by the intentional or criminal conduct of third parties.” (Id. at 1129.) However, the court also held that “a duty to take affirmative action to control the wrongful acts of a third party will be imposed only where such conduct can be reasonably anticipated. Basically, the reasonableness standard is a test which determines if, in the opinion of a court, the degree of foreseeability is high enough to charge the defendant with the duty to act on it.” (Id. at 1131 [internal quotation marks and citation omitted.]

 

The Youth Soccer court held that the evidence indicated abuse of minors participating in the program was indeed a foreseeable occurrence. The Youth Soccer plaintiff had presented evidence that the organization was “aware of incidents of physical and sexual abuse of US Youth Soccer's members by its coaches at a steady yearly rate of between 2 and 5 per year.” (Id. at 1132.) Further, the court considered the fact that the organization had issued literature and created programs around the potential sexual abuse of their child participants. (Id.) From these showings, the court concluded that the organization had ample knowledge of the general risk of sexual abuse of children participating in their programs. (Id.)

 

Turning back to DCFS, the court there contrasted its own evidentiary showings with those in Youth Soccer. The DCFS court found that the alleged safeguards that Children’s Institute failed to implement had little to no propensity to reveal the potential for child sexual abuse. (LADCFS supra 37 Cal.App.5th at 685 [“Children's Institute's failure to conduct a home study, enforce capacity limits, follow up on the removal of a bed, or provide adequate training would not have led to information about the Winston brothers’ criminal propensities.”].) The Court contrasted this to the alleged failures to safeguard in Youth Soccer, which bore a close relationship to the potential for child sexual abuse. (Youth Soccer supra, at 1137 [“The connection between plaintiff’s harm and defendants’ failure to conduct a criminal background check was close. If defendants had conducted a criminal background check of Fabrizio, his prior conviction for domestic violence would have been discovered and it would have been highly unlikely that he would have been hired. Thus, he would have had far fewer, if any, opportunities to sexually abuse plaintiff.].)

 

From DCFS and Youth Soccer it can be understood that a plaintiff with a cause of action for negligence stemming from child sexual abuse must demonstrate:

 

(1)   Evidence that the harm of child sexual abuse was a generally foreseeable phenomenon by the employer/organization to be held accountable, and

(2)   That the alleged failures to safeguard by that employer/organization would tend to prevent such abuse. 

 

The cases relied upon by Plaintiff reinforce this understanding. Plaintiff cites to Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (Lawndale). Lawndale concerned the sexual abuse of a student by a teacher. The student thereafter brought a negligent hiring action against the school district. (Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 129.) Plaintiff specifically cites the following from Lawndale:

 

To the extent Doe v. DCFS suggests that, in all cases where a defendant has a special relationship with a plaintiff, the defendant has a duty to protect the plaintiff from third-party assaults or abuse only if the defendant has actual knowledge of the third party's propensity for assault or abuse, California law does not support such a proposition.

 

(Id. at 129.)

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff that the law in California is not that a plaintiff must prove actual knowledge of a third party’s propensity for abuse. At the same time, the context of this quotation is crucially important. The Lawndale court went on to discuss that the lack of foreseeability in DCFS was informed by the context of the relationship between adults and minors that they host in their homes. (Id.) The Lawndale court distinguished the holding from its own facts, which involved the relationship between a school and its students. (Id.) The Lawndale court’s suggestion that sexual abuse of minors is generally foreseeable is presented within the context of that specific school/student relationship. (Id. at 170 citing C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861 (Hart) [“A school district and its employees have a special relationship with the district's pupils, a relationship arising from the mandatory character of school attendance and the comprehensive control over students exercised by school personnel, analogous in many ways to the relationship between parents and their children.”].)

 

As this case does not concern a school, the principles of Hart and Lawndale are informative, but not directly applicable. DCFS and Youth Soccer make clear that outside of school/student relationships, a plaintiff is required to show some foreseeability of child sexual abuse beyond general awareness. As will be analyzed below, the Court finds the evidence in this case does not support such a finding.

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court finds the evidence supports the existence of a special relationship between Plaintiff and YMCA. Plaintiff’s mother testified that she paid YMCA for after school care for Plaintiff during the 1986-1987 school year. (Exh. 2, D.H. Decl ¶ 2-3.) It was Plaintiff’s mother’s understanding that her son would be taken care of under the supervision of YMCA. (Id. at ¶ 4.) YMCA presents no testimony or evidence to refute the claim that YMCA was paid to watch Plaintiff after school. YMCA’s assertion that at some point Plaintiff was told he did not need to pay to swim at YMCA is not equivalent to such a refutation. (Exh. B., Roe Depo. at 48:13- 49:3, 51:20-52:20.) From these allegations it is sufficient to say that Plaintiff was in the custodial care of YMCA in some capacity. The question thus becomes whether the evidence shows YMCA’s prior knowledge of the potential for child sexual abuse.

 

The record is bereft of evidence from which the Court could conclude that YMCA had actual knowledge of Steve’s propensity for child abuse. Plaintiff testified in his deposition that before the incident he was not afraid of Steve and was always treated kindly by him. (Exh. B. Roe Depo at 54:12-18.) Plaintiff has presented no evidence that Steve was acting inappropriately before the incident or that YMCA would have otherwise been aware. As such, the Court must determine whether there is any evidence that YMCA was more generally aware of the risk of child sexual abuse.  

 

With respect to potential safeguards or programs, no evidence is presented by either party. Instead, Plaintiff submits evidence suggesting no safeguards were in place.  YMCA’s Aquatics Director at the time of the incident, George Saikili (Saikili), merely stated that he “found no policies or any membership documents or any documents whatsoever that pertain to the plaintiff’s allegations. (Exh. 5, Saikili Depo. 10:17-22.) Nor could Saikili recall any policies or procedures from that time or whether YMCA had any. (Id. at 47:2–5, 58: 3-7.) As concerns policies and procedures for employees supervising young children, Saikili only posited that it seemed likely YMCA would have had them at the time of the incident but did not recall what they might have been. (Id. at 65:11–16.)

 

In contrast to the cases he relied upon, here there is no evidence that YMCA was aware of the risk of child sexual abuse by third parties. For example, in Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (Archbishop), the plaintiff presented ample evidence that the defendant was well aware of sexual abuse of minors by member of the clergy. (Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 657, 676.) This is similar to the plaintiff in Youth Soccer showing that the organization was aware of and took some measures to prevent sexual abuse of minors.

 

Here, Plaintiff has made no showing that YMCA, either its Burbank chapter or nationally, was aware of the risks of child sexual abuse by its employees prior to the incident. Plaintiff has not presented any procedures of YMCA as a wider organization or any incidents of sexual abuse at this or other YMCA locations prior to the incident. In the absence of this evidence, the Court does not see how it can be said that a triable issue of fact exists as to whether the incident was reasonably foreseeable by YMCA. The lack of safeguard procedures only becomes relevant once YMCA had prior knowledge of the need to implement those procedures.

 

In short, the Court finds that Plaintiff has presented no evidence which could establish YMCA’s prior knowledge and thus has failed to demonstrate that the incident complained of was foreseeable. Given the failure to show foreseeability, Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate a triable issue of fat as to a key elements of each of his causes of action.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.            LEGAL STANDARD

 

Where a party fails to obey an order compelling answers to interrogatories, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction.” (C.C.P. §§ 2030.300(e).)

 

Where a party fails to obey an order compelling further response to request for admissions, “the court may order that the matters involved in the requests be deemed admitted. In lieu of, or in addition to, this order, the court may impose a monetary sanction…” (C.C.P. § 2033.290(e).)

 

The court maintains broad discretion in determining an appropriate sanction for misuse of the discovery process. (See Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York (2016) 246 Cal. App.4th 566, 604.) In so doing, the court "should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery." (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516 [internal quotation omitted].) The discovery statutes prescribe an incremental approach to sanctions. Sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery. If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse." (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)

 

II.            DISCUSSION

 

Defendant’s August 13, 2024 Responses

 

The Court first addresses what is referred to by Plaintiff as Defendant’s Further Further Responses, issued on August 13, 2024. (See Yadegar Decl. Exh. A.) Plaintiff argues that the Court should entirely disregard these responses, as they were only served after the filing of the instant motion. The Court finds this argument unpersuasive.

 

It is well settled that the purpose of a discovery sanction is to encourage the fair disclosure of discovery, rather than to impose punishment on the previously withholding party. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64.) In other words, the purpose of imposing discovery sanctions is to ensure that the party requesting those sanctions eventually receives the discovery they are requesting. To ignore Defendant’s August 13 responses would be to eschew this purpose. Defendant’s failure to render fully responsive answers to the RFA until after this motion was filed may be supportive of monetary sanctions, but it does not support the more drastic remedy of deeming the RFA matters admitted.

 

This being established, the Court will review the RFA Plaintiff requests be admitted and Defendant’s corresponding responses to determine if further production is needed at this juncture.

Having reviewed Defendant’s responses the Court finds that the August 13, 2024 responses are largely adequate. The Court’s primary basis for previously compelling further responses to the RFA was Defendant’s conflicting statement of an inability to comply while simultaneously denying each of RFA Nos. 1-28. The Court found that Defendant’s responses were incomplete by virtue of both stating an inability to respond and yet responding regardless.

 

Here, Defendant appears to have cured this defect in her August 13 responses, at least as concerns RFA Nos. 7, 8, 12, 13, 14, 16, 17, 19, 20, 23, 27, and 28. The Court notes that several of the June 11 responses still asserted an inability to comply while simultaneously denying the RFA, but this defect appears to have been rectified with the August 13 production. Each of these RFA are now sufficiently answered with a response of Admit, Deny, or Unable to Comply.

 

What remains are RFA Nos. 2 and 3. Plaintiff maintains that the August 13 responses remain improper as to these RFA because they are not a straightforward declaration of admit or deny. Indeed, Defendant’s responses to these interrogatories are qualified by footnotes disputing the meaning of the RFA on definitional grounds.

 

With respect to RFA No. 2, Defendant maintains that her response of “admit” to whether Defendant was married to Decedent is not meant as an admission that the parties were “legally” married. This is unacceptable.   What is before the Court is a legal matter, and the issues which must be resolved are legal ones.  The Court acknowledges that Plaintiff’s RFA does not contain a definition for the word “married” however, that term is defined by law.  Family Code §300 states, “Marriage is a personal relation arising out of a civil contract between two persons, to which the consent of the parties capable of making that contract is necessary. Consent alone does not constitute marriage. Consent must be followed by the issuance of a license and solemnization as authorized … “  For over 120 years, California does not accept the doctrine of common law marriage which was abolished in California by statute in 1895 (see Norman v. Thomson (1898) 121 Cal. 620, 628; Elden v. Sheldon (1988) 46 Cal.3d 267.)  Defendant’s engaging in a semantic argument as to whether she was “legally” or “colloquially” married to Decedent is inconsistent with the RFA.  The question was straight forward, the term “marriage” is well known in the law, both statute and case law.  Accordingly, the Court orders the Defendant to answer the question as to whether Defendant was legally married.  Had the Plaintiff sought to inquire whether Defendant believed she was in a five court days in which to file a code compliant amended response.

 

With respect to RFA No. 3, Defendant’s August 13 response constitutes an admission to the RFA posed. The RFA requests Defendant to admit that Decedent was a patient at Plaintiff’s facility. In the Court’s view, the June 11 response admitting that Decedent was an “unwilling patient” constituted an evasive response. Whether Decedent was present at Plaintiff’s facility willingly or unwillingly is a matter entirely unrelated to the question of whether he was patient in the first place. Regardless, Defendant August 13 response appears to rectify this defect by stating an unqualified admission. Defendant’s inclusion of the footnote asserting that Decedent was not a willing patient at the facility may be an irrelevant inclusion, but it does not render the “admit” response inadequate. Further, there would be no point in deeming this RFA admitted considering Defendant has already admitted its truth in her August 13 responses.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is CONTINUED in part and DENIED in part. The matter will be continued for compliance with the Court’s order or RFA 2 will be deemed admitted.

 

Form Interrogatory 15.1

 

Plaintiff also moves for evidentiary sanctions precluding Defendant from introducing evidence which would have been responsive to Form Interrogatory 15.1. Form Interrogatory 15.1 requests Defendant identify all facts, witnesses, and documents supporting each of her 33 Affirmative Defenses.

 

Defendant’s initial response to this interrogatory was a simple objection. Defendant’s June 11 response appears to rectify this issues by identifying a number of facts, witnesses, and documents which are responsive to the request. (Wilson Decl. Exh. K.) Plaintiff argues that this response remains insufficient because it is not stated as to each of Defendant’s affirmative defenses, instead the information is identified in the aggregate. Defendant’s August 13, 2024 responses attempt to rectify this insufficiency by asserting the same substantive response as in the June 11 responses but repeated 33 times.

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff that the same information is unlikely to be responsive to each of Defendant’s 33 affirmative defenses.  For example, the Court does not see how information which is responsive to Defendant’s affirmative defense of “Laches” would be identical to those responsive to Defendant’s affirmative defense of “Consent” (See Yadegar Decl. Exh. B.)

 

Regardless, the Court does not view these responses as warranting evidentiary sanctions. It is clear that Defendant is attempting to provide responsive information, even if her copy/paste approach is insufficient. As such, the Court believes the appropriate remedy would be to issue further monetary sanctions and compel Defendant to issue another response to Form Interrogatory 15.1. This response must identify individualized information for each of Defendant’s further responses. The Court reserves the right to reconsider evidentiary sanctions should Defendant fail to comply with this order within the next 30 days.

 

Monetary Sanctions

 

Despite the Court’s refusal to order issue or evidentiary sanctions, it is clear that Plaintiff’s filing of this motion was necessary to obtain appropriate responses. Further, Defendant’s response to Form Interrogatory 15.1 remains insufficient. Given the foregoing, the Court finds monetary sanctions are appropriate in this circumstance. The Court awards sanctions in the amount of $2,900 as against Defendant’s counsel. This amount is commensurate with 8 hours of attorney work at a rate of $355 per hour, plus the $60 filing fee for this motion. (Wilson Decl. ¶ 18.)
