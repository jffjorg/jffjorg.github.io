Case Number: 21STCV29691    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: C

HERNANDEZ v. SIMPLIFIED LABOR STAFFING SOLUTIONS, INC., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 21STCV29691 

HEARING: 01/22/2025 @ 10:30 a.m.

 

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Eduardo Hernandez’s motion for relief to file a late opposition pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473 is GRANTED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Plaintiff Eduardo Hernandez (Plaintiff) moves for an order to file a late opposition to Defendant Econo Door Repair Services’ motion to dismiss for lack of prosecution pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473.

 

Background

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff filed this personal injury action against Simplified Labor Staffing Solutions, Inc., MAERSK, SG, MAERSK, Inc., MAERSK Line Limited, and MAERSK Agency, Inc. Plaintiff alleged three causes of action: (1) negligence, (2) negligence per se, and (3) premises liability. Plaintiff alleges that he sustained injuries when a metal ramp guided by a home-made jerry rigged device crashed on to a metal dock where Plaintiff stood. (Complaint, ¶¶ 11-13.) On April 10, 2024, after receiving Defendant Damco Distribution Services, Inc.’s discovery responses, Plaintiff discovered Econo Door Repair Services’s (Econo) involvement and amended the complaint to add Econo as Defendant.

 

On September 23, 2024, Specially Appearing Defendant Econo moved to dismiss with prejudice all causes of action against Econo for lack of prosecution.

 

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for relief to file a late opposition pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473.

 

Legal Standard

 

Discretionary and mandatory relief is available to parties or legal representatives from “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473 subd. (b).) “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any…resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subd. (b) due to Plaintiff’s counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel did not calendar the correct deadline to file an opposition for Econo’s motion to dismiss. An opposition for a motion to dismiss for delay in prosecution is due within 15 days after service of the notice of motion. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1242(b).) Plaintiff’s counsel mistakenly calendared the deadline based on other statutory deadlines pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005. (McElfish Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff additionally argues that Econo will not be prejudiced by a late-filed opposition because the hearing on the motion to dismiss is set to be heard on February 19, 2025.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel has demonstrated mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 473. Plaintiff additionally filed its opposition to Econo’s motion to dismiss soon after realization of the error on December 10, 2024. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s unopposed motion for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473 to file a late opposition to Econo’s motion to dismiss for lack of prosecution is GRANTED.

Proposed Intervenor National Unity Insurance Co. (Proposed Intervenor) moves the Court for an order granting leave to file an answer in intervention on behalf of Defendant Vintage Industries LLC.

 

No opposition filed as of January 18, 2025.

 

Background

 

On April 20, 2023, Leslie Ponce and Leticia Delgafo Fuentes (Plaintiffs) filed this personal injury action against Defendants Vintage Industries LLC and Felix Renteria Rosas alleging general negligence.  Plaintiffs allege that on May 17, 2021, Defendants were travelling in a vehicle at or near Ardine Steet and Quartz Avenue in South Gate when the vehicle collided into Plaintiffs’ vehicle while making a left turn. (Complaint, p. 4.) Defendant Rosas was driving a vehicle owned by Defendant Vintage Industries LLC. (Motion, p. 1:24-26.) 

 

On August 23, 2024, Plaintiffs requested entry of default as to Defendant Vintage Industries LLC. Defendant Vintage Industries LLC has been suspended as a corporation for almost four years, including at the time of the accident. (Steward Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant Vintage Industries LLC had a commercial liability policy in effect with Potential Intervenor on the date of the motor vehicle collision. (Steward Decl., ¶ 11.) Potential Intervenor now moves for leave to intervene on behalf of its insured, Defendant Vintage Cultures LLC.

 

Legal Standard

 

An intervention takes place when a nonparty becomes a party to an action by “uniting with a defendant in resisting the claims of a plaintiff.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 387 subd. (b)(2).) A nonparty petitioning for leave to intervene must include a copy of the proposed complaint or answer in intervention and state the grounds for the intervention. (Code Civ. Proc. § 387 subd. (c).)

 

The Court shall permit an intervention if “[a] provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene” or “[t]he person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest, unless that person's interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 387 subd. (d)(1).)

 

Alternatively, the Court may “permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 387 subd. (d)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Proposed Intervenor moves the Court for an order granting leave to file an answer in intervention on behalf of its insured, Defendant Vintage Industries LLC. Proposed Intervenor argues it has a direct interest in this matter as it relates to both Defendants Vintage Industries LLC and Rosas. No opposition has been filed.

 

It is well-settled under California law that “to be enforceable against an insurer, a ‘judgment need not be based on a contested or adversarial trial, but may rest upon a default hearing held following a settlement or an uncontested trial where the insured settled with the claimant and thereafter presented no defense.’” (Executive Risk Indemnity, Inc. v. Jones (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 319, 330-31.) Therefore, “an insurer should be allowed to intervene in an action against its insured when the insurer may be required to satisfy any judgment entered against the insured and when the insurer's intervention will not enlarge the issues in the case.” (Id. at n. 11, see also Reliance Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 383, 386–387, [insurer allowed to intervene when insured barred from defending because corporate status suspended.) Additionally, “where the insurer may be subject to a direct action under Insurance Code section 11580 by a judgment creditor who has or will obtain a default judgment in a third party action against the insured, intervention is appropriate.” (Reliance Ins. Co., supra, 84 Cal.App.4th at 386.)

 

Here, Proposed Intervenor asserts that Defendant Vintage Industries LLC did not file an answer to Plaintiff’s complaint because Defendant Vintage Industries LLC did not receive notice of this action and it has not been in good standing since 2020. (Steward Decl., ¶ 8.) Proposed Intervenor argues that because Defendant Vintage Industries LLC cannot defend its own interests, Proposed Intervenor will be held financially responsible for a default judgment. Proposed Intervenor additionally asserts it is already litigating this action through the defense of Defendant Rosas.

 

The Court finds that Proposed Intervenor has a sufficient interest to intervene in this case. Additionally, Proposed Intervenor is already litigating this case through its defense of Defendant Rosas and has not enlarged the issues originally raised by the parties.

 

Accordingly, Proposed Intervenor National Unity Insurance Co.’s motion for leave to file an answer in intervention is GRANTED. National Unity Insurance Co. is ORDERED to file its answer in intervention within 20 Court days.

Case Number: 24NWCV00250    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: C

RIVAS, ET AL. v. CAZARES

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00250

HEARING: 01/22/2025 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.                Defendant Sergio Cazares’s unopposed demurrer to the first, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action are SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

II.              Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

III.            Defendant’s motion to strike is taken OFF CALENDAR.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Defendant Sergio Cazares demurs to Plaintiffs Angelica Rivas and Daniel Rodriguez’s entire complaint.

 

No opposition filed as of January 18, 2025.

 

Background

 

This is a wrongful eviction case. On January 24, 2024, Angelica Rivas and Daniel Rodriguez (Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against their landlord Sergio Cazares (Defendant) concerning the residential unit located at 2960 1/2 Clarendon Avenue, Huntington Park, California 90255 (Subject Premises). Plaintiffs allege that on September 27, 2023, Defendant served a Notice of Sales and Entry terminating Plaintiffs’ tenancy at the Subject Premises in retaliation for Plaintiffs’ request for repairs. (Complaint, p. 2.) Plaintiffs also state that Defendant, owners, and agents of the Subject Premises have entered the Subject Premises without proper notice. (Complaint, p. 3.)

 

The complaint asserts seven causes of action: (1) wrongful eviction, (2) trespass, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (4) violation of Business & Profession Code § 17200, (5) intentional misrepresentation, (6) intentional interference with use of premises, and (7) breach of contract & the covenant of good faith & fair dealing.

 

Legal Standard

 

Demurrer Standard 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.) 

 

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)

 

Motion to Strike Standard 

 

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.). (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435-437.) A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

The parties have adequately met and conferred.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to all seven causes of action in Plaintiffs’ complaint on the grounds that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action. Defendant’s demurrer is unopposed.

 

          First Cause of Action – Wrongful Eviction

 

Defendant makes two arguments against the first cause of action. First, that there is no independent cause of action for wrongful eviction, and second, that the complaint does not allege an eviction.

 

“California recognizes the tort of wrongful eviction. ‘The same basic policy against forcible or other wrongful ouster that gives the tenant the summary remedy to obtain restoration of possession gives the tenant a tort action for damages for wrongful eviction.’ ‘An essential element of a wrongful eviction claim is that the tenant has vacated the premises.’” (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 293 [internal citations omitted].)

 

Here, to Defendant’s first argument, California does recognize a tort of wrongful eviction. However, it is not clear whether Plaintiffs are asserting a cause of action for a tort of wrongful eviction. The complaint alleges that Defendant violated Civil Code section 1941.1, relating to untenantable dwellings, by serving an unlawful eviction notice. (Complaint, p. 5.) The complaint recites this statute but does not provide facts establishing how the Subject Premises was untenantable. Additionally, as Defendant argues, Plaintiffs do not allege facts that they were evicted. The compliant states that they “currently reside” in the guest house that they rented out on a month-to-month basis. (Complaint, p. 3.) Plaintiffs also state that Defendant was terminating Plaintiff’s tenancy in retaliation for requesting repairs. (Complaint, p. 2.) However, the complaint goes on to state “plaintiffs will now be forced to leave their rented guest house, and re-locate to another dwelling place, because of the many repairs needed on the premises, without adequate notice.” (Complaint, p. 3.) Plaintiffs state that they are unable to reside in the Subject Premises while it is being repaired due to their health conditions, and have not been provided temporary housing. (Complaint, p. 3.) Thus, the complaint lacks facts alleging that Plaintiffs have left the premises.  The complaint is also unclear about whether Plaintiffs were evicted in retaliation for requesting repairs, or whether they are unable to stay in the Subject Premises while it is being repaired.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

          Second Cause of Action – Trespass

 

“The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant's intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm.” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs plead facts sufficient to meet the elements of trespass. First, Plaintiffs state they were entitled to possession based on a month-to-month lease entered into on January 1, 2019. (Complaint, p. 7.) Plaintiffs allege Defendants made an unauthorized entry “on many occasions” into the Subject Premises to conduct repairs and to show the property to prospective buyers. (Complaint, p. 7.) Plaintiffs state that the entry was made without Plaintiffs permission, without notice, and during non-work hours. (Complaint, p. 7.) Plaintiffs allege this constituted unwarranted intrusions into the peace and quiet of their unit resulting in Plaintiffs suffering monetary damages. (Complaint, p. 7.) 

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to identify with sufficient particularity the date or time of the alleged trespass.  However, these are facts which may be obtained through discovery.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

          Third Cause of Action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants acted in an outrageous manner when they tried to evict the plaintiffs by posting a Notice of Sale. (Complaint, p. 7.) Defendant argues that posting a “Notice of Sale” does not constitute outrageous conduct.  The Court agrees.  Further, as explained earlier, it is not clear that Defendant evicted Plaintiffs.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

          Fourth Cause of Action - Violation of Business & Profession Code § 17200

 

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (UCL), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.) “What constitutes 'unfair competition’ or 'unfair or fraudulent business practice' under any given set of circumstances is a question of fact, the essential test being whether the public is likely to be deceived.” (People v. Toomey (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 1, 16.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that they entered into a “business relationship” with Defendant on January 1, 2019 when Defendants agreed to rent out the Subject Premises to Plaintiffs. (Complaint, p. 8.) They allege that Defendant engaged in an unfair business act by evicting Plaintiffs. (Complaint, p. 8.) However, as Defendants argue, the allegations here do not reflect a business practice which is likely to deceive the public. Moreover, given Plaintiffs’ causes of action alleging breach of contract, this cause of action is improperly asserted under the UCL.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

          Fifth Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Here, the complaint states “Defendants… made false representations to plaintiffs, that the dwelling unit located at [Subject Premises] would be rented out to them on a month-to-month basis, and that defendants will ensure habitability. They also represented to the plaintiffs that the unit was up to code with the County of Los Angeles when such was not the case.” (Complaint, p. 9.)

 

Plaintiffs have not stated facts sufficient to constitute intentional misrepresentation. Plaintiffs do not allege that Defendant made a misrepresentation separate from the terms of the rental agreement. Plaintiffs’ allegations are not pled with particularity, which is required when alleging fraud. Moreover, Plaintiffs do not state how the property was defective.   

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

          Sixth Cause of Action – Intentional Interference with Use of Residential Premises

 

The elements of a claim for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment are: (1) a lease agreement between plaintiff and defendant; (2) absence of language contrary to the implied covenant that tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession; (3) act or omission of the landlord, or anyone claiming under the landlord, which “substantially interfere[s] with a tenant[’]s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy”; and (4) an applicable remedy. (See Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 588-591.)

 

It is unclear what cause of action Plaintiffs allege here. To the extent Plaintiffs allege a breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, Plaintiffs fail to plead facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Plaintiffs state that Defendant interfered with Plaintiffs’ quiet enjoyment by illegally evicting the Plaintiffs without a notice to quit. However, as discussed above, Plaintiffs have failed to allege facts relating to an eviction.

 

          Seventh Cause of Action – Breach of Contract and the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff’s must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiffs.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)

 

“A breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing involves something beyond breach of the contractual duty itself and it has been held that bad faith implies unfair dealing rather than mistaken judgment.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1394.) “If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated … [T]he only justification for asserting a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant is to obtain a tort recovery.” (Id. at pp. 1394-1395.)

 

As a preliminary matter, by claiming breach of contract and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in the same cause of action, the Court assumes that Plaintiffs are not claiming that there is a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing beyond the contract.

 

For Plaintiffs seventh cause of action, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant breached the contract and covenant by evicting Plaintiffs without a notice to quit. (Complaint, p. 10.) While Plaintiffs meet the first element of a breach of contract claim by attaching the Rental Agreement, Plaintiffs plead contradictory facts regarding the alleged eviction. The complaint alleges both that Plaintiffs were served with a Notice of Sale terminating the tenancy and that Plaintiffs currently reside at the Subject Premises and require temporary housing during its repairs. (Complaint, p. 2-3.) Therefore, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently pled facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the seventh cause of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant’s court reservation states that Defendant reserved a demurrer with a motion to strike. However, Defendant has not filed a motion to strike. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is taken OFF CALENDAR.


Case Number: 24NWCV01115    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: C

SONTERRA CAPITAL v. HYUNG DO MIN, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV01115

HEARING: 01/22/2025

 

#6

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Sonterra Capital’s unopposed motion to unseal the record and enter default against Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises, Inc., and Crossing Storage Services, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Plaintiff Sonterra Capital (Plaintiff) moves to unseal the record and enter default against Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises, Inc., and Crossing Storage Services, Inc.

 

No opposition filed as of January 18, 2025. 

 

Background

 

This is an unlawful detainer action. On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed this action against Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises, Inc., Crossing Storage Services, Inc., Pitman Enterprises USA, LLC, and Everytable, PBC. Plaintiff is the owner of the premises located at 3305 E. Vernon Avenue, Vernon, California, 90058 (Subject Premises). Plaintiff alleged that on January 22, 2021, Plaintiff became the landlord of the Subject Premises as successor-in-interest to Vernon-B LLC pursuant to a second amendment to a Standard Industrial Commercial Single-Tenant Lease (Lease) between Vernon-B LLC and Defendants MJ Global Enterprises, Inc. and Hyung Do Min (Defendants). (Complaint, ¶ 10.) Defendants Pitman Enterprises USA, Crossing Storage Services, Inc., and Everytable, PBC were sublessees on the Lease and continued to remain in possession of the Subject Property. (Complaint, ¶¶ 13-15.) In September of 2023, Defendant MJ Global Enterprises, Inc. became delinquent in rent, and on March 28, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendants with a three day Notice to Pay Rent or Quit. (Complaint, ¶¶ 16-17.) On April 1, 2024, Plaintiff received the sum of $170,000, leaving a balance of $223,232.41. (Complaint, ¶ 17.)

 

On September 4, 2024, a jury trial was held. The Court was informed of a settlement between Plaintiff and Defendant Everytable, PBC. The Court entered stipulated judgment for Plaintiff and against Defendant Everytable, PBC. Restitution and possession of the premises were granted for Plaintiff. (Minute Order, 9/4/2024.) The Court was also informed of a settlement as to the remaining Defendants, Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises, Inc. and Cross Storage Services, Inc. (Minute Order, 9/4/2024.) The Court ordered the case sealed pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1161.2 subd. (a)(2). The Court retained jurisdiction to enforce the terms of the stipulation pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.

 

On November 15, 2024, the Court heard Plaintiff’s ex parte application to unseal the record and enter default against Defendants Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises, Inc., and Crossing Storage, Inc. The Court denied Plaintiff’s ex parte application and determined the issue should be adjudicated after briefing by the parties. (Minute Order, 11/15/2024.) The Court specially set the hearing for this date, January 22, 2025. (Minute Order, 11/15/2024.)

 

On December 3, 2024, the Court dismissed Defendant Pitman Enterprise USA, LLC dismissed without prejudice. On January 8, 2025, an Order to Show Cause re Dismissal (Settlement) as to Defendants Hyung Do Min, MJ Global Enterprises Inc, and Cross Storage Services, Inc. was continued to February 20, 2025.

 

Legal Standard

 

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6.)

 

Court records are presumed to be open. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c).) A sealed record may only be unsealed by Court order. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(h).) The Court may order a record be sealed only if it expressly finds facts that establish:

 

(1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record;

(2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record;

(3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed;

(4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and

(5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).) In determining whether to unseal a record, the court considers the matters addressed in California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c)-(e). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(h)(4).) The order unsealing a record must state whether the record is unsealed entirely or in part. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(h)(5).)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to unseal the record and for an order entering default against Defendants. Plaintiff argues good cause exists for the Court to unseal the record and enter default because Defendants breached the terms of the settlement agreement entered into on September 4, 2024.

 

Here, the parties entered a stipulated settlement on September 4, 2024.  Under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, an oral stipulated settlement made in Court is enforceable. Plaintiff asserts that pursuant to the stipulated settlement, Defendants were to pay Plaintiff $300,000 by September 4, 2024, and make monthly payments of $15,000 by September 11, 2024. (McCormack Decl., ¶ 3.) As of September 17, 2024, Defendants failed to make a monthly payment of $15,000. (McCormack Decl., ¶ 5.) In its Minute Order on September 4, 2024, the Court indicated “that a judgment will not be entered if the terms of settlement are complied with. If there is a breach, the matter will be unsealed and a judgment will be entered.” (Minute Order, 9/4/2024.) Defendants have not opposed this motion. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to unseal the record and enter default against Defendants is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 24NWCV01598    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: C

VILLALOBOS V. ROSARIO PASILLAS

CASE NO.: 24NWCV01598  

HEARING: 01/22/2025 @10:30 a.m.

 

#11

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Rosario Pasillas’ demurrer to the first amended complaint is OVERRULED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Defendant Rosario Pasillas demurs to Plaintiff Jose Villalobos’s first amended complaint.

 

Background

 

This is an unlawful detainer action. On May 22, 2024, Jose Villalobos, Trustee of the Villalobos Mercado Living Trust, (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Rosario Pasillas (Defendant) for unlawful detainer. On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC) reclassifying the action as a limited unlawful detainer action. Plaintiff alleges that since 2005, Defendant was a tenant-at-will at 3509 Greenglade Ave., Pico Rivera, CA 90660 (Subject Premises) pursuant to an oral agreement with Plaintiff’s predecessor in interest. The oral agreement terminated when Plaintiff’s father and former trustee Ismael Villalobos passed away on October 22, 2022. Defendant was then served with a 60-day notice to quit on January 12, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) A “[p]laintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” (Ibid.) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41 subd. (a).)

 

Here, the parties have not met and conferred. Defendant does not provide any declaration regarding a meet and confer prior to bringing this demurrer. Plaintiff’s counsel states that Defendant’s counsel did not meet and confer prior to filing this demurrer. (Coleman Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant learned about this demurrer from the Court’s Clerk during a separate conversation about a potential default.  (Coleman Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant demurs to the FAC on the grounds that the pleadings do not state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action because they are inherently uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible.

 

As a preliminary matter, Defendant’s demurrer is procedurally defective. As Plaintiff argues in opposition, Defendant’s demurrer combines the grounds for demurrer as provided under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10 subd. (c) and (f) by stating that the FAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because it is uncertain. While the demurrer’s arguments focus on FAC’s uncertainty, Defendant does not list Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10 subd. (f) as a basis for the demurrer. Further, the demurrer also prays for the complaint to be stricken. However, Defendant demurs to the FAC without a motion to strike. The Court will rule on the merits of this demurrer because the matter was previously called for hearing, and pursuant to oral stipulation, reset for hearing on this date.  However, because this matter is not a limited unlawful detainer action, any future motions should be filed in Department SE-W, the unlawful detainer hub court. 

 

Defendant argues that the spelling of Plaintiff’s name on different filings in this case render the FAC uncertain. Defendant argues that on some portions, Plaintiff’s name is spelled with “double LLs once” and on other portions with “double LLs twice.” (Demurrer, ¶¶ IV-VIII.) In opposition, Plaintiff argues his last name, “Villalobos” is spelled correctly in the attorney identification page of the caption of the FAC and the 60-day notice to quit. Plaintiff admits to spelling the name incorrectly in the caption of the FAC due to inadvertence of Plaintiff’s counsel.

 

Here, the Court finds that the spelling inconsistencies in Plaintiff’s last name do not render the FAC uncertain. The error does not cause the FAC to fail to provide notice to Defendant that Plaintiff is bringing this action. This is exemplified by Plaintiff’s name being correctly identified in Defendant’s notice of demurrer.

 

Next, Defendant argues that the FAC is uncertain as to whether Defendant brings this action as an individual, or in his capacity as trustee. Defendant’s argument relies on Form CP10.5, attached the FAC, which names Plaintiff as “Jose Villallobos.” The Court finds that this error on the CP10.5 does not render the FAC uncertain because the FAC, on Form UD-100, names Plaintiff as “Jose Villallobos, Trustee of the Ismael Villalobos Mercado Living Trust.” Defects in demurrers must be apparent on the face of the pleading. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) Here, the pleading is certain as to Plaintiff’s capacity as trustee. Therefore, the error in the attachment does not render the FAC uncertain because the FAC properly provided notice to Defendant that Plaintiff was bringing this action in his capacity as trustee.

 

Third, Defendant argues that the FAC is uncertain regarding the period of the 60-day notice to quit. Specifically, Defendant asserts that ¶ 9.b.(1) of the FAC improperly states that the 60-day notice to quit expired at the end of the day on January 10, 2024. Defendant argues this is improper because ¶ 10.a of the FAC states that the notice to quit was served on January 12, 2024.  It appears to the Court that the language used in ¶ 9.b.(1) of the form complaint could be clearer.  Plaintiff inserted “January 10, 2024” in that paragraph because that was the date written on the 60-day notice to quit. (Complaint, Ex. 2.)  However, ¶ 9.b.(2) states, “Defendants failed to comply with the requirements of the notice by that date” (italics added).  This suggests that Plaintiff should have inserted the date when the 60-day period expired.  In any event, ¶ 10.a.(3) clearly indicates that the notice was served “By posting a copy on the premises on January 12, 2024.”  It follows that Defendant had 60 days from January 12, 2024 to comply with the requirements of the notice. 

 

Lastly, Defendant argues that the FAC is uncertain because a proof of service was not filed for the 60-day notice, Plaintiff failed to attach an optional “attachment 17,” and a UD-101 form was not attached with the FAC. These issues do not make the FAC uncertain because they do not render the FAC unintelligible to the point of which Defendant cannot reasonablly respond. Plaintiff does not cite, and the Court does not find, legal authority requiring a proof of service to be filed with a notice to quit. Moreover, Plaintiff filed a form UD-101 with the initial complaint.

 

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff improperly seeks attorney’s fees. This argument is not properly raised on demurrer.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC is OVERRULED.
