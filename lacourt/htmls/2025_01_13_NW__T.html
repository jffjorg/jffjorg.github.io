21STCV12144 ORTIZ V UBER

[TENTATIVE] ORDER: Cross-Defendant Matthew Rubeck's Motion for a Prior Separate Trial on Liability Issues Before Trial on Damages is DENIED.  

Introduction

Cross-Defendant Matthew Rubeck (“Cross-Defendant”) moved for an order granting a prior separate trial on liability before trial on damages issues in this action. Defendant and Cross-Complainant MV Wav Services, Inc. (“Cross-Complainant”) opposed the motion. As of January 8, 2025, no reply brief has been filed. Any reply brief was required to have been filed and served at least five court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) 

Procedure

Although filed and served late on January 3, 2025, the Court will consider Cross-Complainant’s untimely opposition brief. (Cal. R. Ct., rule 3.1300(d).)

Discussion

 A court “may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action . . . or of any issue or of any number of causes of action or issues” in furtherance of convenience, judicial economy, or to avoid prejudice. (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 1048, subd. (b).) “The court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby . . . make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue.” (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 598.) “A trial court has discretion to determine the order in which claims or issues are bifurcated and determined, and the selection and scheduling of those phased determinations will not be disturbed absent an abuse of that discretion.” (Antelope Valley Groundwater Cases (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 241, 273.)

Cross-Defendant contends that a prior separate trial on liability in this case will promote the ends of justice, the convenience of witnesses, and judicial efficiency and economy. Further, Cross-Defendant asserts that a prior separate trial on liability will avoid substantial prejudice to Cross-Defendant.

Cross-Defendant’s counsel, Kelly M. Johnson, Esq. (“Johnson”), declares that the only remaining defendant is MV Wav Services, Inc. (Johnson Decl., par. 4.) Cross-Defendant is not named as a defendant in the complaint. (Johnson Decl., par. 3.) The cross-complaint, which was filed by defendant and cross-complainant MV Wav Services, Inc., is asserted against Cross-Complainant and Aaron Stern; however, Mr. Stern has not yet appeared in this matter. (Johnson Decl., par. 5.) The cross-complaint alleges causes of action for indemnity, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief. (Johnson Decl., par. 5.) This case arises from a three-vehicle collision in which Plaintiff was a passenger in a vehicle that rear ended Cross-Defendant’s vehicle. (Johnson Decl., par. 7.) Cross-Defendant disputes liability “as he was stopped at the time of the incident and he is not a defendant in plaintiff’s action.” (Johnson Decl., par. 8.) Counsel then sets forth the various experts designated by the parties. (Johnson Decl., pars. 9, 10, 11.) Very few expert witnesses are expected to testify as to liability in comparison to the majority of the expert witnesses focusing on damages. (Johnson Decl., par. 12.) Counsel indicates that extensive expert testimony will be required to litigate Plaintiff’s damages. (Johnson Decl., par. 13.) According to Ms. Johnson, a prior separate trial on liability will promote efficiency and will avoid wasting judicial resources because “[t]he liability phase will require less time, less expert testimony, and will be the simplest issue for the jury to decide in contrast to the damages portion.” (Johnson Decl., par. 14.)

Here, the Court finds that bifurcation is not appropriate. Cross-Defendant’s counsel has not attested to any prejudice that Cross-Defendant will incur if bifurcation is denied. Also, given that Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendant is responsible for Plaintiff’s injuries, the Court fails to see how bifurcation of liability and damages would promote convenience or judicial economy. Given the dispute as to liability, the Court finds that the liability and damages issues are related.    

The same jury would have to hear the bifurcated trial and this will extend the time of their jury service.  Also, moving party notes that cross-defendant Aaron Stern has not yet been served. He was dismissed on 12/31/2024 so that is no longer an issue.

The case cannot be determined without Mr. Rubeck's participation in the trial as he was one of a chain of cars involved.

The Court DENIES Cross-Defendant’s motion for an order granting a prior separate trial on liability issues before trial on damages issues in this action.   

IT IS SO ORDERED, CLERK TO GIVE NOTICE.

Case Number: 23VECV01739    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: T

23VECV01739 VARDANYAN V JAGUAR

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees is Granted in Part, grants $39,375 in attorneys’ fees, plus the $5,278.61 in costs for a total of $44,653.61.

Introduction

On or about December 8, 2020, Plaintiff leased a new 2020 Land Rover Range Rover Velar distributed by Defendant JLRNA. The Complaint was filed on April 21, 2023, which alleged a violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act against Defendant. Plaintiff alleges there were issues with the Vehicle that Defendant has allegedly been unable to repair. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant has been unable to conform the Vehicle to the applicable warranty and that Defendant failed to comply with its obligations under the Song-Beverly Act. JLRNA denied these claims. The case settled by May 8, 2024.  The parties filed a Notice of Settlement of Entire Case on May 21, 2024.  On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for attorney’s fees and costs. 

Discussion

There is no dispute that Plaintiffs are the prevailing parties and thus entitled fees per statute. Statutory attorney fees are ordinarily determined by the court pursuant to the “lodestar” or “touchstone” method. However, the Court’s discretion is somewhat limited in fee awards under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  The statute takes a different approach from customary statutory or contractual provisions.  The statute requires an “initial determination of the actual time expended; and then to ascertain whether under all the circumstances of the case the amount of actual time expended and the monetary charge being made for the time expended are reasonable.  These circumstances may include, but are not limited to, factors such as the complexity of the case and procedural demands, the skill exhibited and the results achieved. If the time expended or the monetary charge being made for the time expended is not reasonable under all the circumstances, then the court must take this into account and award attorney fees in a lesser amount.”  (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 817.)  In the situation of a contingency fee arrangement, the court in Nightingale stated, “for purposes of section 1794, subdivision (d), a prevailing buyer represented by counsel is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees for time reasonably expended by his or her attorney.”  (Id. at p. 817-818 (citing to Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 104-105, fn. 6.) 

Once the court decides the amount of hourly rate, that may then be adjusted based on factors specific to the case in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided. (Ibid.) These facts include (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. (Ibid.)

Reasonable Hourly Rate

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees for attorneys Plaintiff requests fees and costs in the amount of $172,176.11, consisting of $110,785.00 in attorneys’ fees for 162.4 hours of work at the rate of $650/hour for Hovanes Margarian, and at the rate of $550/hour for 9.5 hours expended by Armen Margarian, plus the 1.5 Lodestar multiplier for a total of $55,392.50, assistant fees equal to $720.00, as well as $5,278.61 in costs and expenses.

In opposition, Defendant argues the attorneys’ fees request is excessive as this was a case without expert depositions, motions for summary judgment or trials, with only “fill in the blank” pleadings.  Defendant also argues that hourly rates of $550 and $650 are not reasonable for a “simple, rote Lemon Law action.” Defendant states that the median hourly rate for this sort of case is $450 per hour.  (Opp., Exhibit 1, Consumer Law Attorney Fee Survey Report.)  Defendant notes that defense counsel billed less than one eighth of Plaintiff’s amount, with total fees and costs coming in at just over $20,000.  (Opp., Wolf Decl. para. 17.) Defendant requests that the attorneys’ fees be granted in an amount no more than $30,000.

The court agrees that the amount of time claimed and the hourly rate are excessive. “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) A reduced award might be fully justified by a general observation that an attorney over litigated a case or submitted a padded bill or that the opposing party has stated valid objections. (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 101.)

Based on this Court’s experience in approving legal rates for lemon law actions, the court approves a rate of $575 per hour for Hovanes Margarian’s and $400 per hour for Armen Margarian.

Reasonable Hours Incurred

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.)

Plaintiff’s counsel indicates that they spent 16.1 hours in pre-filing, 114.3 hours in post-filing/discovery/settlement 7.9 hours in client communication, 9.4 hours in breakdown of expended hours, and 13.2 hours on this fee motion.  Each of these seem very excessive for such experienced attorneys.  If this motion is an example of 13.2 hours of work, then it is clear that there is some obfuscation going on.  A firm which does this work, as claimed by counsel, should have the motion on word processing which takes maybe 2-3 hours to modify to the particular case.  That is inflation of 80%.  Similarly, discovery is standard as well.  Reducing the remainder of the time which the court finds to be excessive as overstated, the Court reduces the amount of hours to 65 hours for Hovanes Margarian. The court similarly reduces Armen Margarian's time to 5 hours at a reasonable rate for an 8th year attorney of $400.  Notably, counsel do not list cases in which they received less per hour than they requested. 

Asking for inflated fees does not help the credibility of the attorneys seeking fees.

Multiplier

While the lodestar reflects the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community, it may be adjusted based on various factors, including “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill displayed in presenting them; (2) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys; (3) the contingent nature of the fee award” and (4) the success achieved. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)

Nonetheless, the court must not consider extraordinary skill and the other Serrano factors to the extent these are already included with the lodestar. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal. 4th 1122, 1138-1139.) “[A] trial court should award a multiplier for exceptional representation only when the quality of representation far exceeds the quality of representation that would have been provided by an attorney of comparable skill and experience billing at the hourly rate used in the lodestar calculation. Otherwise, the fee award will result in unfair double counting and be unreasonable.” (Id. at 1139.)

Plaintiff requests a lodestar multiplier enhancement of 1.5. The Court finds that a lodestar multiplier is not warranted here. Nothing before the Court indicates that the case presented novel issues or that the quality of representation was exceptional representation or far exceeded the quality of representation that would have been provided by attorneys of comparable skill and experience billing at the same rates. The¿contingent risks, skill, and Plaintiff’s attorneys¿assert are absorbed by their hourly rates.¿(See¿Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California. Inc.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 822.)

Accordingly, the Court declines to award a lodestar multiplier.

Costs

Allowable costs “shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) Any items not specifically mentioned by statute “may be allowed or denied in the court's discretion.” (Id., subd. (c)(4).) The Beverly-Song Act allows a successful plaintiff to recover both “costs” and “expenses.” (See Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).)

'“If the items appear to be proper charges the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the defendant [citations], and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the [objecting party].' [Citation.]” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.)

Plaintiff requests a total of $5,278.61 in costs. The cost items appear to be reasonable and proper.

Conclusion 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED IN PART. The Court awards 65 hours at $575.00 per hour for at total of $37,375, plus 5 hours at $400 an hour for $2000 for total fees of  $39,375 plus the $5,278.61 in costs.

          IT IS SO ORDERED, CLERK TO GIVE NOTICE.
