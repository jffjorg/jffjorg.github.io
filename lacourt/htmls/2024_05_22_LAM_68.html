Case Number: 22STCV33658    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68 

Date: 5-22-24 

Case #: 22STCV33658 

Trial Date: 1-9-25 c/f 6-24-24 

 

FURTHER INTERROGATORIES 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, Samuel Welch 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Shamoria Jackson 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories (set one) 

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION 

Plaintiffs were tenants of a single family dwelling at 32270 Saticoy Street, West Hills, and allege unsanitary and/or unsafe conditions on the premises as a result of improper maintenance and upkeep.  

 

On October 17, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their complaint for Failure to Provide Habitable Dwellings, Breach of Covenant of Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property, Nuisance, and Negligence. Defendants answered the complaint on February 23, 2023. 

 

RULING: Granted. 

Defendant, Samuel Welch moves to compel further responses to form interrogatories (set one), numbers 9.1, 9.2, 10.3, and 12.4 from Plaintiff Shamoria Jackson. The responses consist of incomplete replies, with assurances of later production upon entry into a protective order, or references to other parties and non-parties responsible for the provision of information.  

 

The responses are incomplete and fail to respond to all subcategories of the requests. Plaintiff makes no actual objection on privacy. The reliance on the purported protective order in no way excuses the incomplete responses.  

 

The motion is therefore granted. Responding is ordered to serve responses in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.210-20310240. No sanctions requested.  

 

The court calendar shows 13 scheduled motions to compel further responses scheduled through July 22, 2024. The motions appear as a continuation of discovery issues addressed by the court in the 23 motions to compel responses granted on November 22, 2023. 

 

The court does not conduct any informal discovery conferences. While the court understands the prior use and perhaps reliance on the IDC system, the court finds the number of items on the court calendar through the next three months presents a potentially inordinate burden. The court reserves the right to set an OSC re: Discovery Referee in lieu of a hearing on any given motion, and may take off any and all motions in lieu of the OSC hearing. The court invites the parties to continue meeting and conferring, including the provision of supplemental responses, when possible.  

 

Next motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents from Shamoria Jackson on May 29, 2024.  

 

Defendant to give notice. 

 

Case Number: 23STCV23998    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68 

Date: 5-22-24 

Case # 23STCV23998 related to (22STCV18640 (lead case) & 22STCV382369) 

Trial Dates: Not Set 

 

PROTECTIVE ORDER/QUASH 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, LAUSD  

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Jane RS Doe 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED 

Motion for Protective Order/Motion to Quash Deposition Subpoenas 

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION 

Plaintiff Jane RS Doe alleges a school teacher at defendant Los Unified School District Miramonte Elementary School, Mark Berendt, sexually molested Plaintiff beginning in 2007. Plaintiff was born on May 17, 1999. Berendt was arrested in 2012 for the alleged molestation of numerous students on the campus. 

 

On October 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; Negligence; Negligent Supervision; Negligent Hiring/Retention; Negligent Failure to Warn, Train, or Educate; Breach of Fiduciary Duty; Constructive Fraud; Sexual Battery; and, Sexual Assault. RULING: Granted in Part/Denied in Part. 

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted. 

  • The court takes judicial notice of the publication of the articles, but nothing in the content for the truth of the matter asserted on any item addressed in the subject motion. 

 

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD) moves for protective order or alternative motion to quash 11 deposition subpoenas served on both third parties or party defendants (1) Hilda Higgins; (2) Dr. John Deasy, past Superintendent for the School District; (3) Michael Hopwood; (4) Danilo Escalante; (5) Holly P. Diaz; (6) Loren Vivero; (7) Lisa Gil; (8) Shayla Lever; (9) Susan Geisen; (10) Walter Flores; and (11) Dr. George McKenna, current member of the School District Board of Education. LAUSD moves on a number of grounds including, premature timing as to defendant Higgins, apex doctrine as to Deasy and McKenna, and general claims of “abusive” discovery tactics.  

 

Plaintiffs in opposition maintains the necessity for the depositions. Plaintiff agrees to the use of prior deposition testimony, where applicable, but only on condition of an agreed upon expedited trial date. Plaintiff denies application of the apex rule to McKenna and Deasy. Plaintiff requests sanctions. 

 

LAUSD in reply reiterates the offer for a stipulation to utilize prior deposition testimony. LAUSD vigorously maintains the lack of any new information. LAUSD notes the lack of opposition characterizing Deasy and McKenna as alpha level employees, thereby requiring efforts of alternative means of discovery.  

 

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness … the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), … may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).) A Court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).) A deponent may seek that the deposition is not taken at all, the deposition only taken on specified terms and conditions, or only certain matters are allowed for inquiry. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b)(1)(5)(9).) The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks. (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)   

 

A motion for a protective order “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.) The motion contains a sufficient meet and confer effort. [Declaration of Henry Gonzalez, Ex. B.]  

 

The notice of deposition on Hilda Higgins shows a date of February 8, 2024—one day after substituting Higgins for Doe 2. Higgins was not served with the summons and complaint via substituted service until April 5, 2024. Higgins answered the first amended complaint on May 6, 2024. “The plaintiff may serve a deposition notice without leave of court on any date that is 20 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, any defendant. On a motion with or without notice, the court, for good cause shown, may grant to a plaintiff leave to serve a deposition notice on an earlier date.” (Code Civ. Proc., 2025.210, subd. (b).) The deposition notice on a now party defendant was premature. The motion is therefore granted on this basis. Plaintiff may serve new notice of a deposition. 

 

For John Deasy and George McKenna, the court addresses the apex doctrine standard. “It is the general rule in both California and federal courts that the heads of agencies and other top governmental executives are normally not subject to depositions. (Citations.)” [¶] “‘An exception to this general rule exists concerning top officials who have direct personal factual information pertaining to material issues in an action. [Citations.] [¶] A top governmental official may, however, only be deposed upon a showing that the information to be gained from such a deposition is not available through any other source. [Citations.]’ (Citation.)” (Nagle v. Superior Court¿(1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1465, 1467–1468; see Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1289.)  

 

Assuming Deasy remains an employee with the district, while conceding that Deasy no longer serves as the superintendent, LAUSD continues to maintain apex doctrine protects former position holders. LAUSD cites to the leading case considering the appearance of a former director. (Nagle v. Superior Court, supra, 28 Cal.App.4th at p. 1467.) In considering both the current and former director of the California Employment Development Department, the court cited to the policy foundation of the apex employee protections: “... an official's time and the exigencies of his everyday business would be severely impeded if every plaintiff filing a complaint against an agency head, in his official capacity, were allowed to take his oral deposition. Such procedure would be contrary to the public interest, plus the fact that ordinarily the head of an agency has little or no knowledge of the facts of the case.” (Id. at p. 1468.)  

 

The first part of the test lacks applicability given Deasy no longer serves as superintendent. The court finds analogous support in the second part regarding a showing of knowledge of facts of the case. The court finds a lack of support for the necessity of direct deposition testimony without first establishing a lack of the required information from other sources. “Thus, where a party seeks to depose a high government official, and the official moves for a protective order, the burden is on the deposing party to show that compelling reasons exist for permitting the deposition.” (Contractors' State License Bd. v. Superior Court (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 125, 132.) 

 

As for George McKenna, LAUSD relies on the argument that a current member of the School District Board of Education, McKenna also qualifies for apex level status. The court finds no legal support or factual, granular distinctions between a superintendent or clear head of an agency versus a board member overseeing a district. The court accepts the implied argument that a board member may or may not be privy to key information regarding specific teacher misconduct or school administration matters for a single campus. 

 

Regardless, Plaintiff offers no legal or factual dispute to the application of the apex doctrine to the two witnesses, and instead only maintains the witnesses hold exclusive information necessitating the depositions. The court declines to make a determination on the unchallenged claim as to board member McKenna, and instead accepts the position for purposes of the motion.  

 

Plaintiff’s conclusive position regarding necessary knowledge lacks any specific articulation regarding efforts to obtain the information regarding knowledge of the exact case from any and all alternative sources and the insufficiency of such efforts. The court therefore grants the protective order given the lack of sufficient support as to the current action. (Ibid.) 

 

On the challenge by LAUSD suggesting the former depositions should be relied upon for purposes of the deposition, the court finds no basis of support on this specific ground. A party remains entitled to conduct discovery into each and every filed action, without any required reliance on former testimony. Plaintiff’s counter-offer of an “expedited” trial date lacks applicability: the action is not even at issue and demurrer appears scheduled.  

 

The court otherwise finds no basis to preclude Plaintiff from seeking new depositions as party defendants or witnesses. A party may object to the introduction of former deposition testimony, thereby justifying the potential need for case specific discovery. The court declines to offer a potential advisory opinion on the admissibility of former deposition testimony. 

 

The motion lacks any alternative basis of support regarding the “abusive discovery tactics” contention. Again, the court finds the existence of prior deposition testimony in a prior case in no way vitiates the rights of a new party to seek discovery in the instant action. The motion for protective order as to the non-apex witnesses is therefore denied. 

 

In summary, the motion for protective order is granted as to the apex witnesses, McKenna and Deasy, as well as prematurely served defendant Higgins, and denied as to the remainder. The parties may still stipulate to the use of former deposition testimony, but again, the court in no way rules on any consideration regarding the use of said testimony in lieu of depositions as to this Plaintiff. Request for sanctions by Plaintiff denied. 

 

The court does not conduct independent discovery conferences. The court invites the parties to consider a discovery referee if supervised depositions become required. Nothing in this order precludes the parties from executing a stipulation in exchange for bringing the case at issue without further challenge.
