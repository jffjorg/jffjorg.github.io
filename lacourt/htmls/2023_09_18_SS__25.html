Case Number: 19STCP03300    Hearing Date: September 18, 2023    Dept: 25

Joseph L. Shalant v. Nicholas Tepper

19stcp03300

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On August 1, 2019, Petitioner Joseph L. Shalant (“Petitioner”) initiated this action by filing a Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award against Respondent Nicholas Tepper (“Respondent”). The Court granted the Petition on December 5, 2019, and entered a judgment against Respondent in the amount of $3,050.00 plus interest of 10% from August 18, 2018.  (12-5-19 Minute Order.)  The Court also noted that Petitioner could file a noticed motion if he intended to request attorney’s fees. (Ibid.)

 

Petitioner filed his first Motion for Attorney’s Fees on February 27, 2020.  On October 28, 2020, the Court noted that Petitioner had not cited any authority for its request and continued the hearing to allow Petitioner additional time to file supplemental papers.  (10-28-20 Minute Order.)  Petitioner did not appear at the hearing scheduled on December 1, 2020, and the Court found that Petitioner had not filed any supplemental papers as requested, so the Motion was placed off calendar. (12-1-20 Minute Order.)

 

On January 22, 2021, Petitioner filed a Motion to Hear Matter Previously Taken Off Calendar re Issuance of Judgment Pursuant to Confirming Arbitration Award, essentially a request for a hearing on the Motion for Attorney’s Fees.  On June 23, 2021, the Court granted Petitioner’s Motion in the amount of $2,876.25 in attorney’s fees and $635 in costs.  (6-23-21 Minute Order.)

 

On November 18, 2022, the Court found that non-party Anna Viele is not responsible for Respondents’ outstanding debt due to legal separation, and discharged Ms. Viele.  (11-18-22 Minute Order.)  The Court also vacated its Order, dated June 23, 2021, as Petitioner’s counsel, Steven L. Leighton, was not an active member of the bar at the time the Motion for Attorney’s Fees had been filed.  (Ibid.)

 

On November 28, 2022, Petitioner filed a Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Motion for Reconsideration.  On March 6, 2023, the Court identified various defects in the Motion preventing the Court from reaching a determination on the merits.  (3-6-23 Minute Order.)  Specifically, the Court found that it was difficult to discern whether Petitioner was seeking either reconsideration of the Court’s November 18, 2022, Order or an issuance of attorney fee’s in an amount between $5,000 and $6,000.  (Ibid.)  Additionally, the Court found that Petitioner did not specify legal authority that would allow the Court to reconsider its November 18, 2022, Order.  (Ibid.)  Furthermore, the Court found that the Motion did not comply with the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure §§ 1008 and 2012, Evidence Code § 1400, and California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(b).  (Ibid.)  As such, the Court continued the hearing to May 10, 2023, and instructed Petitioner to file and serve supplemental papers to correct the aforementioned defects.  (Ibid.)  On March 27, 2023, Petitioner filed an Amended Motion.  On May 10, 2023, the Court denied Petitioner's Motion for Reconsideration.  (5-10-23 Minute Order.)  However, the Court, on its own motion, reconsidered its November 18, 2022, Order and reinstated the prior grant of attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $3,521.25.  (Ibid.)  Furthermore, the Court ordered Ms. Viele to provide some evidence of the date of her separation, either by declaration or legal documents within twenty (20) days’ notice.  (Ibid.)

 

On May 17, 2023, after the Court issued the final order on Petitioner’s Motion for Reconsideration, Petitioner filed a Supplement to Motion for Reconsideration with additional arguments for the Court to consider in its ruling.

 

On May 30, 2023, Petitioner filed another Motion for Reconsideration (“Motion”). 

 

On August 14, 2023, the Court noted several deficiencies in the Motion.  (8-14-23 Minute Order.)  The Court also indicated that non-party Viele had not submitted evidence of separation, as ordered by the Court on May 10, 2023, and thus, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 28, 2023, Petitioner filed another declaration in support of his Motion.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 1008 provides, in pertinent part:

 

“(a) When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make an application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.

 

(b) A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or in part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on an ex parte motion.

 

…

 

(e) This section specifies the court’s jurisdiction with regard to applications for reconsideration of its orders and renewals of previous motions, and applies to all applications to reconsider any order of a judge or court, or for the renewal of a previous motion, whether the order deciding the previous matter or motion is interim or final. No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(a), (b), (e).)

 

A motion for reconsideration under § 1008 requires that the moving party present new or different facts that were not previously considered by the Court.  (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13.)  However, the burden under § 1008 “is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”  (Ibid.; Even Zohar Construction & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 833 [finding that § 1008 imposes the special requirement of having to not only show new or different facts, circumstances, or law, but also to “show diligence with a satisfactory explanation for not presenting the new or different information earlier…”].)  Reconsideration cannot be granted based on claims that the court misinterpreted the law in its initial ruling because this is not a "new" or "different" matter.  (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Having reviewed Petitioner’s Motion filed on May 30, 2023, the Court made the following findings at the August 14, 2023, hearing.  (8-14-23 Minute Order.)

 

First, the Notice of Motion does not contain the address or name of the courthouse where the hearing on the Motion will take place, as required by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(b).

 

Second, the Court has not received any evidence that Viele was scheduled for a deposition on May 10, 2023, as suggested by Petitioner.

 

            Third, Petitioner does not make an adequate showing that the Court violated Family Code § 70(b).  Section § 70(b) states that “[i]n determining the date of separation, the court shall take into consideration all relevant evidence.”  (Family Code § 70(b).)  Petitioner does not put forth any new or different evidence to show that the Court violated this section.

 

Although the Court was inclined to deny the Motion due to these deficiencies, the Court noted that non-party Viele had not complied with the Court’s May 10, 2023, Order in submitting evidence of the date of separation, within twenty (20) days of notice of the Court’s Order.  (8-14-23 Minute Order.)  For this reason, the Court continued the hearing on Petitioner’s Motion.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 28, 2023, Petitioner filed a declaration presenting facts regarding Viele’s separation.

 

            The Court notes that Petitioner’s declaration does not correct any of the deficiencies previously found in the Motion.  Petitioner has reserved another hearing to be heard on his November 28, 2022, motion for reconsideration and continues to argue that the Court has misapplied and misinterpreted the law.  Petitioner does not present any new or different facts in order for the Court to reconsider its May 10, 2023, Order.  Petitioner had a chance to review the tentative ruling prior to the hearing on the Motion and to argue his case before the Court, and was later served with the final ruling by the Clerk on May 22, 2023.

 

Accordingly, the Court does not find that Petitioner has presented any basis for the Court to grant the relief he seeks through this Motion.  Accordingly, the Court DENIES Petitioner’s Motion for Reconsideration, filed on May 30, 2023.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

The Motion for Reconsideration, filed by Petitioner Joseph L. Shalant on May 30, 2023, is DENIED.

State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. v. Esdras C. Mestanza

21stlc00225

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

            On January 11, 2021, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Edras C. Mestanza aka Esdras N. Mestanza Chicas (“Mestanza”) (“Defendant”) for subrogation, stemming from an automobile collision between Defendant, on the one hand, and an individual insured by Plaintiff’s automobile insurance policy, on the other hand.  (Compl.)  Plaintiff compensated the insured for claimed damages in the amount of $16,108.21 and filed the instant claim against Defendant for allegedly causing the damages.  (Ibid. pp. 2-3.)

 

            On April 30, 2021, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

            On December 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Entry of Judgment in the Event of Default (“Stipulation”), signed by both parties, to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,798.08.  (12-5-22 Stipulation and Order.)  On December 6, 2022, the Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation.  (12-6-22 Order.)

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment (“Motion”).

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

CCP § 664.6, provides a summary procedure that enables judges to enforce a settlement agreement by entering a judgment pursuant to the terms of the parties’ settlement.  In particular, the statute provides:

 

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following:

 

(1) The party.

(2) An attorney who represents the party.

(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer's behalf.

 

CCP § 664.6(a)-(b) (emphasis added).

 

III.            Discussion

 

A. Retention of Jurisdiction

 

“‘[V]oluntary dismissal of an action or special proceeding terminates the court’s jurisdiction over the matter.’  (Conservatorship of Martha P. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 857, 867) [12 Cal.Rptr.3d 142.) ‘If requested by the parties,’ however, ‘the [trial] court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce [a] settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.’ (§ 664.6, italics added.)”  (Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917.)  “‘Because of its summary nature, strict compliance with the requirements of section 664.6 is prerequisite to invoking the power of the court to impose a settlement agreement.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37).)

 

“A request for the trial court to retain jurisdiction under section 664.6 ‘must conform to the same three requirements which the Legislature and the courts have deemed necessary for section 664.6 enforcement of the settlement itself: the request must be made (1) during the pendency of the case, not after the case has been dismissed in its entirety, (2) by the parties themselves, and (3) either in a writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.’”  (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 440).)  “The ‘request must be express, not implied from other language, and it must be clear and unambiguous.’” (Ibid. (quoting Wackeen, supra, 97 Cal.App.4th at 440).)

 

Here, the parties signed a Stipulation for Entry of Judgment in the Event of Default (“Stipulation”) containing the parties’ agreement for the Court to retain jurisdiction pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §664.6 to enforce the terms of the stipulation and enter judgment in the event of default.  (12-5-22 Stipulation ¶ 10.)  Prior to the dismissal of this action, the Stipulation was signed by the parties and submitted to the Court.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  On December 6, 2022, the Court dismissed the entire case without prejudice pursuant to the Stipulation and expressly stated that it “RETAINS JURISDICTION OF THIS CASE IN ACCORDANCE WITH AND PURSUANT TO CALIFORNIA CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 664.6.”  (12-6-22 Order.)

 

The Court finds that the Stipulation complies with § 664.6 requirements and the Court has retained jurisdiction to enter judgment pursuant to the parties’ Stipulation in this action.

 

B. Entry of Judgment

 

The Stipulation Agreement filed on December 5, 2022, provides that Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to dismiss the action on the premise that Defendant would compensate Plaintiff for the settlement amount of $8,798.08.  (12-5-22 Stipulation ¶ 4.)  Pursuant to the Stipulation, Defendant’s insurance carrier Alliance United Insurance Company would make a payment of $4,298.08 within thirty (30) days of Defendant’s execution of the agreement.  (Ibid. at ¶ 2(i).)  Defendant would pay the remaining balance of $4,500 with $100 monthly payments commencing on March 1, 2023, and continuing on the first day of each month, until the balance is paid in full.  (Ibid. at ¶ 2(ii).)  Defendant would also be responsible for the balance not paid by his insurance carrier.  (Ibid. at ¶ 2(i).)  All parties signed the Stipulation.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)

 

The Stipulation also provides that Defendant will have a fifteen (15) day grace period to make payments.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  In the event Defendant fails to make payments, Plaintiff will give written notice of default and Defendant will have an additional ten (10) days to cure the default.  (Ibid.)  If Defendant does not cure the default, “Plaintiff may immediately cause Judgment to be entered pursuant to the terms set forth in this Stipulation for the full amount of the agreed upon judgement as set forth in Paragraph 1 less any monies paid to date of the breach; and shall also file a partial Satisfaction of Judgment for all sums previously paid pursuant to this Stipulation.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  If Defendant’s contact information changes, he is responsible for providing written notice to Plaintiff, otherwise Plaintiff will send the notice of default to the address indicated in the Stipulation.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion alleging that Defendant breached the Stipulation.  (Mot. pp. 1-2.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Susan M. Benson, states that Defendant’s insurance carrier made a payment of $4,289.08.  (Benson Decl. ¶ 5.)  Defendant did not make any payments.  (Ibid.)  On April 13, 2023, Counsel sent a default notice to Defendant to pay the remaining sum of $4,500.00.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. 2.)  However, Defendant has not made any payments.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)  Thus, Plaintiff requests that the Court enter judgment for Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $12,336.78 as follows: principal amount of $16,108.21, less $4,298.08 in payments made by Defendant’s insurance carrier, plus filing costs of $72, and lawsuit filing fee of $454.65.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

 

The Court finds the Stipulation to be valid and enforceable under Code of Civil Proc. § 664.6.  Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant did not make any monthly payments and did not respond to Plaintiff’s written notice of default.  Thus, a valid and signed stipulation agreement was breached and the Court retained jurisdiction to enter judgment upon breach.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Dismissal entered on December 6, 2022, is vacated, and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $12,336.78 as follows: principal amount of $11,810.13, $72.00 in motion filing fees, and $454.65 in lawsuit filing fees.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Dismissal entered on December 6, 2022, is vacated, and judgment is entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant for $12,336.78 as follows: principal amount of $11,810.13, $72.00 in motion filing fees, and $454.65 in lawsuit filing fees.

On January 19, 2023, pursuant to Plaintiff’s oral request, the Court dismissed Defendant USAA without prejudice.  (1-19-23 Order of Dismissal.)

 

On August 21 and 24, 2023, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s filed the two instant Motions:

 

(1)   Motion to Compel Deposition of Gannon Brown and Production of Vehicle for Inspection and Order Imposing Monetary Sanctions of $3,633.80 (“MTC Deposition”);

(2)   Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle for Inspection and Impose Monetary Sanctions of $5,016.30 (“MTC Production”).

 

On September 1, 2023, the Court granted Eli’s Ex Parte Application for Order Continuing Trial (and All Related Dates), or in the Alternative, an Order Shortening Time for Hearing of Same.  (9-1-23 Minute Order.)  The Court ordered the trial continued to November 30, 2023, along with all related discovery and trial deadlines.  (Ibid.)

 

On September 5, 2023, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Brown filed a single Opposition to both Motions.  No reply has been filed.

 

Eli’s filed an Answer to the SACC on September 5, 2023.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.    Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s moves to compel the attendance of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Brown at an oral deposition and the production of the subject 2017 Porsche 911 Carrera (“Porsche”) for physical and photographic inspection at the deposition.  (MTC Dep. p. 2.)  Eli’s also requests that the Court impose monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,633.80 against Brown and his counsel for their willful failure to appear at the deposition and produce the tangible item requested.  (Ibid.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a) provides:

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”

 

The motion shall “(1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice” and “(2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

 

On July 20, 2023, Eli’s served Brown’s counsel with a Notice of Deposition and Demand for Inspection of Vehicle, setting the deposition and inspection for August 3, 2023, at 1:30 p.m.  (Thomas Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. 1.)  On July 21, 2023, Brown’s attorney served the following three objections to the Notice.  (Ibid. at, Ex. 2.)  Counsel objected on the basis that (1) the deposition was set after the discovery cut-off date, (2) the date of inspection was not at least 30 days after service of the Demand for Inspection, and (3) the notice failed to provide a reasonable place of inspection.  (Ibid.)  Eli’s argues that the “objections are specious and without any colorable legal justification” as the discovery cutoff date is August 29, 2023, and providing 30-day notice and location of inspection are not requirements for depositions.  (MTC Dep. p. 4.)  Eli’s adds that the attorneys for each party met and conferred telephonically in good faith regarding the motion and Defendant’s objections on August 16, 2023.  (Thomas Dec.)  However, the attorneys could not come to an agreement and Defendant Brown refuses to submit to a deposition or produce the Porsche for inspection.  (Ibid.)

 

Eli’s argues that it has a right to depose Defendant and request inspection of tangible things pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.010 and has properly served a notice of deposition.  (MTC Dep. p. 5.)  The inspection was to take place at Eli’s in Inglewood and the deposition would take place in the conference room.  (Ibid.)  The location was selected due to its convenience to both parties, special lighting booths for visual and photographic inspection, and because it was the same location where Defendant Brown approved the color match for painting of the Porsche.  (Ibid.)  Eli’s has good cause for demanding production and inspection of the Porsche because it will produce evidence of the matching paint color for the purpose of trial.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-6.)  Moreover, Defendant’s objections are “without any factual or legal justification.”  (Ibid. at p. 6.)

 

Despite earlier objections, Defendant Brown does not oppose the Motion.  (Oppos. p. 3.)  However, he expresses concerns that “Plaintiff would exploit the inspection of the vehicle as an opportunity to refuse to release the vehicle back to him, and instead claim that it had the right to retain possession pursuant to a mechanic’s lien.”  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  The concern arises from the demands made in the Complaint regarding possession of the vehicle.  (Ibid.)  Defense counsel has offered to produce the vehicle in a police or sheriff’s department parking lot, or other neutral outdoor location; however, Plaintiff’s counsel has refused.  (Ibid.)  Defense counsel has also requested a stipulation to a court order to have the vehicle released immediately after inspection or after 30 minutes of inspection, however Plaintiff’s counsel has again refused.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)  Thus, Defendant agrees to the inspection either at a neutral outdoor location or with the condition that the vehicle be released after the conclusion of inspection.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 and is entitled to compel Plaintiff’s attendance and testimony at deposition.  Plaintiff has also presented good cause for compelling production of the vehicle for inspection.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Deposition and Production is GRANTED.  Defendant is ordered to appear at a properly noticed deposition, within thirty (30) days of the Court’s Order.  Furthermore, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.

 

B.     Requests for Production

 

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)  If a party to whom requests for production of documents is directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).)  The party also waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2024.020(a), 2031.300.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiff served Defendant with a Demand for Inspection and Production of Vehicle, set for September 12, 2022, at Eli’s repair facility in Inglewood, CA.  (MTC Prod. - Thomas Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  Defendant did not respond or serve an objection to the Demand.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  On September 8, 2022, defense counsel asked for a three-week extension to respond to the written discovery and to reschedule the vehicle inspection date.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. 3.)  On September 12, 2022, the attorneys discussed rescheduling the vehicle inspection date and agreed to schedule to a mutually convenient date, September 30.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  Plaintiff’s counsel served Defendant with an amended Demand for Inspection and Production of Vehicle, set for September 30, 2022.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7, Ex. 2.)  On September 27, 2022, defense counsel asked for an extension to respond to the written discovery, but agreed to keep the inspection date of September 30, 2022; Plaintiff’s counsel provided a one-week extension to respond to the written discovery.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  Plaintiff’s counsel traveled more than 3.5 hours for the inspection on September 30, 2022, however, Defendant did not appear with the vehicle.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10.)  On October 3, 2022, defense counsel explained that Defendant’s failure to appear was due to a miscommunication between Defendant and his attorney.  (Ibid. at ¶ 11.)  On December 9, 2022, the attorneys met and conferred over the telephone regarding the instant Motion.  (Ibid. at ¶ 12.)  On December 15, 2022, defense counsel offered to present the vehicle for inspection on January 10, 2023, thus, Plaintiff served a Second Amended Demand for Inspection of Vehicle.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 13-14, Exs. 3-4.)  Despite several extensions, Defendant served an objection to the Second Amended Demand for Inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 15-16, Ex. 5.)  Between January 5 and 9, 2023, the attorneys exchanged several meet and confer emails regarding Defendant’s objection and insistence that conditions be imposed on the inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶ 17.)  On January 10, 2023, Defendant Brown failed to present the Porsche for inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶ 18.)  On August 16, 2023, the attorneys met and conferred telephonically but did not come to an agreement.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 19-20.)

 

Plaintiff states that it has a good faith basis for inspection of the Porsche as “a visual and photographic inspection of the Porsche at the same place and under the same conditions s when Mr. Brown approved the color match is of critical importance in this case.”  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 21-22.)

 

Defendant presents the same objections to the instant Motion as he does to the Motion to Compel Deposition and Production of Vehicle.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that it propounded a demand for production on Defendant and Defendant has improperly served objections, after objections had been waived, and has failed to produce the vehicle for inspection.  Although Plaintiff was not obligated to meet and confer with Defendant, Plaintiff’s counsel has extensively attempted to resolve the issue informally.

 

For this reason, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle for Inspection.  The vehicle must be produced for inspection at a time and date determined by Plaintiff.  To address Defendant’s concerns, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.

Case Number: 21STLC06138    Hearing Date: September 18, 2023    Dept: 25

Jose Manuel Gonzalez v. Los Angeles Police Department

21stlc06138

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiff Jose Manuel Gonzalez (“Plaintiff” or “Gonzales”), in propria persona, filed a Complaint against Defendant Los Angeles Police Department (“Defendant” or “LAPD”) for personal injury, property damages, wrongful death.

 

On January 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed Proof of Service of Summons by mail and acknowledgment of receipt of service, indicating that the pleadings were mailed to 100 West 1st Street, Room 228, Los Angeles, CA 90012.

 

On February 17, 2023, the date set for Non-Jury Trial, the Court noted that service was not properly effectuated on Defendant and placed trial off calendar.  (2-17-23 Minute Order.)  The Court set Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service for April 26, 2023.  (Ibid.)  On April 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed Proof of Service indicating that the pleadings had been personally served on Defendant LAPD and the City of Los Angeles, City Clerk, at 200 North Spring Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012.  Accordingly, on April 26, 2023, the Court discharged the OSC Re: Failure to File Proof of Service and set Order to Show Cause Re: Defendant’s Filing of an Answer or Plaintiff’s Entry of Default for June 27, 2023.  (4-26-23 Minute Order.)  Plaintiff filed another Proof of Service of pleadings, indicating that he had personally served LAPD on May 30, 2023, at 200 Spring Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012.

 

            On June 26, 2023, Defendant City of Los Angeles (erroneously sued as LAPD) filed the instant Motion to Dismiss (“Motion”).  On August 28, 2023, Defendant filed Notice of Non-Opposition to Motion.  No opposition has been filed.

 

            On August 28, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to September 18, 2023.  (8-28-23 Minute Order.)

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Defendant City of Los Angeles (“City”) moves to dismiss the Complaint filed by Plaintiff Gonzalez on the grounds that Plaintiff did not serve the Complaint within 60 days, as required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(b) and thus, the Complaint is subject to dismissal pursuant to the Government Code § 68608(b) and Code of Civil Procedure § 575.2(a).  (Mot. p. 2.)

 

First, Defendant argues that dismissal is appropriate because Plaintiff is representing himself and thus, noncompliance with rule 3.110(b) was entirely due to his own conduct.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  Second, less severe sanctions against Plaintiff would not be effective because the record reflects that Plaintiff failed to comply with the rule after the Court set Orders to Show Cause.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)  Plaintiff missed the deadline to serve Defendant by 588 days, or 1.6 years, when he served Defendant on May 30, 2023.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(b) states that a complaint must be served and proof of service must be filed within 60 days after the complaint is filed.  Plaintiff may file an application for a court order to extend the time for service, but plaintiff must file for an extension before the time for service has elapsed.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.110(e).)  If the plaintiff does not comply with rule 3.110(b) and (d), fails to serve the complaint within the permitted time, and does not request an extension, “the court may issue an order to show cause why sanctions shall not be imposed.”  (Ibid. at rule 3.110(f).)

 

Furthermore, pursuant to Government Code § 68608(b), “Judges shall have all the powers to impose sanctions authorized by law, including the power to dismiss actions or strike pleadings, if it appears that less severe sanctions would not be effective after taking into account the effect of previous sanctions or previous lack of compliance in the case. Judges are encouraged to impose sanctions to achieve the purposes of this article.”  According to Code of Civil Procedure § 575.2, “local rules promulgated pursuant to Section 575.1 may provide that if any counsel, a party represented by counsel, or a party if in pro se, fails to comply with any of the requirements thereof, the court on motion of a party or on its own motion may strike out all or any part of any pleading of that party, or, dismiss the action or proceeding or any part thereof, or enter a judgment by default against that party, or impose other penalties of a lesser nature as otherwise provided by law, and may order that party or his or her counsel to pay to the moving party the reasonable expenses in making the motion, including reasonable attorney fees. No penalty may be imposed under this section without prior notice to, and an opportunity to be heard by, the party against whom the penalty is sought to be imposed.”

 

Having reviewed Defendant’s legal arguments and evidence, the Court does not find that Defendant has presented a sufficient basis for dismissal of the Complaint.  California Rules of Court, rule 3.110, provides the Court with the discretionary authority to set an order to show cause regarding sanctions if a party fails to timely serve pleadings and file proof of service.  Here, the Court set an OSC Re: Failure to File Proof of Service on February 17, 2023.  (2-17-23 Minute Order.)  After Plaintiff filed Proof of Service on April 3, 2023, the Court discharged the OSC.  (4-26-23 Minute Order.)  The Court also notes that Government Code § 68608(b) and Code of Civil Procedure § 575.2 both provide the Court with the discretionary power to impose sanctions, including dismissal of an action, but do not require the Court to dismiss the action for Plaintiff’s failure to timely serve Defendant.  Finally, Defendant mistakenly asserts that the record shows a history of non-compliance after the Court’s imposition of less severe sanctions.  To date, the Court has not imposed any sanctions against Plaintiff.  The Court set an OSC regarding Plaintiff’s failure to serve Defendant and discharged the OSC because Plaintiff complied and properly served Defendant.

 

Accordingly, the Court does not find that Defendant has presented sufficient evidence or arguments demonstrating that the Court must dismiss the action because of Plaintiff’s delayed service of the pleadings.  Defendant’s Motion to Dismiss is denied.

 

III.            Conclusion

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

The Motion to Dismiss, filed by Defendant City of Los Angeles (erroneously sued as Los Angeles Police Department), is DENIED without prejudice.

Case Number: 22STLC08083    Hearing Date: September 18, 2023    Dept: 25

Richard Dahl v. Pursche-Gillingham, LLC, et al.

22stlc08083

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On August 5, 2022, Plaintiff Richard Dahl (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Pursche-Gillingham, LLC (“PG”), Apelila and J, LLC (“AJ”), Ono Hawaiian BBQ, Inc. (“Ono”), and Joshua Liang (“Liang”) for an alleged violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.

 

On January 30, 2023, Defendants AJ, Ono, and Liang filed an Answer to the Complaint.  Defendant PG filed an Answer on February 1, 2023.

On March 14, 2023, Plaintiff amended the Complaint to add HGL Restaurant Group, LLC (“HGL”) as Defendant Doe 1.  (3-14-23 Amendment to Complaint.)  HGL filed an Answer on April 19, 2023.

 

On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed another Amendment to Complaint, adding Upside Crenshaw Holding, LLC (“Upside”) as Defendant Doe 6.  (6-14-23 Amendment to Complaint.)

 

On July 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel HGL’s Answer to Interrogatory (“Motion”).  Defendant HGL filed an Opposition on August 3, 2023.

 

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel informed the Court that he had received the response to the interrogatories; however, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion and ordered Defendant to file supplemental proof that the response to Special Interrogatory No. 5 had been properly served on Plaintiff.  (8-16-23 Minute Order.)  The Court indicated that it would address the matter of monetary sanctions at the next scheduled hearing.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 24, 2023, Defendant HGL filed supplemental papers in response to the Court’s August 16, 2023, Order.

 

On August 29, 2023, pursuant to Plaintiff’s request, the Court entered default against Defendant Upside.  (8-29-23 Request for Entry of Default.)

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.     Interrogatories

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.260(a).)  If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).)  Once compelled to respond, the party waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2024.020(a), 2030.290.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant HGL’s verified answer to Interrogatory No. 5 from Special Interrogatories (Set One).  (Mot. p. 1.)  Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $500.  (Mot. p. 1.)

 

On April 24, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant HGL with Special Interrogatories, Set One.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff did not receive a verified response to Special Interrogatory No. 5.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Plaintiff’s counsel sent a follow-up letter to Defendant HGL.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. B.)

 

Defendant HGL opposes the Motion.  Defense counsel explains that Special Interrogatories served on Defendant HGL were double-sided and Special Interrogatory No. 5 was on the second page, which was not scanned into defense counsel’s system.  (Lewis Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.)  Defense counsel requested a two-week extension; however, Plaintiff’s counsel did not agree to the extension, unless the responses were without objections.  (Ibid.)  Thus, defense counsel served written responses to the Special Interrogatories on May 30, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Defense counsel states that Plaintiff had 45 days after the service date to compel responses.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)  Moreover, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to meet and confer on May 31, 2023, but defense counsel thought Special Interrogatory No. 5 was a “copy and paste error” since it was not in his records.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  On June 29, 2023, Defendant served supplemental responses to the Special Interrogatories in defense counsel’s records.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)  After Plaintiff filed and served the instant Motion, defense counsel learned that there was a second page that included Special Interrogatory No. 5.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 8-9.)  Defense counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel of his mistake and intention to serve a verified substantive response, which he served on July 24, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10.)  On July 31, 2023, defense counsel sent a letter requesting that Plaintiff withdraw the Motion and followed up by email and phone on August 2, 2023; however, Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 11-12, Exs. A-B.)  Defendant HGL requests that the Court deny the Motion because it is moot and deny Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.  (Oppos. p. 2.)

 

At the hearing on August 16, 2023, the Court noted that Plaintiff has filed a motion to compel an initial response pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290, not a motion to compel a further response pursuant to § 2030.300.  (8-16-23 Minute Order.)  Thus, there is no time limit for filing the instant Motion, other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial, and there is no pre-filing meet and confer requirement.  (Ibid.; Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2024.020(a), 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

Moreover, the Court noted that Plaintiff has demonstrated that he has propounded Special Interrogatories on Defendant HGL.  (8-16-23 Minute Order.)  Although, Defendant HGL argues that it inadvertently missed Special Interrogatory No. 5 and served a response after the Motion was filed, Defendant did not present the Court with any evidence that the response was properly served.  (Ibid.)  At the hearing, Plaintiff confirmed that he had received Defendant’s response to Special Interrogatory No. 5; however, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion and ordered Defendant to file proof that Plaintiff had been properly served with the response.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 24, 2023, Defendant filed the Supplemental Declaration of Jeremy Lewis in response to the Court’s August 16, 2023, Order.  Counsel has submitted proof that the verified response to Special Interrogatory No. was served on Defendant on July 24, 2023.  (Supp. Lewis Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. A.)  Defendant HGL argues that the Motion has been rendered moot.  (Supp. Brief p. 2.)

The Court finds that Defendant has properly served the response to Special Interrogatory No. 5 and Plaintiff has confirmed receipt at the August 16, 2023, hearing.  Accordingly, the Motion to Compel is placed off calendar as moot.

 

B.    Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

Furthermore, § 2030.290(c) authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions if a party fails to respond to interrogatories, “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Sanctions are calculated based on “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Ibid. § 2023.030(a)).

 

Plaintiff requests $500 in sanctions for Defendant HGL’s failure to respond to the instant discovery request.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 6.)  Plaintiff’s counsel states that his hourly rate is $500.00 per hour, and he has “spent and will spend a cumulative total of no less than one hour in communicating with said Defendant regarding this motion, preparing the motion, and appearing at the hearing on the motion.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendant HGL argues that the Court should deny Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions because it acted with substantial justification and imposition of sanctions would be unjust.  (Supp. Brief pp. 2-3.)  Here, Defendant’s failure to comply “was by no means willful” as it was an inadvertent “administrative error.”  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  After discovering the existence of Special Interrogatory No. 5, “Defendant acted promptly and diligently after realizing the error and provided a verified substantive response to Plaintiff.”  (Ibid.)  Moreover, “Defendant is substantially justified in opposing Plaintiff’s Motion” as it has served a response and the Motion has been rendered moot.  (Ibid.)  Finally, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $500 is excessive and inflated.  (Ibid. at pp. 3-5.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant HGL’s failure to timely respond to Special Interrogatory No. 5 was due to an administrative error and not misuse of the discovery process.  After discovering the error, Defendant acted promptly to provide the response.  Thus, Defendant acted with substantial justification and the imposition of sanctions would be unjust.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is denied.

 

 

III.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

Plaintiff Richard Dahl’s Motion to Compel HGL Restaurant Group, LLC’s Answer to Interrogatory is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.  Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

            On August 14, 2023, Defendants Rockvale LP, Rockvale GP, Rockvale Limited filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint for Damages, set for hearing on September 18, 2023.

 

            On August 17, 2023, Defendant FPI filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint, set for hearing on September 19, 2023.  Plaintiffs filed Oppositions on September 5, 2023.

 

            On August 24, 2023, Defendants Eden Housing, Inc. and Eden Iris Gardens, LLC, filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike First Amended Complaint, set for hearing on September 19, 2023.

 

            On September 7, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff Erica Henderson’s Ex Parte Application for Extension of Time to oppose the Demurrers with Motions to Strike, filed by (1) Defendants Rockvale LP, Rockvale GP, and Rockvale Limited, set for hearing on September 18, 2023, (2) Defendants Eden Housing, Inc. and Eden Iris Gardens, LLC, set for hearing on September 19, 2023, and (3) Defendant FPI, set for hearing on September 19, 2023.  (9-7-23 Ex Parte ¶ 6.)  The Court ordered the oppositions to be filed by September 13, 2023.  (Ibid.)

 

            On September 13, 2023, Plaintiffs filed Proof of Service of the Oppositions on Defendants by mail.  However, the Oppositions have not been filed with the Court.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in the complaint.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.)  There are two types of demurrers – general demurrers and special demurrers.  (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

 

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.)  Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 430.30, 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].”  (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.)  It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (Ibid.). At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him.  (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.)  The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.)  "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence."  (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

 

Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  However, special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction civil actions and any grounds for special demurrers must be raised as affirmative defenses in the answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 92(c).)

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).)  The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(2).)  Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.”  (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)  Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue.  (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 ("Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.").)

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Once arbitration is concluded, “any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.).  Any of the parties may file a petition with the court, which must then “confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (Code of Civil Proc. §§ 1285, 1286; EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.)  “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.”  (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.)  “Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.”  (EHM Productions, supra, at p. 1063-64.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure, § 1285.4 states: “A petition under this chapter shall:

 

(a)   Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators.

(c)   Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”  (Emphasis added.)  In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by filing and serving a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served.  (Code Civ. Proc.  §§ 1288, 1288.4.)

 

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 1290.4, the statute governing proper service of this Petition states, in pertinent part:

 

“(a) A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.

 

(b) If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.

 

III.            Discussion

 

On July 5, 2023, the Court noted that the Petition listed the Van Nuys Courthouse East address, instead of the address for the Spring Street Courthouse.  (7-5-23 Minute Order.)  Pursuant to the request of Petitioner Sanders, the Court continued the hearing to August 16, 2023, and ordered the moving party to give notice.  (Ibid.)

 

            Given that on March 19, 2023, Petitioners served the Court’s Notice of Hearing on Petition, listing the correct address for the Spring Street Courthouse, the Court waives the erroneous address on the Petition.

 

            On August 16, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Petition because there was no indication that Petitioner had served Respondents with a notice of continuance.  (8-16-23 Minute Order.)  The Court gave notice to Petitioner Sanders and Respondent Badler.  (8-16-23 Certificate of Mailing.)

 

            The Court proceeds with ruling on the Petition.

 

            Petitioner has attached a Home Improvement Contract entered into between Petitioner Sanders and Respondent.  (Pet. p. 5.)  The Court notes that Petitioner has submitted an unclear copy of the Contract that is difficult to discern.  There is an initialed box on the contract indicating that the parties agree to arbitration; however, this provision refers to the “Arbitration of Disputes” section which is not attached.  (Ibid.)

 

            Petitioner has also filed a FairClaims Post-Dispute Arbitration Agreement and a FairClaims Arbitration Award issued by Arbitrator Kathy Vogdes Esq.  (Pet. pp. 4, 6-11.)  The Court notes that Petitioner has not presented any evidence that a neutral arbitrator served a copy of the Award on the parties.

 

            On June 27, 2023, Petitioners served Respondents with the Petition, Notice of Hearing, and supporting documents via personal service.  The Court finds that the service requirement has been met.

 

            Although Petitioners have complied with most of the requirements for the instant Petition, the Court finds it difficult to review the copy of the Home Improvement Contract attached.  Thus, the Court orders Petitioners to file a clear copy of the Home Improvement Contract, along with the “Arbitration of Disputes” attachment.

 

            For this reason, the hearing on the Petition is continued.
