DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV02605    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

DEBBIE ANN MARTINEZ, an individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

24TRCV02605

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Sustained

 

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES, a Public Entity; LOS ANGELES WORLD AIRPORTS, a Public Entity; and Does 1-25, Inclusive,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             December 16, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          City of Los Angeles

 

Responding Party:      None

 

HEARING: Demurrer

 

            The Court considered the moving.

 

RULING

 

            The Court sustains the Demurrer with leave to amend within 20 days of this ruling.

           

BACKGROUND

 

            On August 8, 2024, Plaintiff Debbie Ann Martinez filed the Complaint against Defendants City of Los Angeles and Los Angeles World Airports, alleging causes of action for negligence, premises liability, and willful failure to warn arising from an alleged slip and fall incident at Los Angeles International Airport.

 

            On November 7, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer.

 

            Plaintiff has not opposed the Demurrer.

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Meet and Confer 

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) 

 

            Defendant’s Counsel called Plaintiff’s Counsel on October 18, 2024, and spoke about possible deficiencies in the Complaint. (Rosenthal Decl. ¶ 4.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Plaintiff Debbie Ann Martinez alleges that Defendants City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports, and Does 1-25 negligently caused her injuries on July 30, 2023, when she slipped and fell due to a dangerous condition on Defendants' property at Los Angeles International Airport (LAX), Terminal 1. The walking surface was inherently dangerous for its intended use, and a liquid-like substance caused her to slip, resulting in serious bodily injuries.

 

Plaintiff asserts that Defendants were negligent in installing, constructing, maintaining, inspecting, repairing, managing, supervising, and operating the premises, including floors, fixtures, entrance/exit ways, and surrounding areas. She claims Defendants either created the hazardous condition or failed to correct, remove, or warn her of the danger despite having actual or constructive notice. Defendants’ failure to act or warn was a direct cause of her injuries.

 

As a result of Defendants' negligence, Plaintiff sustained serious bodily injuries, requiring past and future medical treatment and related expenses. She also alleges loss of earnings, diminished earning capacity, and ongoing emotional distress, pain and suffering, mental anguish, and loss of quality of life. Plaintiff further names Does 1-25 as unidentified individuals or entities responsible for her injuries, pending further discovery. Damages sought exceed the jurisdictional minimum and will be proven at trial.

 

 

Cause of Action 1 – General Negligence

 

“To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff's damages or injuries.” (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62.) 

 

            Defendant argues that no general negligence claim can be asserted against a public entity as Government Code § 815.2 limits a public entity's liability to acts or omissions of its employees performed within the scope of employment, provided such acts would otherwise give rise to a cause of action against the employee. (Gov. Code § 815.2(a).) If the employee is immune, the public entity is also immune. (Gov. Code § 815.2(b).)

 

Defendant asserts that employees acting within the scope of employment are immune from claims related to dangerous property conditions under Government Code §§ 815.4 and 840. The Complaint fails to identify specific acts or omissions by any named employee of Defendant that caused Plaintiff’s injuries, instead making collective and vague allegations, which are insufficient to state a valid claim.

 

Further, Defendant argues the Complaint does not allege with sufficient detail the essential elements of a negligence claim necessary to attach liability to an employee. (Merrill v. Navegar, Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 465.) The Complaint also fails to address whether any negligent employee would have immunity under Gov. Code § 815.2(b), which must be specifically pled. (Gov. Code § 815.2(b).) Therefore, Defendant concludes that Plaintiff has not sufficiently stated a cause of action for negligence against a public entity and requests that the demurrer to this cause of action be sustained.

 

            Plaintiff has filed no opposition to the Demurrer.

 

            Defendant has shown that Plaintiff's negligence claim is barred because, except as otherwise provided for by statute, there is no liability on the part of a public entity for any act or omission of itself, a public employee, or any other person. (Cal. Gov. Code § 815(a); Susman v. City of Los Angeles (1969) 263 Cal.App.2d 803, 808; De Villers v. County of San Diego (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 238, 247.)

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the first cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 2 – Willful Failure to Warn

 

            The Second cause of action asserts a count for willful failure to warn under California Civil Code § 846.

 

            Plaintiff’s claim of willful failure to warn, based on California Civil Code § 846, is inapplicable to public entities like Defendants. (Civ. Code, § 846.) Liability for public entities regarding the condition of public property is governed exclusively by the Government Claims Act, specifically § 835, which covers dangerous conditions on public property. (Gov. Code § 835.) Section 846 applies only to private property owners who grant access for recreational purposes and does not apply to public entities or commercial travel, such as the incident involving Plaintiff. (Civ. Code § 846.) Furthermore, the Court has explicitly rejected the use of § 846 to impose liability on public entities. (Delta Farms Reclamation Dist. v. Superior Court (1983) 33 Cal. 3d 699, 704-06.) Thus, Plaintiff’s claim for willful failure to warn is barred by the Government Claims Act. (Civ. Code, § 846; Delta Farms Reclamation Dist., supra, 33 Cal. 3d 699, 704-06.)

 

            Plaintiff has filed no opposition to the Demurrer.

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 3 – Dangerous Condition of Public Property

 

            A public entity may be liable for a dangerous condition of public property if the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, the dangerous condition caused the injury, the condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the type of injury that occurred, and either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee within the scope of their employment created the condition, or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to take measures to protect against it. (Gov. Code § 835; Sun v. City of Oakland (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1183.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to sufficiently allege a cause of action for dangerous condition of public property with the required particularity. Defendant contends that vicarious liability for the acts of employees or agents is precluded by Government Code §§ 815.2(b) and 840, which provide immunity for public entities and employees under such claims. Additionally, Plaintiff has not adequately alleged either actual or constructive knowledge of the alleged dangerous condition. (Manual v. PGE (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 927, 945.)

 

Defendant further asserts that the Complaint lacks the necessary allegations to establish the essential elements of a dangerous condition claim under Government Code § 835. Specifically, Plaintiff has failed to allege: (1) that Defendant owned or controlled the property; (2) that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the incident; (3) that the dangerous condition created a foreseeable risk of the kind of injury that occurred; (4) that the dangerous condition was caused by the negligence of an employee acting within the scope of employment, or that Defendant had sufficient notice of the dangerous condition and failed to act; (5) that Plaintiff suffered harm; and (6) that the dangerous condition was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s injuries. (Gov Code § 835.)

 

            Plaintiff has not filed an opposition to this Demurrer.

 

            While Plaintiff has pleaded that she slipped on a water-like substance and fell, the rest of her allegations are overly general. (Complaint p. 4-5.) Plaintff fails to plead how the dangerous condition was caused by the negligence of an employee acting within the scope of their duties, or how the dangerous condition created a foreseeable risk of injury. (Gov Code § 835.) Thus, the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to plead a cause of action for dangerous condition of public property.

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the third cause of action with leave to amend.
