DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV01046    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: H

Brenes v. United Therapy Network, Case No. 23PSCV01046

 

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Defendant United Therapy Network’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

I.        Background

This is an action for professional negligence. In January 2022, Plaintiff Evelia Brenes (“Plaintiff”) received physical therapy treatments from Defendant United Therapy Network (“UTN”) for recovery from a recent knee surgery. During Plaintiff’s third therapy session on January 10, 2022, she alleges UTN’s physical therapist failed to use the appropriate standard of care in administering treatment by using a massage gun on Plaintiff’s knee despite her complaints of pain. Plaintiff alleges UTN’s treatment delayed her recovery and forced her to incur costs for additional medical treatment. 

On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against UTN and Does 1-50, alleging a single cause of action for professional negligence. 

Pending before the Court is UTN’s motion for summary judgment. Because the Court finds that UTN is entitled to summary judgment on the issue of causation, UTN’s motion is granted.

 

II.      Legal Standard 

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)


III.     Discussion

UTN moves for summary judgment on the grounds that UTN did not breach the applicable standard of care and did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries. As discussed below, UTN is entitled to summary judgment on the issue of causation.

To establish a claim for professional negligence, a plaintiff must demonstrate: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional’s negligence.” (Turpin v. Sortini (1982) 31 Cal.3d 220, 229-230, quoting 4 Witkin, Summary of Cal. Law (8th ed. 1974) Torts, § 488 et seq., p. 2749.)

“In professional malpractice cases, expert opinion testimony is required to prove or disprove that the defendant performed in accordance with the prevailing standard of care,¿except in cases where the negligence is obvious to laymen.” (Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 523.) And “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.” (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 985, quoting Hutchinson v. United States (9th Cir. 1988) 838 F.2d 390, 392.) Similarly, “[w]here the complexity of the causation issue is beyond common experience,¿expert testimony is required to establish causation.” (Garbell v. Conejo Hardwoods, Inc. (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1563, 1569.) Plaintiffs must establish “that defendants’ breach of the standard of care was the cause, within a reasonable medical probability, of [their] injury.” (Bushling v. Fremont Medical Center (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 493, 509.) 

The Complaint alleges UTN owed Plaintiff a duty of
care as a physical therapist. (Complaint, ¶ 31.) UTN allegedly breached that
duty when its staff used a massage gun on Plaintiff’s recovering knee notwithstanding
her complaints of pain and medical history. (Complaint, ¶¶ 35-36.) The
Complaint further alleges that the massage gun was the actual and proximate
cause of Plaintiff’s injuries, which included months of unnecessary physical
and emotional pain, time, and expenses. (Complaint, ¶¶ 37-39.) In arguing that UTN
complied with the applicable standard of care and did not cause Plaintiff’s
injuries, UTN points to the opinions of two experts: David Powers, PT, DPT, and
Lawrence Miller, M.D.

UTN first relies on the expert opinion of David Powers, PT, DPT, a licensed physical therapist. Dr. Powers opines that UTN’s therapists acted within the applicable standard of care when they used electrical stimulation and a massage gun during Plaintiff’s treatment. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 40.) Specifically, Dr. Powers states that there is no evidence that the massage gun was applied improperly, as UTN’s therapist appropriately applied the massage gun to the soft tissue around Plaintiff’s knee in a circular motion for approximately five minutes, appropriately increased the massage gun’s volume or rate of oscillation during treatment, and stopped applying the massage gun upon Plaintiff’s request. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 41-43; see Plaintiff’s Separate Statement (PSS), ¶¶ 21-23.) According to Dr. Powers, the fact that Plaintiff felt pain during the treatment was not indicative of any negligence and was to be expected during therapy. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 42-43; see PSS, ¶ 22.)

UTN next relies on the expert opinion of Lawrence Miller, M.D., a licensed physician with experience in evaluating and treating neuropathic pain, complex regional pain syndrome, knee pain, orthopedic pain, and generalized pain complaints. (MSJ, Ex. 4, ¶ 3.) To a reasonable degree of medical probability, Dr. Miller states UTN did not cause or contribute to Plaintiff’s injuries or damages. (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 41.) Dr. Miller opines that Plaintiff’s pain was caused by postoperative peripheral inflammation following her knee arthroscopy—a condition she developed prior to UTN’s treatment, based on her exhibiting “joint effusion, posterior knee tenderness, and pain during her orthopedic visit on December 27, 2021.” (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶¶ 39, 41.) 

In opposition to UTN’s motion, Plaintiff relies on the expert opinion of Philip Lim, DPT, a licensed physical therapist.[FN1] Dr. Lim opines that UTN’s therapists did not act within the applicable standard of care by (1) utilizing a massage gun in close proximity to the area of Plaintiff’s surgery for a period of longer than one or two consecutive minutes and (2) utilizing a massage gun when Dr. Lugo did not specifically request it in Plaintiff’s referral to UTN. (Dr. Lim Decl., ¶ 8, 11.) Dr. Lim further opines that the improper use of a massage gun directly caused Plaintiff to experience pain in an area of the knee that was still healing from surgery and that the type of pain experienced does not normally result from the use of a massage gun. (Dr. Lim Decl., ¶ 10.)

Based on Dr. Lim’s declaration, the Court finds a dispute of material fact exists as to whether UTN’s administration of a massage gun fell within the applicable standard of care. However, Dr. Lim fails to establish that such a breach actually caused Plaintiff’s injuries to a reasonable degree of medical probability. Although Dr. Lim states that the use of a massage gun caused Plaintiff to experience pain, Dr. Lim nowhere addresses Dr. Miller’s opinion that the cause of Plaintiff’s pain was postoperative peripheral inflammation that developed before Plaintiff visited UTN. Moreover, even assuming that the massage gun did cause Plaintiff to feel increased pain in the moment, Dr. Lim fails to connect the use of the massage gun to the broader injuries and damages alleged in the complaint. (See Compl. ¶ 39 [alleging that Plaintiff experienced the following injuries due to the use of the massage gun: physical pain and discomfort, use of cortisone injection, attending numerous doctor appointments, missing hours and days of work, incurring medical bills and costs, and emotion distress].)[FN2] His conclusory opinion that “Plaintiff’s injuries as alleged in her Complaint . . . were caused by” the use of the massage gun is insufficient to establish a causal connection between UTN’s actions and the injuries alleged in the complaint. Because Dr. Miller’s opinion remains unchallenged, Plaintiff has failed to establish the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to causation.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS UTN’s motion for summary judgment.     


[FN1] The Court grants UTN’s request to take judicial notice of the dictionary definition of “diagnosis,” pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h). As to UTN’s objection to Dr. Lim as an expert on medical causation, the Court acknowledges that physical therapists are not licensed physicians who may render medical diagnoses. (Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code section 2052(a).) Even so, the Court need not decide whether Dr. Lim is qualified to opine as to medical causation, because, as explained in more detail below, his proffered opinion fails to establish that the massage gun (as opposed to Plaintiff’s underlying medical condition) caused Plaintiff’s injuries. UTN’s evidentiary objections are otherwise overruled.

[FN2] Dr. Lim’s opinion that the use of the massage gun led Plaintiff to experience pain is not in conflict with Dr. Miller’s opinion. Dr. Miller acknowledges that the use of the massage gun may have been painful but posits that the reason for the pain was “the postoperative induced peripheral inflammatory changes in the knee and developing neuropathic pain condition.” (MSJ, Ex. 3, ¶ 41.)

Case Number: 24PSCV00336    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: H

140 North Orange Investors, LLC v. Baldwin Sun, Inc., Case No. 24PSCV00336

 

Counsel for Defendant Baldwin Sun, Inc.’s (i.e., David D. Fu) Motion to be

Relieved as Counsel is DENIED. 

 

Background 

This is an unlawful detainer action involving the commercial premises located at 140 N. Orange Avenue in the City of Industry. 

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiff 140 North Orange Investors, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint for Unlawful Detainer against Baldwin Sun, Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10. 

On May 13, 2024, “Judgment Pursuant to Stipulation for Judgment for Possession of Real Property” was entered. 

A Case Management Conference and an Order to Show Cause Re: Why Sanctions Should Not Be Imposed for Defense Counsel’s Failure to Appear on September 11, 2024 are set for October 28, 2024. Pending before the Court is David D. Fu’s motion to be relieved as counsel of record for Defendant.

 

Discussion

David D. Fu seeks to be relieved as counsel of record for Defendant Baldwin Sun, Inc. (the “Client”).

The motion was filed on September 20, 2024 (electronically served September 18, 2024) and originally set for hearing on October 17, 2024; the Client is not listed on the accompanying proof of service. Per California Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(c), “[p]roof of service of the moving papers must be filed no later than five court days before the time appointed for the hearing.” To date, moving counsel has not filed a proof of service reflecting that the Client was served with the motion.

On September 25, 2024, the Court issued a “Notice Re: Continuance of Hearing and Order,” wherein the October 17, 2024 scheduled hearing was continued to October 28, 2024; notice was given to Plaintiff’s counsel only. There is no indication on the docket that Plaintiff provided notice of the new October 28, 2024 hearing date or that moving counsel provided notice of the new date to the Client.

Accordingly, the motion is summarily denied, based on these notice deficiencies. 

Case Number: 24PSCV00753    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: H

Guerrero, et al. v. Gonzalez, Case No. 24PSCV00753

ORDER ON DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION 

Plaintiffs Alex Guerrero’s and Sanjuana Renteria’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

Background

Plaintiffs Alex Guerrero and Sanjuana Renteria (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege that they sustained injuries and damages in a November 22, 2022 motor vehicle accident. On March 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting a negligence cause of action against Stephanie Gonzalez (“Defendant”) and Does 1-25.

On August 16, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Defendant.

A Case Management Conference is set for October 28, 2024. Pending before the Court is Plaintiffs’ Application for Default Judgment.

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted: 

1.       In personal injury and wrongful death actions, the complaint must not state the amount of damages sought. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10.) Before a default may be entered, Plaintiffs must serve Defendant with a statement of “the nature and amount of damages being sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11.) The proof of service filed with the Court on June 28, 2024 reflects that Defendant was, in fact, served with two Statements of Damages; however, the Court has not been provided with copies of the Statements of Damages or with any information as to the amounts listed therein. It is unclear to the Court whether or not Defendant has been provided “actual notice of the liability to which he or she may be subjected.” (Schwab v. Rondel Homes, Inc. (1991) 53 Cal.3d 428, 435.)

2.       The Court requests that Plaintiffs attach a copy of the traffic collision report to any future default prove-up application, assuming one exists.

Accordingly, the Court denies Plaintiffs’ application without prejudice and directs Plaintiffs to submit a revised default judgment application addressing the aforementioned issues.

Case Number: 24PSCV01994    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: H

Rosales v. OC Auto Exchange, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01994

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

Defendant OC Auto Exchange dba LA Auto Exchange’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The Court orders the case stayed, pending completion of the arbitration.


Background

Plaintiff Randy Rosales (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

Plaintiff purchased a 2016 Cadillac ATS, VIN No. 1G6AA5RX4G0105443 (“subject vehicle”) from OC Auto Exchange dba LA Auto (sued as OC Auto Exchange dba LA Auto Exchange West Covina) (“OCX”). OCX’s salesperson told Plaintiff that the subject vehicle had not been in any prior accidents and did not have any structural damage. On February 25, 2024, Plaintiff took the subject vehicle to another dealership to trade it in and learned that the subject vehicle had, in fact, been in a prior accident and sustained structural damage.

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against OCX, Great American Insurance Company and Does 1-50 for:

1.       Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act,

2.       Fraud—Misrepresentation,

3.       Fraud—Negligent Misrepresentation,

4.       Fraud—Nondisclosure, and

5.       Claim Against Bond.

 

Pending before the Court is OCX’s motion to compel arbitration. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted.

 

Legal Standard

“[A]rbitration is generally favored under both the FAA and California law.” (Higgins v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1247; see also Engalla v. Permanente Med. Grp., Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-972 [“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability and a requirement that an arbitration agreement must be enforced on the basis of state law standards that apply to contracts in general”].) “[U]nder both the FAA and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.” (Higgins, supra, 40 Cal.App.4th at 1247 [quotation marks and citation omitted].)

Generally, on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must grant the motion unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.)

The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence. (Hotels Nevada v. L.A. Pacific Center, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 754, 761.) The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of the evidence a defense to enforcement (e.g., fraud, unconscionability, etc.) (Id.) “In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Engalla, supra, 15 Cal.4th at 972.)

 

Discussion

OCX moves the Court for an order compelling Plaintiff to arbitrate this action before the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”) and to stay this action pending completion of arbitration.

OCX’s authorized agent and Custodian of Records Masam Aslanpour (“Aslanpour”) represents, and/or the attached exhibits reflect, as follows:

On or about September 18, 2022, Plaintiff and OCX entered into a “Retail Installment Sale Contract—Simple Finance Charge (with Arbitration Provision)” (the “Contract”) concerning Plaintiff’s purchase of the subject vehicle. (Aslanpour Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1A [FN]). Although OCX does not have a complete copy of the Contract as it pertains to Plaintiff, the Contract used in OCX’s transaction with Plaintiff is the same retail installment sale contract that OCX used for its other transactions at the time of the transaction—i.e., “Form No. 553-CA-ARB (REV. 7/16),” which is listed at the bottom of the second page. (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. 1B.) “Form No. 553-CA-ARB (REV. 7/16)” contains an arbitration provision which states, in relevant part, as follows: “Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral binding arbitration. . . You may choose the American Arbitration Association 1633 Broadway, 10th Floor, New York New York, 10019 (www.adr.org), or any other organization to conduct the arbitration subject to our approval . . .” (Id.) 

Plaintiff, in opposition, only provides a conclusory statement that he “does not recall signing an arbitration agreement in this instance.” (Opp., 2:18.) This statement, however, is not supported by a declaration. The Court determines that OCX has proven by a preponderance of the evidence that a valid arbitration agreement exists. 

Plaintiff next claims that he is entitled to elect his choice of JAMS as the arbitrator. Not so. The above language expressly states that Plaintiff is permitted to choose a non-AAA arbitrator subject to OCX’s approval, which has not been given. In any event, “[w]here the parties ‘clearly and unmistakably’ delegate issues of arbitrability to the arbitrator, the arbitrator, not the court, decides such issues as the scope of the arbitration agreement.” (Chin v. Advanced Fresh Concepts Franchise Corp. (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 704, 709.) 

OCX’s motion is granted. The Court stays the instant case, pending completion of the arbitration.

 

[FN] It appears to the Court that what Aslanpour refers to as “Exhibit 1A” is actually “Exhibit 1B” and vice versa. The Court has referenced the correct exhibit.
