DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV32814    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

LISA POPOVICH, an individual, and JOHN PORTER, an individual

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

22STCV32814

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES, a municipal corporation; CITY OF LOS ANGELES DEPARTMENT OF TRANSPORTATION, a municipal agency; MV TRANSPORTATION, INC., a California corporation; HERC RENTALS, INC., a Delaware corporation; and DOES 1 to 50, inclusive

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             October 11, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          Defendant, Cross-Complainant, and Cross-Defendant Herc Rentals Inc.

 

Responding Party:      Defendant, Cross-Complainant and Cross-Defendant MV Transportation, Inc.

 

HEARING: Motion to Compel Further Responses to Herc’s First Set of Special Interrogatories

 

            The Court considered the moving papers. 

RULING 

The Court grants the Motion to Compel special Interrogatories Nos. 12, 13, 29, and 30. Sanctions in the amount of $1,110 are imposed.  Discovery and sanctions  are due by November 12, 2024.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On October 6, 2022, Plaintiffs Lisa Popovich and John Porter filed the complaint against Defendants City of Los Angeles, City of Los Angeles Department of Transportation, MV Transportation INC. “MV,” and HERC rentals “HERC,” alleging causes of action for negligence and loss of consortium arising from a bus crash.

 

            On August 10, 2023, Herc served its first set of special interrogatories on MV.

 

On October 24, 2024, MV served deficient responses.

 

            On December 5, 2024, during a telephonic meet and confer, MV agreed to provide further responses to all of the interrogatories raised in a meet and confer letter sent on November 22, 2023.

 

            Between December and March 2024, Herc inquired multiple times about the promised further responses.

 

            The further responses have yet to be served.

 

            On April 2, 2024, Herc filed the instant motion

           

            On August 22, 2024, the Court continued the motion to October 11, 2024.

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

            LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court compel Defendant to further respond to Special Interrogatories 12,13, 29, and 30.

 

  1. Meet and Confer

 

The Court finds that Herc satisfied the meet and confer requirements as evidenced by Herc’s counsel’s declaration, wherein counsel states that counsel reached out to MV multiple times regarding the Special Interrogatories, Set One, and received no response. (Davis decl. ¶ 9-15.)

 

  1. Special Interrogatories

 

Herc argues that it is entitled to obtain discovery from MV Transportation under California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2017.010, which permits parties to seek non-privileged information relevant to the subject matter of a case, especially if it could reasonably assist in preparing for trial, evaluating a case, or facilitating settlement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

 

This case stems from a June 27, 2022, incident involving a LADOT bus operated by MV employee Derry Fayrel Green, Jr., which collided with a stationary Herc semi-truck. The collision resulted in multiple injuries, including to the plaintiff, Lisa Popovich. Herc, after being sued, filed a cross-complaint against MV for indemnity and negligence and sought discovery to support its claims.

 

Herc's Special Interrogatories seek pertinent information, including details about MV's relationship with the City of Los Angeles, the qualifications and training of MV's bus drivers, and information specific to the actions of bus driver Green leading up to the collision. Special Interrogatory Nos. 12 and 13 asked MV to state why it believes its bus driver did not take defensive measures to avoid and did not attempt to slow down or stop prior to colliding with the Herc Truck. Special Interrogatory Nos. 29 and 30 asked MV to identify all communications between MV and the LA Times and 2310 Imperial LLC, entities that owned and/or operated the property adjacent to where the subject incident occurred.

 

Herc contends that these discovery requests are relevant and narrowly tailored to its claims, particularly to address questions about whether the bus driver fell asleep and if the Herc truck was directed to park at the accident site. Despite agreeing to provide further responses during the meet and confer process, MV continued to deliver incomplete and evasive answers. Herc asks the court to order MV to promptly provide full and complete responses to its Special Interrogatories.

 

MV objected to Special interrogatories Nos. 12, 13, 29, and 30, arguing that MV is not a party and that the interrogatories were premature. However, in response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Here, MV has provided no opposition to this motion justifying their objections.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Special Interrogatories Nos. 12, 13, 29, and 30.

 

Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).) If a motion to compel a party to respond to discovery is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc § 2030.290 subd. (c), 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The monetary sanction includes the moving party’s reasonable expenses, such as attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

 

Herc argues that MV's failure to provide substantive responses to discovery requests for over seven months constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. Despite numerous attempts by Herc, through calls and emails, to resolve the matter, MV's counsel failed to deliver proper responses and eventually suggested that Herc file a motion to compel. Herc’s Counsel states that Herc incurred $1560 in connection with this motion, broken down as follows: 3 hours preparing the motion at $300 an hour, the motion fee of $60, and 2 hours reviewing the opposition, writing the reply, and appearing at the motion at $300 an hour. MV has not opposed the motion, and therefore, the Court lowers the amount of time reviewing the motion, writing the reply and appearing at the hearing to half an hour.

 

Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions of $1,110.

RULING

 

The Court grants the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production. Sanctions are imposed in the amount of $1110. Discovery and sanctions are due by November 12, 2024.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On October 6, 2022, Plaintiffs Lisa Popovich and John Porter filed the complaint against Defendants City of Los Angeles, City of Los Angeles Department of Transportation, MV Transportation INC. “MV,” and HERC rentals “HERC,” alleging causes of action for negligence and loss of consortium arising from a bus crash.

 

            On August 10, 2023, Herc served its first set of requests for production on MV.

 

On October 24, 2023, MV served deficient responses.

 

            On December 5, 2023, during a telephonic meet and confer, MV agreed to provide further responses to all of the requests raised in a meet and confer letter sent on November 22, 2023.

 

            Between December 2023 and March 2024, Herc inquired multiple times about the promised further responses.

 

            The further responses have yet to be served.

 

            On April 2, 2024, Herc filed the instant motion.

           

            On August 22, 2024, the Court continued the motion to October 11, 2024.

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

            LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.310.) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

The Court finds that Herc satisfied the meet and confer requirements as evidenced by Herc’s counsel’s declaration, wherein counsel states that counsel reached out to MV multiple times regarding the requests for production. (Davis decl. ¶ 9-15.)

 

B.    Request for Production

 

Herc requests further responses to Request for Production Nos. 1-2, 7-29, 33-43, 48-49, and 59-64.

 

RPDs 1-2: Documents regarding any relationship between MV and government entities

RPDs 7-11: Documents regarding contracts or agreements relating to the subject bus

RPDs 12: Documents regarding the provision of services for the subject bus

RPDs 13-27, 40, 59-64: Documents regarding the driver of the subject bus.

RPDs 28-29: Documents regarding MV’s policies following driver incidents

RPDs 33-39, 41-43: Documents regarding the incident

RPDs 48-49: Documents regarding the maintenance and repair of the subject bus

 

 

This case stems from a June 27, 2022, incident involving a LADOT bus operated by MV employee Derry Fayrel Green, Jr., which collided with a stationary Herc semi-truck. The collision resulted in multiple injuries, including to the plaintiff, Lisa Popovich. Herc, after being sued, filed a cross-complaint against MV for indemnity and negligence and sought discovery to support its claims.

 

Herc argues that under California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2017.010, discovery is permissible for any non-privileged information relevant to the subject matter of a case, especially if it could lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Herc sought documents through Requests for Production, asking for information about MV's relationship with the City of Los Angeles, MV's public transit system involvement, the qualifications and training of MV bus drivers, and details about the driver involved in the incident, Derry Fayrel Green, Jr. These documents are critical for Herc’s trial preparation.

 

Herc contends that MV’s responses to many requests (Nos. 1-2, 7-28, 33-34, 36, 38-43, 48-49, and 59-64) were unclear, as MV stated they had produced some documents but objected to others, arguing they were not relevant to admissible evidence. This left Herc unsure whether all relevant documents had been provided. Herc requested amended responses to clarify whether any documents were withheld.

 

Herc is also concerned with MV's response to certain RPDs, including No. 12, 29, 25, and 37, which MV objected to but agreed to provide further responses to during the meet and confer process.

 

Despite MV agreeing to provide additional responses, Herc argues that MV has repeatedly failed to provide complete answers, delaying the process. Herc urges the Court to order MV to supply full and substantive responses promptly, as the information is crucial for trial preparation.

 

            MV has not provided an opposition to the instant motion.

 

The Court finds that Herc’s Requests for Production are relevant to the subject matter of the instant case as all requests revolve around the incident, and Herc has shown good cause for the production of documents. (Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.310 subd. (a).) Furthermore, MV has not provided any justification for their objections as MV has not filed an opposition. (Fairmont Ins. Co., 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production.

 

Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).) If a motion to compel a party to respond to discovery is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc § 2030.290 subd. (c), 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The monetary sanction includes the moving party’s reasonable expenses, such as attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

 

Herc argues that MV's failure to provide substantive responses to discovery requests for over seven months constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. Despite numerous attempts by Herc, through calls and emails, to resolve the matter, MV's counsel failed to deliver proper responses and eventually suggested that Herc file a motion to compel. Herc’s Counsel states that Herc incurred $2310 in connection with this motion, broken down as follows: 5.5 hours preparing the motion at 300$ an hour, the motion fee of 60$, and 2 hours reviewing the opposition, writing the reply, and appearing at the motion at 300$ an hour. MV has not opposed the motion, and therefore, the Court lowers the amount of time reviewing the motion, writing the reply and appearing at the hearing to half an hour. Furthermore, given the similarities between the two motions to be heard on October 11, 2024, the Court lowers the amount of time spent on this motion to 3 hours.

 

Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions of $1,110.
