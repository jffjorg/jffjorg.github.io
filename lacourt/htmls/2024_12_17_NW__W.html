DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00202    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: W

CALDERON, ET. AL. V. 14600 SATICOY STREET, LLC, ET AL.

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

Date of Hearing:         December 17, 2024                            Trial Date:       January 27, 2025 

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         22VECV00202

Complaint Filed:          February 8, 2022

FAC Filed:                    January 5, 2024

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiffs Claudia Calderon and Carlos Araujo

Responding Party:       Defendants 14600 Saticoy Street, LLC and Anza Management Company

Notice:                         Proper

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs Claudia Calderon and Carlos Arajo (Plaintiffs”) allege they entered into a month-to-month tenancy with Defendant 14600 Saticoy Street LLC, through its agent Defendant Anza Management Company (“Defendants”). Plaintiffs further allege during their tenancy at the Subject Property, and Defendants’ ownership, management and/or operations of the Subject Property, the building is and has been unsafe, untenantable, in a serious state of disrepair, and in gross violation of building, health, and safety codes.

 

On February 8, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants, alleging: (1) Contractual and Tortious Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability; (2) Violation of Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1941, 1941.1 (Statutory Breach of The Implied Warranty of Habitability); (3) Contractual and Tortious Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (4) Nuisance; (5) Negligence; (6) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq. Unfair, Unlawful, and Deceptive Business Practices; (7) Violation of Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30 (Financial Abuse of Elders and Disabled Persons); and (8) Negligent Hiring, Training and Supervision.

 

On January 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint adding new causes of action.

 

On September 20, 2024, the Court continued the trial date from October 7, 2024 to January 27, 2025. Pre-trial deadlines were not continued and remained based on the previous trial date. (Minute Order 9/20/24.)

 

On November 6, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion to compel the deposition of Anza Management Company’s (“Anza”) Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2055.010 and 2025.450, on the grounds there is good cause to compel compliance. Plaintiffs request $2,810.00 in monetary sanctions.

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition, arguing that the motion is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020. Defendants request $2,337.50 in sanctions.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

1.      Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel the Deposition of Anza Management Company’s Person Most Knowledgeable is DENIED.

2.      Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

3.      Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.      

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

 

“A motion under subdivision (a) [above] shall comply with both of the following:

1. The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

2. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b).)

 

If a motion is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of that party unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 (g).)

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

The Declaration of Johnathan Nielsen, Plaintiffs’ counsel, describes a meet and confer effort that took place to determine if Defendants would produce the PMK for a deposition. (Nielsen Decl. ¶ 4, 6; Exhibits 3, 5.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Plaintiffs seek an order compelling the appearance of Defendant Anza Management Company (“Anza”) Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) for a deposition. The motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Sections 2055.010 and 2025.450, on the grounds there is good cause to compel compliance. Plaintiffs argue that they timely provided required notice of the deposition and Anza’s counsel refused to produce the PMK for deposition.

 

In support, Plaintiffs provide the declaration of counsel Johnathan Nielsen. Attorney Neilsen declares that Plaintiffs properly noticed the deposition by email on August 16, 2024, prior to the discovery cutoff on September 9, 2024. (Nielsen Decl. ¶ 2, Exhibit 1.) However, as a courtesy, Plaintiffs agreed to not go forward with the deposition if available dates were provided. Defendants objected to the deposition on August 30, 2024. (Nielsen Decl. ¶ 2, 4, Exhibits 1, 3.) On September 3, 2024, Defendants’ counsel replied that “alternative dates will be provided.” (Nielsen Decl. ¶ 5, Exhibit 4.) Nielson responded:

“Due to discovery cut-off, I need confirmation that you are waiving the discovery cut-off date for the purpose of taking the depositions we noticed. If I don't have alternative dates and confirmation of waiving discovery cut-off by 10:00 a.m. on Thursday September 5th, I will have to go forward with the deposition on September 6th to protect our clients' rights.”

(Nielsen Decl. ¶ 6, Exhibit 5.)

Defendants’ counsel, Laura Adler, replied to the above email that the deposition would not proceed on September 6th and requested a 90-120 day trial continuance because attorney Ronald Whitaker was no longer with the company and they were unable to proceed as staffed. (Nielsen Decl. ¶ 7, Exhibit 6.) Plaintiffs do not indicate that they responded to the email.

In opposition, Defendants argue that the instant motion is untimely because the deadline to complete depositions of percipient witnesses was September 9, 2024, and the corresponding deadline to hear a motion to compel was September 23, 2024. Defendants claim that there was no agreement to extend the discovery cutoff dates. (Opposition at p. 5.) Defendants assert that although the Court continued the trial from October 7, 2024 to January 27, 2025, the discovery deadlines were not continued. (Minute Order 9/20/24.) Defendants also point out that Plaintiffs did not raise this issue during the hearing on September 20, 2024, and that Plaintiffs stated in their opposition that “Discovery and Expert Discovery are cut-off”. (Page 3, line 17 of Plaintiffs’ Opposition dated September 19, 2024).

 

Defendants further argue that Plaintiffs motion to compel is improper because discovery has closed and Plaintiffs have made no effort to reopen discovery before filing the instant motion, citing Pelton-Shepherd Indus., Inc. v. Delta Packaging Prod., Inc., (2008) 165 Cal. App. 4th 1568, 1585. The Court agrees.

 

Any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action. (Code Civ. Proc.§ 2024.020 (a).) Here, the discovery motion cut-off in this case is closed. The discovery cut-offs are associated with the previous trial date, October 7, 2024. (Minute Order 9/20/24.) Therefore, the last day to hear a discovery motion was September 23, 2024. Since the motions to compel depositions are being heard on December 17, 2024, they are untimely.

 

The Court cannot grant discovery motions after the cutoff date unless the moving party has moved under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 to have the motions heard closer to the trial date. (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1586-1587.) The Court’s discretion in allowing the motions to be heard closer to the trial date than allowed by Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020 is bounded by the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050, which include the trial court’s consideration of the statutory factors. (Id. at p. 1586-1587.) Here, Plaintiffs have not sought leave under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 to reopen discovery, nor have they discussed the statutory factors of section 2024.050.

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel the deposition is denied.

 

Monetary Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request monetary sanctions against Defendant Anza and its Counsel in the sum of $2,810.00 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(g). However, the Court has denied Plaintiffs motion to compel, and therefore section 2025.450(g) does not apply. Plaintiffs also argue that Defendants have refused to produce their PMK or provide alternative dates for their PMK’s deposition, despite Plaintiffs’ Counsel’s numerous attempts to accommodate Anza and their PMK’s schedule.

 

Defendants request monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs and their Counsel in the sum of $2,337.50. Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ act of filing the motion to compel after the motion hearing deadline is an abuse of the discovery process.

 

This is a text-book example of lawyers failing to cooperate in the discovery process. Both sides are guilty, in different ways, for this behavior. As Plaintiffs point out, Defendants did not provide alternative dates for the deposition after indicating that alternative dates would be provided. Also, Plaintiffs’ counsel made numerous attempts to accommodate Defendants’ schedule as a curtesy. On the other hand, Plaintiffs do not address why they did not respond to Defense counsel Laura Adler’s email on September 3, 2024; why they did not bring up this issue during the hearing on September 20, 2024; why they stated in their opposition to the motion to continue the trial that discovery was closed; and why they waited until November 6th to bring the instant motion.

 

Both parties’ requests for sanctions are denied as both parties’ positions were substantially justified, in light of the other parties’ intransigence.

 


Case Number: 24VECV02724    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: W

SALDIVAR, ET. AL. v. TESLA, inc., et al.

 

Motion to compel arbitration and to stay proceedings

 

Date of Hearing:          December 17, 2024                            Trial Date:       None set

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         24VECV02724

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Tesla, Inc.

Responding Party:       None

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 12, 2023, Plaintiffs Jeannette Saldivar and Ryan Saldivar (“Plaintiffs”) leased their 2023 Tesla Model Y (the “Vehicle”) from Defendant Tesla, Inc. (“Defendant”), at which time Plaintiffs executed a lease agreement (the “Lease Agreement”).

 

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging that Defendant is liable for: (1) Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Breach of Implied Warranty; and (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2(b).

 

On July 18, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel arbitration on the grounds that the Lease Agreement requires the Plaintiffs to resolve any disputes concerning the automobile by arbitration.

 

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a notice of non-opposition to Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Proceedings is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

With the moving papers, Defendant submitted a request for judicial notice of the complaint. The request is granted. (Evid. Code, § 452(d).)

 

Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

CCP § 1281.2 permits a party to file a petition to request that the court order the parties to arbitrate a controversy.  The trial court first determines whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties and then whether the plaintiff’s claims are covered by the agreement.  (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)  “California has a strong public policy in favor of arbitration and any doubts regarding the arbitrability of a dispute are resolved in favor of arbitration.”  (Coast Plaza Doctors Hosp. v. Blue Cross of Cal. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.)

 

The party seeking to enforce the arbitration agreement bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.  (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.)  The party opposing the petition to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  (Id.)

 

i.                    Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

Here, Defendant argues that a valid arbitration agreement exists between the parties that requires arbitration of Plaintiff's claims. In support, Defendants have submitted the Lease Agreement, which requires the Plaintiffs to resolve any disputes concerning the automobile by arbitration. Page 3 of the Lease Agreement provides:

 

Agreement to Arbitrate. Please carefully read this provision, which applies to any dispute between you and Tesla, Inc. and its affiliates, (together “Tesla”).

 

If you have a concern or dispute, please send a written notice describing it and your desired resolution to resolutions@tesla.com.

 

If not resolved within 60 days, you agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association (AAA) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. This includes claims arising before this Agreement, such as claims related to statements about our products. You further agree that any disputes related to the arbitrability of your claims will be decided by the court rather than an arbitrator, notwithstanding AAA rules to the contrary.

 

To initiate the arbitration, you will pay the filing fee directly to AAA and we will pay all subsequent AAA fees for the arbitration, except you are responsible for your own attorney, expert, and other witness fees and costs unless otherwise provided by law. If you prevail on any claim, we will reimburse you your filing fee. The arbitration will be held in the city or county of your residence. To learn more about the Rules and how to begin an arbitration, you may call any AAA office or go to www.adr.org.

 

The arbitrator may only resolve disputes between you and Tesla, and may not consolidate claims without the consent of all parties. The arbitrator cannot hear class or representative claims or requests for relief on behalf of others purchasing or leasing Tesla vehicles. In other words, you and Tesla may bring claims against the other only in your or its individual capacity and not as a plaintiff or class member in any class or representative action. If a court or arbitrator decides that any part of this agreement to arbitrate cannot be enforced as to a particular claim for relief or remedy, then that claim or remedy (and only that claim or remedy) must be brought in court and any other claims must be arbitrated.

 

If you prefer, you may instead take an individual dispute to small claims court.

 

You may opt out of arbitration within 30 days after signing this Agreement by sending a letter to: Tesla, Inc.; P.O. Box 15430; Fremont, CA 94539-7970, stating your name, Order Number or Vehicle Identification Number, and intent to opt out of the arbitration provision. If you do not opt out, this agreement to arbitrate overrides any different arbitration agreement between us, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract.

 

(Lease Agreement, Ex. 1 to Kim Decl., page 3.)

 

Defendant has met its burden to show that an arbitration agreement exists. The Lease Agreement requires arbitration of “any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you [Plaintiff] and Tesla.” (Ex. 1 to Kim Decl.) Plaintiff signed the Lease Agreement. (Id.) The Court finds that Defendant has met its burden of proving that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties.

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiffs to establish any facts necessary to show that the agreement should not be enforced. Plaintiffs do not oppose the motion and filed a notice of non-opposition on December 4, 2024. Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiffs did not meet the burden to show that the arbitration agreement should not be enforced.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is granted.

 

Stay of Proceedings

 

Code Civ. Proc. section 1281.4 provides that if the court has ordered the arbitration of a controversy, it “shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.”  Pursuant to Section 1281.4, therefore, the Court stays this action pending conclusion of the arbitration proceedings.    

 

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and to stay proceedings is granted. The Court stays this action pending conclusion of arbitration.
