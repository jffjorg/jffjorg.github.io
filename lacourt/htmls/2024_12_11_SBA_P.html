DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02523    Hearing Date: December 11, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

 

Moving Party: Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.           

Responding Party: Plaintiff Bret Lieberman

 

RULING

 

Having considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply, the motion to compel binding arbitration and stay proceedings is GRANTED.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On August 3, 2023, Plaintiff Bret Lieberman (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”) and DOES 1-10, inclusive alleging five causes of action for violations of Statutory Obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Civ. Code sections 1790-1795.8.

 

On September 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. On November 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. Defendant filed a reply on December 4, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On January 19, 2022, Plaintiff purchased a Tesla Model 3 (the “Vehicle”) manufactured by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

 

Plaintiff received an express written warranty which stated that if a defect developed during the warranty period, Plaintiff could deliver the Vehicle to a repair shop to make repairs. (Complaint, ¶ 9.)

 

During the warranty period, several defects developed. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) However, Defendant failed to repair the Vehicle after several attempts. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) California law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements. (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)       

 

The Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies to any contract evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce which contains an arbitration clause. (Wolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) Section 2 of the FAA provides that arbitration provisions shall be enforced, save upon grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Ibid.) A state court may refuse to enforce an arbitration clause on the basis of generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Ibid.) A state court, however, may not defeat an arbitration clause by applying state laws applicable only to arbitration provisions. (Ibid.)  

 

For the FAA to apply, a contract must involve interstate commerce. (Ibid.) When it applies, the FAA preempts any state law rule that stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the FAA’s objectives. (Carbajal v. CWPSC, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 227, 238.) A party asserting FAA preemption bears the burden to present evidence establishing a contract with the arbitration provision affects interstate commerce, and the failure to do so renders the FAA inapplicable. (Ibid.) Evidence must be presented, in the form of declarations or other evidence, that establishes that the contract affects interstate commerce.  (Ibid.) “[S]ince arbitration is a matter of contract, the FAA also applies if it is so stated in the agreement.” (Davis v. Shiekh Shoes, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 956, 963.)          

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) Thus, in assessing a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the [motion] bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284, citation omitted.)        

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of the complaint filed by Plaintiff. The court may take notice of its own file, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (d).

 

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs consented to the terms of an online Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”), which included an arbitration provision. Both the FAA and California law require Plaintiffs to arbitrate this dispute.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

The arbitration agreement is procedurally unconscionable and unenforceable. Further, the terms of the MVOA were superseded by other written agreements.

 

Reply Arguments

 

Plaintiff ignores the language of the MVOA, which states that any other arbitration agreements are overridden by the MVOA. Plaintiff also cites to cases which are distinguishable from the instant case.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

In support of the motion, Defendant submits the Declaration of Raymond Kim (“Kim”). Kim declares that when Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle, they signed a Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”), thereby agreeing to be bound by its terms, which include an arbitration provision. (Kim Decl., ¶ 3.) Prior to placing the order, Plaintiff had the ability to click on a hyperlink to the MVOA’s terms and conditions, which would have opened in a new window. (Kim Decl., ¶ 4.) Further, once executed, the MVOA would be visible on the customer’s mytesla.com accounts. (Kim Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Courts have enforced similar “clickwrap” agreements. “Clickwrap” is an agreement “in which an internet user accepts a website’s terms of use by clicking an ‘I agree’ or ‘I accept’ button, with a link to the agreement readily available.” (Sellers v. JustAnswer LLC (2021) 73 Cal. App. 5th 444, 463 (internal quotation marks omitted). “[C]ourts have routinely found clickwrap agreements enforceable.” (Berman v. Freedom Fin. Network, LLC (9th Cir. 2022) 30 F.4th 849, 856.)   

As with any other contract, however, “clickwrap” agreements must be supported by mutual assent.  (Sellers, 73 Cal. App. 5th at 461.) “[I]n order to establish mutual assent for the valid formation of an internet contract, a provider must first establish the contractual terms were presented to the consumer in a manner that made it apparent the consumer was assenting to those very terms when checking a box or clicking on a button.”  (Id.)

 

The terms of the MVOA indicate that the parties agree to arbitrate any disputes relating to any aspect of the parties’ relationship. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.) The arbitration provision also provides an opt-out option and states that if it is not exercised, the MVOA’s arbitration provision becomes the controlling agreement between the parties, overriding any other arbitration agreements. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff did not exercise his right to opt-out. (Kim Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

The court finds that with the MVOA, Defendant has met its burden of proving that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties. Further, the language of the provision is broad enough that it covers the dispute at hand. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish any facts necessary to show that the agreement should not be enforced.

 

B.  Grounds for Compelling Arbitration

 

1.  Does the Sales Contract Supersede the MVOA

 

Plaintiff argues that the Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) supersedes the MVOA because it was signed after the MVOA, and the MVOA contradicts the express written agreement between the parties. Defendant argues in opposition that the terms of the MVOA’s arbitration provision expressly state that it overrides any other arbitration agreements, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

The court agrees with Defendant that the terms of the MVOA are clear that the arbitration terms within that document control.  Thus, the relevant arbitration agreement is contained within the MVOA.

 

2.  Unconscionability

 

“Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element, the former focusing on ‘oppression’ or ‘surprise’ due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on ‘overly harsh’ or ‘one-sided’ results. The prevailing view is that procedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability. But they need not be present in the same degree. The more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” ((Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) “Ordinary contracts of adhesion, although they are indispensable facts of modern life that are generally enforced, contain a degree of procedural unconscionability.” (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1244.) 

 

Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it is an adhesion contract. Moreover, Plaintiff lacked any substantial bargaining power because they were forced to simply click through various prompts related to the purchase of the Vehicle.

 

The arbitration agreement at hand does contain an opt-out option, which alone would not insulate it from procedural unconscionability. (Gentry v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 443, 470 (arbitration agreement had a degree of procedural unconscionability even though there was a 30 day opt out provision).) However, any degree of procedural unconscionability would be minimal, as the MVOA is only five pages long, and the arbitration provision is clearly demarcated and distinct from the rest of the terms. (Kim Decl., Exh. 1, p. 3.) Further, there are no facts to indicate that there was a great disparity in bargaining power. In general, customers are free to negotiate with car dealers, even if those dealers use form contracts. (Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 931.) Customers also have the option of purchasing a vehicle with comparable or superior terms from another dealer, and thus, “no presumption is warranted that Plaintiff had no choice or power…” (Ibid.) Here, Plaintiff was not forced to purchase a car from Defendant. Plaintiff had the option of purchasing a comparable car from another dealer without an arbitration agreement.

 

Even if Plaintiff could establish that the MVOA was procedurally unconscionable, the MVOA is not so one-sided that it would be substantively unconscionable to enforce it. The MVOA provides that both parties have the right to compel arbitration. Further, arbitration is to be conducted under the AAA’s rules, which allow for the selection of a neutral arbiter. Defendant also agreed to pay arbitration fees for both parties. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

Accordingly, the court finds slight procedural unconscionability but no substantive unconscionability.

 

3.  Express Warranties

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant cannot enforce the arbitration provision as their claims are distinct from the MVOA. Plaintiff cites several cases that Defendant notes are not instructive, as those cases concern the ability of a manufacturer to enforce an arbitration agreement between purchaser and dealer. Defendant sells vehicles directly to customers, and any agreements signed are directly between Defendant and the buyer.

 

Thus, the cases cited by Plaintiff are not persuasive.

 

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration.

 

B.  Stay of Proceedings

 

Code Civ. Proc. section 1281.4 provides that if the court has ordered the arbitration of a controversy, it “shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.”  Pursuant to Section 1281.4, therefore, the Court stays this action pending conclusion of the arbitration proceedings.    

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED. The court STAYS this action pending conclusion of the arbitration process.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 24TRCV02385    Hearing Date: December 11, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint

 

Moving Parties: Defendants Marriott International, Inc.; Aimbridge Hospitality, LLC; and New  Wynn Li, LP           

Responding Party: None

 

RULING

 

Having considered the moving papers, the court rules as follows: the demurrer is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part. The motion to strike is GRANTED.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On July 17, 2024, Plaintiffs Kathleen Foster (“Kathleen”); Layla Foster (“Layla”); Luca Foster (“Luca”); and Tessa Foster ("Tessa”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Marriot International, Inc. (“Marriott”); LAX Hotel Ventures, LLC (“Ventures”); New Wynn LI, LP (“Wynn”); Uptown LAX LLC (“Uptown”); Perseverance LAX LLC d/b/a Four Points by Sheraton Los Angeles International Airport (“Sheraton”); and Dornell Williams (“Williams”) alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Hiring, Training, and Supervision; (3) Negligent Failure to Warn; (4) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (“NIED”); and (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”).

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) against the same defendants plus Aimbridge Hospitality (“Aimbridge”) and DOES 1-50, inclusive, alleging the same causes of action.

 

On October 31, 2024, Defendants Marriott; Wynn; and Aimbridge (collectively, “Moving Defendants”) filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike.

 

As of December 5, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

On July 18, 2022, Plaintiffs checked into Sheraton, a property owned by the Moving Defendants. (FAC, ¶¶ 13, 22.) Plaintiffs were placed into a room on the sixth floor, which had no other guests. (FAC, ¶ 24.) Plaintiffs tried to lock their hotel room, but the lock did not work. (FAC, ¶ 24.)

 

On July 19, 2022, Plaintiffs woke up and realized that several of their belongings were missing. (FAC, ¶ 24.) Plaintiffs attempted to call 911, but the phone cord in the room was missing. (FAC, ¶ 24.) Plaintiffs asked the front desk employee to call 911, but the employee refused and handed Kathleen a telephone. (FAC, ¶ 24.)

 

Prior to Plaintiffs’ stay, the Sheraton at least nine other similar robberies occurred on the premises within the preceding three months. (FAC, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants conspired with Williams to place Plaintiffs in an unsafe room, which allowed Williams to enter Plaintiffs’ room and steal their belongings. (FAC, ¶ 25.)

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Moving Defendants demur to the fourth and fifth causes of action because Plaintiffs have failed to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action for NIED or IIED. Further, both causes of action are fatally uncertain.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) Motions to strike have a similar meet and confer requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.)

 

Defense counsel declares that the parties conferred in good faith but were unable to reach a resolution. The court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

 

B.  Uncertainty

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616; see Code Civ. Proc. §430.10, subd. (e).) Moreover, “[a] special demurrer should be overruled where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643.)

 

Moving Defendants argue that the fourth and fifth causes of action are uncertain and rely on conclusory allegations. Moving Defendants also point to conflicting allegations that there were “9 similar robberies” and “countless” complaints of robberies. These robberies are not described in any detail.

 

While the court agrees that the allegations surrounding the other robberies lack details, the complaint, taken as a whole, is not so unintelligible that Moving Defendants are not apprised of the issues they are to meet.

 

Accordingly, the special demurrer for uncertainty is OVERRULED.

 

C.  Fourth Cause of Action for NIED

 

“The law of negligent infliction of emotional distress in California is typically analyzed by reference to two theories of recovery: the ‘bystander’ theory and the ‘direct victim’ theory. The negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence. The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply. Whether a defendant owes a duty of care is a question of law.” (Spates v. Dameron Hosp. Ass’n (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213, ellipses, quotation marks, brackets, and paragraph breaks omitted.)

 

A plaintiff may make a bystander NIED claim if he or she is present at the scene of the injury-producing event at the time it occurs, witnesses the physical injury of someone closely related to him or her, and suffers emotional distress beyond that which would be anticipated in a disinterested witness. (See id.; Thing v. La Chusa (1989) 48 Cal.3d 644, 666.)  To make a direct victim claim, the emotional distress suffered by the plaintiff must be a foreseeable consequence of the conduct directed at the plaintiff. (See Molien v. Kaiser Foundation Hospital (1980) 27 Cal.3d 916, 930.)

 

Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiffs do not meet the “direct victim” nor “bystander” categories of NIED. Plaintiffs were asleep when the robbery occurred and were unaware of the complained of conduct. Thus, they do not satisfy the “awareness” element of bystander NIED. Moving Defendants also argue that Plaintiffs cannot be direct victims because the California Supreme Court has only allowed “direct victims” to recover damages in three factual scenarios: (1) the negligent mishandling of a corpse; (2) the negligent misdiagnosis of a disease; and (3) the negligent breach of a duty arising out a preexisting relationship, none of which are present here. Further, the emotional distress damages that Plaintiffs seek in this cause of action are also sought in their other three negligence causes of action. On these grounds, the demurrer should be sustained.

 

The court agrees that Plaintiffs cannot recover under a bystander NIED theory, as Plaintiffs had no awareness of the injury-causing conduct. However, the court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged that they are direct victims. Plaintiffs allege that as innkeepers, Moving Defendants had a duty to keep the premises safe for their guests. Moving Defendants breached this duty by placing Plaintiffs in a room without a functioning lock or phone, which allowed a trespasser to enter Plaintiffs’ room and steal their belongings. Given these allegations, emotional distress of the hotel guests is a foreseeable consequence of conduct directed at plaintiffs.

 

At this stage of the proceedings, these allegations are sufficient to maintain an NIED cause of action.

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Moving Defendants’ demurrer.

 

C.  Fifth Cause of Action for IIED

 

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903 (internal quotations omitted).) “It is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” (Id.) 

 

“A defendant’s conduct is outrageous when it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Jackson v. Mayweather (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 234, 257 (internal citations and quotations omitted).) “‘On the spectrum of offensive conduct, outrageous conduct is that which is the most extremely offensive.’” (Id. (quoting Yurick v. Superior Court (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 1116, 1129).)  A determination of the outrageousness of the conduct requires “a case-by-case appraisal of conduct filtered through the prism of the appraiser’s values, sensitivity threshold, and standards of civility.” (Yurick, supra, 209 Cal.App.3d at 1128.) It is more “intuitive than analytical.” (Id.) The duration of the emotional distress is a factor to be considered in determining its severity. (Fletcher v. Western Nat’l Life Ins. Co., 10 Cal.App.3d 376, 398.) 

 

Moving Defendants demur to this cause of action as Plaintiffs only make conclusory allegations. In order to state a claim for IIED, a plaintiff must state with specificity the outrageous acts and plead specific facts that establish severe emotional distress. Further, a plaintiff must show that the defendant intended to cause harm. Here, Plaintiffs only make conclusory allegations that they experienced “serious mental and emotional trauma.”

 

The gravamen of Plaintiffs’ IIED claim against Moving Defendants is their alleged ratification of their employees’ acts and failure to remedy a known security risk. Plaintiffs allege that

Moving Defendants had notice of numerous crimes on their property. Thus, the failure to take extra security precautions was willful. However, the complaints cited by Plaintiffs provide an insufficient factual basis for imputing notice on Moving Defendants. Several of the reviews are from anonymous guests who stayed at the Sheraton years before Plaintiffs’ visit. The reviewer closest in time to Plaintiffs’ stay accuses the hotel staff of committing robbery because they erroneously charged his credit card. These reviews do not put Moving Defendants on notice that trespassers routinely accessed guests’ rooms to steal their belongings.

 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiffs have not adequately alleged that Moving Defendants acted intentionally or with reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress. At most, Plaintiffs’ claims sound of negligence.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

D.  Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. §436 subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436 subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.) 

 

Moving Defendants seek to strike Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages on the basis that Plaintiffs failed to adequately plead IIED or any other oppressive, malicious, or fraudulent conduct. Further, Moving Defendants are corporate entities and cannot be liable for the conduct of its employees without facts showing ratification or authorization.

 

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in Civ. Code § 3294. (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. at 725.) Oppression is “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).) 

 

Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1004 explained that “punitive damages sometimes may be assessed in unintentional tort actions.” Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-895 noted that “something more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or malice, or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.” Indeed, “punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210 (internal quotation omitted).) 

 

As discussed above, Plaintiffs fail to adequately allege that Moving Defendants acted intentionally or in conscious disregard of their safety. Further, Plaintiffs do not allege any oppressive or fraudulent conduct.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS the motion to strike punitive damages.

 

E.  Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

Though Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition, California courts liberally allow amendments at any stage of the proceedings. (See Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.; Klopstock v. Superior Court in and for City and County of San Francisco (1941) 17 Cal.2d 13, 19-20.)

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is OVERRULED in part and GRANTED in part. The motion to strike is GRANTED. The court GRANTS Plaintiffs leave to amend.

 

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.
