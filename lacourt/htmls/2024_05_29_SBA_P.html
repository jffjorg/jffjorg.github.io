DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV49253    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: P

Motion: Defendants’ Demurrer and Motion to Strike to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint [FAC]

RULING -

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. After review, the Court rules that Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety.

BACKGROUND

A. Factual History

This case stems from a hotel visit where Anthony Soriano (Plaintiff) alleges that he suffered numerous bedbug bites. On December 27, 2018, Plaintiff checked into the Hilton Los Angeles Airport located at 5711 W Century Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90045 (the Hotel). (First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) ¶¶ 4,16.) On December 28, 2018, Plaintiff woke up to an intense itching sensation and discovered he had been bitten by bedbugs all over his body. (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiff

informed the front desk, but Plaintiff alleges that nothing was done to remedy the situation. (FAC ¶ 18.) Plaintiff would later seek medical attention due to the constant itching, pain, and scarring. (FAC ¶¶ 19-21.) Additionally, Plaintiff alleges that he incurred various expenses including medical costs, the cost of the hotel room, and the replacement of his luggage and clothing. (FAC ¶ 21.)

B. Procedural History

Plaintiff filed suit on December 28, 2020. Plaintiff alleges that the parent companies of the Hilton Los Angeles Airport include Hilton Worldwide, Inc. and Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc., while the manager of Hilton Los Angeles Airport is Grant Coonley (collectively, Defendants). Plaintiff filed six causes of action aimed at all Defendants which include: (1) Battery; (2) Negligence; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (4) Fraudulent Concealment; (5) Private Nuisance; and (5) Public Nuisance.

On February 27, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ Demurrer and Motion to Strike in its with leave to amend.

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint alleging four causes of action which include: (1) battery; (2) negligence; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (4) fraudulent concealment.

On April 17, 2024, Defendants filed their Demurrer without a Motion to Strike.

The motion before the Court is Defendants’ Demurrer without a Motion to Strike to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. Defendants demur to the first, third, and fourth, causes of action. Plaintiff opposes the Demurrer and Defendants filed their reply.

DISCUSSION

A. Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a); see also CCP § 435.5 (imposing similar requirements for a motion to strike).)

Defendants present the Declaration of Parshant C. Mahbubani (Mahbubani Decl.) stating that on April 1, 2024, Defense counsel sent correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel addressing issues within the FAC. However, Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. Therefore, the requirements of CCP §§ 430.41(a) and 435.5 remain unsatisfied. However, per CCP § 430.41(a)(4), “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” Therefore, the Court turns its attention to the Demurrer.

B. Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

The question of plaintiff's ability to prove these allegations is of no concern at the pleadings stage. (See Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214. “It is not the ordinary function of a demurrer to test the truth of the plaintiff's allegations or the accuracy with which he describes the defendant's conduct.”)

C. Analysis

Defendants’ main argument upon demurrer is that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to support causes of action for battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED), and fraudulent concealment. As explained below, the Court agrees and sustains the Demurrer in its entirety.

1. First Cause of Action: Battery

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.)

The parties’ contentions on this cause of action surround the element of intent. Plaintiff argues in their opposition papers that “an awareness of a condition that is known to cause harm in such a manner, absent any effort to stop such contact from occurring, constitutes battery.” (Opp’n at p. 7.) Supporting this contention, Plaintiff cites to a 7th Circuit case, Mathias v. Accor Economy Lodging, Inc., (2003) 347 F.3d 672 (“Mathias”). Plaintiff’s reliance on dicta in a non-binding 7th Circuit case is misplaced. In Mathias, hotel guests recovered from a hotel chain after being bitten by bedbugs. In affirming the verdict, the 7th Circuit Court of Appeals noted that there was extensive evidence to show that the hotel was aware of the infestation: (1) an extermination service had discovered bedbugs in the motel and recommended to spray every room, (2) after receiving complaints a manager at the hotel recommended to the district manager that the hotel be closed and sprayed, and finally (3) the district manager who vetoed the manager’s suggestion was a management-level employee fully aware of the risk and failure to take effective steps to eliminate the condition. (Mathias, supra, at 674.) No similar facts are alleged in this Complaint.

Just as he did in response to Defendant’s first Demurrer to the complaint, Plaintiff points the Court to two third-party websites where customers of the hotel can leave complaints, tripadvisor.com and bedbugregistry.com. Plaintiff’s only argument is that “[a]n angry hotel guest

or poor online reviews can spark attention that can ultimately harm a hotel business’s reputation and bottom line. Defendants have an interest to maintain the reputation and integrity of their Subject Hotel and to continuously monitor guest posted reviews on various online platforms such as Expedia, TripAdvisor, and Google Reviews.” (Opp’n at p.6.) However, this is speculative and without foundation, and Plaintiff gives no context to these complaints, nor does he establish a link between the online reviews and the knowledge of the defendants, especially considering that comments can be made on these websites by customers and non-customers alike, regardless of their truth.

Moreover, the FAC alleges that “Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, inclusive, did the aforementioned acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with the body of Plaintiff, or with a reckless disregard of the probability of causing such offensive contact to Plaintiff” (FAC ¶ 46.)

The FAC further alleges “Defendants’, and DOES 2 through 20, inclusive, intentional, deliberate, and reckless actions . . . [is] clearly evidenced by Defendants’, and DOES 2 through 20, inclusive, (1) deliberate actions in failing to properly and thoroughly inspect and clean the room assigned to Plaintiff, and to ensure the room was free from infestations of bedbugs, and the like, prior to renting and assigning the room to Plaintiff; (2) intentional and deliberate failures to treat and eliminate infestation of bedbugs, and the like, occurring in the rooms of the Subject Hotel; (3) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice not to inspect for bedbugs and the spread of bedbug infestations at the Subject Hotel; (4) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice in requiring Plaintiff to be assigned and isolated in the room of the Subject Hotel despite having prior knowledge of the bedbug infestation from the Plaintiff; (5) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice not to properly train housekeeping staff to thoroughly clean and maintain the rooms in a sanitary and habitable condition; (6) willful disregard of knowledge of Cimex lectularius (bedbugs) infestation and other uninhabitable conditions in Plaintiff’s room; and (7) intentional, deliberate, and reckless choice in failing to give notice to Plaintiff of the presence of the said uninhabitable conditions occurring in Plaintiff’s room at the Subject Hotel.” (FAC ¶ 49.)

These allegations are insufficient to show intent and without the element of the intent to harm, a cause of action for battery must fall to demurrer. To sufficiently plead a cause of action for battery under these factual circumstances, plaintiff would need to show that Defendant’s agents knew that there was an infestation in Plaintiff’s room at the time he was given possession of it. Here, Plaintiff merely alleges that there was information available to Defendant, from some unknown point in time, to suggest that hotel guests complained about bedbugs in Defendant’s property. Therefore, the court finds that the FAC does not state facts to support plaintiff’s conclusion that defendants had knowledge of the bedbugs in the hotel room.

As Plaintiff provided no new factual allegations regarding Defendant’s knowledge of the bedbug infestation in the FAC, the Demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

2. Third Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or

extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.)

Plaintiff argues that the inaction of the Hotel which included failure to inspect the room, failure to eradicate the infestation, and failure to warn Plaintiff of the infestation constitutes the intent element of IIED. Plaintiff continues arguing, stating that Defendants ratified this conduct of the Hotel’s employees by not terminating such employees, not training employees regarding bedbug infestations, and turning a blind eye towards previous complaints. The FAC further alleges “[a]lthough Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, had prior knowledge of bedbug infestations in their hotel, Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, failed to eradicate such infestations, including an infestation in the room that Plaintiff was provided” and that “Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, knew that their hotel had a prior bedbug infestation. Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, deliberately and recklessly chose to turn a blind eye to this infestation and previous guest complaints” (FAC, p. 5, ¶25 & ¶27, respectively).

As above, Plaintiff provides no facts that anyone was aware of a bedbug infestation in Plaintiff’s room, at the time Plaintiff was given the right to possess it. Instead, Plaintiff asserts that knowledge should be inferred because there were online reviews which included complaints that there were bedbugs at the property. These conclusory allegations are insufficient to show intent, which is a basis to sustain the demurer. In addition, “it is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1002, citing Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.) No facts in the Complaint show that defendants’ was conduct aimed at Plaintiff nor done in Plaintiff’s presence.

With the absence of the intent element and no facts establishing Plaintiff as the target of the conduct, the Demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained. As Plaintiff provided no new facts alleging knowledge of the infestation in his FAC, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

3. Fourth Cause of Action: Fraudulent Concealment

“The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 189 Cal.Rptr.3d 31, at 36.)

Fraud must be specifically pleaded: “It is essential that the facts and circumstances which constitute the fraud should be set out clearly, concisely and with sufficient particularity to apprise the opposite party of what he is called upon to answer, and to enable the court to determine whether, on the facts pleaded, there is any foundation, prima facie at least, for the charge of fraud. Scafidi v. Western Loan and Bldg. Co. (1946) 72 Cal.App.2d 550, 553.

The FAC alleges ““[a]lthough Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, had prior knowledge of bedbug infestations in their hotel, Defendants, and DOES 2 through 20, failed to eradicate such infestations, including an infestation in the room that Plaintiff was provided” (FAC ¶ 25). The FAC further alleges that “Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, intended to deceive Plaintiff and take advantage of Plaintiff’s lack of knowledge of the infestation in order to turn a profit on a night’s stay at his hotel, and intended to deceive Plaintiff by concealing the fact of the Cimex lectularius infestation” (FAC ¶100).

Here, Plaintiff’s claim fails because, as noted above, there are no facts that support actual knowledge of a bedbug infestation. Without such knowledge, Plaintiff cannot assert the intent element of fraud.

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained. As there are no new facts within the FAC showing actual knowledge of an infestation in the property, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 22TRCV01513    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving papers. No oppositions or reply papers have been filed as of May 24, 2024.

RULING

Defendant Vortex’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff alleges it leased commercial premises to Defendants pursuant to a written lease agreement. Plaintiff served Defendants with a 3-day notice to perform covenants or quit on June 30, 2021. Plaintiff alleges Defendants maintained a cannabis business or activity in violation of Los Angeles County Code.

On December 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint for unlawful detainer. On March 14, 2024, the Court sustained demurrer to the complaint with 20 days leave to amend. On March 25, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint for unlawful detainer.

On April 15, 2024, Defendant Vortex filed a demurrer to the FAC. No opposition or reply has been filed as of May 24, 2024.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.) The court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) On demurrer, courts assume the truth of all well-pled factual allegations in the subject pleading, “shorn of any legal conclusions.” (Fuller v. First Franklin Financial Corp. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 955, 959.)

DISCUSSION

Defendant Vortex demurs to the FAC on grounds that the 3-day Notice to Perform Covenants or Quit was served with insufficient notice. Defendant Vortex argues that the lease agreement requires that all notices be served with five days’ notice, including a Notice to Perform or Quit pursuant to CCP §1161(3). Defendant argues the Plaintiff must make some affirmative showing that this defect in notice can be cured or face demurrer without leave to amend. Defendant argues Plaintiff will be unable to cure this defect and asks that demurrer be sustained without leave to amend.

Exhibit A to the FAC is the written lease agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant Vortex. (FAC, Ex. A.) Pursuant to ¶14 of the Lease Agreement, in the event of Vortex’s default, Plaintiff “may give Lessee notice of such default and if Lessee does not cure any such

default within 05 days, after the giving of such notice..., then Lessor may terminate this lease on not less than 05 days’ notice to Lessee.” (FAC, Ex. A, ¶14.)

The language of paragraph 14 is permissive, not mandatory. Plaintiff “may” provide a 5-day notice to cure or quit. Such language is insufficient to supersede the mandatory language of CCP §1161(3) or to require Plaintiff to provide Vortex with a 5-day notice to perform or quit. “In commercial leases the landlord and commercial tenant may lawfully agree to notice procedures that differ from those provided in the statutory provisions governing unlawful detainer. Thus, if the lease contains service requirements for the notice to quit at variance with the requirements in the unlawful detainer statutes, the lease provisions control.” (Culver Center Partners East #1, L.P. v. Baja Fresh Westlake Village, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 744, 750.) Paragraph 14 does not require Plaintiff to give Defendant a notice of a default, but, if he does, the parties agreed that Defendant had five days to cure a default. Because the lease provisions control and Plaintiff did not comply with those provisions, the three-day notice was invalid.

Defendant Vortex’s demurrer based on failure to state a cause of action for unlawful detainer is SUSTAINED.

A valid Notice to Quit is jurisdictional in an Unlawful Detainer case. Because the Notice in this action is deficient, there is no basis by which the complaint can be amended. Therefore, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

ORDER

Defendant Vortex’s Demurrer to the FAC is SUSTAINED. Defendant Vortex is ordered to give notice and to file a proposed judgment consistent with this ruling.

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production (Set One)

The court considered the moving papers. No oppositions or reply papers have been filed as of May 24, 2024.

RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP No. 3 is granted. Defendant City is ordered to serve further responses within 20 days.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Gilbert Lopez was involved in an auto accident with Defendant Lucia Rivera, who was acting in the course and scope of his employment with City of Rancho Palos Verdes when the accident occurred. On December 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Rivera and City of Rancho Palos Verdes. The operative complaint is the First Amended Complaint alleging (1) negligence; (2) vicarious liability; (3) vicarious liability.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 2031.310

(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with each of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

(3) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.

… (h) Except as provided in subdivision (j), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

Where a party is seeking to compel further responses to requests for document production, the burden is on the moving party to show both relevance to the subject matter and specific facts justifying discovery. (CCP §2031.310(b)(1); Kirkland v. Supr. Ct. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98 (“Once good cause was shown, the burden shifted to Kirkland to justify his objection”); Glenfed Develop. Corp. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.) Once good cause is established by the moving party, the burden then shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure. (Hartbrodt v. Burke (1996, 2nd Dist.) 42 Cal.App.4th 168, 172-174.)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff moves to compel further production in response to his RFP No. 3. Plaintiff argues the objections based on vagueness and ambiguity are improper, boilerplate objections.

Plaintiff argues Defendant City’s objections to RFP No. 3 based on attorney-client privilege and work product doctrine should be overruled. Plaintiff obtained a privilege log from Defendant after meeting and conferring. Plaintiff argues that based on the log, the documents are not protected by either the attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine.

Plaintiff argues that the Employee’s Report of Injury or Illness, Supervisor’s Report of Injury or Illness, Driver’s Reporter of Accident and Patron Accident Report were prepared by public employees as part of their public duties. Plaintiff argues Defendant’s position that the reports were prepared for internal use does not make them privileged or work product protected.

Plaintiff argues the objections based on relevance and privacy are meritless. Plaintiff argues the reports seek information regarding the accident. Plaintiff argues any privacy interest is outweighed by the direct relevance of the information sought. Plaintiff argues Defendants have placed Rivera’s health is at issue, because Rivera claims she suffered a sudden and unexpected medical emergency that excuses her culpability.

Parties met and conferred multiple times on the original responses and the privilege log provided as a supplemental response. (Motion, Cefali Dec., ¶¶4-9.) Parties were unable to reach an agreement. (Id.)

Plaintiff satisfies his burden as the party moving to compel further response to RFP No. 3. RFP No. 3 seeks documents “constituting, reflecting, referring to, and/or evidencing reports about the INCIDENT, including reports to, by and/or from a governmental entity. These documents relate directly to the collision that forms the basis of this complaint.

Plaintiff also correctly points out that, based on the information provided on the privilege log, none of the documents were prepared by an attorney or sent to an attorney. (Cefali Dec., Ex. D.) The authors and recipients are identified as “City Staff,” “Sedgwick, the City of Rancho Palos Verdes’ Third Party Administrator,” Lucia Rivera and City of Rancho Palos Verdes. (Id.) Based on the document titles, a number of the documents were clearly not prepared by any attorney, e.g. “Driver’s Report of Accident,” “Patron Accident Report,” “Supervisor’s Report of Injury or Illness.”

Because Plaintiff has established good cause for further production, the burden shifts to Defendant City to establish that its objections are meritorious, including privacy, attorney client privilege and work product doctrine. Defendant City did not oppose the motion.

For these reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP No. 3 is granted.

ORDER

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP No. 3 is granted. Defendant City is ordered to serve further responses within 20 days.

As the Notice does not demand sanctions, the court declines to award such. See Code of Civ. Pro. § 2023.040.


Case Number: 23TRCV04221    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: PETITION FOR ORDER TO COMPEL ARBITRATION BY DEFENDANTS CULTURAL CARE, INC. AND LATA OWEN

Having considered defendants’ motion, plaintiff’s opposition, defendants’ reply, and the associated exhibits, the court rules as follows: the petition to compel arbitration is granted.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiff M.F. (“Plaintiff”), by and through his guardian ad litem, Megan Montero (“Montero”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Cultural Care, Inc. (“Cultural Care”) and Lata Owen.

On February 8, 2024, the Court granted the application of Megan Montero to be appointed as guardian ad litem for minor plaintiff M.F.

On February 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Cultural Care, Safaa Sofia Boualite (“Boualite”), and Lata Owen (“Owen”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Training, Hiring, and Supervision; (3) Negligence Per Se; (4) Fraudulent Misrepresentation; (5) Negligent Misrepresentation; and (6) Battery.

On April 17, 2024, Defendants Cultural Care and Owen (“Moving Defendants”) filed and served the instant Petition to Compel Arbitration (the “Petition”). On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Petition, to which Moving Defendants filed a reply on May 23, 2024.

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

The FAC arises from Plaintiff being infected with oral herpes under the supervision of Cultural Care. (FAC, ¶ 21.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) California law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements. (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)

The Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies to any contract evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce which contains an arbitration clause. (Wolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) Section 2 of the FAA provides that arbitration provisions shall be enforced, save upon grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Ibid.) A state court may refuse to enforce an arbitration clause on the basis of generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Ibid.) A state court, however, may not defeat an arbitration clause by applying state laws applicable only to arbitration provisions. (Ibid.)

When it applies, the FAA preempts any state law rule that stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the FAA’s objectives. (Carbajal v. CWPSC, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 227, 238.) A party asserting FAA preemption bears the burden to present evidence establishing a contract with the arbitration provision affects interstate commerce, and the failure to do so renders the FAA inapplicable. (Ibid.) Evidence must be presented, in the form of declarations or other evidence, that establishes that the contract affects interstate commerce. (Ibid.) “[S]ince arbitration is a matter of contract, the FAA also applies if it is so stated in the agreement.” (Davis v. Shiekh Shoes, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 956, 963.)

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) Thus, in assessing a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the [motion] bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.”

(Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284, citation omitted.)

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Moving Defendants contend that Ms. Montero and her husband, Chris Flick (“Flick”), entered into a Host Family Agreement (the “Host Agreement”) with Defendant Cultural Care in whereby Ms. Montero and her husband agreed to participate as a host family and welcome an au pair into their home as part of the U.S. Department of State’s au pair cultural and exchange program. Moving Defendants assert that the Host Agreement provides for arbitration of any dispute that may arise under such agreement. Moving Defendants contend that a dispute has arisen between the parties to the Host Agreement regarding an au pair that allegedly passed oral herpes to Ms. Montero’s minor son as set forth in the FAC.

Responding Party’s Opposition

Plaintiff contends that the Massachusetts choice of law provision contravenes fundamental California public policy and is thus unenforceable. Plaintiff further asserts that the arbitration agreement is invalid under California law as it is not an agreement between Plaintiff and Moving Defendants, it is void at execution because Plaintiff is a minor, and Plaintiff exercises his right to disaffirm any agreement with Moving Defendants. Plaintiff further argues that the arbitration agreement is unconscionable.

Moving Party’s Reply

Moving Defendants argue on reply that Plaintiff is bound to the arbitration agreement as a third-party beneficiary. Moving Defendants also assert that the arbitration agreement is not unconscionable, the Host Agreement’s arbitration agreement is valid and enforceable under the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) which preempts state law, and whether the Massachusetts choice-of-law provision or the release is enforceable is a question for the arbitrator to decide.

Merits of the Motion

A. Scope of the Arbitration Agreement

In support of the Petition, Natalie Jordan provides a declaration and states the following: she is Senior Vice President of Cultural Care. (Jordan Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant Cultural Care is a United State Department of State-designated sponsoring organization of the au pair cultural and educational exchange visitor program. (Jordan Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant Cultural Care is a

Massachusetts corporation with its principal office in the Commonwealth of Massachusetts. (Jordan Decl., ¶ 5.) On May 18, 2023, Ms. Montero and her husband signed the Host Agreement. (Jordan Decl., ¶¶ 6-7; Exh. A.)

Paragraph 27 of the Host Agreement provides that the Host Agreement shall be governed by the substantive laws of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, without regard to conflict of law principles. (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 27.) The Host Agreement provides, in part, that “[i]n connection with any claim, dispute, or proceeding arising out of or relating in any manner to this Agreement or your relationship with Cultural Care, or any dispute or claim between the parties whether in contract, tort, or otherwise, or at law or in equity, whether or not related to this Agreement, the parties hereby mutually agree to submit to mandatory, finding, and binding individual arbitration.” (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 27.)

The Host Agreement further provides that “[t]he arbitrator (and not a court or jury) shall decide all issues in or related to any such dispute or claim including but not limited to issues regarding the scope of the arbitrator’s authority, arbitration procedures, legal fees, choice of law and location of the arbitration, the unavailability of class or collective arbitration, and all other issues regarding the application, interpretation, and implementation of this Agreement, or of any issue in dispute covered by this agreement.” (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 27.)

B. Applicability of the FAA

Moving Defendants contend that the Host Agreement triggers application of the FAA as it is between a California plaintiff and Defendant Cultural Care, which is a Massachusetts defendant.

Here, the Court finds that Moving Defendants have met their burden in showing that the Host Agreement affects interstate commerce. Moreover, Plaintiff does not contend that the FAA is inapplicable to the Host Agreement.

Therefore, the Court finds that the FAA does apply to the Host Agreement.

C. The Existence of an Agreement to Arbitrate

Plaintiff contends that the Host Agreement is not between Plaintiff and Moving Defendants and that such agreement is signed by Ms. Montero and Mr. Flick. As such, Plaintiff argues that there is no agreement to arbitrate between Plaintiff and Moving Defendants. On reply, Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff is bound to the Host Agreement as a third-party beneficiary.

“A third party beneficiary is someone who may enforce a contract because the contract is made expressly for his benefit.” (Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, 1336, internal quotations omitted.) “[T]he mere fact that a contract results in benefits to a third party does not render that party a third party beneficiary.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.) “Rather, the parties to the contract must have intended the third party to benefit.” (Id. at p. 1337, internal quotations omitted, emphasis in original.)

“Under the third party beneficiary theory, a nonsignatory may be compelled to arbitrate where the nonsignatory is a third party beneficiary of the contract.” (Philadelphia Indemnity Ins. Co. v. SMG Holdings, Inc. (2019) 44 Cal.App.5th 834, 841.) “Whether a nonsignatory is an intended third party beneficiary to the contract is determined from the parties’ intent, as gleaned from the contract as a whole and the circumstances under which it arose.” (Ibid.)

The Court finds that Plaintiff is a third-party beneficiary of the Host Agreement. (Jordan Decl. at Exhibit A.) The Host Agreement references the au pair doing numerous tasks related to minor Plaintiff such as supervision of play, preparing minor Plaintiff’s meals, straightening minor Plaintiff’s room, doing minor Plaintiff’s laundry, and passive duties such as being present when minor Plaintiff is sleeping. (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 12.) The Host Agreement clearly provides that “the au pair will perform childcare services and light housekeeping related to childcare.” (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 12.) Although not referenced in the Host Agreement as a party, the Court finds that Plaintiff is a third-party beneficiary as it appears from the Host Agreement that the services of the au pair were intended to benefit Plaintiff.

As such, the Court finds that an agreement to arbitrate exists with Plaintiff pursuant to a third-party beneficiary theory. The Court also finds that the Host Agreement covers the claims in this action as the FAC arises from au pair services. The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show any fact necessary to his defense.

D. Plaintiff’s Status as a Minor

Plaintiff argues that the Host Agreement is void at execution because Plaintiff was a minor and that he exercises is right to disaffirm any agreement with Moving Defendants because he did not consent to the Host Agreement.

“A minor is an individual who is under 18 years of age.” (Fam. Code § 6500.) “As a general proposition, parental consent is required for the provision of services to minors for the simple reason that minors may disaffirm their own contracts to acquire such services.” (Berg v. Taylor (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 809, 818.) “[A] contract of a minor may be disaffirmed by the minor before majority or within a reasonable time afterwards or, in the case of the minor’s death within that period, by the minor’s heirs or personal representative.” (Fam. Code § 6710.) The ability of a minor to disaffirm a contract “does not apply to contracts between adults and is therefore not controlling on the question of a parent’s power to bind his child to arbitrate by entering into a contract of which the child is a third party beneficiary.” (Doyle v. Giuliucci (1965) 62 Cal.2d 606, 609.)

Here, the Court rejects Plaintiff’s attempt to disaffirm the Host Agreement in paragraph 8 of the FAC as the Court has determined that Plaintiff is a third-party beneficiary pursuant to such agreement. Also, the Host Agreement was with Plaintiff’s parents and therefore he lacks authority to disaffirm the agreement under Doyle v. Giuliucci, supra, 62 Cal.3d 606, 609. The fact that Plaintiff was a minor at the time of the agreement does not mean that Plaintiff cannot be compelled to arbitrate his claims. (Doyle v. Giuliucci, supra, 62 Cal.2d 606.) Plaintiff’s parents consented to the Host Agreement with Plaintiff as a third-party beneficiary. Thus, the Court does not find the Host Agreement to be void at its execution.

E. Unconscionability

Plaintiff contends that the arbitration provision in the Host Agreement is unconscionable. In their reply, Moving Defendants argue that the arbitration provision is not unconscionable.

“California courts analyze unconscionability as having a procedural and a substantive element.” (Kinney v. United Healthcare Services, Inc. (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 1329.) “[B]oth elements must be present before a contract or contract provision is rendered unenforceable on grounds of unconscionability.” (Ibid.) The doctrine of unconscionability refers to “an absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms which are unreasonably favorable to the other party.” (Sonic-Calabasas A, Inc. v. Moreno (2013) 57 Cal.4th 1109, 1133.) It consists of procedural and substantive components, “the former focusing on oppression or surprise due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on overly harsh or one-sided results.” (Ibid.) Although both components of unconscionability must be present to invalidate an arbitration agreement, they need not be present in the same degree. (Armendariz v. Found Health Psychcare Servs., Inc., supra, 24 Cal.4th 83, 114.) “Essentially a sliding scale is invoked which disregards the regularity of the procedural process of the contract formation, that creates the terms, in proportion to the greater harshness or unreasonableness of the substantive terms themselves. [Citations.] In other words, the more substantively unconscionable the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Ibid.) “The party resisting arbitration bears the burden of proving unconscionability.” (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Dev. (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 247.)

“‘Procedural unconscionability’ concerns the manner in which the contract was negotiated and the circumstances of the parties at that time.¿ It focuses on the factors of oppression and surprise. The oppression component arises from an inequality of bargaining power of the parties to the contract and an absence of real negotiation or a meaningful choice on the part of the weaker party. The component of surprise arises when the challenged terms are ‘hidden in a prolix printed form drafted by the party seeking to enforce them.’” (Nyulassy v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1267, 1281.)¿

Substantive unconscionability focuses on the terms of the agreement and whether those terms are “so one sided as to “’shock the conscience.’” (Kinney v. United Healthcare Services, Inc., supra, 70 Cal. App.4th 1329, 1330.) The California Supreme Court in Armendariz set forth five minimum requirements in requiring that arbitration agreements in the employer-employee context must provide for: (1) neutral arbitrators; (2) more than minimal discovery; (3) a written award; (4) all types of relief that would otherwise be available in court; and (5) no additional costs for the employee beyond what the employee would incur if he or she were bringing the claim in court. (Fitz v. NCR Corp. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 702, 712-713.)

Plaintiff has not made any showing of oppression or surprise to warrant a finding of procedural unconscionability. Plaintiff has not presented any evidence that the Host Agreement was presented to him or his parents on a “take it or leave it basis,” with no opportunity for negotiation of its terms. In fact, Plaintiff presented zero evidence in opposition to the Petition. Plaintiff had

the burden to show unconscionability by a preponderance of the evidence pursuant to Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.

The Court therefore finds that the Host Agreement is not procedurally unconscionable. Due to Plaintiff’s failure to make a showing of procedural unconscionability, the Court need not address the issue of substantive unconscionability.

F. The Choice of Law Issue and Validity of the Release

The Court notes that the parties dispute about whether Massachusetts choice of law provision should apply in determining whether the arbitration clause in the Host Agreement is enforceable.

“A proceeding to compel arbitration is in essence a suit in equity to compel specific performance of a contract.” (Freeman v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1975) 14 Cal.3d 473, 479.) “Arbitration is, of course, a matter of contract, and the parties may freely delineate the area of its application.” (Ibid.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 was drafted . . . to prescribe and limit the power of the superior court in passing upon a petition to compel arbitration.” (Ibid.) CCP § 1281.2 provides that the superior court is required “to determine in advance whether there is a duty to arbitrate the controversy which has arisen. The performance of this duty necessarily requires the court to examine and, to a limited extent, construe the underlying agreement.” (Id. at p. 480.) “The arbitrability of a dispute may itself be subject to arbitration if the parties have so provided in their contract.” (Ibid.) “Even then, it is necessary for the court to examine the contract to ascertain whether the parties have so provided.” (Ibid.)

“Parties to an arbitration may agree to delegate to the arbitrator, instead of a court, questions regarding the enforceability of the agreement.” (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 241.) “They can agree to arbitrate almost any dispute—even a dispute over whether the underlying dispute is subject to arbitration.” (Ibid.) For a delegation clause to be effective two requirements must be met: (1) the language of the delegation clause must be clear and unmistakable; and (2) the delegation must not be revokable under state contract defenses such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Id. at p. 242.)

A choice of law provision will be enforced if: (1) the chosen state bears a substantial relationship to the parties to the contract or a reasonable basis for the parties’ choice; and (2) application of the chosen state’s law must not violate a strong policy of California law. (Nedlloyd Lines B.V. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 459, 479.) “To permit a child care provider to contract away its duty to exercise ordinary care is . . . antithetical to the very nature of child care services.” (Gavin W. v. YMCA of Metropolitan Los Angeles (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 662, 676.) “[E]xculpatory agreements that purport to relieve child care providers of liability for their own negligence are void and against public policy.” (Ibid.)

The Host Agreement provides that Defendants are released from any and all claims or causes of action which Ms. Montero and Mr. Flick may have against Moving Defendants “which arise out of injury, damage, or loss of any other kind to the Host Family or their property resulting from participation in the Cultural Care program. This includes, without limitation, the au pair’s

performance of services for and involvement with the Host Family, regardless of how such injury, damage, or loss may arise...” (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 26.)

Here, the Host Agreement is between Mr. Flick and Ms. Montero who reside in California and Defendant Cultural Care who is based on Massachusetts. (Jordan Decl. at Exhibit A.) The Host Agreement contains a release clause that relieves Defendant Cultural Care and its agents, officers, directors, among other individuals, of all liability. Thus, enforcing the Host Agreement would allow Moving Defendants to contract away all liability arising from the acts alleged in the FAC which is against California public policy. However, as will be explained below, the arbitrator is to decide issues related to choice of law, as well as enforceability of the Host Agreement, and the Court cannot rule on such an issue.

Moving Defendants contend that the arbitrator must decide questions of arbitrability. The Court agrees. As stated above, Plaintiff is a third-party beneficiary to the Host Agreement and is bound by such agreement. As such, based on the express delegation language therein, the arbitrator must decide the applicability of the choice of law provision and any other issues related to the applicability of the release clause as the arbitrator is to decide “issues regarding the application, interpretation, and implementation of [the] [Host] [Agreement].” (Jordan Decl., Exh. A at ¶ 27.)

As such, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s claims against Moving Defendants can be compelled to arbitration pursuant to the Host Agreement.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Petition to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The Court stays this action pending the completion of arbitration between the parties. (CCP § 1281.4.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40 provides that an attorney in good standing in another jurisdiction may apply to appear pro hac vice in this State by filing “with the court a verified application with proof of service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1013a of a copy of the application and notice of hearing of the application on all parties who have appeared in the cause and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office. The notice of hearing must be given at the time prescribed in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 unless the court has prescribed a shorter period.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(c)(1).) “An applicant for permission to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule must pay a reasonable fee not exceeding $50 to the State Bar of California with the copy of the application and the notice of hearing that is served on the State Bar.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(e).)

The written application must provide the following information: (1) applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) representation that the attorney is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the preceding two years, if any; and (5) the name, address, and telephone number of the active California State Bar member who is counsel of record in the local action. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(d).)
