Case Number: 24VECV03765    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: T

24VECV03765 MERCURY INSURANCE COMPANY, ... vs CHRISTOPHER T BARONE

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Cross-Defendant Nairmac, Inc. dba Premier Coach Auto Collision’s Special Motion to Strike the Cross-Complaint is GRANTED as to the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth causes of action.

Cross-Defendant Mercury Insurance Company’s Joinder to the Special Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

The Cross-Complaint’s third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth causes of action as alleged against Cross-Defendant Nairmac, Inc. dba Premier Coach Auto Collision and Cross-Defendant Mercury Insurance Company are STRICKEN. 

Cross-Defendant Nairmac, Inc. dba Premier Coach Auto Collision is DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE from the Cross-Complaint. 

The First Amended Cross-Complaint is STRICKEN in its entirety as to Cross-Defendant Nairmac, Inc. dba Premier Coach Auto Collision.

The First Amended Cross-Complaint’s third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth causes of action is STRICKEN as to Cross-Defendant Mercury Insurance Company.

Introduction

Cross-Defendant Nairmac, Inc. dba Premier Coach Auto Collision (Premier) specially moved to strike Cross-Complainants Christopher T. Barone, Dragana Barone, and Diana Barone’s (collectively, Barones) Cross-Complaint (XC).  The Special Motion to Strike placed into issue the third COA for fraud, the fourth COA for negligent misrepresentation, the fifth COA for intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED), the sixth COA for negligent infliction of emotional distress (NIED), the seventh COA for conversion, the eighth COA for violation of Business and Professions Code, section 17200 (UCL), the ninth COA for declaratory relief, and the tenth COA for conspiracy. 

Cross-Defendant Mercury Insurance Company (Mercury) requested joinder to Premier’s Special Motion.  However, the first COA for breach of contract, the second COA for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and the twelfth COA for breach of duty to defend/appoint independent counsel are not at issue in the instant motion because they are only alleged against Mercury.  Premier’s Special Motion did not place into issue the first, second, and twelfth COAs. The first, second, and twelfth COAs are excluded from Mercury’s joinder to Premier’s Special Motion to Strike.  The Court did not review the first, second, and twelfth COAs. 

The instant Special Motion to Strike also did not place into issue the eleventh COA for professional negligence because it is only alleged against Co-Cross-Defendant Shaver Korff & Castronovo LLP (SKC). 

Premier further reserved their request for attorneys’ fees and costs to be made in a separate motion.

Barones’ opposition was due November 6, 2024 and none was filed.

Procedure

The Court noted that the Special Motion to Strike was entered into the Court’s e-filing system on October 11, 2024 at 9:29 a.m.  Barones filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (FAXC) on the same day, October 11, 2024, at the public counter.  The FAXC was entered into the Court’s e-filing system on the same day at 4:12 p.m. The time entries show that the Special Motion to Strike was entered first.  Because the Special Motion to Strike was entered first, Barones’ filing of the FAXC was improper to avoid the Special Motion to Strike.  (Salma v. Capon (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1275, 1280.)  The Court reviewed the Special Motion to Strike with the Cross-Complaint filed on August 15, 2024 being the operative pleading.

Discussion

The Special Motion to Strike is a two-step process.  The moving party defendant has the initial burden to show that the claims are based upon protected activity.  (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 425.16; Briganti v. Chow (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 504, 508.)  If the initial burden is met, the burden transfers to the opposing party plaintiff to show a probability of prevailing on the claims.  (Id.)  Premier argued that the COAs alleged claims that are protected activity because they were statements made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a judicial body.  (Code Civ. Proc., sec 425.16(e)(2).)  Barones alleged that Mercury/Premier made false statements during the settlement negotiations in the underlying complaint filed in the instant action; coerced Barones into a settlement; and initiated a baseless lawsuit via the underlying complaint in this action.  Barones alleged that Mercury/Premier’s underlying lawsuit sought to impose additional, unwritten conditions that were never a part of the settlement agreement.  (XC pars. 39, 64, 75, 85.)  The fraud-based claims alleged that Mercury/Premier misrepresented the terms of the proposed settlement.  (XC pars. 64, 72, 75, 76.)  The emotional distress claims are grounded on Mercury/Premier’s alleged threats to file legal action and filing a meritless lawsuit.  (XC par. 85.)    The conversion COA and the UCL COA incorporated the allegations in the prior COAs.  (XC pars. 100 and 111.)  The Court finds Premier’s argument persuasive.  Because Barones’ XC alleged claims regarding the settlement agreement in the underlying complaint, the statements upon which Barones base their claims are protected activity.  Premier met its initial burden as to the third COA thru the tenth COA. 

The burden shifted to the Barones to show, through admissible evidence, that the COAs have a probability of prevailing.  Because Barones failed to file an opposition, Barones failed to meet their burden.

IT IS SO ORDERED, CLERK TO GIVE NOTICE.
