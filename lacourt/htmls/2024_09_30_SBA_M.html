Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka      Monday, September 30, 2024

Department M    Calendar No. 11  

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Tony Taleff v. Marcia Lynn Sattgast Taleff, et al.

20TRCV00077

1.      Tony Taleff’s Motion for Replevin

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Tony Taleff’s Motion for Replevin is denied without prejudice.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 22, 2020. Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint was filed on February 24, 2022. Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint was filed on October 6, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that during the course of the parties’ dissolution of marriage proceedings, numerous instances of alleged wrongdoing were uncovered.

 

Plaintiff filed this “motion for replevin.” However, procedurally, the proper term is a writ of possession. The Court notes that Plaintiff also filed an application for writ of possession in connection with this “motion.” Thus, the Court will treat this “motion” as an application for writ of possession. The Court is also unclear as to the “co-declaration” of Tony Taleff and Vera Taleff. Vera Taleff is not a party to this action.

 

Writ of Possession

 

CCP § 512.010 sets forth the requirements in an application for writ of possession.  CCP § 512.010(b) states: “The application shall be executed under oath and shall include all of the following:

 (1) A showing of the basis of the plaintiff's claim and that the plaintiff is entitled to possession of the property claimed. If the basis of the plaintiff's claim is a written instrument, a copy of the instrument shall be attached.

 (2) A showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant, of the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property, and, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the reason for the detention.

 (3) A particular description of the property and a statement of its value.

 (4) A statement, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the location of the property and, if the property, or some part of it, is within a private place which may have to be entered to take possession, a showing that there is probable cause to believe that such property is located there.

 (5) A statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.

 

Pursuant to CCP § 512.040(b): “The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established. The hearing is not for the purpose of determining whether the claim is actually valid. The determination of the actual validity of the claim will be made in subsequent proceedings in the action and will not be affected by the decision at the hearing on the application for the writ.” Pursuant to CCP § 511.090, a “claim has probable validity where it is more likely than not that plaintiff will obtain judgment against defendant on that claim.”

 

Further, “[a]t the hearing, the court shall make its determinations upon the basis of the pleadings and other papers in the record; but, upon good cause shown, the court may receive and consider additional evidence and authority produced at the hearing or may continue the hearing for the production of such additional evidence, oral or documentary, or the filing of other affidavits or points and authorities.”  CCP § 512.050.

 

Plaintiff applies for a writ of possession to possess the following: “Three 1882 S20 Coronet Head Double Eagle Liberty Gold coins Book value approx $63,000 each Gold pendants and chains estimated at 1500.00[.]” (Application for Writ of Possession, ¶ 4.) Here, however, Plaintiff has failed to set forth all the essential elements required in CCP § 512.010(b).

 

Plaintiff has failed to set forth an evidentiary basis for the grounds for Plaintiff’s claim. Plaintiff’s contention that the property is his separate property is conclusory. Plaintiff set forth a redacted part of an order from Case Number BD497594. However, without the full context of the statement, it cannot be determined what exactly the Court ordered, and, in any event, the Court made a statement that as to the “gold coins,” they were not in fact either separate or community property of the parties. First, this statement does not definitively establish that they are the same coins referred to by Plaintiff and second the statement that they are not separate or community property undermines Plaintiff’s contention that they are his separate property. In addition, Plaintiff failed to set forth facts to show that the property is being wrongfully detained. Finally, Plaintiff failed to set forth a “statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.” CCP § 512.010(b)(5).

 

Finally, throughout the application, motion and declaration, Plaintiff makes numerous references which appear to indicate that the property is not his property at all, but, in fact, belongs to Vera Taleff who is not a party to this action. Thus, it is unclear whether Plaintiff Tony Taleff actually is entitled to immediate possession of this property since the property apparently belongs to a non-party named Vera Taleff. Therefore, Plaintiff failed to establish the probable validity of his claim to possession of the property.

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s Motion for Replevin or Application for Writ of Possession is denied without prejudice.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Kristy Redmond, et al. v. Lennox Apartment, et al.   

21STCV16686

1.      Jaime Fabian’s, Guardian Ad Litem for Claimant Destiny Fabian, Petition for Approval of Compromise of a Claim for Minor

 

TENTATIVE RULING


Jaime Fabian’s, Guardian Ad Litem for Claimant Destiny Fabian, Petition for Approval of Compromise of a Claim for Minor is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint was filed on May 3, 2021. Plaintiffs’ Second Amended Complaint was filed on June 10, 2022. Plaintiffs’ Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) was filed on June 23, 2023. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs suffered bed bug bits in their apartment. The apartment was sprayed by Amen Pest Control, Inc. Defendant Mabry Management, Company was the management company that was managing the subject property. Defendant Lennox Apartment owns the apartment complex. Demurring Defendant Beach Front Property Management, Inc. was substituted in for DOE 1 on December 16, 2022. Demurring Defendant Runway Vista LLC was substituted as DOE 2 on January 31, 2023. The connection that these Defendants have with the apartment is not specified at all in the TAC. Plaintiffs allege the following causes of action: 1. Battery 2. Negligence 3. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress 4. Private Nuisance 5. Public Nuisance. 

 

Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action of Minor

 

A claim by or against a minor may be settled through compromise only with the approval of the court.  A petition for approval must be presented to the court by the minor’s guardian.  CCP § 372; Probate Code Secs. 2500-2507; 3413-3605.

 

The court should consider the following factors: the circumstances of the accident, particularly the facts bearing on the payor’s liability and the minor’s damages; the amount of the proposed settlement; the method of payment; the nature of the injury and the minor’s current medical status (the petition should include a recent medical report); and the amount of attorney’s fees being requested.  Before the hearing, the court should make a preliminary determination of whether the proposed settlement and the method of payment appear reasonable in relation to the potential liability and the nature and extent of injuries.  It is especially important to determine whether the minor’s condition is permanent, and whether it is stable or likely to worsen.  The court should also make a preliminary determination as to whether the costs, expenses, and attorneys’ fees appear reasonable.

 

The Court has reviewed the petition for compromise of pending action. The Court determines that the petition is reasonable as to the settlement amount. The Court finds that the settlement is in the best interest of the minor. The proposed petition for compromise of the pending action and the proposed disposition of the proceeds of the settlement funds is hereby approved.

 

Thus, for the foregoing reasons, Petitioner’s Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action of Minor is granted. 

 

Petitioner is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
