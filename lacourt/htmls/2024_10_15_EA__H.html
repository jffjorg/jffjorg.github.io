DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV02386    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: H

Chen, et al. v. Cao, Case No. 23PSCV02386

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Ruzhuan Cao’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED in part (i.e., as to Plaintiff Chen) and OVERRULED in part (i.e., as to Plaintiff Han). At the hearing, the Court will hear from Plaintiff Chen as to whether leave to amend is requested and if so, will require an offer of proof.

 

Background

Plaintiffs Yuzheng Chen (“Chen”) and Jixian Han (“Han”) (together, “Plaintiffs’”) allege that Han and Ruzhuan Cao (“Defendant”) signed a house rental agreement on April 6, 2019 and that Defendant thereafter sublet the house to Xu Wenxiang for Airbnb or commercial use.

On November 6, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting a breach of contract cause of action against Defendant and Does 1-5.

A Case Management Conference is set for January 22, 2025. Pending before the Court is Defendant’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint.

 

Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that, inter alia, there is a defect or misjoinder of parties, that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or that the pleading is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (d)-(f).)

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

 

Discussion

Defendant demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain. Defendant also demurs to the complaint per subdivision (d) on the basis of misjoinder of parties.

A review of Defendant’s demurrer reflects that it is directed as to Plaintiff Chen only and not as to Plaintiff Han. (See Dem., 3:9-10 [“However, the FAC is fatally defective and uncertain as it fails to show Plaintiff YUZHENG CHEN’s standing to sue for Breach of Contract. . .”], 3:12-13 [“As such, Movant Defendant submits that the claim for Breach of Contract should be dismissed as to Plaintiff YUZCHENG CHEN without leave to amend”]; footnote one, page 3 [“The issue regarding Plaintiff CHEN’s standing was already adjudicated during the MSJ proceeding in the Previous Case”]; 4:21-22 [“CHEN has no standing to sue for breach of contract. As such, the within Motion should be granted without leave to amend s to Plaintiff CHEN”]; 4:23 [“The Issue Re: CHEN’s Lack of Standing Was Determined In The Previous Case”] and 5:3 [“the Court should apply the doctrine of Res Judicata on the issue regarding Plaintiff CHEN’s lack of standing to sue”].

“A statement of a cause of action for breach of contract requires a pleading of (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.” (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Constr. Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.)

Further, the essential elements of a contract include (1) parties capable of contracting; (2) their consent; (3) a lawful object and (4) a sufficient cause or consideration. (Civil Code § 1550.)

Plaintiffs have alleged that “Jixian Han and Ruzhuan Cao signed a house rental agreement on 4/6/2019. . .” (FAC, p. 3). Chen is not identified as a party to the contract; accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint is sustained as to Chen only. Defendant’s demurrer is overruled as to Han.

As an aside, the Court declines to consider Defendant’s argument that the Court should apply the doctrine of res judicata on the issue regarding Chen’s lack of standing to sue and denies Defendant’s request for judicial notice. “Issue preclusion prohibits the relitigation of issues argued and decided in a previous case, even if the second suit raises different causes of action. Under issue preclusion, the prior judgment conclusively resolves an issue actually litigated and determined in the first action.” (DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813, 824.) “[I]ssue preclusion applies: (1) after final adjudication (2) of an identical issue (3) actually litigated and necessarily decided in the first suit and (4) asserted against one who was a party in the first suit or one in privity with that party.” (Id. at 825.) Defendant has not made a sufficient showing regarding the existence of these factors.

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer as to Chen and overrules the demurrer as to Han. The Court is disinclined to grant Chen leave to amend but will hear from Chen in this regard at the hearing; if leave to amend is requested, the Court will require an offer of proof.

Case Number: 24PSCV00396    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: H

Plaintiff PHH Mortgage Corporation’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

Background

Plaintiff PHH Mortgage Corporation (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

Glenda S. Ahhaitty (“Ahhaitty”) acquired title to the property located at 1119 Angelcrest Drive in Hacienda Heights (the “subject property”) via a grant deed recorded on July 5, 2001. On or around March 10, 2012, Ahhaitty obtained a loan, which was secured by a deed of trust recorded on April 6, 2012 (the “subject deed”) in favor of Mortgage Electronic Registration Systems (“MERS”), acting solely as nominee for Ally Bank, as beneficiary. The legal description in the subject deed is incorrect. The subject deed was assigned to Plaintiff via a Corporate Assignment of Deed of Trust recorded on April 17, 2019. Notices of Federal Tax Lien were recorded on September 6, 2016 and February 18, 2018, respectively. Notices of State Tax Lien were recorded on November 8, 2017 and June 16, 2022, respectively. The subject deed is presently in default, but Plaintiff is unable to proceed with a nonjudicial foreclosure due to the mistaken legal description therein.

On February 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Ahhaitty, Department of the Treasury-Internal Revenue Service, The Franchise Tax Board of the State of California (“FTB”), and Does 1-10 for (1) reformation and (2) declaratory relief.

On March 26, 2024, a “Disclaimer of the United State of America” was filed.

On April 24, 2024, the Clerk entered default against FTB and Ahhaitty.

An Order to Show Cause Re: Default Judgment is set for October 15, 2024.

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defect is noted:

“[A] claim for reformation of an instrument based on fraud or mistake is subject to a three-year statute of limitations.” (Jo Redland Trust, U.A.D. 4-6-05 v. CIT Bank, N.A. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 142, 162; Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) Further, “[a] claim for declaratory relief is subject to the same statute of limitations as the legal or equitable claim on which it is based.” (Bank of New York Mellon v. Citibank, N.A. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 935, 943.) Plaintiff, through its complaint filed February 7, 2024, seeks reformation of a deed of trust recorded on April 6, 2012. The Court directs Plaintiff to provide supplemental briefing as to the statute of limitations.

Accordingly, the Court denies Plaintiff’s application without prejudice and directs Plaintiff to submit a new default judgment application that addresses the aforementioned issue.            

Case Number: 24PSCV00420    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: H

Borrayo, et al. v. Mojica, Case No. 24PSCV00420

 

Defendant Elsa Moreno Mojica’s Motion to Set Aside Default is DENIED.

 

Background

Plaintiffs Adriana Moreno Borrayo and Jose Ruben Moreno Mojica (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Plaintiffs’ sister, Elsa Armida Moreno Mojica (“Mojica”), falsified Plaintiffs’ mother’s signature on a quitclaim deed involving the property located at 1295 Cornelia St. in Pomona (the “subject property”). On January 25, 2024, Mojica sold the subject property.

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting a fraud cause of action against Mojica.

On April 17, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Mojica.

An Order to Show Cause Re: Default Judgment is set for October 15, 2024. Pending before the Court is Mojica’s motion to set aside default.

 

Legal Standard

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Discussion

Mojica moves the court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b), for an order setting aside the April 17, 2024 entry of default.

Mojica seeks mandatory relief pursuant to an attorney affidavit of fault. Mojica’s counsel John R. Ramos (“Ramos”) represents that when he first consulted with Mojica, she advised him that she had never been served with the summons and complaint (Ramos Decl., 3:15-16); that he appeared in San Bernardino Superior Court on March 29, 2024 to object to Plaintiffs’ petition to be appointed as administrators of their mother’s estate (Id., 3:18-20); that the San Bernardino court subsequently dismissed the probate proceeding there (Id., 3:21-22); that Plaintiffs filed a new probate petition in the Los Angeles Superior Court, the hearing of which was continued to October 26, 2024 (Id., 3:22-25); that he reviewed the ledger in this case and erroneously believed that Plaintiff’s request for entry of default had been rejected on April 17, 2024 (Id., 3:26-28); and that he filed a demurrer to the complaint on Mojica’s behalf based on this mistaken belief (Id., 4:2-8).

The foregoing information suggests to the Court that there is no attorney mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect under the circumstances here, because Mojica is apparently contending that she was never served with the summons and complaint (although no supporting declaration has been submitted in this regard); if true, then there would be no deadline for filing a responsive pleading on Mojica’s behalf in the first instance. The Court declines to grant relief on the basis of attorney affidavit of fault. The motion is denied.

That said, in light of the fact that the motion was unopposed, at the hearing, the Court will inquire of the parties whether they are willing to stipulate to vacate the default as to Mojica.
