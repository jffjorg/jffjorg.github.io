DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV42732    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

August 15, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV42732

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Sterling Jewelers, Inc. and Signet Jewelers Limited

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Jessica Parker

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Jessica Parker (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Sterling Jewelers, Inc. and Signet Jewelers Limited (“Defendants”) for sex discrimination and sexual harassment.  Defendants move for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  Plaintiff opposes the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. . . . There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

EVIDENCE

            Plaintiff objects specifically to several of Defendants’ separate statements of material facts.  The separate statement is not evidence.  As such, the Court cannot rule on evidentiary objections to it.

Defendants’ objections to Plaintiff’s evidence Nos. 14, 17, 18, 21, 22, 23, 24, and 25 are sustained.  Defendants’ other evidentiary objections are overruled.

Defendants advance additional evidence in connection with its reply papers.  The Court cannot consider such evidence, as Plaintiff has not had an opportunity to respond to it.  (San Diego Watercrafts, Inc. v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 308, 316.)  The Court, therefore, disregards the evidence Defendants advance in reply.  Further, Oon August 9, 2020, Defendant filed a pleading entitled “Responses to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Disputed and Additional Material Facts in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition,” to which Plaintiff has objected and moved to strike.  The Court sustains the objection and will not consider that pleading.

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Discrimination

            Plaintiff asserts causes of action for discrimination and retaliation under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA).  To state such a claim, the employee must demonstrate “(1) the employee's membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee's interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  “A defending employer seeking summary judgment in a discrimination case may meet its burden by showing that one or more of these prima facie elements is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.  [¶]  If the employer has met its burden by showing a legitimate reason for its conduct, the employee must demonstrate a triable issue by producing substantial evidence that the employer's stated reasons were untrue or pretextual, or that the employer acted with a discriminatory animus, such that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the employer engaged in intentional discrimination or other unlawful action.”  (Cucuzza v. City of Santa Clara (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1038, internal citation omitted.)  An employer is likewise not liable for discrimination “if the employer proves it would have fired the employee anyway for lawful reasons.”  (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 234.)

Defendants advance the testimony of their district manager, Cody Bingham (“Bingham”).  Bingham testified Defendants laid off Plaintiff and other employees in 2020 based on “[q]uality of work, billing, and productivity, coming to work on time, and [being] part of the team . . . .” (Declaration of Thomas N. McCormick attached to Defendant’s Compendium of Evidence, Exhibit 3, p. 111.)  Defendants advance additional evidence Plaintiff had issues with tardiness.  (Id., Exhibit 1, p. 106.) 

Defendants also present the testimony of Plaintiff’s co-worker, Tracey Beresford (“Beresford”) who testified work was available to Plaintiff, but Plaintiff was not performing at the expected level and, therefore, she could not complete that work.  (McCormick Decl., Exhibit 4, pp. 124-126.) 

Taken together, Defendants’ evidence indicates Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment for non-discriminatory reasons.  Defendants have met their burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on her cause of action for discrimination.  Thus, the burden has shifted to  Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her discrimination claim.

Plaintiff presents evidence of her performance reviews for the period of February 3, 2019 to February 1, 2020 which reveal Plaintiff achieved the expected results for her role.  (Declaration of Afshin Mozaffari, Exhibit 1.)  Plaintiff also provides multiple e-mails addressed to Bingham from Plaintiff’s manager, Anthony Nouneh (“Nouneh”), in which Nouneh requested that Plaintiff be promoted, and an e-mail from Bingham, in which Bingham approved the promotion.  (Id., Exhibits 3-5.)  However, when Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment, Defendants’ record reflects Defendants never promoted Plaintiff.  (Id., Exhibit 29.)  Plaintiff also advances evidence the number of employees in California with Plaintiff’s classification increased, rather than decreased, in 2020.  (Id., Exhibit 46.)  Plaintiff also cites Bingham’s testimony in which Bingham acknowledged positions opened for jewelers at other locations, but Defendants did not offer those positions to Plaintiff.  (Id., Exhibit 51, pp. 101-102.)  Moreover, Plaintiff presents the testimony of Defendants’ person most qualified to testify on specified issues, who stated Defendants always have open jeweler positions.  (Id., Exhibit 52, p. 105.)  The totality of the evidence Plaintiff has presented suggests Defendants’ stated reasons for terminating Plaintiff’s employment were untrue.  Plaintiff, therefore, has raised triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for discrimination. 

Second Cause of Action: Sexual Harassment

            Under FEHA, an employer is strictly liable for a supervisor’s harassment of a subordinate.  (State Dept. of Health Services v. Superior Court (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1026, 1042.)  For purposes of FEHA, a supervisor is defined as “any individual having the authority, in the interest of the employer, to hire, transfer, suspend, lay off, recall, promote, discharge, assign, reward, or discipline other employees, or the responsibility to direct them, or to adjust their grievances, or effectively to recommend that action, if, in connection with the foregoing, the exercise of that authority is not of a merely routine or clerical nature, but requires the use of independent judgment.”  (Gov. Code, § 12926, subd. (t).)  A lead worker who has responsibility to direct an employee's day-to-day duties may be a supervisor under FEHA.  (Chapman v. Enos (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 920, 930-931.)

At her deposition, Plaintiff testified her supervisor “pressured [Plaintiff] to do more of those low end jobs.”  (Mozaffari Decl., Exhibit 50, p. 234.)  Plaintiff testified her co-worker, Josh Alvarez (“Alvarez”) told Plaintiff she could work on the high-value jobs Plaintiff wanted in exchange for sexual favors, but when she refused his offer, Alvarez assigned the high-value jobs to other coworkers.  (Id., Exhibit 50, pp. 246-248.)  Plaintiff testified she reported Alvarez’s harassment to her supervisor and to Defendants’ human resources departments, and Defendants investigated her report, including by interviewing Plaintiff.  (McCormick Decl., Exhibit 1, pp. 259-260.)  Plaintiff’s testimony constitutes evidence of sexual harassment.  However, Defendants contend Alvarez was not a supervisor.

Defendants submit the declaration of Brian Watson (“Watson”), a human resources employee for Defendants, who avers Alvarez’s duties for Defendants “were customer facing, largely clerical, and including serving as a backup to the sales personnel . . . .”  (Declaration of Brian Watson (Exhibit B in Compendium of Evidence), ¶ 9; see also Declaration of Cody Bingham Exhibit C in Compendium), ¶ 5.)  Defendants’ evidence meets their burden to show Defendants are not strictly liable for Alvarez’s sexual harassment of Plaintiff because Alvarez was not a supervisor.

Under FEHA, Defendants are not liable for Alvarez’s harassment if Alvarez was not a supervisor unless Defendants “[knew] or should have known of this conduct and fail[ed] to take immediate and appropriate corrective action.”  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1)).  Again, Defendants rely on Watson’s declaration in this regard.  Watson states Defendants “strictly prohibit[] unlawful discrimination, harassment, and retaliation of any kind and ha[ve] strict policies, a rigorous training program, and a team of HR professional to enforce such policies.  The Company maintains a handbook for employees that highlight these protections and it also publishes a stand-alone Equal Employment Opportunity . . . policy that covers its full commitment to maintaining a workplace free of harassment and discrimination, and ensuring no retaliation occurs for any protected activity.”  (Watson Decl., ¶ 11.)  Watson states Defendants investigated Plaintiff’s claim that Avarez harassed Plaintiff when she reported the behavior to her supervisor on December 30, 2019; Defendants’ investigation substantiated Plaintiff’s claim; and Defendants terminated Plaintiff effective January 11, 2020.  (Id., ¶¶ 18-20.)  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Defendants are not liable for Alvarez’s harassment of Plaintiff, because Defendants promptly addressed Alvarez’s harassment when Plaintiff reported it.  Defendants, therefore, shift the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendants are liable to Plaintiff for such harassment.

In opposition, Plaintiff advances her own testimony.  Plaintiff states Alvarez was the repair liaison at the location where Plaintiff and Alvarez worked, and Alvarez assigned work to the jewelers that came to their work location from the adjacent retail store.  (Declaration of Jessica Parker in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication, ¶ 14.)  Plaintiff adds in Defendants’ interview of Alvarez regarding the allegations of his harassment of Plaintiff, Alvarez discussed his decisions to assign work to Plaintiff.  (Mozaffari Decl., Exhibit 9.)  Moreover, at his deposition, Alvarez confirmed he assigned work to the jewelers as the work came into the repair shop; he used his independent judgment in deciding what job to give to which jeweler; and his supervisors left it up to his discretion.  (Id., Exhibit 54.)  This evidence raises triable issues of fact to be determined by the trier of fact as to whether Alvarez worked in a supervisory role for Defendants and, at a minimum, whether Defendants knew or should have known Alvarez made decisions concerning to whom work was to be assigned, such that Defendants are strictly liable for his harassment of Plaintiff.

Third Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action.  . . .  Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.”  (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.)  In her operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges she complained to Defendants about discrimination and harassment based on sex, and Defendants retaliated against Plaintiff by terminating Plaintiff’s employment.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 41-42.)  As set forth above, Defendants advance evidence Defendants’ terminated Plaintiff for unrelated reasons.  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her third cause of action for retaliation.  Defendants shift the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her third cause of action for retaliation.  Plaintiff again advances evidence to suggest Defendants’ stated reasons for terminating Plaintiff’s employment were untrue.  In addition, Plaintiff Plaintiff, therefore, has  raised triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her third cause of action for retaliation. 

Fourth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment

            This claim is derivative of Plaintiff’s claims in the first and second causes of action. Among other things, Plaintiff alleges her complaints were met with hostility by Defendants and Defendants’ investigation of her claims were inadequate.  As such, Plaintiff has met her burden with respect to this cause of action. 

Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action: Retaliation

Plaintiff’s fifth and sixth causes of action are for retaliation under Labor Code sections 1102.5 and 6310.  To state a cause of action under section 1102.5, Plaintiff must identify “some statute, rule or regulation which may have been violated by the conduct [s]he disclosed.”  (Ross v. County of Riverside (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 580, 592, internal quotations and citation omitted.) Section 6310 prohibits an employer from retaliating against an employee who has made complaints regarding employment safety or health or refused to work in an unsafe and unhealthy work environment. 

In the operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges, she “protested and complained to Defendants about what she reasonably believed to be various illegal, hazardous, unsafe, and unhealthy work conditions, including the lack of ventilation in the shop where Plaintiff worked, which caused jewelers to have to breathe in dust, chemicals, and other toxic particulates; the illegal and/or unsafe handling and disposal of hazardous waste; and the lack of masks available to protect employees from breathing in dust, chemicals and other toxic particulates and from the spread of COVID-19 later in early 2020.”  (Complaint, ¶ 56.)  As set forth above, Defendants advance evidence Plaintiff was  terminated for unrelated reasons.  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on her fifth cause of action for retaliation.  Defendants, therefore, have shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact concerning her retaliation claim.

Plaintiff contends that on March 20, 2020, she told Bingham her workplace lacked ventilation and masks, which made Plaintiff feel unsafe, and this was her last day of work before the shutdown due to COVID-19.  (Parker Decl., ¶ 19.)  As set forth above, Plaintiff also advances evidence that suggests Defendants’ stated reasons for terminating Plaintiff’s employment were untrue.  Plaintiff has sufficiently raised triable issues of material fact as to whether she can prevail on her fifth cause of action for retaliation. 

Seventh Cause of Action:  Retaliation and Wrongful Termination in Violation of Fundamental Public Policy

            As this claim is essentially derivative of those that precede it, Plaintiff has satisfied her burden of establishing triable issue of material fact exists concerning Defendant’s alleged violation of public policy and the statutory provisions on which her claims are based.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication, is denied.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.
