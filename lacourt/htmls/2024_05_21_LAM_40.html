DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 20STCV13703    Hearing Date: May 21, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

DANNY BAKEWELL, SR.,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KEVIN COHEE; ONEUNITED BANK, and DOES 1 to 20, Inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          20STCV13703

 Hearing Date:   5/21/24

 Trial Date:        7/9/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr.’s Motion to Quash Subpoenas Duces Tecum and for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,360.00 Against Defendants Kevin Cohee and OneUnited Bank and Their Counsel of Record Parnell Fox, Esq.

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr. sues Defendants OneUnited Bank (the Bank), Kevin Cohee (Chairman and CEO of the Bank), and Does 1 to 20 pursuant to an April 15, 2020, First Amended Complaint (FAC) alleging claims of (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Fraud and Deceit, (3) Declaratory Relief, (4) Unfair Competition, (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and (6) Fraudulent Sale of Securities.

The claims arise from allegations that Defendant Cohee induced Plaintiff Bakewell into subscribing to $250,000 of OneUnited Bank Preferred Stock by making alleged false representations to Bakewell to the effect that Bakewell would be able to redeem his $250,000 in Preferred Stock, plus corresponding dividends, upon request, only for OneUnited Bank to deny such a request by Bakewell in December 2019.

B. Subpoenas at Issue

On April 8, 2024, Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank served a subpoena for various of Plaintiff’s financial records from Plaintiff’s accountant, Paul G. Joseph, CPA (Joseph, CPA).

On April 10, 2024, Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank served subpoenas for various of Plaintiff’s financial records from the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) and from California’s Franchise Tax Board (FTB).

On April 18, 2024, Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank served subpoenas for various of Plaintiff’s financial records from Northern Trust, an investment firm that appears in Plaintiff’s 2008 tax records.

The subpoenas all checked off a proof of service of notice to consumer or employee that purports to reflect service of the notice by mail to Plaintiff’s counsel.

The subpoenas served on Joseph, CPA, the IRS, and on the FTB contain identical requests for Plaintiff’s financial documents, which seek to subpoena:

1. Plaintiff Bakewell’s income tax returns for years 2005 to 2020 showing 1) dividend payments received from OneUnited Bank, 2) Bakewell’s ownership of company stock, or 3) any deductions taken for losses associated with ownership of stock purchased from OneUnited Bank.

2. All Form 1099s and any other tax records showing Bakewell receiving dividend payments from OneUnited Bank.

3. All Form 1099s and any other tax records showing Bakewell receiving dividend payments from any entity associated with ownership of company stock.

4. All documents showing Bakewell’s receipt and/or deposit of dividend checks received from OneUnited Bank regarding stock purchased from it, including but not limited to bank account statements, and/or statements, cancelled checks, and the like.

The Northern Trust subpoena contains requests for documents related to financial investment opportunities and discussions relating thereto between Plaintiff and Northern Trust and its employees.

C. Motion Before the Court

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiff Bakewell filed a motion to quash the subpoenas served on the IRS and on the FTB. The motion arises from arguments that the information sought here is privileged under the tax privilege, that the information is not relevant, and that even if the information were relevant, the right to privacy overcomes the need for the discovery for various reasons.

On May 6, 2024, the Court held the final status conference in this action, at which time the Court advanced the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to May 21, 2024, and ordered that “[b]riefing [opposing or replying to Plaintiff’s motion] shall include any objections relating to documents sought to be subpoenaed to trial.”

On May 8, 2024, Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s motion. The opposition raises arguments responding to the moving papers’ points and attaches copies of the four subpoenas discussed above, which are attached as Exhibits F-I to counsel’s declaration. Consistent with the Court’s May 6, 2024, directives, the opposition discusses the Joseph, CPA, and the Northern Trust subpoenas.

On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff Bakewell filed a reply to Defendants’ opposition.

Plaintiff Bakewell’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Quash

            INITIAL NOTE: Not all social security numbers have been redacted from the Exhibits to the Opposition, such as virtually every page of Exhibit E. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 1.201.) ) Defendants’ counsel is ordered to immediately review the entire filing and provide a redacted version of the Opposition, plus Exhibits that complies with Rule of Court 1.201(a); once that is done the Court will delete the prior filing and issue a nunc pro tunc with the prior filing date for the newly redacted document.

A. Legal Standard

A motion to quash a deposition subpoena or deposition notice is used to strike, modify, or impose conditions on a subpoena or notice that is procedurally or substantively defective. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1987.1, subd. (a), 2025.410, subd. (c); see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 358, 365 (Catholic Mut.) [motion based on ground that subpoenas sought information outside scope of discovery]; John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186 [motion based in part on ground that subpoenas violated right of privacy]; McClatchy Newspapers v. Superior Court (1945) 26 Cal.2d 386, 391 (McClatchy) [motion based on ground that subpoenas were unreasonable and oppressive]; Far W. S&L Assn. v. McLaughlin (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 67, 71 [motion based on ground that subpoena was not properly served]; City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 883, 888 [procedural remedy for a defective subpoena is generally a motion to quash under § 1987.1].)

Such a motion may be made by:

(1) Any party to the action (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(1));

(2) The subpoenaed witness (e.g., a custodian of another person’s personal records) (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(2); Monarch Healthcare v. Superior Court (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1287-1288 (Monarch));

(3) A person (party or nonparty) whose consumer, governmental, or employment information or records have been subpoenaed (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (g) [party consumer], 1985.4 [state or local-agency employee or any other natural person], 1985.6, subd. (f)(1) [employee], 1987.1, subd. (b)(3) [consumer], 1987.1, subd. (b)(4) [employee]; see also Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1985.3, subd. (g), ¶ 2, 1985.6, subd. (f)(2) [nonparty consumer can make written objections instead of motion to quash]);

(4) A person whose “personally identifying information” within the meaning of Civ. Code § 1798.79.8, subd, (b), subpoenaed in connection with an action involving that person’s exercise of free-speech rights; and

(5) The Court, on its own motion, after giving the parties notice and the opportunity to be heard (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

A motion to quash may be based on various grounds, including that:

(1) The subpoena contains error or irregularities in the deposition notice (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subds. (a), (c));

(2) The subpoena seeks information that is not relevant to the issues in the case (see, e.g., Catholic Mut., supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 365 [motion to quash deposition subpoena because documents were outside the scope of discovery]; Slagle v. Superior Court (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1309, 1314-1315 [motion to quash trial subpoena because medical records were not relevant was properly overruled]; cf. Cadiz Land Co. v. Rail Cycle, L.P. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 74, 122-123 [motion to quash deposition notice because deposition would not lead to admissible evidence]);

(3) The subpoena makes unreasonable or oppressive demands for information (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a); see, e.g., McClatchy, supra, 26 Cal.2d at p. 391 [motion to quash deposition subpoenas because they were unreasonable and oppressive]);

(4) The disclosure of the records sought will violate the consumer’s constitutional right to privacy (see Cal. Const., art. I, § 1; see, e.g., Fett v. Medical Bd. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 211, 213 [petitions to quash to nonparties’ doctor because of privacy rights]; Manela v. Superior Court (2009) 117 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1150-1151 [motion to quash a subpoena to party’s doctor because of privacy rights]); and

(5) The disclosure of records will violate a privilege belonging to the consumer, the witness, or a person with whom the witness has a relationship that requires the witness to assert the privilege on the person’s behalf. (See, e.g., Monarch, supra, 78 Cal.App.4th at p. 1290 [objections to subpoena for business records based on trade-secret privilege]).

B. Court’s Determination

Despite reasonable arguments on both sides, the Court finds in favor of Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank, in part.

First, much of the information sought by Defendants is relevant. The information is relevant to the pleadings insofar as they allege that Plaintiff was not a sophisticated investor when he purchased OneUnited Bank stock. (FAC, ¶ 7; see Opp’n, Fox Decl., Ex. C, 26:4-15.) The information sought is relevant to whether Plaintiff Bakewell has received dividends from OneUnited Bank, a question to which Plaintiff has testified by stating that he does not recall and to which Plaintiff’s reply answers in the affirmative. (Opp’n, Fox Decl., Ex. C, 59:6 [“I don’t believe I ever received any dividends”], 59:1-6, 179:1-21, 208:13-17.) Last, the information sought has conceivably become relevant as impeachment evidence to highlight Plaintiff’s truthfulness or lack thereof.

Second, the Court finds that based on equal evidence going in both directions, the Court cannot grant the instant motion on the ground that Plaintiff Bakewell was never served with notices to the consumer regarding his consumer records held by the IRS, FTB, Joseph, CPA, and Northern Trust, the subpoenaed non-parties. (Compare Mot., pp. 7-8 [arguing no notice to consumer], Ivie Decl., ¶ 2 [purporting to attach two exhibits but failing to do so], with Opp’n, Fox Decl., Exs. F [Joseph, CPA subpoena], G [FTB subpoena], H [IRS subpoena], & I [Northern Trust subpoena] at SUBP-025, p. 2, Proof of Service of Notice to Consumer or Employee and Objection.)

Third, the Court accepts that the tax privilege applies to the requested information.

Fourth, the Court determines that at least some of Plaintiff’s tax information has been put at issue. A party can waive or diminish its right to privacy by raising a claim or defense in a suit that puts the personal information at issue. (See, e.g., John B. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1199 [by putting his own medical condition at issue, defendant substantially lowered expectation of privacy].) The personal information should be disclosed only if (1) it is directly relevant to the party’s claim or defense and (2) disclosure is essential to the fair resolution of the claim or defense. (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 859; Alch v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1425 [a compelling need for discovery exists where the information sought is directly relevant and essential to the fair resolution of the legal proceeding].) Here, as discussed above, the tax information is relevant in several ways, and disclosure is essential to a fair resolution of specific issues (e.g., whether Plaintiff was a sophisticated investor as alleged in the FAC and whether he received dividends, and if so, how much).

Fifth, as briefly summarized at the end of the preceding paragraph, the Court determines that there is a sufficient compelling need for the discovery here to overcome a privacy right held by Plaintiff in his financial records. Where a case involves an obvious invasion of a privacy interest that is fundamental, a compelling interest or need must be present to overcome the privacy interest. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 556.) Here, the Court finds a compelling need not only in the relevance of the discovery, but also in possible impeachment of Plaintiff Bakewell. Of course, before such evidence may be admitted at trial, there will need to be a showing that the documents are in fact impeaching as to some testimony given. Personally investing in stock is far different from having a broker who does the decision-making as to stock purchases.

Finally, while Defendants could potentially have obtained this information in other ways from other sources, including from Plaintiff himself, direct evidence of Plaintiff’s financial records appears to be necessary where Plaintiff has testified that he does not recall the information that may be gleaned from his financial records, and where Defendants are arguing that the substance of the claim of not being a sophisticated investor are false.

On the other hand, however, the records requested as set forth in the subpoenas attached to the Opposition are overbroad. As phrased, they invade privacy rights that have not been put at issue in this litigation. Specifically, the subpoenas request the entirety of the federal and state tax returns, even though there has only been argument set forth as to dividend payments from either OneUnited Bank or other entities. There has been no showing of the relevance of the rest of those tax returns, which also contain private information relating to Mr. Bakewell’s spouse.

Thus, the Court will DENY the motion to quash as it relates to evidence of dividends received by, reported by, or cashed by Danny Bakewell Sr., and it is otherwise GRANTED.

The Court DENIES the sanctions requested by Plaintiff and Defendants. While the Court denied the motion in large part, the importance of the right that Plaintiff Bakewell sought to protect by way of his motion is important to this litigation, as discussed above. To penalize Plaintiff under the circumstances discussed above would ignore the fact that Plaintiff presents a reasonable argument for why his deposition testimony framed him as not being able to recall certain information. Moreover, the documents requested were found by the Court to be overbroad. Overall, sanctions are not merited in either direction where both parties made reasonable arguments. 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr.’s Motion to Quash Subpoenas Duces Tecum and for Sanctions in the Amount of $3,360.00 Against Defendants Kevin Cohee and OneUnited Bank and Their Counsel of Record Parnell Fox, Esq. is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

The Court will address at oral argument a reasonable method for separating the evidence to be produced from the evidence protected. Moreover, the Court will consider a relevant protective order to protect Mr. Bakewell’s privacy even as to those documents that are produced.

Defendants Kevin Cohee and OneUnited Bank’s opposition request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

Defendants’ counsel is ordered to immediately review the entire Opposition and provide a redacted version of the Opposition, plus Exhibits that complies with Rule of Court 1.201(a).

ESTATE OF TAT KWOK, by and through his Successor in Interest, CONAIL KWOK; and CONAIL KWOK, individually,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

RAMON HEALTHCARE ASSOCIATES, INC. dba MISSION CARE CENTER, and DOES 1 through 25,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          20STCV25497

 Hearing Date:   5/22/24

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel [CRS# 8595]; and

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel [CRS# 7010].

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

On August 28, 2019, Conail Kwok—both as an individual and as the Successor in Interest of the Estate of Tat Kwok—and her sister Melissa Kwok Anderson filed a Complaint suing Defendants Ramon Healthcare Associates, Inc. (Mission Care Center; originally sued as Riverside Equities, LLC) and Does 1 through 25.

The Complaint alleged claims of (1) Elder Abuse and Neglect pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code sections 15600, et seq., (2) Violation of Resident Rights pursuant to Health and Safety Code section 1430, subdivision (b), and (3) Wrongful Death.

The claims arose from allegations that Tat Kwok died as a result of Mission Care Center’s failure to prevent a wound that contributed to his death on April 28, 2019.

On November 4, 2019, Plaintiffs Conail Kwok—both as an individual and as the Successor in Interest of the Estate of Tat Kwok—filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC). The FAC alleges the same claims and the same general allegations as the original Complaint but does not include Melissa Kwok Anderson as a plaintiff or defendant or mention Melissa in the pleadings in any way.

B. Dismissal, Reinstatement, and Abatement

On May 3, 2021, this action was dismissed after counsel for the Plaintiffs failed to appear for several scheduled Order to Show Cause (OSC) hearings relating to Plaintiffs’ search for counsel, with the caveat that “should Successor in Interest Plaintiff [Kwok] secure an attorney, Plaintiffs’ case [could] be reinstated.”

On March 21, 2022, the Court granted an opposed motion by Plaintiffs Conail Kwok to vacate the dismissal and reinstate this action.

On March 8, 2023, the Court granted an unopposed motion by Mission Care Center to abate this action pending Melissa Kwok Anderson’s joinder to this action, based on authority holding that all heirs must be added to a wrongful death action, and the action abated until those heirs are joined.

On August 2, 2023, at a status conference regarding alternative dispute resolution (ADR), Plaintiffs’ counsel represented that Plaintiff had identified the heirs and intended to amend the pleadings to add those heirs.

On August 31, 2023, Mission Care Center filed an objection to expert discovery based on Plaintiffs Conail Kwok’s failure to add the remaining heirs to this action.

On September 28, 2023, at the final status conference and a further status conference regarding ADR, Plaintiffs’ counsel informed the Court that more time was needed to add heirs to this action.

On December 20, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel confirmed there was no service on the heirs and therefore, the Court abated this action. The Court noted that the matter will be deemed completed and will be dismissed pursuant to the Five-Year Rule on the next hearing date of August 29, 2024 absent further order of the Court.

On December 21, 2023, Mission Care Center served a notice of ruling for the December 20th order.

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiffs Tat Kwok, through his successor in interest Conail Kwok, and Conail Kwok individually, filed a motion for leave to file a second amended complaint (SAC), set to be heard on July 1, 2024, to add nominal defendants Ada Kwok and Melissa Kwok Anderson.

C. Motion Before the Court

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel Jorge Ledezma filed motions to be relieved as counsel for Conail Kwok in both her capacities.

The April 11th motion attaches a proof of service showing service of the motions on Plaintiffs Conail Kwok.

On April 17, 2024, Jorge Ledezma filed a proof of service reflecting service of his motions on Defendant Mission Care Center.

That same day, Jorge Ledezma filed an amended proof of service relating to Mission Care Center.

As of this date, Jorge Ledezma’s motion are unopposed.

Jorge Ledezma’s motions are now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Relieve Counsel: GRANTED.

A. Legal Standard

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel ([without client consent pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section] 284, subd. (2)) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’” (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)

For an attorney to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with the requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subdivisions (a) and (c) to (e). These subdivisions require that an attorney file and show proof of service of (1) a notice and motion to be relived as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-051, (2) a declaration in support of the motion to be relived as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-052, and (3) a proposed order in support of the motion to be relieved as counsel on Judicial Council form MC-053.

The court should consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.” (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.) Moreover, the motion should also be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)

B. Court’s Determination

The Court GRANTS both motions.

First, Plaintiffs’ counsel Jorge Ledezma complied with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 within 16 court days of hearing, plus additional days to respond based on manner of service. Counsel Ledezma has filed proofs of service reflecting service of all three applicable forms on the parties that have appeared in this case. And a review of the three Judicial Council forms for each Plaintiff shows that the forms are complete and properly filled out.

Second, there is a valid reason for withdrawal: “An irreparable breakdown of attorney-client relationship has occurred that makes it impossible for attorney to represent this client.”

Third, because there is no trial date in this action, and because Plaintiffs Conail Kwok and Defendant Mission Care Center have not filed oppositions to this motion, no prejudice or delay arises here as to deny Jorge Ledezma’s motions. The Court may take additional steps as needed to protect against prejudice, rather than denying outright the relief sought. 

III. Conclusion

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel [CRS# 8595] is GRANTED.

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel [CRS# 7010] is GRANTED.

Superior Court of California

MECCA MORGAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

Alpha Beta Company dba Ralphs; The Kroger Company; Dan Cronin; and Does 1 THROUGH 10, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV23749

 Hearing Date:   5/21/24

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Mecca Morgan’s Motion to Strike Affirmative Defenses.

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Mecca Morgan sues Defendants Alpha Beta Company dba Ralphs (ABC), the Kroger Company (Kroger), Dan Cronin, and Does 1 through 10 pursuant to a January 31, 2024, First Amended Complaint alleging claims of (1) Discrimination, (2) Battery, (3) Negligent Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (4) Violation of California Civil Code Section 52.1, Interference with the Exercise of Civil Rights.

The claims arise from the following allegations. While visiting a Ralphs store as a customer, Plaintiff Mecca Morgan, an African American woman, was discriminated against by store employees. Specifically, Plaintiff Morgan was stopped inside the store by a Ralphs employee and then a security guard that accused Plaintiff of stealing, demanding proof of purchase. Plaintiff Morgan confirmed that she had a receipt but protested that she was not going to let the employees violate her rights, recording the incident as it occurred. Plaintiff Morgan attempted several times to use an exit to leave the store but had her path blocked by the store’s security guard. As a result, Plaintiff Morgan walked to a different exit in the store to leave the premises. However, as Plaintiff attempted to exit the store, she was attacked by the store manager, Ralph Cronin. The pleadings do not describe the alleged battery other than to generally describe offensive and unwelcome touching. These experiences have resulted in economic and non-economic damages, including affecting Plaintiff’s home broker/agent profession and income.

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a proof of service reflecting a February 5, 2024, service of the FAC on counsel for ABC by regular and electronic mail.

On March 1, 2024, ABC filed its Answer to the FAC.

B. Motion Before the Court

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike affirmative defenses from ABC’s Answer. The motion arises from various arguments challenging the sufficiency of the streamlined defenses appearing in ABC’s Answer.

On May 8, 2024, ABC filed an opposition to Plaintiff Morgan’s motion. The opposition was filed twice. The first copy of the opposition attached a proof of service showing service on a person other than Plaintiff Morgan. The second copy of the opposition is identical to first opposition except that it: (1) capitalized Defendants’ names in the caption; and (2) attached the correct proof of service.

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to ABC’s opposition.

On May 17, 2024, two court days prior to this hearing, Plaintiff Morgan filed a Second Amended Complaint that has been received but not filed by the Clerk.

ABC’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Sua Sponte Order Striking Second Amended Complaint

A. Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper, (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading, or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)

For the purposes of a motion to strike pursuant to Sections 435 to 437 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the term “pleading” generally means a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint, (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (a)), and an immaterial allegation or irrelevant matter in a pleading entails (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subds. (b)(1)-(3), (c)).

B. Analysis

“A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. A party may amend the pleading after the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike, upon stipulation by the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).)

Here, Plaintiff filed a proposed Second Amended Complaint (SAC) on May 17, 2024. Plaintiff Morgan did not obtain a Court order that allowed her to file the amended pleading. Neither does the proposed Second Amended Complaint nor the record show that the parties agreed to permit Plaintiff Morgan to file an amended pleading. And ABC had answered the FAC as of March 1, 2024, well before Plaintiff Morgan filed her proposed SAC. Plaintiff Morgan therefore did not have the right to file an amended pleading as to ABC without first filing a motion for leave to amend. At most, Plaintiff Morgan could have amended the pleadings as to any Defendant that has not answered the FAC. (Cf. Barton v. Kahn (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1219-1221 [Interpreting Code Civ. Proc., § 473 and holding that one defendant’s filing of an answer did not divest the plaintiff of the right to amend the complaint with respect to the causes of action brought against other defendants who had demurred to the same pleading].) Yet, a comparison of the proposed SAC and the operative FAC shows that the proposed SAC does not contain any new allegations, let alone new allegations against ABC’s co-Defendants. The only differences between the proposed SAC and the operative FAC are that the proposed SAC adds two things: (1) the proposed SAC identically states the General Allegations, Causes of Action, and Prayer twice, with the General Allegations, Causes of Action, and Prayer from the FAC being identical; and (2) the proposed SAC attaches the summons and civil cover sheet for this case, which were not attached to the FAC. It is thus not clear why Plaintiff filed the proposed SAC if she did not amend the substance of the pleadings in any consequential fashion.

Accordingly, because the proposed SAC was not filed as a matter of right, it is a pleading that was not filed in conformity with the laws of California, for which reason the Court STRIKES the May 17, 2024, Second Amended Complaint. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b).)

 

III. Plaintiff Morgan’s Motion to Strike: DENIED.

B. Legal Standard and Court’s Determination

Referring to the legal standard on motions to strike set forth above, the Court finds in favor of Defendant ABC.

The Court begins by noting that even if Plaintiff’s motion is two days late, as argued out by the Defendant ABC, the Court may and will consider the motion on the merits.

First, Plaintiff points out that she filed the motion on the Tuesday following Cesar Chavez Day, and thus the motion is not late at all. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 1.10.) Second, even if it were, the 30-day requirement is not mandatory, where the words ‘may’ file a motion to strike connotes a permissive standard. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); cf. McAllister v. County of Monterrey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 280 (McAllister) [applying above permissive reasoning to a late demurrer].)

Second, case law holds that “[t]here is no absolute right to have a pleading stricken for lack of timeliness in filing where no question of jurisdiction is involved, and where … the late filing was a mere irregularity,” such that “the granting or denial of the motion is a matter [that] lies within the discretion of the court.” (McAllister, supra, at pp. 281-282, citations omitted.)

However, the Court next determines that the permissive standard in interpreting the sufficiency of answers make the Answer here sufficient for pleading purposes.

The California Supreme Court has discussed the sufficiency of answers and explained that where a defect in pleading does not affect substantial rights, it “is to be disregarded.” (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240 (Harris).) In Harris, the California Supreme Court determined that the City’s answer was sufficient where it pleaded that “‘[a]ny alleged adverse employment actions of which plaintiff complains … were not based on plaintiff’s gender and/or sex, pregnancy or any other alleged discriminatory practice, but instead were based on one or more legitimate nondiscriminatory reasons. Nor were any of the employment actions of defendant taken under pretext.” (Ibid.) The Supreme Court reasoned that this answer had “put Harris on notice that the City intended to defend on the basis that it had not discriminated against her and had a legitimate reason for discharging her.” (Ibid.)

Here, the affirmative defenses raised in ABC’s Answer are comparable to the above quoted affirmative defense from Harris. Moreover, the defenses are clear enough to put Plaintiff on notice of the defenses to be raised by ABC. And even if some defenses are inconsistent with other defenses, this is not a ground to grant Plaintiff’s motion. (Cf. South Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 733-734 [For purposes of a demurrer, each defense in the answer must be considered separately without regard to any other defense, and thus, a separately stated defense that is sufficient in form and substance when viewed in isolation does not become insufficient when, on looking at the answer as a whole, that defense appears inconsistent with other parts of the answer].)

As a result, following Harris’s instruction, the Court determines that the Answer here is sufficiently pleaded.

Plaintiff Morgan’s motion is thus DENIED. 

IV. Conclusion

Plaintiff Mecca Morgan’s Motion to Strike Affirmative Defenses is DENIED.
