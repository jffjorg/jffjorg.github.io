Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Department M                                                                                                                             Calendar No. 4  

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

John Doe 7091 v. El Segundo Unified School District, et al. 

23TRCV02265

1.      El Segundo Unified School District’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint  

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

El Segundo Unified School District’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is overruled.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint was filed on July 13, 2023, and the First Amended Complaint was filed on July 21, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. While a student at El Segundo Middle School, Plaintiff was groomed and sexually abused by a teacher beginning in 2001 through 2003. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Negligence; 2. Negligent Supervision and Retention; 3. Sexual Battery; 4. Sexual Harassment.

 

Meet and Confer

             

Defendant set forth a meet and confer declaration in sufficient compliance with CCP § 430.41.  (Decl. Rebecca L. Gerome.)  

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(a) and (c).

 

Demurrer


A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.)  In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  The Court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law.  (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.App.4th 634, 638.) Because a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint, the plaintiff must show that the complaint alleges facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action.  (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.)  Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer.  (C.C.P., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.App.4th 1112, 1126.)

 

Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  "Whether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer."  (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)  Under Code Civil Procedure § 430.10(f), a demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.”  Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

 

Defendant demurs to the first through second causes of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and uncertainty. CCP § 430.10(e)(f). Specifically, the demurrer is made “on the grounds that, to the extent that Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218"), which was enacted in 2019, purports to retroactively strip statutory government immunity from public entities, it violates Article XVI, section 6 of the California Constitution, which prohibits gifts of public funds.”  (Notice, page 2, lines 3-6.) Defendant also presents Perez v. Roe I (2006) 146 Cal.App.4th 171 for the proposition that the constitutionality of a statute may be properly raised on demurrer.

 

Preliminarily, the Court notes that nothing in CCP § 430.10 specifically mentions the constitutionality of a statute as grounds for demurrer.  In addition, the Court notes that in Perez there had already been a final judgment previously rendered against the plaintiffs.  Thus, the specific grounds raised by the demurrer was failure to state sufficient facts based on res judicata principles.  The reasoning provided was that the attempt to plead a new claim (which had previously been dismissed) based on a newly enacted statute was improper. The Court of Appeal held that the pleading failed to state sufficient facts because the new claim based on the new statute was an improper attempt to unravel a final judgment, and, to that extent, the statute was unconstitutional.  On the other hand, in this case, there is no final judgment upon which the instant Court can analyze the pleading pursuant to res judicata principles.  The causes of action pled here have not been previously alleged as far as this Court is aware.  In any event, as Plaintiff did not oppose the demurrer on this basis, the Court will proceed to analyze the substance of the arguments.

 

“Before suing a public entity, the plaintiff must present a timely written claim for damages to the entity. [. . .] Timely claim presentation is not merely a procedural requirement, but is, as this court long ago concluded, a condition precedent to plaintiff's maintaining an action against defendant[,] and thus an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. Complaints that do not allege facts demonstrating either that a claim was timely presented or that compliance with the claims statute is excused are subject to a general demurrer for not stating facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” Shirk v. Vista Unified School Dist. (2007) 42 Cal.4th 201, 208–209 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

Relevant exceptions to the requirement are provided in Gov. Code § 905 as follows: “(m) Claims made pursuant to Section 340.1 of the Code of Civil Procedure for the recovery of damages suffered as a result of childhood sexual assault. (p) The changes made to this section by the act that added this subdivision1 are retroactive and apply to any action commenced on or after the date of enactment of that act, and to any action filed before the date of enactment and still pending on that date, including any action or causes of action that would have been barred by the laws in effect before the date of enactment.”  Gov. Code § 905.  Thus, AB 218 was passed in 2018 eliminating the exemption for claims occurring after 2009 and stating that the changes are retroactive.

 

Cal. Const., art. XVI, § 6 states, in relevant part:

“The Legislature shall have no power to give or to lend, or to authorize the giving or lending, of the credit of the State, or of any county, city and county, city, township or other political corporation or subdivision of the State now existing, or that may be hereafter established, in aid of or to any person, association, or corporation, whether municipal or otherwise, or to pledge the credit thereof, in any manner whatever, for the payment of the liabilities of any individual, association, municipal or other corporation whatever nor shall it have power to make any gift or authorize the making of any gift, of any public money or thing of value to any individual, municipal or other corporation whatever[.]”

 

“It is well settled that the primary question to be considered in determining whether an appropriation of public funds is to be considered a gift is whether the funds are to be used for a public or private purpose. If they are to be used for a public purpose, they are not a gift within the meaning of this constitutional prohibition.”  Jordan v. California Dept. of Motor Vehicles (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 431, 450.

 

“It is no small matter for one branch of the government to annul the formal exercise by another and coordinate branch of power committed to the latter, and the courts should not and must not annul, as contrary to the constitution, a statute passed by the Legislature, unless it can be said of the statute that it positively and certainly is opposed to the constitution.”  Howard Jarvis Taxpayers Assn. v. Padilla (2016) 62 Cal. 4th 486, 520 (quoting Methodist Hosp. of Sacramento v. Saylor (1971) 5 Cal. 3d 685, 692).)  “If there is any doubt as to the Legislature’s power to act in any given case, the doubt should be resolved in favor of the Legislature’s action.” (Id. at 521.)


            Defendant argues that the legislature “cannot pass a law attempting to impose liability on a public entity for a past occurrence where there is no enforceable claim.” (Demurrer, page 7, lines 21-23.)  See, Heron v. Riley (1930) 209 Cal. 507; See, also, Powell v. Phelan (1903) 138 Cal. 271; See, also, Conlin v. Bd. of Supervisors (1893) 99 Cal. 17; See also Bourn v. Hart (1892) 93 Cal. 321.  Defendant contends that it cannot be disputed that prior to the passage of AB 218, Plaintiff did not have an enforceable claim because Plaintiff’s claim was based on conduct occurring prior to 2009.

 

Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.

 

Plaintiff’s claim was revived by AB 218. “Once a claim has lapsed (under the formerly applicable statute of limitations), revival of the claim is seen as a retroactive application of the law under an enlarged statute of limitations.  Lapsed claims will not be considered revived without express language of revival.”  Quarry v. Doe I (2012) 53 Cal.4th 945, 957.

 

The following passage in Coats v. New Haven Unified School District (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 415, 428 is instructive:

 

“The present case, of course, involves revival of a cause of action barred by a claim presentation requirement, not a statute of limitations. But we are aware of no reason the Legislature should be any less able to revive claims in this context, as it expressly did in Assembly Bill 218: “Notwithstanding any other provision of law, any claim for damages described in paragraphs (1) through (3), inclusive, of subdivision (a) that has not been litigated to finality and that would otherwise be barred as of January 1, 2020, because the applicable statute of limitations, claim presentation deadline, or any other time limit had expired, is revived, and these claims may be commenced within three years of January 1, 2020.” (§ 340.1, subd. (q), italics added.) [¶] The express inclusion of “claim presentation deadline[s]” in Assembly Bill 218 distinguishes it from the 2002 amendment to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1, which revived claims “that would otherwise be barred as of January 1, 2003, solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired....” (Stats. 2002, ch. 149, § 1, p. 752) That revival provision, our Supreme Court held, did not alter the bar imposed by a plaintiff's failure to file a claim with the public entity defendant: “‘[T]he government claim presentation deadline is not a statute of limitations. Had the Legislature intended to also revive in subdivision (c) the claim presentation deadline under the government claims statute, it could have easily said so. It did not.’” (Rubenstein, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 907, 221 Cal.Rptr.3d 761, 400 P.3d 372, quoting Shirk, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 213, 64 Cal.Rptr.3d 210, 164 P.3d 630.) In Assembly Bill 218, the Legislature made clear its intent to revive causes of action previously barred by government claims presentation requirements.”  Id. (emphasis in original).  

 

Thus, AB 218 does not constitute an unconstitutional gift of public funds because AB 218 does not necessarily impose liability for a past occurrence when there was no enforceable claim for that occurrence. Instead, AB 218 specifically provided for a revival of enforceable claims. “It is equally well settled that legislation reviving the statute of limitations on civil law claims does not violate constitutional principles.” Roman Catholic Bishop of Oakland v. Superior Court (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1155, 1161.

 

The following passage in Bickerdike v. State (1904)144 Cal. 681, 692 is instructive:

 

“This construction, it is urged, would render the law obnoxious to the provisions of section 31 of article IV of the constitution, prohibiting the legislature from making any gift of public money or thing of value, the contention being that a waiver of the defense of limitations is a gift within the meaning of such provisions. [¶] The statute of limitations does not, however, go to the substance of the right, but only to the remedy. When the statute has made the defense available to the debtor, his debt has not been extinguished. It still exists, and may be enforced against him unless he chooses to avail himself of the defense afforded by the statute and specially plead it. The payment of such a debt by the debtor is not a "gift," in any proper sense of the word, and there is nothing in the constitutional provision invoked that can be held to prohibit the legislature from paying these claims.”

 

In addition, the cases referred to by Defendant are unavailing.


            In Heron v. Riley (1930) 209 Cal. 507, the Court analyzed now repealed Civ. Code § 1714 1/2 which authorized the issuance of liability insurance to cover vehicles used by government employees.  The state controller declined to authorize payment for insurance premiums believing the statute to be unconstitutional under Cal. Const. art. IV § 24.  The California Supreme Court held that the statute was, in fact, constitutional. However, Defendant attempts to rely on the following dicta: “The legislature has not attempted to create a liability against the state for any past acts of negligence on the part of its officers, agents or employees -- something it could not do, and the doing of which would, in effect, be the making of a gift -- but has provided that 'hereafter' it shall be liable for certain things done which cause damage to its citizens, its liability to be first determined by an appropriate action at law.”  Id. at 517.  Again, as noted above, however, AB 218 does not create liability for past acts of negligence.  Instead, it specifically provided for a revival of existing claims.

 

In Powell v. Phelan (1903) 138 Cal. 271, Defendants, a city and county mayor and others, appealed a judgment granting a juror a writ of mandate to compel payment for his jury duty services.  The Court analyzed the Act of March, 1901, allowing for the payment of such services for those who had served since 1895.  The court ruled that the Act of March, 1901 violated Cal. Const. art. IV, § 31, which prohibited the legislature from making or authorizing gifts of public money.

 

In Conlin v. Board of Supervisors (1893) 99 Cal. 17, Defendant appealed a judgment mandating payment to a contractor pursuant to 1891 Cal. Stat. 513 which allowed for a contractor to obtain payment for work performed for the city.  The statute allowed payment when payment had not been made due to errors made by officials.  The Court held that Cal. Const. art. IV, § 31 prohibited the legislature from making gifts of public money. The Court held that the Act was a "gift" to the contractor, as defined in Civ. Code § 1146.

 

In Bourn v. Hart (1892) 93 Cal. 321, a state employee sought a writ of mandamus directing defendant members of the state board of examiners to allow the employee's claim against the state pursuant to a statute allowing for appropriation of funds to pay for a claim for personal injuries suffered while in the course and scope of employment.  The board demurred to the petition, arguing that the appropriation was unconstitutional under Cal. Const. art. IV, §§ 31, 32, which provided that the legislature had no power to make any gift of public money to any individual or to grant any extra compensation to a public officer or servant of the state after service had been performed.  The court sustained the board's demurrer and entered judgment in favor of the board.

 

Each of those cases did involve payment of funds, as a gift, based on claims that were previously not existent and unenforceable. Here, however, AB 218 does not operate in that manner but instead, as noted above, acts as a revival of such claims which the legislature is fully authorized to do.

 

Further, even assuming arguendo that any payment made to a plaintiff based on the amendments made by AB 218 is considered a “gift,” AB 218 would still be found to be constitutional.  “It is likewise settled that if a public purpose is served by the expenditure of public funds, the constitutional prohibition is not violated even though there may be incidental benefits to private persons.”  Schettler v. County of Santa Clara (1977) 74 Cal. App. 3d 990, 1003.  “The determination of what constitutes a public purpose is primarily a matter for the Legislature and will not be disturbed as long as it has a reasonable basis.” San Diego County Dept. of Soc. Servs. v. Superior Court (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 761, 766.

 

Here, while, of course, an ultimate payment of funds to the Plaintiff would constitute a benefit to Plaintiff, a significant public purpose was served by AB 218.  “In Assembly Bill 218, the Legislature has again attempted to balance the competing concerns of protecting public entities from stale claims and allowing victims of childhood sexual abuse to seek compensation. This time, the Legislature came to a different conclusion, with an express revival provision for claims against public entities as well as those against private defendants.” Coats v. New Haven Unified School District (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 415, 429.  The legislature has determined that a public purpose was served by allowing for the revival of previously stale claims because of the policy of protecting minors regarding their rights especially considering the serious impact of sexual abuse.  The legislature has deemed it appropriate to ensure that all victims of sexual abuse can have the ability to pursue their claims.  The instant Court cannot second guess this public policy since there is certainly a reasonable basis for this decision.

 

Thus, Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.  Defendant is ordered to file and serve an Answer within 10 days of this date.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
