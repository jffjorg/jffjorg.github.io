Hearing Date:                          March 15, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Vortex Alliance, Inc.

Responding Party:                  None

Demurrer to Complaint

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH FIVE DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

            On December 19, 2022, plaintiff Jack Chueng dba Three-C Co (self-represented) filed an unlawful detainer complaint against David Tran and Vortex Alliance based on a three-day notice to perform covenants or quit dated June 30, 2021 at the property at 14909 South Crenshaw Blvd., #201, Gardena, CA 90249.

            On March 3, 2023, the court granted defendant Vortex’s motion to quash service of summons and complaint.

            On May 10, 2023, the court granted defendant Vortex’s motion to quash service of summons and complaint.

            On May 30, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to correct Vortex Alliance to Vortex Alliance, Inc.

            On August 4, 2023, the court denied Vortex’s motion to quash service.

            On November 14, 2023, the court denied Vortex’s motion to quash service.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.  Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal. App. 2d 636, 643-44.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Vortex Alliance, Inc. demurs to the complaint on the grounds that plaintiff lacks standing and the complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

Defendant contends that plaintiff lacks standing because he failed to comply with the fictitious business name laws.  Defendant also points out that although plaintiff alleges that he attached a copy of the written agreement, it is not attached.  Defendant argues that the complaint fails to properly allege lawful service of the notice to quit.

There is no opposition.

The court rules as follows:  The allegations are uncertain as to whether plaintiff complied with the fictitious business name laws.  Bus. & Prof. Code §17910 states:  “Every person who regularly transacts business in this state for profit under a fictitious business name shall do all of the following:  (a) File a fictitious business name statement in accordance with this chapter not later than 40 days from the time the registrant commences to transact such business.”  Under paragraph 2.b., plaintiff marked the box that he has complied with the fictitious business name laws but states that he is doing business under the fictitious name of “Vortex Alliance.”  As alleged, Vortex Alliance is a defendant.  Plaintiff does not state that he has complied with such laws as to “dba Three-C Co.”

Plaintiff also failed to attach a copy of the written agreement at Exhibit 1 as he alleges at para. 6.e.

Plaintiff further failed to sufficiently allege that the three-day notice was served properly.  CCP §1162 states:  “(b) The notices required by Section 1161 may be served upon a commercial

tenant by any of the following methods: (1) By delivering a copy to the tenant personally.  (2) If he or she is absent from the commercial rental property, by leaving a copy with some person of suitable age and discretion at the property, and sending a copy through the mail addressed to the tenant at the address where the property is situated.  (3) If, at the time of attempted service, a person of suitable age or discretion is not found at the rental property through the exercise of reasonable diligence, then by affixing a copy in a conspicuous place on the property, and also sending a copy through the mail addressed to the tenant at the address where the property is situated.  Service upon a subtenant may be made in the same manner.”

Based on the allegations it appears that plaintiff attempted to comply with CCP §1162(2) but it is uncertain.  At para. 10.a., plaintiff alleges that the notice was served on the defendant by leaving a copy with “(name or description):  Employee a person of suitable age and discretion on 7/30/21” at defendant’s business and mailing a copy to defendant at defendant’s place of residence on 7/5/21 because defendant cannot be found at defendant’s residence or usual place of business.  “Employee” is neither a name nor a description.  Further, confusingly, plaintiff alleges that the 3-day notice was left at the business on July 20, 2021 but mailed on July 5, 2021 (although the notice is dated June 30, 2021 and plaintiff alleges that the notice expired on July 5, 2021 at par. 9.b); yet the proof of service on the 3-day notice (Exh. B) states that the notice was served on June 30, 2021 by delivering the notice to the business and mailed on July 1, 2021.

            Thus, the demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

ORDER

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 5 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

RULING

            The Second Amended Complaint received on November 20, 2023 is deemed filed.  The “Fourth Amended Complaint” received on December 4, 2023 is rejected.  The demurrer to “claim for negligent infliction of emotional distress and vicarious liability” is OVERRULED.

BACKGROUND

            On January 17, 2023, Kailer Lawrence (self-represented) filed a complaint against Mercury Insurance for (1) general negligence and (2) motor vehicle negligence.

            On March 13, 2023, plaintiff (self-represented) filed a FAC for (1) negligence, (2) general negligence, and (3) NIED.

On June 23, 2023, the court sustained without leave to amend Mercury Ins. Company’s demurrer and granted plaintiff’s request to amend the FAC to add the proper plaintiff driver.  The motion to strike was granted without leave to amend.

On November 20, 2023, plaintiff submitted a SAC against Abigail Margaret Wright and Katherine Wright for negligence.

On December 4, 2023, plaintiff submitted a “Fourth Amended Complaint.”

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendants Abigail Margaret Wright and Katherine Wright demur to plaintiff’s “claim alleging negligent infliction of emotional distress and vicarious liability” in the “Fourth Amended Complaint” on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action. 

As background, on June 23, 2023, the court sustained Mercury Insurance’s demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend but allowed plaintiff to amend the FAC to add the proper plaintiff driver as a defendant.  Plaintiff attempted to do so improperly via amendments numerous times.  She identified the amendments as “second amendment,” “third amendment,” and “fourth amendment,” which were denied.  Then on November 20, 2023, plaintiff submitted a Second Amended Complaint, which was “received” but not “filed” on ecourt.  In a minute order dated November 29, 2023, the court noted that “[p]laintiff makes reference to a Fourth Amended Complaint, but no complaint is on file” and that “Plaintiff may amend within 10 days if she wishes.”  The minute order references the FAC filed in March 2023 but does not indicate that plaintiff had submitted a SAC in November 2023.

As a procedural matter, as to the Second Amended Complaint received on November 20, 2023, the court deems it filed as of November 20, 2023.  The “Fourth Amended Complaint” received on December 4, 2023 is stricken.  As the SAC and the “Fourth Amended Complaint” appear to be identical, the court deems the demurrer and motion to strike as against the SAC.

            In reviewing the SAC, the court notes that there is only one cause of action being asserted against defendants – that of negligence.  Although there are allegations of negligent infliction of emotional distress and vicarious liability, plaintiff is not asserting separate causes of action as to either.  See CCP §430.10(e) (“The party against whom a complaint . . . has been filed may object, by demurrer . . . to the pleading on any one or more of the following grounds:  . . . (e) The pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.”).  Demurrers do not lie as to only parts of causes of action, where some valid claim is alleged but “must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained.”  Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal. App. 4th 97, 119.  Thus, the demurrer is improper as to allegations of negligent infliction of emotional distress and vicarious liability.

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

Motion to Strike

Defendants request that the court strike the prayer for exemplary and punitive damages.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”  “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.”  Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal. App. 4th 1253, 1255.

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

Civil Code §3294(b) states: “With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

A claim for punitive damages may not be based on conclusory allegations of oppression, fraud or malice, but instead must be based on factual allegations which support such a conclusion.  See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-42 (Court of Appeal issued a peremptory writ directing trial court to issue order striking plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages because “[t]he sole basis for seeking punitive damages are . . . conclusory allegations” which were “devoid of any factual assertions supporting a conclusion [defendants] acted with oppression, fraud, or malice”).

Defendants argue that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a claim for punitive damages because the allegations do not show oppression, fraud, or malice.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages as they do not rise to the level of oppression, fraud, or malice.  Also, “[i]nasmuch as Civil Code section 3294 requires as a prerequisite to the recovery of punitive damages that the defendant ‘has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,’ the cases have uniformly recognized that proof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.”  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87 (citations omitted).

            The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

The “Fourth Amended Complaint” received on December 4, 2023 is reordered stricken.

The demurrer to “claim for negligent infliction of emotional distress and vicarious liability” is OVERRULED.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to exemplary and punitive damages. 

Defendants are ordered to file an answer within 20 days to the SAC, which is the operative pleading.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.  (Plaintiff’s “Opposition” agrees that bifurcation is appropriate but addressed some issues related to the specifics of the manner in which the bifurcated trial would proceed.)

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The trial is bifurcated in two phases:  first phase is for the jury to determine liability and compensatory damages; and the second phase is for the jury to determine punitive damages.

BACKGROUND

On April 11, 2023, plaintiffs Robert Nantz, Jr., in and though his successor-in-interest Barbara Nantz and Barbara Nantz filed a complaint against Driftwood Healthcare Center, Driftwood Healthcare and Wellness Center, LLC dba Driftwood Healthcare Center, Citrus Wellness Centre, LLC, Rechnitz Citrus, GP, and Shlomo Rechnitz for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) violation of Health and Safety Code §1430, (4) willful misconduct, and (5) wrongful death.

On September 25, 2023, the parties stipulated to preference designation and the court ordered and set a preference trial date of March 13, 2024.  It states that the parties also stipulate that there shall be no continuance beyond 15 days from the trial set forth by the court.

On January 8, 2024, plaintiffs filed an amendment designating Rockport Administrative Services, LLC as Doe 1 and Pacific Healthcare Holdings, Inc. as Doe 2.

Trial is set for April 15, 2024.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP §598, “[t]he court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case . . . .”

Under CCP §1048(b), “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action . . . or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues.”

Under Civil Code §3295(d), “[t]he court shall, on application of any defendant, preclude the admission of evidence of that defendant’s profits or financial condition until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finds that a defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud in accordance with Section 3294.”  

DISCUSSION

Pursuant to CCP §§598 and 1048 and 598 and Civil Code §3295(d), defendants request that the court bifurcate the trial in to two phases:  (1) jury trial for liability and compensatory damages; and, if necessary, (2) jury to determine punitive damages.

Defendants argue that bifurcation would be in the interests of justice and judicial economy.  Defendants also argue that preclusion of defendants’ wealth for punitive damages is mandatory under Civil Code §3295(b).  Defendants contend that evidence of their profits and financial status is irrelevant to determining liability and that evidence of such is prejudicial, confusing, misleading, and will necessitate undue consumption of time.

In opposition, plaintiffs assert that they do not oppose bifurcation of the punitive damages phase from the liability phase. but that evidence of financial condition must be presented to the same trier of fact.  Plaintiffs also seek to have the court instruct the jury that there may be a second phase and to allow plaintiffs to voir dire the jurors on punitive damages.  (The issue of voir dire shall be addressed in the future.)

The court rules as follows:  In trials where punitive damages are claimed, bifurcation is mandatory on defendant’s motion:  No evidence of defendant’s wealth (e.g., assets, income, financial condition, etc.) is admissible “until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finding the defendant guilty of malice, oppression or fraud in accordance with Section 3294” (requiring clear and convincing evidence).  Civ. Code §3295(d); see CACI 3948, 3949; see also BAJI 14.72.1, 14.72.2.  “Upon a defendant’s motion, a trial must be bifurcated when a plaintiff seeks punitive damages.”  Westrec Marina Mgt., Inc. v. Jardine Ins. Brokers Orange County, Inc. (2000) 85 Cal. App. 4th 1042, 1050.  This avoids the risk that defendant’s financial condition might taint the jury's determination of the underlying liability case and the issues of “oppression, fraud or malice.”  Medo v. Superior Court (1988) 205 Cal. App. 3d 64, 67–68.  As to instructions to the jury and voir dire, plaintiffs’ request is premature and not encompassed within the herein bifurcation motion.

            The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.  The trial is bifurcated in two phases:  first phase is for the jury to determine liability and compensatory damages; and the second phase is for the jury to determine punitive damages.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendant 1580 Centinela Associates, LLC demurs to the Sixth Cause of Action for premises liability, Seventh Cause of Action for general negligence, and Eighth Cause of Action for general negligence – loss of consortium on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

In the complaint, plaintiffs allege that on August 7, 2021, they were on the subject property to eat dinner at the subject restaurant, Fresh Ethiopian Restaurant.  Plaintiffs were seated inside the Fresh Ethiopian Restaurant at 1580 Centinela Avenue, Unit 6, Inglewood, and it was after 2:00 a.m. and the restaurant defendants (Fresh Ethiopian Restaurant and co-owner Solomon Solomon) were still negligently and carelessly serving alcohol to patrons dining inside, including to defendants Ashanti Latrice Cole and Mario Rosado.  An argument began between other guests or patrons inside the restaurant, involving defendants.  The subject argument did not involve plaintiffs.  The argument continued to escalate as the night went on, and despite the same, the restaurant defendants continued to serve more alcohol, thereby increasing the volatility of the situation and the risk of harm to other patrons, including plaintiffs.  Once the subject argument got unreasonably and/or excessively loud and heated, the restaurant defendants decided to remove, expel, and/or remove all patrons and guests of the restaurant, including defendants, and eventually plaintiffs, and placing or forcing all restaurant patrons and guests into the parking lot of the subject premises at the same time.  Complaint, ¶23.

The complaint further alleges that plaintiffs began to head to their motor vehicle, which was parked in the subject parking lot, in order to head home.  There was another vehicle that was parked in tandem behind plaintiffs’ vehicle.  Defendants entered their motor vehicle and drove it at a high and/or unreasonable rate of speed, causing their vehicle to violently and forcefully collide into the tandem vehicle, causing the tandem vehicle to be pushed forward, crushing plaintiffs Justina Oderoha and Stephanie Oderoha between the tandem vehicle and plaintiffs’ vehicle.  Id., ¶24.  Plaintiffs Justina and Emesaha are married.  Id., ¶15.  Plaintiffs Justina, Stephanie, and Rebecca are sisters.  Id., ¶16.

            Sixth Cause of Action for premises liability

The elements for premises liability are 1. That defendant owned/leased/occupied/controlled the property; 2. That defendant was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; 3. That plaintiff was harmed; and 4. That defendant’s negligence was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  CACI No. 1000.  Defendant “was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property if:  1. A condition on the property created an unreasonable risk of harm; 2. Defendant knew or, through the exercise of reasonable care, should have known about it; and 3. Defendant failed to repair the condition, protect against harm from the condition, or give adequate warning of the condition.”  CACI No. 1003. 

Under this cause of action, plaintiffs allege that property defendant 1580 Centinela Associates, LLC negligently and carelessly owned, managed, operated, controlled, inspected, maintained, protected, cleaned, repaired, guarded, supervised, designed, engineered, constructed, regulated, renovated, monitored, and/or secured the property located at 1580 Centinela Avenue, Inglewood.  The restaurant defendants served patrons food and alcohol, even unlawfully after hours, for profit; that on or before the subject date, property owner defendants knew, or in the exercise of reasonable care should have known, that restaurant defendants continued to operate their restaurant and serve alcohol to guests and patrons after hours; that despite having knowledge of the foregoing, property owner defendants failed to and/or inadequately, improperly, insufficiently, and/or unreasonably warned, reprimanded, punished, stopped and/or evicted restaurant defendants before the subject date; that the restaurant defendants and/or property owner defendants knew, or in the exercise of reasonable care should have known that the more a person ingests alcohol, the more it may lower the person’s inhibitions, change their mood, make them volatile and aggressive, thereby potentially increasing the risk of harm to the individual and others.  Property owner defendants and/or restaurant defendants failed to have and/or had insufficient, inadequate, and/or substandard security and/or safety protocols, controls, and/or measures in place, despite there being a history of other incidents occurring on the subject property and/or at the subject restaurant, including fights, attacks, robberies, burglaries, carjackings, and/or assaults with guns and/or deadly weapons, which the property owner defendants and/or restaurant defendants knew or should have known about.  Complaint, ¶63.

The complaint further alleges that defendants Cole and Rosado chose to flee from the scene of the subject incident and that restaurant and property defendants did not take or use reasonable or adequate measures, steps, means and/or precautions to guide, control, manage, supervise, guard, protect and/or monitor vehicles and/or foot traffic on the subject premises and/or the parking lot, including the subject vehicle to make sure that motor vehicles and the public are kept safely away from each other, thereby increasing the risk of harm to others, including plaintiffs.  Id., ¶64.

Defendant argues that this cause of action (along with the Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action) fails because California law significantly limits third-party liability for alcohol-related accidents, citing to Civil Code §1714 and Bus. & Prof. Code §25602.  Thus, defendant contends, if the actual entity or individual which or who served alcohol is not considered the proximate cause for a subsequent injury “certainly the owner of the building cannot be considered the proximate cause for a subsequent injury.”  Defendant further argues that plaintiffs do not allege a “dangerous condition” of the parking lot.  Rather, defendant asserts, all that is alleged is that there were two vehicles parked, one in front of the other, and one of the vehicles was operated in such a manner as to strike plaintiffs who were standing between the two vehicles.  Defendant also contends that as to plaintiffs’ allegation that defendant “had a duty to control or monitor motor vehicles and foot traffic,” it is unclear from where this duty stems.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that they sufficiently alleged the elements.

The court rules as follows:  Civil Code §1714(b) states that the furnishing of alcoholic beverages “is not the proximate cause of injuries resulting from intoxication, but rather the consumption of alcoholic beverages is the proximate cause of injuries inflicted upon another by an intoxicated person.”  Bus. & Prof. Code §25602 states:  “No person who sells, furnishes, gives, or causes to be sold, furnished, or given away, any alcoholic beverage pursuant to subdivision (a) of this section shall be civilly liable to any injured person or the estate of such person for injuries inflicted on that person as a result of intoxication by the consumer of such alcoholic beverage.”  Thus, to the extent that liability against property owner is hinges on the furnishing of alcohol, the allegations are insufficient as to liability.  Further, the allegations are insufficient because although plaintiffs allege the existence of a “dangerous condition,” they do not allege the condition on the property that created an unreasonable risk of harm. 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Sixth Cause of Action.

            Seventh Cause of Action for general negligence

The Seventh Cause of Action alleges the same as under the Sixth Cause of Action.

Defendant argues that as a matter of law, it did not owe any duty to plaintiffs because as alleged defendants were arguing with other guests and that plaintiffs were not involved in the altercation and thus it was not foreseeable that plaintiffs may be subject to any harm.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that they sufficiently alleged the elements.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as to duty.  A duty of care exists when one person has a legal obligation to prevent harm to another person, such that breach of that obligation can give rise to liability.  Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal. 5th 204, 209.  The allegations are conclusory and do not support a legal obligation on behalf of the property owner to have prevented defendants from driving their car into another car that hit plaintiffs.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Seventh Cause of Action.

            Eighth Cause of Action for general negligence – loss of consortium

Under this cause of action, plaintiffs allege that as a result of defendants’ negligent acts and/or omissions, and due to the severe and permanent physical and bodily injuries suffered by plaintiff Justina, plaintiff Emesaha suffered a loss of consortium.  Complaint, ¶77.

Defendant contends that this claim must fail because it can only be brought by the spouse of an injured person, appears to be brought by all plaintiffs, and cannot be brought by Justina for her own physical injuries.  Defendant notes that the confusion stems from plaintiffs not stating the party asserting the cause of action, as required by Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.112.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that they sufficiently alleged the elements and that the cause of action is brought by plaintiff Emesaha and none of the other plaintiffs.

The court finds that plaintiffs did not comply with Rule 2.112 in identifying plaintiff Emesaha in the caption of the cause of action.  Further, as stated under the Seventh Cause of Action, the allegations are insufficient as to duty.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendant requests that the court strike plaintiffs’ claim for exemplary and punitive damages (prayer at para. 7), claim for prejudgment interest (prayer at para. 9), claim for economic damages in the form of “loss of use” (at 26:9, 33:17, 35:12, 36:3), claim for economic damages in the form of “diminution in value” (at 26:10, 23-24, 33:18, 34:3-4, 35:12, 36:3), and claim for hedonic damages for “impairment of the quality of life (at 36:1).

                        Punitive damages

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

Defendant argues that there are no allegations of malice, oppression, or fraud against moving defendants or sufficient against an entity defendant as required by Civil Code §3294(b).

In opposition, plaintiffs assert that they are not seeking exemplary and punitive damages as against moving defendants.

The court thus finds that the motion is moot as to punitive damages.  The amended pleading should make this clear by expressly so stating.

                        Economic damages/“loss of use”

            “Where a vehicle is damaged by the wrongful act of another, the owner is entitled to recover for the damage done to the vehicle and for the loss sustained by being deprived of its use. . . . [A]n owner’s recovery for being deprived of the use of a damaged vehicle is generally to be determined with reference to the period of time reasonably required for the making of repairs.”  Valencia v. Shell Oil Co. (1944) 23 Cal. 2d 840, 844 (citations omitted).

Defendant argues that plaintiffs do not identify any personal property owned by plaintiffs that was damaged or destroyed in the incident.

            In opposition, plaintiffs contend that the incident involves plaintiffs’ motor vehicle being struck and damaged.

            The court finds that a claim for “loss of use” is not improper.

                        Economic damages/“diminution in value”

            “’Where personal property is injured but not wholly destroyed, one rule is that the plaintiff may recover the depreciation in value (the measure being the difference between the value immediately before and after the injury), and compensation for the loss of use.’”  Hand Electronics, Inc. v. Snowline Joint Unified School Dist. (1994) 21 Cal. App. 862, 870 (citation omitted).

Defendant argues that plaintiffs do not identify any personal property owned by any plaintiff that was damaged or destroyed in the incident or allege any facts showing any “diminution in value” as to any property.

In opposition, plaintiffs contend that the incident involves plaintiffs’ motor vehicle being struck and damaged.

The court finds that a claim for “diminution in value” is not improper.

                        Prejudgment interest

            Defendant cites to Civil Code §3287, which states that a party is entitled to recover prejudgment interest where the damages are “certain” or “capable of being made certain by calculation” from the time the right to recover arises, in arguing that prejudgment interest is not allowed in a tort action because the amount of plaintiffs’ damages for personal injuries is unliquidated and not know to defendant.

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that it is not improper to request pre-judgment interest as permitted by California law and that plaintiffs’ past economic losses could be the basis for an award of prejudgment interest, subject to the jury or factfinder’s discretion.

            The court rules that requesting prejudgment interest is proper under Civil Code §3291, which states:  “In any action brought to recover damages for personal injury sustained by any person resulting from or occasioned by the tort of any other person, corporation, association, or partnership, whether by negligence or by willful intent of the other person, corporation, association, or partnership, and whether the injury was fatal or otherwise, it is lawful for the plaintiff in the complaint to claim interest on the damages alleged as provided in this section,” which references CCP §998 offers.”  Thus, the court finds, to the extent that Civil Code §3291 states that it is lawful for a plaintiff to claim interest as provided in the section, it is not improper to include a request for prejudgment interest in the complaint.

                        Hedonic damages/“impairment of the quality of life”

            Defendant argues that such “hedonic” damages are disallowed under California law, citing to Loth v. Truck-A-Way Corp. (1998) 60 Cal. App. 4th 757, 763.  Defendant further contends that plaintiffs’ claim is duplicative of their claim for general damages and thus they seek a prohibited “double recovery.”

            In opposition, plaintiffs contend that alleging both general damages and hedonic damages is not improper.

            The court rules as follows:  “In California, a pain and suffering award may include compensation for the plaintiff’s loss of enjoyment of life.  Loss of enjoyment of life, however, is only one component of a general damage award for pain and suffering.  It is not calculated as a separate award.”  Loth, supra, at 763.  “Separate instructions on pain and suffering and loss of enjoyment of life are prohibited because they could mislead a jury to award double damages for the same injury.”  Id. at 766.  “’One of the strongest arguments that has been advanced as a reason for not recognizing loss of enjoyment of life as a separate category of damages is that it duplicates or overlaps other categories of damages, such as permanent disability or pain and suffering.’”  Id. at 766-67 (citation omitted).  The complaint though properly requests general damages, including “impairment of quality of life,” and does not request “impairment of quality of life” in a separate category of damages or separate from general damages.

            Accordingly, the motion is MOOT as to exemplary and punitive damages and DENIED as to “loss of use,” “diminution in value,” prejudgment interest and “impairment of quality of life.”

ORDER

            The demurrer to the Sixth, Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action in the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  Any amended pleading shall expressly identify the defendants from whom exemplary damages are sought and those from whom such exemplary damages are not sought.

            The motion to strike is MOOT as to exemplary and punitive damages and DENIED as to “loss of use,” “diminution in value,” prejudgment interest and “impairment of quality of life.”

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
