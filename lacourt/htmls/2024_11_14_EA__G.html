Case Number: 24PSCV01297    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: G

Defendant Miguel Sahagun’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

 

Respondent: Plaintiff Lina Arboleda

 

Defendant Miguel Sahagun’s Motion to Strike Prayer for Punitive Damages and Attorneys Fees of the First Amended Complaint

 

Respondent: Plaintiff Lina Arboleda

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Miguel Sahagun’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED IN PART with twenty (20) days leave to amend as to the first and third causes of action and without leave to amend as to the second cause of action. Furthermore, the Demurrer is OVERRULED IN PART as to the fourth cause of action.

 

Defendant Miguel Sahagun’s Motion to Strike Prayer for Punitive Damages and Attorneys Fees of the First Amended Complaint is deemed MOOT.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action for conversion. On February 2, 2024, Defendant Miguel Sahagun obtained a default judgment in an unlawful detainer action against Plaintiff Lina Arboleda with regards to a property in El Monte. On February 21, 2024, Arboleda quit possession of the El Monte property following a lockout by the sheriff. Subsequently, Arboleda alleges Sahagun has refused to turn over the possession of Arboleda’s personal property that was left at the El Monte property. Instead, Sahagun has allegedly demanded Arboleda pay Sahagun money or sign a waiver of Arboleda’s right to sue.

 

On April 23, 2024, Arboleda filed a complaint against Sahagun and Does 1-15, alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1983 to 1987, (2) civil extortion, (3) trespass to conversion, and (4) trespass to chattels.

 

On August 27, 2024, Arboleda filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same defendants alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1983 to 1987, (2) attempted civil extortion, (3) trespass to conversion, and (4) trespass to chattels.

 

On September 30, 2024, Sahagun filed the present demurrer. On October 7, 2024, Sahagun filed the present motion to strike. On October 29, 2024, Sahagun’s counsel telephonically met and conferred with Arboleda’s counsel. (Nong Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

A hearing on the present motions is set for November 14, 2024, along with a case management conference.

 

ANALYSIS


Sahagun demurs to Arboleda’s entire FAC. For the following reasons, the court SUSTAINS Sahagun’s demurrer to Arboleda’s first and third­­ causes of action with leave to amend and to Arboleda’s second cause of action without leave to amend. The court also OVERRULES the demurrer as to the fourth cause of action.

 

Legal Standard


A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, at p. 747.)

 

Litigation Privilege


Sahagun first argues Arboleda’s second cause of action for attempted civil extortion, third cause of action for trespass to chattel, and fourth cause of action for conversion are barred as a matter of law by the litigation privilege. The court disagrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

“For well over a century,¿communications with ‘some relation’ to judicial proceedings have been absolutely immune from tort liability by the [litigation] privilege” set forth in¿Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b).¿(Rubin v. Green¿(1993) 4 Cal.4th 1187, 1193 (Rubin).) The privilege has “an expansive reach” (id.,¿at p. 1194) and “applies to any action except one for malicious prosecution” (Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. Bear Stearns & Co.¿(1990) 50 Cal.3d 1118, 1132). It also applies to prelitigation communications. (Rubin, supra, 4 Cal.4th at p. 1194-1195 [collecting cases].)

 

The litigation privilege bars liability for “any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that ha[s] some connection or logical relation to the action.” (Silberg v. Anderson¿(1990) 50 Cal.3d 205, 212 (Silberg).) Whether the privilege applies is a question of law. (Kashian v. Harriman¿(2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 892, 913.) And “[a]ny doubt about whether the privilege applies is resolved in favor of applying it.” (Ibid.) “The litigation privilege is interpreted broadly in order to further its principal purpose of affording litigants and witnesses the utmost freedom of access to the courts without fear of harassment in derivative tort actions.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 889.) It “is absolute and applies regardless of malice.” (Ibid.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, Arboleda’s causes of action for civil extortion, conversion, and trespass to chattels are based on a series of statements allegedly made by Sahagun. At an in-person meeting between Sahagun and Arboleda on February 27, 2024, Sahagun demanded $80,000 in exchange for returning Arboleda’s property. (FAC, ¶ 19-20.) Sahagun then reduced the demand to an amount between $18,000 and $10,000. (FAC, ¶ 20.) The alleged purpose of the demand was to compensate Sahagun for past due rent and damages that Arboleda allegedly owed. (FAC, ¶ 21.) Sahagun stated if Arboleda did not make the demanded payment, Sahagun would then file a lawsuit and destroy Arboleda’s property. (FAC, ¶ 22.) And on another occasion, Sahagun told Arboleda through a police officer that Sahagun would release Arboleda’s property if Arboleda first “would sign a document stating that she would not sue him for any reason relating to the property that was left behind after the eviction.” (FAC, ¶ 39.)

 

In arguing Sahagun’s communications were privileged, Sahagun claims they were akin to demand letters where Sahagun advised of the required payment for the return of the property and stated Sahagun’s lawful entitlement to payment of past due rent and reasonable storage fees. This argument, however, misstates the FAC’s allegations. The FAC does not allege Sahagun demanded payment of storage fees in exchange for the return of Arboleda’s property. Instead, it alleges Sahagun demanded the payment as compensation for past due rent and damages to the rental property. (FAC, ¶ 21, Ex. 5, ¶ 4.) Sahagun fails to point to any legal authority that would allow a landlord to retain a former tenant’s personal property until that tenant compensates the landlord for past damages and unpaid rent.

 

Furthermore, Sahagun also threatened to destroy Arboleda’s property if Sahagun did not receive payment. Sahagun fails to address how this threat was a privileged communication and how Sahagun had the lawful entitlement to make such threats. Similarly, Sahagun fails to address the allegation that Sahagun then premised the release of Arboleda’s property on Arboleda’s signing of a waiver. Sahagun fails to establish how this communication was privileged and how Sahagun had the lawful entitlement to retain a tenant’s personal property until they signed waivers of their right to sue Sahagun.

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Sahagun’s demurrer on this ground.

 

Breach of Code of Civil Procedure §§ 1983 to 1987 (First Cause of Action)

 

Sahagun contends Arboleda’s first cause of action for breach of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1983 to 1987 fails because these provisions do not exist. The court finds that this is not technically correct. While there is no Code of Civil Procedure section 1983, there are other existing code sections that fall within this range. (e.g., Code Civ. Proc., § 1985 et seq.) Nonetheless, these provisions are inapplicable here as they involve the use of subpoenas, not the disposal of tenant property. Instead, it appears to the court that the language quoted in the FAC comes from Civil Code section 1983, not Code of Civil Procedure section 1983. (FAC, ¶ 47-48.)

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Sahagun’s demurrer to this cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Attempted Civil Extortion (Second Cause of Action)

 

Sahagun maintains Arboleda’s second cause of action for attempted civil extortion fails to plead sufficient facts to state a claim. The court agrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

“However denominated (e.g., extortion, menace, duress), our Supreme Court has recognized a cause of action for the recovery of money obtained by the wrongful threat of criminal or civil prosecution. [Citations.] It is essentially a cause of action for moneys obtained by duress, a form of fraud.” (Fuhrman v. California Satellite Systems (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 408, 426 (Fuhrman), disapproved of on other grounds in Silberg, supra, 50 Cal.3d at p. 212-213.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, the FAC fails to allege how Sahagun obtained money or property from Arboleda by duress. And to the extent the FAC seeks to hold Sahagun liable for attempting to obtain money from Arboleda through duress, Arboleda fails to provide any authority that establishes a cause of action for attempted civil extortion. In fact, Fuhrman appears to suggest such a cause of action does not exist as it held a plaintiff could not state a cause of action for duress where the plaintiff never actually made paid the money that was demanded. (Id., at p. 426, 428.)

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Sahagun’s demurrer to this cause of action without leave to amend.

 

Trespass to Conversion (Third Cause of Action)

 

Sahagun argues Arboleda’s third cause of action for trespass to conversion fails to plead sufficient facts to state a claim. But while Sahagun’s demurrer appears to treat this as a cause of action for conversion, the court finds this cause of action to be uncertain as it is unclear if the FAC is alleging conversion or trespass to chattels. Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Sahagun’s demurrer to this cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Trespass to Chattels (Fourth Cause of Action)

 

Sahagun contends Arboleda’s fourth cause of action for trespass to chattels fails to plead sufficient facts to state a claim. The court disagrees.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Trespass to chattel . . . lies where an intentional interference with the possession of personal property has proximately caused injury.” (Thrifty-Tel, Inc. v. Bezenek (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1559, 1566.) It is considered the “little brother of conversion” as “[t]hough not amounting to conversion, the defendant's interference must, to be actionable, have caused some injury to the chattel or to the plaintiff’s rights in it.” (Intel Corp. v. Hamidi (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1342, 1350.) The plaintiff “may recover only the actual damages suffered by reason of the impairment of the property or the loss of its use.” (Zaslow v. Kroenert (1946) 29 Cal.2d 541, 551.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, Sahagun contends the FAC fails to sufficiently plead this cause of action because it only alleges Arboleda owned the property and that Sahagun substantially interfered with the property. But the FAC also alleges the essential element of damages by alleging Sahagun’s damaged their personal property. (FAC, ¶ 35, 72-73.) The FAC also alleges the loss of the property has resulted in lost business income. (FAC, ¶ 23-25, 74.) Thus, the court finds this cause of action is sufficiently pled.

 

Accordingly, the court OVERRULES Sahagun’s demurrer to this cause of action.

 

CONCLUSION


Based on the foregoing, Sahagun’s demurrer to Arboleda’s FAC is SUSTAINED IN PART with twenty (20) days leave to amend as to the first and third causes of action and without leave to amend as to the second cause of action. Furthermore, the demurrer is OVERRULED IN PART as to the fourth cause of action.

 

Based upon the recommendations made on the demurrer, Sahagun’s motion to strike is DEEMED MOOT.
