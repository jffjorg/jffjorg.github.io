BACKGROUND

 

On February 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Brittany Perrineau, Harold Perrineau, and Joon Productions, Inc. (“Plaintiffs”) filed the operative second amended complaint (“SAC”) against Defendants Patrick McKenna; Leslie Gornik; Palisades Funding, Inc.; Palisades Capital Management LLC; Palisades Development Company, Inc.; Pali Cap Management 9, LLC; Palisades Capital Fund 4, LLC; Palisades Capital Fund 8, LLC; and Palisades Capital Fund 7, LLC (“Defendants”), alleging eleven causes of action for (1) material misrepresentations and misleading statements in the sale of a security; (2) sale of unqualified securities; (3) joint and several liability pursuant to Corporations Code § 25504; (4) joint and several liability pursuant to Corporations Code § 25504.1; (5) breach of fiduciary duty; (6) violation of California Business and Professions Code §§ 17200; (7) constructive fraud; (8) breach of written contract; (9) breach of written contract; (10) common counts; and (11) declaratory relief. 

 

Plaintiffs now move for summary adjudication on the following issues:

 

Issue One: Plaintiffs are entitled to summary adjudication on the 8th Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint because the Undisputed Material Facts (“UMFs”) set forth in Plaintiffs Separate Statement demonstrate, as a matter of law, that there is no defense to Plaintiffs’ Second Cause of Action against Defendants for their failure to pay, despite demand therefor, all amounts due under the two Promissory Notes held by Plaintiffs in the principal sums of $75,000.00 and $150,000.00 (the “Notes”).

 

Issue Two: That as of the hearing date on the instant Motion for Summary Adjudication on the 8th Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint the total balance due on both Notes, after giving credit for all payments made, is $366,375.00, plus attorney fees and costs of collection, and that interest continues to accrue at the rate of $3,281.25 per month until payment is made.

 

            Defendants oppose the motion and Plaintiffs reply.

LEGAL STANDARDS – MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.” ¿(Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (hereafter Aguilar).) ¿“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.” ¿(Ibid.; Smith v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1474 [summary judgment standards held by Aguilar apply to summary adjudication motions].) 

 

Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.  Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th. at p. 840 [cleaned up]; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty if that party contends that there is no merit to the cause of action, defense, or claim for damages, or if the party contends that there is no duty owed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)  A cause of action has no merit if: (1) one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded, or (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (n); Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 583.)  Once the defendant has shown that a cause of action has no merit, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(2); Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 583.)  Additionally, in line with Aguilar, “[o]n a motion for summary adjudication, the trial court has no discretion to exercise.  If a triable issue of material fact exists as to the challenged causes of action, the motion must be denied. If there is no triable issue of fact, the motion must be granted.”  (Fisherman's Wharf Bay Cruise Corp. v. Superior Court (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 309, 320.) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

1.     Issue 1: Defendants’ Liability on the 8th Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs seek summary adjudication of the eighth cause of action, on the grounds that two separate promissory notes signed by Defendant Palisades Capital Fund 7 (“PCF7”), for $75,000 and $150,000, were defaulted upon.

 

The promissory note for the $75,000 loan was effective June 27, 2019, and provided for monthly payments of $1,041.67, representing only the 12.5% annual interest on the loan for the nine months from the effective date to the maturity date of May 1, 2020[1] when the entire $75,000 principal balance would come due.  (Ex. 2 at sections 1.1-1.6.) 

 

The promissory note for the $150,000 loan was effective August 19, 2019, and provided for monthly payments of $1,500, representing only the 12.5% annual interest on the loan from the effective date to the maturity date of May 1, 2020 when the entire $150,000 principal balance would come due.  (Ex. 5 at sections 1.1-1.6.)

 

Plaintiffs contend that Defendants paid the monthly interest-only payments until April 2020, after which Defendants failed to make any additional payments.  (UMF 10-11.)  The property was sold in October 2021 for less than anticipated, after which Defendants paid Plaintiffs $24,000. 

 

Defendants argue that only PCF7 signed the promissory notes, and although Plaintiffs argue that Defendants are alter egos of each other, Plaintiffs have provided no evidence that they are alter egos, and in fact, despite the notice of motion indicating Plaintiffs seek summary adjudication against “Defendants” the body of the motion concedes, “However, for the purposes of this Motion, Plaintiffs only seek an adjudication of the sums Palisades Capital Fund 7, LLC owes Plaintiffs under the 8th Cause of Action in the Second Amended Complaint.”  (Motion at p. 7.) 

 

The Court agrees that Plaintiffs have not provided evidence demonstrating that Defendants are alter egos of each other, and therefore summary adjudication is not appropriate as to Issue 1.    

 

2.     Issue 2: Outstanding Amount Owed

 

As set forth above, the Court has sustained the evidentiary objections advanced by Defendants regarding the Stein Declaration, paragraph 5, and Exhibit 15 for insufficient foundation for the calculations regarding the balance due under both promissory notes.

 

Moreover, Defendants argue that the simple interest calculation provided by Plaintiff’s counsel is inadmissible because counsel is not a qualified expert to opine on the interest rate calculation damages.  Plaintiffs counter in reply that these are simple interest calculations that do not require an expert, and Defendants have not pointed out any errors in the calculation.

 

In determining whether Plaintiffs have set forth sufficient foundation for the interest calculations, the Court first analyzed the appropriateness of applying the $24,000 October 2021 payment to the outstanding interest balance only, or whether applying some or all of it to the principal balance is appropriate.

 

Both notes provide for a default interest rate of 17.5%, and a late fee of 5% due for each payment not made by the 5th of each month.  (Exs. 2 & 5 at section 2.1.)  Plaintiffs argue that, pursuant to the terms of the promissory notes, Plaintiffs are entitled to recover the default annual interest rate of 17.5%, from the maturity date, until the full amount has been paid in full, plus a 5% late charge for every delinquent payment, minus the $24,000 payment that was made in October 2021, which Plaintiffs have deducted from the outstanding interest only, and not the principal balance. 

           

            With regard to application of the $24,000 payment, the promissory notes provide:

 

1.4  Application of Payments.  If at any time Lender receives, from Borrower or otherwise, any amount appliable to the Indebtedness which is less than all amounts due and payable at such time, Lender may apply the amount received to amounts then due and payable in any manner and in any order determined by Lender in their discretion.  Borrower agrees that neither Lender’s acceptance of a payment from Borrower in an amount that is less than all amounts then due and payable nor Lender’s application of such payment shall constitute or be deemed to constitute either a waiver of the unpaid amounts or an accord and satisfaction.

 

(Exs. 2 & 5 at section 1.4.)

 

As of October 1, 2021, the principal balances totaling $225,000 ($75,000 + $150,000) were overdue by 18 months.  Therefore, the 17.5% default interest due as of October 1, 2021 was $59,062.50 (17.5% annual interest rate * 1.5 years * $225,000 outstanding principal balance.)  As such, the $24,000 payment, applied to the outstanding interest first, did not reach any of the outstanding principal balance.

 

Therefore, the Court agrees that in general, it was proper for Plaintiffs to deduct the $24,000 payment from the outstanding interest owed as of October 1, 2021, it did not reduce the principal balance, and therefore the interest is capable of being calculated by way of simple arithmetic.

 

Notwithstanding, the Court finds errors with Plaintiffs’ counsel’s calculation.  In particular, counsel treats each month after May 2020 as a day “payment is due” and enhances the monthly default interest (calculated at an annual rate of 17.5, prorated monthly), by an additional 5% each month. 

 

The promissory notes provide:

 

(b) Late Payment Fee.  Borrower hereby acknowledges and agrees that Lender will incur additional costs in connection with the processing of any payment that is not paid when due (including the payment of all outstanding Principal Balance and accrued interest on the Maturity Date) which additional costs are difficult to ascertain.  Borrower further agrees that a reasonable estimate of such costs and expenses is five percent (5.0%) of the amount of the delinquent payment (including the payment of all outstanding Principal Balance and interest on the Maturity Date) which fee shall become due and payable on the fifth (5th) calendar day after a payment is due.

 

(Exs. 2 & 5 at section 2.1.)

 

Therefore, by the terms of the notes, the full payment of the loans were due on May 1, 2020.  While Plaintiffs may be entitled to a 5% late fee on the entire principal balance not paid as of May 1, 2020, there is nothing in the agreement providing that Plaintiffs may continue to charge an additional 5% on the pro-rated 17.5% default interest rate each month after May 2020 that the principal remains unpaid. In other words, pursuant to the terms of the notes, May 1, 2020 was the last day a payment became due, to which the 5% late fee provision may apply.

 

As such, the Court denies summary adjudication as to Issue 2.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Because Issue 1 is drafted with respect to all Defendants, yet Plaintiffs have provided no evidence demonstrating that Defendants are alter egos of each other, and because the Court finds insufficient foundation for Plaintiff’s counsel’s calculations regarding the balance due on both promissory notes, the Court denies summary adjudication as to both Issues 1 and 2. 

 

Plaintiffs shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service.
