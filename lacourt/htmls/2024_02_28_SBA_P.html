DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV01505    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: P

Superior Court of California County of Los Angeles

Wilfredo Cardenas, Plaintiff, Case No.: 23TRCV01505

vs. [Tentative] Ruling on Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Written Discovery

11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC,

et al. Defendants.

Hearing Date: February 28, 2024

Department P, Judge David K Reinert

(1) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One

(2) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One

(3) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set One

(4) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One

Moving Party: Plaintiff Wilfredo Cardenas

Responding Party: None.

Ruling: The motion to compel further responses is ruled on as follows:

Form Interrogatories — GRANTED as to nos. 4.1 and 12.1.

Special Interrogatories — GRANTED as to nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, and 42.

Requests for Production — GRANTED as to nos. 7, 8, 9, and 21.

Requests for Admission — GRANTED as to nos. 3, 4, and 9.

Plaintiff is ordered to pay three extra filing fees to the Court within 15 days.

Defendant is ordered to pay sanctions to Plaintiff in the amount of $1,898.40 to be paid within 30 days.

Moving party is to give notice.

The Court considered the moving papers.

BACKGROUND

On May 11, 2023, Plaintiff Wilfredo Cardenas (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant 11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC (“Defendant”) for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability/Tenantability; (3) Breach of Implied Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment; (4) Negligence; (5) Nuisance; and (6) Retaliatory Acts (Violation of California Civil Code section 1942.5).

On January 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motions to compel further responses.

To date, no opposition has been filed.

Multiple Motions Filed As One

The Court finds that Plaintiff has improperly filed four motions to compel further discovery as one motion. Plaintiff seeks an order compelling further responses to four sets of different discovery. Accordingly, Plaintiff is ORDERED to pay three extra filing fees to the Court for the motions.

Motion to Compel Further Responses

Under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, subdivision (a), Section 2031.310, and

2033.290 subdivision (a), parties may move for a further response to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, or requests for admission where an answer to the requests are evasive or incomplete or where an objection is without merit or too general. A motion to compel further response to requests for production has an additional requirement to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(1).)

Notice of motions to compel further responses to discovery must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c), 2031.310, subd. (c), 2033.290, subd. (c).) The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b), 2031.310, subd. (b), 2033.290, subd. (b).) California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 (a)(3)).

Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification, or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (h), 2030.300, subd. (d); 2033.290, subd. (d).)

Plaintiff requests that the Court compel Defendant to respond further to form interrogatories nos. 4.1, 12.1; special interrogatories nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, 42; requests for production nos. 7, 8, 9, 21; and requests for admission nos. 3, 4, 9.

Meet and Confer

The Court finds the meet and confer requirement to be satisfied based on Joshua Greer’s declaration, wherein he states that he sent a meet and confer letter via email to Defendant’s counsel on October 2, 2023, detailing the issues with the discovery responses. (Greer Decl. ¶ 4.) Greer also states that on October 10, 2023, the parties engaged in a teleconference and discussed the outstanding discovery issues at length. (Id. ¶ 5.) The parties then attended an IDC with the Court on November 21, 2023, where Defendant agreed to provide supplemental responses, with a deadline on January 19, 2024. (Id. ¶ 6.) The Court and Counsel also stipulated to keep all remaining hearing dates on calendar. (See November 21, 2023 Minute Order.)

Form Interrogatories

Plaintiff seeks further responses to interrogatories nos. 4.1, 12.1. The form interrogatories at issue seek information regarding any applicable policy of insurance and the name, address, and telephone number for relevant individuals. Defendant’s responses consist of objections and incomplete information.

As to FROG no. 4.1, Defendant claims that the information sought is protected by attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine, but it fails to explain how. The Court finds that identification of any relevant and applicable insurance policies that might apply are subject to response.

As to FROG no. 12.1, Defendant has not included all the information sought as there are omitted persons including property managers, neighbors, vendors, code enforcement officers and handymen. Plaintiff states that at the IDC, the parties discussed the extensive amount of maintenance information that Defendant was required to provide, yet the response was not supplemented.

Thus, further responses are ordered as to FROGS nos. 4.1 and 12.1.

Special Interrogatories

Plaintiff seeks further responses to special interrogatories nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, and 42. The special interrogatories at issue seek information regarding the amount of rent, fees, and other costs Defendant received from Plaintiff during the lease period for leasing the rental unit; the identity of all property managers for the property during the lease period, any communications with a resident of the property regarding a claim of mold at the property (specifically against unit #104 and #107) from January 1, 2021 to present; details of maintenance performed in the rental unit during the lease period; and any notifications by Plaintiff of any health conditions suffered at the premises.

As to SPROG nos. 4 and 5, the Court finds Defendant’s objections to be without merit. The information of how much rent, fees, and other costs Defendant received is relevant to the issue of damages, and Defendant fails to show how this information is private and confidential, protected by attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine.

As to SPROG no. 6, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to a response about the identity of individuals managing the property. Defendant fails to show that the objections are valid.

As to SPROG nos. 9, 15, and 42, the Court finds Plaintiff’s objections to be without merit. The Court agrees with Plaintiff in that the discovery request involves questions about issues with mold at the property and its, which is likely to lead to admissible testimony and evidence. Further, this discovery request is narrowly tailored to a time period and specific unit numbers, and specific rental unit for no. 15. Also, the request as to Plaintiff’s notifications regarding health conditions goes to the reasonableness of the response times, the issue of punitive damages, and damages calculations. Thus, these requests are relevant and reasonably tailored to the issues at hand.

Thus, further responses are ordered as to SPROGS nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, and 42.

Requests for Production

Plaintiff seeks further responses to requests for production nos. 7, 8, 9, and 21. The requests for production seek information regarding communications between Defendant and property managers and J.K. Residential Services Inc., relating to the management and maintenance rental unit during the lease period; and documents relating to any water leak at the rental unit from January 2016 to the present. The Court finds these requests to be relevant to the issues in this case or likely to lead to admissible testimony and evidence. Defendant’s objections are as follows: vague, ambiguous, not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, legal conclusion, premature disclosure of expert information, disclosure of private and confidential business information, and protected by attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine. The Court finds these objections to be without merit. Plaintiff is entitled to further responses.

Thus, further responses are ordered as to requests for production nos. 7, 8, 9, and 21.

Request for Admissions

Plaintiff seeks further responses to requests for admission nos. 3, 4, and 9. The requests for admissions seek Defendant to admit that it had a duty to keep the premises habitable; a duty to ensure quiet enjoyment of premises for all tenants; and that no maintenance was performed on the roof of the rental unit between January 7, 2023 and February 5, 2023. Defendant fails to respond to these requests, and instead provides objections, nearly identical to those in the requests for production and the other discovery requests. However, the Court finds that these discovery requests are relevant to the issues in this action and seeks a straightforward legal question. Plaintiff is entitled to a yes or no response.

Thus, further responses are ordered as to requests for admission nos. 3, 4, and 9.

Sanctions

CCP §§ 2030.300(d), 2031.310(h), and 2033.290(d) provides that the Court must impose monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to [interrogatories, requests for production, and requests for

admissions] unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

Plaintiff requests $2,576.40 as to the motions to compel further responses. This is calculated at .5 hours meeting and conferring, 3.2 hours researching and drafting the motion, 1 hour for reply, and 1 hour for attending the hearing, at an hourly rate of $452. The Court declines to award .5 hours for meet and confer attempts and 1 hour for reply since no opposition was filed. The Court finds the 3.2 hours for the instant motion and 1 hour for attending the hearing to be reasonable. The Court also finds the hourly rate to be reasonable. Thus, the Court awards sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,898.40. Sanctions are payable to moving party on or before March 29, 2024.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23TRCV04049    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: P

MIRA COSTA BAND

BOOSTERS, INC., Plaintiff, Case No.: 23TRCV04049

vs. [Tentative] RULING

ZNK PARTNERS LLC, dba

LONE STAR PERCUSSION, et

al., Defendants.

Hearing Date: February 28, 2024

Moving Party: Defendant Yamaha Corporation of America

Responding Party: Plaintiff Mira Costa Band Boosters, Inc.

DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The DEMURRER is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend. Plaintiff Mira Costa Band Boosters, Inc. is ordered to file and serve its first amended complaint within 20 days of this proceeding.

BACKGROUND

On 12/05/2023, plaintiff Mira Costa Band Boosters, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants ZNK Partners, LLC, a Texas limited liability company, dba Lone Star Percussion (“Lone Star”), Yamaha Corporation of America (“Yamaha”), and Does 1 through 10, inclusive, asserting one cause of action for breach of contract.

The Complaint alleges the following. Yamaha is a corporation existing by the laws of California and authorized to do business in Los Angeles County. Compl., ¶ 3. “[I]n order to purchase Yamaha musical percussion equipment, Yamaha required customers to conduct business through one of its ‘authorized dealers,’ which Yamaha held out to the public as being a viable company in which to purchase Yamaha musical equipment.” Compl., ¶ 6. On three (3) separate occasions, Plaintiff purchased $32,688.75 worth of Yamaha musical equipment from Lone Star, Yamaha’s authorized dealer, for a public high school in Manhattan Beach, California. Compl., ¶¶ 6-9 (alleging Plaintiff purchased the musical equipment in March 2022 (for

$21,194.82), June 2022 (for $6,391.44), and July 2022 (for $5,102.49)); Exhibits A to C – copies of the three (3) sale orders. The defendants, including Yamaha, breached the sale orders by failing to furnish the musical equipment Plaintiff had purchased. Compl., ¶ 12.

On 1/23/2024, Yamaha filed the instant demurrer, arguing that the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

On 2/15/2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition.

On 2/21/2024, Yamaha filed its reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747. When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.

In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not on the evidence or facts alleged.” E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315. As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. Ibid. However, it does not accept as true deductions, contentions, or conclusions of law or fact. Stonehouse Homes LLC v. City of Sierra Madre (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 531, 538.

A demurrer may be sustained “only if the complaint fails to state a cause of action under any possible legal theory.” Sheehan v. San Francisco 49ers, Ltd. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 992, 998.

“If a complaint does not state a cause of action, but there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment, leave to amend must be granted.” Milligan v. Golden Gate Bridge Highway & Transportation Dist. (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1, 6.

DISCUSSION

A. Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to … [section 430.20], the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).

The Court finds that Yamaha has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Declaration of Jordan D. Grotzinger, filed January 23, 2024, ¶ 3 [testifying the parties met and conferred via phone but could not resolve the issues raised in the demurrer].)

B. First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

Yamaha argues Plaintiff’s breach of contract fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because the Complaint neither alleges that Plaintiff had any contract with Yamaha nor alleges facts to show an agency relationship between Yamaha and Lone Star such that Yamaha could be deemed to have been Lone Star’s guarantor. In support of the latter argument, Yamaha cites Alvarez v. Felker Mfg. Co. (1964) 230 Cal.App.2d 987, which stated: “‘One who receives goods from another for resale to a third person is not thereby the other’s agent in the transaction: whether he is an agent for this purpose or is himself a buyer depends upon whether the parties agree that his duty is to act primarily for the benefit of the one delivering the goods to him or is to act primarily for his own benefit.’ [Citation].” Id. at p. 1000.

In opposition, Plaintiff insists that it has adequately pleaded its breach of contract claim by alleging that (1) Lone Star was Yamaha’s agent and (2) Yamaha made affirmative representations regarding requiring purchase through its authorized agents, which includes Lone Star.

In reply, Yamaha reiterates the arguments in its moving papers.

A breach of contract “claim requires a showing of ‘(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.’ [Citations.]” D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.

Here, Plaintiff seeks to hold Yamaha liable under its alleged agency relationship with Lone Star. “Agency is the relationship which results from the manifestation of consent by one person to another that the other shall act on his behalf and subject to his control, and consent by the other so to act.” Gordon v. ARC Manufacturing, Inc. (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 705, 718 (“Gordon”). Stated differently, “‘[a]n agent “is anyone who undertakes to transact some business, or manage some affair, for another, by authority of and on account of the latter, and to render an account of such transactions.” (3 Cal.Jur.3d, Agency, § 1, p. 10.) “The chief characteristic of the agency is that of representation, the authority to act for and in the place of the principal for the purpose of bringing him or her into legal relations with third parties...The significant test of an agency relationship is the principal’s right to control the activities of the agent. It is not essential that the right of control be exercised or that there be actual supervision of the work of the agent; the existence of the right establishes the relationship.” Violette v. Shoup (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 611, 620; see also Gordon, supra, 43 Cal.App.5th at p. 718 (“In order to create an agency, the principal must confirm authority upon the agent.”).

Here, the Court finds that the Complaint has not alleged facts sufficient to show that Lone Star entered the sale orders pursuant to and on behalf of Yamaha, or that Yamaha controlled Lone Star’s actions with regard to sale orders. Indeed, the Complaint makes conclusory and contradictory allegations, including that Yamaha was Lone Star’s employee. See Compl., ¶ 5 (“At all times herein mentioned, each of the defendants was the agent and employee of each of the remaining defendants and was acting within the scope of such agency and employment”).

Therefore, the Court agrees with Yamaha that the Complaint has failed to allege facts sufficient to constitute a breach of contract claim against the defendant.

For those reasons, the Demurrer is sustained, with leave to amend.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
