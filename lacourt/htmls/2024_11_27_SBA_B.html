Hearing Date:                          November 27, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant The People of the State of California, by and through the Department of Transportation (“Caltrans”)

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  Defendant Caltrans is ordered to file its amended cross-complaint within ten days.

BACKGROUND

On April 19, 2023, plaintiff Paul Workman filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles, T.C. Real Estate Ltd., and Coral Realty, Inc. for dangerous condition of public property and negligence.  Plaintiff alleges that on November 4, 2022, plaintiff was riding his bicycle through the parking lot intending to ride onto the city sidewalk to continue his journey.  However, as plaintiff rode from the parking lot toward the nearest crosswalk by the city sidewalk, the front wheel of his bicycle reached a drop of approximately eleven inches between surface of the parking lot and the surface of the city sidewalk.  Said drop was a concealed trap not marked in any meaningful manner and constituted a dangerous condition since the drop in elevation between the parking lot and the sidewalk could not be seen by plaintiff.  As a result, plaintiff flipped off his bicycle onto the sidewalk and sustained serious injuries.

On June 9, 2023, T.C. Real Estate Ltd. and Coral Realty, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against City of Los Angeles.

On July 3, 2023, City of Los Angeles filed a cross-complaint against T.C. Real Estate and Coral Realty.

On September 12, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment designating ADR/Preferred Business Properties as Doe 1.

On December 13, 2023, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion for relief from Gov. Code §945.4 as against State of California.

On January 16, 2024, plaintiff filed amendments designating California Department of Transportation as Doe 2 and State of California as Doe 3.

On March 30, 2024, defendants and plaintiff entered into a stipulation and the Court ordered that the order dated December 13, 2023 be vacated, that the hearing on the motion be rescheduled, and that the motion be re-served on State of California and California Department of Transportation.

On May 31, 2024, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion for relief from Gov. Code §945.4 and permission to file a late government claim.

On July 12, 2024, T.C. Real Estate, LTD, Coral Realty, Inc., and ADR/Preferred Business Prop. Filed an amendment to cross-complaint designating The People of the State of California as Zoe 1.

On July 15, 2024, The People of the State of California (“Caltrans”) filed a cross-complaint.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant The People of the State of California, by and through the Department of Transportation (“Caltrans”) requests leave to file an amended cross-complaint.

Defendant seeks to add allegations as to Caltrans’ compliance with and exemption from the Government Claims Act to address City of Los Angeles’ concerns.  Defendant asserts that Caltrans counsel met and conferred with City of Los Angeles counsel as to the applicability of the Government Claims Act as to Caltrans and whether Caltrans was required to submit a government claim prior to the filing of its cross-complaint. 

The Court finds that defendant Caltrans complied with CRC Rule 3.1324.  See Daniel Choi decl.  Further, “[l]eave to amend is in general required to be liberally granted.”  Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal. App. 4th 402, 412 (citation omitted). 

            The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            Defendant Caltrans is ordered to file its amended cross-complaint within ten days.

            Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED.  The dismissal entered on May 1, 2024 as to County of Los Angeles is VACATED.

BACKGROUND

On January 31, 2024, plaintiff Jeffrey Knight filed a complaint against State of California, County of Los Angeles, and City of Los Angeles for (1) premises liability – negligence and (2) dangerous condition of public property.  The complaint alleges that on May 28, 2023, defendants owned, possessed, worked upon, managed, supervised, modified, repaired, operated, maintained, or controlled a certain sidewalk, pathway, and/or walkway located on Playa Del Rey Beach Trail, in the city of Playa Del Rey.  They were negligent by causing and creating the dangerous condition and allowing it to exist and remain, the unlevel broken section of sidewalk, pathway, and/or walkway with missing concrete.  As a result, plaintiff was injured.

On March 27, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to the first cause of action.

On May 1, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal without prejudice as to County of Los Angeles.

On May 3, 2024, City of Los Angeles filed a cross-complaint against County of Los Angeles.

On October 22, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal without prejudice as to City of Los Angeles.

On October 29, 2024, City of Los Angeles filed a request for dismissal with prejudice as to its cross-complaint against County of Los Angeles.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a . . . dismissal . . . taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief . . . shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the . . . dismissal . . . . was taken. . . .  Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. . . .”

“[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Under CCP §473(b), plaintiff requests that the Court vacate the dismissal as to defendant County of Los Angeles based on attorney mistake and inadvertence.

            Plaintiff’s counsel T. Vincent Consolo states in his declaration that he and his office inadvertently, mistakenly, and erroneously filed a request for dismissal without prejudice as to County of Los Angeles on May 1, 2024, which “occurred due to a sequence of mistakes and misunderstandings by myself and my staff.”  He explains that he was unable to attend the CMC on May 1, 2024, an associate from his office attended the hearing, and the Court had noted that there was no proof of service on defendant County.  After the CMC, the associate tasked the litigation paralegal to inquire as to the status of service of process or to file a request for dismissal.  The paralegal then communicated via text with declarant attorney whether defendant County should be served with summons and complaint or dismissed.  Declarant confused the instant case with another case and mistakenly and inadvertently authorized the paralegal to dismiss defendant County.  The paralegal altered a preexisting form CIV-110 request for dismissal, with incorrect court address, an incorrect date, and stamped counsel’s name and filed it with the court on May 1, the date of the CMC.  Declarant states that he never saw the request for dismissal prior to its filing.  He attests that it appears that his personal inattention, confusion, and miscommunication with his staff resulted in the erroneous and inadvertent filing of the request for dismissal.

            In opposition, County of Los Angeles argues that plaintiff’s counsel has not shown a reasonable excuse to justify relief under CCP §473(b).  County also argues that the statute of limitations has run, preventing plaintiff from pursuing the County in a new action against the County.

            In reply, plaintiff contends that the “sole determination before this Court is whether the facts presented meet the standard of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  Plaintiff argues that the standard has been met based on plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration.

            The Court finds that relief from dismissal is warranted and is mandatory based on attorney mistake and inadvertence.

            The motion is therefore GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.  The dismissal entered on May 1, 2024 as to County of Los Angeles is vacated.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.
