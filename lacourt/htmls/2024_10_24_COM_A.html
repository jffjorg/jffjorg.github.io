DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 22STCV29728    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: A

22STCV29728 Johnna Mike-Price, et al v. Toshiba Lifestyle Products and Services Corp., et al.

Thursday, October 24, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ SIX MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES BY GD MIDEA AIR CONDITIONING EQUIPMENT CO., LTD. AND WUHU MATY AIR CONDITIONING CO., LTD. TO:

1)      SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE

2)      FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET TWO

3)      REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET TWO

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The complaint alleges that Plaintiffs sustained injury when Plaintiff, Commander Mike-Price, who was then six years old, ingested a button battery from the remote for an air conditioning unit made by Defendants. Plaintiffs allege claims for negligence and strict products liability.

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Plaintiffs served three sets of written discovery at issue on Defendant, GD Midea Air Condition Equipment Co., Ltd, (“Midea”) and three sets of written discovery on Defendant, Wuhu Maty Air-Conditioning Equipment Co., LTD., (“Wuhu”) (collectively “Defendants”). Plaintiffs argue Defendants responded with identical, boilerplate objections to each and every one of the requests although Plaintiffs permitted extensions of time to respond.

       Defendants file separate oppositions objecting to the motions because Defendants agreed to supplement their responses to Plaintiffs’ discovery no later than Wednesday, October 23, 2024.

       In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants concede their objection-only responses were deficient. Defendants’ intention to respond three days before the hearing is not grounds to deny the motion. Plaintiffs are entitled to imposition of monetary sanctions against Defendants and their counsel.

LEGAL STANDARDS

       A motion to compel further responses to a document request is proper where the moving party believes the statement of compliance is incomplete, or a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, evasive and/or an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310 (a), Code Civ. Proc., sections 2030.300 subd. (a); 2031.310 subd. (a).) All motions require the parties to meet and confer prior to filing the motion. The motion must show good cause to justify the discovery and must be supported by a meet and confer declaration. (Id. at subd. (b).) Plaintiffs have complied with the foregoing requirements.

       The scope of discovery is liberally construed in favor of disclosure “as a matter of right unless statutory or public policy considerations clearly prohibit it.” (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court of Merced County (1961) 56 Cal. 2d 355, 377-378.) The broad scope of permissible discovery includes “any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

 

III.    DISCUSSION

       The parties filed a joint status reprot on October 17, 2024. Plaintiffs contend the motions are necessary as Defendants did not serve responses.        Defendants do not dispute the relevance and discoverability of issues raised in Plaintiffs’ six motions. The motions remain on calendar as there is no confirmation that Defendants served supplemental responses at any time before the hearing. Plaintiffs served discovery on July 25, 2024 and as of this writing have not received substantive discovery responses.

Case Number: 23CMCV00046    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCV00046 Malcolm Harper v. Maximo Chavez

Thursday, October 24, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS AND REQUEST FOR DISMISSAL

 

       The complaint alleges a claim for negligence arising from an automobile collision causing injury to Plaintiff. On June 4, 2024, the court granted Defendant’s motion to compel responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and request for production of documents ;and imposed monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. (Mot. Ex. A). Defendant served Plaintiff with the court’s order on June 7, 2024. (Mot. Ex. B). Defendant timely served this motion on Plaintiff by mail. Plaintiff did not comply with the court’s order and did not oppose this motion.

        Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting from the lack of information. (Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64.)

       The type of sanction imposed should be “appropriate to the dereliction,” and tailored to accomplish the discovery sought. (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 35.) There are two prerequisites for the imposition of a discovery sanction: “(1) there must be a failure to comply [with a valid discovery order] . . . and (2) the failure must be willful.” (Young v. Rosenthal (1989) 212 Cal. App. 3d 96, 114.) Despite imposition of monetary sanctions, Plaintiff did not provide discovery responses.

       Plaintiff’s failure to respond to discovery propounded by Defendant and failure to comply with the court’s order are abuses of the discovery process warranting terminating sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.)

       Accordingly, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 24CMCV00343    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: A

24CMCV00343 Steen Sprouffske v. George Moa, et al.

Thursday, October 24, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO DETERMINE VALUE AND SEND BUYOUT NOTICE

 

       This is a partition action between Plaintiff and Defendant, George Moa (“Moa”), each of whom own an undivided, one-half interest in real property. Moa answered the complaint on August 20, 2024. On February 15, 1999, Defendant, City of Lynwood (“Lynwood”) recorded liens against the property for nonpayment of water, sewer, and refuse collection. Plaintiff and Lynwood signed a stipulation acknowledging the superiority of Lynwood’s liens and agreeing that Lynwood’s participation in the action is not required. Lynwood agreed to be bound by judgment issued in this case. (Stip. 6/20/24.)

       The complaint alleges that Defendant, Nova Casualty Company (“Nova”) recorded an abstract of judgment against Defendant Moa. The clerk entered default against Nova on July 16, 2024. Therefore, the remaining Defendant is Moa, who has not filed an opposition despite proper notice of the motion.

       Plaintiff requests that the court determine the fair market value of the real property and send a buyout notice to all parties as permitted by statute.

       The court is authorized to determine the fair market value of property if it determines that "the evidentiary value of an appraisal is outweighed by the cost of the appraisal, the court, after an evidentiary hearing, shall determine the fair market value of the property and send notice to the parties of the value." (Code Civ. Proc., § 874.316 subd. (c).) The value of property may be shown only by opinions of the owner or a witness qualified to express such opinions. (Evid. Code, § 813.) That opinion must be "based on matter perceived by or personally known to the witness or made known to the witness at or before the hearing, whether or not admissible, that is of a type that reasonably may be relied upon by an expert in forming an opinion as to the value of property, including but not limited to the matters listed in Sections 815 to 821, inclusive, unless a witness is precluded by law from using such matter as a basis for an opinion." (Evid. Code, § 814.)

       However, the right of an owner to testify to the fair market value is not absolute. (Contra Costa Water Dist. v. Bar-C Properties (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 652, 661 [“an owner is bound by the same rules of admissibility as any other witness.”].)

       There is no evidence demonstrating the second element required to permit the court to determine fair market value, which is that “the evidentiary value of an appraisal is outweighed by the cost of the appraisal.” (Code Civ. Proc., §874.316 subd. (c).) There is no evidence of the cost of an appraisal.

       The court can order an appraisal by appointing a licensed and disinterested real estate appraiser who "shall file a sworn or verified appraisal with the court" on completion of the appraisal. (Code Civ. Proc., § 874.316 subd. (d).)

       Plaintiff is part owner of the real property but is not qualified as an appraisal expert. He declares that the value of the property is $730,000 based on a real property valuation report prepared by Aaron Montelongo, a realtor. (Sprouffske decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2.)  Mr. Montelongo does not establish that he is a licensed real estate appraiser. (Montelongo decl., Ex. 1; Bus. & Prof. Code, § 11314 ["The bureau is required to include in its regulations requirements for licensure and discipline of real estate appraisers that ensure protection of the public interest and comply in all respects with Title XI of the Financial Institutions Reform, Recovery and Enforcement Act of 1989, Public Law 101-731 and any subsequent amendments thereto."].)

       Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

 

 

 

 


Case Number: 24CMCV00550    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: A

24CMCV00550 Amber Hendree v. Santander Consumer USA dba Chrysler Capital & Lithia Motors, Inc., et al.

October 24, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT WITH 30 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The action arises from Plaintiff’s purchase of a vehicle on March 9, 2023, from Costa Mesa-CHD (“Dealer”), who allegedly assigned the finance contract to Defendant, Santander Consumer, USA, Inc. dba Chrysler Capital (“Chrysler Capital”). The first amended complaint alleges that Defendants allegedly misrepresented the finance contract terms, engaged in fraudulent debt collection practices, and wrongfully repossessed the vehicle.  Plaintiff alleges 29 causes of action for statutory violations and related common law claims.

       Chrysler Capital demurs to all 29 causes of action on grounds that Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory and do not allege facts to support the conclusions. Defendant timely filed and served the demurrer on Plaintiff and the Dealer. No opposition has been filed.

II.      LEGAL STANDARDS

       A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing the complaint’s sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations as well as facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded facts. The court may also consider matters properly subject to judicial notice. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

       The court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) Plaintiff is required to allege facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. (Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126.)

       Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

       A demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

       A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.) However, unlike federal courts, California state courts are not a notice pleading jurisdiction, and notice alone is not a sufficient basis for any pleading. California is a fact pleading jurisdiction. Merely putting an opposing party on notice is not sufficient. (Bach v. County of Butte (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 554, 561; see Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 244, 250.)

III.    DISCUSSION

1.      Claims for violations of statute are required to be alleged with specificity.

   Where a claim is based entirely on statutory liability, “it is indispensable that [plaintiff] plead facts demonstrating [the] right to recover under the statute. The complaint must plead every fact which is essential to the cause of action under the statute. (Green v. Grimes-Stassforth Stationery Co. (1940) 39 Cal.App.2d 52, 56.) If the statute contains a limitation in the clause creating and defining the liability, plaintiff must “negative” the limitation in the complaint. (Id.)

   The claims falling within this category are the 1st cause of action for violation of Plaintiff’s civil rights under 42 USC § 1983; 3rd cause of action for violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (“CLRA”), 4th cause of action for violation of the Unfair Competition law (Bus. and Prof Code § 17200, 5th cause of action for violation of Civ. Code, § 1788 (Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices (“RFDCPA”), 6th cause of Truth in Lending Act (“TILA”), 7th cause of action for violation Federal Debt Collection Practices Act, the 8th cause of action for predatory lending, 9 cause of action for failure to perfect the security interest, 11th cause of action for fraudulent transfer of a security interest, 12th cause of action for identity theft, 13th cause of action for filing a “fraudulent return,” 14th cause of action for fraud and false statements on a tax return, 15th cause of action for violating the Consumer Credit Reporting Act, the 16th cause of action for violations of the Fair Debt Collection Practices, 22nd cause of action wrongful repossession by breach of peace, the 23rd cause of action for grand theft, 25th cause of action for forgery, 26th cause of action for violations of the Securities Act,

    Plaintiff does not identify the elements of each statute at issue or specific acts engaged in by Defendant constituting a violation. All allegations are conclusions of law.

   With respect to the section 1983 civil rights claim, Plaintiff does not allege facts to support the assertion that Defendant was operating under color of state law, or the manner in which Plaintiff’s 14th amendment rights were violated. Plaintiff concludes without facts that Defendants’ actions “clearly” deprived Plaintiff of her constitutional rights. (FAC, ¶¶ 3, 5:12-16.)

   With respect to the TILA claim, Plaintiff did not allege actionable conduct on the part of Defendant, only the Dealer.

   The CLRA claim also fails as it does not allege that Plaintiff complied with the pre-filing notice requirement at least 30 days before filing a CLRA damage claim. (Morgan v. AT&T Wireless Services, Inc. (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1235, 1259. Plaintiff did not allege that she notified Defendant and demanded correction, repair, or replacement of the goods. (Id.;  Civ. Code, § 1770.)

   Plaintiff does not allege what “deceptive practice” is prohibited by statute or facts demonstrating the misconduct that Defendant purportedly engaged in. (FAC, ¶5.)

   With respect to the UCL violation, Plaintiff did not allege with “reasonable particularity” the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619 [Where Plaintiff did not identify the statutory scheme which was allegedly violated and the particular facts supporting the violation].)  To the extent the UCL claim is based on underlying statutory violations, it is equally deficient as the underlying claims are not alleged with specific facts.

   Plaintiff does not identify specific acts of misconduct that constitute predatory lending as identified in Finance Code, section 4973.

2.      The fraud and fraud-related claims are not alleged with factual specificity.

       The claims falling in this category are the  2nd causes of action for fraud, 11th cause of action for fraudulent transfer of a security interest, 14th cause of action for fraud and false statements on a tax return, 17th cause of action for fraudulent representation or concealment of facts, 21st cause of action for false or misleading advertising, and the 24th cause of action for fraudulent concealment

       Fraud claims require specific allegations of facts to support the following elements: (1) a misrepresentation, (2) made with knowledge of its falsity, (3) Defendant intended to defraud Plaintiff, i.e., induce Plaintiff’s reliance, (4) Plaintiff justifiably relied on the misrepresentation, (5) causing damage. (Nagy v. Nagy (1989) 210 Cal.App.3d 1262, 1268.)

       Plaintiff’s general and conclusory allegations do not meet these standards.

3.      Plaintiff alleges claims that are not recognized causes of action

       The 18th cause of action fall for failure to include an arbitration clause in the alleged contract falls within this category. The 19th cause of action for willful neglect to arbitrate appears to be  claim for breach of contract, however, material terms of the contract are not alleged, and it also conflicts with the 18th cause of action for failure to include such a clause.

       The 27th claim for unjust enrichment is not a cause of action but is an equitable principle that underlies “various legal doctrines and remedies.” (County of San Bernardino v. Walsh (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 533, 542.) Plaintiff must allege the “receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.” (Lectrodryer v. Seoulbank (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 723, 726.) A person is enriched if the person receives a benefit (any type of advantage) at another's expense. (First Nationwide Savings v. Perry (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1657, 1662.) The claim does not allege facts.

       The 28th cause of action for equitable estoppel must be “especially pleaded in the complaint with sufficient accuracy to disclose facts relied upon.” (Chalmers v. County of Los Angeles 175 Cal.App.3d 461, 467 (1985).) This claim is devoid of any facts.

4.      The common law claims are inadequately alleged.

       The 10th cause of action for conversion, 20th cause of action for breach of contract and the 29th cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are not supported by any facts.  A claim for conversion requires allegations describing Plaintiff’s ownership interest at the time of the conversion and Defendants’ wrongful act or dispossession of Plaintiffs’ property rights. (Moore v. Regents of University of California (1990) 51 Cal.3d 120, 136.) The basic elements are (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) the defendant's disposition of the property in a manner that is inconsistent with the plaintiff's property rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)

       A claim for breach of contract must allege the material terms verbatim or by alleging its legal effect. Plaintiff may also attach a copy of the contract as an exhibit.  (Holly Sugar Corp. v. Johnson (1941)  18 Cal. 2d 218, 225;  Twaite v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1989) 216 Cal. App. 3d 239, 252. The FAC fails to meet any of these requirements.

       The intentional infliction of emotional distress claim concludes that Defendant engaged in outrageous conduct. To prevail on a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, plaintiff must allege facts showing (1) extreme and outrageous conduct with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress, (2) Plaintiff suffered severe or extreme emotional distress, and (3) actual and proximate causation. (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.) The allegations are conclusory, and Plaintiff’s allegations of Defendant’s alleged misconduct are either vague, non-specific, or non-existent.

IV.    CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, the demurrer to the entire first amended complaint is SUSTAINED with 30 days leave to amend. (Colvig v. RKO General, Inc. (1965) 232 Cal.App.2d 56, 69–70 [noting the “well-established rule that, even where the defect is one of substance, a demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if there is a possibility that subsequent amendments will supply omitted allegations and the plaintiff has not had a fair opportunity to so amend."].)
