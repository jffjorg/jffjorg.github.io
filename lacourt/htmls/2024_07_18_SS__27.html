DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV36598    Hearing Date: July 18, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27¿ 

¿ 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL¿ 

Hearing Date: 7/18/24¿¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 19STCV36598 KEVIN FABIAN JIMENEZ vs HOLST BROTHERS, INC.

Moving Party: Cross Defendant Bob Hart’s Electronic

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL¿IS GRANTED 

 

On October 15, 2019, Plaintiff filed the present case against Defendants, including Holst Brothers. On December 30, 2019, Defendant Holst Brothers filed a cross-complaint against Bob Hart’s Electric. On May 31, 2024, Cross-Defendant Bob Hart’s Electric filed its motion for summary judgment against Defendant Holst Brothers, with the MSJ to be heard on February 11, 2025. The current trial date is set for September 18, 2024. Cross-Defendant Bob Hart’s Electric now moves the Court to continue the trial to sometime in March 2025 so its MSJ can be heard.

Numerous courts of appeal have held that a trial court cannot refuse to consider a motion for summary judgment that is timely filed. "A trial court may not refuse to hear a summary judgment filed within the time limits of [Code of Civil Procedure] section 437c. [Citation.] Local rules and practices may not be applied so as to prevent the filing and hearing of such a motion." (Sentry Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 526, 529); accord First State Inc. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 324, 330 [invalidating case management order to the extent it precluded filing motions pursuant to section 437c ]; Wells Fargo Bank v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 918, 923 [local court rule that "require a party filing a complex summary judgment motion to file the motion six months before the date set for trial is void and unenforceable because it is inconsistent with section 437c"].) As the Sentry court explained: "We are sympathetic to the problems the trial courts experience in calendaring and hearing the many motions for summary judgment. However, the solution to these problems cannot rest in a refusal to hear timely motions." (Sentry, supra, at p. 530.) 

On May 31, 2024, Bob Hart filed its motion for summary judgment with a trial date set for September 18, 2024. The MSJ was filed more than 105 days prior to trial and is therefore timely. The court finds good cause to continue the trial so the timely MSJ can be heard. The MSJ cannot be advanced because the court’s calendar is congested, and as counsel has alleged, the earliest hearing date available for the MSJ was February 11, 2025.

Plaintiff filed an opposition, stating that he does not oppose the continuance of the trial so Bob Hart’s MSJ can be heard prior to the trial date but requests a shorter continuance to January 17, 2025. However, January 17, 2025, would be before the current hearing date for the MSJ, and as the court has indicated, the MSJ cannot be advanced due to the court’s congested calendar.

Furthermore, this case was filed on October 15, 2019, and pursuant to Emergency Rule 10, parties have five years and six months to bring this case to trial; a trial date in March 2025 would be within that deadline. Thus, the court grants the present motion.

 The new Trial Date is set for March ____, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. The Final Status Conference is continued to February ____, 2025, at 10:00 a.m. The only basis to continue the trial date is so the motion for summary judgment can be heard. Defendant has not shown good cause to have the fact discovery cut-off follow the new trial date. Should the parties wish to reopen fact discovery, they can do so either through stipulation or motion.  All other case-related deadlines, including expert discovery, will follow the new trial date.

MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE¿ 

Hearing Date: 7/18/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV09722 LAWRENCE EARL ADAMS vs TESLA, INC; 24PSCV00397 ANDREW MARTINEZ, et al. vs TESLA MOTORS INC

Moving Party: Defendant Tesla Motors

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE¿IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.350, subdivision (a) states in relevant part:

 

(1) A notice of motion to consolidate must:

(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record;

(B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest-numbered case shown first; and

(C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated.

 

(2) The motion to consolidate:

(A) Is deemed a single motion for the purpose of determining the appropriate filing fee, but memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed only in the lowest-numbered case;

(B) Must be served on all attorneys of record and all non-represented parties in all of the cases sought to be consolidated; and

(C) Must have a proof of service filed as part of the motion.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350, subd. (a)). Also, the consolidation statute, Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, states in relevant part:

 

(a)¿ When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.

 

(b)¿ The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).)

 

The granting or denial of the motion to consolidate rests in the sound discretion of the trial court and will not be reversed except upon a clear showing of abuse of discretion. (See Fellner v. Steinbaum (1955) 132 Cal.App.2d 509, 511.)

 

Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g) states, “Cases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related into a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”

 

Discussion

 

On March 21, 2022, Plaintiff Lawrence Adams filed the present action, case number 22STCV09722, against Tesla. The claims involve a vehicular accident on State Road 57 (SR-57) in Diamond Bar, County of Los Angeles. Adams alleges that he was driving his Mercedes-Benz northbound on SR-57 when he struck a black Tesla Model X in the early hours of February 11, 2022. Adams alleges that the Tesla vehicle was abandoned or otherwise not operating, and he was unable to see the vehicle with enough notice to avoid colliding with it. Following the collision between himself and the Tesla, Plaintiff Andrew Martinez in the related case, 24PSCV00397, struck the Tesla vehicle. According to reports generated by the California Highway Patrol (CHP), the accidents occurred within minutes of each other. The Adams accident resulted in the Tesla moving further into the middle of SR-57 and blocking the travel lane. On February 7, 2024, Martinez filed his action, case number 24PSCV00397, against Tesla and the driver, Karen Bacolod. Both 22STCV09722 and 24PSCV00397 are in Department 27 of the Spring Street Courthouse. Defendant Tesla now moves the court to consolidate the two cases.

Defendant has met all the requirements under California Rules of Court, rule 3.350. The notice of motion lists all named parties in each case, contains the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest-numbered case shown first, and the notice of motion is filed in case number 24PSCV00397. The notice of motion and motion and supporting papers have been properly served on all parties.

The court finds good cause to consolidate the actions as they share common questions of fact or law. Both Defendants Tesla and Karen MacLeod are defendants in each case. Plaintiffs’ claims are based on accidents that allegedly occurred within minutes of each other. The two accidents are related as the first accident caused defendant's vehicle to travel further towards the middle of the highway, blocking the travel lane, and leading to the second accident. Furthermore, Tesla intends to rely on the same witnesses and experts. The parties will rely on the same CHP personnel to testify at deposition and trial. No opposition was filed alleging prejudice. Thus, the present motion is granted and the two cases are to be consolidated with 22STCV09722 as the lead case.

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL  

Hearing Date: 7/18/2024 at 1:30 p.m.  

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV01479 GAIL BLECKMAN vs THE KROGER CO et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff’s Counsel Sahm Manouchehri

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS GRANTED. 

 

Background

 

Attorney Sahm Manouchehri represents Plaintiff. Manouchehri moves to be relieved as counsel, citing an irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. No opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.)

 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)

¿

Analysis and Conclusion

 

Manouchehri has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Manouchehri seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).)

 

The Court notes that, within the past 30 days, Plaintiff's email address and mailing address was confirmed to be current by counsel through email.

 

The next hearing is an FSC set for 11/5/2024, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel needs additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Thus, the present motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED effective upon service on Plaintiff.

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO RECLASSIFY

Hearing Date: 7/18/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV12222 JERMAINE STERLING vs DEROOSPRO LLC

Moving Party: Defendant DeroosPro and Bryon Deroos

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO RECLASSIFY IS DENIED

 

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 403.040 allows the defendant or cross-defendant to file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to respond to the initial pleading. Defendant has 30 days to file a responsive pleading. CCP § 430.40(a) and CCP § 412.20(a)(3)

(b) If a party files a motion for reclassification after the time for that party to amend that party's initial pleading or to respond to a complaint, cross-complaint, or other initial pleading, the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The case is incorrectly classified and (2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.

In Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257, 262, the California Supreme Court held that a matter may be reclassified from unlimited to limited only if it appears to a legal certainty that the plaintiff's damages will necessarily be less than $25,000. (Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257.) If there is a possibility that the damages will exceed $25,000.00, the case cannot be transferred to limited. (Ibid.) This high standard is appropriate in light of “the circumscribed procedures and recovery available in the limited civil courts.” (Ytuarte v. Superior Court (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 266, 278.)

 

In Ytuarte, the Court of Appeals examined the principles it set forth in Walker and held that “the court should reject the plaintiff's effort to reclassify the action as unlimited only when the lack of jurisdiction as an “unlimited” case is certain and clear.” (Id. at 279.) (Emphasis added.)

 

Discussion¿ 

On March 6, 2024, Defendants Deroospro LLC and Bryon Deroos were served with the summons and complaint through substituted service. On March 27, 2024, Defendants filed their answer. On May 23, 2024, Defendants move the court to reclassify the present case to limited civil court.

This motion is not timely as Defendants were served on March 6, 2024, and the present motion was filed on May 23, 2024, more than 30 days after service. Furthermore, Defendant, in the moving papers, failed to show good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier. In fact, Defendant was aware of all the facts necessary to file the present motion in 2023 when Plaintiff sent Defendants his demand letter (Exhibit A). It does not appear to the court that there is good cause for the delay in filing the present motion.

Additionally, there exists a possibility that Plaintiff's recovery could exceed $25,000. According to Plaintiff’s demand letter, Plaintiff has been diagnosed with strain of the thoracic region, cervicalgia, lumbago, radiculopathy to the lumbar and cervical region, pain and strain to the lumbar and cervical spine, and protrusion of the lumbar disc. An MRI showed a posterior disc bulge. As of September 21, 2022, Plaintiff has incurred $8,819 in medical expenses, anticipates incurring more than $63,000 in future medical expenses and alleges general damages.

Although Plaintiff has only produced medical records totaling $8,819 as of September 21, 2022, it is important to consider that more than two years have passed since those records were produced. Given the extent of Plaintiff's alleged injuries, which include strain of the thoracic region, cervicalgia, lumbago, radiculopathy to the lumbar and cervical region, pain and strain to the lumbar and cervical spine, and protrusion of the lumbar disc, it is possible that Plaintiff may have incurred additional medical expenses since then. The nature of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries also suggests ongoing treatment and potential complications, which could possibility increase the total medical costs.

As to future medical damages, the absence of current evidence regarding the actual cost of future treatments does not preclude the possibility that such evidence will be presented later during litigation.

Furthermore, courts typically consider general damages for pain and suffering calculated as a multiple of special damages. The court is typically inclined to grant general damages three times the amount of special damages. So even assuming Plaintiff’s special damages are limited to $8,819, the total amount of damages, including general damages, would exceed $25,000. Thus, the court finds that there is a possibility for recovery over $25,000.00 and denies the present motion.

Demurrer and Motion to Strike

Hearing Date: 7/18/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV28246 JACKELINE FERNANDEZ CALLES vs LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY et al.

Moving Party: Defendant LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling:

Demurrers to Premises Liability and General Negligence sustained with leave to amend.
Demurrer to Motor Vehicle Negligence is sustained without leave to amend.

Request to strike No. 1 is overruled.
Requests to strike Nos. 2, 3, and 5 are sustained without leave to amend.
Request to strike No. 4 is overruled.
Request to strike No. 6 is moot.

DEMURRER

 

Background

 

On November 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present action against Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“Metro”), City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, State of California, and California Department of Transportation. Plaintiff alleges three causes of action for dangerous condition of public property, general negligence, and motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff alleges that she was injured when the train she was on was stopped in an unreasonable and unsafe manner. On May 29, 2024, Defendant Metro admits that the individual operating the train where Plaintiff was allegedly injured is an employee of Defendant Metro. Defendant now demurs to the complaint on the following bases: (1) the premises liability cause of action does not allege what constitutes the dangerous condition; (2) the general negligence and motor vehicle negligence causes of action are duplicative; and (3) no common law negligence causes of action exist against a public entity.

 

Judicial Notice

 

The court takes judicial notice of Exhibit A: Defendant’s Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority’s Admission Employee was Acting within the Course and Scope of Employment at Time of Alleged Accident pursuant to Evidence Code §§ 452(h) and 453.

 

Legal Standard

 

1.  Demurrer

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (CCP §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [internal citations omitted].)

 

2.  Government Claims

 

Pursuant to the Tort Claims Act, Plaintiff must allege a statutory basis for holding the government liable for trespass. (See Govt. Code §815; Bates v. Franchise Tax Board (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 367, 381.) 

 

A public entity is not liable for an injury unless that liability is provided for by statute. (Gov. Code, § 815(a).) “[T]o state a cause of action every fact essential to the existence of statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity, including the existence of a statutory duty. [Citation.]” (Searcy v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 802.) “Since the duty of a governmental agency can only be created by statute or ‘enactment,’ the statute or ‘enactment’ claimed to establish the duty must at the very least be identified.” (Ibid.) 

“[B]ecause under the Tort Claims Act all governmental tort liability is based on statute, the general rule that statutory causes of action must be pleaded with particularity is applicable.”  (Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795.)  “Thus, ‘to state a cause of action against a public entity, every fact material to the existence of its statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity.’” (Id. (quoting Peter W. v. San Francisco Unified Sch. Dist. (1976) 60 Cal.App.3d 814, 819).)

 

Plaintiff’s Claim for Dangerous Condition of Public Property

Under Government Code §835 “a public entity may be liable for an injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury .... the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury, which was incurred, and .... [t]he public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition [in time to prevent the injury].” (Ibid.)

“A claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations [citation] but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.’ [Citation.] A plaintiff’s allegations, and ultimately the evidence, must establish a physical deficiency in the property itself. [Citations.] A dangerous condition exists when public property ‘is physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective in such a way as to foreseeably endanger those using the property itself,’ or possesses physical characteristics in its design, location, features or relationship to its surroundings that endanger users. [Citation.].” (Mixon v. Pacific Gas and Electric Company (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 124, 131

The complaint does not allege what constitutes the dangerous condition in this case. In the case of a train, a physical feature or design of the train that was dangerous must be specified, as every fact essential to the existence of statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity. (See Searcy v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 802.) Since Plaintiff failed to allege the defect in question, the court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend,  as the Court finds a reasonable possibility of successful amendment by alleging the dangerous condition with particularity.

Plaintiff’s Claim for General Negligence

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for general negligence does not contain a statutory basis for recovery and is therefore based on common law general negligence. Accordingly, Defendants contend the cause of action cannot stand against a government entity. The Court agrees in part. Plaintiff seeks recovery under a theory of negligent entrustment, hiring, and supervision, alleging that Metro negligently “entrusted the motor vehicle” to the train operator.  A governmental entity is not liable for negligent hiring and supervision. (De Villers v. County of San Diego (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 238, 253 [“there is no statutory basis for declaring a governmental entity liable for negligence in its hiring and supervision practices”].) Therefore, Plaintiff’s second cause of action based on negligent entrustment, supervision, and hiring is groundless and cannot be maintained.

However, it appears that negligent hiring or entrustment may not be Plaintiff’s only theory of recovery for negligence. Plaintiff explicitly cites Government Code Section 815.2 and Vehicle Code Section 17001 as bases for recovery (Complaint at pg. 10.) and alleges that “Defendant JOHN DOE, suddenly and without warning, abruptly applied the brakes as the train was in motion and Plaintiff fell off her seat, causing Plaintiff to sustain serious injuries and damages. The actions of the Defendants and/or its employees were below the standard of care and was the cause of Plaintiff’s injuries”.

Government Code section 815.2 provides that a public entity is vicariously liable for the torts of their employees committed within the scope of employment if the employee is liable. (See Govt. Code § 815.2(a); Chambi v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 822, 827; Hoff, supra, 19 Cal.4th at 932; Lawson v. Superior Court (2010) 180 C.A.4th 1372, 1388 [state was vicariously liable for any negligence by jailers in failing to seek care for infant's respiratory ailment].) In the Complaint, Plaintiff has alleged that John Doe, the operator of the train whom Metro admits was its employee (RFJN Ex. A), acted in a manner that fell below the standard of care by suddenly, without warning, applying the brakes, causing Plaintiff’s injuries. Arguably, then Plaintiff has alleged enough facts regarding John Doe’s negligence to make Defendant liable under a theory of vicarious liability.  However, as presented, the Complaint is ambiguous as to whether that is what is being pled.  Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action for general negligence is sustained, but with leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s Claim for Motor Vehicle Negligence

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s causes of action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence are duplicative and contain the same allegations and therefore one of the causes of action should be dismissed. The Court reviewed the complaint and observed that they do indeed include identical allegations. A demurrer may be sustained when a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action and “thus adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery.” (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501; see Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.) The Court therefore sustains Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s motor vehicle negligence cause of action without leave to amend.

MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendant also filed a motion to strike along with the demurrer seeking to strike the following allegations:

1. “Defendants negligently, recklessly and/or wantonly owned, maintained, managed operated, controlled and safeguarded the premises on a Line A train…” (Complaint, “Premises Liability” Attachment, page 7.)

2. “Defendants failed to properly train their employees and/or supervise their staff and failed to barricade the areas and/or to warn of the dangerous condition…” (Complaint, “Premises Liability” Attachment, page 7.)

3. “The defendants who entrusted the motor vehicle are…Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority.” (Complaint, “Motor Vehicle” Attachment, page 8, at MV-2d.)

4. “…the negligence carelessness and/or recklessness of Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority.” (Complaint, “Motor Vehicle” Attachment, page 9 and identical allegations in “General Negligence” Attachment, page 10.)

5. “…so negligently, wantonly, carelessly, and/or recklessly entrusted, owned, operated a train in an unreasonable and unsafe manner…” (Complaint, “Motor Vehicle” Attachment, page 9 and identical allegations in “General Negligence” Attachment, page 10.)

6. Third cause of action for General Negligence, in its entirety (Complaint, ¶ 10a, and “General Negligence” Attachment, pages 10-11.)

Request 1 is overruled because to prevail in a dangerous condition of public property claim, Plaintiff must establish that: (1) the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, (2) the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, (3) the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and (4) either:

(a) A negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of their employment created the dangerous condition; or

(b) The public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition. (Cal. Gov. Code § 835.)

Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendants negligently, recklessly, and/or wantonly owned, maintained, managed, operated, controlled, and safeguarded the premises of a Line A train is relevant to Cal. Gov. Code § 835(a) where a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of their employment created the dangerous condition.

Requests 2-3, and 5 are sustained without leave to amend as there is no cause of action or theory of recovery for negligent entrustment, hiring, or supervision against a public entity, as discussed in the foregoing analysis for the demurrer.

Request 4 is overruled because the allegation of “negligence, carelessness, and/or recklessness of Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority” in the “General Negligence” Attachment, page 10, is not baseless as Defendant Metro could, as discussed above, be found vicariously liable for negligence based on the conduct of its employee who Plaintiff alleges was negligent in operating the train.

Request 6 is moot as the Court has sustained the demurrer to the motor vehicle negligence cause of action without leave to amend. The Court believes that general negligence, instead of motor vehicle negligence, is the more appropriate cause of action to maintain, though, as noted, the Court believes that cause of action should be amended to make it more clear.

Plaintiff may amend consistent with this ruling within 20 days of today.
