DEPARTMENT 76 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV13542    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: 76



The hearing on Attorney Seth Schechter’s motion to be relieved as counsel for Defendant Boston Pro Inc. is CONTINUED to July 15, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. Counsel is to provide proof of service upon the client by mail as to this motion and notice of continuance, submit a fully-filled out Form MC-053, and submit a supplemental declaration explaining why counsel is seeking to withdraw with only four months left before trial, as counsel vaguely claims the working relationship has ceased. 


Case Number: 22STCV27380    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: 76



Defendant Screamline Investment Corporation’s demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the first through fourth causes of action. 

ANALYSIS

Demurrer

Meet and Confer

            The form Declaration of Sandra J. Vivonia reflects that Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to meet and confer efforts. This satisfied Civ. Proc. Code, § 430.41(a)(3)(B). 

Discussion

Defendant Screamline Investment Corporation demurs to the Second Amended Complaint as follows:

1.         First Cause of Action (Breach of Contract).

Defendant argues that this cause of action is based upon a contract to which Defendant Screamline is not a party. Attached as Exhibit A to the 2AC is a policy whereby the named insured is Screamline Investment Corp., and the Insurer is New York Marine and General Insurance Company, not Plaintiff Coaction Specialty Insurance Group. Thus, Defendant argues, Plaintiff Coaction is not the real party in interest and lacks standing to assert this cause of action. 

The 2AC alleges at ¶ 5:

 

5. Plaintiff is an insurance carrier. The entity New York Marine & General is a State of New York corporation.  This entity, New York Marine & General, is a wholly owned subsidiary of Plaintiff COACTION SPECIALTY INSURANCE GROUP, INC. (F/K/A PROSIGHT SPECIALTY INSURANCE). The policy was written under the New York Marine & General entity name.  Plaintiff has the right to collect any outstanding balance from Defendant. 

 

This is the only basis alleged which forms the basis of standing to sue for Plaintiff Coaction Specialty Insurance Group, Inc. (f/k/a Prosight Specialty Insurance).

 

However, there is no basis offered for a parent company having standing to sue to enforce contracts entered into by the subsidiary, absent an assignment of that right, or language in the contract whereby the parent was an intended third-party beneficiary.  “Corporate entities are presumed to have separate existences, and the corporate form will be disregarded only when the ends of justice require this result.” (Citations omitted).  In particular, there is a strong presumption that a parent company is not the employer of its subsidiary's employees. (Citation omitted.)”  (Laird v. Capital Cities/Abc (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 727, 737.)

 

The insurance contract attached as Exhibit A to the 2AC does not contain language whereby Coaction Specialty Insurance Group, Inc. (f/k/a Prosight Specialty Insurance) is an intended third-party beneficiary of the contract.

 

“California law permits third party beneficiaries to enforce the terms of a contract made for their benefit.” (Citation omitted.) That authority is codified in Civil Code  [*1022]  section 1559, which states: “A contract, made expressly for the benefit of a third person, may be enforced by him at any time before the parties thereto rescind it.”

. . .

 

 [*1023] 

On the other hand, “the third person need not be named or identified individually to be an express beneficiary.” (Citations omitted.) “A third party may enforce a contract where he shows that he is a member of a class of persons for whose benefit it was made.” (Citations omitted.)

While intent is pivotal, there is no requirement that “both of the contracting parties must intend to benefit the third party … .” (Citation omitted.) Rather, “it is sufficient that the promisor must have understood that the promisee had such intent.” (Citations omitted.) Thus, a third party will qualify as an intended beneficiary where “the circumstances indicate that the promisee”—here, Mobile—“intends to give the beneficiary the benefit of the promised performance.” (Rest.2d., supra, § 302, subd. (1)(b).)

(Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1021-22, 1023 [bold emphasis added].)

 

Nor did Plaintiff plead that it is an assignee of the right to sue to enforce the insurance contract. 

Finally, Plaintiff’s argument that New York Marine & General was acting as agent for Plaintiff in entering into the insurance contract is not persuasive, as nowhere in the insurance policy is Plaintiff Coaction Specialty Insurance Group, Inc. (f/k/a Prosight Specialty Insurance) identified as a party to the contract. 

Plaintiff was previously warned that it would only be given one final opportunity to pleads facts demonstrating that it is the real party in interest as to this cause of action. That opportunity has now been exhausted.

The demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
