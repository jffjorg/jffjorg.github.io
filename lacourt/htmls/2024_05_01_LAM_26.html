DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 19STCV32575    Hearing Date: May 1, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

 

HEATHER A. AKER BUCK,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

ALLSTATE NORTHBROOK INDEMNITY COMPANY, ET AL.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:       19STCV32575

 

 Hearing Date: May 1, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant allstate northbrook indemnity coMpany’s motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication

 

 

 

Background

            On July 18, 2016, Plaintiff Heather A. Aker Buck (“Plaintiff”) suffered personal injury as a result of a car accident with non-party Alyssa R. Garcia.  Plaintiff filed a lawsuit against Garcia on February 3, 2017.  Plaintiff settled her lawsuit against Garcia for Garcia’s policy limits in the amount of $25,000, far less than her alleged injuries due to the accident.. 

            Plaintiff holds an auto liability insurance policy with Defendant Allstate Northbrook Indemnity Company.  Plaintiff submitted her claim to Allstate on July 18, 2016 for the Garcia accident.  On August 22, 2018, Plaintiff informed Allstate of her settlement with Garcia and made a formal demand under the Underinsured Motorist coverage of her Allstate policy.  Plaintiff submitted her bills and invoices in support of her demand. 

            On May 31, 2019, Allstate offered Buck $28,500 to settle her claim.  Plaintiff alleges the offer was far below the $400,000 in damages she suffered. Plaintiff alleges Allstate failed to provide any basis for its low offer and appeared to ignore the medical reports and billings Plaintiff had provided.  Plaintiff claims Allstate’s refusal to pay Plaintiff’s claim in full was the result of bad faith. 

            On September 12, 2019, Plaintiff filed this action against Allstate alleging (1) breach of insurance contract; (2) insurance bad faith; and (3) declaratory relief.  On December 3, 2019, the parties stipulated to stay this action pending arbitration.  On April 12, 2023, parties stated at a Post-Arbitration Status Conference that the parties had settled the underlying uninsured motorist claim and lifted the stay on this action

            On October 5, 2023, Defendant Allstated filed the Motion for Summary Judgment or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication currently before this Court.  On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.  A reply was filed on April 26, 2024.

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  CCP § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense.  (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

“If the defendant meets this burden, then the burden of production shifts to the plaintiff to establish the existence of a triable issue of material fact.  [Citation.]”  (Donohue v. AMN Services, LLC (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1068, 1077.)  “A triable issue of material fact may not be created by speculation or a ‘stream of conjecture and surmise.’  [Citations.]  Instead, the plaintiff must produce ‘substantial responsive evidence.’  [Citation.]”  (Miller v. Fortune Commercial Corp. (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 214, 221.)  “There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.  [Citation.]’  [Citation.]”  (Gabrielle A. v. County of Orange (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 1268, 1282.)

Discussion

Parties’ Positions

            Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment

            Defendant argues the breach of contract fails as a matter of law, because Defendant undisputedly paid Plaintiff the full Underinsured Motorist (“UIM”) policy limit.  Defendant also argues the breach of contract claim fails as a matter of law, because it is duplicative of the insurance bad faith claim. 

            Defendant argues the insurance bad faith claim also fails as a matter of law.  Defendant argues Plaintiff’s claim is now limited to delay in payment, since Defendant has paid fully the policy limit amount in settlement of Plaintiff’s UIM claim.  Defendant argues that it is not liable for insurance bad faith based on the undisputed facts, which are (1) it was actively investigating the claim during the period of delay; (2) Plaintiff refused to appear for deposition in violation of her contractual obligation to cooperate, providing Defendant with an absolute defense to any claim that she should have been paid sooner than July 13, 2022, after Defendant was finally able to depose her; and (3) the undisputed facts raised legitimate questions about Buck’s ongoing symptoms and treatments, whether they were the result of the Garcia accident or conditions that preexisted the Garcia accident; (4) the amount Plaintiff’s damages and as a result, the value of her claim.  Defendant argues the undisputed evidence establishes that there was a good faith, a genuine dispute over Plaintiff’s claim, and no bad faith claim can therefore be maintained.   

            Defendant argues the declaratory relief claim fails as a matter of law.  Defendant argues Plaintiff has an adequate legal remedy and she only alleges past wrongs. 

            Defendant argues the punitive damages claim fails as a matter of law.  Defendant argues there is no evidence of malice, fraud or oppression, nor is there any evidence that the wrongful conduct was committed or ratified by an officer director or managing agent.

            Plaintiff’s Opposition

            Plaintiff argues whether an insurer has committed insurance bad faith is a question of fact for the jury and can only be determined as an issue of law where the facts are undisputed and reasonable minds could only conclude that there was no bad faith.  Plaintiff argues the genuine dispute doctrine can only be resolved as a question of law where the evidence is undisputed and viewing the facts in the light most favorable to the insured, a jury could not find that the insurer acted unreasonably. 

            Plaintiff argues there are triable issues of fact regarding whether Allstate performed a thorough investigation and fair evaluation of her claim.  Plaintiff argues Defendant had originally reserved $65,000 in additional money to Garcia’s insurance payment for Plaintiff’s UIM claim.  However, Allstate reduced that amount to $28,315.04 and $39,455.04 on May 28, 2019.  Plaintiff argues Defendant’s re-evaluation failed to take into account Plaintiff’s serious injuries and extensive medical records.  Plaintiff argues Defendant selectively relied only on certain facts that supported Defendant’s position and disregarded others that supported Plaintiff’s position. 

            Plaintiff argues Defendant’s bad faith is also evidenced by the fact that Defendant waited until July 5, 2022, over three years from its initial re-evaluation, to conduct another re-evaluation.  Plaintiff argues Defendant claims it only learned of Plaintiff’s serious injuries after her deposition on June 30, 2022.  Plaintiff argues this is evidence of bad faith given that her serious injuries were reflected in the medical records originally submitted to Defendant much earlier.  Plaintiff argues the low-ball settlement initially offered ($28,315.03) was followed by two additional offers of $32,500 and $36,500.  Plaintiff argues the undisputed evidence establishes that Defendant’s initial valuation of Plaintiff’s claim was $28,315.03 to $39,455.03.  Plaintiff argues Defendant’s decision to offer the lowest of its evaluated range followed by likewise low-ball offers is evidence of bad faith.  Plaintiff argues her own insurance expert testifies that the failure to offer the $65,000 reserved initially was itself bad faith and violated insurance industry custom and practice.  Plaintiff argues Defendant ignored Plaintiff’s severe injuries, multiple surgeries and years of treatment in making these low-ball offers. 

            Plaintiff argues there are also triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant engaged in bad faith tactics designed to frustrate Plaintiff’s right to promptly receive benefits due to her. Plaintiff argues Defendant refused to cooperate in appointing an arbitrator to resolve Plaintiff’s UIM claim for over two years.  Plaintiff also argues Defendant unreasonably and improperly filed a separate lawsuit against Plaintiff for the purpose of harassing her. 

            Plaintiff also argues Defendant’s claim that she refused to cooperate in the investigation of her claim misconstrues the evidence.  Moreover, Plaintiff argues an insured’s breach of a cooperation clause does not excuse the insurer of its duty of good faith and fair dealing.  Plaintiff argues an insurer’s duty of good faith is not conditional as it is absolute. 

            Plaintiff argues Defendant can still be held liable for breach of contract based on unreasonable delay in paying policy benefits as there are triable issues of fact remaining as to whether Defendant unreasonably delayed in paying benefits and therefore summary judgment must be denied as to the contract claim. 

            Plaintiff argues triable issues of fact also exist as to her punitive damages claim.  Plaintiff argues summary adjudication of the punitive damages claim is improper when the bad faith insurance claim still survives. 

            Defendant’s Reply

            Defendant reiterates on reply that there was a genuine dispute over Plaintiff’s general damages.  Defendant argues it reduced Plaintiff’s medical expenses to the amount accepted by her medical providers, as allowed under Howell v. Hamilton Meats & Provisions (2011) 52 Cal.4th 541.  Defendant argues the dispute was therefore not over her medical expenses but her general damages, which are notoriously difficult to value.

            Defendant also maintains Plaintiff violated her duty to appear for deposition.  Defendant argues it discovered new information after her deposition that justified increasing the claim payment.  Defendant maintains Plaintiff’s refusal to sit for deposition is a complete defense to Plaintiff’s claims. 

            Defendant argues it did not unreasonably delay, because it was investigating Plaintiff’s claims.  Defendant disagrees that it was unreasonable to question Plaintiff’s initial claim given Plaintiff’s medical history and her subsequent accidents.  Defendant also disputes that it is required to pay its reserves before completing its investigation. 

            Defendant argues the punitive damages claim fails, because Plaintiff does not have any evidence of malice, fraud or oppression or ratification or authorization of the alleged wrongful conduct by a managing agent, officer or director.

Triable Issues of Fact Remain

            1.  Applicable Law

            “Every contract imposes on each party an implied duty of good faith and fair dealing.  Simply stated, the burden imposed is that neither party will do anything which will injure the right of the other to receive the benefits of the agreement.  Or, to put it another way, the ‘implied covenant imposes upon each party the obligation to do everything that the contract presupposes they will do to accomplish its purpose.’”  (Chateau Chamberay Homeowners Ass'n v. Associated Intern. Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 335, 345-346.)  In the insurance context, the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing with respect to handling a claim “is not the requirement mandated by the terms of the policy itself-to defend, settle, or pay” but the requirement that the insurer must act “fairly and in good faith in discharging its contractual responsibilities.”  (Id. at 346.)  “The reasonableness of an insurer's conduct is ordinarily a question of fact, except in the exceptional instance when only one reasonable inference can be drawn from the evidence.”  (Mt. Hawley Insurance Company v. Lopez (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1424.)

            In order to hold an insurer liable for its delay or denial in the payment of policy benefits as bad faith, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the insurer acted unreasonably or without proper cause.  (Id. at 347.)  In California, an insurer cannot be held liable for conduct in handling a claim where a genuine dispute exists over either the existence of coverage or the amount of a particular claim and it advances its side of the dispute.  (Id.)

            Thus, under the “genuine dispute” doctrine, an insurer relying on the advice and opinions of independent experts may be able to assert the genuine dispute doctrine as a complete defense, depending on whether the investigation and the expert were subject to bias or bad faith distortion.  (Id. at 348 (reliance upon expert testimony in evaluating claim does not “automatically” protect an insurer from a bad faith claim).)  “Where the parties rely on expert opinions, even a substantial disparity in estimates for the scope and cost of repairs does not, by itself, suggest the insurer acted in bad faith. Despite the ‘special relationship’ between an insurer and its insureds, an insurer may give its own interests consideration equal to that it gives the interests of its insureds.” (Fraley v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1293.)  Likewise, the “mistaken or erroneous withholding of policy benefits, if reasonable or if based on a legitimate dispute as to the insurer's liability under California law, does not expose the insurer to bad faith liability.”  (Chateau Chamberay Homeowners Ass'n, supra, 90 Cal. App.4th at 347.)

However, an insurer’s reliance on expert opinion to deny benefits does not automatically insulate it from liability for bad faith denial of claim.  (Chateau, supra, 90 Cal.App.4th at 348.)  For example, if an insured were able to present evidence of one of the following scenarios, whether a “genuine dispute” existed had to be determined by a jury:  “(1) the insurer was guilty of misrepresenting the nature of the investigatory proceedings; (2) the insurer's employees lied during the depositions or to the insured; (3) the insurer dishonestly selected its experts; (4) the insurer's experts were unreasonable; and (5) the insurer failed to conduct a thorough investigation.”  (Id.)  (citations omitted).

            The “reasonableness of the insurer's decisions and actions must be evaluated as of the time that they were made; the evaluation cannot fairly be made in the light of subsequent events which may provide evidence of the insurer's errors.”  (Id.)  “Although an insurer's bad faith is ordinarily a question of fact to be determined by a jury by considering the evidence of motive, intent and state of mind, the question becomes one of law when, because there are no conflicting inferences, reasonable minds could not differ… Thus, the issue of bad faith may, in specific instances, be treated as an issue of law.”  (Id. at 350 (citations omitted).)

            2.  Defendant fails to establish that it acted reasonably and in good faith as a matter of law and whether there was a genuine  

            Plaintiff submitted a UIM claim to Defendant for $75,000 after settling with the other driver for policy limits of $25,000 in September 2018.  (Defendant’s SSUMF No. 8.)  Defendant submits the declaration of its adjuster to establish that its rejection of Plaintiff’s $75,000 claim and its offers ranging from $28,500 to $36,500 were made in good faith after a reasonable investigation.  (Zogg Dec., ¶2.)  Zogg’s testimony does not establish that the offer was reasonable as a matter of law.  Zogg does not cite to any expert testimony relied upon her to determine the reasonableness of Plaintiff’s demand and merely states that the offer was reasonable based on the information available.  (Zogg Dec., ¶14.)  Zogg testifies she did not believe $75,000 to be a reasonable valuation of Plaintiff’s claim, because Plaintiff had prior injuries, her nasal fracture seemed to resolve after the accident and Plaintiff suffered injuries due to subsequent accidents.  (Zogg Dec., ¶¶13-14.)  Zogg’s explanation for her assessment of Plaintiff’s value is conclusory and does not establish reasonableness or even a genuine dispute as a matter of law.  Based on Zogg’s testimony, the Court cannot determine precisely what Zogg reviewed and what was done to investigate the claim. 

In fact, Defendant does not submit all the medical records it received from Plaintiff, rather Defendant only submits select “excerpted pages” of those medical records.  (Zogg Dec., ¶13; Notice of Filing (“NOF”), Ex. 11.)  Essentially, Zogg testifies that she thought the value of the claim was $28,500 to $36,500 based on her unexplained assessment of Plaintiff’s medical records, which are not fully provided or discussed.  The Court cannot evaluate whether Defendant’s assessment of Plaintiff’s claim was reasonable and in good faith as a matter of law without copies of what Plaintiff provided to Defendant in support of her claim. 

Even based on the excerpts, a dispute of fact as to whether Defendant’s assessment was reasonable and in good faith exists.  Apart from a multitude of other injuries, Plaintiff suffered from a fractured nose and was to “proceed with surgery.”  (NOF, Ex. 11, p. 151.)  Plaintiff raises this issue as well, arguing that the offer was not reasonable in light of the severe injuries she suffered, undermining Defendant’s reliance on preexisting injuries and other subsequent accidents as justification for the refusal to pay Plaintiff’s $75,000. 

Moreover, Defendant ultimately paid Plaintiff $75,000 on her claim in July 2022, claiming that the new offer was based on new information Plaintiff provided in her deposition.  (Zogg Dec., ¶¶20-21; NOF Ex. 4 at CN000390 and CN000388 and Ex. 14.)  Defendant fails to identify what new information they received that they did not have before.  Exhibit 4, Bates Nos. CN000390 and CN000388, do not identify any new information upon which the claim was reevaluated.  They merely indicate that the claim was reevaluated.  Document Bates No. CN000388 merely indicates that the full value of Plaintiff’s claim would be paid.  Exhibit 14 is merely the settlement check.  As such, Defendant fails to establish that its ultimate decision to pay the full claim was justified by any new information that it did not previously have. 

Plaintiff also submits evidence that nothing new was revealed at her deposition.  Plaintiff submits any information of value to the assessment had been in Defendant’s possession since she provided her medical records.  (Frangiamore Decl., ¶¶ 8, 10-17, 35; HamrickDecl., Ex. A - CN_165-166, CN_593_595; Hamrick Decl., Ex. C - ALL_3111-3113, ALL_3114-3117, ALL_3121-3122.) 

3.  Defendant fails to establish that Plaintiff refused to be deposed in breach of her obligations under the Insurance Code or the policy

            Defendant argues Plaintiff refused to cooperate and appear for deposition in violation of the Insurance Code and the policy.  (Defendant’s SSUMF Nos. 25-28.)  However, based on Defendant’s own evidence, Plaintiff did not unreasonably refuse to attend.  Plaintiff had to reschedule several times due to unavailability and ultimately wanted to schedule the deposition after the arbitrator had been selected.  (Id.)  Plaintiff’s evidence confirms that she did not want to sit for deposition until after an arbitrator was selected, because Allstate delayed in selecting an arbitrator.  (Plaintiffs’ Additional Material Facts (“AMF”) Nos. 17-19.)  Plaintiff was deposed on June 30, 2022, shortly after an arbitrator was selected in May 2022. (Plaintiff’s AMF No. 19.)  A triable issue of fact therefore exists as to whether Plaintiff “refused” to sit for deposition in violation of Insurance Code §11580.2(f)(3), which provides that a deposition may be taken without leave of court and in accordance with CCP §2025.010, et seq., or in breach of the policy terms. 

Triable issues of fact remain as to the breach of contract cause of action

            Defendant fails to establish that it is entitled to summary judgment on the breach of contract claim as an issue of law.  Defendant argues it cannot be held liable for breach if it paid the full amount of Plaintiff’s claim.  Defendant overlooks Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim based on the delay in paying her claim and the failure to properly investigate her claim.  “Unreasonable delay in paying policy benefits or paying less than the amount due is actionable withholding of benefits which may constitute a breach of contract as well as bad faith giving rise to damages in tort.”  (Intergulf Development LLC v. Superior Court (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 16, 20.) 

            Moreover, the breach of contract claim is not duplicative of the insurance bad faith claim.  Breach of contract sounds in contract and can only support contract damages, while insurance bad faith can sound in tort, giving rise to tort damages including punitive damages.  (Id.)  By definition, punitive damages are not recoverable on a contract claim.

Triable issues of fact remain as to the punitive damages claim

            “In the usual case, the question of whether the defendant's conduct will support an award of punitive damages is for the trier of fact, since the degree of punishment depends on the peculiar circumstances of each case.  But the issue may be resolved on summary judgment, giving due regard to the higher proof standard. While the clear and convincing evidentiary standard is a stringent one, it does not impose on a plaintiff the obligation to prove a case for punitive damages at summary judgment.  However, where the plaintiff's ultimate burden of proof will be by clear and convincing evidence, the higher standard of proof must be taken into account in ruling on a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, since if a plaintiff is to prevail on a claim for punitive damages, it will be necessary that the evidence presented meet the higher evidentiary standard.  Summary judgment on the issue of punitive damages is proper only when no reasonable jury could find the plaintiff's evidence to be clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression.”  (Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 762; Pacific Gas and Electric Company v. Superior Court (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1150, 1158–1159.)

            As the party moving for adjudication of Plaintiff’s punitive damages claim, Defendant bears the initial burden of production on Plaintiff’s punitive damages claim.  (CCP §437c(f)(1) and (2); Johnson & Johnson v. Supr. Ct. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 761.)  A defendant may satisfy its initial burden on summary judgment by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Summary judgment still requires, however, the presentation of evidence in the form of “affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice” to demonstrate that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain necessary evidence to establish his or her claim.  (Id. at 854.)

            For this reason, a defendant does not meet its burden under CCP §437(c)(o)(1) by merely “pointing out” or “arguing” that the plaintiff does not possess or cannot reasonably obtain necessary evidence.  (Id.; Lona v. Citibank, N.A. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 89 (defendant bank did not meet its burden on summary judgment of wrongful foreclosure by merely arguing that plaintiff lacked evidence to support her claim and only documentary evidence submitted were the loan documents); Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 890 (defendant did not establish plaintiff’s lack of evidence or inability to reasonably obtain evidence of plaintiff’s ability and willingness to perform by pointing to plaintiff’s refusal to answer certain questions during deposition on grounds of a meritless privacy objection; such a refusal, regardless of its merit, was neither an admission nor a factually devoid discovery response).)

            Defendant fails to establish as an issue of law that it acted in good faith or that there was a genuine dispute over the amount to be paid on the claim after reasonable investigation.  Defendant cannot merely argue that Plaintiff lacks clear and convincing evidence of malice based on despicable conduct undertaken in conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights.  Given the existence of triable issues of fact on the underlying cause of action for insurance bad faith, the Court cannot find that Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages fails as a matter of law.

Plaintiff’s declaratory relief cause of action fails as a matter of law

            Declaratory relief is improper where “the rights of the complaining party have crystallized into a cause of action for past wrongs, all relationship between the parties has ceased to exist and there is no conduct of the parties subject to regulation by the court.”  (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners, LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 376.)

            However, as explained in Osseous, this does not meant that declaratory relief is improper in every case where a breach of contract alleged.  The Court of Appeal created a conceptual framework classifying declaratory relief into three types for the purpose of determining whether the trial court erred by dismissing a declaratory relief cause of action. In a “ ‘Type 1’ declaratory relief cause of action, the complaint alleges only a past breach of contract, a breach of contract remedy is available, and declaratory relief is unnecessary to guide future conduct.  (Id. at pp. 365, 366–368.)  The court must dismiss the Type 1 type of declaratory relief claims.  (Id.)  A “Type 2” declaratory relief cause of action alleges an actual and ongoing controversy, such as a continuing contractual relationship, and future consequences that depend on the court's interpretation of the contract.  (Id. at pp. 369–371.)  A trial court must not dismiss a Type 2 declaratory relief cause of action. (Id. at p. 365.)  A “Type 3” declaratory relief cause of action alleges a current controversy over a past breach of contract and the potential a declaration of the parties' rights under a contract might be necessary to guide the parties' future conduct in a continuing contractual relationship.  (Id. at pp. 374–376.)  A trial court has discretion to dismiss a Type 3 declaratory relief cause of action.  (Id. at p. 365.)

            Plaintiff’s declaratory relief claim is a Type 1 declaratory relief claim under Osseous and must be dismissed.  As such, summary adjudication is granted as to the declaratory relief cause of action.     

Conclusion and ORDER

            Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.  Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the third cause of action for declaratory relief is GRANTED.  Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of contract, second cause of action for insurance bad faith and punitive damages is DENIED.
