DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02835    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 8


Tentative Ruling


HEARING DATE: June 12, 2024


CASE NUMBER: 23TRCV02835


CASE NAME: Nancy Jarmen Betlazar, individually and as Trustee of The Betlazar Family Trust Dated November 8, 2013 v. Angour Corporation, et al.


MOVING PARTY: Defendants, Angour Corporation and Amir H. Amiri


RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Nancy Jarmen Betlazar, individually ad as trustee of The Betlazar Family Trust Dated November 8, 2013 (No Opposition)


TRIAL DATE: May 5, 2025


MOTION: (1) Motion for Leave to File Amended Answer

Tentative Rulings: (1) GRANTED. Defendants shall file the amended answer as a stand-alone document, not as an attachment to another document, within 5 court days.

I. BACKGROUND

A. Factual


On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff, Nancy Jarmen Betlazar, individually and

as Trustee of The Betlazar Family Trust dated November 8, 2013 (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant, Angour Corporation, Amir H. Amiri, and DOES 1 through 100. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Money Lent; (2) Money Had and Received; (3) Account Stated; (4) Breach of Contract; (5) Fraud and Deceit – Intentional Misrepresentation and Concealment; (6) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (7) Financial Abuse of Elder; (8) Unfair Competition; (9) Accounting; and (10) Imposter of a Constructive Trust

On October 20, 2023, Defendants, Angour Corporation and Amir H. Amiri filed their original answer. After some discovery and investigation, Defendants seek Leave to Amend their Answer.

B. Procedural

On May 15, 2024, Defendants filed a Motion for Leave to File Amended Answer. On June 6, 2024, Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Opposition. To date, no opposition has been filed.

II. ANALYSIS 


A. Legal Standard

California Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1) provides, in relevant part: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.¿ The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”¿ (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.)¿ Ordinarily, the court will not consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading in ruling on a motion for leave since grounds for a demurrer or motion to strike are premature.¿ (See Id. at p. 1048.)¿ The court, however, does have discretion to deny leave to amend where a proposed amendment fails to state a valid defense as a matter of law and the defect cannot be cured by further amendment.¿ (See California Casualty General Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 281, overruled on other grounds by Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)¿

Under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.¿ Under California Rule of Court Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted, and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.¿ In most cases, the factors for timeliness are: (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.¿ If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.¿ (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)¿ Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.¿ (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.)¿

B. Discussion

Preliminarily, this Court notes that Defendants have stated that Plaintiff stipulated to the requested relief to allow Defendants to amend their answer.

Defendants note that while they originally have asserted an affirmative defense based on various statutes of limitation in their operative Answer, the specific statutes cited in that affirmative defense must now be updated to address the gravamen of certain causes of action raised by Plaintiff that have been elaborated upon through discovery, and to identify certain subsections that were inadvertently left out of Defendants’ original Answer. Further, Defendants have complied with California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324(a), as they have: (1) included a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which is serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) states which allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) states which allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Further, Defendants have not completely complied with California Rule of Court Rule 3.1324(b). Although their moving papers do speak to: (1) the effect of the amendment, (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper, (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier, Defendants do not do so via the separate declaration. But because Plaintiff has stipulated to the filing of an amended answer, the Court will not require strict compliance with Rule 3.1324(a)’s requirements.

III. CONCLUSION


Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ Motion for Leave to Amended Answer is GRANTED. Defendant shall file the amended answer as a stand-alone document, not as an attachment to another document, within 5 court days. Unless waived by all parties, Defendants are ordered to give notice.

I. BACKGROUND¿¿  

 ¿ 

            On May 5, 2024, Plaintiff, Lilla Dee (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Rishi Khatri, Dragonfly Holding Corp., Holistic Mental Health LLC, Pannado Group LLC, and DOES 1 through 10. On June 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Fraud; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Legal Malpractice; (4) Appointment of a Receiver; (5) Accounting of Dragonfly Holding Corp.; (6) Accounting of Holistic Mental Health LLC; (7) Accounting of Pannado Group, LLC; (8) Judicial Dissolution of Dragonfly (under Wyoming Statutes § 17-16-1430); (9) Judicial Dissolution of Holistic Health (under California Corporation Code § 17707.04; (10) Judicial Dissolution of Pannado (under Arizona Revised Statutes §29-3804); and (11) Defamation (Libel).

 

            Plaintiff previously brought an ex parte Application for Appointment of Receiver over Defendants Dragonfly Holdings Corp. and Holistic Mental Health LLC and a related Temporary Restraining Order and Preliminary Injunction. The Court continued the hearing on that application to June 12, 2024.  In the Application, Plaintiff asserts that she is a 50% owner of all entities involved, and seeks appointment of a receiver over the following mental health treatment entities, (1) Dragonfly Holding Corp. (“Dragonfly”); and (2) Holistic Mental Health LLC (“Holistic Health”) (collectively, “Clear Mind”).  The Application asserts a variety of alleged wrongdoing by Defendant Khatri, including but not limited to his (a) using the Clear Mind entities as a “front,” (b) impersonating a medical doctor to staff, patients, and insurance companies, (c) providing medical treatment to patients at the subject treatment facilities, (d) likely to be destroying records and covering his wrongdoings that the State of California is actively investigating; and (e) solely in charge of oversight of Clear Mind’s ketamine program without Ms. Dee having access to the facilities to protect her financial interests and the claimed interests of patients and staff.  

 

II. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

A. Legal Standard  

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 564(b) provides that the court has authority to appoint a receiver in any of the following pertinent circumstances: in an action by a vendor to vacate a fraudulent purchase of property, or by a creditor to subject any property or fund to the creditor's claim, or between partners or others jointly owning or interested in any property or fund, on the application of the plaintiff, or of any party whose right to or interest in the property or fund, or the proceeds thereof, is probable, and where it is shown that the property or fund is in danger of being lost, removed, or materially injured; and in all other cases where necessary to preserve the property or rights of any party. The appointment of a receiver is a drastic remedy to be utilized only in “exceptional cases.”  As such, a receiver should not be appointed unless absolutely essential and because no other remedy will serve its purpose. (City & County of San Francisco v. Daley, (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 734, 744 (“Daley”).) A plaintiff who seeks appointment of a receiver of certain property under Code of Civil Procedure section 564(b)(1) has the burden to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that plaintiff has a joint interest with defendant in the property, that the property is in danger of being lost, removed or materially injured, and that plaintiff's right to possession is probable. (AlhambraShumway Mines, Inc. v. Alhambra Gold Mine Corp., (1953) 116 Cal.App.2d 869, 873.)

 

B. Discussion 

 

Plaintiff’s Argument Summary

 

            Plaintiff argues that the grounds to immediately appoint a receiver exist because: (1) Defendant Khatri has been and continues to actively impersonate a licensed medical doctor and engages in the unauthorized practice of medicine; (2) Defendant Khatri will likely destroy evidence of his wrongdoing; (3) Defendant Khatri’s unauthorized medical decision-making and potential record “tampering” poses a direct risk to the health and safety of the patients, making immediate intervention crucial; (4) Defendant Khatri has engaged in wrongful past practices, raising a risk that he will “cover up” his potential legally actionable wrongdoings; (5) Defendant Khatri is allegedly running Dragonfly without any corporate governance limitation; (6) that Defendant Khatri is fully running Clear Mind by himself without Plaintiff to ensure that Clear Mind is being run within compliance, and that an independent receiver would ensure that the Clear Mind facilities comply with health care regulations and standards and that only authorized medical professionals perform medical services; (7) that Defendant Khatri has a history of fraud and deceit; (8) That a receiver can provide necessary financial oversight, potentially neutrally identifying financial misconduct, mismanagement, or other wrongdoing; (9) that Defendant Khatri’s actions, including how he allegedly orchestrated a “coup” over Clear Mind’s operations – are evidence of “shareholder oppression”; and (10) that the appointment of a receiver can help restore trust among patient, staff, and stakeholders, ensuring that the Clear Mind facilities operate in compliance with legal and ethical standards.

 

Defendant Khatri’s Argument Summary

 

            In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to submit admissible evidence to support appointment of a receiver.  The Opposition papers assert that Defendant and his counsel have established a series of interim measures to address a number of the concerns Plaintiff has raised including steps to preserve evidence, to admonish staff to maintain records in light of the pending dispute between the owners, to provide visibility for Plaintiff into the companies’ financial accounts, and other measures.  Defendant also argues that if there was a need for interim relief, that this Court has less dramatic means available. Defendant also argues for the posting of a bond to secure against the claimed risk that the expenses and involvement of a receiver could result in the destruction of the very entities who Plaintiff seeks to guard and protect.   

 

Court’s Disposition

 

This Court’s tentative ruling is to DENY the request for the appointment of a receiver. First, this Court notes that there is an ongoing and uncompleted Department of Consumer Affairs investigation into the claimed authorized practice of medicine by Defendant Khatri. Fort he Court to authorize a receiver when the DCA may make unknown determinations as to the future of the companies or the future involvement of Mr. Khatri in them seems to the Court to be premature.  Moreover, there are numerous factual disputes between the parties that may need an evidentiary hearing with cross examination to resolve, factual disputes that have bearing on what the role of the Receiver would be.

 

            Next, Plaintiff argues that immediate appointment of a receiver is warranted due to claimed likelihood of the destruction of records and preventing cover-up of misconduct. However, this Court notes that the Opposition provides proof that Defendant has retained SETEC Investigations, a computer forensics firm, to ensure that all electronic records, including documents, emails, and WhatsApp messages were and are preserved. Moreover, pursuant to the opposition brief, Defendant Khatri argues that Plaintiff will continue to have view access to the companies’ Quickbooks and financial records.

 

            Further, this Court notes that the provisional remedy of a receiver is very expensive, based on the submitted Receiver expenses the Court has reviewed in other pending cases.  The Court tentatively finds that Plaintiff has not carried her burden of demonstrating that a less drastic remedy or remedies would be sufficient. (Daley, supra, 16 Cal.App.4th at 744.)  One of those other remedies that Plaintiff in facts seeks by this ex parte application is a temporary restraining order and/or preliminary injunction whereby Defendant Khatri is enjoined from a wide range of activities.  Based on the Opposition papers and declarations, the Court believes that the defense may well be willing to stipulate to a TRO or preliminary injunction to maintain the status quo as to the interim measures put in place to address the claim of spoliation of evidence and the denial of access by Plaintiff to various books and records of the companies including the Quickbooks accounts.   

 

            The Court will inquire at the hearing whether Defendant Khatri would be willing to stipulate to a preliminary injunction that he refrain from the practice of medicine, and to maintain the interim measures regarding access to financial records, and continued preservation of evidence.

 

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion for Appointment of Receiver is DENIED. Pending oral argument, this Court will discuss a potential stipulation to a preliminary injunction on Defendant Khatri that he refrain from the practice of medicine, refrain from holding himself out to the public and to patients as a licensed medical doctor, and to maintain the interim measures regarding access to financial records preservation of evidence.
