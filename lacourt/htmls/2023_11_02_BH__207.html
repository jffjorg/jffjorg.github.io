DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV37567    Hearing Date: November 2, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING - NO. 1

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

November 2, 2023

CASE NUMBER

21STCV37567

MOTION

Motion to Strike Portions of Fifth Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Dale Prokupek, M.D.; Dale Prokupek, M.D., Inc.; Millennium Medical Associates, Inc.; and Andrew Gelis

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Pericles Chamis

 

MOTION

 

Defendants Dale Prokupek, M.D.; Dale Prokupek M.D., Inc.; Millennium Medical Associates, Inc.; and Andrew Gelis (“Defendants”) seek to strike portions of Plaintiff Pericles Chamis’s (“Plaintiff”) Fifth Amended Complaint for medical malpractice, breach of fiduciary duty, and intentional misrepresentation.  Plaintiff opposes, and Defendants reply.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of (1) the Notice of Ruling Re: Defendant Jeanne K. Hershey-Weber’s (“Hershey-Weber”) Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of the Third Amended Complaint, filed by Defendant Hershey-Weber on April 10, 2023; (2) Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Add a Claim for Punitive Damages, filed on January 19, 2023; and (3) the Notice of Ruling Re: Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Add a Claim for Punitive Damages, filed by Defendants Dale Prokupek, M.D.; Dale Prokupek M.D., Inc.; and Millennium Medical Associates, Inc. on April 25, 2023.  Plaintiff opposes the request on the grounds that the records are hearsay. 

 

Judicial notice may be taken of records of any court in this state.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d)(1).)  Because the requested documents are all part of the Court’s record for this case, the Court may take judicial notice of them.  (Ibid.)   However, “while courts are free to take judicial notice of the existence of each document in a court file, including the truth of results reached, they may not take judicial notice of the truth of hearsay statements in decisions and court files.  Courts may not take judicial notice of allegations in affidavits, declarations and probation reports in court records because such matters are reasonably subject to dispute and therefore require formal proof.”  (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [cleaned up].)  Accordingly, the Court takes judicial notice of the existence of these documents as a matter of court record, but not the truth of the arguments or allegations contained therein.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.) 

 

Defendants move to strike the following from the Fifth Amended Complaint:

 

·         Page 3, Lines 2-3: the words “established by Defendant Dale Prokupek to avoid collection of judgments against the business proceeds of Dale Prokupek M.D. Inc.”

 

·         Page 3, Line 7: the word “fraudulently”

 

·         Page 3, Lines 9-10: the words “to falsify medical records if and when their fraudulent activities were uncovered or a patient was injured”

 

·         Paragraphs 40-41, 44-47, and 55-62 in their entirety

 

·         Page 15, Lines 3-4 the words “and to further the criminal enterprise established by Defendant Dr. Prokupek and Defendant Soma Surgery Center”

 

·         Page 17, Lines 23-24: the words “and to further the criminal enterprise established by Defendants Dr. Prokupek and Soma Surgery Center”

 

·         Page 19, Lines 25-26: the words “and to further the criminal enterprise established by Defendants Dr. Prokupek and Soma Surgery Center”

 

Defendants move to strike these allegations on the bases that they are improper and directly contravene the Court’s prior Orders.

 

By way of background, on April 6, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant Hershey-Weber’s demurrers to the second and third causes of action alleged in the Third Amended Complaint on the grounds that when Plaintiff substituted her in as a Doe defendant, Plaintiff did not allege any specific facts or conduct as to Defendant Hershey-Weber.  (April 6, 2023 Minute Order.)  The Court granted Plaintiff leave to amend the allegations as to Defendant Hershey-Weber with respect to the second and third causes of action.  (Ibid.)

 

The Court also granted Defendant Hershey-Weber’s unopposed motion to strike the request for punitive damages and related allegations.  (Ibid.)  The Court noted there was an upcoming motion for leave to include punitive damages, and denied leave to amend with respect to the punitive damages claims and allegations until that motion was heard.  (Ibid.)

 

On April 24, 2023, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Add Punitive Damages on the grounds that it was untimely brought and did not present evidence that Defendants fell below the applicable standard of care to show Plaintiff had a substantial probability of prevailing on the merits.

 

Defendants argue that paragraphs 40-62 are improper to include because they “are practically verbatim the ‘Background Facts’ as set forth in the Motion for Leave to Add Punitive Damages on pages 3 to 8; the facts that would have been included in the amended pleading had leave to amend been granted” and that the remaining allegations should be stricken as conclusory.

 

The allegations in paragraphs 40-62 are detailed factual allegations derived from information Plaintiff apparently obtained through discovery and at deposition.  For example, paragraph 40 indicates:

 

Defendant Dr. Prokupek testified at his deposition on October 19, 2022, that: (1) Plaintiff's primary care provider was a Nurse Practitioner named Jeanee Hershey-Weber; (2) Hershey-Weber was the person who improperly instructed Plaintiff on stopping his Xarelto regimen; (3) Hersey-Weber performed minor surgery on Plaintiff without physician supervision; (4) Defendant Dr. Prokupek was not present when Plaintiff was treated by Jeannee Hershey-Weber; (5) Defendant Dr. Prokupek had not seen the patient since 2016 prior performing surgery on Plaintiff on November 4, 2020; (6) Defendant ANDREW GELIS while employed by Defendant MILLENNIUM MEDICAL ASSOCIATES was responsible for altering Plaintiff’s medical records, to cover Hershey-Weber’s improper instructions she gave to Plaintiff regarding stopping his Xarelto regimen, and (7) Defendant SEAN RAVAEI was in charge of the Defendants unlawful scheme to injure Plaintiff. None of these facts are referenced in Plaintiff’s medical records and at no time during the first year of litigation in this matter did Defendants disclose these facts.

 

            That these allegations were also included in Plaintiff’s motion for leave to add punitive damages (which was ultimately denied as untimely and for insufficient evidence that Defendants fell below the applicable standard of care) does not mean the allegations themselves are inappropriate to add to the complaint.  These allegations specify the various defendants’ conduct and are relevant to issues separate and apart from punitive damages claims.  The Court therefore denies Plaintiff’s request to strike paragraphs 40-41, 44-47, and 55-62.

 

            As for the remaining allegations, the Court declines to strike them at this juncture.  Ultimately, whether Defendants were motivated to avoid collection judgments, fraudulently presented themselves as doctors, or falsified medical records are factual issues and questions to be examined through discovery and ultimately at trial.  The Court cannot, at this stage of the litigation, strike disputed allegations as false. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court denies Defendants’ Motion to Strike in its entirety, and orders Defendants to file and serve an Answer to the Fifth Amended Complaint on or before November 15, 2023. 

 

Defendants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

TENTATIVE RULING - NO. 2

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

Thursday, November 2, 2023

CASE NUMBER

21STCV37567

MOTION

Demurrer

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Andrew Gelis

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Pericles Chamis

 

MOTION

 

On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff Pericles Chamis (“Plaintiff”) filed the fifth amended complaint (“5AC”) against various doctors and a surgery center, alleging causes of action for (1) medical malpractice; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; and (3) intentional misrepresentation.  Defendant Andrew Gelis (“Defendant” or “Gelis”) demurs to all three causes of action for failure to state causes of action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e).

 

            Plaintiff opposes the demurrer and Defendant replies.  On reply, Defendant has requested that the Demurrer be converted to a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.    First Cause of Action – Professional Negligence (Medical Malpractice)

 

“Medical providers must exercise that degree of skill, knowledge, and care ordinarily possessed and exercised by members of their profession under similar circumstances. (Powell v. Kleinman (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 112, 122.)  “Thus, in any medical malpractice action, the plaintiff must establish: (1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Code of Civil Procedure defines “Health care provider” as “any person licensed or certified pursuant to Division 2 (commencing with section 500) of the Business and Professions Code, or licensed pursuant to the Osteopathic Initiative Act, or the Chiropractic Initiative Act, or licensed pursuant to Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1440) of Division 2 of the Health and Safety Code; and any clinic, health dispensary, or health facility, licensed pursuant to Division 2 (commencing with Section 1200) of the Health and Safety Code.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5m, subd. (1).)  “Health care provider” includes the legal representatives of a health care provider.”  (Ibid.)

 

Further, “Professional negligence” is defined as “a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services, which act or omission is the proximate cause of a personal injury or wrongful death, provided that such services are within the scope of services for which the provider is licensed and which are not within any restriction imposed by the licensing agency or licensed hospital.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5, subd. (2).)

 

Here, Gelis argues that the first cause of action alleges that the conduct of the various doctors and nurses attending to Plaintiff before and during the subject medical procedure, fell below the appropriate standard of care, resulting in Plaintiff’s injuries.  However, Gelis is not alleged to be a licensed nurse or doctor, etc., or anyone who provided health care to Plaintiff at all.  Rather, Gelis is alleged to be an employee of “Millennium Medical Associates, Inc.” which allegedly was established by Defendant Dale Prokupek. (Fifth Amended Complaint [5AC] ¶¶ 11, 14, 40) who is allegedly the person who altered Plaintiff’s medical records “after [Dr. Prokupek] found out he was being sued[….]”  (5AC ¶ 85; see also 5AC ¶ 82.)

 

Plaintiff argues in reply, “Based on the information obtained by Plaintiff, he is informed and believes that Gelis provided documentation and medical research to Dr. Prokupek and Nurse Hershey-Weber to mislead Plaintiff into agreeing to stop taking his Xarelto and agree to a very risky surgery at SSC.”  (Opp. at p. 4.)  However, these allegations appear nowhere in the 5AC, but even if they did, Plaintiff does not allege Gelis is a licensed medical professional to whom professional negligence applies.

 

Thus, the 5AC does not allege that Gelis is a “health care provider” or that Gelis “rendered professional services,” to Gelis.  As such, the Court sustains Gelis’ demurrer to the first cause of action for professional negligence.

 

B.     Second Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty – Lack of Informed Consent

 

To prevail on a claim of breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) breach of the fiduciary duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by the breach. (Jameson v. Desta (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1144, 1164.) “A physician has a fiduciary duty to disclose all information material to the patient’s decision when soliciting a patient’s consent to a medical procedure. (Ibid.) 

 

“A claim based on lack of informed consent—which sounds in negligence—arises when the doctor performs a procedure without first adequately disclosing the risks and alternatives.”  (Saxena v. Goffney (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 316, 324; see also Moore v. Regents of University of California (1990) 51 Cal.3d 120, 129 [“[I]n soliciting the patient's consent, a physician has a fiduciary duty to disclose all information material to the patient's decision”].)  “[W]e hold, as an integral part of the physician's overall obligation to the patient there is a duty of reasonable disclosure of the available choices with respect to proposed therapy and of the dangers inherently and potentially involved in each.” (Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 243.) 

 

[T]he patient's right of self-decision is the measure of the physician's duty to reveal.  That right can be effectively exercised only if the patient possesses adequate information to enable an intelligent choice. The scope of the physician's communications to the patient, then, must be measured by the patient's need, and that need is whatever information is material to the decision.  Thus the test for determining whether a potential peril must be divulged is its materiality to the patient's decision.  . . .   There must be a causal relationship between the physician's failure to inform and the injury to the plaintiff. Such causal connection arises only if it is established that had revelation been made consent to treatment would not have been given. Here the record discloses no testimony that had plaintiff been informed of the risks of surgery he would not have consented to the operation.

 

(Id. at p. 245 [cleaned up]

To prevail on a claim for failure to obtain informed consent, a plaintiff must prove (1) the defendant performed a medical procedure on Plaintiff; (2) the defendant did not disclose to Plaintiff the important potential results and risks of and alternatives to the medical procedure; (3) a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would not have agreed to the medical procedure if that person had been adequately informed; and (4) Plaintiff was harmed by a result or risk that the defendant should have explained.  (CACI No. 533.)

 

As to Gelis, Plaintiff alleges that Gelis altered Plaintiff’s medical records “to obscure the cause of Plaintiff’s massive stroke” “after [Dr. Prokupek] found out he was being sued[….]”  which “further delayed the proper diagnosis and treatment of Plaintiff” and “further exasperated his injuries.” (5AC ¶¶ 82, 85, 95-96.)  There is no allegation that Gelis performed any medical procedure on Plaintiff, or that Gelis had any conversation with Plaintiff prior to Plaintiff’s procedure with Dr. Prokupek. 

 

Thus, the Court finds that the 5AC fails to state a cause of action for lack of informed consent as to Gelis. 

 

C.    Third Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

As to Gelis, Plaintiff alleges Gelis was “employed by Defendant Millennium Medical Associates” (5AC ¶¶ 40, 82), a company alleged to be established, owned, and operated by Defendant Prokupek (5AC ¶¶ 11, 118.)  In the course of this employment, Gelis allegedly altered Plaintiff’s medical records “to obscure the cause” of Plaintiff’s stroke after Dr. Prokupek “found out he was being sued” (5AC ¶ 118, 120, 121.)  Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants, working “in concert,” communicated the false information in the altered medical records to Plaintiff’s representatives, which delayed the determination and treatment of Plaintiff’s condition by nearly a year.  (5AC ¶¶ 110-112.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts as to Gelis to withstand demurrer as to the third cause of action.

 

D.    LEAVE TO AMEND

 

A plaintiff has the burden of showing in what manner the complaint could be amended and how the amendment would change the legal effect of the complaint, i.e., state a cause of action. (See The Inland Oversight Committee v City of San Bernardino (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 771, 779; PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 156, 189.) A plaintiff must not only state the legal basis for the amendment, but also the factual allegations sufficient to state a cause of action or claim. (See PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.App.5th at p. 189.) Moreover, a plaintiff does not meet his or her burden by merely stating in the opposition to a demurrer or motion to strike that “if the Court finds the operative complaint deficient, plaintiff respectfully requests leave to amend.” (See Major Clients Agency v Diemer (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1133; Graham v Bank of America (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 618 [asserting an abstract right to amend does not satisfy the burden].)

 

Here, Plaintiff contends in Opposition that “Based on the information obtained by Plaintiff, he is informed and believes that Gelis provided documentation and medical research to Dr. Prokupek and Nurse Hershey-Weber to mislead Plaintiff into agreeing to stop taking his Xarelto and agree to a very risky surgery at SSC.”  (Opp. at p. 4.)  But even if Plaintiff were to add these allegations to the complaint, Plaintiff still does not allege that Gelis is a licensed medical professional who administered healthcare treatment to Plaintiff, to substantiate a claim for professional negligence or failure to obtain informed consent.

 

Plaintiff also explains that “Gelis’ deposition is currently scheduled for December 21, 2023” and “The Defendants text messages, emails, and interoffice memos which have not been produced in discovery to date, will contain the smoking gun evidence to determine each of the Defendant’s role in the Defendants collective actions to intentionally convince Plaintiff to agree to a highly risky and unlawful surgery for financial gain.”  (Opp. at pp. 2-3; 5.)  Plaintiff also asserts “A trial court has discretion to continue a demurrer pending discovery.”  (Opp. at p. 5.) 

 

But professional healthcare licenses are matters of public record.  Therefore, Plaintiff’s ability to plead causes of action against Gelis for professional negligence and failure to obtain informed consent does not hinge on any discovery Plaintiff may obtain.  As such, the Court denies leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court sustains Gelis’s demurrer as to the first and second causes of action for professional negligence (medical malpractice) and breach of fiduciary duty – lack of informed consent without leave to amend.  The Court overrules Gelis’ demurrers regarding the third causes of action. 

 

Further, the Court orders Gelis to file and serve an answer to the Fifth Amended Complaint on or before November 22, 2023.

 

Gelis shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

Case Number: 22SMCV01666    Hearing Date: November 2, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

November 2, 2023

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01666

MOTION

Motion for Terminating and Monetary Sanctions

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Blue Lake Capital LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

(none)

 

MOTION

 

            Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Blue Lake Capital LLC (“Plaintiff”) moves for terminating and monetary sanctions against Defendant and Cross-Complainant Amanda Moore (“Defendant” or “Moore”) for failure to comply with the Court’s September 13 and 20 orders.

 

            Moore has not opposed the motion.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

When a party misuses the discovery process by disobeying a court order to provide discovery, the court in its discretion may impose a terminating sanction by striking a party’s pleading or dismissing the action of the party. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010, subd. (g), 2023.030, subds. (d)(1) & (d)(3); 2025.450, subd. (h), 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).)

 

California discovery law authorizes a range of penalties for a party's refusal to obey a discovery order, including monetary sanctions, evidentiary sanctions, issue sanctions, and terminating sanctions. A court has broad discretion in selecting the appropriate penalty, . . . . Despite this broad discretion, the courts have long recognized that the terminating sanction is a drastic penalty and should be used sparingly. A trial court must be cautious when imposing a terminating sanction because the sanction eliminates a party's fundamental right to a trial, thus implicating due process rights. The trial court should select a sanction that is tailored to the harm caused by the withheld discovery. Sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.

 

(Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604 [cleaned up].)

 

When a party fails to respond to the opposing party's interrogatories, the court should begin by imposing monetary sanctions and ordering the party to respond. If a party then fails to obey an order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction.  In general, a court may not impose issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions unless a party disobeys a court order. 

 

(Moofly Productions, LLC v. Favila (2020) 46 CalApp.5th 1, 11 [cleaned up] [citing Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c)].)  However, “a terminating sanction issued solely because of a failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction is never justified.” (Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 615.)

 

ANALYSIS

            Deposition

 

On November 7, 2022, Moore failed to appear for her Noticed Deposition, to which she had not objected.  (Perkin Decl. ¶¶ 4, 6, Exs. B & D.)  Plaintiff requested sanctions, which the Court denied, upon the representations made in opposition that Moore had been dealing with personal issues and illness.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 8.)  Subsequently, the parties engaged in some initial written discovery and attended an unsuccessful mediation.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 9.) 

 

Immediately following the mediation, Moore’s counsel withdrew as counsel, and Moore is currently representing herself. (Perkin Decl. ¶ 10.)  Thereafter, Moore did not respond to Plaintiff’s efforts to meet and confer regarding setting a date for Moore’s deposition.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 13.)  On August 17, 2023, Plaintiff moved to compel Moore’s deposition.  (Ibid.)

 

On September 13, the Court ordered Moore to appear for deposition within 20 days of notice of the Court’s order, and ordered Moore to pay $500 in monetary sanctions.  (September 13, 2023 Minute Order.)  Moore did not attend the hearing.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 15.)  The Clerk mailed notice of the Court’s order on September 18, 2023.  (See September 18, 2023 Certificate of Mailing.)  Plaintiff noticed Moore’s deposition for October 3, 2023.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 18, Ex. H.)  Moore did not object, and did not appear, thus violating the Court’s September 13 Order.  (Perkin Decl. ¶¶ 23, 24, Ex. K.) 

 

Written Discovery

 

Moore did not respond to Plaintiff’s meet and confer correspondence or attend the July 27, 2023 informal discovery conference regarding deficiencies in Moore’s discovery responses.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 11.)  On July 31, 2023, Plaintiff filed four motions to compel further written discovery responses, which Moore did not oppose.  (Perkin Decl. ¶¶ 12, 14.) 

 

On September 20, Moore did not appear at the hearing on Plaintiff’s discovery motions, and the Court adopted its tentative ruling, granting Plaintiff’s motions to compel further discovery responses in their entirety, ordering Moore to serve further verified and code compliant responses, without objections, to Special Interrogatories, Set 1; Form Interrogatories (General and Employment), Set 1; Requests for Production of Documents, Set 1; and Requests for Admission, Set 1, within 20 days of notice of the Court’s September 20, 2023 order, and to pay additional monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,040.00.  (September 20, 2023 Minute Order.)

 

Plaintiff served Moore with notice of the Court’s ruling on October 4, 2023, via overnight delivery and email.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 21 and Ex. J.)

 

As of the filing date of the motion, Moore has not complied with the Court’s September 13 or 20 orders.  (Perkin Decl. ¶ 28.)   

 

Plaintiff is prejudiced by Moore’s failure to engage in the discovery process, because Plaintiff is unable to discover any critical facts, documents, and percipient witnesses from Moore relating to Plaintiff’s claims, Moore’s defenses, and Moore’s cross-claims against Plaintiff. 

 

Moreover, Moore has not opposed the motion, and has thus waived the right to argue that a terminating sanction is unwarranted. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Having found that Moore has failed to comply with the Court’s September 13 and 20 orders, the Court grants Plaintiff’s unopposed motion for terminating sanctions.  As such, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivisions (d)(1) & (d)(3), the Court strikes Moore’s Answer to the Complaint filed on February 24, 2023 and dismisses Moore’s cross-complaint also filed on February 24, 2023. 

 

With the Court granting terminating sanctions against Moore, the Court finds the imposition of monetary sanctions to be moot. 

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service of such.
