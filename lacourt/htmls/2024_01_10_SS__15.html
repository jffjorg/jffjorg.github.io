DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV30677    Hearing Date: January 10, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION TO QUASH

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiffs Lucille Lazio and John Lazio filed this action alleging that Lucille Lazio developed malignant mesothelioma as a result of exposure from asbestos in products from Cover Girl, Revlon, and Estee Lauder.  (Opposition at p.1.)  On October 26, 2023, Defendant Cosmetic Specialties Inc. filed its motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction.  The court continued the hearing to allow Defendants to respond to evidence that Plaintiffs filed shortly before the hearing.  .

A defendant may move to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)  The court may dismiss without prejudice the complaint in whole, or as to that defendant, when dismissal is made pursuant to Section 418.10.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 581, subd. (h).)

"A court of this state may exercise jurisdiction on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States."  (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.)  "The Due Process Clause protects an individual's liberty interest in not being subject to the binding judgments of a forum with which he has established no meaningful 'contacts, ties, or relations."'  (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 471-472.)  A state court may not exercise personal jurisdiction over a party under circumstances that would offend "traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice."  (Asahi Metal Industry Co., Ltd., v. Superior Court of California, Solano County (1987) 480 U.S. 102, 113.)

When a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.  (Jayone Foods, Inc. v. Aekyung Industrial Co. Ltd. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 543, 553.)  Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, the defendant has the burden to demonstrate the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.  (Ibid.)  "The plaintiff must provide specific evidentiary facts, through affidavits and other authenticated documents, sufficient to allow the court to independently conclude whether jurisdiction is appropriate. [Citation.]  The plaintiff cannot rely on allegations in an unverified complaint or vague and conclusory assertions of ultimate facts.  [Citation.]"  (Strasner v. Touchstone Wireless Repair & Logistics, LP (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 215, 222.)

A defendant is subject to a state's general jurisdiction if its contacts "are so continuance and systematic as to render [it] essentially at home in the forum State."  (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 127.)  A nonresident defendant may be subject to the specific jurisdiction of the forum "if the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits [citation], and the 'controversy is related to or "arises out of' a defendant's contacts with the forum.' [Citations.]"  (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446.)  This test does not require a "causal relationship between the defendant's in-state activity and the litigation."  (Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial District Court (2021) 141 S.Ct. 1017, 1026.) The "arise out" of standard "asks about causation," but "relate to" does not.  (Ibid.)  "[W]hen a corporation has 'continuously and deliberately exploited [a State's] market, it must reasonably anticipate being haled into [that State's] court[s]' to defend actions 'based on' products causing injury there."  (Id. at p. 1027.)

Defendant provided evidence that it is incorporated and has its principle place of business in New Jersey.  (Katerndahl Decl., Exs. C, D; Grexa Decl., ¶ 5.)  Therefore, it is not a resident of California and not subject to its general jurisdiction. Plaintiffs do not argue otherwise.

Defendant argues that it has never engaged in business in California.  (Motion at pp. 9-10.)  

Plaintiffs contend Defendant facilitated supply of asbestos-containing talc in California, which was used to manufacture talcum powder and cosmetic products, including Cover Girl, Revlon, and Estee Lauder products.  (Opposition at p. 4.)  Plaintiffs cite evidence that Defendant distributed talc used to make cosmetics.  (Opposition at p. 2; Gamble Decl., Ex. B at pp. 36:25-37:1, 37:19-25.)  On December 1, 2023, Plaintiffs filed additional evidence that they contend shows Defendant distributed talc in California – deposition testimony about a distribution agreement for Defendant to distribute IMI Fabi’s talc in California and other states. 

Defendant contends the deposition testimony from another case is inadmissible in this case and only shows that IMI Fabi wanted to do business in California, not that it actually did business in California via Defendant’s activities in California.

The admissibility issues concerning the deposition testimony can be addressed by allowing jurisdictional discovery in this case on the issue whether Defendant distributed IMI Fabi’s talc for use in Cover Girl, Revlon, and Estee Lauder products in California during the period that Lucille Lazio was using Cover Girl, Revlon, and Estee Lauder products.

The motion is CONTINUED to April 10, 2024 at 9 a.m.  Plaintiffs may file a supplemental opposition and Defendant may file a supplemental reply pursuant to statutory deadlines.

            The moving party is to give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV11355    Hearing Date: January 10, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

On May 18, 2022, Plaintiff Virginia Adams filed this action alleging she developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure to asbestos-containing products.  Defendant Estee Lauder Companies Inc. filed a motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s request for punitive.

Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The court did not rely on the Alfieri Declaration to which Plaintiff objects.

When the motion targets a request for punitive damages, a higher standard of proof is at play.  “Although the clear and convincing evidentiary standard is a stringent one, ‘it does not impose on a plaintiff the obligation to “prove” a case for punitive damages at summary judgment [or summary adjudication.’  [Citations.]  Even so, ‘where the plaintiff’s ultimate burden of proof will be by clear and convincing evidence, the higher standard of proof must be taken into account in ruling on a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, since if a plaintiff is to prevail on a claim for punitive damages, it will be necessary that the evidence presented meet the higher evidentiary standard.’  [Citation.]”  (Butte Fire Cases (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1150, 1158-1159.)  “Summary judgment or summary adjudication ‘ “ ‘on the issue of punitive damages is proper’ only ‘when no reasonable jury could find the plaintiff’s evidence to be clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression.’ ” ’.  [Citation.]”  (Id. at p. 1159.)

For a corporate defendant, the oppression, fraud or malice “must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b).)  That requirement can be satisfied “ ‘if the evidence permits a clear and convincing inference that within the corporate hierarchy authorized persons acted despicably in “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” ’ [Citation.]”  (Morgan v. J-M Manufacturing Company, Inc. (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 1078, 1090.)  A plaintiff also “can satisfy the ‘managing agent’ requirement ‘through evidence showing the information in the possession of the corporation and the structure of management decision making that permits an inference that the information in fact moved upward to a point where corporate policy was formulated.’  [Citation.]”  (Id. at p. 1091.)

“ ‘[I]ntentionally marketing a defective product knowing that it might cause injury and death is ‘highly reprehensible.’  [Citation.]”  (Bankhead v. ArvinMeritor, Inc. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 68, 85.)  Punitive damages may be available when a defendant knows the dangers of asbestos, took action to protect its own employees from the hazard, knew that its products were likely to pose a danger to users, and did not warn them.  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.Ap.4th 1270, 1300.)  Such evidence “was sufficient to show malice, that is, despicable conduct coupled with conscious disregard for the safety of others.”  (Id. at pp. 1300-1301.)  On the other hand, a defendant’s knowledge of trace amounts of asbestos in talc does not necessarily mean that the defendant knew that asbestos in talc “would cause a high probability of injury.” (McNeal v. Whittaker, Clark & Daniels, Inc. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 853, 873.)

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s discovery responses are factually deficient, citing Plaintiff’s responses to a special interrogatory asking for all facts supporting punitive damages.  (Motion at p. 5; Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) 10.)  Plaintiff stated she used Defendant’s various cosmetic and talcum powder products, such as White Linen dusting powder, from the early 1960s to the 1980s.  (Defendant’s Index, Ex. E at pp. 2, 8.)  Defendant was as a member of the Cometic, Toiletry, and Fragrance Association (“CTFA”) in 1971 and had the ability to test its products, including with the J4-1 method and electron microscopy, but instead relied on suppliers’ and distributors’ XRD testing to determine the presence of asbestos in talc.  Plaintiff contends that testing had known deficiencies.  (Id. at pp. 2-5.)  Plaintiff names Whittaker, Clark, & Daniels Inc. as one of Defendant’s talc suppliers and cites a 1976 memorandum to the FDA alluding to concerns about asbestos in talc.  (Id. at pp. 4-5.)  Plaintiff states Defendant knew about the dangers of asbestos, referring to a report from the CTFA to its active members in 1971.  (Id. at p. 5.)  

Plaintiff’s interrogatory response did not provide evidence that Defendant knew the talc it used contained asbestos that, when incorporated into Defendant’s products, would cause a high probability of injury to consumers, and that Defendant made a corporate decision to ignore  that information and not warn customers.  (McNeal, supra, 80 Cal.App.5th at p. 873.)  Therefore, Defendant shifted the burden.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant knew about the hazards of asbestos and the potential for asbestos contamination of talc by 1971 but allowed its suppliers to perform inadequate talc testing and did not warn consumers about asbestos.  (Opposition at p. 2.)  Plaintiff cites to testimony of Defendant’s corporate representative confirming Defendant’s CTFA membership in 1971, its attendance and reception of minutes and documentation from CTFA meetings and Defendant’s receipt of information on talc and asbestos from CTFA.  (Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. B at pp. 62-24.)  Defendant’s membership included various senior personnel – i.e., president, vice president, and director of research and development.  (Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. C at p. 19.)  

Plaintiff contends Defendant “knew that asbestos is known to cause mesothelioma based on a 1971 CFTA report on asbestos and talc.”  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7.)  For that assertion, Plaintiff cites a notice to CTFA members referring to “possible asbestos content of talcum powders” and attaching a report about an August 3, 1971 symposium about asbestos and talc at the FDA.  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. E at p. 1.)  The report does not state that asbestos in talc can cause mesothelioma or was a hazard to users of talcum powder and cosmetics.  This is the same symposium which, according to the McNeal court, did not conclude “that there was no safe level of exposure to asbestos.”  (McNeal, supra, 80 Cal.App.5th at p. 874.) 

Plaintiff also cites an article about detecting asbestos in talc.  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. F.)  Plaintiff does not show that Defendant received this article.  Also, the article does not conclude that asbestos in talc in cosmetics and talcum powder can be harmful to consumers.

Plaintiff also cites a 2020 document about a 2018 working group on asbestos in consumer products.  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. G.)  A 2020 document could not have given Defendant notice about the dangers of asbestos in talc used in cosmetics during the period of Plaintiff’s use of Defendant’s products from the early 1960s through 1986.  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact Nos. 1, 2.)

Plaintiff cites an April 10, 1972 letter attaching an August 25, 1971 report of talc concluding there was “[n]o detectable amounts of any of the asbestiform minerals.”  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. H at p. 2.)  This document does not show Defendant knew about the dangers of asbestos in talc used in Defendant’s products.

Finally, in support of the contention that Defendant “knew that asbestos is known to cause mesothelioma based on a 1971 CFTA report on asbestos and talc” (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7), Plaintiff cited page 129:19-24 of the deposition of a witness who worked at Defendant.  (Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 7; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. B.)  The testimony merely states that the witness was shown a 1972 talc analysis for asbestos.  (Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. B at p. 129:19-24.)

None of this cited evidence shows that during the period of Plaintiff’s exposure from the 1960s through 1986, Defendant knew that asbestos in talc used in Defendant’s products was dangerous to consumers.

Plaintiffs also cite CTFA’s 1973 report to the FDA stating that Italian talc grade 1615 tested as false negatives and recommending transmission electronic microscopy and electric diffraction as the “best, most reliable method” of detecting asbestos.  (Opposition at p. 3; Plaintiff’s Disputed Fact No. 13; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. J at pp. 3, 6.)  Plaintiff argues that despite this knowledge, Defendants did not urge its vendors to test with transmission electron microscopy.  (Opposition at p. 3; Plaintiff’s Index, Ex. B at pp. 138-139.)  Again, this evidence does not show that Defendant was aware of the hazards presented by its products.

Plaintiff did not present evidence that Defendant knew asbestos in talc posed a probable danger to consumers of cosmetic products made with that talc.  Plaintiff does not mention McNeal or its holding that knowledge that asbestos is dangerous does not allow the inference that therefore asbestos in talc in cosmetics is dangerous to consumers.  (McNeal, supra, 80 Cal.App.5th at pp. 873-874.)  In sum, Plaintiff did not show a disputed issue about whether Defendant knew its talc products were dangerous and yet continued to market them to consumers without warnings.

The motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED on the claim for punitive damages.

The moving party is to give notice.
