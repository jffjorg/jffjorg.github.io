Notice:                         OK

 

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Antonio Calderon, Ana Canchola, and Frank G. Martinez filed this action against Defendant, SCE for damages arising out of an electrical explosion and fire in an underground high-voltage vault. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to RPDs

a.     History of Discovery Dispute

Plaintiffs allege the explosion at issue in this lawsuit was caused by deliberate and willful improper construction and maintenance of the subject circuit that exploded.  In response to FROGs, SCE indicated that Hampton Tedder Electric opted not to replace the deadbreak elbows when given the opportunity to do so.  When asked what documents support this contention, it responded, “Purchase Order No. 4501045265, Master Services Agreement, and associated documents.” 

 

Upon receipt of the foregoing, Plaintiffs propounded SROGs, including SROGs 103 and 104, which are at issue on this motion.  SROG 103 asks:

If YOU contend that Purchase Order No. 451045265 allowed Hampton Tedder Electric to opt ‘not to replace the deadbreak elbows when given the opportunity,’ please IDENTIFY every clause of the Purchase Order supporting YOUR contention. 

 

SROG 104 asks:

If YOU contend that the Master Services Agreement allowed Hampton Tedder Electric to opt ‘not to replace the deadbreak elbows when given the opportunity,” please IDENTIFY every clause of the Master Services Agreement supporting YOUR contention.

 

SCE’s responses to each SROG were substantially the same, replacing only “Purchase Order No. 451045265” with “Master Services Agreement” between 103 and 104.  SCE responded: 

Objection. Calls for a legal determination. Without waiving said objection, and subject thereto, responding party responds as follows: Purchase Order No. 451045265 is one document of a legal universe of documents; rules; regulations; industry practices; contracts and terms and conditions thereto; contractor orientations; manuals and handbooks bound to contractor's performance of the work via contractor orientations; Red Book professional standards for qualified electrical workers; OSHA rules and regulations; CPUC rules, regulations, and orders; safety manuals of SCE and Hampton Tedder (which adheres to Fed Osha, Cal Osha, Red Book and any and all SCE specific requirements identified in ISN and has adopted SCE’s APM Manual); and the work actually performed pursuant to the purchase order, all of which taken together clearly demonstrates that while performing its work in the subject vault, SCE's contractor, Hampton Tedder, and its employees, had the opportunity to evaluate all electrical components in the subject vault for repair, maintenance, upgrade, and safety and to perform associated work as part of this project –it regularly did so, using "B" materials and billed Edison accordingly, including on this job relative to deadbreak elbows on one circuit but not other deadbreak elbows or other components until the Hampton Tedder crew who finished the job did so later on the day of plaintiffs' accident.

 

The parties met and conferred, but were unable to resolve issues.  This motion follows. 

 

b.     Analysis re: Sufficiency of Responses

CCP §2030.220(a) requires answers to interrogatories to be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.  SCE, in opposition to the motion, argues its responses meet this standard because, “There is simply not a specific clause in either the Purchase Order…or the Master Services Agreement between SCE and Hampton that speaks to the specific act of ‘replacing the deadbreak elbow.’”  It contends these documents are broad, but, when read together, they are relevant because they help to define the scope of the duties Hampton had on the subject property, especially when read in conjunction with numerous other documents. 

 

Plaintiffs, in reply, correctly note that Defendant should have simply indicated, in its sworn discovery responses, that there is no specific clause in either document that supports their assertions.  The motion to compel a further response is granted.  The responses are not as complete and straightforward as the Code requires. 

 

c.     Sanctions

Plaintiffs seek sanctions in connection with the motion.  Plaintiffs argue the responses were not made in good faith, and argue SCE’s conduct in connection with other discovery matters in this case shows an ongoing pattern of obstruction.  Defendant, in opposition to the request, argues its responses were made in good faith, and also argues Plaintiffs’ attempt to meet and confer prior to filing the motion was insufficient. 

 

The threshold issue is whether the meet and confer attempts were sufficient, as the Court will not impose sanctions absent such efforts. 

 

On 8/07/23, Plaintiffs’ attorney, Joseph Brent, emailed Defense Counsel, Amelia S. McDonald, with detailed meet and confer arguments re: SROGs 103 and 104.  The letter concluded, “We are therefore asking you to provide verified, code complaint responses by the close of business on August 11.”  Brent declares SCE did not respond to the letter.  Plaintiffs filed the motion on 8/14/23. 

 

In opposition to the motion, Defense Counsel, McDonald, declares she and Plaintiffs’ co-counsel, Lynn P. Whitlock, had been meeting and conferring during August, and on 8/04/23, McDonald received detailed correspondence from Whitlock concerning all discovery issues, and SROGs 103 and 104 were not mentioned.  She declares she and Whitlock were meeting and conferring and CCing Brent on all communication during the subject period.  She declares she was out of the office “during the time when Mr. Brent filed the motion,” as she was visiting colleges with her daughter. 

 

Notably absent from the opposition is any copy of the meet and confer correspondence with Whitlock for the Court to review.  Additionally, in reply, Brent provides a copy of a 9/15/23 letter from Whitlock to McDonald wherein Whitlock indicates she is confirming that McDonald represented she would be providing further responses to SROGs 103 and 104.  This correspondence happened after the motion was filed, but two months before SCE filed opposition to the motion. 

 

While additional attempts to meet and confer are always possible, the Court finds the attempts were sufficient. 

 

The second issue is whether SCE made its responses in good faith and with substantial justification, such that sanctions should not be imposed.  In light of the correspondence showing SCE agreed to supplement its responses but failed to do so, the Court cannot make this conclusion.  The Court finds sanctions are appropriate.  The motion was made necessary by SCE’s refusal to supplement its responses, despite its agreement to do so.  Notably, Plaintiffs seek sanctions in the very low amount of $627.66, which represents one hour of their attorney’s time plus associated costs.  The request is granted in full.

Dept. S27

8:30 a.m.

November 30, 2023

 

Moving Party:               Defendant, Reza Askari, M.D.

Opposing Party:           Plaintiff, Chelsey Binz 

Notice:                         OK

 

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Chelsey Binz filed this action against Defendants, LBMMC, et al. for damages arising out of complications that arose during her labor and delivery.  The complications ultimately caused damage to her child, Indiana Rayne Van Den Oever.   

 

Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for medical negligence, corporate hospital liability, concealment, and NIED.  The first and fourth causes of action are pled against Defendant, Dr. Askari.

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

3.     Standard on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)

Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

  1. Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff filed objections with her opposition papers.  Her objections are overruled.  Defendant filed objections with his reply papers.  The objections are overruled.

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

Defendant moves for judgment on the complaint, contending (a) he is immune from liability pursuant to the Good Samaritan law, and (b) Plaintiff cannot establish the elements of her claims for negligence and/or NIED against him. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  She argues (a) Dr. Askari is not a Good Samaritan under governing law, (b) expert evidence raises a triable issue of material fact concerning breach of the standard of care and damages, and (c) her claim for NIED should survive summary judgment. 

 

Defendant, in reply, contends Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of material fact concerning the applicability of the Good Samaritan law and/or standard of care/causation. 

 

  1. Good Samaritan Immunity

The Court heard Drs. Okuyemi and Milone’s summary judgment motion on 9/12/23.  The Court held that they were entitled to judgment as a matter of law because they acted merely as Good Samaritans in assisting with the emergency delivery of Plaintiff’s child.  The Court found they were not Plaintiff’s regular physician, but instead were called to the scene due to the emergency that was developing in connection with the birth.  On 11/28/23, the Court heard and denied Plaintiff’s motion for a new trial on the issue, again finding the defense applied and provided full immunity.

 

Similarly, Dr. Askari provides evidence that he was not Plaintiff’s treating physician, and was called to aid in treating Plaintiff because an emergency was developing.  Specifically, facts 1-42 in the separate statement detail the events that unfolded and that required Dr. Askari’s assistance, and fact 43 establishes that Dr. Askari had no prior relationship with Plaintiff.  Plaintiff, in opposition to fact 43, provides evidence that Dr. Askari was a hospitalist/laborist on duty, and it was part of his job description to respond to emergency situations in the event the regular physician was not available.  She contends this is sufficient to defeat application of the Good Samaritan rule. 

 

The Court has reviewed Burciaga v. St. John’s Hospital (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 710, 716.  Both parties argue Burciaga supports their position.  Notably, this was the primary case upon which Defendants relied in their original motion.  The Court has reviewed Burciaga a second time in drafting this ruling.  The Burciaga Court held, as is relevant to this motion, “The heart of the application of the Good Samaritan statutes is the inquiry whether a duty of professional care pre-existed the emergency. (Comment, Good Samaritans and Hospital Emergencies (1981) 54 So.Cal.L.Rev. 417, 418.) Gibson's declaration provides a reasonable inference that he did not have an existing duty to treat William and that he acted as a volunteer. William was not Gibson's patient and Taft did not customarily refer patients to him. Gibson was present in the hospital only because he was treating his own hospitalized patients. He cancelled his office appointments for the day in order to treat William. That a medical emergency fortuitously occurred while he was in the hospital creates no duty to plaintiff. There was no evidence that Gibson was employed by the hospital, and it is not a reasonable inference that because he was an active staff member, the hospital had designated him to treat newborns in the event of an emergency.”

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s evidence is sufficient to defeat application of the Good Samaritan defense.  Whereas in Burciaga the defendant doctor was only in the hospital because he was treating his own patients, which he canceled in order to attend to the unfolding emergency, in the instant case Dr. Askari was assigned to treat patients in emergency situations such as the one Plaintiff experienced.  The Burciaga Court noted the lack of evidence that the hospital had designated the defendant doctor to handle the types of emergencies that arose on the date in question; in this case, however, Plaintiff provides evidence that this was exactly Dr. Askari’s assigned job.  The motion for summary judgment on the ground that the Good Samaritan rule provides a complete defense to Plaintiff’s claims is therefore denied.

 

  1. Breach of Standard of Care and Causation

Defendant provides evidence, by way of the Declaration of Stephen V. DiMarzo, M.D., that he complied with the standard of care and that nothing he did caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s damages.  This is sufficient to meet the moving burden on summary judgment, and the burden shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact. 

 

Plaintiff does so by way of the Declaration of Howard C. Mandel, M.D.  Defendant, in reply, argues Dr. Mandel’s opinion is not well-reasoned and should be disregarded.  The Court cannot, at the summary judgment stage, weigh the evidence or determine the strength of Dr. Mandel’s opinions.  The Court has reviewed the declaration, and finds it is sufficient, at the summary judgment stage, to raise triable issues of material fact.

 

  1. Conclusion

Plaintiff raised triable issues of material fact concerning application of the Good Samaritan defense and also concerning breach of the standard of care and causation.  The motion for summary judgment is therefore denied.

The assault, battery, IIED, negligence, negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, and negligence – vicarious liability causes of action are all pled against Demurring Defendant, Far West. 

2.     Demurrer

3.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the attorneys met and conferred over the phone but were unable to come to agreement concerning the issues presented on demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is therefore satisfied. 

 

c.     Initial Note re: Opposition

Any opposition to the demurrer was due on or before 11/15/23 in light of the Thanksgiving holiday.  CCP §1005(b).  The Court has not received timely opposition to the demurrer, and will not consider late-filed opposition.  In the future, if Plaintiff does not intend to oppose a matter, the Court asks Plaintiff to meet and confer in good faith to resolve all issues without the need for a hearing.

 

d.     Analysis

Defendant establishes that respondent superior liability for a tort committed by an employee does not attach unless the tort was in some way connected to the employee’s duties on the job.  See Carr v. Wm. Co. Crowell Co. (1946) 28 Cal.2d 652, 654.  Notably, while not cited in the moving papers, the modern test is whether the act was either required by the employee or incidental to the employee’s duties, OR whether the employee’s misconduct was reasonably foreseeable by the employer, even if not required or incidental.  Crouch v. Trinity Christian Ctr. of Santa Ana, Inc. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 995, 1015. 

 

In this case, Plaintiff alleges, at ¶7 of her complaint, that Wendy Doe came out of Far West with another employee[1], Wendy Doe screamed “that’s my man” in reference to the person with whom Plaintiff had the collision, and then both women began repeatedly punching and kicking Plaintiff, pulling her hair, spilling sodas on her, and stealing $400 from her. 

 

This appears to constitute an admission that the acts that form the basis of the lawsuit were not required by or incidental to employment duties, and were not reasonably foreseeable to the employer.  Because each and every cause of action against Far West is based on Wendy Doe and the other employee’s conduct, the demurrer is sustained in its entirety. 

 

e.     Leave to Amend

It is not up to the Court to figure out how the complaint can be amended to state a cause of action.  Rather, the burden is on the plaintiff to show in what manner he or she can amend the complaint, and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.  Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349; Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.

A plaintiff may not amend as a matter of right, and in order to be in a position to complain upon appeal of an abuse of discretion in sustaining a demurrer without leave to amend, he must ask permission to amend, unless it is made to appear that he had no opportunity to ask such permission.  Williams v. Hinckley (1930) 109 Cal.App. 574, 576.

 

In light of the lack of opposition, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.
