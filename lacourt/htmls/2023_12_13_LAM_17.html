DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV02085    Hearing Date: December 13, 2023    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

SEBASTIAN DONOVAN, et al

 

 

         vs.

 

QUANTGENE, INC., et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV02085

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  December 13, 2023

QUANTGENE, INC., et al.

 

            vs.

 

SEBASTIAN DONOVAN, et al.

 

 


Plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses are GRANTED. Defendants are each sanctioned, jointly and severally with counsel, $1,050.00 per motion.

 

On 1/31/2023, Plaintiffs Sebastian Donovan and Arconex (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against Quantgene, Inc. and Johannes Bhakdi (collectively, Defendants), alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) unjust enrichment; (4) intentional misrepresentation; (5) negligent misrepresentation; and (6) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.

 

            On 3/3/2022, Cross-Complainants Quantgene, Inc. and Johannes Bhakdi filed a cross-complaint (XC) against Cross-Defendants Sebastian Donovan and Arconex, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) false promise; (4) intentional misrepresentation; (5) concealment; (6) negligent misrepresentation; (7) conversion; (8) unjust enrichment; (9) intentional interference with contractual relations; and (10) unfair business practices.

 

            Now, Plaintiffs move to compel Mr. Bhakdi and Quantgene (collectively, Defendants) to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2.2, 2.5, 15.1, and 50.1, and Nos. 9.2, 12.1, 15.1, 17.1, and 50.1.

 

            Plaintiffs also move to compel Defendants to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 3, 7, and 10-13, and Nos. 9-12.

 

            For ease, the Court has consolidated its analysis into a single ruling.

 

            The motions are unopposed.

 

Discussion  

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2.2, 2.5, 15.1, and 50.1, and Nos. 9.2, 12.1, 15.1, 17.1, and 50.1, and Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 3, 7, and 10-13, and Nos. 9-12 are evasive, incomplete, and contain objections that are without merit or too general. (CCP § 2030.300(a)(1),(3).)

 

            Defendants did not oppose these motions and thus are considered to have conceded to the merits of the motions.

 

            Moreover, given their lack of opposition, they have not offered any substantial justification for their failure to adequately respond to discovery.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses are granted. Defendants are each sanctioned, jointly and severally with counsel, $1,050.00 per motion. ($350/hr x 3 hr.)

 

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  December    , 2023

                                                                                                                                                          

   Hon. Jon R. Takasugi

Case Number: 23STCV08440    Hearing Date: December 13, 2023    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JOSE GUADALUPE SOTO-

RODRIGUEZ, et al.

 

 

         vs.

 

AKG TRADING (USA) INC., et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV08440 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  December 13, 2023

           

            Defendant Francini’s demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to the generalized product identification language allegations, but DENIED as to the prayer for punitive damages.

 

            Defendant Caesarstone’s demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

            Defendant Parsoda’s demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to the generalized product identification language allegations, and MOOT as to the prayer for punitive damages.

 

Defendant ASG’s demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to the generalized product identification language allegations, and MOOT as to the prayer for punitive damages.

 

On 4/17/2023, Plaintiffs Jose Guadalupe-Soto Rodriguez and Sara Sanchez (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against 39 Defendants, alleging: (1) negligence; (2) strict liability—warning defect; (3) strict liability—design defect; (4) fraudulent concealment; (5) breach of implied warranties; and (6) loss of consortium.

 

            Now, Defendants Parsoda USA, Inc., Caesarstone USA, Architectural Surfaces Group, LLC, and Francini, Inc. (collectively, Defendants) all separately demur to the fourth cause of action for fraudulent concealment. For ease, the Court has consolidated its analysis into a single ruling.

 

Factual Background

 

            This is a toxic tort case arising from Plaintiff Jose Guadalupe Soto-Rodriguez’ (Plaintiff) claim that he developed silicosis and consequential injuries through his employment working with stone products manufactured, distributed, and/or supplied by Defendants.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action for fraudulent concealment is vague, ambiguous, and uncertain.

 

            After review, the Court disagrees and agrees in part.   

 

Francini

 

            As for Francini, Plaintiffs allege that Francini knew that its product was inherently dangerous due to its high crystalline silica content, and did not provide warnings and instructions that are adequate to prevent silicosis.

 

            In its motion, Francini argues that these allegations are insufficiently specific because Plaintiffs do not alleged facts which could show that Francini willfully concealed this information with an intent to defraud, or that could show that Francini knowingly concealed this information. However, facts which could show or establish Francini’s knowledge or intent are contemplated by the discovery process, not the pleading stage. Indeed, without discovery, it would be nearly impossible for any Plaintiff to allege facts which could show what a defendant knew or intended. Rather, to satisfy the specificity requirement, Plaintiffs must allege facts which set forth “the what, how, when, where, to whom, and by what means” of the underlying claim. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Here, Plaintiffs allege that Francini knew its product was inherently dangerous due to its high crystalline silica content, and that it did not provide warnings and instructions that are adequate to prevent silicosis. Moreover, Plaintiffs include allegations that analyze the specific language in the Safety Data Sheet provided by Francini and identify the ways in which the warnings were inadequate and were intended to conceal the true risks. As such, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts which could show what was allegedly concealed, and how, when, where, to whom, and by what means Francini allegedly concealed information.

 

            These allegations are sufficient at the pleading stage to maintain a fraudulent concealment claim against Francini.

 

Parsoda and ASG

 

            The Court finds the allegations against Parsoda and Architectural Surfaces Group (ASG) to be inadequate to state a claim for fraudulent concealment. Unlike with Francini, Plaintiffs’ Complaint does not contain a section which sets forth allegations specific to Parsoda or ASG. Rather, the claims against ASG and Parsoda are based on generalized allegations that all defendants failed "to fully disclose the toxic and fibrogenic properties of their products," (Complaint, ¶ 418) and that all defendants "were aware that commonly used and recommended protective measures (e.g., use of wet processing methods and air purifying respirators) were inadequate to prevent fabricators and installers from getting silicosis," (Complaint, ¶ 498)

 

            Allegations referring to all 39 Defendants are insufficiently specific. Just as Plaintiffs alleged specific facts against Francini, Plaintiffs must advance specific allegations as to what Parsoda and ASG are alleged to have concealed, how they allegedly concealed it, when they allegedly concealed it, why they had a duty to disclose, and by what means they allege Defendants concealed this information from them. 

 

Caesarstone

 

            Finally, as for Caesarstone, the Court finds the allegations to be sufficient at the pleading stage.

 

Under Bockrath, “[a] plaintiff must allege facts albeit as succinctly as possible, explaining how the conduct caused or contributed to the injury.” (Id. at p. 78.) Specifically, a plaintiff must plead the following in his or her toxic tort complaint: 1) That he or she was exposed to each of the toxic materials claimed to have caused a specific illness; 2) Each product that allegedly caused the injury; 3) That as a result of the exposure, the toxins entered his body; 4) That he suffers from a specific illness, and that each toxin that entered his body was a substantial factor in bringing about, prolonging, or aggravating that illness; and 5) That each toxin he absorbed was manufactured or supplied by a named defendant. (Id. at p. 80.)

 

As such, a reference to undetermined products is improper: “[t]he law cannot tolerate lawsuits by prospecting plaintiffs who sue multiple defendants on speculation that their products may have caused harm over time through exposure to toxins in them, and who thereafter try to learn through discovery whether their speculation was well-founded.” (Id. at p. 81.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint devotes an entire section to Caesarstone products. These allegations include that:

 

Caesarstone first began producing and exporting its artificial stone product in 1987, the officers, directors, and managing agents of the company knew that Caesarstone was an extremely toxic and dangerous product because it contained extremely high concentrations of crystalline silica and the product had to be fabricated and installed by workmen, which involved cutting, grinding, drilling, edging, and polishing the product with electric-powered saws and tools that generates huge amounts of respirable crystalline silica dust.

 

At the time Caesarstone first began producing its artificial stone product in 1987, the company’s officers and directors were aware that the product presented extraordinary risks of silicosis because, unlike marble (which contained about 5% crystalline silica) and granite (which contained about 35% crystalline silica), Caesarstone contained as much as 95% crystalline silica.

 

            (Complaint ¶ 119-120.)

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff has alleged that he was exposed to the toxic crystalline silica in Caesarstone products, and that this exposure caused his silicosis. Plaintiffs alleges that while Caesarstone markets different products, “all Caesarstone was essentially the same artificial stone product made of the same essential ingredients (crystalline silica, polymeric resin, pigments and additives), and all of these “collections” had the same essential toxic properties and hazards.”  (Complaint ¶ 122.)

 

            Taken together, Plaintiff has alleged facts to satisfy the Brockrath factors as to each Caesarstone product.

 

            Plaintiff has also alleged sufficient facts to state a claim for fraud. Plaintiff’s Complaint has an entire section dedicated to setting forth facts which could show Caesarstone’s knowledge of the silicosis epidemic. (See Complaint ¶ ¶ 152-173.) This includes the 2016 Research report analyzing silicosis from Caesarstone, and Caesarstone’s January 2020 Safety Data Sheet. Plaintiff has alleged facts which could show what was allegedly concealed, and how, when, where, to whom, and by what means Caesarstone allegedly concealed information.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.

 

Motions to Strike

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to support a prayer for punitive damages. Defendant Francini also argues that the generalized product identification language “[a]nd other stone products to be identified through the course of discovery” violates the toxic tort pleading requirements and should be stricken.

 

            As for the punitive damages prayer, the Court overruled Francini’s and Caesarstone’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action. As such, Plaintiffs have necessarily alleged sufficient facts to show Francini and Caesarstone engaged in fraudulent conduct.

 

However, the Court sustained ASG’s and Parsoda’s demurrers, with leave to amend. Accordingly, their motions to strike are moot.

 

This leaves only the contention that the generalized product identification language should be struck (Caesarstone did not raise any argument concerning generalized product identification language).

 

As for the generalized product identification language, the Court agrees the “[a]nd other stone products to be identified through the course of discovery” language must be stricken.

 

Under Brockrath, “[a] plaintiff must allege facts albeit as succinctly as possible, explaining how the conduct caused or contributed to the injury.” (Id. at p. 78.) Specifically, a plaintiff must plead the following in his or her toxic tort complaint: 1) That he or she was exposed to each of the toxic materials claimed to have caused a specific illness; 2) Each product that allegedly caused the injury; 3) That as a result of the exposure, the toxins entered his body; 4) That he suffers from a specific illness, and that each toxin that entered his body was a substantial factor in bringing about, prolonging, or aggravating that illness; and 5) That each toxin he absorbed was manufactured or supplied by a named defendant. (Id. at p. 80.)

 

As such, a reference to undetermined products is improper: “[t]he law cannot tolerate lawsuits by prospecting plaintiffs who sue multiple defendants on speculation that their products may have caused harm over time through exposure to toxins in them, and who thereafter try to learn through discovery whether their speculation was well-founded.” (Id. at p. 81.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motions to strike are moot in part, denied in part, granted in part. Defendants ASG’s and Parsoda’s motions are moot as to punitive damages. Defendant Francini’s and Caesarstone’s motions are denied as to punitive damages. Defendants ASG’s, Parsoda’s, and Francini’s motions are granted as to the generalized product identification language.

 

It is so ordered.
