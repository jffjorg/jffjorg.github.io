DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV38741    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: T

Tentative Ruling

Judge Ian C. Fusselman

Department T

Hearing Date:                         October 24, 2024

Case Name:                            Juan Andres Espinosa Torres v. Edmond Trapp, et al.

Case No.:                                20STCV38741

Motion:                                  Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs

Moving Party:                         Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority and Edmond Trapp

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Juan Andres Espinosa Torres

Tentative Ruling:                    Grant in part

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 8, 2020, Plaintiff Juan Andres Espinosa Torres (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Edmond Trapp (“Trapp”), Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“LACMTA”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and DOES 1 to 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) motor vehicle; and (2) general negligence. Plaintiff alleges that he was injured on February 14, 2020, while on Figueroa Street due to the actions of Defendant Trapp, whom Plaintiff alleges is employed by Defendant LACMTA.  

            On November 13, 2020, Defendant LACMTA filed an answer to the complaint.

            On May 14, 2021, Defendant Trapp filed an answer to the complaint.  

            On July 15, 2024, jury trial in this action commenced in Department T of the Alhambra Courthouse before the Honorable Ian C. Fusselman. (07/15/24 Minute Order.)

            On July 23, 2024, outside of the presence of the jury, Plaintiff’s counsel informed the Court that Plaintiff would waive special damages and proceed only as to general damages, which obviated “the need for some witnesses to testify.” (07/23/24 Minute Order.)

            On July 24, 2024, the jury returned a special verdict finding that Defendant Trapp was negligent, and that the negligence of Defendant Trapp was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm. (07/24/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The jury found that Plaintiff’s past economic loss was $375,000.00 and Plaintiff’s future economic loss was $375,000.00, for a total of $750,000.00. (07/24/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) Thus, the Court’s minute order indicated that judgment was to be entered in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendants Trapp and LACMTA in the total of $750,000.00. (07/24/24 Minute Order at p. 3.)

            On August 21, 2024, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendants for $750,000.00. (08/21/24 Judgment at p. 4:3-7.) Plaintiff was deemed the prevailing party. (08/21/24 Judgment at p. 4:3-4.) The judgment also provides that Plaintiff is entitled to his recoverable costs pursuant to the filing of a memorandum of costs. (08/21/24 Judgment at p. 4:8-9.)

            On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Memorandum of Costs (Summary) and Memorandum of Costs (Worksheet), in which Plaintiff seeks total costs of $260,257.27. Plaintiff seeks the following costs: (1) filing and motion fees totaling $2,269.07 (Item 1); (2) jury fees of $793.90 (Item 2); (3) deposition costs of $15,408.44 (Item 4); (4) service of process costs totaling $3,103.58 (Item 5); (5) witness fees of $53,000.00 (Item 8); (6) costs for court-ordered transcripts in the amount of $3,218.35 (Item 9); and (7) “other” costs in the sum of $181,963.93 (Item 16).

            On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Order Entering Judgment Which Includes Prejudgment Interest Pursuant to Plaintiff’s CCP § 998 Offer to Compromise and Civil Code § 3291 Which was Rejected by Defendant.

            On September 16, 2024, Defendants filed a Motion for New Trial.  

            On September 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice taking the motion for prejudgment interest off calendar.

            On September 24, 2024, Defendants filed and served the instant Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs. Defendants seek to tax and/or strike $207,331.95 from Plaintiff’s claimed costs as follows: $133.64 from Item 1(c) for filing and motion fees; $4,248.57 from Item 4(b) for deposition costs; $593.84 from Items 5(a) through 5(c) for service of process; $14,570.00 for the work of Plaintiff’s billing expert, Jan Roughan, and striking $3,000.00 each for the work done by Dr. Kashani and Dr. Samimi from Item 8(b) for expert witness fees; $3,218.35 from Item 9 for court-ordered transcripts; $177,937.02 from Item 16(39) for prejudgment interest; $496.69 from Item 16 for travel and parking expenses; $8.63 from Item 16 for photocopying expenses; and, $125.00 from Item 16 for investigation expenses. (See Not. of Mot. at p. 2:4-4:7.)

            On October 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion, to which Defendants replied on October 17, 2024.

            On October 18, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court denied Defendants’ motion for a new trial. (10/18/24 Minute Order.)  

            In his opposition brief, Plaintiff indicates that he is no longer seeking prejudgment interest in the amount of $177,837.02 but is still seeking “to recoup all other expenses as set forth in his Memorandum of Costs.” (Opp’n at p. 11:12-14.) The Court therefore GRANTS Defendants’ request to strike prejudgment interest from Item 16(39) in the amount of $177,937.02 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

            Defendants contend that Plaintiff cannot recover certain categories of costs that are not included under CCP § 1033.5(a) and are specifically precluded under CCP § 1033.5(b).

 

 

OPPOSITION

            Plaintiff argues that he is entitled to his costs as the prevailing party. Plaintiff further argues that the items on his memorandum of costs are proper, reasonable, and were necessarily incurred.    

REPLY

            On reply, Defendants assert that they have shown that the challenged costs were not reasonable and necessary.   

ANALYSIS

      I.          Legal Standard/Applicable Law

            “Except as otherwise provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) “The right to recover . . . costs is determined entirely by statute.” (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 71.) “In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court’s first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face.” (Ibid.) “If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show the costs to be unnecessary or unreasonable.” (Ibid.) “Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary.”  (Ibid.) “Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.) California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 1033.5(c)(4) gives a court discretion to allow or deny a claimed cost where it is not explicitly allowed or prohibited by Section 1033.5.

            “[T]he items on a verified cost bill are prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred.” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum.” (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1267.) “Only if the costs have been put at issue must supporting documentation be submitted.” (Ibid.) “If the items appearing on a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.) “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ibid.) “[T]he mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a proper objection to an item, the necessity of which appears doubtful, or which does not appear to be proper on its face.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131, internal quotations omitted.)

    II.          Declaration of Defendants’ Counsel

In support of the motion, Defendants’ counsel, Allen L. Thomas (“Thomas”), provides a

declaration. Mr. Thomas relevantly states the following: Plaintiff served his CCP § 998 offers to settle this case on Defendants on April 8, 2022. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. A.) Each offer was in the amount of $250,000.00 for a total of $500,000.00. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. A.) Defendants designated retained experts were Edward P. Milich, P.E.; Kevin M. Ehrhart, M.D.; and Vicki J. Schweitzer, R.N., BSN. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. 2.) Jan Roughan of Roughan & Associates was listed as Plaintiff’s billing expert. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 5; Exs. 3, 4.)

            At trial, Plaintiff introduced evidence of his medical bills during his case in chief and Plaintiff’s designated billing expert, Jan Roughan, did not testify at trial. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s cost bill claims $14,570.00 for Ms. Roughan’s preparation and testimony at trial. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 7.) When Plaintiff rested his case in chief, he withdrew his claim for economic damages including his medical bills. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 7.) Mr. Thomas was then unable to introduce his clients’ experts to challenge the reasonableness and necessity of Plaintiff’s claimed medical bills during Defendants’ case in chief. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 7.) The jury was instructed to not consider Plaintiff’s medical bills during their deliberations and in their verdict. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 7.)

            Mr. Thomas states that, on July 16, 2024, Houman M. Kashani, M.D., testified remotely at the trial for less than one and one half hours. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 10; Ex. 5.) Dr. Kashani’s fee schedule indicates that his charge is $7,500.00 per half day for trial testimony. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 10; Ex. 5.) Mr. Thomas states that, on July 17, 2024, Babak Samimi, M.D., testified remotely at the trial for barely over two hours at most. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 11; Ex. 6.) Dr. Samimi’s fee schedule indicates that his charge is $7,500.00 per half day for trial testimony. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 10; Ex. 6.) A reasonable fee for both Dr. Samimi and Dr. Kashani testifying would be $4,500.00. (Thomas Decl., ¶¶ 10, 11.)

            Mr. Thomas further declares that Plaintiff’s dismissal of his economic damages claims and his medical bills caused the testimony of Defendant LACMTA’s billing expert, Vicki Schweitzer, RN, to be rendered moot, and there was no longer any reason for Ms. Schweitzer to be called to testify at trial. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 12.) Ms. Schweitzer was not called as a witness to testify during trial and Plaintiff did not attempt to use the video of Ms. Schweitzer’s deposition transcript at the trial, and the video was not used in any manner at the trial. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 12.) As for the deposition of defense expert, Edward P. Milich, P.E., Mr. Thomas states that Plaintiff’s attorney took the deposition of such expert. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 14; Ex. 8.)

 III.           Appropriateness of Striking the Claimed Costs at Issue

Defendants seek to strike various cost items from Plaintiff’s memorandum of

costs on the grounds that such costs were unnecessary or unreasonable. Plaintiff asserts that all costs asserted in the memorandum of costs are proper. As indicated above, Plaintiff has withdrawn his request for prejudgment interest.

A.    Filing and Motion Fees (Item 1)

            Defendants contend that Item 1(c) of Plaintiff’s cost bill improperly claims $133.84 to file the complaint and that such cost item is duplicative. Defendants argue that Item 1(d) therein properly accounts for the filing of the summons and complaint. Plaintiff’s opposition does not address this contention raised by Defendants.

            Filing, motion, and jury fees are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).)

            The Court has reviewed Plaintiff’s Memorandum of Costs (Worksheet). Item 1(c) therein appears to be a duplicative entry as Item 1(d) therein already accounts for the filing fee for the complaint, summons, and civil case cover sheet. (Memorandum of Costs at p. 2.)

            The Court therefore finds it appropriate to strike Item 1(c) from the memorandum of costs as it appears that Plaintiff is seeking to recover twice for the filing of the summons and complaint.

            The Court therefore GRANTS Defendants’ request to strike Item 1(c) from the memorandum of costs in the sum of $133.64.

B.    Deposition Costs (Item 4)

            Defendants seek to strike deposition costs in the sum of $4,248.57 from Item 4 in Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs. (Mot. at p. 11:16-12:15.)

            CCP § 1033.5 provides that “[t]aking, video recording, and transcribing necessary depositions, including an original and one copy of those taken by the claimant and one copy of depositions taken by the party against whom costs are allowed” are recoverable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(3)(A).)

            Defendants assert that they do not challenge Plaintiff deposing their expert, Edward P. Milich, P.E., whom Defendant LACMTA retained at its liability expert. (Mot. at p. 11:18-20.) Defendants take issue with Plaintiff claiming a cost of $3,924.82 for a “1 Day Expedite” for Mr. Milich’s deposition. (Mot. at p. 11:20-21; Thomas Decl., ¶ 15; Ex. 9.)

            Plaintiff presents evidence that such costs was incurred due to Defendants producing Mr. Milich’s expert file the afternoon before his February 20, 2024 scheduled deposition, of which was not accessible and was untimely. (Tamazyan Decl., ¶¶ 3-4; Ex. B, C, D.) Plaintiff presents evidence that Mr. Milich’s February 20, 2024 deposition was cancelled due to the untimely production of records by Defendants. (Tamazyan Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. E.)

            Here, Plaintiff has presented a proper costs memorandum, which is prima facie evidence that the costs were necessarily incurred. Defendants have presented no evidence that the expedited deposition of Mr. Milich was not reasonable or necessary. The declaration of Mr. Thomas does not attest to the unreasonableness or lack of necessity of an expedited deposition. Defendants have therefore not met their burden in challenging such cost item. Defendants have not met their burden in showing that “the deposition was unnecessary or that the costs of taking the deposition were unreasonable . . . .” (County of Kern v. Ginn (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 1107, 1113.)

            Defendants also take issue with the $323.75 to video the deposition of Ms. Schweitzer. (Mot. at p. 11:25-28.) The fact that Plaintiff dismissed his economic damage claim after resting his case, and that the video was not seen or that Ms. Schweitzer testify at trial, does not render such cost item unreasonable or unnecessary. Defendants’ citation to Great Western Bank v. Converse Consultants, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 609, 615 is inapposite as such case involved the recovery of costs where an action never went to trial. Here, a trial did occur and therefore Great Western Bank v. Converse Consultants, Inc., supra, 58 Cal.App.4th 609, 615 is factually distinct. Further, Mr. Thomas does not attest that such cost was unnecessary or unreasonable. Defendants have therefore not met their burden in challenging such cost item.

            The Court therefore DENIES Defendants’ request to strike $4,248.57 from Item 4 in Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

C.    Service of Process Costs (Item 5)

            Defendants seek to strike $593.84 from Items 5(a) through 5(c) in Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs as Defendants contend that Plaintiff unnecessarily attempted service on Defendant Trapp five weeks after he appeared in this action and conducted an expensive stakeout. (Not. of Mot. at p. 2:19-23.)

            “If service is by a process server registered pursuant to Chapter 16 . . . of Division 18 of the Business and Professions Code, the recoverable cost is the amount actually incurred in effecting service, including, but not limited to, a stakeout or other means employed in locating the person to be served, unless those charges are successfully challenged by a party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(4)(B).)

            The Court has reviewed the cost memorandum. Item 5(a) is a $164.98 charge to serve Defendant Trapp; however, Item 5(b) is to serve “Officer Smith-Chickering” in the amount of $82.36 and Item 5(c) is a cost item in the sum of $82.36 to serve “Officer Matthew Charles Ryan.” (Memorandum of Costs (Worksheet) at p. 2.) Such items only add up to a total of $329.70 and not $593.84 as Defendants contend. Further, not all of those challenged items relate to service on Defendant Trapp. 

            Defendant Trapp was served via substituted service on April 29, 2021. (See 04/29/21 Proof of Service of Summons.) Here, Plaintiff provides an April 16, 2024 invoice indicating that there were additional service attempts on Defendant Trapp and, relevantly, a stakeout/waiting time charge of $400.00. (Thomas Decl., Ex. 10.) Plaintiff also provides a May 3, 2021 invoice showing substitute service on Defendant Trapp. (Thomas Decl., Ex. 10.)

            Plaintiff presents a June 24, 2021 invoice indicating non-service on Defendant Trapp for a charge of $133.84. (Thomas Decl., Ex. 10.) The Court opines as to why Plaintiff attempted service on Defendant Trapp after he had already been served in this action and filed an answer to the complaint on May 14, 2021. The Court therefore will strike the costs claimed in the June 24, 2021 invoice. The Court fails to see how such cost was reasonable or necessary since it was incurred after Defendant Trapp had already appeared in this action.

            The Court therefore GRANTS IN PART Defendants’ request to strike Item 5 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs. The Court taxes $133.84 from such cost item. The Court reduces Plaintiff’s service of process costs from the claimed amount of $3,103.58 to $2,969.74.

D.    Expert Witness Fees (Item 8(b))

             Defendants seek to tax $3,000.00 in costs from the fees charged by Dr. Kashani and Dr. Samimi from Item 8(b) of Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs on grounds that such experts each charge $7,500.00 for a half day’s testimony “despite Dr. Kashani testifying remotely for one and one half hours, and Dr. Samimi testifying for two hours.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 3:1-4.) Defendants also seek to strike costs for the work done by Jan Roughan. (Mot. at p. 13:10-14:11.)

            “Fees of expert witnesses ordered by the court” are recoverable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(8).)

            Here, both Dr. Kashani and Dr. Samimi each charge $7,500.00 per half day of trial. (Thomas Decl., Ex. 5, 6.) The Court, however, fails to see the substantiation for Defendants’ argument that the cost item for each doctor should be reduced by $3,000.00. (Mot. at p.14:12-15:4.) Defendants present no empirical calculation as to how they arrived at their requested reduction of $3,000.00 in fees for each doctor. Moreover, Mr. Thomas does not attest in his declaration that such fees were unnecessary or unreasonable. Defendants’ argument that the minimum charge of such doctors is unreasonable has not been substantiated as Defendants have failed to meet their burden on such point of argument.

            As to Defendants’ argument to strike the entirety of Ms. Roughan’s fees in the amount of $14,570.00, the Court DENIES Defendants’ request. Defendants have not presented any evidence that such costs were unnecessary or unreasonable. Further, as stated above, this case is distinguishable from Great Western Bank v. Converse Consultants, Inc., supra, 58 Cal.App.4th 609, upon which Defendants rely to strike the costs for Ms. Roughan’s work. Plaintiff has presented a memorandum of costs, which is a prima facie showing that such costs were necessary and reasonable.

            As such, the Court DENIES Defendants’ request to strike and/or tax Item 8(b) for expert witness fees from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

E.    Court Ordered Transcripts (Item 9)

            Defendants contend that Item 9 of Plaintiff’s cost bill, which claims $3,218.35 for court-ordered transcripts, should be stricken. (Mot. at p. 15:5-20.) Plaintiff contends that the cost bill contains an error as such amount is not for court-ordered transcripts. Plaintiff indicates that such cost is for trial transcripts that were “necessary for Plaintiff’s preparation and filing of the proposed judgment in this matter, as well as Plaintiff’s oppositions to motions brought by and necessitated by Defendants.” (Opp’n at p. 10:18-21.)

            “Transcripts of court proceedings order by the court” are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(9).) “Court report fees as established by statute” are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(11).)

            The Court finds that Plaintiff has not substantiated the necessity of any trial transcripts via the declaration of his counsel. Transcripts of court proceedings that are not ordered by the Court cannot be recovered as an item of costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(5).)

            The Court finds that striking Item 9 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs is appropriate. The Court therefore GRANTS Defendants’ request to strike Item 9 for Court-ordered transcripts from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs in the sum of $3,218.35.

F.     Other Costs (Item 16)

            Defendants seek to strike travel expenses from Item 16(5)-(9), 16(13), 16(24)-(35), as well as parking expenses from Item 16(36)-(37), which total $496.69. (Mot. at p. 17:3-10.) Only “[t]ravel expenses to attend depositions” are recoverable as a cost item. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(3)(C).) None of the challenged parking or travel expenses reference a deposition. Instead, such expenses involve counsel traveling to and from the courthouse and Plaintiff traveling to and from doctor’s appointments. While CCP § 1033.5 does not explicitly allow or prohibit other parking or travel costs, the Court exercises its discretion and STRIKES such costs from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

            Defendants also seek to strike Item 16(10) for the “Glucroft Investigation” in the amount of $125.00 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs. (Not. of Mot. at p. 4:5-7.) Investigation expenses are not allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(2).) Thus, the Court STRIKES Item 16(10) in the amount of $125.00 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

            Defendants also move to strike Item 16(38) from the memorandum of costs, which is a cost of $8.63 for UPS Printing. While “[m]odels, the enlargements of exhibits and photocopies of exhibits . . . may be allowed if they were reasonably helpful to aid the trier of fact” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(a)(13)), the vague description in the memorandum of costs does not allow the Court to ascertain what document or documents were printed. “Postage, telephone, and photocopying charges, except for exhibits” are not allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(3).) The Court therefore STRIKES Item 16(38) from the memorandum of costs.

            As to Item 16, which includes the prejudgment interest stricken pursuant to the Court’s discussion above, the Court taxes $178,567.34 therefrom.

RULING

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART Defendants’ Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs. The Court taxes Plaintiff’s costs in the sum of $182,053.17. Thus, the Court reduces Plaintiff’s costs articulated in the memorandum of costs from $260,257.27 to $78,204.10.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.   


Case Number: 20STCV47672    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted

Moving Party: Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority
Responding Party: None
Tentative Ruling: Deny

BACKGROUND

On December 14, 2020, Plaintiff David Bailey (“Plaintiff” or “Bailey”) filed an action against Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“MTA”), City of Los Angeles (“City”), County of Los Angeles (“County”), and Does 1 through 50 for premises liability.  Plaintiff alleges that, while attempting to board the Gold Line Train from the platform, he stepped on an unknown substance and sustained injuries, implying that he tripped and fell on the unknown substance.

On April 6, 2022, Defendant MTA filed an Answer and on April 11, 2022, Defendant County filed an Answer.

Pursuant to Plaintiff’s request, the Court dismissed Defendant City on April 8, 2022, and Defendant County on May 31, 2022.

On July 5, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s counsel Elina Shakhbazyan’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel.

On September 30, 2024, Defendant MTA filed the instant Motion for Order Deeming Requests for Admission (Set Two) Admitted and Request for Monetary Sanctions.  On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed Notice of Non-Opposition to the Motion.  No opposition has been filed.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

On August 5, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with Request for Admissions (Set 2).  Plaintiff has not responded to the discovery request.  Defendant moves for a court order deeming the Requests for Admission admitted.  Defendant also requests monetary sanctions for Plaintiff’s failure to serve responses in the amount of $1,225.

OPPOSITION

No opposition has been filed.

REPLY

No reply has been filed.

ANALYSIS
I. Legal Standard
Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 2033.280, subdivision (b), failure to respond to requests for admission in a timely manner allows the requesting party to “move for an order that…the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted” by the party that failed to respond. The requesting party’s motion must be granted by the court, “unless [the court] finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).) Since such motion is in response to failure to respond, there is no requirement to meet and confer prior to moving to deem the requests for admission admitted.  (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007), 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)  By failing to timely respond, the party to whom the requests are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or work product.  (Code Civ. Pro. § 2033.280, subd. (a).)

Courts are obligated to impose monetary sanctions in cases where a “failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c)).)  Sanctions are calculated based on “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (a)).

II. Motion

According to defense counsel, on August 5, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with Request for Admissions (Set 2) (“RFAs”).  (Nervez Decl. ¶¶ 3-4, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff has not responded to the discovery request.  (Id. at ¶ 5.)  Defendant also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,225, as follows: attorney’s fees at an hourly rate of $350 per hour for 0.5 hours to review the file, 1 hour to prepare the moving papers, 1 hour to review the opposition and prepare a reply, and 1 hour to attend the hearing on the Motion.  (Id. at ¶¶ 6-7.)

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not been properly served with the RFAs or moving papers.  Plaintiff was served with the RFAs by electronic service on August 5, 2024, and with the moving papers by electronic service on September 30, 2024.  (Nervez Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Ex. A; Motion, p. 14.)

Electronic service is authorized with conditions, such as express consent or court order.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.6; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251(a).)  According to Rules of Court, rule 2.251, documents may be served by electronic service by express consent or, if electronic service is required by local rules or court order, then self-represented parties are to be served by non-electronic methods, unless they affirmatively consent to electronic service.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251(b), 2.251(c)(3)(B)).  A self-represented party may expressly consent to be served by electronic service by:

(i) Serving a notice on all parties and filing the notice with the court.
(ii) Manifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court or the court’s electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party’s electronic address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service. The act of electronic filing shall not be construed as express consent.

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.6, subd. (c).)  Here, there is no evidence that self-represented Plaintiff has consented to electronic service.

For this reason, Defendant’s Motion is denied.

RULING

Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority’s Motion for Order Deeming Requests for Admission (Set Two) Admitted and Request for Monetary Sanctions as to Plaintiff David Bailey is DENIED.

Case Number: 21STCV45366    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings 

Moving Party: Defendant Lyft, Inc.       
Responding Party: None 
Tentative Ruling: Grant

BACKGROUND

On December 13, 2021, Plaintiff Marguerite Repola (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Lyft, Inc. (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive, alleging that Defendant “carelessly slammed [a] vehicle’s trunk on Plaintiff’s head,” which occurred on December 13, 2019. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges that she had legally contracted services from Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) 

On February 16, 2024, Defendant filed its answer to the complaint. 

On April 29, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Lynne M. Hobbs to the Honorable Ian C. Fusselman sitting in Department T at Alhambra Courthouse effective May 13, 2024. 

On September 20, 2024, Defendant filed and served the instant unopposed Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings. Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant seeks an order compelling arbitration and staying this action pending the completion of arbitration. Defendant contends that: (1) the Federal Arbitration Act (the “FAA”) governs the parties’ arbitration agreement; (2) the Court must compel Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant to arbitration; and (3) the Court should stay the proceedings against Defendant pending arbitration. 

OPPOSITION

As of October 23, 2024, no opposition brief has been filed.   

REPLY 

As of October 18, 2024, no reply brief has been filed.  

ANALYSIS 

I. Legal Standard/Applicable Law 

“[W]hen an agreement provides that its enforcement shall be governed by the FAA, the FAA governs a party’s motion to compel arbitration.” (Victrola 89, LLC v. Jaman Properties 8 LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 337, 346.) Under the FAA, “any arbitration agreement within its scope shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable.” (Chiron Corp. v. Ortho Diagnostic Systems, Inc. (9th Cir. 2000) 207 F.3d 1126, 1130.) An arbitration agreement that is subject to the FAA will be enforced if the following two factors are satisfied: (1) a valid agreement to arbitrate exists; and (2) the arbitration agreement encompasses the dispute at issue. (Ibid.) If both factors are met, the court must “enforce the arbitration agreement in accordance with its terms.” (Ibid.) 

“If any suit or proceeding be brought in any of the courts of the United States upon any issue referable to arbitration under an agreement in writing for such arbitration, the court in which such suit is pending, upon being satisfied that the issue involved in such suit or proceeding is referable to arbitration under such an agreement, shall on application of one of the parties stay the trial of the action until such arbitration has been had in accordance with the terms of the agreement, providing the applicant for the stay is not in default in proceeding with such arbitration.” (9 U.S.C. § 3.) 

II. The Scope of the Terms of Service  

Defendant seeks to compel arbitration pursuant to Defendant’s August 26, 2019 Terms of Service, to which Plaintiff agreed, according to Defendant’s Safety Program Lead, Paul McCachern (“McCachern”). (McCachern Decl., ¶¶ 1, 12, 15, 16; Ex. 1, 4.) The August 26, 2019 Terms of Service were in effect at the time of the alleged incident on December 13, 2019, as alleged in Plaintiff’s complaint. (McCachern Decl., ¶ 14.) The agreement reads in pertinent part: 

“These Terms of Service constitute a legally binding agreement (the “Agreement”) between you and Lyft, Inc., its parents, subsidiaries, representatives, affiliates, officers, and directors . . . governing your use of the Lyft application, website, and technology platform.” (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 1.) “By entering into this Agreement, and/or by using or accessing the Lyft Platform you expressly acknowledge that you understand this Agreement (including the dispute resolution and arbitration provisions in Section 17) and accept all of its terms.” (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 1.) 

The Terms of Service, in section 17 therein, contains a Dispute Resolution and Arbitration Agreement (the “Arbitration Agreement”). (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17.) The Arbitration Agreement provides that Plaintiff and Defendant mutually agree to waive their respective rights to resolution of disputes in a court of law by a judge or jury and agree to resolve any dispute by arbitration. (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).) The Arbitration Agreement applies to “all Claims . . . between [Plaintiff] and Lyft, including [its] affiliates, subsidiaries, parents, successors and assigns, and each of [its] respective officers, directors, employees, agents, or shareholders.” (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).) The Arbitration Agreement defines “Claims” as all disputes between Plaintiff and Defendant. (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).) Such Claims “include, but are not limited to, any dispute, claim or controversy, whether based in past, present, or future events, arising out of or relating to: [the] Agreement and prior versions thereof . . ., the Lyft  Platform, [or] the Rideshare Platform . . .  and all . . . federal and state statutory common law claims.” (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).) 

The Arbitration Agreement further provides that “[a]ll disputes concerning the arbitrability of a [c]laim . . . shall be decided by the arbitrator, except as expressly provided” in the Arbitration Agreement. (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).) The arbitration is to be administered by the American Arbitration Association. (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(b).) The Arbitration Agreement is “governed by the Federal Arbitration Act . . . .” (McCachern Decl., Ex. 4 at § 17(a).)

III. A Valid Arbitration Agreement Exists and it Covers the Dispute at Issue

Initially, the Court finds that the FAA applies to the Arbitration Agreement as it
is expressly stated that the FAA governs such agreement. 

Further, the Court finds that a valid arbitration agreement exists between Plaintiff and Defendant. Defendant presents evidence that Plaintiff expressly consented to its Terms of Service—which includes the Arbitration Agreement—on five separate occasions. (McCachern Decl., ¶ 12.) Such consent was given both prior to and after the subject accident. (McCachern Decl., ¶ 12.) 

Next, the Court finds that the Arbitration Agreement covers the dispute at issue in this litigation. The complaint arises from Plaintiff’s use of Defendant’s rideshare services. (McCachern Decl., ¶ 13.) The Arbitration Agreement provides that any claims arising from use of Defendant’s platform are subject to binding arbitration. Thus, Plaintiff’s claims fall within the scope of the Arbitration Agreement. 

In sum, the Court finds that a valid arbitration agreement exists and that such agreement covers the claims asserted against Defendant in this action. Given that Plaintiff failed to oppose the instant motion, the Court deems the lack of opposition as “a consent to the granting of the motion.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 8.54(c).)  

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings. 

The Court STAYS this action pending the completion of arbitration pursuant to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement. (9 U.S.C. § 3.)  The Court sets a post-arbitration status conference for October 27, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. 

Defendant Lyft, Inc. is ordered to give notice.
