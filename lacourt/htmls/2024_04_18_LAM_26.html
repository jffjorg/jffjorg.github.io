DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 20STCV26898    Hearing Date: April 18, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

 

RAUL GUTIERREZ,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

KIA MOTORS America, INC., and does 1 through 10, inclusive,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.: 20STCV26898

 

 Hearing Date: April 18, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

DEFENDANT KIA MOTORS AMERICA, INC.’S MOTION TO TAX COSTS

 

 

 

Background

            On February 3, 2021, Plaintiff Raul Gutierrez (“Plaintiff”) filed the Operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against defendant Kia Motors America, Inc. (“Defendant”). The FAC asserted six causes of action for (1) Violation of Subdivision (D) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (2) Violation of Subdivision (B) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (3) Violation of Subdivision (A)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (4) Breach of Express Written Warranty; (5) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; and (6) Fraud by Omission.

            On June 1, 2023, at the conclusion of a jury trial, the jury found in favor of Plaintiff. (Special Verdict 6/1/23.) On September 12, 2023, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff pursuant to the jury’s special verdict. (Judgment 9/12/23.)

            On July 31, 2023, Plaintiff filed a memorandum of costs. On August 17, 2023, Defendant filed the instant motion to tax costs. The motion is unopposed.

Legal Standard

“A prevailing party is entitled ‘as a matter of right’ to recover costs in any action or proceeding unless a statute expressly provides otherwise.”  (Segal v. ASICS America Corp. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 651, 658.)  “Section 1033.5 sets forth the types of expenses that are and are not allowable as costs under section 1032. Specifically, subdivision (a) of section 1033.5 describes items that are ‘allowable as costs,’ subdivision (b) describes items ‘not allowable as costs, except when expressly authorized by law,’ and section 1033.5(c)(4) provides that ‘[i]tems not mentioned in this section and items assessed upon application may be allowed or denied in the court's discretion.’”  (Ibid.)

To recover a cost, it must be reasonably necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount. (Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises (l992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 244.) If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-74.)  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.) 

Discussion

Timeliness of the Instant Motion

California Rule of Court, Rule 3.1700 requires that “[a]ny notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1).)  Additionally, “the court may extend the times for serving and filing the cost memorandum or the notice of motion or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(3).) 

            Here, the memorandum of costs was served electronically on July 31, 2023.  Accordingly, Defendant had until August 18, 2023 to timely file the instant motion. The instant motion was filed on August 17, 2023. Therefore, the instant motion was timely filed. 

Reasonably Incurred Costs

            “It is not necessary that the [moving party], …, accompany the motion to tax cost with any affidavit.”  (State of California v. Meyer (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 1061, 1075.)  Rather, “[i]f items on a memorandum of costs appear to be proper charges on their face, those items are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services are proper and necessarily incurred. [Citation.] The burden then shifts to the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.”  (Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 693.)  “[T]he objecting party has the burden to show that a cost item is unrecoverable because it was not necessary to the litigation.”  (Hooked Media Group, Inc. v. Apple Inc. (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 323, 338.)  “However, more than a bare objection is needed to rebut the presumption that fees were reasonable and necessarily incurred.”  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

            Defendant contends that the (1) Mediation fees, (2) Court Reporter fees, (3) Veritext Hearing Attendance costs for Defendant’s MSJ, and (4) Veritext Hearing Attendance costs for Final Status Conference should be taxed.

            Court reporter fees including the Veritext Hearing Attendance for Defendant’s MSJ and Final Status Conference are expressly permitted as costs.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(a)(11).)  Thus, the burden falls on Defendant to show that these claimed costs are unreasonable. Defendant fails to do so and appears to misinterpret the statutory language concerning court reporter fees being allowable cost “ as established by statute” by arguing Plaintiff cannot cite any statute that authorizes costs for a court reporter at demurrer hearing. However, court reporter fees are allowable costs pursuant to Government Code Section 68086(a)(2)(c), which states in pertinent part “[t]he costs for the services of the official court reporter shall be recoverable as taxable costs by the prevailing party as otherwise provided by law” in civil actions or cases and apply in Superior Court. Therefore, Defendant has not shown that the court reporter fees are unreasonable.

            As to the mediation fees, they may be allowed or denied in the Court’s discretion. Thus, the burden falls on Defendant to show that the claim costs is unreasonable. Defendant fails to do so and merely argues Plaintiff did not make a settlement demand or any effort to resolve the case prior to the mediation, thus Plaintiff cannot argue the mediation was necessary to resolve the case. However, Defendant proffers no legal authority to support its contention that Plaintiff needed to make a settlement demand prior to mediation. Furthermore, Defendant concedes that the mediation did take place and presumably in an effort to resolve the case prior to trial. Therefore, Defendant has not shown the mediation fee is unreasonable. 

Conclusion and ORDER

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant Kia Motors America, Inc.’s motion to tax costs is DENIED.

Background

            On January 27, 2023, Plaintiffs Dong Park; Ramblewood Holdings, Ltd., and DP Textile, Inc. (“Plaintiffs”) filed the instant action arises out of the legal representation of Plaintiffs by Defendants Boris Treyzon, A. Monica Szkopek, Katrina Yu, Kevin Khonsari, Elizabeth Isisquiza, Abir Cohen Treyzon Salos LLP, ACTS Law, and DOES 1 through 50 (“Defendants”).

            On October 5, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants asserting seven causes of action for (1) Professional Negligence; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Breach of Contract; (4) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (5) Fraud and Deceit; (6) Constructive Fraud; and (7) Negligent Misrepresentation.

            On November 7, 2023, Defendant Abir Cohen Treyzon Salos, LLP (“ACTS LLP”) filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike the FAC. On April 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition to the demurrer. On April 11, 2024, ACTS LLP filed a reply as to the opposition and notice of non-opposition to the motion to strike.

 

Allegations of  the Operative FAC

The allegations contained in Paragraphs 1 through 44 are referred to and incorporated by reference. (FAC ¶ 44.) Defendants represented Plaintiffs and each of them in the Underlying Action and shared an attorney client relationship. (FAC ¶ 45.) Due to the attorney-client relationship, Defendants had a legal duty to exercise that degree of learning, and use the degree of care and skill, ordinarily possessed by a reputable attorney or law firm, practicing under similar circumstances. (FAC ¶¶ 46, 54-55, 82.) Defendants had a duty to use reasonable diligence and their best judgment in the exercise of skill and the application of that learning. (Id.) (FAC ¶ 48.) Defendants breached their duties and professional obligations owed to Plaintiffs. (FAC ¶¶ 49, 56-57, 63-66, 71, 83-85.) Defendants breach of duties were he direct and proximate cause of Plaintiffs’ damages sustained in the Underlying Action. (FAC ¶¶ 51, 59, 67, 73, 79, 89, 94.)

Plaintiffs performed each and every obligation to be performed by and pursuant to the Fee Agreement except to the extent excused by the conduct of the Defendants. Defendants have failed and refused to provide Plaintiffs with a copy of the Fee Agreement. (FAC ¶ 62.) (FAC ¶¶76-77, 86-87, 92-93.) In reasonable reliance on said false representations, among others, Plaintiffs did not take steps to retain competent legal counsel or to take other steps to protect their interests. (FAC ¶ 78, 88.) Had Plaintiffs known the true facts, they would not have taken such actions and/or would have taken other actions. (Id.)

 

Legal Standard

Demurrer Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). (Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.)

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

Motion to Strike Standard

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.).  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 435-437.)  A party may file a motion to strike in whole or in part within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, however, if a party serves and files a motion to strike without demurring to the complaint, the time to answer is extended.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 435(b)(1), 435(c).)

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

Meet and Confer Requirement

Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41, subdivision (a) requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer¿in person or by telephone¿with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due and if they are unable to meet the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(2).)  The demurring party must also file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts.  (Id.¿at (a)(3).)¿ If an amended pleading is filed, the parties must meet and confer again before a demurrer may be filed to the amended pleading.  (Id.¿at (a).)  There is a similar meet and confer requirement for motions to strike.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.)

Defendant ACTS LLP has fulfilled the meet and confer requirement.  (Besa Decl. ¶¶ 4-5, Exs. A-B.)

 

Discussion- Demurrer

Second Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

            Defendant ACTS LLP contends the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty is (1) duplicative of the first cause of action for professional negligence and (2) fails to state sufficient facts to plead a cause of action as to Defendant ACTS LLP. 

To state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, a plaintiff must establish “(1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship, (2) its breach, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.” (O’Neal v. Stanislaus County Employees’ Retirement Assn. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1184, 1215.)

However, the California courts are split on whether or not a demurrer may be sustained where a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action. In Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, the court held that duplicity is not a ground on which a demurer may be sustained but rather a motion to strike is the proper way to address such issue. (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 890 [that a cause of action is duplicative “is not a ground on which a demurrer may be sustained”].) By contrast, the court in Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, held that a demurrer may sustained on the basis of duplicity where a cause of action contains allegations of other causes, thus adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery. (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.) Nevertheless, in Buehler v. Sbardellati (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 1527, the court noted “there is authority for the view the breach of fiduciary duty theory is separate from the professional negligence theory” because it has been recognized as an action in tort, not contract “which is a distinct and independent from professional negligence but still comprises of legal malpractice.” (Buehler v. Sbardellati (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 1527, 1545, fn. 9.)

Here, Plaintiffs undoubtedly allege legal malpractice against Defendants under the theories of professional negligence and breach of fiduciary concerning the Underlying Action. Although the allegations for the first cause of action for professional negligence and second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty contained allegations for failure to provide legal services professionally, the second cause of action does plead additional facts that adds to the FAC such as Defendants failure to timely provide accurate billing statements and Defendants acting in their own interest. (FAC ¶ 56.) Furthermore, Plaintiff sufficiently pleads facts to support that the parties had a fiduciary relationship by virtue of the attorney-client relationship, Defendants’ breach that fiduciary duty, and Defendants breach was the proximate cause of Plaintiffs’ damages.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

Defendant ACTS LLP contends the third cause of action for breach of contract fails to state sufficient facts (1) to ascertain whether the alleged contract is written, oral, or is implied by conduct and (2) to plead a cause of action as to Defendant ACTS LLP.

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, plaintiff must plead: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.)

Plaintiffs allege the parties entered into a Contingency Fee Agreement for Defendants to provide legal services to Plaintiffs in 2016. (FAC ¶ 23.) The FAC further alleges that the parties entered into an Hourly Fee Agreement on or about June 17, 2017. (Id. ¶ 24.) The FAC also allege Plaintiffs performed each and every obligation under the Fee Agreement except to the extent excused by Defendants’ conduct. The FAC goes on to allege that Defendants breached the agreements, which resulted in damages to Plaintiffs. (Id. ¶¶ 63-67.) As such, Plaintiffs sufficiently plead a cause of action for breach of contract.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP’s demurrer as to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

Defendant ACTS LLP contends the fourth cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is duplicative of the third cause of action for breach of contract as to Defendant ACTS LLP.

“The [implied] covenant of good faith and fair dealing [is] implied by law in every contract. The covenant is read into contracts and functions ‘as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party's rights to the benefits of the contract.’” (Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230, 1244.) As such, “A breach of the implied covenant of good faith is a breach of the contract.” (Id.)¿

Plaintiffs’ claim for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is predicated on the existence a contract. As discussed above, Plaintiffs have plead that two contracts existed between the parties for legal services in exchange for payment. Furthermore, the FAC alleges Defendants breached the contracts and Plaintiffs’ suffered damages as a proximate result of Defendants’ breach. However, Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for breach of contract and fourth cause of action for breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are based on the same two contracts and the same factual allegations. Moreover, Plaintiffs’ allegations in the fourth cause of action do not go beyond mere contract breach and “absent those limited cases where a breach of a consensual contract term is not claimed or alleged, the only justification for asserting a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant is to obtain a tort recovery.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.) There is no indication from the pleadings that Plaintiffs assert the fourth cause of action as basis for tort recovery. As such, Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action is duplicative of the third cause of action.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP demurrer as to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED with Leave to Amend.

 

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Fraud and Deceit

Defendant ACTS LLP contends the fifth cause of action for fraud and deceit is (1) duplicative of the first, sixth, seventh, and eight cause of action for fraud in the inducement[1] and (2) fails to state sufficient facts with specificity to plead a cause of action as to Defendant ACTS LLP.

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1060.) “Each element must be alleged with particularity.” (Id.)

Plaintiffs merely allege Defendants made false, fraudulent representations to Plaintiffs concerning fully protecting their interests; truthfully advising them in the Underlying Action on the proper course of action, using best efforts to keep Plaintiffs informed about the Underlying Action, the adequacy of discovery responses, and their expertise in handling business litigation matters. Plaintiffs also allege Defendants made these representations to them knowing or should have known they were false and concealing the true facts with the intent to defraud and deceive Plaintiffs. Furthermore, Plaintiffs allege in reasonable reliance on these false representations, they did not take steps to retain competent legal counsel or protect their interests resulting in damages. Moreover, Plaintiffs allege the procedural posture of the discovery responses and sanctions. (FAC ¶¶ 26-39.) However, none these facts allege which Defendant made what representations, what specific statements were made regarding the adequacy of the discovery responses, when these representations were made to Plaintiffs, and the method by which these representations were made such as orally, email, text message, etc. As such, Plaintiffs have not plead the fifth cause of action with particularity.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP demurrer as to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with Leave to Amend.

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Constructive Fraud

Defendant ACTS LLP contends the sixth cause of action for constructive fraud is (1) duplicative of the first, fifth, and seventh causes of action and (2) fails to state sufficient facts with specificity to plead a cause of action as to Defendant ACTS LLP.

Under Civil Code Section 1573, “[c]onstructive fraud consists: (1) [i]n any breach of duty which, without an actually fraudulent intent, gains an advantage to the person in fault, or any one claiming under him, by misleading another to his prejudice, or to the prejudice of any one claiming under him; or (2) [i]n any such act or omission as the law specially declares to be fraudulent, without respect to actual fraud.” (Civ. Code § 1573.)

“A breach of a fiduciary duty usually constitutes constructive fraud. Constructive fraud, like actual fraud, must be pleaded with specificity.” (Knox v. Dean II (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 417, 434.)

Here, Plaintiffs allege Defendants breached their fiduciary duties owed to Plaintiffs as explained above. However, Plaintiffs have not plead any specific facts as to Defendant purported false representations/misrepresentations. Plaintiffs merely allege Defendant made misrepresentations and concealed facts that Defendants had a duty to disclose with the intent to induce Plaintiffs reliance. Plaintiffs also merely allege they relied on those misrepresentations resulting in damages. As such, Plaintiffs fail to plead the sixth cause of action with particularity.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP demurrer as to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED with Leave to Amend.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

Defendant ACTS LLP contends the seventh cause of action for negligent misrepresentation is (1) duplicative of the first, fifth, and sixth causes of action and (2) fails to state sufficient facts with specificity to plead a cause of action as to Defendant ACTS LLP.

“The elements of negligent misrepresentation are ‘(1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage.’” (National Union Fire Ins. Co. of Pittsburgh, PA v. Cambridge Integrated Services Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 35, 50.) As to a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation “[t]he plaintiff must allege the specifics of his or her reliance on the misrepresentation to show a bona fide claim of actual reliance.” (Cadlo v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (2004) 125 Cal.App.4th 513, 519.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendants made misrepresentations during the course of their legal representation of Plaintiffs concerning protecting Plaintiffs’ interests, truthfully advising them, using best efforts to inform them about the case status, and adequacy of the discovery responses in the Underlying Action. Plaintiffs also allege they relied on these misrepresentations resulting in damages. Although Plaintiffs allegations in the seventh cause of action for negligent misrepresentation are almost identical to the allegations set forth in the fifth cause of action, Plaintiffs do allege additional facts concerning Defendants concealment of the Discovery Referee and Court Orders. Nevertheless, Plaintiffs do not plead any specific facts of how they actually relied on Defendants purported misrepresentations such as emails, text messages, oral conversations with Defendants of the alleged misrepresentations. As such, Plaintiffs have not stated sufficient facts to support the seventh cause of action.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP demurrer as to the seventh cause of action is SUSTAINED with Leave to Amend

.

Discussion – Motion to Strike

Defendant ACTS LLP moves to strike numerous paragraphs of the FAC. Specifically, Defendant ACTS LLP moves to strike paragraphs 52, 60, 74, 80, 90, 95, 98, 108, and 109.

Defendant ACTS LLP contends (1) Plaintiffs are not entitled to punitive damages since no evidence of malice, oppression, or fraudulent conduct is sufficiently pled; and (2) Plaintiffs are not entitled to disgorgement since no fees were paid to ACTS LAW and no “egregious” ethical breach is alleged.

California Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a), provides: “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”  “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. at (c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Id. at (c)(2).) “‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Id. at (c)(3).) Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95.)  

Moreover, a demand for punitive damages for the commission of any tort requires more than the mere conclusory allegations of “oppression, fraud, and malice.”  (Civ. Code § 3294; see Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 117 Cal. App.3d 1, 6-7.) 

As set forth above, the FAC fails to sufficiently plead facts to support the fifth sixth, and seventh causes of action based on fraudulent misrepresentations. However, the FAC does sufficiently allege facts to support the second and third causes of action. As such, a reasonable jury could find that Defendants conduct in allegedly informing Plaintiffs’ that their discovery responses were adequate when there were court orders stating otherwise was done with intent to cause injury to Plaintiffs. Likewise, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action.

Therefore, Defendant ACTS LLP’s motion to strike is GRANTED, in part as to the punitive damages allegations.

 

Conclusion and ORDER

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant Abir Cohen Treyzon Salos LLP’s demurrer is OVERRULED IN PART as to the second and third causes of action and SUSTAINED IN PART with 30 days leave to amend as to the fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action.

            Defendant Abir Cohen Treyzon Salos LLP’s motion to strike is GRANTED, in part as to the punitive damages allegations with 30 days leave to amend.
