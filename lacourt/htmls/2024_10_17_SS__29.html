DEPARTMENT 29 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 29 - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS IMPORTANT  (PLEASE SEND YOUR E-MAIL TO DEPT. 29 NOT DEPT. 2)

Communicating with the Court Staff re the Tentative Ruling 1. Please notify the courtroom staff by email not later than 9:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion. The email address is SSCDEPT29@lacourt.org. Please do not use any other email address. 2. You must include the other parties on the email by "cc." 3. Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the Subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. THE COURT WILL HEAR ARGUMENT UNLESS BOTH SIDES SUBMIT ON THE TENTATIVE.  4. Include the words "SUBMISSION BUT WILL APPEAR" if you submit, but one or both parties will nevertheless appear. 5. For other communications with Court Staff a. OFF-CALENDAR should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed to have a matter placed off-calendar. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) date of proceeding. b. CASE SETTLED should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed that the case has settled for all purposes. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) whether notice of settlement/dismissal documents have been filed; (c) if (b) has not been done, a date one year from the date of your email which will be a date set by the court for an OSC for dismissal of the case. c. STIPULATION should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have stipulated that a matter before the court can be postponed. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) what proceeding is agreed to be postponed e.g. Trial, FSC; (c) the agreed-upon future date; (d) whether all parties waive notice if the Court informs all counsel/parties that the agreed-upon date is satisfactory. This communication should be used only for matters that are agreed to be postponed and not for orders shortening time. 6. PLEASE MAKE SURE THAT ALL COMMUNICATIONS WITH COURT STAFF DEAL ONLY WITH SCHEDULING AND ADMINISTRATIVE MATTERS AND DO NOT DISCUSS THE MERITS OF ANY CASE. (UPDATED 6/17/2020) 
IMPORTANT:  In light of the COVID-19 emergency, the Court encourages all parties to appear remotely.  The capacity in the courtroom is extremely limited.  The Court appreciates the cooperation of counsel and the litigants. 
ALSO NOTE:  If the moving party does not contact the court to submit on the tentative and does not appear (either remotely or in person), the motion will be taken off calendar.  THE TENTATIVE RULING WILL NOT BE THE ORDER OF THE COURT.



Case Number: 19STCV16639    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 29

Pope-Morgan v Chen
19STCV16639
Plaintiffs’ Motion for Order Extending Five-Year Period to Bring Matter to Trial or, in the Alternative, for a Preferential Trial Setting

 

Tentative

 

The motion is denied.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs Angela Pope-Morgan, Asia Morgan, Alma Morgan, Kirk Morgan, and Sarah Morgan (“Plaintiffs”) allege that they were injured on May 12, 2017, in a collision with a motor vehicle driven by Defendant Leilei Chen on Interstate I-40 in New Mexico. 

 

On May 13, 2019, Plaintiffs filed the Complaint in this action against Defendants Leilei Chen, FBM Group Corporation (collectively, “Defendants”), and Does 1 through 25. 

 

Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on August 13, 2019. 

 

Defendants filed their Answer to the FAC on September 6, 2019.

 

On January 5, 2023, the court, at the request of Plaintiff Alma Morgan, dismissed the causes of action in the FAC asserted by Plaintiff Alma Morgan with prejudice.

 

On September 1, 2023, the Court determined the matter to be long cause and ordered the parties to submit the Long Cause Trial Package Sheet to Department 1 for long cause determination. 

 

By Minute Order dated September 20, 2023, Department 1 (the Hon. Michelle Williams Court, J.) determined that the Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties was not complete and that the parties were not trial ready. 

 

On October 23, 2023, Department 1 received a second Long Cause Trial Package from the parties. 

 

By Minute Order dated November 8, 2023, Department 1 (Judge Court) determined that this second Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties was not complete and that the parties were not trial ready.

 

On March 14, 2024, Department 1 received a third Long Cause Trial Package from the parties.

 

By Minute Order dated April 24, 2024, Department 1 (Judge Court) determined that this third Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties was not complete and that the parties were not trial ready.

 

On September 4, 2024, Department 1 received a fourth Long Cause Trial Package from the parties.

 

By Minute Order dated September 18, 2024, Department 1 (Judge Court) determined that fourth third Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties was not complete and that the parties were not trial ready.

 

Previously, on February 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion seeking an order tolling and extending the five-year period to bring this matter to trial.  No opposition was filed.  On March 13, 2024, the Court denied the motion, without prejudice, as premature.

 

On September 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this motion to extend the five-year period to bring this matter to trial or, in the alternative, for a preferential trial setting.  Defendants filed an Opposition on October 4.  Plaintiffs filed a Reply on October 10.

 

Legal Standard

 

Five Year Rule

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310 provides: “An action shall be brought to trial within five years after the action is commenced against the defendant.”   (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.310.)  If an action is not brought to trial within five years, the action “shall be dismissed by the court on its own motion or on motion of the defendant, after notice to the parties.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.360, subd. (a).)  The five-year period is “mandatory” and is “not subject to extension, excuse, or exception, except as expressly provided by statute.”  (Id., subd. (b).)

 

The five-year period may be extended by written stipulation or an oral agreement of the parties made in court, “if entered in the minutes of the court or a transcript is made.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.330.) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340 provides that in calculating the five-year period,

 

“[T]here shall be excluded the time during which any of the following conditions existed:

 

“(a) The jurisdiction of the court to try the action was suspended.

 

(b) Prosecution or trial of the action was stayed or enjoined.

 

(c) Bringing the action to trial, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile.”

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 583.350, if less than 6 months remains to bring the action to trial at the end of a statutory period of tolling or extension, the action may not be dismissed if it is brought to trial within 6 months after that period has ended.

 

Emergency Rule 10, subdivision (a), extends the time in which to bring a civil action to trial by six months.  (Emergency Rules Related to COVID-19, Emergency rule 10.)  The six-month extension of section 583.350, however, does not apply in in addition to the six-month extension of time to bring a civil action to trial under subdivision (a) of Emergency Rule 10.  (Ables v. A. Ghazale Bros. (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 823, 828-829.)

 

The California Supreme Court has explained that in applying the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340, subdivision (c):

 

“[T]he trial court must determine what is impossible, impracticable, or futile in light of all the circumstances in the individual case, including the acts and conduct of the parties and the nature of the proceedings themselves.  The critical factor in applying these exceptions to a given factual situation is whether the plaintiff exercised reasonable diligence in prosecuting his or her case.  A plaintiff’s reasonable diligence alone does not preclude involuntary dismissal; it is simply one factor for assessing the existing exceptions of impossibility, impracticability, or futility.  Every period of time during which the plaintiff does not have it within his power to bring the case to trial is not to be excluded in making the computation.  Time consumed by the delay caused by ordinary incidents of proceedings, like disposition of demurrer, amendment of pleading, and the normal time of waiting for a place on the court’s calendar are not within the contemplation of these exceptions.  Determining whether the subdivision (c) exception applies requires a fact-sensitive inquiry and depends on the obstacles faced by the plaintiff in prosecuting the action and the plaintiff’s exercise of reasonable diligence in overcoming those obstacles.  Impracticability and futility involve a determination of excessive and unreasonable difficulty or expense, in light of all the circumstances of the particular case. …  The reasonable diligence standard is an appropriate guideline for evaluating whether it was impossible, impracticable, or futile for the plaintiff to comply with [the statute] due to causes beyond his or her control.”

 

(Bruns v. E-Commerce Exchange (2011) 51 Cal.4th 717, 730-731 [citations and internal quotations omitted]; accord Gaines v. Fidelity National Title Ins. Co. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1081, 1101-1102.)  “The plaintiff bears the burden of providing that the circumstances warrant applicable of the section 583.340(c) exception.”  (Bruns, 51 Cal.4th at p. 731.)

 

The exceptions under section 583.340 “must be liberally construed consistent with the policy favoring trial on the merits.” (Dowling v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 685, 693; accord Seto v. Szeto (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 76, 85.)

 

Trial Preference

 

Code of Civil Procedure section governs motions for trial preferences.  Subdivision (e) provides:

 

“Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the court may in its discretion grant a motion for preference that is supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting this preference.”

 

“A plaintiff is not automatically entitled to a preferential trial setting merely because a failure to specially set would lead to expiration of the five-year statute.”  (Howard v. Thrifty Drug & Discount Stores (1995) 10 Cal.4th 424, 440.)  “Rather, [the plaintiff] must generally make some showing of excusable delay in order to receive the preference.”  (Ibid.)

 

“[T]he discretion to grant or deny a preferential trial setting rests at all times in the sound discretion of the trial court in light of the totality of the circumstances.”  (Salas v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 342, 344.)  Factors considered for trial preference include the condition of the court's calendar, dilatory conduct by plaintiff, prejudice to the defendant of an accelerated trial date, and the likelihood of eventual mandatory dismissal if the early trial date is denied.  (Id. at p. 346.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiffs move for a court order extending the five-year period to bring their case to trial.  The action was filed on May 13, 2019, and the five-year period (plus six months under Emergency Order 10) expires on November 13, 2024.

 

Plaintiffs argue that despite their diligent efforts to bring this case to trial, circumstances have rendered it impossible, impracticable, and futile for trial to commence within the five-year period.  These circumstances include (1) trial being continued several times pursuant to Defendants’ request, most recently over Plaintiffs’ objection, (2) the present long cause determination, which has been pending for thirteen months and continues to remain unresolved, and (3) the high probability that even after a long cause determination is made, there will be a delay before the case is assigned to a long cause courtroom for trial.  Plaintiffs argue that they have fully and diligently pursued discovery and the prosecution of the case.  (See Painter Decl. ¶¶ 2-9.) 

 

What Plaintiffs do not explain in their papers, however, is why there have been such long delays in connection with the submission of the Long Cause Trial Package to Department 1. 

 

On September 1, 2023, the Court determined the matter to be long cause and ordered the parties to submit the Long Cause Trial Package Sheet to Department 1 for long cause determination.  Plaintiff does not explain why now, more than a year later, the parties have failed to submit a proper and complete Long Cause Trial Package.

 

On September 20, 2023, Judge Court issued a detailed order explaining the defects in the first Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties.  The parties waited a month before resubmitting.

 

On November 8, 2023, Judge Court issued a detailed order explaining the defects in the second Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties.  The parties then waited four months before resubmitting.

 

On April 24, 2024, Judge Court issued a detailed order explaining the defects in the third Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties.  The parties again waited more than four months before resubmitting.

 

On September 18, 2024, Judge Court issued a detailed order explaining the defects in the fourth Long Cause Trial Package submitted by the parties.  Even though the mandatory period to bring the action to trial was set to expire in less than sixty days from that order, there is no indication in the file that the parties have resubmitted a revised Long Cause Trial Package to Department 1.

 

The issue is not, as Plaintiffs contend, the unavailability of a courtroom for the trial, or delays in having a courtroom available.  The issue is that the parties are not ready for trial, as Judge Court stated in four separate minute orders issued in September 2023, November 2023, April 2024, and September 2024.  Plaintiffs, who have the burden of proof on this motion, have not shown why they are not ready for trial now, approximately 65 months after the complaint was filed, or what diligence (if any) they have shown over the past thirteen months in submitting the documents necessary to show that this case is ready for trial.

 

On this record, the Court finds, Plaintiffs have not demonstrated reasonable diligence in prosecuting their case over the past thirteen months.  They have not show (for example) that they were ready for trial but no courtroom was available.  Nor have they show that preparing the basic documents required for every long cause case was unreasonably difficult under the circumstances of the case or that Defendants (or other circumstances) substantially interfered with their diligent efforts to get their case ready for trial. 

 

The motion for an order extending the mandatory period to bring the case to trial is denied.

 

The denial is based on the evidence in the record and the arguments in the moving and reply papers.  The denial is without prejudice to any additional evidence or argument that Plaintiffs may submit in the event that the mandatory period expires and Defendants or the Court set a hearing on a motion or Order to Show Cause regarding dismissal.

 

The Court has not considered the arguments made and evidence presented by Plaintiffs for the first time in connection with their reply papers that Defendants agreed to stipulate to an extension and then unreasonably changed their position, to the unfair detriment to Plaintiffs.

 

In the alternative, Plaintiffs request a discretionary preferential trial setting under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (e), to serve “the interests of justice.”  As noted above, however, the California Supreme Court has held that plaintiffs are “not automatically entitled to a preferential trial setting merely because a failure to specially set would lead to expiration of the five-year statute,” particularly absent a “showing of excusable delay.” (Howard, supra, 10 Cal.4th at p. 440; accord 3 Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (2024), ¶ 12:259.) 

 

The Court has considered all of the facts and circumstances of this matter in exercising its discretion under subdivision (e) of section 36.  On this record, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not shown reasonable diligence or excusable delay, particularly over the past 13 months.  This factor weighs strongly against granting the request and outweighs the other factors that support granting relief (including the possibility of dismissal).

 

The request for preferential trial setting is denied.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion to toll and extend the period to bring this action to trial or, in the alternative, for a preferential trial setting.

 

Defendants, as prevailing parties, are ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 21STCV35122    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 29

Velasco v. Nuestra Senora de Guadalupe, Inc.
21STCV35122
Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

Tentative

The motion is denied.

Background

On September 22, 2021, Plaintiff Marta Velasco (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Nuestra Senora De Guadalupe, Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 30 for premises liability and general negligence arising out of a fall on September 26, 2019.

Several requests for entry of default were rejected in 2023.

On November 29, 2023, Plaintiff amended the complaint to correct the name of Defendant to “Our Lady of Guadalupe Church.” 

On February 13, 2024, there was no appearance at the Trial Setting Conference.  The Court dismissed the entire action without prejudice.

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to set aside the dismissal.  The hearing was set for July 24.  There was no appearance by Plaintiff.  As the hearing was shortly after a cyber attack on the Los Angeles County Superior Court that disrupted some court functions, the Court continued the hearing to August 20, 2024.  The Clerk gave notice.

On August 20, 2024, there was again no appearance by Plaintiff.  The Court placed the motion off calendar.

On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second motion to set aside the dismissal.  The hearing was set for September 20.

On September 20, 2024, there was again no appearance by Plaintiff.  The Court placed the motion off calendar.

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed this third motion to set aside the dismissal.  The hearing is set for October 17.  On September 26, Plaintiff submitted a supplemental declaration in support of the motion.    

No opposition has been filed.

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief from dismissal.

As to discretionary relief, the statute states: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  (Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subd. (b).) Where such an application for discretionary relief is made, the motion must be accompanied by a copy of the answer or pleading proposed to be filed; “otherwise the application shall not be granted.”  (Ibid.)  The application for relief must be made within a reasonable time, and in no case exceeding six months after the judgment. (Ibid.)

The statute also provides for mandatory relief from dismissal, default, or default judgment:

“whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect … unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” 

(Ibid.)

A request for discretionary relief under section 473, subdivision (b), must be made (subject to certain exceptions) “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months.”  (Ibid.)  A request for mandatory relief must be made within six months.  (Ibid.)

Discussion

Plaintiff moves to set aside the dismissal under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).  The dismissal order was entered on February 13, 2024.  This motion was filed on September 25, 2024.

Plaintiff has submitted two supporting declarations from counsel.  The first, from Dean Hakkak, states that the failure to appear at the OSC hearing was the result of counsel’s “inadvertent mistake.”  (Hakkak Decl., ¶ 6.)  This declaration says nothing about the failures to appear on August 20 and September 20.  The second, from Neema Shojal, states that the failure to appear on September 20 was the result of a family medical emergency.  (Shojal Decl., ¶ 3.)  This declaration says nothing about the failure to appear on August 20.

This third motion to set aside the dismissal was filed more than six months after the dismissal order was entered.  So was the second motion.  Accordingly, the Court must deny the motion for statutory relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).  The Court has no power or discretion to extend the statutory deadline. 

Conclusion

The Court DENIES the motion of Plaintiff Marta Velasco to set aside the dismissal under the statutory authority of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).

Moving party is ordered to give any required notice.

Case Number: 22STCV34488    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 29

Austin v. Lyft
22STCV34488
Application to be Admitted Pro Hac Vice filed by Counsel Randall J. Pattee

 

Tentative

 

The application is granted.

 

Background

 

On October 27, 2022, Eric Jason Austin (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Lyft, Inc. (“Lyft”), Amadu Tejan Bah (“Bah”), and Does 1 to 100, asserting causes of action for general negligence; negligent hiring, training, retention, and supervision; and motor vehicle negligence arising out of an accident that occurred on October 14, 2021, at or near the intersection of Vermont Avenue and 43rd Street in Los Angeles. 

 

Lyft and Bah filed their answers on January 27 and May 1, 2023.

 

On September 11, 2024, Counsel Randall J. Pattee filed the instant Application to be Admitted Pro Hac Vice on behalf of Defendant Lyft (“Application”).  On October 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel of Record, Ryan T. Moore Pattee filed an additional declaration in support of the Application.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

California Rules of Court, rule 9.40 provides that an attorney in good standing in another jurisdiction may apply to appear as counsel pro hac vice in the State of California by filing a verified application together with proof of service by mail of a copy of the application and notice of hearing on all parties who have appeared in the case and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office, with payment of a $50.00 fee, so long as that attorney is not a resident of the State of California, and is not regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40.)

 

“The application must state: (1) the applicant’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission; (3) that the applicant is a member of good standing in those courts; (4) that the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5) the title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and (6) the name, address, and telephone number of the active member of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record in the local action.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(d).)

 

Discussion

 

“A person desiring to appear as counsel pro hac vice in a superior court must file with the court a verified application together with proof of service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1013a of a copy of the application and of the notice of hearing of the application on all parties who have appeared in the cause and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office. The notice of hearing must be given at the time prescribed in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 unless the court has prescribed a shorter period.” (California Rules of Court, rule 9.40(c)(1).)

All substantive and procedural requirements for the application have been satisfied.  The application is granted.

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS Counsel Randall J. Pattee’s Application for Admission as Counsel Pro Hac Vice.

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV08463    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 29

Lawrence v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority
23STCV08463
Motion to be Relieved as Counsel, filed by Plaintiff’s Counsel Brian Jacobson, Esq. of Wilshire Law Firm

Tentative

The motion is denied without prejudice on procedural grounds.

Background

On April 17, 2023, Charlette Ann Lawrence (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 50 for negligence arising out of bus accident on August 22, 2022.

 

On August 14, 2024, Defendant filed an answer and a cross-complaint against Tanesha Anderson and Roes 1 through 100.

 

On September 25, 2024, Brian Jacobson, Esq. of Wilshire Law Firm (“Counsel”) filed this motion to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff. No opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. (b).) An attorney is permitted to withdraw where conflicts between the attorney and client make it unreasonable to continue the representation. (See Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct 3-700(C)(1).) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.)   

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a)), MC-052 (Declaration) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.136(c)), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e)).   

 

Further, the requisite forms must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).) The court may delay effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).) 

 

Discussion

 

Counsel seeks to be relieved as Plaintiff’s attorney of record. There are numerous issues with the filing and service, however. 

First, the proof of service is not signed.

Second, service on the client is untimely.  Because of an intervening court holiday, sixteen court days before the hearing date of October 17 was September 24.  The motion was served by mail, which adds five calendar days to the service deadline, meaning that the last day for service by mail was September 19, 2024.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)  According to the proof, the client was served by mail on September 20.

Third, Counsel failed to serve Defendant with the motion. That is required.

Fourth, the motion was not timely filed.  It was filed on September 25, but sixteen court days before the hearing date was September 24.

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED without prejudice.

Conclusion

 

The motion to be relieved as counsel is DENIED without prejudice. 

 

Moving counsel to give notice.
