DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01454    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

James Hansen,

        

         Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Allen Eggers,

 

         Defendant.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

     22VECV01454         107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING: Demurrer

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

Defendant Allen Eggers demurs to the complaint brought by Plaintiff James Hansen. This court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff James Hansen (“Plaintiff”) and Defendant Allen Eggers (“Defendant”) were co-owners of a corporation, WJA Management, Inc. (“WJAM”), for which Defendant acted as the chief financial officer (“CFO”). (Complaint pp. 1–2.) WJAM “was operated for the purpose of managing income and benefits from operations of Western Jet Aviation,” a company that provided aircraft services. (Id. at p. 2.) In December 2021, Defendant dissolved WJAM without Plaintiff’s knowledge and retained “exclusive control over the assets and property of WJAM, and over the records and business affairs of WJAM.” (Id. at p. 3.)

 

On September 30, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) Corporations Code §§ 2004, 2005, and 2006; (2) accounting; (3) breach of fiduciary duty; (4) conversion; (5) unjust enrichment; and (6) constructive fraud.

 

On March 12, 2024, Defendant and WJAM filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Western Jet Aviation, and Patricia Hansen.

 

On March 18, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the Complaint. On April 16, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the demurrer and on April 19, 2024, Defendant replied to Plaintiff’s opposition.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement¿ 

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41(a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.

 

Defendant’s counsel submits with the Demurrer a declaration that prior to bringing the Demurrer, he met and conferred with counsel for Plaintiff. (Rees Decl. ¶ 2.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

¿ 

  1. Timeliness of Filings¿ 

¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint.

¿¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).) 

 

Service of the Complaint was effected by certified mail on July 24, 2023. (Notice of Withdrawal of Motion to Quash p. 2.) Thus, any demurrer should have been filed by 30 days of July 24, 2023, or by August 23, 2023. Sixteen court days before April 30, 2024 was April 8, 2024; nine court days before April 30, 2024 was April 17, 2024; and five court days before April 30, 2024 was April 23, 2024.

 

Thus, the Demurrer is untimely as against service of the Complaint, and the Demurrer, the opposition thereto, and the reply to the opposition were all timely as against the hearing. Because neither party raises the issue of timeliness, however, and both parties were able to fully brief their positions on the Demurrer, this court declines to strike the untimely Demurrer.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

“Shareholders may bring two types of actions, a direct action filed by the shareholder individually (or on behalf of a class of shareholders to which he or she belongs) for injury to his or her interest as a shareholder, or a derivative action filed on behalf of the corporation for injury to the corporation for which it has failed or refused to sue.” (Schuster v. Gardner (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 305, 311–12, emphasis omitted.) “[A] derivative suit is one in which the shareholder seeks redress of the wrong to the corporation.” (Bader v. Anderson (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 775, 793.) “Thus, an action is derivative, i.e., in the corporate right, if the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock and property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.” (Ibid., quoting Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93, 106.) “A single cause of action by a shareholder can give rise to derivative claims, individual claims, or both.” (Goles v. Sawhney (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 1014, 1018, fn. 3.)

 

Generally, a shareholder wishing to bring a derivative claim must first make a pre-suit demand on the corporation’s board of directors that the corporation act on its own behalf. (Bader v. Anderson, supra, 179 Cal.App.4th 775 at 789.) “In deference to the managerial role of directors and in order to curb potential abuse,  the shareholder asserting a derivative claim must make a threshold showing that he or she made [such] a presuit demand on the board to take the desired action.” (Apple Inc. v. Superior Court (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 222, 232.) This demand requirement is codified in California Corporations Code section 800, subdivision (b)(2) (“Section 800”). (See Corp. Code, § 800, subd. (b)(2).) “Under [Section 800], a plaintiff must plead with particularity the attempts that were made to secure board action before bringing suit, or, alternatively, the factual basis upon which the plaintiff believes that a demand on the board was unnecessary, i.e., that a demand would have been futile.” (Apple Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, at 232.)

 

“[T]he particular stockholder who brings the suit is merely a nominal party plaintiff. It is the corporation that is the ultimate beneficiary of such a derivative suit. Thus, the corporation is the real party plaintiff in the action.” (Patrick v. Alacer Corp. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 995, 1003.) Though the corporation is essentially the plaintiff in a derivative action, “[w]hen a derivative suit is brought to litigate the rights of the corporation, the corporation is an indispensable party and must be joined as a nominal defendant.” (Grosset v. Wenaas (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1100, 1108.) “The corporation must be joined because its rights, not those of the nominal plaintiff, are to be litigated, and to offer the real defendants res judicata protection from later suits.” (Patrick v. Alacer Corp., supra, at 1004, internal citations omitted.) “Naming the corporation a defendant, not a plaintiff, follows from the joinder rules: ‘If the consent of any one who should have been joined as plaintiff cannot be obtained, he may be made a defendant.’” (Ibid., quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 382.)

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

  1. Derivative Claims

 

Plaintiff brings all causes of action on behalf of himself as an individual and derivatively on behalf of WJAM. (Complaint p. 1.)

 

Defendant demurs to each cause of action in the Complaint under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) on the grounds that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against him and under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (d) on the grounds that they each fail to name WJAM, which Defendant contends is an indispensable party. (Notice of Demurrer pp. 3–6.) Defendant contends that all of Plaintiff’s claims are derivative claims of WJAM; that Plaintiff “lacks standing to pursue derivative claims” because the Complaint “fails to allege the essential facts required of a derivative suit pursuant to Corporations Code § 800[, subdivision] (b)”; and that, as “the corporation on whose behalf a derivative suit is brought,” WJAM “is an indispensable party to the lawsuit.” (Demurrer pp. 6–7.) Defendant does not demur to any of the causes of action to the extent they are brought directly by Plaintiff on his own behalf.

 

The Complaint does not comply with Section 800 because it pleads with particularity neither Plaintiff’s attempts to make a pre-suit demand on WJAM’s board nor “the factual basis upon which the plaintiff believes that a demand on the board was unnecessary.” (Apple Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, 18 Cal.App.5th 222 at 232.) Although Plaintiff argues that “demand is not a prerequisite to a derivative action under the circumstances of this case,” even if he is correct that demand would have been excused as futile, Section 800 nonetheless requires that he plead “with particularity plaintiff's efforts to secure from the board such action as plaintiff desires, or the reasons for not making such effort.” If demand would have been futile, he must plead as much in the Complaint. (Corp. Code, § 800, subd. (b)(2); Opposition p. 6.)

 

Additionally, to the extent that Plaintiff brings each cause of action derivatively on behalf of WJAM, then WJAM “is an indispensable party and must be joined as a nominal defendant.” By failing to join WJAM as a defendant, Plaintiff has thus failed to join a necessary party.

 

  1. Leave to Amend

 

“Where the defect raised by a¿motion¿to¿strike¿or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave¿to¿amend¿is routinely and¿liberally¿granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) “[An order granting a motion to strike] is analogous to an order sustaining a demurrer.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303.) 

 

We note that this is the first time a demurrer has been sustained in this case and that Plaintiff has not yet amended the Complaint for any reason. We believe the defects raised by the Demurrer are reasonably capable of cure.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer with leave to amend.

Defendant Harmick Taroian, DDS. moves for summary judgment as to the complaint filed by plaintiff Kristina Reveles. This court GRANTS the motion for summary judgment.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On November 1, 2019, plaintiff Kristina Reveles (“Plaintiff”) consulted with defendant Harmick Taroian, DDS (“Defendant”) in his capacity as a dentist. (Taroian Decl. ¶ 3.) On May 7, 2020, while Defendant was performing dental services on Plaintiff, a dental tool broke and a piece of it became lodged in one of her teeth where it remained at least until the date of the filing of the Complaint (as defined below). (Id. at ¶ 8; Complaint p. 3.) Plaintiff alleges that she did not give informed consent to the dental services performed by Defendant and that Defendant performed such services negligently. (Complaint pp. 3–4.) Plaintiff notified Defendant of her intention to sue before the filing of the Complaint (as defined below). (Id. at p. 5.)

 

On February 27, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging a single cause of action for dental malpractice.

 

On November 15, 2023, Defendant filed the instant motion for summary judgment (the “MSJ”). On January 26, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition to the MSJ, to which the Plaintiff responded five days later. On February 2, 2024, this court continued the hearing on the MSJ.

 

On April 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) to the MSJ, and on April 25, 2024, Defendant filed a reply (the “Reply”) to the Opposition.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Separate Statement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(1) requires that a motion for summary judgment “be supported by affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken. The supporting papers shall include a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (b)(1).) Defendant includes with the MSJ a separate statement of undisputed material facts that contains a list of facts that Defendant contends are undisputed, each followed by a reference to submitted supporting evidence. This separate statement therefore complies with California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(1).

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(3) requires that opposition papers to a motion for summary judgment “include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court's discretion, for granting the motion.” (§ 437c, subd. (b)(3).) Plaintiff submits with the Opposition a document titled separate statement, which contains a mixture of alleged facts and arguments. These facts and arguments do not, however, necessarily respond to the allegedly undisputed facts in the separate statement submitted by Defendant, and none of the alleged facts in Plaintiff’s separate statement refers to submitted supporting evidence. Plaintiff’s separate statement therefore does not comply with California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(3). However, while we do disregard arguments raised in the separate statement as well as alleged facts that are unsupported by submitted evidence, we decline to grant the MSJ on these grounds.

 

  1. Timing

 

A motion for summary judgment “may be made at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c, subd. (a).) “Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing” with additional days depending on how the notice is served. (Ibid.) “The motion shall be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.” (Ibid.) “An opposition to the motion shall be served and filed not less than 14 days preceding the noticed or continued date of hearing, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise” and “[a] reply to the opposition shall be served and filed by the moving party not less than five days preceding the noticed or continued date of hearing, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise. (Id. at subd. (b).) Supporting documents to filings should be filed by the same deadlines. (See, e.g., id. at subd. (b)(2)–(b)(3).)

 

“A trial court has broad¿discretion¿under¿rule 3.1300(d) of the California Rules of Court¿to refuse to consider papers served and filed beyond the deadline without a prior court order finding good cause for late submission.” (Bozzi v. Nordstrom, Inc. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 755, 765.) Similarly, courts have wide latitude to grant continuances. 

 

Here, Plaintiff appeared in this lawsuit by filing the Complaint more than 60 days prior to the filing of the MSJ. Notice of the MSJ was received by Plaintiff on November 18, 2023, which is more than 75 days before the MSJ is set for hearing. Fourteen days before the hearing was April 16, 2024. Five days before the hearing was April 25, 2024. Thus, the MSJ was timely, the Opposition was timely, and the Reply was timely. However, Plaintiff’s evidence in support of the Opposition was not filed until April 25, 2024, the same date on which the Reply was due. And, we note that in the Reply, Defendant states that he had by that time not received Plaintiff’s supporting evidence. (Reply p. 7.) We nonetheless decline to refuse to consider Plaintiff’s submitted supporting evidence.

 

III. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Defendant objects to the document filed by Plaintiff labelled “Declaration and Exhibits in support of opposition of Plaintiff Kristina Reveles to defendant Harmick [Taroian] motion for summary judgement” “to the extent it is an intent to submit a declaration on behalf of [P]laintiff” on the grounds that the matters therein are not declared under oath. (Objection to Declaration and Exhibits No. 1.) It is improper for a court to consider in support of a summary judgment motion a declaration that does not declare under penalty of perjury that the facts set forth therein are true. (Baron v. Mare (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 304, 308.) This court hereby sustains this objection and overrules the rest of Defendant’s objections to this document as moot.

 

Defendant additionally objects to Plaintiff’s separate statement of undisputed material facts on the grounds that it “fails to substantially comply with Cal[ifornia] Rules of Court, Rule 3.1350(f), (g).” For the reasons explained above in our discussion on the separate statement, we overrule this objection.

 

IV. LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (a)(1).) The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure § 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “A triable issue of material fact exists where ‘the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.’” (California Medical Assn. v. Aetna Health of California Inc. (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 660, 665, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra at 850.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A cause of action “cannot be established” if the undisputed facts presented by defendant prove the contrary of plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law. (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) 

 

“ ‘Statute of limitations’ is the collective term applied to acts or parts of acts that prescribe the periods beyond which a plaintiff may not bring a cause of action.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 806.) “Generally, statute of limitations issues raise questions of fact that must be tried, however, when the uncontradicted facts are susceptible of only one legitimate inference, summary judgment is proper.” (Kline v. Turner (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1369, 1374.) California Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5 (“Section 340.5”) provides that “[i]n an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person's alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 340.5.) Section 340.5 applies to dental malpractice claims. (Kitzig v. Nordquist (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 1384, 1391.) “[A] medical act performed without a patient's informed consent… is medical negligence.” (Massey v. Mercy Medical Center Redding (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 690, 698.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 364, subdivision (a) provides that “[n]o action based upon the health care provider's professional negligence may be commenced unless the defendant has been given at least 90 days' prior notice of the intention to commence the action.” California Code of Civil Procedure section 364, subdivision (d) tolls the statute of limitations for a period of up to 90 days “[i]f the notice is served within 90 days of the expiration of the applicable statute of limitations.”

 

“Generally speaking, a cause of action accrues at the time when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements. An important exception to the general rule of accrual is the ‘discovery rule,’ which postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 906–07, internal citations omitted.) “Under the discovery rule, the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff suspects or should suspect that her injury was caused by wrongdoing, that someone has done something wrong to her.” (Jolly v. Elli Lilly & Co. (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1103, 1110.) “A plaintiff need not be aware of the specific ‘facts’ necessary to establish the claim; that is a process contemplated by pretrial discovery. Once the plaintiff has a suspicion of wrongdoing, and therefore an incentive to sue, she must decide whether to file suit or sit on her rights. So long as a suspicion exists, it is clear that the plaintiff must go find the facts; she cannot wait for the facts to find her.” (Id. at 1111.)

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff’s sole cause of action is for dental malpractice based on her allegations that Defendant performed dental procedures on her without her informed consent and her allegations that Defendant was professionally negligent. Thus, Section 340.5 applies to her all of her claims. The applicable statute of limitations period for her claims is one year after Plaintiff either discovered or should have discovered her injury through reasonable diligence, or three years, whichever is sooner.

 

Defendant submits evidence that Plaintiff identifies the broken-off piece of dental tool buried in her tooth as the injury on which she bases her claims of negligence; that on November 19, 2021, Plaintiff, upon admission through an emergency room for facial swelling and dental pain, advised medical staff that she has a broken off piece of a dental tool buried in her tooth; and that Plaintiff served her notice of intent to sue on November 25, 2022. Thus, Defendant submits evidence that the injury Plaintiff upon which Plaintiff bases her claim of negligence is the broken-off piece of a dental tool that became lodged in one of her teeth during the dental procedure that Defendant performed on Plaintiff on May 7, 2020. Defendant additionally submits evidence that Plaintiff was aware of her injury and believed such injury to be the source of her harm no later than November 19, 2021, more than a year prior to the filing of the Complaint on February 27, 2023. Defendant additionally submits evidence that Plaintiff served a notice of intent to sue pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 364, subdivision (a) on November 25, 2022. As this date is more than a year after November 19, 2021 and not within the 90 days preceding the expiration of the statute of limitations, it would not have tolled the statute of limitations in this case. Even if it did, 90 days after November 25, 2022, the date on which Plaintiff served the notice of intent to sue, was February 23, 2023, which is still earlier than February 27, 2023, the date on which Plaintiff filed the Complaint. Thus, Defendant submits evidence to show that the Complaint was after the statute of limitations applicable to Plaintiff’s claims had expired. Defendant thus shifts the burden to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists.

 

None of Plaintiff’s evidence controverts these pieces of evidence brought by Defendant. None of Plaintiff’s evidence contradicts or challenges Defendant’s evidence that Plaintiff was aware of her injury and believed it to be the source of her harm by November 19, 2021. While Plaintiff raises evidence that she was later informed of her injury within one year of the filing of the Complaint, this does not challenge Plaintiff’s evidence that she was also informed of her injury and admitted awareness of her injury at the earlier date more than one year before the filing of the Complaint.

 

Plaintiff argues in the Opposition that her “action based on negligence is not barred because the action was filed within 1 year of the manifestation of undisclosed complication. The facts of the case and the points of law stated during the tolling of the statute of limitations was unaware of the foreign body and the relationship to her injuries [citation] the role foreign body played in the patients suffering negligent cause of injury.” (Opposition p. 2) Plaintiff cites Ashworth v. Memorial Hospital (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1046 (hereinafter, Ashworth) in support of her arguments. (Ibid.) Ashworth discusses the “foreign body” exception to the statute of limitations for medical negligence prescribed by Section 340.5, which it explains “only tolls the three-year outside limitation period and not the one-year ‘discovery’ period.” (Ashworth, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d at 1055–58.) “What this means, of course, is that the ‘foreign body’ exception in [S]ection 340.5 lifts the three-year outside limit entirely if a nontherapeutic ‘foreign body’ has been left inside a patient. It gives that patient an unlimited time to discover or to use reasonable diligence to discover the presence of the ‘foreign body.’ But once the patient discovers or through reasonable diligence would have discovered the ‘foreign body’ and its role in her injury she only has one year to file her lawsuit against the practitioners responsible for leaving the ‘foreign body’ inside her.” (Id. at 1058.) Therefore, because Defendant does not contend that Plaintiff’s claims are barred by Section 340.5’s three-year “outside” statute of limitations but rather contends that Plaintiff’s claims are barred by its one-year “discovery” period, the “foreign body” exception to the statute of limitations for medical negligence prescribed by Section 340.5 is not applicable to this case and does not toll the statute of limitations period for Plaintiff’s claims.

 

The Opposition then argues the issue of informed consent and alleges that Defendant did not receive her informed consent prior to performing the dental procedure on her during which the dental tool piece become lodged in her tooth. However, firstly, this does not contradict or challenge Defendant’s evidence regarding the statute of limitations. That is to say, even if Plaintiff can argue and prove that Defendant acted negligently by failing to obtain her informed consent, her claims may still be barred by the statute of limitations. Plaintiff must first overcome Defendant’s challenge to her claims on statute of limitations grounds before her arguments about his negligence and failure to obtain her informed consent become relevant. Secondly, we are concerned at the summary judgment stage with evidence as well as argument. (See Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th 826 at 843.) To overcome a summary judgment challenge, a plaintiff must show that she possesses evidentiary support for any arguments raised so as to create a triable issue of material fact. (Ibid.) In other words, it is not sufficient at this stage to present argument unaccompanied by evidence to support those arguments; rather, the plaintiff must submit evidence that contradicts or challenges the evidence submitted by the defendant who brought the motion for summary judgment. (Ibid. [“The moving party must ‘support[ ]’ the ‘motion’ with evidence including ‘affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice’ must or may ‘be taken.’ ([Cal. Code Civ. Proc.] § 437c, subd. (b).) Likewise, any adverse party may oppose the motion, and, ‘where appropriate,’ must present evidence including ‘affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice’ must or may ‘be taken.’ (Ibid.) An adverse party who chooses to oppose the motion must be allowed a reasonable opportunity to do so.”].) Here, however, Plaintiff does not submit or refer to any evidence in support of her argument that Defendant acted without her informed consent, and we therefore do not consider arguments Plaintiff presents that are unsupported by submitted evidence.

 

Plaintiff states in her separate statement that she “was only made physically aware that the foreign body exist on 12/8/21 but did not become aware of the extent of injuries from delayed adverse reaction to the foreign body and the relation, the role of all elements, commenced.” (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement p. 2.) She also states that her injury “worsened over time from a delay adverse reaction to the dental instrument/file and nickel and titanium toxicity. I did not have a name for the symptoms I was experiencing until I saw Dr. J Wilson DDS Kaiser Maxillofacial dept on October 19, 2023.” (Id. at p. 4.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges that she became “physically aware” of the existence of the dental tool fragment in her tooth only on December 8, 2021 but that her condition worsened over time thereafter and that she only became aware of the full extent of her injury and its factual cause on October 19, 2023, almost two years later. As to the date of her “physical awareness,” Plaintiff submits no evidence in support of this contention that contradicts Defendant’s evidence that she told ER staff on November 19, 2021 that she had a dental tool fragment buried in her tooth that she believed to be the source of her injuries. As to her argument that she did not discover the factual cause or the full extent of her injuries until much later than the date she learned of the dental tool fragment being left buried in her tooth, neither of these are sufficient to toll the statute of limitations under the discovery rule. (See Jolly v. Elli Lilly & Co., supra, 44 Cal.3d 1103 at 1110–11.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff fails to meet her burden to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the statute of limitations.

 

Accordingly, this court grants the MSJ.
