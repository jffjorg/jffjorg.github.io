DEPARTMENT 14 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV05221    Hearing Date: August 7, 2024    Dept: 14

Ex Parte Meredith v Tissera

Case Background

This case arises from a dispute over the sale and attempted importation of 16 Japanese sports cars (more precisely, 16 Nissan Skyline GT-Rs). Plaintiff alleges that Defendants told him they were experts in vehicle importation and could deliver these specific vehicles to him with a minimum of fuss. Plaintiff further alleges that he paid nearly $4 million for the vehicles, only to discover that Defendants did not own them, could not own them, and had no way to deliver them as promised. Defendants in turn allege that they delivered at least one car, and Plaintiff refused to pay for it.

On February 15, 2019, Plaintiff Edwin Hammond Meredith (“Meredith” or “Plaintiff”) filed his Complaint for (1) Fraud; (2) Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; (3) Violation of Civil Code §§ 1790 et seq; (4) Fraud – Violation of Penal Code § 470(d); (5) Breach of Contract; (6) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (7) Negligence Per Se; and (8) Negligence against Defendants Franz Gerald Tissera (“Franz”); Phase9MotorSports, Inc. (“Phase 9”); and DOES 1-20. On March 13, 2019, Defendants Franz and Phase 9 filed their Answer.

On May 13, 2019 Plaintiff filed two “Amendments to Complaint,” substituting Defendants Angela J. Chisholm (“Chisholm”) and Frederick Tissera (“Frederick”) in lieu of DOES 1-2, respectively. On October 31, 2019, this court sustained the demurrer of Defendants Chisholm and Frederick, with leave to amend.

On November 21, 2019 Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) for (1) Fraud; (2) Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; (3) Violation of Civil Code §§ 1790 et seq; (4) Negligence Per Se; (5) Negligence; (6)-(21) Breach of Contract; and (22) Conversion against Defendants Franz, Chisholm, Frederick, Phase 9, and DOES 1-20. The 1st cause of action is asserted against Defendants Franz, Chisholm, and Frederick only; the 2nd, 3rd, and 6th-21st causes of action are asserted against Phase 9 only. On May 5, 2021, Defendants Franz, Chisholm, Frederick, and Phase 9 filed their joint First Amended Answer.

On November 18, 2019, Defendants Franz and Phase 9 Filed their Third Amended Cross-Complaint (“TAXC”) for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of the Implied Covenant; and (3) Quantum Meruit against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Meredith and ROES 1-20. On February 18, 2020, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Meredith filed his Answer.

On April 26, 2024, the first phase of jury trial concluded. The jury found in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendants.

The second phase of trial, a non-jury trial on alter ego liability, will proceed on September 17, 2024.

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a supplemental brief in support of his ex parte application for entry of judgment.

Instant Pleading

Plaintiff moves ex parte for entry of judgment after the first phase of trial.


Discussion

Plaintiff requests that the Court enter judgment based on the results of phase one of trial. Plaintiff alleges there is another proceeding pending in the Orange County Superior Court against the same Defendants involving similar fraudulent sales. Plaintiff argues that if judgment is not entered now, his judgment will lose priority.

The Court is concerned that entering judgment now will violate the one final judgment rule.

Generally, only one final judgment may be entered in an action unless an exception applies. (Leader v. Cords (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 1588, 1594.) A judgment is the final determination of the rights of the parties in an action or proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc., section 577.) A judgment is final when it leaves nothing to be decided between one or more parties and their adversaries, or that can be amended to encompass all controverted issues. (Morehart v. County of Santa Barbara (1994) 7 Cal.4th 725, 741.)

Here, phase two of trial concerns whether the individual Defendants are liable under an alter ego theory of liability. Because this second phase of trial will determine whether the individual Defendants are liable for additional damages as alter egos of Phase 9 Motor Sports, a determination of the individual defendants’ rights remains in this action. Any judgment entered now before the second phase of trial concludes would not be final. Therefore, the proposed judgment would violate the one final judgment rule.

Additionally, this is a bifurcated proceeding and the Court may not prepare a proposed judgment unless an interlocutory or separate judgment may properly be entered. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1591; Morehart at pp.737-738.)

Some interlocutory judgments may be appealable and are excepted from the one final judgment rule and Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1591. (Code Civ. Proc., section 904.1(8)-(14).) However, those exceptions do not apply here. Thus, the Court lacks the authority to issue an interlocutory judgment.

To enter a judgment now, an exception to the one final judgment rule must apply and there must be grounds to enter a separate judgment.

There are two exceptions to the one final judgment rule, when an interim judgment is a collateral final judgment, and when a judgment is final as to a party.

A collateral final order must be (1) a final determination, (2) of a collateral matter, and (3) direct the payment of money or performance of an act. (Sanchez v. Westlake Services, LLC (2022) 73 Cal.App.5th 1100, 1107.) This exception applies only where a trial court’s ruling on a collateral issue is substantially the same as a final judgment in an independent proceeding in that it leaves the court no further action to take on a matter which is severable from the general subject of litigation. (San Joaquin County Dept. of Child Support Services v. Winn (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 296, 300.)

Here, the proposed order would not be a final determination on a collateral matter because the issue of the individual Defendants’ liability would remain after judgment is entered. Defendants’ liability is not severable from this litigation.

As for the second exception, the one final judgment rule is not violated where an action involves multiple parties and a judgment is entered which leaves no issue to be determined as to one party. (Nguyen v. Calhoun (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 428, 437.)

“Ordinarily, a corporation is regarded as a legal entity separate and distinct from its stockholders, officers and directors. Under the alter ego doctrine, however, where a corporation is used by an individual or individuals, or by another corporation, to perpetrate fraud, circumvent a statute, or accomplish some other wrongful or inequitable purpose, a court may disregard the corporate entity and treat the corporation's acts as if they were done by the persons actually controlling the corporation.” (Communist Party v. 522 Valencia, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 980, 993.)¿¿ 

“Amending a judgment to add an alter ego does not add a new defendant but instead inserts the correct name of the real defendant.” (Misik v. D'Arco (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1065, 1072.) “A court may amend its judgment to properly designate the real defendants at any time…, including after judgment.” (Id. at p.1075.)

Here, the first phase of trial resolved all issues with respect to Defendant Phase 9 Motor Sports. The second phase of trial on alter ego issues would not affect the outcome of trial against Phase 9 Motor Sports. Any amendment to a judgment against Phase 9 Motor Sports after the second phase of trial would at most correct the name of Phase 9 Motor Sports to include the individual defendants. However, there are no issues remaining to be determined as to Phase 9 Motor Sports. Therefore, a judgment against Phase 9 Motor Sports would not violate the one final judgment rule.

Supplemental Brief

In his supplemental brief, Plaintiff argues that the proposed judgment does not violate the one final judgment rule because adding an alter ego merely inserts the name of the real defendant and does not create a new obligation. Plaintiff cites Greenspan v. LADT, LLC, 191 Cal.App.4th 486, 508 (Cal. Ct. App. 2010) (sic), Misik, and other cases which support the general rule that amending a judgment to add an alter ego does not add a new defendant but instead inserts the correct name of the real defendant. The cases also support the rule that adding a judgment debtor as an alter ego is permissible even after judgment. However, none of these cases state that a judgment may be entered while alter ego liability issues are pending without violating the one final judgment rule.

The issue is not whether the proposed judgment may be amended, but whether the individual defendants’ rights remain at issue. Plaintiff essentially alleges the individual defendants are liable for damages incurred Phase 9 Motor Sports’ because they are alter egos. This is a controverted issue concerning the individual defendants’ rights because the individual defendants are either liable for Phase 9 Sports’ damages or they are not. It does not matter whether the judgment may be amended to include the individual defendants. Therefore, a judgment against the individual defendants would violate the one final judgment rule.

Conclusion

Plaintiff’s application is GRANTED in part. The Court will enter judgment as to Defendant Phase 9 Motor Sports only. Plaintiff is to submit a new proposed judgment.
