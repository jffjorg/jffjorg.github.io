ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On November 30, 2022, this Court entered as Judgment sister-state Judgment from the Agua Fria Justice Court, Maricopa County, Arizona, in favor of Western American Loan, Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “Creditor”) and against Albert Padilla and Doe Spouse Padilla, a marital community (“Defendants” or “Debtors”).

 

Creditor filed a Writ of Execution and Memorandum of Costs After Judgment, Acknowledgment of Credit, and Declaration of Accrued Interest on August 2, 2023.

 

On July 15, 2024, Creditor filed the instant Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs, requesting “an award of at least $6,825.31 in Attorneys’ Fees and Costs incurred post-judgment.”  (Mot. p. 1.)

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Request for Judicial Notice

 

Judgment Creditor requests that the Court take judicial notice of:

 

(1)   Exhibit 1 –Judgment entered in the Agua Fria Justice Court, County of Maricopa, State of Arizona, entitled Western American Loan, Inc. v. Albert Padilla and Doe Spouse Padilla, Case No. CC2020-090838RC.

 

(2)   Exhibit 2 - Notice of Entry of Judgment on Sister-State Judgment entered in the instant action.

 

(RJN Exs. 1-2.)

III.              Legal Standard

 

A prevailing party is entitled to recover costs, including attorney’s fees, as a matter of right.  (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1032, subd. (a)(4), 1032, subd. (b), 1033.5.)  Attorney’s fees are allowable as costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)

 

“The judgment creditor is entitled to the reasonable and necessary costs of enforcing a judgment.  Attorney’s fees incurred in enforcing a judgment are not included in costs collectible under this title unless otherwise provided by law.  Attorney’s fees incurred in enforcing a judgment are included as costs collectible under this title if the underlying judgment includes an award of attorney’s fees to the judgment creditor pursuant to subparagraph (A) of paragraph (10) of subdivision (a) of Section 1033.5.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.040.)

 

“The judgment creditor may claim costs authorized by Section 685.040 by noticed motion.  The motion shall be made before the judgment is satisfied in full, but not later than two years after the costs have been incurred.  The costs claimed under this section may include, but are not limited to, costs that may be claimed under Section 685.070 and costs incurred but not approved by the court or referee in a proceeding under Chapter 6 (commencing with Section 708.010) of Division 2.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080, subd. (a).)

 

“The notice of motion shall describe the costs claimed, shall state their amount, and shall be supported by an affidavit of a person who has knowledge of the facts stating that to the person’s best knowledge and belief the costs are correct, are reasonable and necessary, and have not been satisfied.  The notice of motion shall be served on the judgment debtor.  Service shall be made personally or by mail.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080, subd. (b).)

 

The calculation of attorney’s fees in California begins with the “lodestar” method – multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award.  The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)  Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary.  (Ibid. at p. 48, fn. 23.)  After the trial court has performed the lodestar calculations, it shall consider whether the total award so calculated under all of the circumstances of the case is more than a reasonable amount and, if so, shall reduce the section 1717 award so that it is a reasonable figure.  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096.)

 

As explained in Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc.:

 

“[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. [Citation.]  The purpose of such adjustment is to fix a fee at the fair market value for the particular action. In effect, the court determines, retrospectively, whether the litigation involved a contingent risk or required extraordinary legal skill justifying augmentation of the unadorned lodestar in order to approximate the fair market rate for such services. . . . This approach anchors the trial court's analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney's services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary.” [Internal citations and internal quotation marks omitted.]

 

((2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154.)  “It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.  [Citations.]  The value of legal services performed in a case is a matter in which the trial court has its own expertise. . . . The trial court makes its determination after consideration of a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.  [Citations.]”  (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623-624.)

 

No specific findings reflecting the court’s calculations are required.  The record need only show that the attorney fees were awarded according to the “lodestar” or “touchstone” approach.  The court’s focus in evaluating the facts should be to provide a fee award reasonably designed to completely compensate attorneys for the services provided.  The starting point for this determination is the attorney’s time records.  (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of Calif. State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 395-397 [verified time records entitled to credence absent clear indication they are erroneous].)  However, California case law permits fee awards in the absence of detailed time sheets. (Sommers v. Erb (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1644, 1651; Dunk v. Ford Motor Co. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1794, 1810; Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 103.)  An experienced trial judge is in a position to assess the value of the professional services rendered in his or her court.  (Ibid.; Serrano, 20 Cal.3d 25 at 49.)

A.     Proof of Service

 

Plaintiff/Creditor has filed Proof of Service indicating that Defendants/Debtors were served with the moving papers by mail on July 15, 2024.  (7-15-24 Proof of Service.)  The Court finds that service was proper.

 

B.     Attorney’s Fees

 

Plaintiff/Creditor seeks “an award of at least $6,825.31 in Attorneys’ Fees and Costs incurred post-judgment.”  (Mot. p. 1.)  Plaintiff/Creditor argues that as part of the Arizona judgment, Plaintiff is entitled to “all costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred by Plaintiff after submission of this judgment for entry by the Court in collecting the amounts listed in this Judgment."  (Id. at pp. 3-4; RJN, Ex. 1.)

 

The Court finds that Creditor is entitled to attorney’s fees pursuant to the sister-state Judgment, which awarded attorney’s fees and costs, and Code of Civil Procedure section 685.040.

 

Creditor supports its request with a declaration of its counsel.  (See Baillio Decl.)  Creditor states that its Motion is timely because the Judgment has not been satisfied.  (Baillio Decl. ¶ 4.)  Furthermore, Creditor is requesting fees and costs incurred between July 18, 2022, and July 2, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 13.)

 

Creditor argues that the attorney’s fees and costs requested are “reasonable and necessary” since the attorneys in this case provided extensive legal services to pursue collection of the Judgment.  (Mot. pp. 5-6.)  Creditor’s attorneys had to “(i) domesticate the Arizona Judgment to California; (ii) attempt [to] serve notice of entry of judgment, (iii) draft an application for service by publication and publish notice in a legal newspaper; (iv) obtain a writ of execution and a wage garnishment, (v) search for assets owned by Debtors to use to satisfy judgment; (vii) draft memorandum of costs after judgment; and (viii) draft this Motion.”  (Baillio Decl. ¶ 5.)

 

Counsel Baillio served as counsel on this matter and his billing rate was $325.00 per hour before January 1, 2023, and $375.00 per hour, after.  (Baillio Decl. ¶ 6.)  These rates are commensurate with those of other attorneys with similar levels of experience and specialization in Maricopa County.  (Id. at ¶ 8.)  Between July 18, 2022, and July 2, 2024, Counsel Baillio spent 7.4 hours in collection efforts and charged four flat fees of $450 for obtaining writs of execution and earnings withholding orders.  (Id. at ¶ 7.)  The firm’s legal support team was also critical to the case and spent a total of 2 hours to support the collection efforts, at a billing rate of $150 per hour, and 1 hour at a billing rate of $175 per hour.  (Id. at ¶¶ 9-11.)  Moreover, Counsel Baillio spent 1.5 hours, at a rate of $375.00 per hour, drafting the instant Motion and accompanying papers and anticipates spending another 0.4 hours to attend the hearing on the Motion, plus $61.65 in motion filing fees.  (Id. at ¶ 12.)  In total, Creditor requests $4,862.50 in attorney’s fees, expended between July 18, 2022, and July 2, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 13.)  Counsel Baillio has attached an itemized billing statement demonstrating the legal work performed for Creditor in pursuit of collecting the Judgment.  (Id. at ¶ 14, Ex. A.)

 

The Court reviews counsel’s billing statement for tasks billed between July 18, 2022, and July 2, 2024, in pursuit of enforcing the Judgment.  The Court considers factors such as counsel’s expertise, number of hours expended on each task, and whether each task was expended in pursuit of the Judgment.  The Court finds that Creditor’s request for $4,862.50 is slightly excessive.  For example, the instant Motion, regularly drafted by Counsel, does not warrant 1.5 hours of attorney time at a billing rate of $375.00.

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Court finds a reduced amount of $4,000.00 in attorney’s fees to be reasonable.

Service shall be made personally or by mail. The memorandum of costs shall be executed under oath by a person who has knowledge of the facts and shall state that to the person's best knowledge and belief the costs are correct, are reasonable and necessary, and have not been satisfied.”

The notice of motion shall be served on the judgment debtor. Service shall be made personally or by mail.”

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff Kriss Halpern (Plaintiff) filed the operative first amended complaint (FAC) against Defendants Ikonic Auto Garage (Ikonic) and Omar Bin Mahfouz (Mahfouz; collectively Defendants). Plaintiff alleges five causes of action for res ipsa loquitor, conversion, fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and breach of fiduciary duty.

 

Plaintiff alleges Ikonic is an auto repair show owned and managed by Mahfouz. In January 2022, plaintiff’s BMW (subject vehicle) broke down and he had it towed to Ikonic due to a preexisting relationship. At the time, the subject vehicle had a sunroof made of transparent material. The sunroof was stuck and would not close entirely, e.g. when closed, it was slightly raised. At the time plaintiff presented the subject vehicle to defendants the sunroof was not the issue; the issue was repair what had caused the breakdown. (FAC¶¶7-14)

 

Plaintiff further alleges when the vehicle arrived, Mahfouz advised him that they would take possession and advise plaintiff of what work the subject vehicle needed with respect to the breakdown, and then contact him before doing any work. Prior to leaving, Plaintiff mentioned the issue with the sunroof. Defendants responded they would look at the vehicle and let him know the cost to repair it. At some point Plaintiff contacted Defendants to inquire about the repairs. They told him they found something broken in the roof and a glass piece was missing. Plaintiff advised Defendants he would look at it when the repairs were concluded.  (FAC¶¶7-22)

 

Plaintiff alleges, Defendants did not contact plaintiff until after they had completed their repairs. When plaintiff arrived at Ikonic, Mahfouz advised him the subject vehicle was running well and explained the repairs. Plaintiff paid and waited outside for delivery of the subject vehicle. When the subject vehicle arrived, the sunroof was missing. When plaintiff confronted Mahfouz about the missing sunroof Mahfouz stated that was how the subject vehicle arrived. Plaintiff alleges Mahfouz refused to explain what happened to the sunroof of the subject vehicle. (FAC¶¶22-36)

 

Defendants now demur to all causes of action. Defendants also move to strike the fraud claim entirely, the punitive damages allegations (FAC, ¶¶66, 67, 81, 82, 89, 90, 91, 92, 108), and the prayer for punitive damages.

 

II.                 Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request for judicial notice of the original complaint is GRANTED.  Evid. Code §453.  

 

III.              Demurrer Legal Standard

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (See Schultz v. Harney (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1622, as modified on denial of reh’g (Sept. 29, 1994).) The court “take[s] as true all properly pleaded material facts, but not conclusions of fact or law asserted in the complaint.” (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 916.)

 

At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “The allegations of the pleading demurred to must be regarded as true.” (South Shore Land Co. v. Peterson (1964) 266 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)

 

IV.              Demurrer Discussion

 

A.    Res Ipsa Loquitor

 

“The elements for negligence cause of action are duty, breach, causation and damages.” (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)¿ The presumption of res ipsa loquitor arises when the evidence satisfies three conditions: “(1) the accident must be of a kind which ordinarily does not occur in the absence of someone's negligence; (2) it must be caused by an agency or instrumentality within the exclusive control of the defendant; (3) it must not have been due to any voluntary action or contribution on the part of the plaintiff.” (Vebr v. Culp (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1044, 1058 [internal citations omitted].)

 

Here, plaintiff has alleged a viable negligence claim and has included facts regarding a presumption of res ipsa loquitor. Plaintiff alleges he brought the subject vehicle to Ikonic to fix the breakdown it experienced on the road. At that time, it had a sunroof, albeit one which did not operate fully. Defendants owed a duty to exercise due care for the vehicle while it was in their care. By virtue of the disappearance of the sunroof, plaintiff alleges defendants breached their duty of care. Finally, due to the destruction of the sunroof and the cost to repair it, plaintiff has been damaged. (FAC¶¶45-58)

 

Defendants’ argument that plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged the duty of care is unpersuasive. Plaintiff has alleged a general duty of care a bailee has to exercise over property in their possession. While the allegations do not explicitly use the word “bailee”, there are sufficient foundational facts supporting the theory. Defendant has not identified an authority requiring a heightened pleading standard for a negligence claim. Defendant also argues there are no allegations of causation. Plaintiff has alleged grounds for the presumption of res ipsa loquitor and, therefore, causation. He alleges that the disappearance of the sunroof does not normally occur absent misconduct (FAC, ¶51), that defendants had the subject vehicle when the disappearance occurred, and plaintiff did not have possession when the disappearance occurred. This is sufficient.

 

The demurrer as to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.   

 

B.     Conversion

 

Conversion is “the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225 [internal quotations omitted].) To establish a claim for conversion, a plaintiff must plead: “(1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Ibid. [internal quotations omitted].) “[C]onversion is a strict liability tort. It does not require bad faith, knowledge, or even negligence; it requires only that the defendant have intentionally done the act depriving the plaintiff of his or her rightful possession.” (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 1141, 1158.)

 

“Conversion is a strict liability tort. It does not require bad faith, knowledge, or even negligence; it requires only that the defendant have intentionally done the act depriving the plaintiff of his or her rightful possession.” (Vorik v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 1141, 1158) “[Conversion] must be knowingly or intentionally done, but a wrongful intent is not necessary.” (Taylor v. Forte Hotels Int’l (1991) 235 Cal.App. 3d 1119, 1124.)

 

Here, plaintiff alleges that he had an ownership right to his sunroof—a part of his vehicle—defendant took the sunroof without permission or authority, and in fixing the removal he has incurred damages. Plaintiff further alleges Malfouz then lied to Plaintiff about the roof to invent an explanation for why the sunroof was missing from the vehicle when it was returned by them to Plaintiff.  (FAC¶¶62-67). Plaintiff alleges he went into the office and found Mahfouz and asked what happened to the roof of his car.  Mahfouz responded that the vehicle was fixed and ready to go. Halpern told Mahfouz there was no roof on the vehicle and demanded to know what happened to the roof, and how he was supposed to drive the car without a roof. (FAC¶32-33) This is sufficient.

 

The demurrer as to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

C.     Fraud

 

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal. 4th 631, 638.) “[F]raud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. . . .This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar, supra, 12 Cal. 4th at 645 [internal quotation marks and ellipses omitted].) The law holds plaintiffs to a higher stand where there is a corporate defendant. In that case plaintiff must “allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Id. at p. 645.)

 

Here, contrary to defendants’ assertion, plaintiff has alleged misrepresentations with sufficient detail. He alleges that Mahfouz lied to him when they told him during the repairs that with respect to the sunroof they found broken glass in the subject vehicle and when they told him that the subject vehicle arrived without a sunroof. He alleges Mahfouz was Ikonic’s owner and employee, thus, implicitly that he had the authority to speak for Ikonic. He also alleges approximately when the misrepresentations took place, sometime between January 26, 2022, and February 1, 2022. (FAC¶¶70-82)

 

However, plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged any intent to induce reliance or justifiable reliance. Plaintiff alleges that after the call with defendants wherein they told him about the sunroof he took no further action. He did not ask defendants to attend to the issue or discuss it. He did not ask them to inspect the issue further or repair the issue. Then when he arrived to pick up his vehicle the car lacked a sunroof, and defendants told him that it had arrived without a sunroof. Again, plaintiff does not allege he relied on this statement to his detriment in any way. At present, plaintiff has alleged that his property was damaged, and defendants were untruthful about the cause. However, there is no misrepresentation claim without some kind of justifiable reliance and damages due to that reliance. The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. (FAC¶¶70-82)

 

In the opposition, plaintiff argues that the complaint alleges Mahfouz lied to induce him to pay for repairs. However, according to the allegations the lie regarding the sunroof took place after plaintiff had already paid for the repairs. Thus, plaintiff’s allegations do not support reliance on a misrepresentation. Plaintiff argues in the opposition that defendant concealed the injury to the subject vehicle to induce him to pay for the repairs prior to seeing the issue, e.g., fraudulent concealment, but plaintiff has not alleged that fact in the complaint. (See Nissan Motor Acceptance Cases (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 793, 826 [stating the elements of a claim for fraudulent concealment].) In either case, plaintiff must allege sufficient facts to show the basis of either the fraud based on a misrepresentation or a concealment theory.

 

The demurrer as to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

D.    Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

 

To state a claim for IIED, a plaintiff must allege “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant’s extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the severe emotional distress.” (Crouch v. Trinity Christian Center of Santa Ana, Inc. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 994, 1007). Conduct is outrageous if it is of “such substantial quantity or enduring quality” that an individual in civilized society should not be expected to endure it. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227; see also Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal App.4th 488, 494 [stating that the conduct must be “so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious. . .”].)

 

Here plaintiff has not alleged any conduct giving rise to a claim for IIED for several reasons. First, plaintiff alleges he experienced emotional distress as a result of defendants’ conduct. However, this is a legal conclusion without any foundational facts explaining the scope of his emotional distress. The pleading must include “specific facts that establish severe emotional distress resulting from [a] defendant’s conduct.” (Michaelian v. State Comp. Ins. Fund (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1114; see also Hailey v. California Physicians’ Service (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 452, 474-77 [finding facts supporting IIED claim where plaintiff plead resulting “depression, anxiety, and physical illness,” which manifested as “vomiting, stomach cramps, and diarrhea”].) Second plaintiff has not alleged extreme and outrageous conduct. Presently, plaintiff has alleged several tort claims, but nothing rising above those allegations.


The demurrer as to the fourth cause of action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress is SUSTAINED.  

 

E.     Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (NIED)

 

“Negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence. The traditional elements of duty, breach, causation, and damages apply.” (Eriksson v. Nunnink (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 708, 729 [Internal citations, quotations, and ellipses omitted].) However, “there is no duty to avoid negligently causing emotional distress to another” (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 464.) Instead, damages are “recoverable only if the defendant has breached some other duty to the plaintiff.” (Ibid.) “The independent duty may be imposed by law, assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship between the parties.” (Ibid.) Negligence Infliction of Emotional Distress “has been permitted in two types of situations, referred to as “bystander” and “direct victim” cases.” Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal. 4th 1064, 1072. 

 

Here, plaintiff has not demonstrated duty beyond general negligence and the duty defendants had when they took possession of the subject vehicle. (FAC, ¶95.) Plaintiff has not alleged any special relationship which would give rise to an NIED claim. At present the claim is simply the negligence claim restated.

 

The demurrer as to fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

F.      Breach of fiduciary duty

 

To establish a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, plaintiff must plead “(1) existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) breach of the fiduciary duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by the breach.” (Gutierrez v. Girardi (2011) 194 Cal. App. 4th 925, 932.) A fiduciary relationship requires that a relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.  (Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 25, 29 [internal citations omitted].) A relationship ordinarily exists when “a con¿dence is reposed by one person in the integrity of another, and . . . the party in whom the con¿dence is reposed, if he voluntarily accepts or assumes to accept the con¿dence, can take no advantage from his acts relating to the interest of the other party without the latter’s knowledge or consent.’” (Ibid.)

 

Here, plaintiff has not alleged any facts demonstrating the existence of a fiduciary relationship. Plaintiff alleges that in taking possession of the vehicle, defendants assumed a fiduciary duty “not to steal from and/or break the vehicle, then conceal and lie about it.” (FAC, ¶104.) This is insufficient. Plaintiff has not alleged fact indicating the existence of a fiduciary relationship such as the duty to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the party and one wherein confidence is reposed. Nor has plaintiff alleged facts supporting that the relationship of an auto mechanic to their customer is analogous to the traditional examples of a fiduciary, such as “trustee/beneficiary, corporate directors and majority shareholders, business partners, joint adventurers, and agent/principal.” (Gilman v. Dalby (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 606, 614)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues this claim turns on issues of fact regarding the scope of the existing fiduciary duty. However, plaintiff has alleged a legal conclusion, that a fiduciary relationship exists by virtue of leaving the subject vehicle  in the possession of an auto mechanic. There are no facts suggesting that this service relationship rises to the level of a fiduciary relation.

 

The demurrer as to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

V.                Legal Standard Motion to Strike

 

To succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.) Punitive damages are available in non-contract actions where a defendant is guilty of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c) [defining each term].)

 

Regarding a corporation, “[a] corporation is not deemed to ratify misconduct, and thus become liable for punitive damages, unless its officer, director, or managing agent actually knew about the misconduct and its malicious character. A ‘managing agent’ is an employee with authority to establish corporate policy, that is, the broad principles and rules of general application which govern corporate conduct.” (Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 163.)

 

Defendants also move to strike the fraud claim entirely, the punitive damages allegations (FAC, ¶¶66, 67, 81, 82, 89, 90, 91, 92, 108), and the prayer for punitive damages. The motion to strike the fraud claim and the allegations in paragraph 66, 67, 81, 82, 89, 90, 91, 92, and 108 is denied as moot based on the court’s ruling on demurrer. As there is no basis for punitive damages without those causes of action the motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages is GRANTED.

 

VI.              Conclusion & Order

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED in part and overruled in part. The motion to strike is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part.

 

The demurrer as to first cause of action for negligence and second cause of action for conversion are OVERRULED.

 

The demurrer as to third cause of action for fraud, fourth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, fifth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, and sixth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are SUSTAINED with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is ordered to file the Second Amended Complaint within 30 days.

 

The motion to strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. The motion to strike the fraud claim and the allegations in paragraphs 66, 67, 81, 82, 89, 90, 91, 92, and 108 is DENIED as moot based on the court’s ruling on demurrer. The motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages, ¶109(e), is GRANTED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

            No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

            Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; CCP § 372.) “‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid.  [Citation.] [¶] Nor is the settlement binding [on the minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’” (Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.)  A minor, like Claimant, “shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that he or she has a right to compromise the minor’s claim under California Probate Code section 3500.

 

            Regarding the substance of the Petition, to obtain court approval of the settlement of a minor’s claims, the petitioner must file a complete and “verified petition for approval of the settlement and must disclose ‘all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.’ [Citations.]”  (Barnes v. Western Heritage Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 249, 256; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) (Italics added.)

 

Under Probate Code § 3505, if a petition is unopposed, the Court must issue a decision on the petition at the conclusion of the hearing.
