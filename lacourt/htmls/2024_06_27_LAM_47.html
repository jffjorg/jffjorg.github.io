DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STLC09822    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     June 27, 2024             TRIAL DATE: September 10, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Stanford Neighborhood Association LLC v. Miranda Logistics Enterprise, Inc.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STLC09822           

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants/Cross-Complainants Miranda Logistics Enterprise, Inc., Grit & Gravel Inc, and United Pacific Waste

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Stanford Neighborhood Association, LLC

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         11/24/20: Complaint filed

·         02/09/21: Cross-Complaint filed as to Stanford Neighborhood Association, LLC; DGT Holdings LLC, and LA Catering Truck MFG, Inc.

·         02/26/21: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         04/09/21: Cross-Complaint filed as to Grit & Gravel Inc. and Miranda Logistics Enterprise, Inc.

·         07/01/21: Second Amended Complaint filed.

·         07/21/21: First Amended Cross Complaint filed as to Stanford Neighborhood Association, LLC; DGT Holdings LLC, and LA Catering Truck MFG, Inc.

·         08/09/21: First Amended Cross-Complaint filed as to Grit & Gravel Inc. and Miranda Logistics Enterprise, Inc.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for trespass, nuisance, negligence, and intentional interference with contract. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants have failed to comply with local zoning ordinances pertaining to a building materials salvage yard which they operate.

 

Defendants move for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, for summary adjudication of all causes of action.

 

//         

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Defendants move for summary judgment on the Second Amended Complaint and on the First Amended Cross-Complaint of Stanford Ventures, LLC.

 

            For the reasons stated below in connection with Defendants’ alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

            Defendants move in the alternative for summary adjudication of each cause of action asserted against them in the Second Amended Complaint and the First Amended Cross-Complaint of Stanford Ventures, LLC.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendants request that the Court take Judicial Notice of (1) the South Coast Air Quality Management District’s Rule 403 regarding PM10 particulate levels; (2-13), Defendants’ various filings in support of their opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction and Motino for Summary Judgment and the Court’s rulings thereon; (14) the Certificate of Occupancy for the subject property; (15) the South Coast Air Quality Management Board’s particulate readings from 2014 to 2024; and (16) the Declaration of Donald Velez in Support of the Motion for Terminating Sanctions filed on May 2, 2023.

 

            Defendants’ Request Nos 1 and 14 are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(c) (official acts) and Requests Nos. 2 through 13 and 16 are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records). Request No. 15 is DENIED as immaterial to the Court’s ruling. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 [“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of various records. As none of these documents are material to the Court’s ruling, Plaintiff’s requests are DENIED. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 [“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Plaintiff’s Improper Evidentiary Objections

 

            Plaintiff asserts numerous evidentiary objections in its Separate Statement of Material Fact in Opposition to the Motion. California Rule of Court 3.1354(b) requires that all written objections to evidence be served and filed separately from the other papers in opposition to the motion. As Plaintiff has not complied with this requirement, the Court refuses to consider Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections.

 

Challenge to Merits of Second Amended Complaint.

 

            Defendants contend that they are entitled to summary adjudication of each of the seven causes of action in the Second Amended Complaint for (1) trespass, (2) nuisance, (3) intentional interference with contractual relations, (4) intentional interference with economic relations, (5) negligent interference with economic relations; (6) negligence, and (7) negligence per se on the basis that Plaintiffs have no evidence to support any of the elements of any of these causes of action.

 

            As the moving party on a motion for summary adjudication, Defendants bear a burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of a triable issue of any material fact. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.) Defendants must demonstrate that one or more elements of each cause of action cannot be established by Plaintiffs. (Id. at 853.) Defendants carry this burden by demonstrating, first, that the Plaintiffs do not possess needed evidence, and, second, that they cannot reasonably obtain that evidence. (Id. at 854.)

 

            Defendants first offer sweeping assertions that Plaintiffs cannot establish the requisite intent, causal connection, or harm elements of any of their causes of action. These contentions are based entirely on bare conclusions in the Separate Statement supported by sweeping citations to more than a dozen exhibits filed by Defendants in connection with previous motions. (Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts Nos. 1-2, 9-11, 18-24, 31-34, 41-47, 54-55, 62-64.) None of these exhibits are included in Defendants moving papers, nor do copies of these exhibits accompany Defendants’ request for judicial notice. Even if these exhibits were properly cited and discussed—and they are not, as will be discussed shortly—referencing the exhibits in this manner rather than providing them with the papers obligates the Court and Plaintiffs to plumb the depths of the docket to locate and review these exhibits, wasting considerable time and resources. What is more, these bulk citations do not satisfy the procedural requirements of a separate statement by citing to exhibits with title, page, and line numbers. (Cal. Rules of Court 3.1350(d)(3).) Moreover, as stated in Aguilar, a burden of production “entails . . . the presentation of evidence.” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.) While presentation does not necessarily require that the evidence be persuasive, even though the moving party also bears a burden of persuasion, (Id. at 850), the moving party nevertheless bears the burden to show that the opposing party cannot prevail. (Id. at 853-54.) Vague citations to more than a dozen exhibits show nothing of value, nor do they satisfy either of the purposes of a separate statement. (Parkview Villas Assn., Inc. v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1197, 1210 [“The requirement of a separate statement from the moving party and a responding statement from the party opposing summary judgment serves two functions: to give the parties notice of the material facts at issue in the motion and to permit the trial court to focus on whether those facts are truly undisputed.”].) Defendants’ challenge to these elements are woefully deficient, both procedurally, because they do not comply with the rules for a separate statement, and substantively, as they do not satisfy the burden of production because they do not contain a proper presentation of evidence.

 

            While Defendants also purport to present evidence that their particulate emissions and noise levels were within regulatory boundaries, and that Defendants employed noise mitigation measures, as with their challenge to Plaintiffs’ ability to produce evidence, Defendants merely cite to reams of exhibits filed in support of other motions, without specificity. (SSUMF Nos. 3-8, 12-17, 25-30, 35-40, 48-53, 56-61, 65-70.) For the reasons discussed above, these arguments are likewise procedurally and substantively deficient.

 

            Defendants also challenge each cause of action as to Plaintiff and each assignor with respect to their claims against Grit & Gravel, Inc. and United Pacific Waste by arguing that Plaintiff does not have assignments to pursue claims against these parties. As above, Defendants’ position is based on a bulk citation without specifics. (SSUMF No. 75.) For the reasons stated above, this is not sufficient to carry Defendants’ burden on summary adjudication.

 

            Finally, Defendant United Pacific Waste seeks summary adjudication of each cause of action on the basis that it is not the owner of the subject property as of May 19, 2022. (SSUMF No. 74.) Defendants cite no authority for the conclusory assertion that, even though Defendant United Pacific Waste owned the subject property when the alleged misconduct occurred, all claims against this entity are invalid now that United Pacific Waste no longer owns the property. Defendants have thus failed to carry their burden to demonstrate that Plaintiffs cannot prevail against this Defendant on this basis.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the Second Amended Complaint is DENIED.

 

Challenge to Merits of First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

            Defendants also move for summary adjudication of the sole cause of action of Stanford Ventures, LLC’s First Amended Cross-Complaint for invasion of privacy. The First Amended Cross-Complaint, filed on August 9, 2021, alleges that Defendants have installed security cameras which are pointed into Cross-Complainant’s property. (FAXC ¶ 14.) Cross-Complainant asserts the claim for invasion of privacy under Civil Code section 1708.8. This section states, in relevant part:

 

(a) A person is liable for physical invasion of privacy when the person knowingly enters onto the land or into the airspace above the land of another person without permission or otherwise commits a trespass in order to capture any type of visual image, sound recording, or other physical impression of the plaintiff engaging in a private, personal, or familial activity and the invasion occurs in a manner that is offensive to a reasonable person.

 

(b) A person is liable for constructive invasion of privacy when the person attempts to capture, in a manner that is offensive to a reasonable person, any type of visual image, sound recording, or other physical impression of the plaintiff engaging in a private, personal, or familial activity, through the use of any device, regardless of whether there is a physical trespass, if this image, sound recording, or other physical impression could not have been achieved without a trespass unless the device was used.

 

(Civ. Code § 1708.8(a)-(b).) Defendants contend that, first, the cameras were removed on October 3, 2022, and, second, that the cameras never made any recordings of Stanford Ventures because they were not pointed at their property, but at the street in front of 6510 Stanford Avenue. (SSUMF Nos. 72-73; Declaration of Mario Antonio Miranda ISO Mot. ¶ 5.) Whether the cameras were removed in October 2022 is immaterial where, as here, the Cross-Complainant is seeking relief in part for the alleged invasion of privacy up to that point. Defendants also contend that Cross-Complainant’s evidence does not demonstrate that any recording was made, but their citation to that evidence is, once again, deficient. (SSUMF No. 71.) That said, Defendants’ evidence that the camera was never pointed at the Cross-Complainant’s property is sufficient to carry their burden of production to show that Cross-Complainant cannot prevail on this cause of action. In opposition, Cross-Complainant squarely addresses this contention by offering a sworn affidavit that the cameras were visible from Cross-Complainant’s property and pointed directly into the property. (Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement of Material Fact No. 73; Declaration of Martin Smith ISO Opp. ¶¶ 3-4.) Cross-Complainant has therefore established a triable issue of fact as to whether the cameras were pointed at the property, and, thus, whether any recording was, in fact, made. Defendants are therefore not entitled to summary adjudication of this cause of action.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the First Amended Cross-Complaint by Stanford Ventures, LLC is DENIED.

           

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     June 27, 2024             TRIAL DATE: October 8, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Annabel Aghajanian v. City of Glendale, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 BC720314           

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND ANSWER

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants City of Glendale, Stephen Zurn, Burhan Alshanti, and Matthew Doyle

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Annabel Aghajanian

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         08/30/18: Complaint filed by Annabel Aghajanian

·         09/20/18: Complaint filed by Vahik Haddadian

·         11/19/21: Cases ordered consolidated.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for employment discrimination and wrongful termination. Plaintiff alleges that she was subjected to a campaign of harassment by Defendant Alshanti on the basis of her race, national origin, religious beliefs, and marital status, and that she was terminated by Defendant City of Glendale in retaliation for complaining about this treatment.

 

Defendants move for leave to amend their answer to assert the affirmative defense of Plaintiff’s failure to mitigate.  

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for Leave to Amend the Answer is DENIED. 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants move for leave to amend their answer to assert the affirmative defense of Plaintiff’s failure to mitigate. 

 

Legal Standard

 

The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on such terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 473(a)(1).) Amendments of pleadings may be permitted up to and during trial, absent prejudice to the adverse party. (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.) Judicial policy favors liberal exercise of discretion to permit amendment of the pleadings. (Royal Thrift & Loan Co. v. County Escrow, Inc. (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 24, 41-42.) Absent prejudice to the Plaintiffs, a denial of leave to amend is an abuse of discretion. (Kittridge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.) At the same time, “if a party seeking amendment has been dilatory and/or the delay has prejudiced or will prejudice the opposing party, the trial court in its discretion may deny leave to amend.” (M&F Fishing, Inc. v. Sea-Pac Ins. Managers, Inc. (2012) 202 Cal. App. 4th 1509, 1534.)

 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must meet the following requirements:

 

(a) Contents of motion

 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

 

(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

 

(2) State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

 

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

 

(b) Supporting declaration

 

A separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify:

 

(1) The effect of the amendment;

 

(2) Why the amendment is necessary and proper;

 

(3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

 

(4) The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

(Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1324.)

 

Contents of Motion

 

Defendants included a copy of the proposed Amended Answer as Exhibit 3 with the moving papers. Defendants have therefore substantially complied with California Rule of Court 3.1324(a)(1). The motion does not, however, set forth the proposed amendments by page, paragraph, and line number as required by Rule 3.1324(a)(2) and (a)(3). However, as the proposed amendment is a single affirmative defense which is plainly shown in the proposed amended pleading, the Court finds that Defendants have substantially complied with subdivision (a).

 

Supporting Declaration

 

            The Declaration of Antony R. Pizarro in support of the motion does not directly state the effect of the proposed amendment, as required by Rule 3.1324(b)(1), although the motion plainly states its purpose in seeking to assert the affirmative defense of failure to mitigate. The declaration also does not state why the amendment is necessary and proper beyond a bare assertion that Defendants intend to argue that Plaintiff failed to mitigate her damages and “recently discovered that the failure to mitigate affirmative defense was inadvertently omitted from the answer.” (Declaration of Atony R. Pizarro ISO Mot. ¶ 5.) No details are provided showing when the facts giving rise to these allegations were discovered, nor why the request for amendment was not made earlier. For this reason alone, the Court would be inclined to deny the motion for failure to satisfy the requirements of Rule 3.1324(b).

 

Prejudice to Plaintiff

 

            Even if Defendants’ motion were procedurally compliant, Plaintiff’s forceful opposition demonstrates that leave to amend would not be proper in this instance. To demonstrate that Plaintiff failed to mitigate her damages, Defendants would be required to establish (1) that employment substantially similar to her former job was available; (2) that she failed to make reasonable efforts to seek and retain that employment; and (3) the amount that she could have earned from that employment. (Parker v. Twentieth Century Fox Film Corp. (1970) 3 Cal.3d 176, 182.) As Plaintiff states in her opposition, by seeking to assert this affirmative defense at such a late date, after the close of discovery, Plaintiff would not be able to retain a vocational expert in time for trial. Moreover, any such testimony would require an expert to opine on the state of the job market in 2017, seven years ago, calling the value of that testimony into question. While Defendants contend in reply that Plaintiff had fair notice of this issue and declined to pursue discovery on the subject, the Court has already determined in the context of Defendants’ Motion for Leave to Reopen Discovery that the record belies that position. (May 14, 2024 Ruling on Matter Taken Under Submission.) As the Court stated then, Defendants raised the issue in a deposition on September 29, 2021 and then entirely failed to follow up until February 2024. (Ruling p. 4.) Defendants’ laxness in pursuing this discovery does not and cannot constitute fair notice to Plaintiff that a failure to mitigate defense would be an issue for trial. Further, Defendants offer no indication as to what facts or evidence would support the affirmative defense, which is itself asserted only as a bare legal conclusion that “Plaintiff has failed to mitigate damages,” (Defendants’ Exh. 3). Leaving aside the evidentiary issues, a bare legal conclusion such as the proposed amendment would be subject to demurrer for failure to allege sufficient facts. (Westly v. Board of Administration (2003) 105 Cal. App. 4th 1095, 1117.) Permitting amendment to assert this affirmative defense would be facially prejudicial to Plaintiff, as the defense would be insufficiently pled, and Plaintiff would be deprived of a fair opportunity to pursue discovery on the issue. For that reason, the Court could not countenance granting leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Leave to Amend the Answer is DENIED.
