DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV08857    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: B

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

DOMINIC PAINTER,

 

                        Plaintiff,

 

            vs.

 

FAROKH AMINZADEH RABBI,

 

                        Defendant.

 

CASE NO:  21STCV08857

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL INDEPENDENT MEDICAL EXAMINATION OF PLAINTIFF AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Dept. B

8:30 a.m.

OCTOBER 15, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Defendant Farokh Aminzadeh Rabbi’s Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination of Plaintiff and Request for Sanctions

 

I. INTRODUCTION

Defendant Farokh Aminzadeh Rabbi (“Defendant”) moves to compel the independent medical examination (“IME”) of Plaintiff Dominic Painter (“Plaintiff”), contending that there is good cause for the motion because Plaintiff has placed his physical injuries in controversy. (Notice of Motion, p. 2:5-25.) 

            “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court …, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action …, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

Where any party seeks to obtain discovery by a physical examination, the party shall obtain leave of the court. (CCP § 2032.310(a).) The Court shall grant the motion only for good cause shown. (CCP§ 2032.320(a).)  

 

II. PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

Meet and Confer

Defense counsel asserts that, on August 27, 2024, she attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel over the phone. (Notice of Motion, Wilbur Decl. ¶ 9.) Defense counsel claims that, at this time, Plaintiff agreed to submit to the IME if it was set on a mutually agreeable date and if Plaintiff was reimbursed for reasonable travel expenses. (Ibid. at ¶ 10.) Defendant thereafter attempted to schedule the IME with Plaintiff on numerous occasions. (Ibid. at ¶ 11, Exh. C [record of defense counsel’s meet and confer attempts].) To date, Plaintiff has failed to set a date for the examination. (Ibid. at ¶ 12.) Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer declaration requirement. 

Separate Statement Requirements 

A motion for “medical examination over objection” must be accompanied by a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(a).)  

“A separate statement is a separate document filed and served with the discovery motion that provides all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses to it that are at issue.” (CRC Rule 3.1345(c).) 

Here, Defendant failed to file a separate statement. Plaintiff served Defendant with a Response and Objection to Demand for a Defense Medical Examination of Plaintiff. (Notice of Motion, Exh. B [copy of Plaintiff’s response and objection].) Therefore, Defendant is required to file a separate statement to comply with CRC Rule 3.134(a). Accordingly, the Court must deny Defendant’s motion. 

This procedural issue noted, the Court considers the merits of Defendant’s motion below. 

III. GOOD CAUSE

Defendant contends that there is good cause to compel Plaintiff’s IME because Plaintiff placed his physical injuries in controversy through his Complaint. For example, in the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that as a result of Defendant’s negligence he “has received severe injuries to [his] body and shock and injuries to [his] nervous system, all of which cause[s] him severe pain and discomfort and Plaintiff is informed and believes . . . that he will in the future suffer severe pain and discomfort.” (Complaint ¶ 10.) Defendant also alleges that, during a phone call with Plaintiff’s counsel, counsel stated that Plaintiff’s treatment may be more expensive than previously disclosed. (Wilbur Decl. ¶ 5.) Furthermore, Defendant claims that Plaintiff has failed to provide supplemental discovery responses as requested by Defendant, therefore necessitating the IME. (Wilbur Decl. ¶ 7.) 

            Plaintiff contends that Defendant has failed to establish good cause why Plaintiff should be required to travel from his current residence in Washington, D.C., to Los Angeles for submission of the IME. (Opp. p. 4:11-13.) Plaintiff argues that the IME should be conducted by a physician within a reasonable distance of his current residence in compliance with the 75-mile requirement of CCP § 2032.220(a)(2). 

            In their reply, Defendant argues that they have demonstrated good cause for the travel necessitated by the IME pursuant to CCP § 2032.320(e). § 2032.320(e) allows for an exception to the 75-mile rule if (1) the Court determines that there is good cause for the travel involved and (2) the Court order is conditioned on the advancement by the moving party of the reasonable expenses and costs for travel to the place of examination. Defendant argues that good cause for the travel exists because trial will be held in Los Angeles and Defendant has already retained Dr. Steven Nagelberg to guarantee Dr. Nagelberg’s availability to testify for trial in California. (Reply p. 2:15-18.) The Court notes Defendant is ready and willing to conduct the IME within 75 miles of Plaintiff but, pursuant to Defendant’s motion, still chooses to consider good cause for travel so that Dr. Nagelberg can conduct the IME. 

            There is little California case law on what constitutes good cause for travel under CCP § 2032.320(e). In Hansen v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd., the Court found that it was an abuse of discretion under Cal. Labor Code § 4051 to order a worker with a claimed industrial heart condition to attend an IME more than 185 miles away from his residence. (Hansen v.Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 717, 721-722.) However, the instant action differs significantly from Hansen. Labor Code § 4051 requires that determining the location of any such examinations considers “the convenience of the employee and his physical condition and his ability to attend at the time and place affixed.” (Labor Code § 4051.) In Hansen, Plaintiff filed a worker’s compensation claim after suffering from an industrial heart condition that made extensive travel especially difficult. (Ibid.) Unfortunately for Plaintiff in the instant action, CCP §§ 2032.220 and 2032.320, unlike Labor Code § 4051, give no such consideration for convenience. Additionally, in Hansen, Plaintiff’s requested physicians were still in California, where the facts giving rise to his claim occurred. In contrast, Plaintiff here voluntarily moved across country after allegedly suffering injuries due to Defendant’s negligence. Plaintiff does not contend that his injuries prevent him from travelling. In fact, his cross-country move seems to indicate otherwise. Additionally, the incident which gave rise to Plaintiff’s injuries occurred in Los Angeles. Defendant’s retained expert can testify at trial in Los Angeles. Finally, Defendant has offered to advance Plaintiff’s reasonable travel expenses as required by CCP § 2032.320(e). (Wilbur Decl. ¶ 10.) 

            Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant has shown good cause to compel the IME conducted by Dr. Nagelberg in his location of Downey, California. (Wilbur Decl. ¶ 16.) 

Required Specifications of the Proposed IME 

            Notwithstanding the above findings, a motion for an examination must specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. (CCP § 2032.310(b).)  

            While Defendant has identified Dr. Nagelberg, they have failed to specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination as required by CCP § 2032.310(b). This failure constitutes further procedural grounds upon which the Court must deny Defendant’s motion. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION

For the reasons set forth above, the motion to compel Plaintiff’s independent medical examination is DENIED.  In light of the Court’s tentative decision and its analysis of good cause, the parties are ordered to further meet and confer immediately, but no later than end of week, to make every good faith effort to resolve the issued of Plaintiff’s IME without the need for further court intervention.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On August 12, 2021, Plaintiff Reyes Valadez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint in the instant action against Defendants County of Los Angeles (“County”), California Department of Transportation (“CalTrans”), Southern California Edison Company (“Edison Co.”), Frontier California, Inc. (“Frontier”), and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”), alleging two causes of action: (1) premises liability; and (2) general negligence. The complaint arises from a July 13, 2020, incident, in which Plaintiff alleges that, while bicycling, they were struck and injured by exposed cables negligently maintained by Defendants. 

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to their complaint identifying DOE 1 as Spectrum Pacific West, LLC (“Spectrum”). 

On June 5, 2024, the matter was assigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024. 

On March 15, 2024, Frontier filed the instant motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s complaint. 

On May 17, 2024, Frontier filed a Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement for a $5,000 settlement with Plaintiff. On September 5, 2024, the Court denied the motion on the grounds that Frontier’s potential liability for indemnity to its co-defendants rendered the settlement amount disproportionate. (See September 5, 2024, Minute Order.) 

On October 1, 2024, Spectrum and CalTrans filed their respective oppositions to the instant motion. 

On October 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

On October 10, 2024, Frontier filed replies to Spectrum, CalTrans, and Plaintiff’s oppositions. 

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

            Frontier moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s complaint containing two causes of action for premises liability and general negligence. Frontier alleges that they did not own, operate, or maintain the cable which struck and injured Plaintiff. (Frontier Separate Statement of Facts “F-SSF” Nos. 2-4.) Specifically, Frontier denies its ownership or control of utility pole 977901E (the “Utility Pole”) which hoists the cables that struck Plaintiff. (Ibid. at 1-4.) Therefore, Frontier argues, they cannot be held liable for Plaintiff’s injuries as a matter of law. 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material fact issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)      

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections 

On October 1, 2024, CalTrans filed an evidentiary objection to the declaration of Frontier’s Senior Network Engineer Phillip Olivas (“Olivas”). CalTrans’s objections as to Nos. 1 and 2 are overruled. 

Defendant’s Burden 

Frontier meets their initial burden. 

"One who owns, possesses, or controls [property] has a duty to act reasonably to protect others from a dangerous condition [stemming from] that property.” (Soto v. Union Pacific Railroad Co. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 168, 177.) “The ‘crucial element’ for imposing a duty in such circumstances is control . . . the rationale being that whoever has the means to control the property can take steps to prevent the harm.” (Ibid.) Conversely, “[a] defendant cannot be held liable for the defective or dangerous condition of property which it did not own, possess, or control.” (Isaacs v. Huntington Memorial Hospital (1985) 38 Cal.3d 112, 125.) 

Frontier argues that it does not owe Plaintiff a legal duty because they did not own, maintain, or control the utility pole which hoisted the cables that struck Plaintiff and caused his injuries. (F-SSF Nos. 1-4.) It is undisputed that Plaintiff struck the guy-down cables attached to a utility pole visibly tagged as “977901E.” (F-SSF No. 3; Terzakarian Decl. ¶ 5; Olivas Decl. ¶ 11.) Frontier provides evidence that it relinquished ownership of the Utility Pole in 1965. (F-SSF No. 4; Olivas Decl. ¶¶ 5-6 and 12-14; Exh. A [1965 bill of sale for the Utility Pole].)  

Therefore, Frontier offers evidence that it did not own or control the Utility Pole at the time of Plaintiff’s injuries and owes no legal duty to Plaintiff. Frontier has met their initial burden. 

Plaintiff’s Burden 

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to present controverting evidence. 

Plaintiff disputes Frontier’s assertion that it does not own the Utility Pole. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts (“P-SSF”) Nos. 2-7.) Specifically, Plaintiff alleges Frontier jointly owned the utility pole with Edison Co. at the time of the underlying incident. (P-SSF No. 3; Fradkin Decl. ¶ 4; Exh. 1 [Dolney Decl. ¶ 14; Exh. A (Utility Pole ownership record)].)  

            Spectrum’s Regional Senior Construction Manager Dave Dolney (“Dolney”) investigated the ownership of the Utility Pole. (Dolney Decl. ¶ 1.) For decades, Dolney reviewed and submitted applications to the Southern California Joint Pole Committee (the “Committee”), which maintains records of each regional jointly-owned pole to monitor entities’ ownership interest in aerial power lines. (Ibid. at ¶ 3.) During his investigation, Dolney obtained the Committee ownership report for the Utility Pole in question. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 8-10.) The report shows that the pole was jointly owned by Frontier and Edison Co. at the time of Plaintiff’s injuries. (Ibid. at ¶ 14.) Moreover, it shows that Frontier owned aerial lines attached to the Utility Pole which were supported by guy-down cables. (Ibid. at ¶ 21.) Dolney represents that it is industry standard for each utility whose lines are supported by guy-down cables to own, maintain, and take responsibility for those cables. (Ibid. at ¶ 23.) 

Therefore, Plaintiff presents evidence that there is a triable issue of material fact as to who owned and controlled the Utility Pole during the underlying incident. Plaintiff meets their burden. Accordingly, Frontier’s motion for summary judgment must be denied. 

Frontier’s Replies 

            Frontier’s replies object to Dolney’s declaration on the grounds that it relies on inadmissible evidence and speculation. Particularly, Frontier writes that “. . . Dolney wrote his declaration only after reviewing unauthenticated, unverified photos from the internet.” (Reply, p. 2 lines 10-11.) While the Dolney declaration attaches unverified photos as Exhibits C and D, Dolney mainly relies on his experience on the Committee and his review of the Committee’s ownership records regarding the Utility Pole. Consequently, the Court does not find merit in Frontier’s argument. Additionally, Frontier only included this argument in their replies and failed to properly raise the issue as an evidentiary objection. 

Frontier also argues that Dolney’s declaration, which discusses Utility Pole ownership by a company called Crown Castle during July 2020, proves that Frontier did not own or control the Utility Pole at the time of Plaintiff’s injuries. (See Dolney Decl. ¶¶ 29 and 30.) However, Crown Castle’s ownership of a portion of the Utility Pole does not definitively preclude Frontier’s ownership.  

Finally, Frontier argues that the opposing parties misconstrue Frontier’s legal argument. Frontier claims that it “never asserted” that Frontier has no ownership in the Utility Pole. Instead, Frontier argues, it merely claimed it had no ownership or control over the injurious guy-down cables. However, the question of utility pole ownership is directly related to control and ownership of the cables, as it is industry standard for each utility whose lines are supported by guy-down cables to own, maintain, and take responsibility for those cables. (See Dolney Decl. at ¶ 23.) At the very least, Frontier’s replies demonstrate that control and ownership of the cables remains a triable issue of material fact in this action.  

 

V.       CONCLUSION

             A triable issue of material fact exists as to Frontier’s ownership and control of the Utility Pole and its allegedly injurious guy-down cables. Accordingly, Frontier’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

Case Number: 22STCV06617    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: B

Re:                         22STCV06617 Shakiba Yegan vs Uber Technologies, Inc., et al.

 

 

Plaintiff Shakiba Yegan’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury

 

Defendants: Uber Technologies, Inc., et al.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

           

Plaintiff’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury is GRANTED.

           

BACKGROUND

           

On February 23, 2022, Plaintiff Shakiba Yegan (“Plaintiff” filed a complaint against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Uber”), Raiser, LLC (“Raiser”), Raiser-CA, LLC (“Raiser-CA”), Cesar Augusto Campos Orellana (“Orellana”), Francisco J. Giron (“Giron”) and DOES 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) motor vehicle negligence; and (2) general negligence. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants, driving negligently, struck her vehicle from behind and caused Plaintiff’s vehicle to collide with the car in front of her. As a result, Plaintiff claims serious injuries and property damage.

 

On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for order granting relief from potential waiver of trial by jury.

 

Defendants did not file an opposition.

 

A non-jury trial is set for February 24, 2025.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The right to a trial by jury as declared by Section 16 of Article I of the California Constitution shall be preserved to the parties inviolate.”  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc., § 631(a).)  “In civil cases, a jury may only be waived pursuant to subdivision (f).”  (Ibid.)  CCP section 631(f) provides that a party waives trial by jury by (1) failing to appear at the trial; (2) written consent filed with the clerk or judge; (3) oral consent, in open court, entered in the minutes; (4) failing to announce that a jury is required, at the time the cause is first set for trial, if it is set upon notice of stipulation, or within five days after notice of setting if it is set without notice or stipulation; (5) failing to timely pay the fee described in subdivision (b), unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee; or (6) failing to deposit with the clerk or judge, at the beginning of the second and each succeeding day’s session, the sum provided in subdivision (e).  (Ibid., § 631(f).) “The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.”  (Ibid., § 631(g).)  “The trial court should grant a motion for relief of a jury waiver ‘unless, and except, where granting such a motion would work serious hardship to the objecting party.’”  (Mackovska v. Viewcrest Road Properties LLC (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1, 10 [quoting Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 806, 809].)  “When there is doubt about whether to grant relief from a jury trial waiver, the court must resolve that doubt in favor of the party seeking a jury trial.”  (Ibid.)  

 

“In a motion for relief from waiver of a jury trial, the crucial question is whether the party opposing relief will suffer any prejudice if the court grants relief.”  (Mackovska, supra, 40 Cal.App.5th at 10.)  “‘The prejudice which must be shown from granting relief from the waiver is prejudice from the granting of relief and not prejudice from the jury trial.’” (Ibid. [quoting Massie v. AAR Western Skyways, Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 405, 411].) “‘The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.’” (Ibid. [quoting Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811].) “Denying relief where the party opposing the motion for relief has not shown prejudice is an abuse of discretion.” (Ibid.)  

 

ANALYSIS

 

This action was commenced on February 23, 2022. Therefore, jury fees were required to be posted no later than February 23, 2023. CCP § 631(c)(2))(requiring that, if no initial case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, fees are due no later than 365 days after filing the initial complaint.) Here, Plaintiff posted jury fees on June 15, 2023, nearly four months after the statutory deadline.

 

Plaintiff does not proffer a reason for the late posting of jury fees. However, while delayed posting of jury fees constitutes waiver of trial by jury, it is “not a sufficient reason for denying relief from waiver.” (TriCoast Builders, Inc. v. Fonnegra (2024) 15 Cal.App.5th 766, 782.) Therefore, the Court accepts Plaintiff’s late posting for the purposes of CCP § 631(f).

 

Plaintiff argues that granting this motion will not pose undue hardship on Defendants. Plaintiff claims that all parties have been preparing for a jury trial. (Eiden Decl. ¶ 3.) Both Defendants and Plaintiff have made jury trial demands and posted fees. Furthermore, Plaintiff notes that trial in this matter is months away. The Court also notes that this motion remains unopposed.

 

Given the Court’s liberal policy of granting relief from waiver of trial by jury, as well as Defendants’ lack of opposition, Plaintiff’s motion for relief is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s motion for relief from potential waiver of trial by jury is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
