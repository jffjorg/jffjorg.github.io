DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV20750    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 73

6/12/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

FULLER, et al. v. SKETCHBOX LLC, et al. (23STCV07569)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Virginia Ksadzhikyan (B B Law Group LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/ opposing party: Alton Burkhalter (Burkhalter Kessler Clement & George LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE (filed on 05/30/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57 is GRANTED. Defendant shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 is DENIED.

The Court DENIES requests for sanctions.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 24, 2022, Plaintiffs Torie Fuller (“Fuller”) and Shady Peach Solutions, LLC brought this action against Defendants Sketchbox LLC (“Sketchbox”) and Jon Gougeon (“Gougeon”).

 

On July 13, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) asserting 7 causes of action for: (1) breach of oral contract; (2) breach of implied contract; (3) breach of written contract; (4) breach of the implied duty of good faith and fair dealing; (5) fraudulent inducement by misrepresentation; (6) accounting and (7) declaratory relief.

 

The SAC alleges the following. Defendant Gougeon formed Sketchbox on or about May 2015, as a single member Limited Liability Company, with Gougeon being its only member. Shortly after the company was formed, Fuller began assisting Gougeon in operating and managing Sketchbox. On or around September 2015, Gougeon represented to Fuller that he intended to sell Sketchbox when it reached 100,000 subscribes, and promised to pay Plaintiff 10% of the profits from the sale in exchange for Plaintiff’s services, which included assisting managing Sketchbox to make it operational and functional, including but not limited, to assisting in customer services, billing, operations, and later supervision of employees. On or about 2016, Gougen and Fuller modified their oral agreement and agreed that Fuller would be given a 10% profit share interest in Sketchbox, which she would receive through periodic profit share distributions. However, Fuller alleges that Defendants made periodic profit share distributions to Fuller, but they rarely included the full amount owed to Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants promised to pay the remainder of Plaintiffs’ payments at a later time when additional funds came in. On or about April 11, 2022, Defendants terminated Plaintiffs’ services with Defendants and have refused to pay any of the debt owed to Plaintiff.

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiffs initially filed the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded by Defendant Jon Gougeon. On May 6, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded by Defendant Jon Gougeon, arguing:

·       In response to RFP Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57, Defendant only objected and refused to produce any responsive documents

·       Plaintiff initiated an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) with the Court. At the IDC, the Court conveyed that Defendant should provide supplemental responses and Defendant agreed to do so. (Ksadzhikyan Decl., ¶ 11 & Exh. 6.) On March 8, 2024, Defendant produced supplemental verified responses. Defendant did not, however, provide any further response and refused to provide responsive documents.

·       Plaintiffs request that the court impose a monetary sanction in the amount of $4,287.

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The Disputed Discovery Requests Seek Information Neither Relevant to the Cause of Action for Breach of Written Contract Nor Likely to Lead to the Discovery of Admissible Evidence.

·       Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions should be denied and instead sanctions should be imposed against Plaintiffs in the amount of $5,000. In the alternative, the Court should not impose sanctions against Gougeon.

 

Plaintiffs filed a reply maintaining that Defendants must provide further response and produce responsive documents.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel submitted a declaration stated the parties met and conferred as to Defendants’ allegedly deficient RFP responses. (Ksadzhikyan Decl., ¶¶ 6-14.) The parties were unable to resolve these issues and the parties attended an Informal Discovery Conference with the Court to address these issues. After the IDC, Defendants indicated they would provide supplemental responses. On March 8, 2024, Defendants produced supplemental responses. Plaintiffs state Defendants did not provide additional responses to RFP Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, or 57. Thus, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs’ failed to meaningfully meet and confer after receiving Defendants’ supplemental responses. However, the Court will analyze the motions on the merits despite this deficiency because it recognizes that the parties previously met and conferred on these issues and Defendants’ supplemental responses to RFP Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57 did not include any new production.

 

C.      Discussion

 

As an initial matter, to the extent Defendants asserts a privacy privilege, they must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].)

 

Further, the Court notes that to the extent Defendants believe that no further documents exist or that the documents have already been produced, Defendants are ordered to state that fact.  

 

                                                    i.     RFP Nos. 14-15

 

RFP No 14 seeks: Please produce all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA from YOUR bank that RELATE TO payments YOU made to PLAINTIFF.

 

RFP No. 15 seeks: Please produce all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA from YOUR bank that RELATES TO payments YOU made to Plaintiff Shady Peach.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. The lawsuit pertains to whether Defendants made all payments allegedly owed to Plaintiff. Thus, the requested information is relevant and the request is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14 and 15 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                   ii.     RFP Nos. 21, 36, 37

 

RFP Nos. 21, 36 and 37 seek Defendants’ tax returns.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Plaintiff is alleging that an agreement was reached entitling Plaintiffs to 10% of Sketchbox, LLC’s profits. In order to calculate damages – i.e., what 10% of the profits was – information from Sketchbox, LLC’s tax returns would be relevant to show Sketchbox, LLC’s income and expenses – i.e., its profit.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 21, 36 and 37 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                 iii.     RFP No. 34

 

RFP No. 34 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS related to YOUR termination, suspension, or modification of any and all contracts entered into with PLAINTIFF.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. The responsive documents would likely contain evidence pertaining to Defendant’s reasons and justifications for the termination, including whether it was done to avoid fulfilling Defendants’ contractual obligations.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 34 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                 iv.     RFP Nos. 38-39

 

RFP No. 38 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and Defendant Sketchbox, LLC’s accountant.

 

RFP No. 39 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and Mark Dionne that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF.

 

The Court finds Request No. 38 is overbroad as it pertains to “all” communications between Defendants and its accountant. Plaintiff should limit the scope of the request as to subject matter or time.

 

The Court finds Request No. 39 is sufficiently limited as to subject matter as it only requests communications relating to Plaintiff. The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 is DENIED.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 39 is GRANTED.  

                                                   v.     RFP Nos. 43-46

 

RFP Nos. 43-46 seek communications between Defendant and Christopher Burke, an attorney that worked for Sketchbox, relating to Plaintiff, Plaintiffs’ profit share interest, Plaintiffs’ salary, and Plaintiffs’ employment.

 

The Court finds this information may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The requests seek communications or other documents that are likely to provide evidence as to what Defendants knew Plaintiff was entitled to, whether and to what extent Defendants acknowledged that they had an obligation to make profit share distributions to Plaintiffs, what monies were owed to Plaintiffs, and what was actually paid to Plaintiffs. To the extent Defendants asserts a privacy privilege, they must submit a privilege log for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Defendants have not submitted a privilege log to assist the Court in evaluating these claims.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 43 to 46 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                 vi.     RFP Nos. 50-51

 

RFP No. 50 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain profit share distributions made to Defendant JON GOUGEON.

 

RFP No. 51 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER to, RELATE to, or pertain salary payments made to Defendant JON GOUGEON.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Plaintiffs allege that payments were made to Plaintiffs constituting partial payment of the profit share to which Plaintiffs were entitled, but that Defendants did not make the whole payments as required. The requested information is relevant to show the extent to which Defendants obtained a profit.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 50 and 51 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                vii.     RFP Nos. 54, 57

 

RFP No. 54 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and MIKE BENDER that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF.

 

RFP No. 57 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and MIKE BENDER that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF’s employment.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Mark Bender is an employee that worked for Defendants. These requests seek communications that are likely to provide evidence as to what Defendants knew Plaintiff was entitled to, whether and to what extent Defendants acknowledged that they had an obligation to make profit share distributions to Plaintiff, what monies were owed to Plaintiff, what work was performed by Plaintiff, and what was actually paid to Plaintiff. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 54 and 57 is GRANTED.  

 

D.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Defendants request that the Court award monetary sanctions against Defendants and its attorney of record in the sum of $4,347. In opposition, Defendants requests sanctions of $5,000 on grounds that Defendants brought this motion without substantial justification.¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ motion is granted and denied in part. The Court does not grant either parties’ request to impose sanctions.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57 is GRANTED. Defendant shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 is DENIED.

The Court DENIES requests for sanctions.


Case Number: 23STCV16554    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 73

06/12/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

FEAR OF GOD, LLC, et al. v. ART CITY CENTER LLC, et al. (23STCV16554)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  John Cha (Raines Feldman Littrell LLP.)

Counsel for Defendant Art City Center LLC/moving party: Daniel Zohar (Zohar Law Firm, P.C.)

 

Demurrer to SECOND amended complaint (filed by Defendant Art City Center LLC on 04/25/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action.

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to the third cause of action.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 14, 2023, Plaintiffs Fear of God, LLC and FOG Essentials, LLC (collectively “Plaintiffs” or “Tenants”) filed this action against Defendants Art City Center, LLC (“Art City” or “Landlord”), Phillip Ruhl (“Ruhl”), and CBRE Brokerage Service (“CBRE”).

 

On February 26, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint with leave to amend.

 

On March 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) declaratory relief, (2) rescission and (3) negligent misrepresentation.

 

The SAC alleges the following. Plaintiffs are tenants to a lease (the “Lease”) for a commercial property located at 1360 E. 6th Street, Los Angeles (the “Premises”). Prior to the Lease, the Premises was used for many years as a warehouse. However, Defendants marketed the Premises as suitable for conversion to an office space. Plaintiffs’ intent and plan, which was communicated to Defendant Art City and its broker, Phillip Ruhl, verbally and in writing, was to create a multi-use space, including a retail showroom, general office, flex office, creative studio, marketing, content production, light manufacturing, light distribution, entertainment and media related space, and its corporate headquarters (“Intended Use”). Of particular concern to Plaintiff during Lease negotiations was the availability of electrical power and its capacity for the Premises such that Plaintiff could properly operate its business in accordance with the Intended Use. Plaintiff was repeatedly assured, both verbally and in writing, by both Defendants and Defendants’ Broker that the Premises has “enough power” for the Plaintiffs’ Intended Use. However, after the Lease was executed, Plaintiff alleges Defendants have failed to provide Plaintiffs with the full power that was promised and that is needed to use the premises for Plaintiffs’ Intended Use.

 

On April 25, 2024, Defendant Art City filed a Demurrer to the SAC, arguing:

·       The first cause of action for declaratory relief fails because there is no actual, ripe controversy.

o   The facts alleged in the FAC are controverted by the attached Lease, which contains an integration clause and clearly provides that: a) Art City has no obligation to provide power to the Premises; b) Plaintiffs accepted responsibility to coordinate with LADWP regarding their infrastructure capacity and available fault current, as well as inspect field conditions by a State licensed electrical contractor prior to adding any increase in power load from that used by the previous tenant; c) Plaintiffs must provide detailed architectural and engineering plans to Art City before Art City is obligated to install the HVAC system; d) to delay the Lease Commencement Date, Plaintiffs must actually and substantially be delayed in making their improvements because of the failure to install HVAC; and e) in order to toll the Lease Commencement Date, Plaintiffs must give notice of this delay, provide a factual explanation of how the delay was caused, and give Art City an opportunity to cure – none of which Plaintiffs are alleged to have done.

o   This cause of action is also uncertain because it is unclear what the alleged dispute is. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).)

·       The second cause of action for rescission is uncertain and fails to state a claim

o   The alleged mutual mistake of fact concerns a unilateral error in judgment on the part of Plaintiffs regarding whether the property would have enough power for the facility that they had yet to design, much less, construct. Rescission for mutual mistake of fact must concern a present or past, objective fact at the time of contracting.

o   Plaintiffs fail to adequately allege fraud upon which they can base their claim for rescission.

·       The third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation fails to state a claim

o   Negligent misrepresentation only applies to past or existing facts. The purported fraud concerned a future condition – whether the property would have sufficient power once the yet-to-be-designed facility was operational.

o   The claim does not meet the heightened pleading standards for fraud. For example, the FAC fails to provide specific details how or why Defendants alleged statements were untrue at the time they were made and how Plaintiffs reasonably relied on such statements despite the clear language of the lease.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue:

·       The allegations of the SAC are sufficient to support each and every cause of action alleged

·       The first cause of action for declaratory relief does not fail. The SAC sufficiently alleges two concrete disputes with the requisite specificity under Code Civ. Proc. §1060: (1) does the Lease require Art City to provide the Premises for Plaintiffs’ Intended Use (SAC ¶¶24-25, 28-29, 47-48, 51), and (2) has the Lease Commencement Date occurred such that Plaintiff is obligated to pay rent (SAC, ¶¶36-42, 51).

·       The second cause of action for rescission does not fail. Either Art City was operating under a mistaken belief, or it made intentional misrepresentations about the available power. The SAC specifically identifies concrete, pre-Lease statements (both verbally and in writing) that Art City made describing the then-existing state of affairs.

·       The third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation does not fail. Defendants made misrepresentations to Plaintiffs about the specific amount of power, Defendants knew its representations were not true because the calculations were made by its electricians and they knew the HVAC would use most of the available power.

 

In reply, Art City maintains that the demurrer should be sustained because Plaintiffs fail to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he sent Plaintiffs’ counsel a letter outlining the alleged deficiencies in the SAC. (Zohar Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.) The parties subsequently met and conferred telephonically regarding the alleged deficiencies. (Ibid.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

A.     First Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief

 

Art City argues the first cause of action for declaratory relief fails because there is no actual, ripe controversy and it is uncertain.

 

Any party claiming rights under a contract or under a written instrument other than a will or trust, or with respect to property, may bring an action for a declaration of his or her rights or duties with respect to the other.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.)  “To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.”  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.) 

 

A cause of action for declaratory relief should not be used as a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues raised in another cause of action.  (General of America Insurance Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.)  “The availability of another form of relief that is adequate will usually justify refusal to grant declaratory relief.”  (California Insurance Guarantee Association v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624).  Further, “there is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.”  (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, quotation marks omitted.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff is requesting a judicial declaration “so that Tenant may ascertain its rights under the Lease with respect to the Premises including (a) whether Landlord has an obligation to provide the Premises to Tenant such that Tenant can operate its Intended Use; (b) whether the Lease Commencement Date has occurred, and if so, why; and (c) whether Landlord is entitled to draw rent from Tenant’s letter of credit, and if so, when that entitlement began.” (SAC, ¶ 51.)

 

Art City argues the SAC is vague as to how much electrical power was promised or needed. However, the SAC alleges that Plaintiffs contend that Art City has an obligation to provide the electrical power for Plaintiffs’ intended use of the premises as stated in the Power Availability Assessment Letter. (SAC, ¶ 16.) Before executing the Lease, in a May 5, 2022, email, Art City’s Broker, presented Plaintiffs with an October 31, 2019, “Power Availability Assessment” letter. The Power Availability Assessment letter was prepared and signed by Adi Ben-Shahar, CEO, of E-lectric, Inc., who Tenant is informed and believes at all relevant times from 2019 to the present acted as Landlord’s electrician with respect to the Premises. The Power Availability Assessment letter identifies the “available power” for “new tenant” use, and states that, in the aggregate, the available power to the Premises is 487,680 watts and the maximum allowable loads per existing service at 80% of capacity is 390,144 watts. (Ibid.) In or around July of 2022, Landlord’s Broker, expressly represented to Tenant that the amount of available power stated in the Power Availability Assessment letter was, based on the Landlord’s electrician’s analysis, the amount of electrical power available at the Premises for Tenant’s use, and would be sufficient for Tenant’s Intended Use. (SAC, ¶ 18.) In or about January 18, 2023 (nearly five months after the Lease was executed), notwithstanding Landlord’s prior representations that the full 487,680 watts (and 80% capacity) would be available to Tenant and “more than enough power for your office and retail operation,” Landlord informed Tenant for the first time that Landlord will be using substantially all of the 487,680 watts (and 80% capacity) for the HVAC system alone. (SAC, ¶ 28.) Thus, the Court finds Plaintiffs have specified the amount of power they were promised and needed to use the premises.

 

Art City also argues there is no actual, ripe controversy because the Lease provides that Plaintiffs are obligated to contract with LADWP for services and the Landlord is not required to provide a specific amount of electricity. However, while Plaintiffs agreed to contract with LAWDP for the purchase of power service and to coordinate with LAWDP concerning the installation of new loads, Plaintiffs allege Art City frustrated Plaintiffs’ ability to obtain the power needed for their contractually-bargained-for use of the Premises by both indicating the installation of an HVAC system that will divert available power from Plaintiffs and allegedly taking action to preclude LAWDP from delivering additional power to the Premises to meet Plaintiffs’ needs. Given these allegations in the SAC, Plaintiffs have met their burden of setting forth facts establishing an actual controversy.

 

Art City asserts the conditions precedent to invoking the tolling of the Lease Commencement Date have not been alleged, therefore, an actual, justiciable controversy has not been presented to the Court. However, the SAC alleges that pursuant to the Lease, Landlord’s delivery obligations have two deadlines, one for all of the “Building Items” and another for the installation of the HVAC units. With regard to the Building Items, Landlord is to substantially complete them within 5 months from the effective date of January 24, 2023. The Lease provides that if the Building Items are not substantially completed by the Building Items Delivery Date, the Lease Commencement Date is extended on a day-for-day basis for each day beyond August 1, 2023 that Tenant is actually delayed in completing its Tenant improvements. As regards the HVAC, the Lease, Exhibit E, paragraph 1.3(b) says that Landlord’s failure to install the HVAC in the time frame stated (8 months after the effective date of January 24, 2023), means that the Lease Commencement Date has not occurred. Tenant contends that the Lease Commencement Date has not occurred because Landlord has failed to provide the Building Items, including the exterior walls, exterior windows and doors, roof, floors, interiors, stairs, railings and ramps, restrooms, the building core and shell, plumbing, and security, along with the HVAC system, in the time frames and manner required by the Lease. (SAC 37-40.) Based on these allegations, the Court finds Plaintiffs have alleged the requirements in order to extend the Commencement Date.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is uncertain pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f). In applying the stringent standard for demurrers filed on this ground, the Court finds that the complaint is not “so incomprehensible” that Defendants cannot respond, especially given the extensive analysis they have argued against the pleading. “Where the complaint contains substantive factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled or plaintiff given leave to amend.” (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (2011) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 fn.2.) Accordingly, the demurrer is overruled on this basis. 

 

The Court finds the allegations sufficient to state a claim for declaratory relief. Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

 

B.     Second Cause of Action: Rescission

 

Civil Code section 1689 describes circumstances justifying rescission of a contract: 

 

“(b) A party to a contract may rescind the contract in the following cases: 

(1) If the consent of the party rescinding, or of any party jointly contracting with him, was given by mistake, or obtained through duress, menace, fraud, or undue influence, exercised by or with the connivance of the party as to whom he rescinds, or of any other party to the contract jointly interested with such party. 

(2) If the consideration for the obligation of the rescinding party fails, in whole or in part, through the fault of the party as to whom he rescinds. 

(3) If the consideration for the obligation of the rescinding party becomes entirely void from any cause. 

(4) If the consideration for the obligation of the rescinding party, before it is rendered to him, fails in a material respect from any cause. 

(5) If the contract is unlawful for causes which do not appear in its terms or conditions, and the parties are not equally at fault. 

(6) If the public interest will be prejudiced by permitting the contract to stand. 

(7) [Cross-references.]” 

 

(Civ. Code, § 1689.) 

 

“[I]t is said that a breach of a contractual right in a trivial or inappreciable respect will not justify rescission of the agreement by the party entitled to the benefit in question, [though] a default in performance will not be tolerated if it is so dominant or pervasive as in any real or substantial measure to frustrate the purpose of the undertaking.” (Medico-Dental Bldg. Co. of Los Angeles v. Horton & Converse (1942) 21 Cal.2d 411, 433.) A party to a contract may rescind only if there is a material breach, although a slight breach at the outset may justify termination if it indicates future difficulty in obtaining performance. (Pennel v. Pond Union School Dist. (1973) 29 Cal.App.3d 832.) 

 

a.     Rescission on grounds of Mutual Mistake

 

“[R]escission for a mistake of fact must relate to present or past facts. There is no ground for rescission based on a mistake regarding future events.” (Rescission Cause of Action (CA), Practical Law Standard Clauses w-021-3666, citing Paramount Petroleum Corp. v. Super. Ct. (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 226, 245.) In addition, a mistake of fact must be about an “objective, existing fact” not an “error in judgment.” (Hedging Concepts, Inc. v. First Alliance Mortgage Co. (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1421, as modified on denial of reh'g (Feb. 22, 1996).)

 

The Court had previously found there were no foundation allegations as to what the parties believed about Plaintiff’s intended use of the premises and what level of power it needs or requires for its operations. (Court’s 02/26/24 Minute Order, p. 2.) As a basis for a mutual mistake, the cause of action is silent as to what was or is needed by Plaintiff. (Ibid.)

 

The SAC alleges Plaintiffs had communicated their intended use to Defendants and the parties understood Plaintiffs’ intended use. (SAC, ¶¶ 11-12.) The SAC also alleges that Defendants had told Plaintiffs the full 487,680 watts (and 80% maximum allowable load of 390,144 watts) would be sufficient for Plaintiffs’ intended use of the premises, and that amount would be available for Plaintiffs. (SAC ¶¶16-19, 21-22). The Court finds these allegations sufficient to state a mistake of fact as to the electrical power the parties believed would be available and needed by Plaintiff.

 

Defendants also argue there are no grounds for rescission as the alleged mistake is based on opinions on a future event as the facility plans had not yet been designed. However, as the SAC alleges, Plaintiffs contend the parties knew what the premises were intended to be used for and Defendants pre-lease statements attested to the specific amount of electrical power presently available for use on the premises. The Court finds these allegations sufficient and overrules the demurrer to the cause of action for rescission based on mutual mistake.

 

b.     Rescission on grounds of Fraud

 

A pleading seeking rescission based on fraud must allege not only “the facts constituting the fraud,” but also “[e]very element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner (i.e., factually and specifically).” (See Hall v. Department of Adoptions (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 898, 904.)

 

The Court had previously found Plaintiff failed to allege Defendants had knowledge of falsity or intent to deceive Plaintiffs. (Court’s 02/26/24 Minute Order, p. 2.)

 

Plaintiffs allege Art City’s had knowledge of falsity because the amount of power that was presented to Plaintiffs as being available in the “Power Availability Assessment” letter was calculated by Art City’s electrician. (SAC, ¶ 16) Plaintiffs contend Art City knew or should have known that it would be using substantially all of the available wattage for the HVAC, and therefore, the electrical power was not available for Plaintiffs’ Intended Use. (SAC, ¶ 27).

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged Defendants had an intent to deceive. Plaintiffs do not allege how Art City would have known it would be using the available wattage for the HVAC and thus, intended to deceive Plaintiffs. The Court does not find the SAC’s allegations of rescission based on fraud sufficient.

 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action.  (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97,119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Since the Court finds the allegations sufficient to state a claim for rescission based on mutual mistake, the Court does not sustain the entire second cause of action.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled.

 

C.     Third Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation are: (1) the defendant must have made a representation as to a past or existing material fact; (2) the representation must have been untrue; (3) regardless of the defendant’s actual belief, the defendant must have made the representation without any reasonable ground for believing it to be true; (4) the representation must have been made with the intent to induce the plaintiff to rely on it; (5) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the falsity of the representation and must have acted in justifiable reliance on the truth of the representation; and (6) as a result of reliance on the truth of the representation, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. (Continental Airlines, Inc. v. McDonnell Douglas Corp. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 388, 402 (reh’g denied and opinion modified on Jan. 5, 1990).) 

As discussed above, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged that Defendants “had no reasonable grounds for believing the representation was true when they made it.” Plaintiffs do not allege how Art City would have known it would be using the available wattage for the HVAC and thus, intended to deceive Plaintiffs.

Thus, the demurrer is sustained as to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action with leave to amend. 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action.

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to the third cause of action.
