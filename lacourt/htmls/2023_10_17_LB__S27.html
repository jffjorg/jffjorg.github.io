DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21LBCV00160    Hearing Date: October 17, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Heather Szafranski filed this action against Defendants, Michael Hollinghurst and his company, Network Financial Service, for quiet title, fraud, cancellation of instruments, rescission of contracts, and declaratory relief.  The crux of the complaint is that Plaintiff signed a promissory note with Defendant, but believed the transaction was canceled; Defendant, in violation of the parties’ agreement, filed the deed of trust on the property. 

 

Plaintiff filed the complaint on 3/26/21.  The Clerk, at Plaintiff’s request, entered Defendant’s default on 11/04/21.  On 11/17/21, after a default judgment prove-up hearing, the Court issued judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant.  The judgment makes clear that Plaintiff owns the subject property and Defendants have no interest in the subject property.  It also makes clear that the promissory note upon which Defendants base their claim is null and void.  The judgment provides, “Defendants are to cancel the promissory note and Improper Deed of Trust (document number 8-0719664) and to deliver it to the Plaintiff or to the clerk of this Court for cancellation.”

 

2.     Ex Parte Application for Temporary Restraining Order

On 10/05/23, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for a temporary restraining order, seeking an order restraining Defendant from taking any action with respect to the subject property.  Plaintiff sought the order because Defendant, in violation of the judgment, recorded a notice of default on the property and commencing foreclosure proceedings. 

 

On 10/06/23, the Court set a hearing on the requested preliminary injunction for 10/17/23. 

 

3.     Analysis

The Court has numerous procedural concerns with the ex parte application for a temporary restraining order and request for OSC re: preliminary injunction. 

 

First and foremost, TROs and preliminary injunctions are issued pre-trial to maintain the status quo pending the outcome of trial, at which time a permanent injunction can be issued.  In this case, there is already a judgment.  It is unclear how the “preliminary injunction,” if issued, would work.  Would it remain in place forever?  Plaintiff cites no authority permitting the Court to impose a post-judgment preliminary injunction.

 

Second, the judgment in this case already provides for the relief requested.  It declares the deed of trust void and requires Defendant to cancel the note and deed of trust, among other things.  If Defendant has not complied with the terms of the judgment, Plaintiff can pursue post-judgment remedies to ensure compliance.  Again, Plaintiff has cited no authority for the position that a preliminary injunction is among the forms of available relief.

 

Third, the 10/06/23 order required Plaintiff to give notice.  There is no notice of ruling in the file. 

 

The Court finds it is in the interest of justice to have the temporary restraining order remain in place while Plaintiff takes steps to enforce the default judgment. 

 

CCP §527 permits the Court to impose a temporary restraining order for 22 days, and permits a continuance of the hearing on the OSC re: preliminary injunction for an additional 15 days.  37 days after 10/06/23 falls on Sunday, 11/12/23.  The Court is continuing the OSC re: preliminary injunction to Thursday, 11/09/23 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27.  The Court is also setting an OSC re: resolving the temporary restraining order for the same date and time.

 

If Plaintiff believes a preliminary injunction is procedurally proper, she must provide authority in support of her position at least two weeks prior to the continued hearing date.  The Court notes that Defendants are in default, which typically means they are not permitted to participate in court proceedings.  However, because this is a post-judgment proceeding, it is unclear if the rules precluding them from participating in the matter apply.  The Court will also permit Defendants to file a brief and any evidence in opposition to the OSC at least two weeks prior to the continued hearing date if they wish to do so.

b.     Law Governing Relief Requested

CCP §386(b) provides:

(b) Any person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom double or multiple claims are made, or may be made, by two or more persons which are such that they may give rise to double or multiple liability, may bring an action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims.

When the person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom such claims are made, or may be made, is a defendant in an action brought upon one or more of such claims, it may either file a verified cross–complaint in interpleader, admitting that it has no interest in the money or property claimed, or in only a portion thereof, and alleging that all or such portion is demanded by parties to such action, and apply to the court upon notice to such parties for an order to deliver such money or property or such portion thereof to such person as the court shall direct; or may bring a separate action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims. The action of interpleader may be maintained although the claims have not a common origin, are not identical but are adverse to and independent of one another, or the claims are unliquidated and no liability on the part of the party bringing the action or filing the cross–complaint has arisen. The applicant or interpleading party may deny liability in whole or in part to any or all of the claimants. The applicant or interpleading party may join as a defendant in such action any other party against whom claims are made by one or more of the claimants or such other party may interplead by cross–complaint; provided, however, that such claims arise out of the same transaction or occurrence.

 

CCP §386.5 provides:

Where the only relief sought against one of the defendants is the payment of a stated amount of money alleged to be wrongfully withheld, such defendant may, upon affidavit that he is a mere stakeholder with no interest in the amount or any portion thereof and that conflicting demands have been made upon him for the amount by parties to the action, upon notice to such parties, apply to the court for an order discharging him from liability and dismissing him from the action on his depositing with the clerk of the court the amount in dispute and the court may, in its discretion, make such order.

 

CCP §386.6 provides:

(a) A party to an action who follows the procedure set forth in Section 386 or 386.5 may insert in his motion, petition, complaint, or cross complaint a request for allowance of his costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred in such action. In ordering the discharge of such party, the court may, in its discretion, award such party his costs and reasonable attorney fees from the amount in dispute which has been deposited with the court. At the time of final judgment in the action the court may make such further provision for assumption of such costs and attorney fees by one or more of the adverse claimants as may appear proper.

2.             Motion to Sever or Bifurcate

a.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant moves to sever or bifurcate the trial.  He contends his affirmative defenses of statute of limitations, lack of subject matter jurisdiction, res judicata, and collateral estoppel must all be tried to the Court, rather than to the jury, and therefore the Court should try these issues first. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  She argues Defendant has not clearly stated what relief he seeks.  She contends the factual issues, including evidence and witnesses, will overlap between her case in chief and Defendant’s articulated affirmative defenses, such that bifurcation is not in the interest of judicial economy.  She contends there are factual issues relating to the legal defenses, and those factual issues must be determined by the jury. 

 

In reply, Defendant asks the Court to set a hearing date, in advance of the trial date, to decide the issues presented by way of the affirmative defenses.  He contends this will take less than a half day, and it is in the interest of justice to have these issues heard first. 

 

b.     Analysis

As Plaintiff correctly notes in opposition to the motion, the moving papers lack any discussion or the various factors to be considered by the Court when ruling on a motion to sever or bifurcate.  Defendant merely quotes CCP §§591, 597, 598, and 1048, and then concludes the Court should bifurcate the subject affirmative defenses. 

 

§591 does not provide for bifurcation, but rather merely makes clear that issues of law must be tried by the Court.  This does not mean, however, that the issues must be bifurcated, or must be tried first.  Each of the remaining sections provides for discretionary bifurcation.  The sections then set forth the criteria to be used in determining whether to bifurcate the action or not.

Additionally, the Court notes that Defendant, in reply, asks for a “hearing” in advance of the trial date to consider the affirmative defenses.  The Court knows of no authority permitting it to hold a trial by “hearing,” or to set a hearing date in advance of the currently scheduled trial date, which is 12/18/23.

Case Number: 23LBCV00319    Hearing Date: October 17, 2023    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jazikio Carter filed this action against Defendant, Johnnie Miller on 2/27/23.  Plaintiff filed his operative First Amended Complaint on 5/25/23.  The FAC includes a sole cause of action for breach of contract.  Plaintiff alleges he was renting an apartment from Defendant, and on 11/09/22, he called Defendant and informed him water had suddenly commenced leaking from his ceiling.  Defendant responded that he would have someone come out right away, but nobody came for 13 days, which resulted in damage to his clothing and furniture. 

 

  1. Demurrer to FAC

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares she mailed Plaintiff a letter detailing the deficiencies in the FAC.  The letter indicates Plaintiff’s FAC does not cure the deficiencies in the complaint, the parties are at an impasse, and Defendant will file a demurrer. 

 

This is not an attempt to meet and confer.  It is not by telephone or in person, as required by the Code.  It also merely states that the FAC is deficient and subject to a demurrer, and does not offer to meet and confer to discuss deficiencies or potential resolution of those deficiencies. 

 

The Court understands that Plaintiff is self-represented.  This does not, however, change the obligations to meet and confer in good faith pursuant to the Code. 

 

c.     Analysis

The Court is continuing the hearing to require the parties to meet and confer in good faith, as will be detailed below.  The Court offers the following guidance to the parties to assist in their meet and confer efforts.

 

Defendant’s contention is that Plaintiff has not alleged any details about the water leak, and has not alleged which portion of the parties’ contract was breached.  Additionally, Defendant contends the parties’ lease requires Plaintiff to have insurance for his personal belongings, because Defendant’s insurance does not cover Plaintiff’s possessions.

 

While Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege details about the leak and also does not allege which provision of the contract was breached, Plaintiff does highlight portions of the parties’ contract, which is attached as an exhibit to his FAC.  It appears Plaintiff is attempting to allege that the highlighted portions of the contract are relevant and/or form the basis of his allegation that Defendant breached the contract.  At ¶16 of the parties’ contract, which is highlighted by Plaintiff, the contract states:

Damage to Premises.  If the demised premises, or any part thereof, shall be partially damaged by fire or other casualty not due to Lessee’s negligence or willful act or that of his employee, family, agent, or visitor, the premises shall be promptly repaired by Lessor and there shall be an abatement of rent corresponding with the time during which, and the extent which, the leased premises may have been untenantable; but, if the lease promises should be damaged other than by Lessee’s negligence or willful act or that of his employee…to the extent that Lessor shall decide not to rebuild or repair, the term of this lease shall end and the rent shall be prorated up to the time of the damage.

 

Plaintiff clearly alleges a part of his premises were damaged due to an act outside of his control.  Plaintiff also clearly alleges Defendant did not repair the premises.  Plaintiff does not, however, seek rent abatement or other relief consistent with this provision of the contract.  If Plaintiff wishes to sue based on the alleged breach of this provision of the lease, Plaintiff must seek the damages contemplated by the lease agreement.

 

Defendant notes that the parties’ lease requires Plaintiff to carry personal property insurance and makes clear Defendant’s insurance does not cover personal property.  It does not seem this would bar a claim for negligence or breach of contract against Defendant if one were properly stated.  Nothing in the provision disclaims Defendant’s liability for his own negligence and/or breach of the parties’ lease. 

 

The parties must meet and confer, with the above guidance in mind, to determine whether Plaintiff can amend his FAC to state a claim for breach of contract or any other cause of action.  Plaintiff is admonished that Defendant’s attorney does not represent Plaintiff and need not assist Plaintiff in determining the proper basis for his claims against Defendant.  Plaintiff is advised that he should retain an attorney if he wishes to protect his interests in this litigation, as the Court notes that Defendant has numerous motions on calendar in the near future in this action, and rulings on those motions could seriously affect Plaintiff’s ability to prosecute his action against Defendant if Plaintiff does not retain an attorney to assist him. 

 

The hearing on the demurrer is continued to 12/12/23, to be heard concurrently with Defendant’s scheduled discovery motions.  The parties must meet and confer by telephone or in person within the next two weeks.  If the parties are unable to agree concerning the filing of a Second Amended Complaint to cure the FAC’s defects, they must each file briefs and declarations at least two weeks prior to the continued hearing date.  The declarations must detail their meet and confer efforts, and the briefs must address the legal issues remaining in dispute between the parties.

 

Additionally, the Court advances the 12/14/23 hearing on the motion to deem RFAs admitted to today’s date and continues it to 12/12/23, to be heard concurrently with the other discovery motions and the above-discussed demurrer.
