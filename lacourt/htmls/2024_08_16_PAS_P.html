DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV00927    Hearing Date: August 16, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO WRITTEN DISCOVERY AND TO HAVE MATTERS DEEMED ADMITTED AND DENYING REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This case arises out of a vehicle collision that resulted in the death of Santos Gabino Larrynega (“Decedent”). On October 27, 2022, Plaintiffs Francisca Gutierrez, individually and as successor to Decedent, along with Raul Lareinaga Gutierrez, and Saul Lareinaga Gutierrez (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants Manuel Reyes (“Reyes”), DLR Express, Inc., and Adrian De La Rosa, alleging causes of action for survival, and  wrongful death. The complaint alleges that while Decedent’s vehicle was proceeding straight within his lane, Defendant Reyes’ vehicle negligently and suddenly merged in front of Decedent’s vehicle, suddenly braked and stopped without signaling, and Decedent was unable to stop, and collided with Defendants’ vehicle. The complaint further alleges that Defendant DLR Express was Reyes’ employer, and De La Rosa owned the vehicle Reyes was operating.

          On June 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this motion to compel a response to written discovery from Defendant Reyes, for sanctions, and to have matter in request for admission deemed admitted.

          Defendant Reyes filed an opposition on August 6, 2024.

          Plaintiffs have not filed a reply as of August 15, 2024.  

          Because Defendant Reyes responded, the motion is denied.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

If a party to whom an inspection demand was directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses without objections. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b).) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)  

If the court finds that a party has unsuccessfully made or opposed such a motion, the court “shall impose a monetary sanction . . . unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) Sanctions may be awarded even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (CRC Rule 3.1348, subd. (a).

If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the requesting party may move for an order that the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court “shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing¿on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) It is “mandatory that the Court impose a monetary sanction…on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

  As a preliminary matter, Defendant’s motion is improper because it combines four separate motions to compel (1. Form Interrogatories; 2. Special Interrogatories; 3. Request for Production of Documents and 4. Request for Admission).  Multiple motions should not be combined in a single filing.  “Every direction of a court or judge, made or entered in writing, and not included in a judgment, is denominated an order. An application for an order is a motion.” (CCP § 1003.) Government Code section 70617 requires payment of a $60 filing fee for each motion filed with the Court. A party may not circumvent the Government Code with an omnibus filing. An additional filing fee is required before the Court may hear the motion.


 "In addition, Plaintiff filed a single motion for what should have been three separate motions. Combining discovery motions allows the moving party to avoid paying the requisite filing fees. Filing fees are not jurisdictional and it is mandatory for court clerks to demand and receive them.” (See Duran v. St. Luke’s Hospital (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 457, 460.)  This alone is sufficient basis to deny the instant motion.  (See also Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011) ("Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.")) 





  Plaintiffs served demands for production and requests for admission on Defendant Reyes’ counsel on April 22, 2024. (Walston Decl. ¶ 2; Exs. A and B.)  Defendant Reyes did not provide responses by the May 24, 2024 deadline. (Walston Decl. ¶ 3.)  Plaintiffs therefore seek an order an order to compel a response to written discovery from Defendant Reyes and to have the requests for admission deemed admitted.

          Defendant Reyes opposes the motion arguing that he served the outstanding responses on August 6, 2024, prior to the hearing on this motion. (Bridgman Decl. ¶ 3; Ex. 2.)  The Court therefore denies Plaintiffs’ motion as moot.

          The only remaining issue is sanctions. Plaintiffs request $1,750 in monetary sanctions against Defendant Reyes and/or his counsel under section 2033.280(c). (Walston Decl. ¶ 3.) This amount represents 1.5 hours drafting this motion, 1 hour in drafting a reply, and 1 hour in preparing for and attending the hearing at counsel’s hourly rate of $500. (Walston Decl. ¶ 3.)

The Court declines to award sanctions.  As noted above, Plaintiffs’ motion was improper and, therefore, it would be improper to award sanctions.  Furthermore, there was no meet and confer in this matter.  In his declaration, Mr. Bridgman states that Plaintiffs never attempted to meet and confer.  (Bridgman Decl. ¶ 10.)  Defendant does not appear to dispute this.  Since there was no meet and confer, Defendant had no opportunity to provide the requested discovery prior to the drafting of the motion.  

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiffs’ motion is denied.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING THE DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AND SECOND CAUSES OF ACTION IN THE VERIFIED SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

I.        INTRODUCTION

          In this real property boundary dispute, Plaintiffs Ralston C. Riggs and Tina M. Riggs allege that there is a dispute regarding the boundaries of their property located at 1190 Morada Place, Altadena, CA 91001, following a survey conducted by Defendant Petra Wennberg in 2019 but not disclosed to Plaintiffs until February 2022.

          The verified second amended complaint contains six causes of action: (1) Implied Agreement of Boundary Fence/Driveway; (2) Implied Agreement of Boundary Fence/Driveway (Estoppel); (3) Implied Easement; (4) Equitable Easement; (5) Declaratory Relief; and (6) Injunctive Relief.

          Defendant Wennberg filed this demurrer on May 6, 2024. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on August 5, 2024. Defendant Wennberg filed a reply on August 9, 2024.

Before the Court are Defendant Wennberg’s challenges to the first and second causes of action of the verified second amended complaint. Defendant Wennberg contends that both causes of action fail to state sufficient facts and/or are uncertain.

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to both causes of action.

II.      DEMURRER

A. Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. (CCP § 430.10(e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Aubry v. Tri-Defendants Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.) 

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.)

Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all the properly pleaded material facts and consider matters which may be judicially noticed, but we do not treat as admitted contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 958 [102 Cal.Rptr.3d 343].) Further, ‘ “ ‘we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.’ ”’ (Ibid.) Because a demurrer tests only the legal sufficiency of the pleading, we accept as true even the most improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff's ability to prove its factual allegations. (Ibid.) ‘“Facts appearing in exhibits attached to the first amended complaint also are accepted as true and are given precedence, to the extent they contradict the allegations.”’ (Paul v. Patton (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1091.) Although a demurrer does not ordinarily reach affirmative defenses, it ‘“will lie where the complaint ‘has included allegations that clearly disclose some defense or bar to recovery.’ ”’ (Casterson v. Superior Court (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 183, Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406. )

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. (CCP § 430.10(f).) Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

          B. Meet and Confer Requirement Met

          The Declaration of Osvaldo Rocha is offered in support of counsel’s compliance with Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.41. Rocha declares that he met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel via email on April 29, 2024 and May 6, 2024. (Rocha Decl. ¶ 4.)

The meet and confer requirement has not been met, as there is no indication that the parties met and conferred in person, by telephone, or by video conference. (CCP § 430.41(a).) Nevertheless, the Court may not overrule a demurrer for failure to adequately meet and confer. (Id., § 430.41(a)(4).) The Court will still consider the demurrer, but admonishes Defendant Wennberg to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure going forward.

          C. Demurrer

Defendants contend that the demurrer to the first and second causes of action should be sustained because they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain. (See Demurrer p. 3:5-14.)[1] The Court agrees. The second amended complaint is verified, which subjects it to additional restrictions when pleading. “ ‘A complaint may plead inconsistent causes of action [citations], although it be verified, if there are no contradictory or antagonistic facts [citation].’ [Citation.] Thus, ‘unless the alternate pleadings contain antagonistic statements, the statement of facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action in one count is not a bar to the maintenance of a separately stated count in the same pleading based upon inconsistent allegations.’ [Citation.]” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 944-945 (“Berman”).)

Plaintiffs allege in the first cause of action in the second amended complaint that the prior owners were uncertain of the true boundaries “when they built the Boundary Fences, the driveway access strip and the paved asphalt driveway serving the 1190 Lot.” (SAC, ¶ 27.) On the other hand, Plaintiffs allege in the second cause of action that the prior owners “knew they were not building the Boundary Fences, the concrete vehicle access strip, and the paved driveway serving the 1190 Lot on the 1190 Lot; and they knew that major parts of the Boundary Fences, and most of the concrete vehicle access strip and most of the paved driveway at the entrance and other places at the South end of the 1184 Lot were not on the 1190 Lot on the 1184 Lot… That knew that if the Boundary Fences were installed where the true boundary between the two lots was would effectively prevent any sale of the 1190 Lot.” (SAC, ¶ 40(a), emphasis in original.) These allegations directly conflict with each other, which is not permissible in a verified complaint. (Berman, supra,  56 Cal.App.4th at pp. 944-945; Faulkner v. California Toll Bridge Authority (1953) 40 Cal.2d 317, 328 (“Faulkner”), internal citations and quotation marks omitted [“Furthermore, although a plaintiff may plead inconsistent counts or causes of action in his complaint [citation] even where, as here, it be verified, if there are no contradictory or antagonistic facts… the rule does not permit the pleader to blow both hot and cold in the same complaint on the subject of facts of which he purports to speak with knowledge under oath.”])

The Court finds Plaintiffs’ various citations to Witkin regarding the criticisms of the rule set forth in Beatty v. Pacific States Savings & Loan Co. (1935) 4 Cal.App.3d 697 (“Beatty”), and later found in Berman and Faulkner, to be unavailing here because a treatise is not binding authority. (Ammerman v. Callender (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1087.) To the extent Plaintiffs rely on Tanforan v. Tanforan (1916) 173 Cal. 270 (“Tanforan”) for the common proposition that parties are generally free to plead inconsistent legal theories, Tanforan did not involve a verified complaint. (See generally, 183 Cal. 270.) Faulkner, on the other hand, is California Supreme Court precedent issued almost 40 years after Tanforan that deals directly with the issue of inconsistent allegations in a verified complaint and incorporates the rule set forth in Beatty. (Faulkner, supra, 40 Cal.2d at p. 328.) The other authorities Plaintiffs rely on, namely Adams v. Paul (1995) 11 Cal.4th 593 and Walton v. Walton (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 292, do not address inconsistent allegations vis-à-vis verified complaints, nor do they address the holding of Beatty found in Faulkner, Berman, and other cases. Plaintiffs cited no other controlling authority in their opposition on that this issue.

Notwithstanding the foregoing, the Court will permit Plaintiffs one more opportunity to amend. The Court notes that Defendant Wennberg’s moving papers’ only real issue was the inconsistent allegations and did not address any other defects in the second amend complaint.[2] Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the first and second cause of action with leave to amend.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant Wennberg’s demurrer is sustained to the first and second causes of action with leave to amend.

Plaintiffs Ralston C. Riggs and Tina M. Riggs are given 30 days to amend the second amended complaint.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On April 5, 2023, Plaintiffs Luxor Properties, Inc., and Hooman Dayani, as Trustee of the Dayani Family United Trust filed an unlawful detainer action against Defendant Che Bri Inc. Agency Incorporated dba My Place Cafe.

The FAC further alleges that on June 27, 2023, the date set for trial of this action on the issue of possession, Defendant surrendered possession of the Premises. Plaintiffs allege that because Defendant held over without payment after March 31, 2023, Defendant owes $150 per day as the reasonable rental value of the premises from April 1, 2023 through June 27, 2023 in the amount of $13,200. Plaintiffs allege they are owed $51,742.

On October 30, 2023, Defendant Che Bri Inc. Agency Incorporated (Cross-Complainant) filed a cross-complaint against Ayodele Henderson and Deanna Henderson, Successor Trustees of the Jeanette McDonald Henderson Living Trust Dated August 28, 2008 (Henderson Cross-Defendants), and Roes 1-10, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and (3) declaratory relief.

The cross-complaint alleges that Cross-Complainant entered into a lease agreement with the Henderson Cross-Defendants to rent the property in Pasadena, and in March of 2020, shortly after opening the cafe´, the Covid-19 pandemic began. Cross-Complainant was forced to close the cafe´ during the months of April, May, June and July because of the pandemic. Cross-Complainant alleges that during the above-referenced months, Cross-Complainant’s rent was waived by the Henderson Cross-Defendants, by verbal agreement. In the months that followed, Cross-Complainant was informed by the Henderson Cross-Defendants that a lower monthly rental amount could be paid and that amount would depend on the sales of the cafe´. Cross-Complainant claims that in reliance on these statements, Cross-Complainant paid a lower rental amount each month, which varied depending on the cafe´’s sales.

Beginning in 2022, Cross-Complainant alleges it resumed making full rental payments to Cross-Defendant.

In August of 2022 the property was sold by the Henderson Cross-Defendants to Luxor Properties, Inc., and Homayoon Dayani, As Trustee of The Dayani Family United Trust (Luxor Cross-Defendants). Cross-Complainant alleges that the Henderson Cross-Defendants failed to inform Luxor of the rent which was waived by the Henderson Cross-Defendants and the Covid relief which was applied for.

Cross-Complainant alleges that in violation of the promises and obligations under the Agreement and the subsequent verbal agreements made by Cross-Complainant and the Henderson Cross-Defendants, Cross-Complainant was entitled to remain on the Property without fear of an Unlawful Detainer action.

On February 7, 2024, Cross-Complainant dismissed the Henderson Cross-Defendants from the cross-complaint.

On February 8, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed an amendment to the cross-complaint, substituting for Roe 1 the Luxor Cross-Defendants.

Before the court is a demurrer with motion to strike the cross-complaint filed by the Luxor Cross-Defendants. The demurrer is sustained with 30 days to leave as the cross-complaint combines the allegations of all the defendants together and it is therefore uncertain; further, the amendment is improper insofar as one Roe was substituted for two cross-defendants. As the demurrer is sustained, the motion to strike is moot.

II.  DEMURRER

A.   Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. (CCP § 430.10(e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Aubry v. Tri-Defendants Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.)¿ 

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.) 

 

Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)¿ 

 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all the properly pleaded material facts and consider matters which may be judicially noticed, but we do not treat as admitted contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 958 [102 Cal.Rptr.3d 343].) Further, ‘ “ ‘we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.’ ”’ (Ibid.) Because a demurrer tests only the legal sufficiency of the pleading, we accept as true even the most improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff's ability to prove its factual allegations. (Ibid.) ‘“Facts appearing in exhibits attached to the first amended complaint also are accepted as true and are given precedence, to the extent they contradict the allegations.”’ (Paul v. Patton (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1091.) Although a demurrer does not ordinarily reach affirmative defenses, it ‘“will lie where the complaint ‘has included allegations that clearly disclose some defense or bar to recovery.’ ”’ (Casterson v. Superior Court (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 183, Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.) 

 

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. (CCP § 430.10(f).) Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

B.    Meet and Confer Requirement

 

The Declaration of Krause-Leemon is offered in support of counsel’s compliance with Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.41 and 435.5. Krause-Leemon declares that he met and conferred with Cross-Complainant’s counsel by telephone on March 7, 2024. (Krause-Leemon Decl., ¶ 3.)  

 

The meet and confer requirement has been met.  

 

C.    Demurrer 

The Luxor Cross-Defendants demur to the cross-complaint arguing that the first through third causes of action for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and declaratory relief fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

 

1.     Amendments to Cross-Complaint

 

The Luxor Cross-Defendants argue Cross-Complainant cannot substitute Cross-Defendants in place of a fictiously named cross-defendant because Cross-Complainant was aware of Landlords’ identities at the time as they are the Plaintiffs in this matter. Thus, Cross-Defendant argues the amendment was not proper.

“When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint, or the affidavit if the action is commenced by affidavit, and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly . . . .”  (CCP § 474.)   

Ignorance of the name of a defendant has not been interpreted literally.  (Optical Surplus, Inc. v. Superior Court (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 776, 783.)  “Ignorance” may include situations where the plaintiff knew the identity of the person but was ignorant of the facts giving him a cause of action or knew the name and all the facts but was unaware that the law gave him a cause of action.  (Ibid.)  Ignorance of the facts is the critical issue.  (Ibid.)  The question of the plaintiff's good faith in this regard is for the determination of the trial court.  (Ibid.) 

In opposition, Cross-Complainant argues that it became aware of the agreement between the Hendersons and Luxor after it filed its cross-complaint.

Here, while the cross-complaint identifies the Luxor Cross-Defendants by name, “ignorance” may include situations where the plaintiff knew the identity of the person but was ignorant of the facts giving him a cause of action or knew the name and all the facts but was unaware that the law gave him a cause of action.  (Ibid.) As the Luxor Defendants note in their demurrer and motion to strike, the cross-complaint does not contain any allegations against them. Whether the plaintiff had knowledge of sufficient facts at the time of filing is generally a question of fact. (McOwen v. Grossman (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 937, 945.) If the cross-complaint does not contain any allegations against them, their basis for liability has not been stated either. As a result, demurring on this basis is inappropriate as it requires a review of the evidence, which is not allowing on a demurrer.

Moreover, the cross-complaint alleges that Cross-Defendant ROES 1 through 10 are sued under fictitious names because their true names and capacities are unknown to Cross-Complainant. For the purpose of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleading in the complaint are assumed to be true.  (See Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966.)  To defeat the amendment, the burden is on Luxor to prove Cross-Complainant’s earlier awareness of its identity and facts creating its liability.  (See Fara Estates Homeowners Ass’n v. Fara Estates, Ltd. (9th Cir. 1998) 134 F.3d 377, 377 (applying California law); Breceda v. Gamsby (1968) 267 Cal.App.2d 167, 179.) Cross-Defendants have failed to provide any judicially noticeable documents or point to any allegations within the cross-complaint that prove Cross-Complainant had knowledge of the facts or law giving Cross-Complainant a cause of action at the time it filed the cross-complaint.   

However, Cross-Defendants also argue that Cross-Complainant improperly seeks to substitute two named parties – Luxor and Dayani – into one fictitiously named cross-defendant. This should be corrected. The demurrer is sustained on this basis.

          Cross-Defendants also argue that Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30 required Cross-Complainant to file its cross-complaint at the time Cross-Complainant filed its answer. Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30, properly applied, should be used to prevent rather than cause a forfeiture of claims.  (See, e.g., McKee v. Mires (1952) 110 Cal.App.2d 517, 523 (“greatest liberality is to be encouraged in the allowance of [bringing causes of action] in order to see that justice is done”); see also Silver Orgs. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98-99 (section 426.30 must be liberally construed to avoid forfeiture of causes of action); see also Foot's Transfer & Storage Co. v. Superior Court (1980) 114 Cal.App.3d 897, 901 (Foot’s Transfer) (“the long-standing rule favor[s] the allowance of amended pleadings”).) 

While Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30 employs the mandatory word "shall" with respect to allowing an amendment of a pleading or the filing of a cross-complaint, it also provides that the court shall grant leave to a party to amend his pleading or to file a cross-complaint "if the party who failed to plead the cause acted in good faith." 

 

          As a result, the demurrer is overruled on this basis.

 

2.     Failure to State Claims and Uncertainty

The Luxor Cross-Defendants also argue that all Cross-Complainant’s claims are about the Henderson’s. They argue that when Roe 1 is read into the cross-complaint, the allegations make no sense. For example, if Cross-Defendants are Roe 1, then the cross-complaint alleges that they entered into a contract for the lease of the property in 2020, but the cross-complaint then inconsistently alleges that Luxor did not even own the premises until August of 2022. (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 8, 17.)

In opposition, Cross-Complainant argues that the cross-complaint alleges that the Luxor Cross-Defendants entered into a purchase agreement with the Hendersons whereby they would assume the assets, liability and all contractual obligations incurred by the Hendersons.

The cross-complaint fails to allege what Cross-Complainant alleges in its opposition. Upon careful review of the cross-complaint, the court agrees that the cross-complaint fails to state sufficient facts against Cross-Defendants and is uncertain as against the Luxor Cross-Defendants. The cross-complaint combines the allegations against all Cross-Defendants and fails to clarify the theory of liability against the Luxor Cross-Defendants. A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing that defendant cannot tell how he or she is supposed to respond. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139.) The cross-complaint is entirely confusing for the reasons the Luxor Cross-Defendants argue and more. The only allegation specifically against the Luxor Defendants is that they purchased the subject property in August of 2022 and they sued when Cross-Complainant did not pay rent. It is unclear how they breached any agreement and whether it was the oral agreement to waive the rent or the original lease agreement. Thus, the Luxor Cross-Defendant cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated. (Khoury, supra, 14 Cal.App.4th at 616.)

As such, the demurrer is sustained in its entirety, with 30 days leave to amend.

          III.     MOTION TO STRIKE 

A.   Legal Standard 

 

Code Civ. Proc. §436 provides: “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: 

(a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. 

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”        

B.    Motion to Strike

As the demurrer is sustained the motion to strike is denied as moot.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Cross-Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED with 30 days leave to amend. The motion to strike is denied as moot.

The motion to advance the hearing on the motion for summary judgment is granted as the motion was timely filed and served, and a trial court may not refuse to hear a timely filed motion for summary judgment.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1335 states as follows:  

 

(a) Noticed motion or application required 

A party seeking to advance, specially set, or reset a case for trial must make this request by noticed motion or ex parte application under the rules in chapter 4 of this division. 

(b) Grounds for motion or application 

The request may be granted only upon an affirmative showing by the moving party of good cause based on a declaration served and filed with the motion or application. 

 

            III.      DISCUSSION

            Defendant R. Torosian (Defendant) moves to advance the hearing on his Motion for Summary Judgment (MSJ), which is currently scheduled for October 17, 2024, to a date before and closer to the current October 8, 2024 trial date. Defendant argues that there is good cause to advance because he timely filed and personally served his motion on June 25, 2024, which was 105 days before trial, and a court cannot refuse to hear a timely filed MSJ. Defendant argues he was unable to obtain a hearing date 30 days before the trial date since there were no open dates on the court’s online reservation system. (Hill Decl., para. 9; Ex. A-F.)

A party may move for summary judgment “at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).)  Notice of the motion and supporting papers must be served on all other parties at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.  (Id., subd. (a)(2).)  The motion must be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.  (Id., subd. (a)(3).)   

 

“The importance of providing the minimum statutory notice of a summary judgment hearing cannot be overemphasized.”  (Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1262 (Robinson).)  “Because it is potentially case dispositive and usually requires considerable time and effort to prepare, a summary judgment motion is perhaps the most important pretrial motion in a civil case.”  (Ibid.)  “Therefore, the Legislature was entitled to conclude that parties should be afforded a minimum notice period for the hearing of summary judgment motions so that they have sufficient time to assemble the relevant evidence and prepare an adequate opposition.”  (Ibid.)  Thus, without the parties’ consent, “in light of the express statutory language, trial courts do not have authority to shorten the minimum notice period for summary judgment hearings.”  (Ibid.)   

 

 Here, the MSJ was filed and personally served on June 25, 2024. 105 days (75 days plus 30 days) from June 25, 2024 would be October 8, 2024, the date of trial here. As such, the MSJ was timely filed and served.  It is well established that a court may not refuse to hear a summary judgment motion that is timely filed.¿ (Wells Fargo Bank v. Superior Court¿(1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 918, 919¿[“We are asked to determine whether the trial court may refuse to hear a summary judgment motion filed within the time limits of Code of Civil Procedure section 437c.¿We determine it may not”]; Sentry Ins. Co. v. Superior Court¿(1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 526, 530¿[“We are sympathetic to the problems the trial courts experience in calendaring and hearing the many motions for summary judgment. However, the solution to these problems cannot rest in a refusal to hear timely motions.”].)¿¿¿¿ 

Plaintiff takes issue with the way the notice dates are calculated, arguing that the 75 days and 30 days should not be combined into 105 days before trial, but that the court must count backwards 30 days from the trial date, which lands on a Sunday, and thus, the date would fall on the Friday before. Then, Plaintiff argues the court should count 75 days from that Friday. However, the court in Cole v. Superior Court (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 84, 87, and Wells Fargo, supra, 206 Cal.App.3d 918 combined the dates this way in its calculation as well.

Plaintiff also argues that the notice Defendant provided is invalid, and thus, a new 75-day notice period is required. As such, Plaintiff argues that the court lacks authority to grant Defendant relief or to reset the hearing date as there is not enough time before trial. Plaintiff cites to Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1268 for the proposition that it has been held that for the motion to be valid, the court must make the good cause finding before the motion is filed.  The court in Robinson stated: “[d]efendants noticed their motion for hearing within 30 days of the trial date without first obtaining a determination of good cause from the trial court. [Citation.] Unless and until the trial court found good cause, the notice of the hearing was invalid. . .” However, the inverse is true here. Defendants did not notice their motion within the 30 days of trial. The motion is noticed outside the 30 days, and they are asking for a good cause determination here.

Plaintiff also argues that personal service of the motion was made on Tabatabai on June 26, 2024. However, all Plaintiffs were represented by Okorocha, who was personally served on June 25, 2024. It was not until after personal service on Plaintiffs’ Counsel was completed, that Okorocha was substituted out. Further, Defendant served the MSJ by personal service, and electronic service does not come into play.

“The fact remains that the motion was timely filed, and calendaring issues are not a basis on which the trial court can refuse to hear a timely filed summary judgment motion, absent an indication that it was defective under section 437c.” (Cole, supra, 87 Cal.App.5th at 88.)

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The motion to advance the hearing of the motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.
