DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV41315    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Thursday, May 9, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   19STCV41315

CASE NAME:                        Sean Michaels, et al. v. Christopher M. Stevens, et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendants Christopher M. Stevens and the Law Office of Christopher M. Stevens

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiffs Sean Michaels and PB Consulting, LLC

TRIAL DATE:                        17 September 2024

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer with Motion to Strike FAC

OPPOSITION:                        26 April 2024

REPLY:                                  2 May 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Stevens Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. The motion to strike is denied as moot. Plaintiffs are granted 30 days leave to amend. Defendants to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On November 15, 2019, Sean Michaels (“Michaels”) and PB Consulting, LLC (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Christopher M. Stevens; the Law Office of Christopher M. Stevens (collectively “Stevens”); LKP Global Law (“LKP”) and Does 1 to 10.)

 

The Complaint alleges a single cause of action for Professional Negligence. According to the Complaint, in 2015 Michaels retained LKP and later, Stevens, to represent him in a dispute against his business partner Daniel Cooper (“Cooper”) regarding the business entity Reliant Life Shares, LLC (“Reliant”) in an action entitled Reliant Life Shares, LLC v. Daniel B. Cooper, et al. (LASC Case No. BC604858) (the “Underlying Action”).

 

On April 16, 2021, this court granted a stay of this action pending completion of the appeal of the Underlying Action. On April 4, 2023, the Second District issued a published opinion, Reliant Life Shares, LLC v. Cooper (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 14.

 

On December 27, 2023, Defendant LKP filed a Motion for Judgment of Pleadings (MJOP) in which Defendants argued Plaintiffs were collaterally estopped from bringing this action against LKP. Plaintiffs opposed the Motion. On January 25, 2024, the court granted the MJOP with leave to amend.

 

On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging a single cause of action for professional negligence. The Stevens Defendants now demurrer and move to strike the FAC. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

Demurrer with a motion to strike fAC[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP § 430.30(a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿ 

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

¿Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿

III.      Discussion

 

At the January 25, 2024 MJOP hearing, the court agreed that the Plaintiffs were collaterally estopped from basing their malpractice claim on the findings of alter ego liability because the appellate court in Reliant Life Shares, LLC v. Cooper (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 14 affirmed the trial court in the Underlying Action (i.e., Reliant Life Shares, LLC v. Daniel B. Cooper, et al. (LASC Case No. BC604858)] and its related cross-complaint), wherein Plaintiffs were found to have engaged in contemptible conduct by improperly withholding and diverting money owed to a rightful member of a limited liability company, Daniel Cooper (“Cooper”).

 

Therefore, because the trial court and the Court of Appeal in the Underlying Action found that the Plaintiffs were alter-egos of the parties, the fact that Defendant LKP did not obtain conflict waivers or develop separate theories of defense for the Plaintiffs that were separate and apart from the other parties, is insufficient to establish breach and proximate causation to support a professional negligence cause of action. Moreover, the Complaint is devoid of facts as to what negligent acts or omissions LKP failed to do that would have precluded the finding of alter-ego liability in the underlying action.

 

The Complaint further fails to state what specific law and motion LKP failed to bring that would have reduced or eliminated the claims Cooper brought against the Plaintiffs. The Complaint fails to state what specific defenses existed that were separate and apart from the defenses to be used by Reliant and Grady. The Complaint also fails to state what early resolution LKP failed to seek that was separate and apart from the resolution that benefitted Reliant and Grady. Lastly, the Complaint fails to state what efforts Plaintiff Michaels could have taken to safeguard his personal assets from the imposition of a constructive trust. In this manner, Plaintiffs’ Complaint is conclusory, and thus deficient because it is devoid of facts. (See Interior Systems, Inc. v. Del E. Webb Corp. (1981) 121 Cal.App.3d 312, 31 [“These are but legal conclusions which the trial court was authorized to ignore conclusory allegations without facts to support them are ambiguous and may be disregarded”].)

 

(1/25/2024 Order.)

                         

The Stevens Defendants now demur to the FAC on the basis that the FAC remains too conclusory and fails to state sufficient facts to support a cause of action for professional negligence against the Stevens Defendants and that Plaintiff is collaterally barred by re-litigating alter ego issues.

 

“The elements of a cause of action in tort for professional negligence are: the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; a breach of that duty; a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.” (Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064, 1066–1067.)

 

Plaintiffs assert that the allegations against the Stevens Defendants are not on alter-ego issues but on specific acts and omissions of and by Stevens and his law firm as set forth in Paragraph 24 of the FAC. (FAC, ¶¶ 24(c), (i)-(n), (p)-(r).) The specific allegations against the Stevens Defendants are as follows:

 

  • Defendants Stevens and LKP failed to enforce the December 18, 2017 Settlement Agreement which would have resulted in Cooper dismissing certain intentional torts against Michaels without Prejudice. (FAC ¶ 24(c), (k)(l).) Because Plaintiff did not engage in any further intentional torts, Cooper could not have renewed his tort claims against Michaels. (FAC ¶ 24(c).) “Thus, had Defendants sought to enforce the settlement agreement reached between Michaels and Cooper on December 18, 2017, Cooper would be unable to proceed with his intentional tort claims against Michaels, which subsequently caused the trial court to issue a judgment of alter ego against Plaintiffs.” (FAC ¶ 24(c).)

 

  • Defendant Stevens surprised Plaintiff with his departure from LKP and filed a substitution of attorney only one to two weeks in advance of the phase one trial, depriving Plaintiff of the opportunity to seek or retain independent counsel. (FAC ¶ 24(i)-(j).)

 

  • “Defendant Stevens never prepared Plaintiff for any of my trial testimony or cross examination at any time prior to and during phase one of the trial.” (FAC 24(m).)

 

  • “None of the defenses presented by Defendant Stevens during phase one of the trial were focused on Plaintiff, but instead were all directed at defending RLS in connection with RLS’s termination of Daniel Cooper.” (FAC 24(n).)

 

  • Defendant Stevens failed to oppose motions in limine filed by Dan Cooper’s counsel in connection with phase two of the trial[.]” (FAC ¶ 24(q).)

 

  • “Defendant Stevens failed to immediately provide and organized and complete litigation file to Plaintiffs new counsel, that Plaintiffs were forced to retain, and as of the filing of this action has continued to fail to produce necessary and available document vital to Plaintiffs defense of phase two of this action.” (FAC ¶ 24(r).

 

“[I]n a legal malpractice action, whether a court or jury decides the issue of causation ‘does not turn on the identity or expertise of the trier of fact, but whether the issues are predominately questions of fact or law.’ ” (Mireskandari v. Edwards Wildman Palmer LLP (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 247, 265 (Mireskandari).)  Plaintiffs assert that had the Stevens Defendants moved to enforce the December 18, 2017 Settlement Agreement, “Daniel Cooper would be unable to proceed with his intentional tort claims against Michaels, which subsequently resulted in the entry of a judgment against Plaintiffs.” (Opp. at pp. 6:27-7:1.) Plaintiffs want the court to presume that if the intentional torts against Plaintiff Michaels were dismissed in the Underlying Action pursuant to the settlement agreement, there would have been no judgment against Plaintiffs. But the court cannot ignore that Plaintiff Michael’s liability rested on other causes of action, not just the intentional torts Copper alleged against him.

 

Phase one of the trial in the Underlying Action focused on the Plaintiffs’ request for declaratory relief as to whether the ouster of Cooper was proper and Cooper’s claims for accounting and constructive trust. (Reliant Life Shares, supra, 90 Cal.App.5th at p. 18.) The trial court found that the ouster of Cooper from the LLC was improper and “ordered an accounting, and ultimately imposed a constructive trust over certain assets to compensate Cooper for millions of dollars wrongfully transferred from the LLC to Michaels and Grady.” (Id. at p. 19.) Phase two of the trial focused on “Cooper's claims against Michaels and Grady for breach of contract, fraud, breach of the duty of loyalty, aiding and abetting breaches of common law duty (as to Grady), and fraudulent transfer.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiffs fail to explain how the dismissal of the constructive trust claim would have prevented the trial court in the Underlying Action from finding alter ego liability and precluded judgment against Plaintiffs despite the existence of other common law claims asserted against Plaintiffs by Cooper. For this reason, the court agrees that Plaintiff has failed to show that Defendants' failure to enforce the Settlement Agreement proximately caused Plaintiffs to be damaged. Plaintiffs need to allege facts that show how the dismissal of the constructive trust claim from the Underlying Action would have prevented Plaintiffs from suffering “loss of use of said assets, ongoing attorney fee amounts, exposure of his personal property[.]” (FAC ¶ 34.)

 

Second, the FAC fails to allege facts to show that had the constructive trust claim against Plaintiff Michaels been dismissed, this would have precluded the trial court in the Underlying Action from finding alter ego liability as to Plaintiffs. “An alter ego defendant has no separate primary liability to the plaintiff. Rather, plaintiff's claim against the alter ego defendant is identical with that claimed by plaintiff against the already-named defendant.” (Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1358.) The FAC fails to allege that if the intentional tort claims against Plaintiff Michaels had been dismissed, the trial court in the Underlying Action would not have found alter ego liability. Alter ego is a procedure that allows the court to disregard the corporate entity and hold the alter ego individuals liable for the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is used to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, and promote injustice. (Id. at p. 1359.) Here, the FAC fails to allege facts to show that Plaintiffs could have avoided the judgment in the underlying action but for the Defendants conduct.

 

In other words, Plaintiffs fail to allege specific facts to show how the other allegations outlined in Paragraph 24 proximately caused Plaintiffs’ damages. “Proximate cause involves two elements.” (PPG Industries, Inc. v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 310, 315 [italics original] (PPG Industries).) One element is “cause in fact,” “sometimes referred to as ‘but for’ causation.” (State Dept. of State Hospitals v. Superior Court (2015) 61 Cal.4th 339, 352 [internal citations and quotations omitted] (State Dept. of State Hospitals).) “An act is a cause in fact if it is a necessary antecedent of an event.” (PPG Industries, at p. 315.)

The second aspect of proximate cause “ focuses on public policy considerations. Because the purported [factual] causes of an event may be traced back to the dawn of humanity, the law has imposed additional ‘limitations on liability other than simple causality.’ [Citation.] ‘These additional limitations are related not only to the degree of connection between the conduct and the injury, but also with public policy.’ [Citation.] Thus, ‘proximate cause ‘is ordinarily concerned, not with the fact of causation, but with the various considerations of policy that limit an actor's responsibility for the consequences of his conduct.’ [Citations]

 

(State Dept. of State Hospitals, at p. 353.)

The FAC fails to allege specific facts as to how the Stevens Defendants presenting a substitution of attorney form one to two weeks from Phase one of the trial in the Underlying Action proximately caused Plaintiffs’ damages. (FAC ¶ 24(i)-(j).) How did Defendant Stevens' failure to prepare Plaintiff for his testimony or cross-examination proximately cause the Plaintiffs’ damages? (FAC ¶ 24(m).) What specific defenses focused on Plaintiff Michaels should Defendant Stevens have advanced that would have prevented the Plaintiffs’ damages? (FAC ¶ 24(n).) What specific evidence did Defendant Stevens fail to present that would have prevented the court from finding alter-ego liability? (FAC ¶ 24(o).) How did Defendant Stevens fail to represent Plaintiffs’ interest and how did this failure proximately cause Plaintiff’s damages? (FAC ¶ 24(p).) What specific motions in limine did Defendant Stevens fail to oppose that would have prevented Plaintiff’s damages? (FAC ¶ 24(q).) How did Defendant Stevens's failure to “immediately provide and organized and complete litigation file to Plaintiffs’ new counsel” proximately cause Plaintiff’s damages? (FAC ¶ 24(r).)

 

“‘The purpose of the causation requirement is to safeguard against speculative and conjectural claims and to ensure that damages awarded for the attorney's malpractice actually have been caused by the malpractice.’ ” (Mireskandari, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at p. 265.) “ ‘A plaintiff cannot recover damages based upon speculation or even a mere possibility that the wrongful conduct of the defendant caused the harm. ’ ” (Ibid.) As Plaintiff has failed to allege facts that show proximate causation, the court sustains the Stevens Defendants’ demurrer with leave to amend.

           

As the demurrer has been sustained, the motion to strike is denied as moot.

 

Conclusion

 

Stevens Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend. The motion to strike is denied as moot. Plaintiffs are granted 30 days leave to amend.
