Case Number: 20STCV23943    Hearing Date: October 13, 2023    Dept: 31

DEPT:  

 

31 

OSC DATE: 

 

10/13/2023 

CASE NAME/NUMBER: 

 

20STCV23943 EVELIA CAMPUZANO vs ELANI EXPRESS, INC., et al. 

REQUEST FOR COURT JUDGMENT AGAINST [DEFAULTING PARTY]: 

 

  1. Elani Express, Inc.; and  

  1. Yeghish Manukyan 

RECOMMENDATION: 

 

GRANT in the following amounts: 

Principal damages: $82,928 

TOTAL: $82,928 

 

 

TENTATIVE 

 

Plaintiff seeks $20,732 in special damages and $100,000 in general damages, for the principal sum of $120,732, against Defendants Elani Express, Inc. and Yeghish Manukyan, jointly and severally.  

 

Plaintiff avers she suffered severe back, neck, and shoulder pain from the motor vehicle incident, wherein she received physical therapy, radiological studies, and epidural injections. Plaintiff provides she incurred medical expenses of $20,732 and sustained $3,039.51 in property damage.  

 

Plaintiff submits sufficient evidence to support the claim for $20,732 in special damages. Plaintiff further provides records for property damage. However, Plaintiff does not seek property damage as part of her special damages computation in CIV-100 and JUD-100, and neither is property damage indicated on the Statement of Damages. Therefore, no claim for property damage will be awarded.   

 

As for general damages, Plaintiff declares she suffers from discomforts and daily limitations, in addition to loss of enjoyment of life. However, the records Plaintiff provides do not evidence the extent of Plaintiff’s averred pain and suffering. The radiological impressions from 2018 indicate that Plaintiff’s shoulder has mild degenerative changes, that her lumbar spine has mild loss of lordotic curvature with degenerative changes noted at L5-S1 level, and that her cervical lordotic curvature was likely due to muscle spasm with degenerative changes. Furthermore, Plaintiff’s thoracic spine demonstrated no evidence of compression fracture, and degenerative changes were seen with marginal osteophytes. 

 

As of December 12, 2018, Plaintiff complained of slight insomnia due to her right shoulder, with moderate right shoulder pain and stiffness, and moderate back pain and stiffness. Plaintiff provides no records outside of the year of the injury, and thus there is no support for the claims of severe emotional distress and psychological disorders arising from the incident, or of lingering injuries to the extent claimed. 

 

Given the absence of supporting documentation, the Court will reduce Plaintiff’s request for general damages of $100,000 to $62,196.¿¿ 

 

The request for default judgment submitted on June 21, 2023 is GRANTED for the total amount of $82,928 against Defendants Elani Express, Inc. and Yeghish Manukyan, jointly and severally.¿¿¿ 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.¿¿¿¿

BC657226 MARVIS DAVIS VS SHAUN MICHAEL HUTTAR 

REQUEST FOR COURT JUDGMENT AGAINST [DEFAULTING PARTY]: 

 

SHAUN MICHAEL HUTTAR 

RECOMMENDATION: 

 

DENY for reasons stated below, and dismissal of action. 

 

TENTATIVE 

 

The request for default judgment submitted on June 21, 2023 is DENIED for the following reasons: 

 

The Court previously noted in the previous default judgment denials, on July 27, 2022 and on December 16, 2022, that Plaintiff must submit evidence demonstrating proper service of the Statement of Damages on Defendant Shaun Michael Huttar (“Defendant”) prior to entry of default.  

 

In this fourth default judgment request, Plaintiff’s counsel (“Counsel”) declares that he served the Statement of Damages upon the Defendant prior to entry of default on November 18, 2019. (Wolfson Decl. ¶24; Exh, 6.) This Statement of Damages, dated October 29, 2019, was mailed via USPS to the address 422 Warren Lane, Inglewood, CA 90302 (“Warren Address”) on October 29, 2019. However, there is no indication Defendant received this mail, especially since Plaintiff filed an application for publication thereafter. The application for publication provided that the process server found Defendant no longer lived at the Warren Address, and that a skip trace found a second address in Los Angeles, California, where Defendant only had rented a room and had already moved out. (Appl. Publ., May 23, 2019.) Thereafter, a private investigator was unsuccessful in locating Defendant. (Ibid.) 

 

In personal injury and wrongful death cases, a plaintiff must serve a statement of damages setting forth the nature and amount of damages to be sought before default may be taken. (Code Civ. Proc., section 425.11.) The purpose of the statement of damages is to ensure that a defendant who declines to contest an action does not thereby subject himself to open ended liability. (Schwab v. Rondel Homes, Inc. (1991) 53 Cal.3d 428, 435 (quoting Greenup v. Rodman (1986) 42 Cal.3d 822, 826.) The Court finds that Defendant did not receive proper notice of the Statement of Damages served via mail on October 29, 2019 at the Warren Address, because as of March 9, 2018, Plaintiff’s process server already declared that the Warren Address was a “bad address per Joseph Matthew Pare, the current occupant, who moved in March 2018. He believes subject could have been the previous tenant but does not personally know the subject.” (Appl. Publ., May 23, 2019 at p. 9; Canas Decl.) The purported service of the Statement of Damages on October 29, 2019 is invalid. 

 

The Court then turns to Plaintiff’s service of the Statement of Damages by publication. (Wolfson Decl. ¶26; Exh. 8.) However, the affidavit of publication, indicating service on August 14, 2022, August 21, 2022, August 28, 2022, and September 4, 2022, is after Plaintiff obtained Defendant’s entry of default on November 18, 2019. A statement of damages must be served on a defendant before default is entered by the Court Clerk. (Hamm v. Elkin (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 1343, 1345-46). A default judgment is void if the required statement of damages was not served on the defendant before the default was taken. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert, supra, 196 Cal.App.4th at p. 1521, 127 Cal.Rptr.3d 542.) Formal notice “is an essential prerequisite to a valid default judgment.” (Engebretson & Co. v. Harrison (1981) 125 Cal.App.3d 436, 443-444, 178 Cal.Rptr. 77 [mail service constituted inadequate notice].) Accordingly, the default against Defendant is VOID.  

 

Aside from the default being void, the Court further notes the Statement of Damages served by publication provide notice of the following special damages: medical expenses ($17,500), prescriptions and walking device ($1,147), future medical expenses (knee replacement) ($53,750), and vehicle personal property damage ($1,502).  

 

However, the special damages sought in Plaintiff’s request for default judgment exceeds the Statement of Damages. The amount sought cannot exceed the amount pled in the operative complaint/cross-complaint or set forth in the statement of damages. (Code Civ. Proc., § 580(a); Airs Aromatics, LLC v. CBL Data Recovery Technologies, Inc. (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 1013, 1018.) It appears that Plaintiff changed the breakdown of Plaintiff’s damages from the Statement of Damages served by publication. The “future medical expenses (knee replacement)” on the Statement of Damages is now demarcated into two separate items, one for “Surgery and Rehab Right Knee” and the other for “Future Medical Surgery and Rehab Left Knee,” both seeking $50,500 as to each. Plaintiff may not recover an amount in excess of the damages noticed to Defendant. The served Statement of Damages appears to provide notice of only a single knee replacement, and not for a double knee replacement at double the noticed cost.  

 

Lastly, the Court previously remarked on December 16, 2022 that Plaintiff’s counsel does not establish personal knowledge of Plaintiff’s medical bills to be able to authenticate the medical bills. (Min. Order, Dec. 16, 2022.) Plaintiff must authenticate his medical bills. (Ibid.) However, Plaintiff’s counsel again submits unauthenticated medical records in his own declaration. Plaintiff’s counsel is neither an authorized custodian of the records nor a witness with personal knowledge of the damages suffered to attest that the medical bills are true and correct bills and services rendered to Plaintiff as a result of the incident.   

 

Once more, the Court notes the age of this case, which at this point is six years and six months old. CCP § 583.310 requires an action be brought to trial within five years after the action is commenced against the defendant. Otherwise, “[a]n action shall be dismissed by the court on its own motion or on motion of the defendant, after notice to the parties, if the action is not brought to trial within the time prescribed in this article.” (CCP § 583.360(a).) Dismissal of the action is “mandatory and ...not subject to extension, excuse, or exception except as expressly provided by statute.” (CCP § 583.360(b).) “Sections 583.310 and 583.360 subserve the purpose of “encourag[ing] the expeditious disposition of litigation.” [Citation.] The aim of the statutes is not to have trials, but to bring cases to a conclusion, to secure for plaintiffs the relief, and to defendants, the repose, to which the law entitles them, and to free the court's resources for the efficient adjudication of other claims.” (Hughes v. Kimble (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 59, 69-70.) 

 

In the context of a default judgment, courts have held that a reasonable period of time between the defendant's default and the entry of the default judgment should be excluded from the calculation of the five-year period. (See Howard v. Thrifty Drug & Discount Stores (1995) 10 Cal.4th 424, 439-40 [one week between finalization of arbitrator’s award and entry of judgment was indubitably reasonable period]; cf. Hughes v. Kimble, supra, 5 Cal.App.4th at 70 [gap of three years between default and judgment unreasonable and did not toll five-year statute].) “[T]he time between entry of a default and entry of a default judgment should be excluded from the five-year time to bring a case to trial if and only if the court finds that the plaintiff used due diligence to obtain entry of the judgment, and that in spite of such due diligence, it was impossible, impracticable, or futile to obtain a judgment.” (Hughes v. Kimble, 5 Cal.App.4th at 71; see also Vanyek v. Heard (1971) 18 Cal.App.3d 467, 471 [“The fair test is whether the plaintiff had a reasonable opportunity throughout the entire five-year period to bring the action to trial.”].) 

 

The Court previously scheduled an Order to Show Cause re: Dismissal for failure to enter default judgment prior to the expiration of the five-year dismissal statute for March 10, 2023. Plaintiff was put on notice that the Court considered dismissing this action pursuant to CCP §583.360, and that Plaintiff must show cause why the action should not be dismissed. (Min. Order, Dec. 16, 2022.) Furthermore, the Court stated that oral argument alone would be insufficient to show reasonable diligence, and required admissible evidence to be submitted at least sixteen (16) court days prior to the March 10th OSC. (Ibid.) 

 

On March 10, 2023, Counsel represented they needed additional time, and the OSC Re: Dismissal was continued to June 22, 2023. (Min. Order, March 10, 2023.) On June 22, 2023, Counsel represented that a new default judgment was in process and the court continued the hearing to October 13, 2023. (Min. Order, June 22, 2023.) In light of the most recent submitted default judgment package with all of the above-noted and uncured defects, Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate reasonable diligence in seeking default prior to the expiration of the five-year dismissal statute.  

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s action is hereby DISMISSED. (CCP §583.360.)
