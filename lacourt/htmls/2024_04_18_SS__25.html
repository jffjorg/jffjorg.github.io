BACKGROUND

 

On August 2, 2022, Plaintiff Georgy Ekpenisi (“Petitioner”) and minor Plaintiff Chimchetaram Macauley-Ekpenisi (“Minor Claimant” or “Claimant”) filed an action against Defendant Emmanuel Tyrone Bull (“Defendant”) relating to a motor vehicle accident that occured on November 5, 2021. Petitioner was appointed Claimant’s guardian ad litem on August 3, 2022.

 

On August 5, 2022, Petitioner filed an Expedited Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim. The Court denied the Expedited Petition on August 15, 2022. The Court additionally denied Petitioner’s Ex Parte Application for an Order Shortening Time for Hearing Plaintiff’s Expedited Petition for Minor’s Compromise.

 

On December 14, 2022, Petitioner filed a Petition to Approve Minor’s Compromise of Disputed Claim. On January 18, 2023, the Court denied the Petition due to excessive deficiencies.

BACKGROUND

 

On January 26, 2023, Plaintiff Alejandra Perez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Full of Flavor, LLC, Peter Trinh (“Defendants”) and Does 1 through 10 for (1) violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.

 

Plaintiff asserts claims under the Unruh Civil Rights Act (without injunctive relief). She seeks damages against Cult, a restaurant located in the City of Santa Monica, the website address of which is https://www.cultsantamonica.com/, alleging that the website could not be read by screen reader software ("SRS").

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s cause of action fails because she cannot prove that she had a bona fide intent to patronize the business because she has previously sued 11 restaurants and did not visit them thereafter (Mot. p. 2-3.) Defendants also argue that Plaintiff fails to show that she was denied access to the business because of the website since “Defendant’s website, on October 1, 2022, offered two versions of the menu: the first in image format (See Exhibit B), and the second in PDF.” (Id. at p.12.) Defendants further argue Plaintiff lacks standing under the Unruh Act because she failed to present credible evidence that she visited Cult's website, https://www.cultsantamonica.com/, with the requisite bona fide intent to use its services. (Id. at p.8.) Defendants also argue that Plaintiff’s statement that she is permanently blind is an inadmissible legal conclusion and lacks sufficient foundation and detail to meet the initial burden placed upon Plaintiff because she does not offer any medical or other records that details the nature or severity of her vision problems or provide the opinion of a doctor or other health care professional regarding her vision problems, or any evidence that she has been regarded by others as having an impairment. (Id. at p.9.)

 

OPPOSITION

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ arguments are meritless because of precedent. (Opp. p.3.) Additionally, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff had no intent to patronize the cafe at the time she visited the website misses the point because she is arguing that unequal access to the three menus prevented her from deciding whether to patronize. (Id. at p. 4.)

 

REPLY

 

            On Reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s argument that she “could not have formed an intent to patronize the cafe without such information” is at best, specious, and in fact, misleading because she could have used other search sites, asked Siri for Cult’s telephone number and spoken to staff, or used the “Order Online” button which would have been able to read the menu with her Screen Reader. (Reply p. 2-4.)  Defendants also argue that Plaintiff’s allegation that she visited the website to determine whether she liked the food and drinks is not sufficient to establish standing because it shows that she did not have a bona fide intent to patronize.  (Id. at p.5-6.) Defendants also argue that precedent does not support her claim because the issue here is lack of standing for not having a genuine intent to patronize. (Id. at p. 6-7.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.            Legal Standard

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c); Vesely v. Sager (1971) 5 Cal.3d 153.) The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733.) 

 

When a Defendant or Cross-Defendant seeks summary judgment, he/she must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) When a Plaintiff or Cross-Complainant seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence on each element of each cause of action on which judgment is sought. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions or ‘ultimate’ facts” and be strictly construed. (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519; Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639.)  

 

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial movant’s burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832.) Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p).) The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151.) As to any alternative request for summary adjudication of issues, such alternative relief must be clearly set forth in the Notice of Motion and the general burden-shifting rules apply but the issues upon which summary adjudication may be sought are limited by statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) 

 

II.            Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff Alejandra Perez objects to evidence cited by Defendants Full of Flavor, LLC and Peter Trinh in support of their motion for summary judgment.

California Rules of Court Rule 3.1354 requires a party making written objections to evidence to submit a proposed order that includes places for the Court to indicate whether it has sustained or overruled each objection and a place for the signature of the judge.  CRC rule 3.1354(c) identifies the mandatory format for the proposed order.  A Court must rule on individual objections only when they are timely and in the proper form. (Demps v. San Francisco Housing Authority (2007) 149 Cal. App. 4th 564, 578.)

Here, Plaintiff’s proposed order is not in one of the two mandatory forms, as a result, this is grounds to overrule all objections for the failure to comply with CRC rule 3.1354. Nevertheless, the Court rules as follows:

OVERRULED: 1-3, 5-6, 12, 15

III.            Discussion

Defendants move for summary judgment on the basis that there are no triable issues of material fact concerning the essential elements of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (Mot., p. 2.)  

Plaintiff asserts a cause of action for violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.

The Unruh Civil Rights Act (Unruh Act) provides that “all persons within the jurisdiction of this state are free and equal, and no matter what their sex, race, color, religion, ancestry, national origin, disability, or medical condition are entitled to the full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services in all business establishments of every kind whatsoever.” (Civil Code, § 51, subd. (b).) The Unruh Act incorporates the protections created by the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA), 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq., and mandates that “[a] violation of the right of any individual under the [ADA] shall also constitute a violation of this section.” Thus, any violation of the ADA is a per se violation of the Unruh Act, regardless of the defendant's intent. (See Civil Code § 51, subd. (f); Lentini v. California Center for the Arts, Escondido (9th Cir. 2004) 370 F.3d 837, 847; Munson v. Del Taco, Inc. (2009) 46 Cal. 4th 661, 665.)

           

“A plaintiff can recover under the Unruh Civil Rights Act on¿two alternate theories: (1) a violation of the ADA (Civ. Code, § 51, subd. (f)); or (2) denial of access to a business establishment based on intentional discrimination. [Citations.]”  (Martinez v. San Diego County Credit Union¿(2020) 50¿Cal.App.5th 1048, 1059.)¿(Emphasis added.)  Intentional discrimination, however, need not be proved to obtain damages under the Unruh Civil Rights Act when the plaintiff establishes a violation of the ADA.  (Munson v. Del Taco, Inc., (2009) 46 Cal.4th 661, 665.) 

 

On October 1, 2022, Plaintiff visited Defendants’ restaurant’s website, however, the website allegedly did not have a PDF version of the menu. (Davis Decl. ¶12; Complaint ¶¶ 3-5; Decl. Perez ¶¶3-6.) Therefore, Plaintiff believes she is entitled to Unruh Damages and does not request injunctive relief. (UMF No. 14.) Plaintiff states that she is legally blind and has required screen reader software (“SRS”) to vocalize visual website content on computer and mobile device screens. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Material Fact [PSSMF] No. 1.) However, Defendants dispute this fact because Plaintiff does not offer medical records or any evidence proving her vision impairment. (Separate Statement of Material Fact [DSSMF] No.1.) The parties do not dispute that Plaintiff did not call the restaurant directly or refer to third-party sites such as DoorDash or Caviar to find the restaurant’s menu. (Undisputed Material Fact “UMF” Nos. 6,11.) Plaintiff only visited the website to review the restaurant’s menus. (UMF No. 7.) Additionally, both parties agree that the restaurant did not intentionally discriminate against Plaintiff. (UMF No. 8.)

 

            Defendants argue that the Cult restaurant website on October 1, 2022, was accessible and there were alternative methods to find the menus. In support, they provide the declaration of Defendant Peter Trinh who is a partner of Cult Santa Monica and the Manager/Member of Full of Flavor, LLC. They also provide the expert declaration of Craig Davis, who is the principal of Accessible Crew LLC, a company that conducts accessibility audits for a variety of organizations including commercial and noncommercial entities and has experience in auditing and remediating websites for compliance with the Americans with Disabilities Act and WCAG 2.0+. (Davis Decl. ¶2.) However, multiple statements made by Mr. Davis are inadmissible because they are irrelevant to the websites condition on October 1, 2022. However, in Mr. Davis’s supplemental declaration replying to the opposition, he states that he “can verify that the option and presumably the service to “Order Online” was available on all pages of the Defendant’s website as far back as January 2022 by checking available scans of the web page at Internet Archive, also known as The Wayback Machine, located at https://web.archive.org.” (Supplemental Davis Decl. ¶ 8.) In Peter Trinh’s declaration, he states that they “have a readable PDF version of our menu listed on the “Menu” page on the website [and] [a] readable version of the menu is also available on DoorDash and Caviar.” (Trinh Decl. ¶7.) However, it is unclear whether he is referring to the date Plaintiff accessed the website or currently. Defendants also argue that Plaintiff has previously sued about eleven other restaurants.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the drinks, food, and dim sum menus appearing at https://www.cultsantamonica.com/menu on October 1, 2022, were JPEG images that did not contain and had not been encoded with any actual text that could be recognized and navigated by screen reader software which would have been impossible for a blind individual to learn about the items. (Plaintiff’s Additional Separate Statement Nos. 1-2.) In support, Plaintiff presents her own declaration along with the declarations of Joseph Allen Jr. and Beth A. Franssen. Beth A. Franssen, is an “an expert in accessibility of websites to visually impaired individuals who require and use screen reader software to vocalize visual website content on computer and mobile device screens.” (Franssen Decl. ¶1.) Ms. Franssen states that she “examined the native electronic version of the website at https://www.cultsantamonica.com as it existed in October 2022, as well as the native electronic versions of the three menus (drinks, food, and dim sum) appearing on the website at the time.” (Id. at ¶3.) Further, she states “[a]s they existed in October, 2022, the website and its constituent webpages did not contain any PDF versions of the menus or links to any downloadable PDF versions of the menus. The language "for a pdf readable version of our menu, click here" did not appear anywhere on the website or its webpages as they existed in October, 2022.” (Id. at ¶¶ 6-7.) Joseph Allen Jr. states that he was working as a legal investigator in October 2022 for the Law Office of Morse Mehrban and downloaded the entire website. (Allen Decl. ¶1.) Moreover, Mr. Allen states that he “examined the entire website and its webpages to determine whether the contents of those menus appeared anywhere on the website in either text or PDF form. I could not locate the contents of those menus anywhere on the website in either text or PDF form.” (Id. at ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff also presents her own declaration stating that she visited the website on October 1, 2022, to review its drink and food menus but was unable to access that information because there were no PDF versions of the menus. (Perez Decl. ¶ 3-5.)

 

             Defendants’ argument regarding the requirement for medical records or other evidence to determine the severity of Plaintiff’s visual impairment are meritless because Defendants fail to cite to any authority requiring Plaintiff to provide medical records or other evidence to prove a claimed disability.

 

Defendants’ argument regarding standing shows that it is a triable issue of material fact. The Court agrees with Plaintiff that the facts in Martinez v. San Diego County Credit Union and Thurston v. Midvale Corp are similar to the facts in this case. For instance, in Martinez, the plaintiff who was blind, brought an action against defendant credit union, claiming its website was incompatible with software permitting him to read website content. (Martinez v. San Diego County Credit Union (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 1048, 1048.) The Martinez plaintiff asserted a single cause of action under the Unruh Civil Rights Act (Civ. Code, § 51 et seq.) based on two alternate theories: (1) defendant's website violated the Americans with Disabilities Act (42 U.S.C. § 12111 et seq.) (ADA); and (2) defendant's actions constituted intentional discrimination prohibited by the Unruh Civil Rights Act. (Civ. Code, § 51 et seq.; 42 U.S.C. § 12101 et seq.) (Id.) In this case, Plaintiff is also blind and alleges that the restaurant’s website was incompatible with her screen reader software because the menu was not in PDF form. However, in Martinez, the Court of Appeal did not address standing. (Id. at 1075, fn. 9.) Moreover, in Thurston v. Midvale Corp, the plaintiff was blind and used a screen reader to access the Internet, when she tried to use appellant’s website for its restaurant her screen reader could not read the menu. (Thurston v. Midvale Corp. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 634, 637.) The Court of Appeal found that plaintiff had standing under the Unruh Act because she had intent to use its services but encounters an exclusionary policy or practice that prevents him or her from using those services. (Id. at 641.)

 

Plaintiff’s bona fide intent to use Defendants’ services is a triable issue of material fact. Here, Plaintiff stated in her opposition that “Plaintiff’s Complaint is based upon her inability to access information (three menus) appearing on Defendants’ website. Plaintiff may or may not have ultimately decided to patronize Defendants’ Cafe depending on what she learned about its menu items. However, she never got to the point where she could make that decision because she was deprived of information relevant to it. The issue is unequal access to information.” (Opp. p.4.) Plaintiff’s cited case law do not show that the cases support standing as unequal access to information. Nevertheless, the Supreme Court in White v. Square, Inc. noted that “standing analysis is limited to the pleadings, in which [plaintiff] unequivocally alleges his intention to use [defendant’s] services.” (White v. Square, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1019, 1032.) Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges “[o]n or about October 1, 2022, Plaintiff visited the website in order to review the restaurant’s menus but was unable to do so because the menus were in image form and incompatible with screen reader software.” (Complaint ¶5.) In Thurston v. Omni Hotels Management Corp. the Court of Appeal affirmed the trial court’s decision to instruct jurors to make the determination whether plaintiff possessed a bona fide intent to use its services, because it was “unclear [] whether her factual allegations could overcome [defendant’s] assertion that she did “not actually possess a bona fide intent … to use its services.” (Thurston v. Omni Hotels Management Corp. (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 299, 309.)

 

The Court finds that the evidence presented by the Plaintiff and Defendant give rise to triable issues of material fact regarding the accessibility of the contents on the date of the website visit. Thus, there is a genuine dispute of fact as to whether Plaintiff was denied full and equal access to the Defendants’ restaurant by not providing the menus in PDF form and if Plaintiff had a bona fide intent to patronize.

 

IV.       Conclusion

           

            In all, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

            On January 25, 2024, Plaintiff moved for default to be entered against Defendant, and the Clerk entered default the same day.

            On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Nunc Pro TunC Order to Correct Clerical Errors in Clerk’s Default Judgment Entered on February 07, 2024.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plainitff prays for the Court to issue a nunc pro tunc order that corrects the clerical errors in the Clerks’ Default Judgement entered on February 7, 2024, and grants Plaintiff with the full measure of relief to which it is entitled in the Judgment on the grounds that the Judgment amount is inaccurate as a result of an inaccurate pre-judgment interest figure that conflicts with the averments in the Complaint.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

REPLY

 

            No reply has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure § 473(d) provides that, “the court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed.”  A court has the inherent power to correct clerical errors in its records so as to make these records reflect the true facts.  (In re Candelario (1970) 3¿Cal.¿3d¿702, 705.)  “The test which distinguishes clerical error from possible judicial error is simply whether the challenged portion of the judgment was entered inadvertently (which is clerical error) versus advertently (which might be judicial error, but is not clerical error).  Unless the challenged portion of the judgment was entered inadvertently, it cannot be changed post judgment under the guise of correction of clerical error.”  Tokio Marine & Fire Ins. Corp. v. Western Pacific Roofing Corp. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 110, 117 (internal citation omitted).  “An amendment that substantially modifies the original judgment or materially alters the rights of the parties, may not be made by the court under its authority to correct clerical error, therefore, unless the record clearly demonstrates that the error was not the result of the exercise of judicial discretion.”  (In re Candelario, 3 Cal. 3d. at¿705.) 

 

II.        Discussion

Plaintiff moves for a nunc pro tunc order to correct the clerical errors which understate the pre-judgment interest due and owed to the Plaintiff as of date of entry of Judgment. (Mot. p. 2.) Plaintiff argues that per the averments in the Complaint, the pre-judgment interest component of the Judgment should be $7,931.68, and not $3,782.03 as specified in the Judgment, bringing the judgment amount to $30,171.19 instead of the $26,021.54 entered by the Clerk. (Id.) Plaintiff provides the Court with the Declaration of its counsel who states that she,

 

filed a proposed form of Clerk’s Judgment by Default for entry by the Clerk which mistakenly included the wrong figures for pre-judgment interest and attorneys’ fees. While the Clerk corrected the error in the attorneys’ fee figure, the Clerk did not correct the error in the pre-judgment interest figure so that it would conform to the averments in the Complaint. As a result, the pre-judgment interest figure in the Judgment is understated and consequently so is the amount of the Judgment.

 

(Collen M. Auer Decl. ¶ 3.) Per the Complaint, there was $2,328.26 in prejudgment interest due and owing by Defendant through May 5, 2021, with prejudgment interest continuing to accrue at the Contract rate of 9.95%, or $5.57 per day from May 06, 2021, until the date of Judgement on February 07, 2024. (Id. ¶ 4.) Meaning that the prejudgment interest due and owing by the Defendant was $7,931,68 instead of the $3,782.03 specified in the Judgment. (Id.)

 

The Court finds the evidence sufficient to show that the interest amount of $3,782.03 specified in the February 07, 2024, Judgment was a clerical error. Noting both the Complaint and Plaintiff’s declaration, prejudgment interest accrued at the Contract rate of 9.95%, or $5.57 per day from May 06, 2021, until the date of Judgment.

 

Accordingly, the Court amends the Judgment to reflect the correct prejudgment interest amount of $7,931,68, bringing the total Judgment amount to $30,171.19.

 

II.        Conclusion

           

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Nunc Pro Tunc Order to Correct Clerical Errors in Clerk’s Default Judgment Entered on February 07, 2024, is GRANTED.

 

The prejudgment interest amount shall be amended to reflect the correct amount of $7,931,68, bringing the total Judgment amount to $30,171.19.

 

Counsel for Plaintiff is ordered to electronically submit a proposed form of amended judgment within 10-days.

 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.
