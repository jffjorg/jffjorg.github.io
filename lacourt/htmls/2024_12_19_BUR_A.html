DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify "all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue."  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is requested.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 22BBCV00619    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: A

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22BBCV00619

 

MP:  

Abimelec Burgos (Plaintiff)

RP:  

General Motors, LLC (Defendant)

 

NOTICE:

 

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is requested and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

 

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

 

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Abimelec Burgos (Plaintiff) brings action against General Motors, LLC (GM) for GM’s alleged violation of the Song Beverly Act. The Complaint alleges five causes of action for (1) violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(d), (2) violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(b), (3) violation of Civil Code § 1793.2(a)(3), (4) breach of express written warranty, and (5) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability.

 

Before the Court is motion by Plaintiff to recover attorney fees in the amount of $47,120.50. GM opposes and Plaintiff replies.  

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A prevailing party in an action under the Song-Beverly Act may be entitled to attorney's fees. (Civ. Code § 1794(d); see also C.C.P. § 1032(a)(4).)

 

The calculation of attorney's fees under the Song-Beverly Act is based on the lodestar method, which multiplies the number of hours reasonably expended by a reasonable hourly rate. (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154; Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 817-819.) Nonetheless, the prevailing party should not receive a "windfall" through an award of reasonable attorney's fees. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.) Thus, a prevailing buyer under the Song-Beverly Act has the burden of "showing that the fees incurred were 'allowable,' were 'reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation,' and were 'reasonable in amount." (Levy v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 807, 816.)

 

Once an attorney has presented evidence of her or his actual time spent and hourly rate charged, the time and hourly rate are presumed to be reasonable. (Mandel v. Lackner (1979) 92 Cal.App.3d 747, 761.) An award of attorney fees may be based on counsel's declarations, without production of detailed time records. (Raining Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.) The verified time statements of attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous. (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

In opposing this motion, GM argues (1) much of the time billed by Plaintiff’s counsel was not reasonably incurred and (2) that Plaintiff’s counsel has not demonstrated its rates are reasonable. The Court will address each of these arguments in turn.

 

Reasonable Hours Billed

 

Absent circumstances rendering an award unjust, an award of attorney's fees should ordinarily include compensation for all hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to obtaining the fee award. (See Ketchum vs. Moses supra, 24 Cal.4th at p. 1133.) The prevailing party has the burden to present verified time records showing the hours spent on the case, but once properly submitted, such records are "entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous." (Horsford v. Board of Trustees (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.) Nonetheless, "[a] trial court may not rubber stamp a request for attorney fees, but must determine the number of hours reasonably expended." (Donahue v. Donahue (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 259, 271-272.)

 

Where a party is challenging the reasonableness of attorney's fees as excessive that party must attack itemized billing with evidence that the fees claimed were not appropriate or obtain the declaration of an attorney with expertise in the procedural and substantive law to demonstrate that the fees claimed were unreasonable. (Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 563-64.) A reduced award might be fully justified by a general observation that an attorney over litigated a case or submitted a padded bill or that the opposing party has stated valid objections. (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 101.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has submitted billing entries of the attorneys and paralegals who worked on this case as Exhibit 12. It appears that a total of 128.7 hours were billed in this matters for a total of $42,120.50, including the time billed in bringing this motion. (Manno Decl. Exh. 12.) The billing entries are split between attorney Alessandro Manno (Manno) (at rate of $400 per hour), attorney Nancy Zhang (Zhang) (at a rate of $525 per hour), and paralegal Robert Aguilar (Aguilar) (at a rate of $210 per hour). (Id.)

 

The vast majority of GM’s argument is that the hours billed in relation to propounding discovery and bringing discovery related motions are unreasonably inflated. GM argues that Plaintiff’s counsel relies substantially upon templates in both creating its discovery requests and bringing motions to compel. (Kay Decl. ¶ 6.) GM contends that many of the entries related to discovery should be reduced by considerable amounts, in most cases over 75%. (See Oppo. pgs. 9-11.) GM extends this argument to the documents prepared for trial and Plaintiff’s motions in limine.

 

The Court acknowledges that several of the discovery related entries appear to be unreasonable in duration. It can be easily observed that the discovery propounded in this case echoes the discovery Plaintiff’s counsel regularly propounds in Song-Beverly matters, both in form and content. At the same time, the use of templating is a legal reality which appears to the Court inescapable. Although Plaintiff’s counsel undoubtedly used some form of templating in propounding discovery in this matter, it does not follow that their efforts were trivial.

 

As with many things, the Court finds the reasonable amount of hours likely lies somewhere in the middle. The Court will reduce the number of hours billed in connection to discovery, but not in the drastic amount GM requests. These reductions are listed below:

 

·         December 19 & 28, 2022 and February 12, 2024: Drafting Discovery/Deposition (Manno Decl., Exh. 12 pgs. 53, 51, 16.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 2.7 hours to 1.2 hours.

o   Zhang: reduced from 1.2 hours to 0.8 hours.

 

·         March 3 & 17, 2023: Drafting Meet and Confer Correspondence (Manno Decl., Exh. 12 pgs. 41-42.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 2.1 hours to 1 hour.

 

·         April 15, 2024, June 26, 2024, and July 3 & 5, 2024: Drafting Motion to Compel Deposition of GM’s PMK (Manno Decl., Exh. 12, pgs. 9-11 & 13.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 6.2 hours to 4 hours.

 

·         March 20, 2023, April 13, 20 & 21, 2023: Drafting Motions to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production (Manno Decl., Exh. 12, pgs. 38-33.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 10.7 hours to 3.2 hours.

o   Zhang: reduced from 1.2 hours to 0.8 hours.

 

·         November 29, 2023, December 1 & 12, 2023, January 8 & 29, 2024 and February 1 & 9, 2024: Drafting Motion to Compel Compliance (Manno Decl. Exh. 12, pgs. 26, 24, 17-19.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 5.6 hours to 1.4 hours.

o   Zhang: reduced from 1.1 hours to 0.6 hours.

 

 

·         December 26, 2023, February 23, 2024, and July 9-10 & 15-16, 2024: Drafting Trial Documents (Manno Decl. Exh. 12, pgs. 7-9, 15 & 22.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 8.9 hours to 6 hours.

 

·         December 20, 2023: Drafting Motions in Limine (Manno Decl., Exh. 12, p. 23.)

 

o   Manno: reduced from 4.7 hours to 2 hours.

 

GM further argues that the entirety of the 12.2 hours billed by paralegal Aguilar should be stricken because it consists of clerical work not befitting the expertise of a paralegal. GM then goes on to argue that Plaintiff’s paralegal engaged in “block billing”, thereby preventing a determination as to what work performed was appropriate for a paralegal. The billing entries under Aguilar appear reasonably particularized. For example, on February 12, 2024, Aguilar billed .2 hours to review, format, finalize, and draft the proof of service for Plaintiff’s Amended Notice of Deposition. (Manno Decl., Exh. 12, p. 16.) Further still, necessary overhead support services that secretaries and paralegals provide to attorneys may be included in an attorney fees award. (City of Oakland v. McCullough (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1, 7.) As such, the Court declines to strike or reduce the billing entries for the paralegal work.

 

GM further argues that the 4 hours billed in drafting a repair chronology should also be stricken. GM argues that this task could be easily performed by a paralegal and thus should not be billed at the attorney rate. The Court finds this unpersuasive. GM’s declaration is silent as to the complexity of a repair chronology and the work performed on November 30, 2022, December 13, 2022, March 5 & 17, 2023 and April 4, 2024. Without evidence to the contrary, Plaintiff’s submissions are presumably valid. The Court declines to exercise its discretion to strike or reduce the billing entries for the repair chronology.

 

Lastly, the Court finds a reduction of the amount of billable hours in connection with the instant motion should be reduced. Plaintiff’s counsel appears to have billed 11.9 hours in the preparation of this motion. (Manno Decl., Ex. 12, pgs. 1-6.) Although this motion is quite lengthy (Plaintiff’s declaration/exhibits total 756 pages), the Court finds the bulk of the material presented is duplicative of previous filings in this case. It appears to the Court that the vast majority of effort in drafting this motion was incurred in compiling discovery and related motions, compiling court orders for attorney’s fees in other cases, and attaching the billable hours. In the Court’s estimation these tasks did not reasonably necessitate 11.9 hours of attorney work from Plaintiff’s counsel Manno. As such, the hours are reduced from 11.9 hours to 4 hours.

 

In short, the Court exercises its discretion in reducing the billable hours from 128.7 to 97.4 (a total reduction of 33.3 hours.) This reflects a total reduction from 9.9 hours to 8.6 hours for work attributable to Zhang, and a reduction from 106.6 hours to 76.6 hours for work attributable to Manno.

 

Reasonable Rates

 

GM argues that Plaintiff has failed to uphold his burden to demonstrate the hourly rates of his counsel are reasonable, instead substituting a number of trial court orders approving motions for attorney’s fees were the moving party claimed a similar rate. GM argues that Plaintiff should be required to demonstrate their rates are reasonable within the scope of the local legal community.

 

The Court does find a reduction in the hourly rate is warranted based on the showing Plaintiff has made. The declaration of Plaintiff’s attorney Manno reflects that he has been litigating Song-Beverly matters since August 2022, shortly before this case started. (Manno Decl. ¶ 33.) In the Court’s experience, attorneys with such relatively limited experience in a new area of law cannot reasonably expect to bill at a rate of $380 per hour. Based on its experience and expertise, the Court finds a reasonable rate for Manno in the pendency of this litigation would be no more than $325 per hour.

 

As concerns the entries billed by Plaintiff’s attorney Zhang, the Court finds Plaintiff showing as to the rate of $500 per hours is insufficient. While Manno explains that Zhang is a senior partner who has been litigating Song-Beverly cases since 2017, the fact remains that the $500 per hour rate is far above average. (Manno Decl. ¶ 32.) As such the Court finds a reasonable rate for the Zhang would be no more than $400 per hour for the complexity of these cases.  

 

Conclusion

 

The motion for attorney fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $30,897.  Given that this is a contingency case, the court will apply a multiplier of 1.2 for total attorney’s fees of $37,076.40.  

 

This amount reflects 76.6 hours of work by Manno at a rate of $325 per hour ($24,895), 8.6 hours of work by Zhang at a rate of $400 per hour ($3,440), and 12.2 hours of work by paralegal Aguilar at a rate of $210 per hour ($2,562).

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Jonathan Lee (Plaintiff) brings this action against Zane and Gina Cazares (Defendants). Plaintiff alleges he was injured by virtue of Defendants’ negligent operation of a motor vehicle.

 

Defendants now move to compel Plaintiff’s participation in a second independent medical exam (IME). Defendants claim another IME is necessary because Plaintiff subsequently underwent surgery to repair the damage he alleges was caused by Defendants. Defendants further state that they were not informed of the surgery prior to the first IME.

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion, arguing that Defendants have not demonstrated good cause for a second IME. Plaintiff argues that Defendants were aware of the surgery prior to the IME and that alternative means exist by which they can obtain the information they now seek.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Where any party seeks to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in C.C.P. § 2032.220, or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of the court. (C.C.P. § 2032.310(a).) A mental examination shall be performed only by a licensed physician, or by a licensed clinical psychologist who holds a doctoral degree in psychology and has had at least five years of postgraduate experience in the diagnosis of emotional and mental disorders. (C.C.P. § 2032.020(c)(1).) A motion for an examination shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (C.C.P. § 2032.310(b).)

 

The Court shall grant the motion only for good cause shown. (C.C.P. § 2032.320(a).) To show good cause, the moving party must provide specific facts justifying discovery and show that the inquiry is relevant to the subject matter of the case or is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)  The good-cause requirement serves as a barrier to excessive and unwarranted intrusions. (Id.)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Timeline  

 

On December 5, 2023, Defendants’ counsel took Plaintiff’s deposition. (Aziev Decl., Exh. 1.) During this deposition, Plaintiff stated that he intended to undergo surgery to treat his hernia in late December 2023 at Keck Medicine of USC (USC Keck). (Id. at p. 77, lines 19-24.)

 

On February 4, 2024, Defendants subpoenaed Plaintiff’s medical records from USC Keck. (Aziev Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 2.) The date of production was March 5, 2024. (Id.) Responsive records were produced on February 19, 2024. (Aziev Decl., Exh. 3.)

 

On February 14, 2024, Defendants served Plaintiff with notice of the first IME with Defendants’ designated physician Mark Spoonamore (Spoonamore). (Aziev Decl., Exh. 4.) Plaintiff responded with a series of objections. (Aziev Decl., Exh. 5.) Counsel for the parties met and conferred, ultimately agreeing to the IME upon Defendants’ acceptance of 17 conditions. (Aziev. Decl., Exhs. 5-7.) One of these conditions, number eight sates that “Plaintiff’s counsel must receive written confirmation that no additional orthopedic examination will be demanded by or on behalf of any other defendant.” (Aziev Decl., Exh. 5 at p. 2.)

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiff appeared for the first IME with Spoonamore. (Stevens. Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. B.)

 

On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff underwent the surgery to repair his hernia at USC Keck. (Stevens Decl., Exh. B.) The surgery was performed by Jeffrey Wang, M.D. (Wang). (Id.)

 

On October 3, 2024, Defendants noticed depositions of USC Keck’s Custodian of Records and Wang. (Aziev Decl., Exhs. 8, 9.) Plaintiff did not object to these depositions and his counsel in fact appeared to defend them. (Aziev Decl., Exh. 10.) On October 23, 2024, Defendants’ counsel did not appear for either deposition, instead taking the depositions off-calendar via email a few minutes before they were slated to start. (Aziev Decl. Exhs. 10-11.) Plaintiff contends he has never been given a reason for the cancellation.

 

On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff was served with notice of a second IME with Spoonamore. (Steven Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. C.) Plaintiff objected and Defendants subsequently brought the instant motion.

 

Merits

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s argument that Defendants have not demonstrated good cause to compel a second IME to be persuasive.

 

There is no limit on the number of mental or physical examinations, provided that a showing of good cause is made to justify more than one mental or physical examination of a party. (See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.) The good cause requirement is intended to check for potential harassment of plaintiffs by repetitive examinations. (Id.; See also Sporich v. Superior Court (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 422 superseded by statute on other grounds at 428.) Good cause in this context is established by facts that appear in the record and must be shown such that an impartial tribunal would be satisfied that the request may be granted without abuse of the inherent rights of the adversary. (Sporich supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at 427, 428.)

 

Shapira concerned an order to compel a second medical examination of a plaintiff who claimed both physical and mental injuries. (Shapira supra, 224 Cal.App.3d at 1255.) The Shapira court noted that "multiple examinations should not be ordered routinely; the good cause requirement will check the potential harassment of plaintiffs by repetitive examinations," and "multiple examinations by medical specialists in different fields" may be necessary based upon a plaintiff's claimed injuries. (Id.)

 

There is a surprising dearth of case law addressing the concept of “good cause” for purpose of compelling further IME. Shapira and Sporich are the most relevant recent authorities concerning this issue, and neither case provides clear guidelines for finding good cause has been shown. While these cases make clear that good cause has not been shown were a further IME is harassing, the opinions contain little guidance on what constitutes facts, “…such that an impartial tribunal would be satisfied that the request may be granted without abuse of the inherent rights of the adversary.”

 

Most case law, including federal court authority relied upon by both parties, look to the ruling in Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833 for guidance. In Vinson, the California Supreme Court discussed good cause in the context of the former C.C.P. § 2036, the statute which previously governed motions such as the one before this Court. (Vinson supra, 43 Cal.3d at 840.) At the time Vinson was decided, C.C.P. § 2036 had actually been repealed by the legislature and not yet replaced by any equivalent provision. (Id. at fn.6.) Regardless, the Vinson court concluded that the legislature did not intend to obviate the good cause requirement. (Id.) Indeed this requirement was reintegrated when the Legislature enacted the current C.C.P. § 2032.320(a).

 

Given the above, the Court finds the factors for determining good cause as explained in Vinson are still helpful where a party seeks to compel a second IME. Vinson concluded that good cause required (1) a showing of relevancy to the subject matter, (2) specific facts illustrating a need for the information, and (3) a lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Id.) It is the third Vinson factor which is particularly relevant to the instant motion.

 

Here, Defendants’ stated need for a second IME is to obtain information related to the “performance, outcome and subsequent prognosis of the surgery.” (Mot. p. 6.) Defendants argue that they also expect Wang will testify at trial as to the need for surgery, thus Defendants need an independent examination by Spoonamore. While Defendants may assert the need for this information, they do not address whether this information can be obtained through sources other than a second IME. Defendants’ motion and their reply make no reference to the cancelled depositions of USC Keck’s Custodian of Records and Wang. It appears to the Court that information about the “performance, outcome and subsequent prognosis of the surgery” is all easily obtainable through deposition of these third parties. This would be a significantly less obtrusive means of obtaining such information and Defendants have presented no argument to the contrary, this is especially true had counsel not cancelled these deposition within minutes of their anticipated starting as represented by Plaintiff.   

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel Plaintiff’s second IME is DENIED.

ALLEGATIONS: 

 

Matthew C. Moore (Plaintiff) brings this action against Kathleen F. Davis (Defendant). Plaintiff alleges causes of action for Wrongful Eviction, Theft, Civil Extortion, and Defamation as well as Property Damage. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant locked him out of a residential living he rented from Plaintiff and refused him access to his personal belongings.

 

Defendant, in pro per, now brings these motions to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form/Special Interrogatories (FROG/SPROG), Request for Production of Documents (RFPD), and Request for Admissions (RFA.) Plaintiff opposes the motions and Defendant replies.

  

ANALYSIS: 

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARD 

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories

 

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete, (2)¿an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response, or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. §¿2030.300(a).) The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections thereto. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-21.)

  

Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFPD

 

A motion to compel further responses to RFPD may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (C.C.P. § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See C.C.P. § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) 

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses on RFA

 

On receipt of a response to requests for admission, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) an answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete or (2) an objection to a particular request is without merit or too general. (C.C.P. § 2033.290(a).)

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to meet and confer prior to bringing these motions. Plaintiff states that although Defendant emailed his counsel several times in reference to these discovery responses, these attempts do not evidence her good faith attempt to obtain informal resolution of the specific issues at hand. The Court disagrees.

 

The purpose of the meet and confer requirement under C.C.P.§ 2016.040 is to “…to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order...This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.)

 

Considerations as to whether a good faith meet and confer effort has been made entrusted to the trial court's discretion and judgment, with due regard for all relevant circumstances. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 432.) “A single letter, followed by a response which refuses concessions, might in some instances be an adequate attempt at informal resolution, especially when a legitimate discovery objective is demonstrated. The time available before the motion filing deadline, and the extent to which the responding party was complicit in the lapse of available time, can also be relevant. An evaluation of whether, from the perspective of a reasonable person in the position of the discovering party, additional effort appeared likely to bear fruit, should also be considered” (Id. [emphasis added].)

 

Here, Defendant emailed Plaintiff’s counsel on August 21, 2024 expressing her belief that the responses consisting purely of objections were, “…designed to avoid producing any evidence in support of [Plaintiff’s] claims against me.” (Mot. to Compel Interrogatories, Davis Decl., Exh. 1 at p. 75.) Plaintiff’s email further addressed the objections on grounds that the interrogatories improperly referenced the Complaint. (Id.) Plaintiff further stated her belief that there was nothing in her discovery which requested material subject to the attorney client privilege. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel responded with a series of less than cordial emails. The response of Plaintiff’s counsel does not address any of the arguments raised by Defendant’s email. (Id.) Instead, Plaintiff’s counsel glibly states that he disagrees with Defendant and that she interposed some of the same objections in her discovery responses. Defendant responded to this email again addressing the objections in substance. (Id.) Plaintiff’s counsel responded once more, explaining his belief that Defendant has not met and conferred in good faith. (Id. at 76.)

 

In reviewing the above correspondence, the Court finds Defendant adequately met and conferred in good faith. Defendant presented clear arguments as to her issues with Plaintiff’s objections in her initial correspondence. What she received in response was a simple “I disagree,” followed by a lecture on the meet and confer process. Regardless of how one reads the tone of Plaintiff’s counsel in reply, Defendant was reasonable in concluding that any further meet and confer attempts would not produce the responses she was looking for. Defendant was not required to continue the meet and confer process in the face of such obstreperous responses.

 

FROG

 

Defendant moves to compel further response to FROG Nos. 2.12, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 12.7, 13.113.2, 14.1, 14.2, and 17.1. Plaintiff objected to each of the FROG on the various grounds which are listed below:

 

·         This interrogatory seeks information that is not relevant to the subject matter of this litigation, and not reasonably calculated [sic] to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. This interrogatory calls for speculation as to any other party.

 

·         This interrogatory seeks information that is not relevant to the subject matter of this litigation, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, vague and ambiguous as to “other damages.”

 

·         This FI seeks identifying information regarding persons interviewed by counsel or other persons on the client’s behalf which is protected by the qualified work product doctrine.

 

·         This FI is vague and ambiguous as to the terms “cited” and “charged.”

 

Despite the fact that Plaintiff has stated objections on each of the above grounds, his opposition briefs none of the objections with specificity. The only argument Plaintiff presents as to the objections is that he is permitted to assert each of these objections even where the interrogatories are prepared by form. As Plaintiff has presented no argument with respect to the content of his objections, the Court finds he has necessarily failed to uphold his burden in opposing this motion.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further responses to FROG is GRANTED.

 

SPROG

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to each of her 20 SPROGs. Defendant objected to each of these on various grounds, as stated below:

 

·         This interrogatory is not full and complete in and of itself in violation of C.C.P. § 2030.060(d); this interrogatory contains subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive question in violation of C.C.P. § 2030.060(d) and (f).

 

·         Vague and ambiguous as to the term “ownership.”

 

·         Irrelevant and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

As with FROG, Plaintiff bears the burden to demonstrate his objection are meritorious. The only objection briefed in Plaintiff’s opposition is that Defendant’s SPROG, “…asked for information or documents and added as alleged in a specific cause of action or paragraph of [Plaintiff’s] complaint.” (Opp. p. 4.) Plaintiff argues that because each of the SPROG concerns his contentions “as stated in Paragraph [X] of the Complaint,” they are not full and complete within the meaning of C.C.P. § 2030.060(d). The Court finds this argument unpersuasive.

 

In making the above argument, Plaintiff relies on Catanese v. Superior Court (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1159. In Catanese, the Court of Appeal found a set of interrogatories violated C.C.P. § 2030.060(d) where they cross-referenced the propounding party’s deposition. The following are two examples of these interrogatories:

 

9. Do you contend that [Ray] has given any answers to any of the questions asked of her in any of the volumes of her deposition taken on or before September 28, 1995, which are untrue?

 

10. Unless your answer to the preceding interrogatory is an unqualified negative answer, identify the question asked and [Ray's] answer by stating the date of deposition, volume number, page and line numbers of any of the questions which you contend [Ray] answered untruthfully.

 

(Catanese supra, 46 Cal. App.4th at 1161-1162.)

 

The Court of Appeal found these interrogatories were an impermissible attempt to avoid the 35 SPROG limit imposed by what is now C.C.P. § 2030.030(b). (Id. at 1165.) The court found that by cross-referencing a large number of questions asked in deposition, the SPROG sought to vitiate the statutory limit. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff also cites to Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277. In Clement the opposing party objected to a special interrogatory which asked the party to, “Please state the amount of such damages as identified in interrogatory number 1” (Clement supra 177 Cal.App.4th at 1282.) The court found that the objection was without merit and contrasted these interrogatories with those in Catanese, finding no attempt to circumvent the 35 interrogatory limit. (Id. at 1287.) The Court observed that because, “…plaintiffs seized on what might have been at most an arguable technical violation of the rule, to object to interrogatories that were clear and concise where the interrogatories did not even arguably violate the presumptive numerical limitation set by statute.” (Id. at 1287, [emphasis in original].) “In so doing, plaintiffs themselves engaged in the type of gamesmanship and delay decried by the drafters of the Act.” (Id.)

 

Here, the Court finds the challenged interrogatories are closer to those in Clement than Catanese. Although Defendant’s interrogatories refer to an outside document (the Complaint), they do not function to circumvent the interrogatory limit. Defendant’s interrogatories simply ask Plaintiff to identify facts in support of the claims made in his Complaint. Taken at face value, Plaintiff’s argument suggests that Defendant’s interrogatories would be proper if she just deleted the phrase “as stated in Paragraph [X] of the Complaint.”

 

In short, Defendant’s interrogatories request information which does not require the type of extrinsic search that Catanese contemplates. Nor do they function to circumvent the 35 interrogatory limit. Instead, Plaintiff’s objections reflect the exact type of technical violation decried by the holding in Clement.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further responses to SPROG is GRANTED.

 

RFPD

 

Each of Defendant’s RFPD asks Plaintiff to produce documents related to either claims made in his Complaint or related to his response to a certain interrogatory. For example, RFPD No. 6 asks Plaintiff to identify all documents related to any psychiatric treatment he sought as the result of Defendant’s alleged conduct. As another example, RFPD No. 10 requests Plaintiff produce any social media postings they made pertaining to Defendant’s alleged conduct.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause of further production as to each request. Each of these requests related to the dispute between the parties and the contentions made by Plaintiff in the Complaint. As such, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to justify his objections thereto.

 

Plaintiff objected to each of Defendant’s RFPD as follows:

 

This RFP violates the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.030(c)(1) in that it fails to designate the documents, things, etc. to be inspected, copied, tested, or sampled either by specifically describing each individual item or by reasonably particularizing each category of item; Lack of relevance; no tendency to lead to discovery of admissible evidence; overbroad.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff’s burden on opposition is to justify his objection to Defendant’s RFPD. Plaintiff’s opposition does not do this with particularity, instead generally asserting that this objection is meritorious without discussing the RFPD to which they are directed. The only RFPD which Plaintiff’s opposition addresses specifically is RFPD No. 1, which the Court will discuss below. Further, despite the fact that Plaintiff asserted many objections to each RFPD, his opposition briefs only his objection on grounds that each of the RFPD fail to particularize the documents to be produced. The Court finds this objection to be without merit.

 

A party cannot be expected to specifically describe an individual item without first ascertaining its existence. (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 22.) To this end the discovery statute requires that a propounding party specifically describe the item or “reasonably” particularize each category of item. “The ‘reasonably’ in the statute implies a requirement such categories be reasonably particularized from the standpoint of the party who is subjected to the burden of producing the materials.” (Id.)

 

In the Court’s view, Defendant’s requests are reasonably particularized within the meaning of C.C.P. § 2031.030 (c)(1). Even where Defendant’s requests are broad (for example RFPD No. 1 requests all documents relied upon in responding to FROG), the requests are still reasonably particularized within the meaning of the statute. Each request clearly identifies a category of items to be produced (documents relied upon in FROG, documents evidencing medical damages, documents evidencing social media posts, etc.). The Court does not see how Plaintiff could interpret these requests as asking them to produce documents beyond those narrow categories.

 

Further, Defendant’s RFPD are simply not the type of request which C.C.P. § 2031.030 (c)(1) seeks to prohibit. This statute is intended to prohibit impermissibly vague “fishing expeditions” at preliminary states of discovery. (Id. at 224.) Defendant’s RFPD do not request “all documents Plaintiff intends to rely on trial”, they instead request reasonably particularized categories of documents in connection with claims Plaintiff has explicitly made.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further responses to RFPD is GRANTED.

 

RFA

 

Defendant moves to compel further responses to each of her 16 RFA. Plaintiff objected to each RFA with the following:

 

This RFA violates the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 2033.060(f).

 

The only additional objection interposed was to RFA No. 12, which reads as follows:

 

Q: Admit that MATTHEW C. MOORE’s tenancy at KATHLEEN F. DAVIS’s home alleged in Paragraph 17 of the COMPLAINT was at will.

 

A: calls for a legal conclusion from a lay witness; assumes the existence of other facts.

 

The Court first notes Plaintiff’s objection to each RFA is grounded in a code section which does not apply to RFA. C.C.P. § 2033.060(f) specifically contemplates the format of interrogatories, not RFA. The form RFAs must take is governed by C.C.P. §§ 2033.010 – 2033.080. These sections have no equivalent prohibition on subparts, or compound/conjunctive/disjunctive questions. As such, these objections are without merit.

 

As concerns the objection on grounds that RFA No. 12 calls for a legal conclusion, Plaintiff presents no argument in support of this objection. It follows that he has thus failed to carry his burden to justify this objection.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further responses to RFA is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (C.C.P. § 2031.310(h).)

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff opposed the instant motions without substantial justification. Plaintiff could not have reasonably believed that each of the objections he presented were meritorious, and indeed he fails to brief many of them.  Plaintiff’s opposition to the motion concerning RFA appears even less reasonable, as it is premised on a code section which is inapplicable on its face; however, on December 16, 2204 the Court received a Notice of Serving Supplemental Responses  which the Court is optimistic are compliant.  Nevertheless to the extent they are not, the Court will rule on the motion to minimize further motion practice concerning this discovery.

 

The Court notes that Defendant is pro per and is requesting monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,060 plus filing fees.  In reviewing the Defendant’s request, the Court is unable to locate any Defendant declaration supporting this dollar amount including reasonable expenses in preparing the motions.  Nonetheless, a pro per defendant is entitled to recovery of reasonable expenses the Court will impose sanctions in the amount of $180.00 which is reimbursement for the filing fee for each motion ($60 x 3).   These are the reasonable expenses associated with the filing.
