DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV26388    Hearing Date: June 18, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

J.P., an individual;

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

EASTERN REVENUE, INC., a California Corporation; EMMANUEL UNIVERSITY, INC., a Georgia Corporation; KYLE SHANAHAN, an individual; MATT MCREE, an individual, JOANN S. HARPER, an individual; and DOES 1-30, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV26388

 Hearing Date:   6/18/24

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff’s Motion to Recover Expenses Incurred in Serving Defendants, Reasonable Attorney Fees & Filing Fees (California Code of Civil Procedure S 415.30(d)) [Res ID # 4281]; and

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order to Compel the Deposition of PMK for Eastern Revenue, Inc. and to Produce for Inspection Any Document, Electronically Stored Information, or Tangible Thing and Request for Sanctions Against Defendant Eastern and Its Attorney Marcus Dong, Jointly and Severally [Res ID # 4456].

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff J.P. (Joseph Parker) sues Defendants Eastern Revenue, Inc. (Eastern Revenue), Emmanuel University, Inc. (Emmanuel University), Kyle Shanahan (Eastern Revenue’s President), Matt McRee, Joann S. Harper, and Does 1-30 pursuant to an October 30, 2023, Complaint alleging claims sounding in fraud, unfair business practices, and breach of contract.

The Court briefly notes that the face of the Complaint does not specifically identify Plaintiff’s counsel now before the Court as the counsel referred to in the pleadings—i.e., Shiloh A. Parker, Esq., Plaintiff’s spouse. However, attachments to the Complaint show that Eastern Revenue and Shanahan have served documents on “Shiloh Angel Bentacourt” as counsel for Plaintiff. Bentacourt thus appears to be an alternative surname for Shiloh A. Parker, Esq.

B. Motion to Recover Expenses, Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30, subd. (d)

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to recover expenses incurred in serving Defendants Eastern Revenue, Shanahan, and Emmanuel University.

On June 5, 2024, Defendants Eastern Revenue and Shanahan filed a joint opposition.

On June 6, 2024—one court day late—Emmanuel University filed an opposition.

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to the June 5 opposition. The reply does not address Emmanuel University’s opposition.

Plaintiff’s motion is now before the Court.

C. Motion to Compel Person Most Qualified (PMQ) Deposition and Request for Sanctions

On March 27, 2024, Plaintiff served a notice of deposition on Eastern Revenue for the April 29, 2024, deposition of Eastern Revenue’s person most knowledgeable. The notice contains seven topics of deposition and 29 requests for production.

On April 24, 2024, Eastern Revenue served objections to the deposition notice, declaring unavailability on the noticed deposition date of April 29, 2024, and objecting without any substantive response to the seven topics of deposition and 29 requests for production.

That same day, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel the deposition of Eastern Revenue’s PMQ and request for sanctions against Eastern revenue and counsel, jointly and severally.

On June 5, 2024, Eastern Revenue filed an opposition, and on June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

Plaintiff’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Recover Expenses, Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30, subd. (d)

A. Legal Standard

A summons may be served by mail to the person to be served as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.30. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30, subd. (a).)

If a person to whom a copy of the summons and of the complaint are mailed pursuant to section 415.30 fails to complete and return the acknowledgement form set forth in subdivision (b) within 20 days from the date of such mailing, the party to whom the summons was mailed shall be liable for reasonable expenses thereafter incurred in serving or attempting to serve the party by another method permitted by this chapter, and, except for good cause shown, the court in which the action is pending, upon motion, with or without notice, shall award the party such expenses whether or not he is otherwise entitled to recover his costs in the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30, subd. (d).)

B. Analysis

1. Motion to Recover Expenses, Defendants Eastern Revenue and Shanahan: GRANTED as to Eastern Revenue but DENIED as to Kyle Shanahan.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion as to Defendant Eastern Revenue but DENIES the motion as to Defendant Kyle Shanahan.

a. Kyle Shanahan

The Court agrees with the opposition’s argument that Plaintiff’s expenses in serving the summons and Complaint on Kyle Shanahan on April 16, 2024, are not recoverable as moot. (Mot., p. 3 at § A.)

While the notice of acknowledgment documents were mailed around November 27, 2023 (Mot., Parker Decl., Ex. B), the case had been removed to federal court on December 15, 2023, and was not remanded until March 8, 2024, and by March 22, 2024, as argued by Eastern Revenue and Kyle Shanahan, these Defendants had made general appearances by, for example, posting jury fees, thus making service redundant. (Roy v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 337, 341 [“A defendant who has not yet answered has been held to have made a general appearance … if he invokes the authority of the court on his behalf, or affirmatively seeks relief”].)

The $105 in claimed costs for service in relation to Kyle Shanahan, the associated attorney’s fees in relation to that service, and the fees and costs in relation to this motion are thus not reasonable and thus not awardable as against Defendant Kyle Shanahan.

Plaintiff’s motion is thus DENIED as to Defendant Kyle Shanahan.

b. Eastern Revenue

Plaintiff has presented evidence that then-counsel for Defendant Eastern Revenue was served with summons by mail around October 30, 2023, as provided for in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.30. (Mot., Parker Decl., Exs. C [Eastern Revenue]; see Reply, p. 2 at § A.)

Plaintiff has also presented evidence that Defendant Eastern Revenue did not timely respond to the request for notice of acknowledgment, requiring Plaintiff to employ an alternate method of service on November 30, 2023, prior to removal on December 15, 2023. (Mot., Parker Decl., ¶¶ 4-5, Exs. A [proof of service].)

This evidence entitles Plaintiff to relief pursuant to section 415.30, subdivision (d).

The Court does not find merit in the opposition arguments.

First, the Court does not find merit in the opposition’s argument that Plaintiff did not mail the notice of acknowledgment to Eastern Revenue. (Mot., p. 3 at § A.) Prior to sending the notice of acknowledgment to defense counsel, Plaintiff’s counsel confirmed with Richard J. Perr—Eastern Revenue and Shanahan’s prior defense counsel—that Perr still represented Eastern Revenue and Shanahan, with Perr replying in the affirmative. The Court finds this satisfies the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 416.10.

Moreover, Eastern Revenue and Kyle Shanahan fail to point to authority supporting their arguments that Plaintiff also needed to mail the summons to Eastern Revenue directly or exhaust all methods of confirming service by mail prior to using other methods of service. (Mot., p. 3 at § A.) No such language appears in section 415.30, subdivision (d).

Third, the Court does not find merit in the opposition’s argument that Plaintiff is unable to recover fees for work expended by his spouse as counsel.

Defendants Eastern Revenue and Kyle Shanahan rely on Cohen v. American Credit Bureau, Inc. (2012) 2012 WL 847429 *1, *12 (Cohen), an unpublished New Jersey federal district court ruling, which is not binding on this Court although it may be persuasive. (People v. Leal (Jul. 25, 2023) 93 Cal.App.540 1143, 1157, fn. 1 [While “‘[California courts] are bound by decisions of the United States Supreme Court interpreting the federal Constitution [citations], [they] are not bound by the decisions of the lower federal courts even on federal questions,” [though] “they are persuasive and entitled to great weight’”], citing People v. Bradley (1969) 1 Cal.3d 80, 86.) Moreover, Cohen is distinguishable. Cohen involved entitlement to fees under cost-shifting statutes (Cohen, supra, at p. *12), such as the Fair Employment and Housing Act and the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act in California. (See Marron v. Superior Court (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1049, 1065 [defining fee-shifting statute].) Similar authority exists in California. (See, e.g., Flannery v. Prentice (2001) 26 Cal.4th 572, 584-586 (Flannery).)

In contrast, however, here, expenses in the form of costs and attorney’s fees are sought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 415.30, subdivision (d), which is not a cost-shifting statute, but rather, a general sanctions type statute like those in the Discovery Act.

Fourth, the Court finds no merit to the comparison to allowable costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 and relief under section 415.30, subdivision (d). Defendants Eastern Revenue and Kyle Shanahan provide no authority in support for this argument. Moreover, this argument is belied by the polar opposite timing of relief requested under these statutes, i.e., costs to the prevailing party after judgment versus expenses incurred in relation to service at the onset of the case.

Last, the Court determines that the costs sought are reasonable as to the service fees, attorney’s fees related to service, and fees related to drafting this motion and appearing at the hearing in relation to Eastern Revenue.

Plaintiff’s motion is thus GRANTED as to Defendant Eastern Revenue in the amount of $3,110.

However, the Court notes that the award is imposed as against Eastern Revenue alone, not current counsel Marcus Dong, which the opposition admits is a more recent counsel on the matter and was not the counsel on whom service by mail was attempted.

2. Motion to Recover Expenses, Defendant Emmanuel University: GRANTED or, in the alternative, CONTINUED.

Here, the opposition by Defendant Emmanuel University was filed one court day late on June 6, 2024. The opposition raises a factually valid basis to deny the motion; specifically, that the attorney served, Daniel Cohen, was not authorized to accept service. Despite seemingly valid service attached to the opposition, no reply to the Emmanuel University opposition appears in the record, and no arguments in response to Emmanuel University’s opposition appear in the reply on file. It is not clear whether Plaintiff had adequate notice of the opposition. The Court is therefore inclined to deny the motion as to Emmanual University, but will consider continuing the hearing if Plaintiff contends there is a basis for a reply.

 

III. Motion to Compel Deposition and Request for Sanctions

A. Motion to Compel Deposition: DENIED.

1. Legal Standard

If, [1] after service of a deposition notice, [2] a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, [3] without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, [4] fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, [5] the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a) [Arabic numerals added for clarity].)

If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230.)

The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).)

The motion shall also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)

If the proposed deponent made some response to the deposition notice, then a separate statement must accompany any motion to compel deposition attendance or production. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (b).)

2. Analysis

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion, without prejudice, for various reasons.

First, as argued by the opposition (Opp’n, p. 2), Plaintiff’s motion does not contain a meet and confer declaration or a declaration establishing a follow-up with Eastern Revenue following the April 24, 2024, objections. The only declaration attached to the moving papers is a declaration from counsel relating procedural history up to Eastern Revenue’s service of objections to the deposition notice. Plaintiff filed this motion that same day. (Mot., Parker Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.)

Second, Plaintiff has filed a motion with points and authorities that are cursorily brief and do not provide much detail on why each of the requested topics of deposition or requests for production is proper and warrants an order compelling the requested deposition and production. (Mot., 3:22-25 [entirety of substantive argument]; see Reply, 2:17-19 [same].) Third, neither did Plaintiff file a separate statement despite the fact that objections were served here. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(b).) The purpose of the separate statement is to streamline adjudication of discovery motions. (Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893 (Mills).) Lacking the detailed information for the merit of the many topics of discussion and requests for production, the Court lacks sufficient grounds to grant relief as identified in the moving papers.

Last, and related to the preceding paragraph, the moving papers fail to elaborate good cause for production. (Mot., 3:22-25 [entirety of substantive argument]; see Reply, 2:17-19 [same].) This omission shows failure to meet the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (b)(1). Moreover, the opposition raises good faith objections to some of the categories requested, which are not rebutted in the reply. (Opp’n, 3:3-18; Reply, 2:2-23.)

The Court briefly notes that though the second and third bases discussed above are not raised in the opposition, they constitute violations of an additional two of four statutory/court authorities that must be satisfied for the Court to grant the relief requested. Relief is inappropriate under these circumstances.

B. Request for Sanctions: DENIED.

1. Legal Standard

If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

2. Analysis

The request for sanctions is DENIED in line with the Court’s ruling on the motion to compel deposition.
