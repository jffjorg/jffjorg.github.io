DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV05451    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

SONIA GRAMAJO-OLIVARRI,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

21STCV05451

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Denied

 

 

ALLIANTE INTERNATIONAL,

NORTHGATE GONZALEZ MARKET, INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, CARMEN MORENO, AN INDIVIDUAL, AND DOES 1-10, INCLUSIVE

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             January 14, 2025

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff Sonia Gramajo-Olivarri

 

Responding Party:      Defendants Alliance International and Northgate Gonzalez Market

 

HEARING: Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers.

 

RULING

           

            The Court denies the Motion for Leave to Amend.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 10, 2024, Plaintiff Sonia Gramajo-Olivarri filed the Complaint against Defendants Alliante International, Northgate Gonzalez Market, Roblez, and Moreno.

 

On May 21, 2021, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC) against Defendants Alliante International, Northgate Gonzalez Market, Christian Roblez, and Carmen Moreno alleging causes of action for negligence, negligence per se, negligent hiring, supervision, or retention, false arrest, false imprisonment, assault, battery, and IIED.

 

On December 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File the Second Amended Complaint.

 

            On December 31, 2024, Defendants filed the Opposition.

 

            No reply has been filed.

 

            Trial is set for May 5, 2025.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Code of Civil Procedure §473 subdivision (a)(1) states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars and may, upon like terms, allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”   

 

Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and therefore, the courts have held that “there is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296–297; see also Ventura v. ABM Industries, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 268) [“Trial courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial where the adverse party will not be prejudiced.”].) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend, and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 543; see also Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [“[I]t is an abuse of discretion to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.”] 

 

Pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court 3.1324(a), a motion to amend must: (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered; and (2) state what allegations are proposed to be deleted from or added to the previous pleading and where such allegations are located. Cal. Rules of Court 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration that accompanies the motion, stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            The case centers on an incident at Northgate Gonzalez Market on January 19, 2020, where Plaintiff Sonia Gramajo-Olivarri alleges she was assaulted and falsely imprisoned by two loss prevention agents, Christian Roblez and Carmen Moreno, employed by Defendant Alliante International. Both agents are no longer employed by Alliante. Plaintiff initially filed the Complaint on February 10, 2021, alleging multiple causes of action, including negligence, false imprisonment, assault, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. The FAC was filed on May 21, 2021, identifying Roblez and Moreno as Defendants. Plaintiff now seeks to file a Second Amended Complaint (SAC). Plaintiff states that she wishes to add Christian Roblez as a named Defendant. Christian Roblez was named in the FAC but was dismissed on November 13, 2024.

 

            Plaintiff argues that the court should grant leave to file the SAC due to new evidence and developments uncovered during the discovery process. Initially, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Alliante, Northgate, Carmen Moreno, and Christian Roblez, but later dismissed Roblez without prejudice after he voluntarily agreed to a deposition. Plaintiff now seeks to rename Roblez and serve Moreno as Defendants based on their involvement in the alleged incident and Defendants’ conduct during the discovery process, which Plaintiff claims has been obstructive and delayed the resolution of the case.

 

Plaintiff contends that the amendment relates back to the original complaint filed on February 2, 2021, as it arises from the same set of facts and course of conduct. (Smeltzley v. Nicholson Manufacturing Co. (1977) 18 Cal.3d 932, 939-94.) Plaintiff further asserts that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the SAC because it does not introduce new legal theories or require additional preparation costs. Moreover, the trial has already been rescheduled to May 2025, allowing sufficient time for Defendants to address the amended claims.

 

Plaintiff highlights significant challenges in obtaining discovery, including last-minute deposition cancellations and alleged evasive behavior by Roblez after he became represented by defense counsel. Plaintiff argues that these actions have delayed the trial and impeded their ability to prepare the case.

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's motion for leave to file the SAC should be denied due to procedural deficiencies, lack of evidentiary support, and futility of the amendment. Defendants assert that Plaintiff has failed to comply with the California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires the motion to identify the specific amendments, their locations within the pleading, and the newly discovered facts or legal theories justifying the amendment. Plaintiff’s motion does not attach the proposed SAC and explicitly states that it is identical to the FAC, undermining the necessity of the amendment. Defendants further highlight that Plaintiff has not provided an adequate explanation for the timing of the amendment or new evidence warranting the renaming of Christian Roblez as a Defendant.

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff dismissed Roblez as a Defendant on November 14, 2024, because he was deemed unnecessary to the lawsuit and agreed to sit for a deposition. Plaintiff’s subsequent request to reinstate Roblez as a Defendant is unsupported by new facts and appears to be motivated by dissatisfaction with Roblez’s deposition testimony. Defendants contend that this request is retaliatory and indicative of gamesmanship.

 

Defendants also emphasize that the amendment is futile because no new evidence or facts justify Roblez’s inclusion as a necessary party to the lawsuit. Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not demonstrated good cause for the amendment, and the motion arises from the same facts already known at the beginning of the case. (Record v. Reason (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 472, 473.) Defendants highlights that courts deny amendments based on untimely, unsupported, or retaliatory motives, particularly when the trial date is approaching, and the amendment would disrupt proceedings without adding substantive value.

“There is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler, supra, 39 Cal.3d 290, 296–297.) However, a motion to amend must contain the facts that led to the amendment and when they were discovered.  (Cal. Rules of Court 3.1324(b).) Here, however, the Plaintiff has not provided any new facts that would require amendment. In fact, the stated reason for the amendment is the Defendant’s counsel’s gamesmanship and Roblez’s evasiveness during the deposition. Neither of these are facts that would lead to new causes of action or theories of liability.

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion for Leave to Amend.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437 subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)  

 

As to each cause of action, as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2); see also Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Patricia Agee has been employed with the Department of Homeland Security, Transportation Security Administration (TSA) at LAX for 16 years, serving as a Lead Transportation Security Screening Officer for the past four years and a Transportation Security Officer for the prior 13 years. (UMF 1, 3.) Her duties include inspecting items that trigger alarms, opening passenger suitcases, and ensuring no prohibited items are present. (UMF 4.) Plaintiff's work assignments rotate every six months. (UMF 5.) On May 8, 2022, she began a new rotation in Terminal 6, having previously been assigned to Terminal 7. (UMF 6.)

 

 The day before the incident, she traversed the same area approximately three times during her new assignment. (UMF 10.) On May 9, 2022, at around 3:30 a.m., Plaintiff missed the step and fell while leaving an office to clock in for her shift. At the time of the incident, the step was painted yellow, but Plaintiff was looking straight ahead rather than at the floor. (UMF 11,12.)

 

            Cause of Action 1 – Dangerous Condition of Public Property

 

            A public entity may be liable for a dangerous condition of public property if the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, the dangerous condition caused the injury, the condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the type of injury that occurred, and either a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee within the scope of their employment created the condition, or the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to take measures to protect against it. (Gov. Code § 835; Sun v. City of Oakland (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1183.)

 

            Workers Compensation

 

            Defendants argue that California's workers' compensation system provides the exclusive remedy for injuries sustained by employees arising out of and in the course of employment. (Labor Code §§ 3600, 3602.) This exclusivity extends to all claims derivative of workplace injuries, barring civil actions regardless of their form. (Huffman v. Interstate Brands Companies (2004) 121 Cal. App. 4th 679, 694.) An employee is considered to be in the "course of employment" when performing duties reasonably connected to their job or benefiting their employer. (LaTourette v. W.C.A.B. (1998) 17 Cal. 4th 644, 651.) Injuries are deemed to "arise out of employment" if there is any causal connection between the job and the injury, even if the employment is only a contributory cause. (Maher v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (1983) 33 Cal. 3d 729, 734.)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s injury occurred while she was walking in an employee-only area at LAX to clock in for her shift, clearly situating the incident within the course and scope of her employment. Plaintiff's prior judicial admissions, including her workers’ compensation claim and discovery responses, confirm she was acting in her employment capacity at the time of the incident. This acknowledgment further supports the applicability of the workers' compensation exclusivity defense.

 

Although Defendants did not initially include the workers' compensation defense in their answer, California law permits unpled affirmative defenses to be raised at the summary judgment stage if the opposing party has adequate notice and opportunity to respond. Cruey v. Gannett Co. (1998) 64 Cal. App. 4th 356, 367; Nieto v. Blue Shield of California Life & Health Ins. Co. (2010) 181 Cal. App. 4th 60, 75.) Defendants argue Plaintiff had sufficient notice, given her own workers’ compensation claim and admissions.

 

Based on these factors, Defendants contend that the claim is barred under the workers' compensation exclusivity rule, as Plaintiff’s injury was proximately caused by her employment and falls squarely within the workers' compensation system's jurisdiction.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that TSA, not LAWA, is her employer, as TSA exercises control over her wages, hours, and working conditions. (Labor Code §2810.8). TSA is a federal agency, not a leasing or temporary services employer, further solidifying its employer status. (California Unemployment Insurance Code §606.5)

 

Plaintiff contends that injuries sustained on an employer's premises during employment are compensable under workers' compensation laws. (Employers Mutual Liability Insurance Company of Wisconsin v. Industrial Accident Commission, 18 Cal. Comp Cases 286, Nov. 13, 1953). However, Plaintiff asserts that LAWA is not TSA's premises, and she was merely performing her TSA duties on LAWA property when the incident occurred.

 

Plaintiff argues that under California law, workers' compensation does not preclude a civil action against third parties. (Bailey v. Reliance Ins. Co., 65 Cal. Comp Cases, (2000), Rodgers v. Workers Compensation Appeals Bd. of California, 49 Cal. Comp. cases 513, (1984); Labor Code § 3852). Workers' compensation exclusivity applies only between an employee and their employer, in this case, TSA, not a third-party entity like LAWA.

 

Plaintiff concludes that LAWA cannot claim the protection of the workers' compensation exclusivity rule because it is not her employer. LAWA was merely a premises where Plaintiff performed her duties assigned by TSA, and thus, any claims against LAWA fall outside the scope of the workers' compensation system.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that The Workers Compensation Act presumes an employment relationship if services are rendered, and this presumption applies to Plaintiff’s dual employment with both TSA and LAWA. (Labor Code § 3357, 3351.) Plaintiff’s emphasis on being employed by TSA does not preclude a dual-employer relationship, as LAWA had control over her work, including her rotations, assignments, and workspace.

 

Defendants highlight that Labor Code § 3602 bars recovery in civil actions against either employer in a dual-employment scenario. (Wedeck v. Unocal Corp. (1997), 59 Cal. App. 4th 848, 856-857.)  Plaintiff concedes LAWA controlled aspects of her work, thus establishing an employment relationship. Secondary factors from the Borello test, such as lack of distinct occupation, no specialized skill, LAWA-provided tools and workspace, and integration of Plaintiff's work into LAWA’s operations, further confirm this relationship. (See, S. G. Borello & Sons, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations (1989) 48 Cal.3d 341, 404.)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff was injured in the course of her employment at an employee-only area, linking the incident to her work duties. Plaintiff’s workers’ compensation claim constitutes a judicial admission that her injuries arose from her employment with LAWA. Defendants maintain that Plaintiff’s sole remedy lies within the workers' compensation system, regardless of comparative fault or the intentionality of the conduct.

 

California's workers' compensation system provides the exclusive remedy for injuries sustained by employees arising out of and in the course of employment. (Labor Code §§ 3600, 3602.) “Any person rendering service for another, other than as an independent contractor, or unless expressly excluded herein, is presumed to be an employee.” (Labor Code § 3357.) “An employee may have more than one employer for purposes of workers’ compensation, and, in situations of dual employers, the second or ‘special’ employer may enjoy the same immunity from a common law negligence action on account of an industrial injury as does the first or ‘general’ employer. Identifying and analyzing such situations ‘is one of the most ancient and complex questions of law in not only compensation but tort law.'” (Santa Cruz Poultry, Inc. v. Superior Court (1987) 194 Cal.App.3d 575, 578.)

 

The crucial factor in determining whether a relationship is an employee-employer relationship is whether the employee is subject to the employer's control and direction. (Borello, supra, 48 Cal.3d 341, 353. However, the Court also considers  “(1) the alleged employee's opportunity for profit or loss depending on his managerial skill; (2) the alleged employee's investment in equipment or materials required for his task, or his employment of helpers; (3) whether the service rendered requires a special skill; (4) the degree of permanence of the working relationship; and (5) whether the service rendered is an integral part of the alleged employer's business.” (Id. at 355.)

 

            When determining whether there is preemption under the Workers Compensation Act, the Court first looks to whether the injury was sustained by an act arising out of employment. (See Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal. 3d 1, 15.) If the injury did arise in the course of employment, the Court then looks to whether it was caused by an intentional act of an employee; if it was, then the claim is preempted by the Workers Compensation Act. (Id.)

 

            Here, regarding Defendant’s control of Plaintiff, nowhere is it stated the Defendant controls Plaintiff's rotations at LAX, nor who Plaintiff reports to at LAX. However, it has been shown Plaintiff has worked at LAX for 16 years, has no opportunity for profit, does not invest in her equipment and is an integral part of running the business. While Defendants have shown that many of the secondary factors lay in favor of a finding of an employee-employer relationship, the crucial factor of control has not been shown. (See, Borello, supra, 48 Cal.3d 341, 353.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendants have not shown that the action is barred by the Workers Compensation Act. (Labor Code §§ 3600, 3602.)

 

            Dangerous Condition

 

            Defendants argue that a landowner is not required to eliminate a dangerous condition if adequate warning of the danger is provided. A landowner is not an insurer of safety and can assume that individuals will recognize and avoid obvious hazards. (Denieley v. Goldmine Ski Associates, Inc. (1998) 218 Cal. App. 3d 111, 121.) “If a danger is so apparent that an invitee can reasonably be expected to notice and protect against it, the condition itself serves as an adequate warning.” (Matherne v. Los Feliz Theatre (1942) 53 Cal. App. 2d 660, 666.)

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff had sufficient warning of the step where the incident occurred. Plaintiff had walked up the step approximately 20 minutes before the fall and three times the day before. Additionally, the step was painted yellow to warn of its presence. Plaintiff admitted she fell because she was not looking at the floor but was looking straight ahead. Defendants conclude that the step was an open and obvious condition, for which Plaintiff received adequate warning, precluding liability for the incident.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that even if the step qualifies as an “open and obvious” condition, Defendants still have a duty to remedy the danger if it is foreseeable that the condition could cause injury, especially when necessity requires individuals to encounter it. (Osborn v. Mission Ready Mix (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 104.) Despite Defendant's claims that the step was adequately marked, Plaintiff contends the yellow tape was dusty and worn, the area was poorly lit, and Defendants failed to provide proper warnings or illumination.

 

Plaintiff acknowledges familiarity with the step but notes prior encounters were in daylight, contrasting with the incident’s poor lighting. Plaintiff asserts Defendant’s failure to address the step’s condition constituted a breach of duty, directly causing her injuries.

 

Plaintiff also contends that liability for dangerous conditions does not depend on the user’s due care but rather on whether the property posed a significant risk. Comparative negligence, if any, should only reduce damages and is unrelated to determining Defendant’s liability. Since reasonable inferences could be drawn in Plaintiff’s favor, summary judgment cannot be granted. Plaintiff concludes that the Court cannot determine her conduct as negligent as a matter of law, emphasizing that questions of contributory negligence must be left to the trier of fact. (See, Sparks v. Allen Northridge Market (1959) 176 Cal.App.2d. 694, 700-701.)

 

While Defendants argue that a landowner is not required to eliminate a dangerous hazard if an adequate warning is provided, Plaintiff has shown even when a danger is open and obvious, a landowner still has a duty to remedy the danger if it may cause injury. (Osborn, supra, 224 Cal.App.3d 104 115-116.) Thus, the Court finds that Defendants have not met their burden of proof to show that one or more of the elements of the cause of action cannot be proven. (Scalf, supra, 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion for Summary Judgement.
