DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV00354    Hearing Date: February 7, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

ANTRANIG KHATCHIG OHANNESSIAN,

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

LEGACY PARTNERS, LLC, et al.,

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    23VECV00354                  107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

                 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

[Tentative] Ruling

Sustained with Leave to Amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

A. Factual

 

The following factual background is drawn from the FAC (as defined below). Plaintiff Antranig Khatchig Ohannessian (“Plaintiff”) is the lessee and resident of a unit in a residential apartment complex (the “Premises”) owned and/or managed by the defendants, Legacy Partners, LLC (“Legacy Partners”), 6041 Variel, LLC (“6041 Variel”), 6041 Variel Managing Member, LLC (“Managing Member” and, collectively with Legacy Partners and 6041 Variel, the “Answering Parties”), and BCEGI, LLC (“Moving Party” and, collectively with Legacy Partners, 6041 Variel, and Managing Member, “Defendants” and, each, a “Defendant”). All of Plaintiff’s claims arise from incidents that occurred on the Premises. Plaintiff complained repeatedly to his building manager and other employees of Legacy Partners about, among other things, other tenants’ loud pool parties; animal waste and noise; the condition and maintenance of Plaintiff’s unit; and harassment, discrimination, “gossip,” and antisemitic behavior perpetrated against him by other tenants and employees of Defendants, but Legacy Partners did not adequately resolve Plaintiff’s complaints. Instead, employees of Legacy Partners actively ignored Plaintiff’s complaints, encouraged him to vacate the Premises, and retaliated against Plaintiff through “confrontational communication and/or disregard, and hostile intimidating acts.” Plaintiff thereby suffered emotional harm and incurred costs in mitigation efforts.

 

B. Procedural

 

On January 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants. On March 27, 2023, three of Defendants, the Answering Parties, answered the complaint. On May 5, 2023, the remaining Defendant, the Moving Party, filed a demurrer. Plaintiff opposed, but on July 18, 2023, this court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend.

 

On October 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”) against Defendants pleading six causes of action: (1) tortuous breach of the warranty of habitability, (2) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (3) nuisance, (4) Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq., (5) negligence, and (6) breach of contract. On October 31, 2023, the Answering Parties filed an answer. On November 27, 2023, the Moving Party filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) without a motion to strike. On January 22, 2024, Plaintiff opposed (via the “Opposition”) the Demurrer. On January 31, 2024, the Moving Party replied to the Opposition.

 

II. MEET AND CONFER REQUIREMENT

           

Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer. The Moving Party’s attorney submits with the Demurrer a declaration that he and Plaintiff’s attorney were unable to satisfy this requirement because Plaintiff’s attorney did not respond to his requests for such. This is the second time Plaintiff’s attorney has ignored the Moving Party’s attorney’s attempts to meet and confer before the filing of a demurrer in this case. (See Alvarez Decl. p. 1.) We remind parties of their obligations to meet and confer with each other in good faith.

           

III. ANALYSIS

 

The Demurrer

 

The Moving Party’s Grounds for the Demurrer

 

The Moving Party demurs to the FAC’s first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action, all on the grounds that they are “uncertain, ambiguous, unintelligible, and fail to state facts sufficient to provide adequate notice of the specific allegations against [the Moving Party].” (Notice of Demurrer pp. 1–2.) The FAC’s second and sixth causes of action are made only against 6041 Variel and not against the Moving Party.

 

The Moving Party “has no idea” which allegations of the FAC are made specifically against it as opposed to the other Defendants, in part because of grammatical errors and inconsistent capitalization and pluralization of the word “defendant(s)” in the FAC, and denies that the FAC provides enough clarity for the Moving Party to “know why it is named as a Defendant in this suit.” (Demurrer p. 5.)

 

            Demurrers Generally

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)

 

            The FAC Generally

 

The FAC identifies the Moving Party as one of the “owners or stakeholders of the [Premises], getting monies and or assets from the running and operations of the [Premises].” (FAC ¶ 6.) In the same sentence, the FAC continues, “this defendant at all material times managed and controlled [t]he [Premises],” a pluralization inconsistency that makes it unclear if that management and control of the Premises is alleged of the Moving Party, another Defendant, or multiple Defendants, including or excluding the Moving Party. (Ibid.) The FAC makes no other specific mention of the Moving Party, though it does make general allegations about all Defendants. Notably, Plaintiff alleges that “all[] Defendants are or should be aware of these ongoing concerns and complaints, and have decided to intentionally disregard him, and the [Premises]….” (FAC ¶ 33.) The FAC makes factual allegations against the Moving Party’s co-defendant Legacy Partners, whom it identifies as the “active onsite manager” of the Premises, and its agents, but the FAC does not allege any relationship between Legacy Partners and the Moving Party. (Id. at ¶ 5.) Plaintiff attaches to the FAC a copy of a lease (the “Lease”) between himself as tenant and 6041 Variel as landlord. (FAC Unmarked Exhibit.) The Lease identifies Legacy Partners as the property manager and is signed by Plaintiff and by Gheeta Sethi, whom the FAC identifies as an agent of Legacy Partners. (FAC ¶ 17, Unmarked Exhibit.) Thus, nowhere in the FAC does Plaintiff allege that the Moving Party is his landlord or the Premises’ property manager, nor does Plaintiff allege that the Moving Party is related to his landlord of the Premises’ property manager.

 

The FAC does not cite any legal authorities for its causes of action.

 

            The FAC’s First Cause of Action for Tortuous Breach of the Warranty of Habitability

 

California’s residential habitability standard is set out in Cal. Civ. C. § 1941.1. (See also Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 922–25.) Additionally, breach of an implied warranty of habitability for residential leases was recognized as an affirmative defense to an unlawful detainer action in Green v. Superior Court (1974) 10 Cal.3d 616, 629 and as a cause of action in tort against a landlord in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, at 918–19.

 

Plaintiff alleges that his unit lacked sufficient insulation and sound dampening and was not maintained in accordance with Cal. Civ. C. § 1941.1(a)(6), and that Legacy Partners had notice of these deficiencies. As noted above, the FAC also alleges that all Defendants possessed actual or constructive knowledge of all of Plaintiff’s complaints. However, knowledge of breaches of the warranty of habitability cannot create liability for third parties to a lease. Plaintiff has not alleged that the Moving Party is party to the lease, nor that the Moving Party is his landlord, nor that the Moving Party owed Plaintiff any duty under statute or the implied warranty of habitability to ensure his unit’s habitability. Thus, the FAC does not plead facts sufficient to show a cause of action for tortious breach of the warranty of habitability against the Moving Party.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the first cause of action as to the Moving Party.

The FAC’s Third Cause of Action for Nuisance

 

Plaintiffs may bring claims for private nuisance as defined in Cal. Civ. C. § 3479 per Cal. Civ. C. § 3501(1). (Adams v. MHC Colony Park, L.P. (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 601, 610.) San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. San Diego Regional Water Quality Control Bd. establishes the elements to a private nuisance cause of action and notes that “[t]he conduct necessary to make the actor liable for either a public or a private nuisance may consist of (a) an act; or (b) a failure to act under circumstances in which the actor is under a duty to take positive action to prevent or abate the interference with the public interest or the invasion of the private interest. A connecting element to the prohibited harm must be shown.” (San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. San Diego Regional Water Quality Control Bd. (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 427, 436 (internal quote marks and citations omitted); Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262–63.) In other words, to establish a defendant’s liability for private nuisance, the plaintiff must show that the defendant caused the nuisance.

 

Here, again, Plaintiff pleads he was harmed by private nuisance on the Premises and that the Moving Party knew of Plaintiff’s harm and its causes, but Plaintiff does not plead any facts to establish that Moving Party caused or was otherwise responsible for that harm or its causes. Thus, the FAC does not plead facts sufficient to show a cause of action for nuisance against the Moving Party.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the third cause of action as to the Moving Party.

 

The FAC’s Fourth Cause of Action for [Violation of] Bus. & Prof. C. § 17200 et seq.

 

Cal. Bus. & Prof. C. § 17200 et seq. relates to unfair business practices and relief thereunder may be sought by a private party pursuant to Cal. Bus. & Prof. C. § 17204, but “a private person has standing to sue only if he or she has suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property as a result of such unfair competition.” (Daro v. Superior Court (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1097 (internal quote marks omitted).) “[A] private plaintiff's remedies are generally limited to injunctive relief and restitution.” (Kasky v. Nike, Inc. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 939, 950 (internal quote marks omitted).)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that the Moving Party, among the other Defendants, engaged in unfair business practices related to its ownership and management of rental properties. However, Plaintiff does not allege any facts showing that the Moving Party’s unfair business practices are related to his injury. Plaintiff alleges no facts that support that injunctive relief or restitution could be appropriately sought from the Moving Party. Thus, the FAC does not plead facts sufficient to show a cause of action for unfair business practices against the Moving Party.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the fourth cause of action as to the Moving Party.

 

The FAC’s Fifth Cause of Action for Negligence

 

“The elements of a cause of action for negligence are: duty; breach of duty; legal cause; and damages. The existence of a duty is the threshold element of a negligence cause of action.” (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 463.)

 

The FAC alleges that “[a]s owners, operators and managers of [t]he [Premises], the Defendants… owed Plaintiff the duty to exercise reasonable care in the ownership, management and control of [t]he [Premises]. These duties owed by Defendants to exercise reasonable care include, but are not limited to: the duty to refrain from interfering with Plaintiff’s full use of and quiet enjoyment of their rented premises; the duty to comply with all applicable state and local laws governing Plaintiff’s rights as tenants; the duty to maintain Plaintiff’s premises in a safe, healthy and habitable condition for the entire term of Plaintiff’s tenancy and the duty to not obstruct Plaintiff’s full use and occupancy of their rented residences. Defendants, by the acts and omissions alleged above, so negligently and carelessly maintained, operated, and managed [t]he [Premises] as to breach the duties that they owed to Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 62–64.) Plaintiff alleges that the Moving Party owns the Premises but does not allege that the Moving Party manages, controls, maintains, or operates the Premises. Thus, while Plaintiff may allege broadly that the Moving Party owes Plaintiff a duty to exercise reasonable care in its ownership of the Premises, Plaintiff has not alleged any more particular duty than that nor any breach of the Moving Party’s duty. Thus, the FAC does not plead facts sufficient to show all the elements of a cause of action for negligence against the Moving Party.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the fifth cause of action as to the Moving Party.

 

Leave to Amend

 

The FAC follows a prior demurrer, which this court sustained in its entirety with leave to amend, against the Plaintiff’s original Complaint by the Moving Party.

 

Leave to amend on a demurrer “must be granted if there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 69 (quoting City of Dinuba v. County of Tulare (2007) 41 Cal.4th 859, 865) (internal quote marks omitted).)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s causes of action against the Moving Party generally fail because Plaintiff does not assert any connection between his complaints regarding management of the Premises and the Moving Party. In other words, the FAC does not raise any allegations that show the Moving Party is responsible for Plaintiff’s injuries and therefore the proper defendant in this case. Plaintiff broadly argues in the Opposition that the Moving Party’s status as a sophisticated real estate developer, combined with its ownership interest in the Premises, creates its liability for the Plaintiff’s causes of action. (Opposition p. 3–4.) Plaintiff describes the Moving Party as “the owner, investor and otherwise a property manager of the [Premises] and [] thus both statutorily and theoretically responsible and liable for the substandard and illegal acts and conditions at the [Premises].” (Id. at 4.)

 

It is at least possible that additional pled facts related to the Moving Party’s relationship to the Premises or to the other defendants could cure the defects of the FAC, so we grant leave to amend as to all causes of action demurred against here.

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.
