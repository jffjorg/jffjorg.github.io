DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19NWCV00820    Hearing Date: August 8, 2024    Dept: C

BLAINE HOLDING & DEVELOPMENT, LLC v. VIVERA PHARMACEUTICALS, INC.

CASE NO.: 19NWCV00820

HEARING:  08/08/24

 

#1

 

Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor BLAINE HOLDING & DEVELOPMENT, LLC’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees Pursuant to CCP §128.5 is DENIED.

 

Opposing Party to give notice.

 

This action for unlawful detainer was filed by Plaintiff/Judgment Creditor BLAINE HOLDING & DEVELOPMENT, LLC (“Plaintiff” or “Blaine”) on September 15, 2021. Default was entered against Defendant LOS ANGELES PARTYWORKS, INC. (“Defendant” or “LA Partyworks”) on November 12, 2019, and Default Judgment was entered against Defendant on September 15, 2021.

 

On April 11, 2024, this Court DENIED Defendant LA Partyworks’ Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment.

 

On April 23, 2024, Blaine filed and served the subject Motion for Attorney’s Fees pursuant to CCP §128.5. Blaine argues that it is entitled to attorney’s fees under CCP §128.5 due to LA Partyworks “frivolous” Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment.

 

Subdivision (a) of CCP §128.5 provides in relevant part: “A trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” 

 

Subdivision (f)(1)(B) provides: “If the alleged action or tactic is the making… of a written motion…that can be withdrawn or appropriately corrected, a notice of motion shall be served as provided in Section 1010, but shall not be filed with or presented to the court, unless 21 days after service of the motion or any other period as the court may prescribe, the challenged action or tactic is not withdrawn or appropriately corrected.”

 

The Motion is DENIED. Blaine was required to comply with CCP §128.5’s safe harbor provision, and they did not. CCP §128.5(f)(1)(B) requires that a Motion for Sanctions under CCP §128.5 be served at least 21 days before filing, allowing for a safe harbor period to withdraw or correct the challenged action or tactic. That was not done here. Here, Blaine did not serve this motion 21 days before filing it. They filed and served it the same day—April 23, 2024—and after the Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment had been heard and denied. The Court cannot grant a motion for sanctions under CCP §128.5 under these circumstances. 

 

Although denied on procedural grounds, the Motion would be properly denied on the merits.

 

Under CCP § 128.5(a)(b)(2), a “frivolous” action is one that is “totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.”  The fact that a motion or motions lack merit is not enough by itself to justify an award of sanctions under Section 128.5. It is error to award sanctions if it was not unreasonable for the moving party to think the issues raised were arguable, and there is no evidence of subjective bad faith or improper motive. (Garcia v. Sterling (1985) 176 Cal.App.3d 17, 20.) “[A] showing that a party’s action or tactic is totally and completely without merit does not settle the issue of whether the action or tactic was in bad faith. Such a showing is certainly evidence that the action is brought in bad faith [Citation], because a trial court is entitled to infer from the utter lack of merit that the party knew that it lacked such merit, and yet continued to pursue the action for some ulterior motive. However, the trial court may not be willing to draw the inference if it is convinced that, despite the complete lack of merit, the party was acting in the good faith (albeit erroneous and even unreasonable) belief that the action was meritorious. Thus, the inference of bad faith is one which the trial court may make, but it is not mandatory that it do so.” (Summers v. City of Cathedral City (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1047, 1073.)

 

Blaine argues that Partyworks’ CEO and attorney lied when, in support of the motion to set aside default, they represented to the Court that they did not become aware of the default until August 2023.  Blaine submits evidence that Blaine’s enforcement counsel communicated with Partyworks’ CEO and attorney about the default as early as April and May 2023. 

 

Misrepresentations to the Court are taken seriously.  In his February 15, 2024, declaration in support of the motion to set aside default, Mr. Elkaim stated he became aware of the UD Case “sometime around August 2023”. (Elkaim Decl., ¶10.) The evidence submitted by Plaintiff proves this statement is false.  The issue here, however, is whether Plaintiff has established that Partyworks’ motion to set aside default was wholly without merit and made in bad faith or with improper motive.  In this regard, Plaintiff has failed to address Partyworks’ main argument in support of its motion -- that it did not receive actual notice of the UD case in time to defend.  Moreover, Partyworks gained nothing from misrepresenting when it learned of the default.  Relief under CCP § 473.5 was denied because Partyworks did not file the motion on or before September 15, 2023. (4/11/24 Minute Order.) Whether Partyworks discovered the default in April 2023 or August 2023 was immaterial to the Court’s ruling.  While a party must demonstrate diligence in seeking equitable relief, the Court determined Partyworks was ineligible for equitable relief because it presented no evidence of extrinsic fraud or mistake to show that Plaintiff deliberately concealed the action from Defendant, or that Defendant should be excused from its failure to respond to the action. (Id.) Thus, on the merits, Plaintiff has not established entitlement to relief under CCP §128.5. 

 

Accordingly, the motion for attorney’s fees is DENIED.

Despite clear objective factors that Decedent had taken drugs/narcotics, and was having significant medical problems, Defendants negligently and carelessly did not bring Decedent in for medical treatment, did not seek appropriate medical care or treatment for Decedent, nor did they notify Plaintiffs.” (Id. ¶30.) “After the foregoing days of Decedent being very ill, subsequent to his departure from the home, Defendants, and each of them, negligently and carelessly left Decedent in the subject home alone, and unsupervised. Defendants knew that Decedent had used drugs when he left the home and was complaining of medical issues in the days that followed the time when he returned to them home. (2) Survival Action;

Uncertainty

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ claims are fatally uncertain. This argument lacks merit because “[a] special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach the failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed at the uncertainty existing in the allegations actually made.” (Butler v. Sequeira (1950) 100 Cal.App.2d 143, 145-146.) Moreover, demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond, i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif. Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Ibid.) Here, it is clear from Defendants’ other arguments that they understand what Plaintiffs at least attempt to allege, and there is no true uncertainty. The demurrer is not sustained on the basis of uncertainty.

 

First Cause of Action – Negligence/Gross Negligence/Wrongful Death

 

“ ‘Gross negligence’ long has been defined in California and other jurisdictions as either a ‘want of even scant care’ or ‘ an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct.’” (City of Santa Barbara v. Sup. Ct. (2007) 47 Cal.4th 747, 754.) Gross negligence is pled by alleging the traditional elements of negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages, along with an allegation of extreme conduct on the part of the defendant. (Rosencrans v. Dover Images, Ltd. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1082.)

 

Plaintiffs allege “Defendants breached their duty of care when they carelessly and negligently: (1) failed to have any written policies and procedures in place of what to do if a resident exhibited signs of drug withdrawal, and/or health related issues, that posed a risk of injury or death to persons such as Decedent, (2) failed to properly train personnel and the residence was not properly staffed which included the presence of volunteers who were not properly supervised, qualified, competent or trained in handling, supervising, and protecting physically and disabled individuals such as Decedent, (3) failed to provide personnel qualified and competent to assess, evaluate, and/or perform risk assessments to detect injuries including but not limited to life threatening injuries due to drug addiction, (4) failed to properly provide any training on what protocols to follow in the event of drug withdrawal and/or health related issues and/or how to recognize such withdrawal symptoms, and (5) failed to have any Narcan (naloxone) in the residence and available for administration to residents in the event of an emergency.” (FAC ¶56.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs have alleged as part of the negligence claim that “[t]he foregoing conduct of Defendants…was grossly negligent, and a complete departure from the care exercised by other facilities that provide the same services.” (FAC ¶59.) This allegation is sufficient to support a claim for gross negligence against Defendants at this this stage in the litigation. Defendants’ arguments raise factual determinations inappropriately resolved at this time, and impose higher pleading requirements than necessary to maintain this claim. The demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Second Cause of Action – Survival Action

 

The demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED. Plaintiffs have standing to assert separate causes of action which belonged to the Decedent, and survive death by statute. Such claims—such as negligence— are enforceable by the Decedent’s successor(s) in interest. (CCP §377.30.) The interests protected under this theory are limited to the loss or damage that the decedent sustained or insured before death, including penalties or punitive or exemplary damages that the decedent would have been entitled to recover had the decedent lived, and do not include damages for pain, suffering, or disfigurement. (CCP §377.34; Grant v. McAuliffe (1953) 41 Cal.2d 859, 864.)

 

Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Causes of Action – Fraud: Intentional Misrepresentation; Negligent Misrepresentation; Concealment; and False Promise

 

The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresenation are 1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); 2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); 3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; 4) justifiable reliance; and 5) damages. (See Cal. Civ. Code §1709.)

 

“The elements of negligent misrepresentation are (1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage…. [T]he allegations of reliance, must be specifically pleaded.” (National Union Fire Ins. Cor. Of Pittsburgh, PA v. Cambridge Integrated Services Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 35, 50.)

 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for fraud and deceit based on concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (f) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Marketing West, Inc. v. Sanyo Fisher (USA) Corp. (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 603, 612-613.)

 

The elements of a claim for fraudulent false promise are: (1) a promise made regarding a material fact without any intention of performing it; (2) the existence of the intent at the time of making the promise; (3) the promise was made with intent to deceive or with intent to induce the party to whom it was made to enter into the transaction; (4) the promise was relied on by the party to whom it was made; (5) the party making the promise did not perform; (6) the party to whom the promise was made was injured. (Muraoka v. Budget Rent-A-Car, Inc. (1984) 160 Cal.App.3d 107, 119.)

 

Whether intentional or negligent in nature, fraud actions are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216.) “Fraud must be pleaded with specificity… [t]o withstand a demurrer, the facts constituting every element of the fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings. (Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’” (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.) “The requirement of specificity in a fraud action against a corporation requires the plaintiff to allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

Plaintiffs allege that “Defendant ROGELIO A. SOTO, on behalf of Defendants made representations of immaterial fact, including but not limited to representations that the home was a ‘residential sober living facility’ operated by Defendants, which proved to be false. Such misrepresentation was made to JUSTIN TRUJEQUE as he was filling out the intake form on July 2, 2021. Additionally, Defendant ROGELIO A. SOTO made representations and promises that if Justin Trujeque experienced any negative symptoms from substance abuse, he would be referred to a medical facility, which was not done, and such failure proximately led to his death. Finaly, Defendant ROGELIO SOTO made a representation to Plaintiff, DAVID TRUJEQUE that ‘we will get him fixed/we will get him right.’ (FAC ¶75.) “At the time the misrepresentations were made, Defendants, including Defendant ROGER SOTO and Eli Barajas knew that Defendants’ staff members and/or employees, agents, independent contractors, and volunteers did not receive any medical training, including training regarding Fentanyl or the withdrawal effects from using that drug or other drugs. At the time of Decedent’s death, Defendants knew that Fentanyl and other drugs were dangerous, and knew they made a promise to refer Decedent to a medical facility in the event of a relapse but failed to do so. [¶] Plaintiffs… allege that Defendants through their staff members…. Intentionally grossly understated and misrepresented the above facts with the intent to induce Decedent to sign the written agreement and gain the monetary consideration thereof. In consideration of the agreement, Decedent… agreed to sign over his public assistance checks (ATM card) to offset the cost for his room and board and was forced to agree to work at the Victory Outreach ‘fundraisers and/or life skills trainings’ for free.” (FAC ¶¶79-80.) Plaintiffs allege that the residential sober living facility was owned, leased, operated, managed, and/or controlled by all named defendants. (See FAC ¶¶8-11.) Plaintiffs also allege that “[t]he directors of such home were Defendants, ROGER [or Rogelio] SOTO and JASMINE GALVEZ…. Defendants AUGIE BARAJAS and MARY BARAJAS are either the owners, pastors, officers, and/or directors of the home and responsible for the residents thereof, and/or vicariously liable for the acts and/or omission of the directors of the home.” (FAC ¶12.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs adequately allege that Defendant Rogelio Soto, on behalf of all entity defendants, made a number of oral misrepresentations in order to induce the Decedent to sign the client intake form on July 2, 2021 for money. These facts, as alleged, are sufficient to withstand the demurrer stage. The demurrer to the third, fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action is OVERRULED as to Defendants Victory Outreach International; Defendant Inner City Recovery Homes International, Inc. dba Victory Homes International, which was erroneously sued as Victory Homes International; Defendant Inner City Recovery Homes International, Inc.; Defendants Victory Outreach; and Victory Outreach Eagle Rock.

 

However, the demurrer to the third, fourth, fifth, and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to Defendants Roger Soto; Jasmine Galvez; Augie Barajas; and Mary Barajas. Plaintiffs have not alleged sufficient facts against these defendants to support a finding of their liability for claims of fraud in their individual capacities.

 

Motions to Strike

 

The Motions to Strike punitive damages are DENIED as to the entity defendants— Defendants  Victory Outreach International; Defendant Inner City Recovery Homes International, Inc. dba Victory Homes International, which was erroneously sued as Victory Homes International; Defendant Inner City Recovery Homes International, Inc.; Defendants Victory Outreach; and Victory Outreach Eagle Rock, given the Court’s ruling on the fraud claims above.

 

The Motion to Strike punitive damages is GRANTED with 20 days leave to amend as to Defendants Roger Soto; Jasmine Galvez; Augie Barajas; and Mary Barajas based on the Court’s ruling on the fraud claims as to them, in their individual capacities, above.

If a party to whom interrogatories and document demands are directed fails to respond at all, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a court order compelling answers thereto. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.) All that needs to be shown is that the discovery was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. The moving party is not required to show a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the matter informally before filing this motion. A motion to compel initial discovery responses need not show good cause, meeting and conferring, or timely filing, and need not be accompanied by a separate statement. Here, Defendant has shown that Special Interrogatories (set one), Form Interrogatories (set one) and Request for Production of Documents (set one) were properly served onto Plaintiff on September 13, 2023. The deadlines to respond have expired, and no responses of any kind have been provided.  These Motions were filed December 7, 2023— almost three months after service of the discovery. As of August 6, 2024, no Oppositions have been filed to the subject Motions.

As of August 6, 2024, no Oppositions have been filed to the subject Motions.

 

The unopposed Motions to Compel are GRANTED, and Plaintiff ERNESTO ESCOBAR CHAVEZ’s is ORDERED to provide verified responses and documents, without objection by no later than 30 days from date of the Court’s issuance of this Order. This date may be extended by stipulation of the parties. If any objections are asserted, it will be tantamount to no response at all and will be deemed a violation of this Court’s order.

 

Reasonable sanctions are GRANTED in the total amount of $705.00 Plaintiff ERNESTO ESCOBAR CHAVEZ’s and their counsel of record are jointly and severally ORDERED to pay Defendant and their counsel of record sanctions in the total amount of $705.00, payable within 30 days from the date of the Court’s issuance of this Order.
