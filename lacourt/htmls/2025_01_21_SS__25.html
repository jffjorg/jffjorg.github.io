ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On August 28, 2023, Plaintiff Silvia Vargas (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Bryan Titus Allison (“Defendant”) and Does 1-25 alleging causes of action for Motor Vehicle and General Negligence. (Compl., ¶ 10.)

 

On June 28, 2024, Counsel for Plaintiff, Barry Carl Eisenberg (“Counsel”) of the Eisenberg Law Group filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel.

 

On August 1, 2024, the Court found several deficiencies with Counsel’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – namely, the Motion lacked a MC-052 Form (Declaration of Counsel), MC-053 Form (Proposed Order), and Proof of Service of the Motion. (8/1/24 Order.) The Court denied the motion without prejudice. (8/1/24 Order.)

 

On August 8, 2024, Counsel filed the instant Motion to be Relieved as Counsel (the “Motion”). 

 

On September 24, 2024, the Court did not call a hearing and determined Counsel’s proposed Order (MC-053 Form) granting the Motion to be deficient because the Order was blank. The Court, on its own motion, continued the Hearing on the Motion to allow Counsel to serve and submit a perfected proposed Order. (9/24/24 Order.)

 

On September 30, 2024, Counsel filed a new proposed Order (MC-053 Form).

On December 11, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion from December 11, 2024, to January 21, 2025, due to various deficiencies in the Motion. (12/11/24 Order.) The Court ordered that Counsel must filed and serve supplemental papers correcting the errors noted therein at least 16 court days before the next scheduled hearing. (12/11/24 Order.) Additionally, the Court scheduled an OSC Re: Status as to Proof of Service for January 21, 2025, and advanced and continued the Non-Jury Trial, on the Court’s own motion, from February 24, 2025, to April 10, 2025. (12/11/24 Order.)

 

On December 18, 2024, Counsel filed Notice of Ruling.

 

Also on December 18, 2024, Counsel filed a new Notice of Motion and Motion (MC-051 form) and Declaration (MC-052 form).

 

To date, no Opposition to the Motion has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 284 states that “the attorney in an action…may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination, as follows: (1) Upon the consent of both client and attorney…; (2) Upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 284; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133); see Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Forms MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) 

 

In addition, California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 subsection (d) requires that the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case by personal service, electronic service, or mail. If the notice is served by electronic service, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating that the electronic service address is the client’s current electronic service address. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (d)(2).) As used in this rule, “current” means:

 

“[T]he address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client’s last known address and was not returned or no electronic delivery failure message was received is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current.”

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)

 

III.              Discussion

 

On August 8, 2024, Counsel moved the Court to relieve him as attorney of record for Plaintiff. (MC-051.) Counsel filed MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053 Forms. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) Counsel seeks to be relieved due to a breakdown of the attorney-client relationship (MC-052, ¶ 2.) The Court is satisfied with Counsel’s reasons for seeking to be relieved.

 

While Counsel rectified most of the past deficiencies, the Proposed Order (MC-053 Form) still fails to list the proper next hearing date (MC-053, ¶ 7) and now also fails to include the newly updated OSC Re: Failure to File Proof of Service and Non-Jury Trial dates. (MC-053, ¶¶ 8-9.)

 

Although the Court is satisfied with Counsel’s reasons for seeking to be relieved, the Court cannot grant the Motion without adequate forms. The Court will GRANT the Motion conditional upon receipt of proper MC-53 form before the hearing date by hearing on Tuesday January 21, 2025 at 10:00 a.m. By next scheduled hearing, Counsel must file and serve a new MC-053 form. Failure to do so will result in the Motion being placed off calendar or denied. 

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

Although the Court is satisfied with Counsel’s reasons for seeking to be relieved, the Court cannot grant the Motion without adequate forms. The Court will GRANT the Motion conditional upon receipt of proper MC-053 form before the hearing date on Tuesday January 21, 2025 at 10:00 a.m. Failure to do so will result in the Motion being placed off calendar or denied. 

 

Proposed Order having been received; the Motion is granted.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff AC Hospitality Consulting LLC (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendants Town Hall Eatery LLC (“Town Hall Eatery”), Town Hall, and Shaya Stauber (“Stauber”) (collectively, “Defendants”) and Does 1-20 alleging causes of action for Breach of Contract and Common Counts. (Compl., ¶ 8.) This action arises out of a written agreement between the parties – namely the “Consulting Agreement” – and Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants failed to pay Plaintiff fees for services under the agreement.” (Compl., ¶¶ BC-1, BC-2, Attach.) In the Complaint, Plaintiff also stated that “Plaintiff waives damages in excess of $35,000.” (Compl., ¶¶ 10(d), BC-4.)

 

On September 25, 2024, Town Hall Eatery and Stauber filed an Answer, and Town Hall Eatery filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, Alissa Conte (“Conte”), and Roes 1-20.

 

On November 6, 2024, Conte filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint, and Plaintiff filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

 

On November 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) (the “Motion”).

 

On January 7, 2025, Town Hall Eatery and Stauber filed an Opposition.

 

On January 13, 2025, Plaintiff filed a Reply.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

Plaintiff files proof of service of the Motion and accompanying papers on Town Hall Eatery and Stauber; however, Plaintiff fails to file proof of service on Town Hall.

 

            However, given that Town Hall has not appeared in the action, the Court exercises its discretion to address the Motion on the merits.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

            Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a), which states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

“Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation].  A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

 

            A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires that a motion to amend must:  “[i]nclude a copy of the proposed… amended pleading… [and] state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be [deleted and/or added], if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the [deleted and/or additional] allegations are located.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1321(a).)

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also include “[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1) The effect of the amendment; (2) Why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1321(b).)

 

IV.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks leave of court to file a FAC that would assert additional causes of action – namely, promissory estoppel, negligent misrepresentation, and specific performance – and increase the damages sought from $35,000 to at least $72,000. (Jensen Decl., ¶ 13.)

 

In Opposition, Town Hall Eatery and Stauber contend the Motion should be denied because “(1) [the] amendments are futile, as the newly asserted claims fail to state valid causes of action under California law, (2) Plaintiff has unreasonably delayed seeking leave to amend despite having knowledge of the proposed claims and facts for nearly a year, and; (3) The proposed amendments would cause substantial prejudice to Defendants by expanding the scope of discovery and altering the trajectory of the litigation.” (Opp., p. 2.) Specifically, Town Hall Eatery and Stauber challenge the sufficiency of each cause of action alleged in the proposed FAC; argue the FAC brings a new set of allegations entirely unrelated to the original claims, as evidenced by the new request for damages that nearly doubles the damages originally claimed; and state the amendments would significantly increase the burden of litigation. (Opp., pp. 3-9.) Further, Town Hall Eatery and Stauber highlight the first round of discovery has already been completed and argue Plaintiff inexcusably delayed in seeking leave to amend because Plaintiff has known the facts underlying the proposed amendments since at least December 2023. (Opp., p. 9.)

 

In Reply, Plaintiff refutes Town Hall Eatery and Stauber’s arguments in their entirety, stating their challenges to the cause of action are improperly raised in an opposition to a motion for leave to amend (Reply, pp. 3-4); case law promotes liberality in granting leave to amend and Town Hall Eatery and Stauber fail to raise any legal authority stating otherwise (Reply, pp. 4-5); and Town Hall Eatery and Stauber are not prejudiced by this proposed FAC and fail to state a non-conclusory argument that shows otherwise. (Reply, pp. 5-6.) As to any alleged delay in failing to bring the Motion, Plaintiff disputes this allegation and states: “Defendants are the sole cause of any delay. Plaintiff attempted to seek consent to filing its FAC months ago; those requests were ignored by Defendants’ predecessor counsel and then by its current counsel. (See Decl. of Michael Jensen ISO Mot. at ¶¶ 3-12.) Defendants’ claim that Plaintiff delayed [is] dubious and undermined by their own conduct.” (Reply, p. 6.)

 

Here, the Court finds Plaintiff’s Motion substantially complies with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a). The Motion includes a copy of the proposed FAC and sets forth the allegations to be added and/or deleted. (Jensen Decl., pp. 1, 3, Exh. A.) While the allegations to be added/deleted do not include page, paragraph, or line number demarcations, the Court finds this reasonable given that Plaintiff is changing the form of the Complaint from a Judicial Council form complaint to a complaint on pleading paper. (Compl.; Jensen Decl., ¶ 13.) However, the Court notes Plaintiff fails to set forth in the Motion that the FAC does not include a Common Counts claim, which was alleged in the original Complaint.

 

Additionally, the Motion includes a separate declaration from attorney Michael J. Jensen as is required California Rules of Court, rule 3.1321(b). Jensen’s declaration states: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) the reasons why the amendment is necessary and proper – namely, the amendments do not fundamentally alter the nature of the case or involve new areas of factual inquiry not implicated by prior pleadings, trial does not take place until December 2025, there is no prejudice to Defendants, and allowing Plaintiff to raise all claims against Defendants furthers justice; (3) the facts giving rise to the amendments were discovered while Plaintiff conducted further investigation of Plaintiff’s claims and the recently filed Cross-Complaint in September 2024 (the Court notes that the Motion was filed six days after Plaintiff filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint); and (4) “[t]his amendment is being sought at the earliest stage of litigation. The reason this request for amendment was not made earlier is because counsel for Defendants has not responded to any of [Plaintiff’s counsel’s] attempts to resolve this via stipulation. Further, due to the aggressive nature of the Cross-Complaint, Plaintiff must now respond accordingly by asserting all available claims.” (Jensen Decl., pp. 3-4, ¶¶ 3-16.)

 

Accordingly, the Court finds Plaintiff has fully complied with the requirements set forth in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

 

The Court also addresses the contentions raised in the Opposition. As to Town Hall Eatery and Stauber’s challenges to the sufficiency of each of Plaintiff’s proposed new causes of action, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that an opposition to a motion for leave to file a FAC is not the proper procedural mechanism to address such claims. As to Town Hall Eatery and Stauber’s claims of prejudice and that Plaintiff inexcusably delayed in filing the Motion, the Court notes this Motion was filed less than two months after Town Hall Eatery and Stauber appeared in the action, and Town Hall Eatery and Stauber did not address or dispute that Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to professionally resolve the FAC and issues raised therein by way of stipulation and/or mediation prior to this Motion.

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion is GRANTED.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff AC Hospitality Consulting LLC’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is to electronically file and serve the First Amended Complaint within 10 days of this Court’s order.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
