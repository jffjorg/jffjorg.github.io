DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV00591    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Doe v. Weinstein, Case No. 23SMCV00591

Hearing Date: April 25, 2024

Plaintiff’s Motion for Stay of Discovery Pending Resolution for Parallel Criminal Proceedings

 

On December 19, 2022, defendant Weinstein was convicted of raping plaintiff Jane Doe, who now sues based on that assault. Plaintiff seeks a stay of the proceeding pending resolution of Weinstein’s criminal appeal.

 

Courts have broad discretion to stay cases in the interest of efficiency and justice. Every court has the “inherent power, in its discretion, to stay proceedings when such a stay will accommodate the ends of justice.” See St. Paul Fire and Marine Ins. Co. v. AmerisourceBergen Corp. (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 1, 6-7.  Courts are empowered to “provide for the orderly conduct of proceedings before it.” C.C.P. §128(a)(3); see C.C.P. §187 [conferring on courts and judicial officers “all the means necessary” to carry out their jurisdiction].

 

“The decision whether to stay civil proceedings in the face of a parallel criminal proceeding should be made ‘in light of the particular circumstances and competing interests involved in the case.’ [Citation.] This means the decisionmaker should consider ‘the extent to which the defendant's fifth amendment rights are implicated.’ [Citation.] In addition, the decisionmaker should generally consider the following factors: (1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.” Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885, quoting Keating v. Office of Thrift Supervision (9th Cir. 1995) 45 F.3d 322, 324.

 

In Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court petitioners were not criminal defendants, but were threatened with criminal prosecution and granted a stay to prevent prosecutors from monitoring the civil proceedings, hoping to obtain incriminating testimony from petitioners via civil discovery. The court held this would undermine the Fifth Amendment privilege and violate concepts of fundamental fairness. Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 686, 690.

 

Weinstein cites Avant to support his contention that plaintiff has no basis for seeing a stay other than to reap the benefits of the appeal and not prosecute the case. Bains held the trial court did not abuse its discretion in declining a stay when it was moving party did not satisfy the factors outlined. Avant! Corp. Bains v. Moores (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 445, 485-486. Here, plaintiff has an interest in the appeal being resolved, as the outcome determines her ability to raise issue preclusion. Weinstein would not be burdened because, as plaintiff’s counsel notes, neither party has taken depositions, and the matter is in the early stages of discovery. Shukry Decl. ¶¶ 2,7. Weinstein failed to show a stay would burden him, particularly given that he began scheduling depositions only after plaintiff indicated her intention to seek a stay. Id. ¶ 2. The matter is further distinguishable from Bains, as the proceedings have not run as long, nor has the court previously issued a stay.

 

A stay would additionally promote judicial efficiency by avoiding duplicative efforts for the parties and the court. Furthermore, a stay would be in the public interest, noting that public interest favors a stay to prevent the subversion of a criminal proceeding. Thus, plaintiff has met her burden. GRANTED.


Case Number: 23SMCV00779    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

O’Dell v. Powell, et al. Case No. 23SMCV00779

Hearing Date: April 25, 2024

Defendants Il Pastaio’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

 

Plaintiff was shot by unknown assailants while dining at defendant Il Pastaio and alleges defendants failed to keep the premises safe. Defendants Il Pastaio and Drago demurrer to the third and fifth causes of action for negligence and negligent infliction of emotional distress. No opposition was filed.

 

Negligence

To state a claim for negligence, a party must allege (1) duty, (2) breach, and (3) causation. CACI 400, see, e.g. McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671. For purposes of negligence liability, a landlord has a duty to take reasonable precautions to protect patrons from highly foreseeable third-party crimes. Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal.4th 666, 679. Foreseeability can be proven through similar past incidents, but they are not required. Absent prior similar incidents, a duty of care can be shown with other factors such as “the nature, condition, and location” of the premises.

Defendants argue the negligence claim fails because plaintiffs have not alleged prior similar incidents, so fail to establish a duty of care. Per Ann M., prior similar incidents are not the only way to establish a duty of care.

Plaintiff alleges “In the six years prior to the robbery at the Premises,” defendants had “over 300 calls to the premises by law enforcement,” which included “disturbances for fighting, robbery, battery, grand theft, petty theft, assault, and keeping the peace, among others.” Complaint ¶25. The complaint further alleges defendants had “sufficient knowledge of the dangerous condition of the Premises and the potential hazards to restaurant[.]” Id. ¶26. For pleading purposes, this sufficiently alleges the attack was “highly foreseeable.” OVERRULED.

Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (NIED)

 

“The law of negligent infliction of emotional distress in California is typically analyzed by reference to two theories of recovery: the ‘bystander’ theory and the ‘direct victim’ theory. The negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort, but the tort of negligence. The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply. Whether a defendant owes a duty of care is a question of law.” Spates v. Dameron Hosp. Ass’n. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 208, 213.

 

Defendants contend the NIED claim repackages the negligence cause of action and focuses on emotional distress rather than physical injury damages.

 

The complaint alleges plaintiff was lawfully on the premises when she was robbed. Complaint ¶¶ 33-34. Plaintiff further alleges that as a result of the robbery, she fractured her shoulder and suffered pain in her spine. Id. ¶ 36. This is sufficient to state a cause of action at the pleadings stage. OVERRULED. Defendants to answer within 10 court days.


Case Number: 23SMCV04014    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: P


Tentative Ruling

Tenser v. Ryan, et al., Case No. 23SMCV04014

Hearing date April 25, 2024

Defendants County of Los Angeles, Silverman, Mokayef, Cotter, Martindale and Gascon’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint

Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendant Townsend’s anti-SLAPP Motion

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend the FAC

On February 7, 2024 the County, Silverman, Mokayef, Cotter, Martindale and Gascon (collectively, “County Defendants”) filed a demurrer to the FAC. As of April 23, 2024, no opposition has been filed. Tenser filed a motion for leave to amend and file a Second Amended Complaint, which is set to be heard on the same day as this motion, on April 25, 2024.

Request for Judicial Notice

The County Defendants request judicial notice of the fact that Los Angeles County District Attorney George Gascón took office on December 7, 2020, and of several filings and court orders in the matters of Tenser v. Ryan, et al., United States District Court, Central District of California (Case Number CV 19-05496 VBF) and Tenser v. Ryan, et al., Los Angeles County Superior Court (Case Number 20SMCV01690). The latter was dismissed, and the dismissal was affirmed on January 17, 2024, by the Court of Appeal.

“Judicial notice may be taken of . . . [r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United Sates or of any state of the United States” and of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (d) and (h).)  Section 452 provides a proper basis for judicial notice of the documents in the County Defendants’ request. 

Upon taking notice of court records, the court accepts as true only that (1) they were filed and (2) the assertions therein were made; the court does not take notice of the truth of their contents.  (See Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374-375; see also Day v. Sharp (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 904, 916.)

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.  Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.  The court accepts as true all material factual allegations and affords them a liberal construction, but it does not consider conclusions of fact or law, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or judicially noticed facts.  Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254. A demurrer will be sustained without leave to amend if there exists no reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.

“Suits for money or damages filed against a public entity are regulated by . . . the Government Claims Act.” DiCampli-Mintz v. County of Santa Clara (2012) 55 Cal.4th 983, 989. Under the Act, “[b]efore suing a public entity, the plaintiff must present a timely written claim for damages to the entity.”  Shirk v. Vista Unified Sch. Dist. (2007) 42 Cal.4th 201, 208; see also Gov’t. Code §945.4 (“no suit for money or damages may be brought against a public . . . until a written claim therefor has been presented to the public entity and has been acted upon by the board, or has been deemed to have been rejected . . . .”). “[F]ailure to timely present a claim for money or damages to a public entity bars a plaintiff from filing a lawsuit against that entity.”  State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1239.

 

A plaintiff is required to present his claim to the public entity no later than one year after accrual of the cause of action. Gov. Code §911.2(a). Plaintiff must generally file suit within six months after the rejection. Gov. Code §945.6. “[A] plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating or excusing compliance with the claim presentation requirement.  Otherwise, his complaint is subject to a general demurrer for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.”  Bodde, supra at 1243.

 

Tenser’s allegations against the County Defendants arise from conduct that occurred in July 2018. See FAC ¶¶ 24–35, 49–60, 86-90. Government Code section 911.2 requires a claim to be presented “not later than one year after the accrual of the cause of action.” Gov. Code §911.2(a).  If claimant misses the deadline, he may apply to the County to file a late claim. Gov. Code §911.4(a). That application must be made within “a reasonable time not to exceed one year after the accrual of the cause of action.” Gov. Code §911.4(b). 

 

Tenser alleges he complied with Gov. Code §§911.2 and 945.4 by mailing, on August 28, 2023, the County’s form claim notice to the County Board of Directors (the “Claim Notice”). FAC ¶ 87. The claim was deemed rejected on October 13, 2023 because the County failed to take action. FAC ¶ 88. Tenser filed this action December 26, 2023. 

 

Pursuant to Gov. Code §§911.2 and 911.4, Tenser’s one-year deadline to file a claim or apply for leave to file a late claim ran in July 2019. Tenser makes no showing that he filed any

government claim or application to file a late claim by that deadline. To the contrary, the FAC shows Tenser missed that deadline by over four years. 

 

Tenser appears to claim his claim accrued when he discovered Townsend’s podcast episode on the Leibel trial on August 25, 2023. FAC ¶ 87. However, this allegation only supports Tenser’s claim against Townsend, not County Defendants. The FAC alleges the podcast is slanderous because it “repeats” Silverman and Mokayef’s slanderous statement. FAC ¶ 20. Tenser does not allege he discovered the alleged slanderous statement through the podcast. This is because Tenser was made aware of the slanderous statement the moment it was uttered by Silverman and Mokayef, because Plaintiff was present at the scene and within earshot when Silverman and Mokayef called him a stalker.  FAC ¶ 25.

 

Tenser files no opposition, so provides no counter to the County Defendants’ arguments. Per Gov. Code section 950.2, the claims against the individual defendants are similarly barred by the Government Claims Act. SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

Plaintiff attorney Tenser represented Blake Leibel in a highly publicized murder trial. Plaintiff alleges defendant Townsend published a defamatory podcast episode that described plaintiff as a stalker.

Motion to Strike: The first amended complaint alleges defamation, invasion of privacy and fraud against Townsend. On March 4, 2024, Townsend filed a special motion to strike (anti-SLAPP) which is set for hearing on June 6, 2024. On March 11, plaintiff filed the instant motion “to strike” Townsend’s motion to strike. Townsend filed her opposition on April 3, 2024. As of April 16, 2024, no reply has been filed.

Plaintiff purports to make this motion to strike Townsend’s motion to strike pursuant to CCP §436. Plaintiff described section 436 to “authorize the striking of a pleading due to improprieties in conformity the laws of this state.” Motion, 10:15-16. CCP § 435(a)(2) defines “pleading” to mean “a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint.” Townsend’s motion is none of those. DENIED.

Motion for Leave to Amend: The FAC (1) defamation, (2) invasion of privacy against Townsend, (3) fraud against Townsend, and (4) public entity liability against the County of Los Angeles. The first cause of action contains five counts: (a) slander per se against Silverman and Mokayef, (b) libel against Ryan, (c) conspiracy to qualify defamation against Silverman, Mokayef, Cotter, Martindale and Ryan, (d) District Attorney’s ratification against DA Gascon, and (e) slander per se against Townsend.

Silverman and Mokayef are/were LA County Deputy District Attorneys. Cotter and Martindale are/were Los Angeles Sheriff Department homicide detectives. Ryan was a prosecution witness in the trial.

On February 7, 2024, the County, Silverman, Mokayef, Cotter, Martindale and Gascon filed a demurrer to the FAC. On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a proposed second amended complaint (“SAC”). On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for leave to amend the FAC.

The proposed SAC would remove the invasion of privacy and fraud causes of action against Townsend. The remaining claims would be consolidated into a single cause of action for defamation, with the following proposed counts: (a) slander per se against Townsend, (b) slander per se against Silverman and Mokayef, (c) libel against Ryan, (d) conspiracy to qualify defamation against Silverman, Mokayef, Cotter, Martindale and Ryan, (e) public entity liability against the County of Los Angeles and Gascon, and (f) Government Claims Act waiver against the County and Gascon. The count of slander per se against Townsend would be supported by new allegations that Townsend violated Canada’s Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act.

Amendment as of right

Plaintiff moves for leave to amend based on an incorrect reading of CCP §472 that guarantees a right to amend any pleading before the deadline to oppose a demurrer. Section 472(a) provides “a party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time . . . after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike.” CCP § 472(a) (emphasis added). A plaintiff’s right to amend without a Court order is limited to the original pleading. Plaintiff had the right to file an FAC as of right, and he did so. Leave to amend is required for subsequent amendments. He did not seek leave prior to filing the SAC.

Prejudice to defendants

Plaintiff moves for leave to amend on the ground that the proposed changes do not prejudice defendants, arguing the SAC “effects no change in the nature of the case” and “merely provides the addition of matters essential to make the original cause of action complete.” Motion, 6:11-14.

Leave to amend is generally liberally granted, as judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties. See Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 412. Townsend agreed to stipulate to the amendment (Tenser Decl., ¶ 2) and filed notice of non-opposition.

Plaintiff contends the remaining proposed changes were made in response to the demurrer filed by County defendants, so there is good cause to grant the amendment. County defendants argue the proposed SAC only addresses one of the seven grounds raised in the demurrer.

Plaintiff’s motion does not meet the procedural requirements of Cal. Rules of Court rule 3.1324. A motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; (2) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. CRC 3.1324(a). A separate supporting declaration specifying (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier must accompany the motion. CRC 3.1324(b).

The redline attached to the reply shows the FAC and SAC are substantially different. In County defendants’ words, the SAC includes “numerous other additions, rearrangements, and deletions, with no explanation and therefore without any showing of how justice could possibly be served by them.” Opp. 4:3-6. Nowhere does plaintiff state by page, paragraph and line number the deletions and additions. DENIED without prejudice. Alternatively, the court will consider granting the motion if plaintiff complies with CRC 3.1324.
