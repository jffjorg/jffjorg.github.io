DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV02832    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

Maria Skaleris and Peter Daniel Doss,

 

PLAINTIFF,

 

      

   V.

 

Rafael Fajardo and Does 1 to 25,

DEFENDANT.

   CASE NUMBER             DEPARTMENT

       22STCV02832                     107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff

 

 

 

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This case arises out of a car accident. (Complaint p. 4.) On January 24, 2022, Plaintiffs Maria Skaleris and Peter Daniel Doss filed a complaint against Defendants Rafael Fajardo and Does 1 to 25, alleging Defendants “owned and operated their vehicle negligently and carelessly, proximately causing property damage and personal injury to Plaintiffs.” (Complaint p. 4.)

 

On February 5, 2024, Defendant Rafael Fajardo (“Defendant”) moved to compel Plaintiff Daniel Doss’ (“Plaintiff”) appearance at a deposition. The court denied Defendant’s motion because Defendant had not submitted a meet and confer declaration. (04/08/24 Minute Order.)

 

On April 26, 2024, Defendant filed a second motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. The court denied Defendant’s motion without prejudice because Defendant had not given Plaintiff the requisite statutory notice. (05/20/24 Minute Order.)

 

On June 3, 2024, Defendant filed a third motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. The case was then reassigned to this department. (07/30/24 Minute Order.) On August 12, 2024, Defendant filed the motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition with this department. (the “Motion”). No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              DISCUSSION

  

A.    Meet and Confer

 

“[W]hen the deponent fails to attend the deposition . . . [the motion must be accompanied] by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450(b)(2).) "Implicit in the requirement that counsel contact the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance is a requirement that counsel listen to the reasons offered and make a good faith attempt to resolve the issue," including by rescheduling.  (Leko v. Cornerstone Bldg. Inspection Serv. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1109, 1123.)

 

Defendant’s counsel’s declaration states he scheduled Plaintiff’s deposition four times between April 6, 2023, and January 4, 2024, but Plaintiff failed to appear. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶¶ 10-13.) On April 18, 2024, Defendant’s counsel contacted Plaintiff about his non-appearance. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 15.) Plaintiff has not responded. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 16.)

 

The court is satisfied that counsel inquired about Plaintiff’s nonappearance and has made a good faith attempt to resolve the issue.

 

B.    Notice

 

“[A]ll moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc.§ 1005.) “However, if the notice is served by mail, the required 16-day period of notice before the hearing shall be increased by five calendar days if the place of mailing and the place of address are within the State of California.” (Code Civ. Proc.§ 1005(b).)

 

The hearing is set for October 1, 2024.  Sixteen court days before October 1, 2024, is September 6, 2024. Plaintiff was served by mail.  (08/12/24 Proof of Service.) Thus, Plaintiff must have been served with the moving papers no later than September 1, 2024.  Defendant served Plaintiff on August 12, 2024. (08/12/24 Proof of Service.) 

 

Thus, Defendant provided Plaintiff with the requisite statutory notice.

 

C.    Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance

 

A motion to compel deposition attendance may be brought when a party fails to appear for a deposition following service of a deposition notice without having served a valid objection under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.410. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450(a).) 

 

Defendant’s counsel’s declaration states that after receiving no communication from Plaintiff regarding his availability for the deposition, Defendant unilaterally set Plaintiff’s deposition for April 6, 2023. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 10.)  Plaintiff was served on March 16, 2023, but he failed to appear at the deposition. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 10.) Defendant again unilaterally set Plaintiff’s deposition for May 15, 2023. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 11.) Plaintiff was served on April 27, 2023, but failed to appear. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 11; Ex. B.) Defendant rescheduled Plaintiff’s deposition for November 21, 2023. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 12.) Plaintiff was served on October 27, 2023, but failed to appear. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 12; Ex. C.) Defendant rescheduled Plaintiff’s deposition for January 4, 2024. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 13.) Plaintiff was served December 1, 2023, but failed to appear. (Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 13; Ex. E.)

 

Accordingly, because Plaintiff was served with multiple deposition notices, but did not appear or serve a valid objection, Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition attendance is granted.

 

D.    Sanctions 

 

If a motion to compel deposition attendance is granted, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.410(g)(1).) “As to the amount of¿sanctions,¿section 2030.030, subdivision (a) authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing ‘the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result’ of a party's misuse of the discovery process.” (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.) 

 

Plaintiff requests $1,050.00 in attorneys’ fees. (Motion p. 5; Ahdoot Declaration ¶ 23.) This amount reflects $210.00 hourly rate for “[four] hours of attorney time in connection with preparing [the] Motion and related documents, and an additional hour . . . to appear for the hearing.” (Motion p. 5; Ahdoot Declaration ¶¶ 21-23.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust. The court also finds that Plaintiff’s failure to attend the scheduled depositions represents a misuse of the discovery process. The court also finds a reduction of time spent preparing the Motion is warranted because the Motion is not complex. The court therefore reduces the four hours requested to two hours.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff is granted in the reduced total amount of $630.00, reflecting two hours of preparation for the Motion and one hour for the hearing at $210.00 per hour.

 

III.            CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for his deposition within thirty (30) days, on a date agreeable to Defendants. Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $630.00.

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS¿ 

¿ 

A.    Timeliness of Filings¿ 

¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).)

 

Plaintiffs served Defendants with the Second Amended Complaint on April 4, 2024. Defendants filed a declaration of demurring or moving party in support of automatic extension on May 2, 2024. Defendants filed its demurrer on May 31, 2024. Plaintiffs filed the opposition on August 5, 2024.

 

Thus, the filings are timely.

¿ 

B.    The Meet & Confer Requirement¿ 

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41, subd. (a)(3).)¿ 

¿ 

Defendants filed a declaration by counsel indicating Defendants’ counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiffs’ counsel but the meet and confer did not result in any resolution. (Reich Declaration ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

¿ 

III. LEGAL STANDARD 

¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

IV. DISCUSSION 

 

  1. Statute of Limitations

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs failed to substantiate the delayed discovery of the alleged fraud and Plaintiffs’ causes of action are therefore barred by the statute of limitations. (Memorandum pp. 4-5.) Plaintiffs argues they “became aware of the fraudulent intentions and purpose of Defendants and each of them on or about April 2020” when “FBI agents approached the Plaintiff on or [a]fter April 2020.” (Opposition p. 4.) Plaintiffs further argue that “Defendant Michael Henschel, Shellback and the other defendants mounted serious and credible undue influence on the Plaintiff and convince[d] the Plaintiff who was well over 82 years in 2020, and failing in health, who depends on caregivers for food, daily upkeep and welfare to trust the Defendants thereby preventing the Plaintiff from filing this action immediately.” (Opposition p. 4.)

 

“The statute of limitations for a fraud based ca[u]se of action is three years.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Association (2009), 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1390.)  The discovery rule postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff “discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.” (Pooshs v. Philip Morris USA, Inc., et al. (2011) 51 Cal.4th 788, 797.)

 

Plaintiffs’ second amended complaint alleges that on January 16, 2015, “Arrowhead Group recorded a Grant Deed, Instrument # 20150060515 and transferred title and interest in the Highland Property to Todd J. Schelbach, Co-Trustee of the Orion Trust . . . The Grant Deed was recorded by Islas as Trustee of Arrowhead Group” and filed as Exhibit J. (SAC ¶ 53.) Plaintiffs then allege that “[i]n spite of the existence and recordation of Exhibit J, on or about April 1, 2020, defendants approached [Plaintiff] to sign fresh documents transferring the Highland Properties to Orio Trust” and “Plaintiffs became aware of defendants’ intention to use the falsely procured, documents to deceive the FBI and cover Defendants’ crimes, after [Plaintiff] had signed the fresh documents and transferred the Highland Properties to Orion Trust. When defendants approached plaintiffs to sign the documents, defendants pleaded for plaintiffs protection and dissuaded plaintiffs from cooperating with or trusting FBI, and to convince the FBI that defendants were good people.” (SAC ¶¶ 53-54.)

 

Plaintiff Michael Yoakum filed his complaint on April 13, 2023. Plaintiffs allege they discovered Defendants’ intentions to use the falsely procured documents after Defendants recorded the deed on April 1, 2020. Three years after April 1, 2020, is April 1, 2023. Thus, if Plaintiffs discovered or had reason to discover Defendants’ fraudulent intentions before April 13, 2020, then Plaintiffs’ causes of action for fraud would be time-barred.  Plaintiffs alleged facts showing they discovered or had reason to discover the cause of action after April 1, 2020, but it is unclear when Plaintiffs discovered or had reason to discover Defendants’ fraudulent intentions. Accordingly, Plaintiffs have not plead sufficient facts to show the statute of limitations does not bar his claim. 

 

  1. Fraud

 

Defendants argue all of Plaintiffs’ causes of actions fail because Plaintiffs failed to sufficiently plead fraud. (Memorandum p. 7.)

 

i.                 Fraud in the Inducement

 

In the second amended complaint, the first cause of action is for cancellation of instruments under Civil Code § 3412. Civil Code § 3412 states that: “A written instrument, in respect to which there is a reasonable apprehension that if left outstanding it may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable, may, upon his application, be so adjudged, and ordered to be delivered up or canceled.”

 

Fraud in the inducement when “the promisor knows what he is signing but his consent is induced by fraud, mutual assent is present and a contract is formed, which, by reason of the fraud, is voidable. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal. 4th 394, 415.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs alleges they own “real properties located at 4170 Rogers Street, Los Angeles, CA 90063-11154, described as the “Rogers Property”; and 2276 Highland Vista Drive, Arcadia, CA 91006-1535, described as the “Highland Property”, which properties are referred to herein collectively as “the Properties” or “Plaintiffs’ Properties”.” (SAC ¶ 65.) The second amended complaint alleges that “Defendants through related and associated entities and trusts, fraudulently took over Yoakum’s Properties and registered bogus title transfer documents in defendants’ favor with which they took over the Properties.” (SAC ¶ 83.) Specifically, Plaintiffs allege “plaintiffs and defendants entered into a series of contract by the terms of which defendants would modify plaintiffs’ loans and in order to reduce his payments and help manage other costs.” (SAC ¶ 19.) And, “[r]elying on defendants’ assurance as to outcomes, plaintiffs engaged defendants to audit the mortgage payments, modify the loan, and manage the Properties and plaintiffs paid the necessary fees and provided to defendants, information and documents required.” (SAC ¶ 21.) Plaintiffs also allege Yoakum “became emotionally attached to, and dependent on defendants [but u]beknownst to plaintiffs, the friendship was part of a ploy by defendants to warm themselves into Yoakum’s confidence in furtherance of the scheme to defraud plaintiffs and acquire title to the Properties.” (SAC ¶ 25.) Plaintiffs next allege Defendants diverted mail, “deliberately managed the Properties with a view to orchestrating foreclosure so that defendants would acquire the Properties” and “defendants’ loan modification service was a scam and part of a nationwide scheme to defraud, exploit, and take over properties of the elderly and vulnerable adults.” (SAC ¶¶ 26-30.)  Further, the second amended complaint states that Plaintiffs entrusted Defendants with managing his properties and Defendants “without plaintiffs’ authorization and/or knowledge changed the mailing and notification address of the Properties, thereby diverting plaintiffs’ mail.” (SAC ¶ 36.) The second amended complaint then alleges Defendants’ actions on specific dates regarding specific properties. (SAC ¶ ¶ 36-63.)  

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently pleaded fraud in the inducement.

 

ii.               Financial Elder Abuse

 

Financial elder abuse occurs when a person or entity: “(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30.) Section 15610.30 also states that “A person or entity shall be deemed to have taken, secreted, appropriated, obtained, or retained property for a wrongful use if, among other things, the person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains the property and the person or entity knew or should have known that this conduct is likely to be harmful to the elder or dependent adult.” And “For purposes of this section, a person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property when an elder or dependent adult is deprived of any property right, including by means of an agreement, donative transfer, or testamentary bequest, regardless of whether the property is held directly or by a representative of an elder or dependent adult.” (Id.) An “’[e]lder’ means any person residing in [California], 65 years or older.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.27.)

 

Here, in addition to the allegations discussed above, Plaintiffs allege that “[a]t the time plaintiffs entered into contract with defendants and at all times material to this lawsuit, Yoakum was at least 67 years old.” (SAC ¶ 22.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently plead financial elder abuse.

 

iii.             Cancellation of Irrevocable Trust

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ claim for cancellation or modification of the irrevocable trust, which are based on Probate Code Section 15493, fails because there is no Probate Code Section 15493. (Memorandum p. 13.)

 

The second amended complaint refers to the third cause of action as “Revocation and Termination of Irrevocable Trust Procured Through Fraud in Factum (Probate Code Section 15403.) Plaintiffs also refer  to Probate Code section 15493 in the second amended complaint. (SAC ¶ 93.)  Probate Code section 15493 does not exist, but section 15403 refers to modification or termination of irrevocable trusts by all beneficiaries.

 

Section 15403 allows beneficiaries of an irrevocable trust to petition the court for modification or termination of the trust. Plaintiffs allege “continuation of the trust is not necessary as it was created by defendants to defraud the plaintiffs and deprive them of their properties” and allege “there is good cause to terminate the said irrevocable trust.” (SAC ¶¶ 95-96.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently pleaded their third cause of action.

 

iv.             Quiet Title

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 761.020 states a complaint for quiet title “shall be verified and shall include all of the following: (a) A description of the property that is the subject of the action. In the case of tangible personal property, the description shall include its usual location. In the case of real property, the description shall include both its legal description and its street address or common designation, if any. (b) The title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title. If the title is based upon adverse possession, the complaint shall allege the specific facts constituting the adverse possession. (c) The adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought. (d) The date as of which the determination is sought. If the determination is sought as of a date other than the date the complaint is filed, the complaint shall include a statement of the reasons why a determination as of that date is sought. (e) A prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.”

The second amended complaint is verified. (SAC p. 1.)  It contains legal descriptions and addresses of the property. (SAC ¶¶ 16-17). The second amended complaint alleges defendants “claim some right, title, estate, lien or interest in the Properties, and the real property interests are adverse plaintiffs’ property interests at issue in this action.” (SAC ¶ 99.) Plaintiffs seek quiet title to the properties as of April 13, 2023. (SAC ¶ 101.) And Plaintiffs request a “judicial declaration that plaintiffs are the owners of the Properties and to judgment in quieting title as at, or on the day of filing this action.” (SAC ¶ 100.)

Accordingly, Plaintiffs sufficiently pleaded their fourth cause of action.

 

  1. Vague and Ambiguous

 

Defendants further argue that paragraphs of Plaintiffs’ second amended complaint are vague and ambiguous. (Memorandum p. 9.) Specifically, Defendants argue that paragraphs of Plaintiffs' second amended complaint do not identify which defendant is referenced and do not state when the alleged action occurred. (Memorandum p. 15.) Finally, Defendants argue paragraphs of the second amended complaint contradict other portions of the second amended complaint. (Memorandum at p.12.)

 

The court may sustain a demurrer when the pleading is uncertain, including when it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

 

Plaintiffs’ second amended complaint is not vague or ambiguous. Plaintiffs describe the properties at issue. (SAC ¶¶ 16-19.) Plaintiff alleges the parties entered into a contract “defendants being appointed managers,[and] began to devise schemes to get title to the Properties.” (SAC ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs also describe the actions taken by Defendants in interfering with Plaintiffs’ properties along with the specific dates of the alleged actions and reference to documents purportedly created by Defendants’. (SAC ¶¶ 32, 36-63.) Further, although the complaint refers to actions taken by “defendants” the complaint alleges that Defendants and each of them in carrying out the conduct stated here were acting as agents, principals, co-venturers, partners, and attorneys in fact for each other.” (SAC ¶ 12.)

 

Defendants also argue the second amended complaint contradicts itself. (Memorandum p. 12.) Defendant appears to argue that Exhibits H and I contradict the second amended complaint’s allegations that the “defendants” created the documents, even though Exhibits H and I purportedly show a single signature. (SAC ¶ 49; Ex. H; Ex. I.) But the second amended complaint alleges that “Defendants and each of them in carrying out the conduct stated here were acting as agents, principals, co-venturers, partners, and attorneys in fact for each other.” (SAC ¶ 12.) Accordingly, the second amended complaint is not contradictory.

 

  1. Plaintiffs’ Interest in the Properties

 

Defendants argue the second amended complaint fails to demonstrate Plaintiffs have any interest in the property subject to his second amended complaint. (Memorandum p. 18.)

 

Plaintiffs’ second amended complaint alleges Yoakum owns “real properties located at 4170 Rogers Street, Los Angeles, CA 90063-11154, described as the “Rogers Property”; and 2276 Highland Vista Drive, Arcadia, CA 91006-1535, described as the “Highland Property”, which properties are referred to herein collectively as “the Properties” or “Plaintiffs’ Properties”.” (SAC ¶ 65.)  The second amended complaint also states that Plaintiff Highland Associates is a Limited Partnership wholly owned and operated by Yoakum. (SAC ¶ 2.) The second amended complaint then alleges Highland Associates is “the owner of title, and in possession of that real property legally described as Lot 323 of Tract No. 7812, in the County of Los Angeles, State of California, as per map recorded in book 82 pages 1 to 7 inclusive of maps, in the office of the County Recorder of said County, commonly known as 4170 Rogers Street, Los Angeles, California, A.P.N. No. 5226-017-014 (the “Rogers Property”). Plaintiff Yoakum the owner of Highland Associates L.P., acquired the Rogers Property in Highland Associates L.P. name by a Grant Deed recorded on June 9, 1994, as Instrument # 94-1110911. Highland Associates holds possession of the Rogers Property through tenants; and none of the defendants has lived in or been in possession of Rogers Property.” (SAC ¶ 17.)  The second amended complaint then alleges that Defendants “got Yoakum to disclaim interest in the Highland Property as part of defendants’ fraudulent activities in creating the title documents” and that Defendants “recorded Notice of Default on the Rogers Property” and “recorded a fictitious and forged Deed of Trust . . . against the Roger Street property.” (SAC ¶¶  56, 38-39)  Plaintiffs also identify deeds recorded against Plaintiffs’ interest in the Properties. (SAC ¶ 67.) Plaintiffs then allege that “Defendants and each of them, acting as agents of each other, fraudulently created and recorded the instruments described above on Plaintiff’s Properties.” (SAC ¶ 66.) Finally, the second amended complaint states “Plaintiffs dispute the validities of the Instruments . . . because, there are numerous defects regarding the creation, authorization, notification and recordation, of the documents.” (SAC ¶ 68.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have alleged an interest in the properties.

 

  1. Leave to Amend 

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303.)¿ 

¿ 

Although Plaintiffs have filed two amended complaints, the court sees a reasonable possibility that Plaintiffs’ causes of action can be cured by amendment.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer with leave to amend.

Judgment on the Pleadings

 

 

 

 

 

Defendant City of Los Angeles moves for judgment on the pleadings without leave to amend and a judgment dismissing the complaint.

 

The court grants Defendant’s motion.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

A tree fell on Orig Zelig’s residence. (Complaint ¶¶ 7, 9-10.) Plaintiff Insurance Company of America, Zelig’s insurer, paid for the damage. (Complaint ¶ 10.) On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant City of Los Angeles and Does 1 through 25, alleging that on or about February 24, 2023, Defendants did not properly maintain the tree that caused the damage. (Complaint ¶ 9.)

 

On August 26, 2024, Defendant City of Los Angeles (“Defendant”) filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings. Plaintiff filed no opposition. On September 19, 2024, Defendant filed a reply to Plaintiff’s non-opposition.

 

II.             JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests the court take judicial notice of Plaintiff’s complaint. Defendant’s request is denied, as there is no need to take judicial notice since the court can review the records of the case at hand.

 

Defendant also requests the court take judicial notice of City Clerk Michael Valdivia’s declaration. “If a plaintiff alleges compliance with the claims presentation requirement, but the public records do not reflect compliance, the governmental entity can request the court to take judicial notice under Evidence code section 452, subdivision (c) that the entity’s records do not show compliance." (Gong v. City of Rosemead (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 363, 376.) Defendant’s request for judicial notice of the City Clerk’s declaration is granted.

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

Under the Government Claims Act, “[a] claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property . . . shall be presented . . . no later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.” (Gov. Code § 911.4(a).) The party may file a lawsuit for money or damages only if the public entity denies or rejects the claim (either expressly or by operation of law). (Gov. Code §§ 905, 945.4; City of Ontario v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 894, 898.)¿ The claims presentation requirement provides the public entity with an opportunity to evaluate the claim and decide whether to pay on the claim.¿ (Roberts v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 474, 480.)¿

¿¿ 

A party may apply for leave to present an untimely claim (Govt. Code § 911.4), which the board “shall grant or deny … within 45 days after it is presented to the board.” (Govt. Code § 911.6(a). “The claimant and the board may extend the period within which the board is required to act on the application by written agreement made before the expiration of the period.” (Gov. Code § 911.6(a).) “The board shall grant the application if one of more of the following is applicable: (1) The failure to present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect and the public entity was not prejudiced in its defense of the claim by the failure to present the claim within the time specified in Section 911.2.” (Gov. Code § 911.6(b).)¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

“If the board fails or refuses to act on an application within the time prescribed by this section, the application shall be deemed to have been denied on the 45th day or, if the period within which the board is required to act is extended by agreement pursuant to this section, the last day of the period specified in the agreement.” (Gov. Code § 911.6(c).) “In all circumstances, a late claim application is deemed denied after 45 days, even though section 911.6(b)(2) would entitle the [individual] to relief if the application had merit. By placing this limitation on the entity’s time to act, the Legislature ensured that applications would not languish.” (J.M. v. Huntington Beach Union High School Dist. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 648, 653.)¿ 

¿ 

“If an application for leave to present a claim is denied or deemed to be denied pursuant to Section 911.6, a petition may be made to the court for an order relieving the petitioner from Section 945.4.” (Gov. Code § 946.6(a).) The petition shall show each of the following: (1) the application was denied or deemed denied, (2) the reason for the failure to present a timely claim, and (3) information required in section 910. (Gov. Code § 946.6(b).) “The applicant has six months to seek relief in court ‘after the application to the [entity] is denied or deemed to be denied.’ The six-month period ‘operates as a statute of limitations. It is mandatory, not discretionary.’” (J.M., supra, 2 Cal.5th at 653 (alterations and emphasis in original, citations omitted).)¿ ‘

 

b.     Analysis

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that on or about February 24, 2023, Defendant “negligently, carelessly, and or recklessly cared for, managed, owned, possessed, and or otherwise controlled, the area around its power lines, including but not limited to allowing for years the power lines to rub up against trees” and “[a]s a direct and proximate result . . . Insured sustained substantial damage to his property when a tree having suffered damage from the years of rubbing against a power line fell onto his residence.” (Complaint ¶¶ 8-9.) Because the injury occurred on or about February 24, 2023, Plaintiff had until August 24, 2023, to file a claim under the Government Claims Act.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to file a timely government claim within six months after the incident. (Motion p. 4.) Defendant submits a declaration by a Deputy City Clerk dated August 21, 2024, stating that “[t]he City Clerk was requested by the Office of the Los Angeles City Attorney to determine whether a Claim for Damages (“Claim”) had been filed by, General Insurance Company of America on behalf of its insured Ori Zelig for an alleged incident on February 24, 2023 at or near 6008 Melvin Avenue in the City of Tarzana.” (Valdivia Declaration ¶ 4.) The Deputy City Clerk’s declaration states that “[a]fter a diligent search of the City’s records, it was determined that the Office of the City Clerk does not have any record of receiving a claim from or on behalf [of] Orig Zelig and/or from plaintiff General Insurance Company of America for an incident date of February 24, 2023 at the subject location.” (Valdivia Declaration ¶ 5.)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges it was required to and did comply with the applicable claims statute, but Plaintiff filed no opposition showing it filed a timely government claim. (Complaint ¶ 4.)

 

Therefore, because the record shows that Plaintiff did not timely file a claim, the action is barred.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

In this case, granting leave to amend would be futile because the cause of action is time-barred.

 

Accordingly, the court grants Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings without leave to amend.

Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff is GRANTED.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

This dispute arises out of a car accident. (Complaint at p. 4.) Plaintiff filed a complaint on March 4, 2024, alleging three causes of action. Plaintiff filed an amended complaint on May 20, 2024.

 

Counsel filed this motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”) on August 30, 2024. No opposition has been filed.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284(2).) California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.     Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2.     A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3.     Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4.     The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) “The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.” (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-051).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on form MC-052 that “Due to an ongoing situation between Plaintiff and their counsel, the nature of which is subject to attorney-client privilege, Plaintiff’s counsel has determined that they can no longer legally and ethically represent the Plaintiff in this matter.” (MC-052.) Counsel also explains that “Communication between the Plaintiff and her counsel has completely broken down, and Plaintiff’s counsel is unable to effectively represent the Plaintiff due to her inability and difficulty in communicating with counsel.” (MC-052.)

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service or mail. If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.     The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

2.     The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

 

As used in this rule, “current” means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011, subdivision (b) applies.

 

When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

Counsel has not filed a proof of service showing the filings were served on Plaintiff, but Counsel’s signed declaration states that Counsel has served the client by mail at the client’s last-known address and has been unable to confirm the address is current or to locate a more current address for the client after mailing the motion papers to client’s last known address, return receipt requested, calling the client’s last known telephone numbers, and “[c]ontacting client via electronic mail and SMS/texting.” (MC-052.)

 

(D) Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.

 

MC-053 complies with this requirement.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel is ordered to serve a copy of the order granting leave to withdraw on the client and all parties who have appeared in the action or proceeding. Counsel will be relieved upon filing proof of service on the client of the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel--Civil (Judicial Council form MC-053).

 

Plaintiff’s counsel is ordered to give notice of the ruling on the motion to be relieved. Counsel for Plaintiff is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.
