DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV03989    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Kamran Dehdashtinejad,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Lurie Physical Therapy, P.C., et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

     24VECV03989                      107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

  

Demurrer Without Motion to Strike

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

This court overrules the demurrer.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

Plaintiff Kamran Dehdashtinejad (“Plaintiff”) was injured when a Suspension Body Fitness Trainer Strap (“G-Strap”) at a premises located at 7320 Woodlake Avenue, Suite 200, West Hills, CA 91307 (the “Premises”) “failed, tore, ripped, pulled apart, broke and/or ruptured.” (Complaint ¶¶ 7, 13-14.) On August 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Lurie Physical Therapy, P.C. dba Balance and Vestibular Center, Saskia Anna Van Ommeren Egberts, and Does 1 through 25, alleging causes of action for premises liability and negligence.

 

On September 12, 2024, Defendant Saskia Anna Van Ommeren Egberts (“Defendant”) demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. On October 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On October 15, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

 

II.             JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Citing Evidence Code section 452 subdivisions (b) and (c), Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of printouts from the California Secretary of State’s website showing Defendant is not the owner or operator of Lurie Physical Therapy, P.C. (Request for Judicial Notice, p. 2; Ex. B.)

 

Plaintiff objects to Defendant’s request for judicial notice. Plaintiff argues the printouts lack authentication and they are not the proper subject for judicial notice because they are not official records. (Plaintiff’s Objections to Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice pp. 1-2.) Plaintiff further argues that even if the court takes judicial notice of the printouts, the court may only accept the fact of their existence, not the truth of their contents. (Plaintiff’s Objections to Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

“Subdivision (c) of Evidence code section 452 authorizes the taking of judicial notice of ‘official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.’” (People v. Thacker (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 594, 597.)  “However, to be judicially noticed pursuant to subdivision (c) the matter must meet the test of an official act.” (Id. at p. 598.) In People v. Thacker, the court stated “We have found no authority and none has been cited for the proposition that materials prepared by private parties and merely on file with state agencies may be judicially noticed pursuant to subdivision (c). (Id.)

 

Moreover, although the court may take judicial notice of a document’s existence, “[w]hen judicial notice is taken of a document . . . the truthfulness and proper interpretation of the document are disputable.” (StorMedia, Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 449, 456 fn. 9; see also Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375 [“Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning. [Citation.] While courts take judicial notice of public records, they do not take notice of the truth of matters stated therein.”]) Accordingly, even if this court took judicial notice of the existence of the printouts, their truthfulness and proper interpretation are subject to dispute.

 

The printouts in Exhibit B appear to be materials prepared by private parties and filed with the California Secretary of State. (Ex. B.) Accordingly, the printouts are not official acts under Evidence Code section 452 subdivision (c). Further, the printouts are not regulations or legislative enactments under Evidence Code section 452 subdivision (b). Thus, this court denies Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

III.           PROCEDURAL MATTERS¿ 

¿ 

A.    Timeliness

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

 

This court finds that the demurrer, opposition, and reply were timely filed.

 

B.    The Meet & Confer Requirement¿ 

¿ 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating counsel sent a meet and confer letter and spoke to Plaintiff’s counsel, but the parties were not able to resolve the issues. (Bridwell Declaration ¶¶ 3-4; Ex. A.)

 

Accordingly, this court finds the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

¿ 

IV.           LEGAL STANDARD 

¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

V.              DISCUSSION 

 

A cause of action for premises liability requires showing: (1) duty; (2) breach; (3) causation; and (4) damages. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (“Ortega”) (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.) The elements of a negligence action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.) Thus, the elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for a negligence cause of action. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state a premises liability or negligence cause of action against Defendant because it does not allege Defendant owned or controlled the Premises. (Demurrer p. 6.) Defendant further argues Plaintiff has not alleged Defendant “was negligent in her use/maintenance of the property, ex[cept] conclusory allegations which are insufficient.” (Demurrer p. 6.) Finally, Defendant argues Plaintiff has not alleged Defendant “knew of any dangerous condition at the property before the alleged incident/injury occurred” and “no facts plead that Plaintiff was injured due to Defendant[‘s] . . . conduct or that the conduct was a substantial factor in causing any of the alleged harm to Plaintiff.” (Demurrer p. 6.)

The first element requires Plaintiff to allege Defendant owed him a duty of care. Plaintiff alleges Defendants “owned, maintained, repaired, cleaned, constructed, designed, inspected, serviced, controlled, managed, furnished, leased, rented, operated and/or constructed for use to their business invitees, clientele and/or authorized members of the general public” the Premises. (Compliant ¶ 7.) As to the premises liability cause of action, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants . . . owed a duty of care to their business invitees, clientele and/or authorized members of the general public, including but not limited to the Plaintiff, to use reasonable care to discover any unsafe conditions of the PREMISES, the EQUIPMENT and/or G-STRAP located at the PREMISES[.]” (Complaint ¶ 11.) As to the negligence cause of action, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants and each of them, owed a duty of care [and] were under a duty to exercise such reasonable care as that exercised by reasonably prudent persons within the community, for the safety of persons lawfully on the PREMISES and lawfully using the EQUIPMENT and/or the G-STRAP located at the PREMISES, including the Plaintiff.” (Complaint ¶ 18.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges he was “lawfully at the PREMISES using the EQUIPMENT, including the G-STRAP at the PREMISES with the consent, permission and/or direction of Defendants[.]” (Complaint ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant argues she owed Plaintiff no duty because she is “not the owner or operator of Lurie Physical Therapy P.C., which is the business where the alleged incident/injury occurred.” (Demurrer p. 6.) But Plaintiff also alleges Defendant “controlled” the Premises. (Complaint ¶ 7.) “A defendant need not own, possess and control property in order to be held liable; control alone is sufficient.” (Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, 1162.) Accordingly, even if Defendant did not own or operate the Premises, it is sufficient that Plaintiff alleged Defendant controlled the Premises.

 

Thus, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that Defendant owed him a duty.

 

The second element requires Plaintiff to allege Defendant breached her duty. As to the premises liability cause of action, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants . . . breached their applicable duty of care to Plaintiff by” among other things, “negligently failing to adequately inspect, service, maintain, repair, replace, install, construct, test, own, control, manage, place, upkeep, control, lease, rent, supervise, own, operate and/or secure the PREMISES and its contents, including but not limited to the EQUIPMENT and/or the G-STRAP in a safe and reasonable manner . . .” (Complaint ¶ 13.) As to the negligence cause of action, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants, and each of them, breached their applicable duty of care to Plaintiff by 1) negligently failing to adequately inspect, service, maintain, repair, install, replace, construct, design, manufacture, assemble, distribute, test, fabricate, analyze, recommend, advertise, promote, sell, own, control, manage, place, upkeep control, lease, rent, supervise, own, operate and/or secure the PREMISES and its contents including but not limited to the EQUIPMENT and/or the G-STRAP in a safe and reasonable manner . . .” (Complaint ¶ 20.) As to both causes of action, Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants . . . knew, or in the exercise of reasonable care should have known the defective and dangerous character of the PREMISES and/or its EQUIPMENT, including the G-STRAP, and that they were unsafe for the use and purpose for which it was intended. However, the defective and dangerous character of the PREMISES, and/or its EQUIPMENT, including the G-STRAP, was not made known to their business invitees, clientele and/or other permissible users, including Plaintiff, by any of the Defendants herein or their agents or employees.” (Complaint ¶¶ 14, 21.) Accordingly, Plaintiff has alleged Defendant breached her duty.

 

The third and fourth elements require Plaintiff to allege Defendant’s breach caused him damages. As to the premises liability cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants’, and each of their negligence . . . Plaintiff was injured in his health, strength and activity, sustaining injury to his person, all of which injuries have caused, and continue to cause Plaintiff great mental, physical and nervous pain and suffering and have caused permanent injury and disability to him.” (Complaint ¶ 15.) Plaintiff further alleges that “[a]s a result of such injuries, Plaintiff has suffered and will continue to suffer general damages in an amount according to proof.” (Complaint ¶ 15.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants', and each of their negligence . . . Plaintiff has been required to obtain and incurred the costs of hospital and medical services, past, present and future; has suffered loss of income and earnings, past, present and future; has suffered incidental and consequential damages, past, present and future and has suffered other monetary and non-monetary damages[.]” (Complaint ¶ 16.)

 

As to the negligence cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants', and each of their negligence . . .  Plaintiff was hurt and injured in his health, strength and activity, sustaining injury to his person, all of which injuries have caused, and continue to cause Plaintiff great mental, physical and nervous pain and suffering and have caused certain permanent injury and disability to him. As a result of such injuries, Plaintiff has suffered and will continue to suffer general damages in an amount according to proof.” (Complaint ¶ 22.) Plaintiff further alleges that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of Defendants', and each of their negligence . . .  Plaintiff has been required to obtain and incurred the costs of hospital and medical services, past, present and future; has suffered loss of income and earnings, past, present and future; has suffered incidental and consequential damages, past, present and future and has suffered other monetary and non-monetary damages[.]” (Complaint ¶ 23.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s breach of her duty caused his injuries and Plaintiff has alleged damages resulting from Defendant’s breach of her duty.

 

Defendant argues the allegations discussed above are insufficient because they are conclusory. (Demurrer p. 6.) Citing Hahn v. Mirda, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747, Plaintiff argues
negligence may be pleaded generally, and because premises liability is a form of negligence, Defendant’s demand for factual specificity within the Complaint is not a basis for sustaining her demurrer.” (Opposition p. 8.)

 

“Negligence may be alleged in general terms; that is, it is sufficient to allege an act was negligently done without stating the particular omission which rendered it negligent.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) Further, “[t]here is no requirement that the plaintiff identify and allege the precise moment of the injury, or the exact nature of the wrongful act.” (Id.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) Finally, although a “plaintiff must indicate the acts or omissions which are said to have been negligently performed”, “[o]rdinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms, without specific facts showing how the injury occurred.” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527.)

 

Here, as discussed above, Plaintiff has alleged Defendant owed him a duty, Defendant breached that duty by performing acts or omissions identified in the complaint, and that Defendant’s breach of her duty caused Plaintiff damages. Thus, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged causes of action for premises liability and negligence.

 

Finally, Defendant argues that “[l]umping all ‘Defendants’ together is insufficient.” (Demurrer p. 6.) This court finds that the complaint sufficiently identifies which allegations are alleged against which Defendants.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION

 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court overrules the demurrer.
