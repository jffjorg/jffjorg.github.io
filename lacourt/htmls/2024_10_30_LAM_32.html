DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV10915    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 32

 

BENJAMIN DADBIN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

PALAZZO WEST, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV10915

  Hearing Date:  October 30, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 15, 2023, Plaintiff Benjamin Dadbin filed this action against Defendants Palazzo West; LA Park La Brea A, LLC; OP Property Management, LP; Chase Sherbert; Enio Castellanos; and Elijah Doe. The complaint stems from injuries Plaintiff sustained as the result of an alleged bedbug infestation at an apartment owned or operated by Defendants.

            On September 20, 2024, Defendants LA Park La Brea A, LLC and OP Property Management, LP filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike. Plaintiff filed his opposition on October 16, 2024. Defendants filed their reply on October 23, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part of that pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike (1) any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading and (2) all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id., § 437.)

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendants have complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Aslanyan Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Demurrer

            a. Battery

The elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) the defendant touched the plaintiff, or caused the plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend the plaintiff; (2) the plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) the plaintiff was harmed or offended by the defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in the plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching. (Carlsen v. Koivumaki (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 879, 890.)

            Here, Plaintiff’s claim stems from Defendant’s alleged failure to remediate a bedbug infestation. This cannot form the basis of a battery claim, which requires an affirmative act, not mere omission. Plaintiff cites a nonbinding federal case analyzing Illinois law for the contention that a bed bug infestation can constitute battery. (See Mathias v. Accord Econ. Lodging, Inc., 347 F.3d 672 (7th Cir. III.2003).) However, the Mathias decision is not based on California law and is not on point. Mathias focused on punitive damages and did not discuss the elements of battery or how a bed bug infestation satisfies those elements.

Plaintiff cites no authority suggesting that a bedbug infestation constitutes battery just because the landlord knew of an infestation prior to renting the unit. (See Compl. ¶ 59.) Even if that was intentional, it was still an omission (failure to disclose), not an affirmative act. Additionally, it would not constitute unwanted contact between one person and another. Renting out an insect-infested apartment is not analogous to throwing water on someone. (See Mount Vernon Fire Ins. Co. v. Busby (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 876, 881 [describing battery by indirect contact].)  

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the first cause of action.

            b. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

To state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must establish: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.)

“For conduct to be outrageous, it must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated by a civilized community.” (Faunce v. Cate (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 166, 172.) “Severe emotional distress [is] emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.” (Fletcher v. Western Life Insurance Co. (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 376, 397.) A landlord’s willful failure to correct defective conditions can constitute intentional infliction of emotional distress. (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1069.)

Here, the allegations show that Defendants knew of a bedbug infestation but failed to take adequate remedial actions. The facts show that Defendants employed an exterminator multiple times, but the treatment was ineffective. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants executed chemical treatments when Plaintiff preferred heat treatments, and improperly charged Plaintiff for some of the treatments. Plaintiff eventually moved out because the infestation was not eradicated. As a matter of law, these facts do not amount to the outrageous conduct necessary for IIED.  

Plaintiff also fails to allege severe emotional distress. The conclusory allegation that “Plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress that has caused Plaintiff to sustain severe, serious and permanent injuries to their person” is insufficient. (See Compl. ¶ 95.)

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the third cause of action.

c. Unfair Competition Law

Business and Professions Code section 17200 prohibits unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts or practices. Each of the three prongs is an independent basis for relief. (Smith v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 700, 718.) Unlawful conduct is defined as any practice forbidden by law. (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 377, 383.) UCL actions alleging unlawful conduct “borrow” from other statutes or common law causes of action outside Section 17200. (Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1383.)

Here, Defendants do not challenge the sufficiency of Plaintiff’s claims for negligence, violation of Civil Code sections 1941 and 1941.1, breach of the warranty of habitability, breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, breach of contract, and private nuisance. These alleged breaches and violations constitute “unlawful” conduct that serves as the predicate for a UCL claim. The complaint contains sufficient facts for pleading purposes, and factual ambiguities or conflicts should be addressed in discovery.

Defendants also correctly point out that damages are not recoverable under the UCL. However, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants made “unlawful demands and collections of monies from Plaintiff.” (Compl. ¶ 139.) For example, Defendants collected rent from Plaintiff and charged Plaintiff for certain extermination treatments. Plaintiff’s failure to allege a precise amount does not render the complaint defective. Whether Defendants took any money from Plaintiff that may be the subject of restitution, and the corresponding amount, are factual matters unsuited for resolution on a demurrer.

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the sixth cause of action.

d. Civil Code Section 1941.3

Civil Code section 1941.3 governs a landlord’s obligations with regards to doors and windows. Plaintiff concedes that he mistakenly alleged section 1941.3 and requests leave to amend to add a cause of action under section 1942.3. However, section 1942.3 does not give rise to a cause of action. That section merely governs the standard of proof “[i]n any unlawful detainer action by the landlord.” (Civ. Code, § 1942.3(a).)

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the eighth cause of action.

 

 

e. Civil Code Section 1942.4

A violation of Civil Code section 1942.4 does not occur unless four conditions are met, one of which is “[a] public officer or employee who is responsible for the enforcement of any housing law, after inspecting the premises, has notified the landlord or the landlord’s agent in writing of his or her obligations to abate the nuisance or repair the substandard conditions.” (Civ. Code, § 1942.4(a)(2).)

The complaint fails to allege that a public officer or employee responsible for housing law enforcement notified the landlord in writing of the nuisance. Plaintiff’s opposition fails to address this defect.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the ninth cause of action.

f. Public Nuisance

A nuisance is “[a]nything which is injurious to health” (Civ. Code, § 3479), and a “public nuisance is one which affects at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons” (id., § 3480). To prevail on a public nuisance claim, Plaintiff must show “that a defendant knowingly created or assisted in the creation of a substantial and unreasonable interference with a public right.” (People v. ConAgra Grocery Prods. Co. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 51, 79.)

The nature of the allegations makes clear that this is a private dispute between landlord and tenant. Plaintiff does not point to any allegations demonstrating that the bedbug infestation affected a substantial number of persons or the public at large such that it could be considered a public nuisance. Plaintiff simply concludes without authority or explanation that the bedbug infestation should be likened to a smallpox epidemic.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the thirteenth cause of action.

 

 

II. Motion to Strike

            a. Punitive Damages

“In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id., subd. (c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud is “intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Id., subd. (c)(3).)

Here, the complaint alleges that Defendants rented a unit to Plaintiff knowing of a bedbug infestation on the premises and intentionally concealed the defect from Plaintiff. (Compl. ¶ 59.) Furthermore, Defendants allegedly failed to properly train their employees on maintenance and sanitation and turned a blind eye to previous complaints of infestations. (Id., ¶ 86.) For pleading purposes, these facts sufficiently demonstrate a conscious disregard for Plaintiff’s rights and safety, as well as conduct that subjected Plaintiff to unjust hardship.

The motion to strike is DENIED as to punitive damages

b. Attorney’s Fees

“Except as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1021.) Therefore, a plaintiff must allege either a statutory or contractual basis for attorneys’ fees.

Here, Plaintiff has not pled a statutory or contractual basis for attorney’s fees. The only possible statutory basis would have been the ninth cause of action for Civil Code section 1942.4, but as discussed above, that cause of action fails. Plaintiff’s opposition does not address attorney’s fees.

The motion to strike is GRANTED as to attorney’s fees.

CONCLUSION

            Defendants LA Park La Brea A, LLC’s and OP Property Management, LP’s demurrer is SUSTAINED in part as set forth above without leave to amend. Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend as to attorney’s fees and DENIED as to punitive damages.


Case Number: 24STCV04556    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 32

 

HECTOR ALMAZAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

VISTA DEL MAR CHILD AND FAMILY SERVICES, INC.,

                       

                       Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV04556

  Hearing Date:  October 30, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants' motion to compel arbitration

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On February 23, 2024, Plaintiff Hector Almazan filed this action for employment discrimination and wage violations against Defendants Vista Del Mar Child and Family Services, Inc.; People Systems Child Family Therapy, Inc.; and Mark Walker.

            On September 12, 2024, Defendants Vista Del Mar Child and Family Services, Inc. and Mark Walker filed the instant motion to compel arbitration. Plaintiff filed his opposition on October 17, 2024. Defendants filed their reply on October 23, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

The Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) states that “[a] written provision in any . . . contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce to settle by arbitration a controversy thereafter arising out of such contract or transaction . . . shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.” (9 U.S.C. § 2.) The term “involving commerce” is interpreted to mean simply “affecting commerce” to give the FAA the broadest reach possible, and does not require a transaction that is actually “within the flow of interstate commerce.” (See Allied-Bruce Terminix Co. v. Dobson (1995) 513 U.S. 265, 273-74; Citizens Bank v. Alafabco, Inc. (2003) 539 U.S. 52, 56.) Moreover, parties may agree to apply the FAA notwithstanding any effect on interstate commerce. (Victrola 89, LLC v. Jaman Properties 8 LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 337, 355.)

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists….” (Code Civ. Proc, § 1281.2.) “The party seeking arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement, and the party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving any defense, such as unconscionability.” (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236.)

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff’s Objections

  • Plaintiff’s objections are overruled.

Defendants’ Objections

  • Defendants’ objections to the Declaration of Hector Almazan are overruled.
  • Defendants’ objections to the Declaration of Christopher DeClue are overruled.

DISCUSSION

I. Prima Facie Proof of Agreement

            “The moving party ‘can meet its initial burden by attaching to the motion or petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the opposing party's signature.’” (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165.)

            On June 4, 2021, Plaintiff hand-signed a “Mutual Agreement to Arbitrate” stating the following:

 

“Both Employer and Employee voluntarily agree that any claim, dispute, and/or controversy of any kind between Employee and Employer (or its owners, directors, officers, employees, agents, related and affiliated entities, and parties affiliated with its employee benefit and health plans unless such plans provide a different arbitration procedure), including but not limited to claims or controversies arising out of the employment relationship between Employee and Employer, past, present, or future, or its termination, shall be subject to final and binding arbitration.”

(McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) 

            Plaintiff does not dispute the existence of the agreement, its terms, or his signature on it. Plaintiff argues that the agreement was not properly authenticated. However, “[f]or purposes of a petition to compel arbitration, it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication.” (Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218.) “The statute does not require the petitioner to introduce the agreement into evidence. A plain reading of the statute indicates that as a preliminary matter the court is only required to make a finding of the agreement's existence, not an evidentiary determination of its validity.” (Id. at p. 219.) Because Plaintiff does not challenge the existence of the agreement, the Court finds that an agreement to arbitrate exists regardless of whether the specific document presented by Defendants has been authenticated.

            Furthermore, the document is properly introduced through the declaration of Cassandra McDonald, Defendant Vista Del Mar’s human resources director. (McDonald Decl. ¶ 2.) “[T]he custodian of a document need not have been present or employed when the document was created or signed to authenticate a document in a company's files.” (Iyere v. Wise Auto Group (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 747, 758.) “[A]ny qualified witness knowledgeable about documents may lay foundation for business records.” (Id. at p. 759.) There is “no strict requirement as to how a party authenticates a writing.” (Ibid.) Ms. McDonald has demonstrated sufficient knowledge of Defendant’s onboarding and document procedures to lay foundation for the document. (See McDonald Decl. ¶¶ 3-7.)

            Plaintiff states that he does not “remember” signing the arbitration agreement. (Almazar Decl. ¶ 12.) However, “[i]f a party confronted with his or her handwritten signature on an arbitration agreement is unable to allege that the signature is inauthentic or forged, the fact that that person does not recall signing the agreement neither creates a factual dispute as to the signature's authenticity nor affords an independent basis to find that a contract was not formed.” (Iyere, supra, 87 Cal.App.5th at p. 758.) Again, Plaintiff does not dispute that the agreement exists and that his signature is on it. Thus, Plaintiff’s failure to recall the agreement is irrelevant.     

            In sum, Defendants have satisfied their burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement.

II. Claims Against Defendant Walker

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant Walker has no standing to compel arbitration because Walker did not sign the agreement. “Enforcement is permitted where the nonsignatory is the agent for a party to the arbitration agreement, or the nonsignatory is a third party beneficiary of the agreement.” (Fuentes v. TMCSF, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 541, 547.)

Here, the agreement states that it applies to Vista Del Mar’s “owners, directors, officers, employees, [and] agents.” (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) Plaintiff alleges that Walker is an employee and agent of Vista Del Mar. (See Compl. ¶¶ 6, 28.) Therefore, Walker is entitled to enforce the agreement as an agent and third-party beneficiary.

III. Unconscionability

a. Procedural Unconscionability

Procedural unconscionability “focuses on two factors: ‘oppression’ and ‘surprise.’ ‘Oppression’ arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and ‘an absence of meaningful choice.’ ‘Surprise’ involves the extent to which the supposedly agreed-upon terms of the bargain are hidden in the prolix printed form drafted by the party seeking to enforce the disputed terms.” (Zullo v. Superior Court (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 477, 484, internal citations omitted.)

            1. Contract of Adhesion

Plaintiff argues that the agreement is procedurally unconscionable because he had to sign it as a condition of employment. (See Almazar Decl. ¶¶ 7, 15.) However, the agreement plainly states that it is voluntary. (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) Moreover, the adhesive nature of a contract represents only a minimal degree of procedural unconscionability. (Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 695, 704.) “[A] compulsory predispute arbitration agreement is not rendered unenforceable just because it is required as a condition of employment.” (Lagatree v. Luce, Forward, Hamilton & Scripps (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1127.)  

            2. Surprise

Plaintiff argues that the agreement is surprising because he was not informed that he was waiving his jury rights. (See Almazar Decl. ¶ 10.) However, the agreement plainly states, in all caps and in bold: “BY VOLUNTARILY AGREEING TO THIS ARBITRATION AGREEMENT, EMPLOYER AND EMPLOYEE BOTH GIVE UP THEIR RIGHTS TO TRIAL BY JURY.” (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) “[O]ne who signs an instrument which on its face is a contract is deemed to assent to all its terms.” (Marin Storage & Trucking, Inc. v. Benco Contracting & Engineering, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1042, 1049.) Again, Plaintiff does not dispute his signature on the agreement. Defendant had no affirmative obligation to explain the agreement to Plaintiff. (See Brookwood v. Bank of America (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1667, 1674 [“No law requires that parties dealing at arm's length have a duty to explain to each other the terms of a written contract”].) Plaintiff has no evidence that he tried to ask questions, that Defendant refused to answer his questions, or that Defendant prohibited him from taking more time or seeking assistance to review the agreement.  

Plaintiff also argues that the agreement is surprising because he did not know he would have to arbitrate claims against Walker. However, as discussed above, the agreement on its face covers claims with Vista Del Mar’s employees and agents, which includes Walker. Thus, the agreement is not surprising. A contract does not become surprising merely because a party failed to read it prior to signing or forgot about its terms. “Reasonable diligence requires the reading of a contract before signing it. A party cannot use his own lack of diligence to avoid an arbitration agreement.” (Brookwood, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at p. 1674.) 

            3. Arbitration Rules

Plaintiff argues that the agreement fails to inform him of the applicable arbitration rules. However, “the failure to attach the [arbitration] rules, standing alone, is insufficient grounds to support a finding of procedural unconscionability.” (Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1472.) The agreement states that arbitration will proceed under “JAMS’ Employment Arbitration Rules and Procedures.” (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) The agreement further informs Plaintiff that the rules may be obtained from HR and also provides a website link to the rules. Thus, the agreement sufficiently provided for access to JAMS rules.

In sum, the Court finds no procedural unconscionability.  

b. Substantive Unconscionability

Substantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create overly harsh or one-sided results as to shock the conscience. (Suh v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1504, 1515.)  

            1. Fees Unique to Arbitration

For an arbitration agreement to be valid, the employer must bear all costs unique to arbitration. (Armendariz v. Foundational Health Psychcare Servs. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 111-12.) Here, the agreement provides that “Employer will pay the reasonable and necessary fees and expenses of the Arbitrator, and all other types of expenses that Employee would not be required to pay in a court action.” (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) Thus, the agreement clearly requires Defendant to bear the costs unique to arbitration.

            Plaintiff argues that the arbitration agreement “may” require him to pay all filing fees without the possibility of a fee waiver. However, Plaintiff provides no evidence that he would be prevented from seeking a fee waiver in arbitration. Nor does Plaintiff cite any authority suggesting that the availability of a fee waiver affects the enforceability of an arbitration agreement.

Plaintiff also argues that he would have to pay an additional filing fee for arbitration after he already paid a filing fee in court. However, the agreement never required Plaintiff to pay filing fees in both court and arbitration. Plaintiff’s payment of the court filing fee is a result of Plaintiff’s own decision to file a lawsuit in contravention of the arbitration agreement. Plaintiff cites no authority suggesting that this renders the arbitration agreement unconscionable.

                        2. Minimal Discovery

            “[A]rbitration is meant to be a streamlined procedure. Limitations on discovery . . . is one of the ways streamlining is achieved.” (Dotson v. Amgen, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 975, 983.) Therefore, an arbitration agreement is only required to “provide[] for more than minimal discovery.” (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 102.)

            Plaintiff argues that the JAMS rules provide for only one deposition, which is insufficient. However, the rules allow for “at least one” deposition; they do not impose a limit of one deposition. (See JAMS Rule 17(b).) Plaintiff cites no authority holding that JAMS’ discovery rules are unconscionable.

                        3. Attorney’s Fees

            Plaintiff argues that the agreement requires him to bear his own attorney’s fees in contravention of his right to recover attorney’s fees under FEHA. The agreement does no such thing. Instead, the agreement provides that “[u]nless the parties agree otherwise or unless a party prevails on a claim for which attorneys' fees and/or costs may be awarded to the prevailing party under applicable law and the Arbitrator awards fees and/or costs, each party will pay its own attorneys' fees and expenses associated with the arbitration.” (McDonald Decl., Ex. A.) Thus, the agreement expressly allows Plaintiff to recover attorney’s fees if he prevails on a claim, such as FEHA, that provides for an award of attorney’s fees.  

            In sum, the Court finds no substantive unconscionability. With a no procedural unconscionability and no substantive unconscionability, the agreement remains enforceable.

CONCLUSION

            Defendants Vista Del Mar Child and Family Services, Inc.’s and Mark Walker’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. The case is stayed in its entirety pending the outcome of arbitration.
