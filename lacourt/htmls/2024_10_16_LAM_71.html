DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.

1.     Hightech’s Demurrer

Request for Judicial Notice

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party must meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading and obviate the need for filing the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a), emphasis added.)

The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either of the following: (A) The means by which the demurring party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer. (B) That the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Hightech’s counsel declares that on June 10, 2024, he attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiffs’ counsel via email.  (Decl. of Inlow ¶¶2-3.)  Moving Defendants’ counsel’s declaration fails to state whether he attempted to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference, and is therefore insufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a).  However, failure to sufficiently meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(4); Dumas v. Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 348, 355; Olson v. Hornbrook Community Services District (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 502, 515.)  Therefore, the Court will consider Hightech’s demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Summary of Demurrer

Hightech demurs to the 2nd cause of action in the 5AC on the basis the causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Hightech due to the holding in Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, and the cause of action is uncertain.  (Demurrer, pg. 3; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f).)

 

Failure to State a Cause of Action

Negligence (2nd COA)

A cause of action for negligence requires the following elements: (1) defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty; (2) breached the duty; and (3) the breach caused (4) damages.  (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)  A complaint which lacks allegations of fact to show that a legal duty of care was owed is fatally defective.  (See Jones v. Grewe (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 950.)  The existence of a legal duty of care is properly challenged by demurrer and is a question of law for the court.  (Hegyes v. Unjian Enterprises, Inc. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1103, 1111.)

Plaintiffs allege Greg, Edith, and Hightech owed Plaintiffs and Plaintiffs’ agents, EWL and Suddarth, a legal duty of care pursuant to California Financing Law, as well as other statutes and common law, because they knew or should have known that Plaintiffs would reasonably rely on the inaccurate, unverified, and misrepresented information that Defendants Greg, Edith, and Hightech presented regarding their principal, Defendant Hedger.  (5AC ¶40.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendants Greg, Edith, and Hightech knew with substantial certainty that Plaintiffs would rely to their detriment on the inaccurate, unverified, and misrepresented financial information and documentation that Defendants Greg, Edith, and Hightech prepared and disseminated. (5AC ¶40.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants Greg, Edith, and Hightech owed a duty of care to Plaintiffs and Plaintiffs’ agents, EWL and Suddarth, and is therefore liable for breaching the duty of care because they knew the transaction would affect Plaintiffs and Plaintiffs’ agents, and they knew that the harm to Plaintiffs was foreseeably connected to the production of inaccurate, unverified, and misrepresented financial information and documentation.  (5AC ¶40.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendants EWL, Suddarth, Greg, Edith, and Hightech failed to exercise due care and diligence in the vetting and investigation of Hedger regarding his suitability as a borrower and risk as a borrower for investment purposes.  (5AC ¶41.)  Plaintiffs allege they reasonably relied on the information provided by Defendants EWL and Suddarth regarding the lending and investment opportunities.  (5AC ¶41.)

Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the negligence of EWL, Suddarth, Next Day Defendants, AAMC Defendants, Greg, Edith, and Hightech.  (5AC ¶42.)  Plaintiffs allege the negligence of EWL, Suddarth, Next Day Defendants, AAMC Defendants, Greg, Edith, and Hightech was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm, including but not limited to monetary damages and losses to be determined by a jury at trial.  (5AC ¶43.)

Plaintiffs fail to allege Hightech owed a cognizable duty to Plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs merely allege Hightech owes a duty under California Financing Law and common law duties.  Plaintiffs fail to allege Hightech was in privity with Plaintiffs, had a special relationship with Plaintiffs, or had a fiduciary duty to Plaintiffs.

Accordingly, Hightech’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ 2nd cause of action is sustained without leave to amend as to Hightech.

 

Conclusion

Hightech’s demurrer to the 2nd cause of action in Plaintiffs’ 5AC is sustained without leave to amend.

Failure to State a Cause of Action

          Negligence and Negligent Misrepresentation (2nd and 4th COAs)

A cause of action for negligence requires the following elements: (1) defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty; (2) breached the duty; and (3) the breach caused (4) damages.  (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)  A complaint which lacks allegations of fact to show that a legal duty of care was owed is fatally defective.  (See Jones v. Grewe (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 950.)  The existence of a legal duty of care is properly challenged by demurrer and is a question of law for the court.  (Hegyes v. Unjian Enterprises, Inc. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1103, 1111.)

“Negligent misrepresentation requires an assertion of fact, falsity of that assertion, and the tortfeasor’s lack of reasonable grounds for believing the assertion to be true. It also requires the tortfeasor’s intent to induce reliance, justifiable reliance by the person to whom the false assertion of fact was made, and damages to that person. An implied assertion of fact is ‘not enough’ to support liability.”  (SI 59 LLC v. Variel Warner Ventures, LLC (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 146, 154, internal citation omitted.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants failed to exercise due care in the investigation, viewing, and opining regarding the market value of the Calabasas Property.  (5AC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants created and distributed an inaccurate and erroneous appraisal report.  (5AC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that the appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and/or entities, such as Plaintiffs, with interest in the Calabasas Property.  (5AC ¶37.) 

Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants owed a duty as licensed real estate appraisers to Plaintiffs as it was known that they would rely on the erroneous appraisal reports.  (5AC ¶38.)  Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants were required to exercise due care and diligence in performing real property appraisal services as a similarly situated licensed real property appraiser would exercise.  (5AC ¶38.)

Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants failed to act carefully and with due diligence in the performance of their appraisal services by failing to investigate and appraise the Calabasas Property carefully and diligently and to not distribute erroneous appraisal reports.  (5AC ¶39.)  Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the negligence of Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶39.)  Plaintiffs allege the negligence of Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.  (5AC ¶39.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants represented facts that were true and/or concealed material facts concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property.  (5AC ¶52.)  Plaintiffs allege the representations and/or concealment of Defendants Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property were false.  (5AC ¶53.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants knew or should have known the representations were false and should have disclosed the material facts that were true.  (5AC ¶54.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants made the representations and/or concealments recklessly and without regard for the truth.  (5AC ¶55.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants intended for Plaintiffs to rely on the representations or should have known that Plaintiffs would have relied on the representations.  (5AC ¶56.)

          Plaintiffs allege they reasonably relied on the representations and/or material omissions of Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶57.)

          Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the misrepresentations and/or material omissions of Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶58.)

          Plaintiffs allege their reliance on the representations and/or material omissions of Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.  (5AC ¶59.)

An appraiser designated by a lender to value its borrower’s collateral for financing purposes generally owes its duty of care to the lender, not to the borrower.  (Greenwald & Bank, Cal. Practice Guide: Real Property Transactions, Ch. 6-L, 6:701.)  Indeed, “[w]hether the lender conducts the appraisal in house or hires an outside appraiser, the considerations are the same. The appraisal ordered by the lender is for its own protections and the borrower has [their] own means of ascertaining the desirability of the property.”  (Willemsen v. Mitrosilis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 622, 629 [noting borrower should know lender’s appraisal is intended for lender’s benefit, not to ensure success of borrower’s investment]; see also Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1253-1254, [stating lender’s appraiser had no duty of care to borrowers for inaccurate property description as appraisal was intended to support lender’s evaluation of collateral, not borrowers’ decision whether to purchase property].)

Here, Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that AAMC Defendants’ appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and entities such as Plaintiffs with interest in the valuation of the Calabasas Property.  (5AC ¶37.)  AAMC Defendants’ appraisal report was provided for Lender/Client AAMC, not for Defendant Hedger, identified as the Borrower, and most importantly, not for Plaintiffs.  (A-RJN, Exh. 1.)  Here, as in Willemsen, there is no indication that AAMC Defendants were aware that Plaintiffs hoped to use the appraisal report at issue as an investigational tool upon which to base their decision to approve or reject the Calabasas Property when it was prepared for EWL.  (Willemsen, 230 Cal.App.4th at pg. 631.)

Further, the Loan Servicing Agreement (“LSA”) creating the principal-agency relationship between Plaintiffs and EWL was entered into on April 24, 2018 (e.g., after AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal).  (See A-RJN Exh. 2.)  As such, EWL was not acting as Plaintiffs’ agent when EWL retained AAMC for its 2017 Appraisal. Moreover, the LSA does not discuss the existence of any agency relationship between Plaintiffs and EWL related to the decision to make the loan or obtain AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal which allegedly induced Plaintiffs to make the loan.  Therefore, because the 2017 Appraisal was made earlier in time than Plaintiffs’ LSA with EWL, AAMC Defendants did not owe Plaintiffs a duty of care and made no representation to Plaintiffs as a matter of law.  

Plaintiffs’ arguments in their opposition based on Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. (1992) 3 Cal.4th 370 and Soderberg v. McKinney (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1760 are inapposite.  Unlike in Bily, here, Plaintiffs do not allege that AAMC knew Plaintiffs were considering loaning money secured by the property they appraised. AAMC’S 2017 Appraisal does not refer to or mention Plaintiffs in any way and there is no allegation that Plaintiffs paid for AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal or received a copy of it directly from AAMC.  Further, the 5AC contains no allegation that AAMC knew Plaintiffs relied on AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal in investing money. AAMC did not manifest the requisite intent to supply information for Plaintiffs’ use in determining whether the Subject Property was suitable for investment for them.  Rather, AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal specifically limited its intended use to EWL. Thus, there was no “undertaking” on AAMC’s part to guide Plaintiffs relative to their investment decision.  (See Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. (1992) 3 Cal.4th 370, 414-415.)

Further, unlike in Soderberg, Plaintiffs do not allege that AAMC issued its appraisal with such knowledge or intent found in Soderberg.  (Soderberg v. McKinney (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1760, 1770.)  Rather, AAMC knew and intended only that EWL would use AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal in determining whether the Subject Property had sufficient value to serve as collateral.  (Willemsen, 230 Cal.App.4th at pgs. 629-632.)

Finally, Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by the statute of limitations and after amending their complaint six times, Plaintiffs cannot again amend to include allegations of delayed discovery.  AAMC Defendants raised the issue of statute of limitations in their demurrers to the SAC, 3AC, and 4AC.  Plaintiffs cannot now argue that such bases were not raised on AAMC Defendants’ prior demurrers.  (Opposition, pg. 13.)

Causes of action for professional negligence and negligent representation premised on a claim of professional negligence are governed by C.C.P. §339(1)’s two-year statute of limitations. 

Here, the Deed of Trust (Recorder’s Instrument No. 20180427661) memorializing Plaintiffs’ $168,000 loan to Hedger was recorded on May 2, 2018, in the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office.  (A-RJN, Exh. 3.)  Plaintiffs did not commence this action until September 15, 2022, or more than four years after AAMC’s 2017 Appraisal was prepared and more than four years after Plaintiffs’ Deed of Trust on the 2018 loan was recorded.  Therefore, Plaintiffs’ causes of action against AAMC Defendants are barred by the two-year statute of limitations set forth in C.C.P. §339.1(1).

Accordingly, AAMC Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ 2nd and 4th causes of action is sustained without leave to amend as to AAMC Defendants.

 

Conclusion

AAMC Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ 5AC is sustained without leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

3.     Next Day Defendants’ Demurrer

Request for Judicial Notice

Next Day Defendants’ 7/24/24 request for Judicial Notice of (1) Varon’s appraisal dated March 21, 2019 (N-RJN, Exh. A); and (2) the Loan Servicing Agreement between Plaintiffs and EWL (N-RJN, Exh. D) is granted.

Next Day Defendants’ 7/24/24 request for judicial notice of this Court’s 1/29/24 Ruling and 6/14/24 tentative ruling is denied, as this Court does not need to take judicial notice of matters on the instant docket or tentative rulings.

Plaintiffs’ 10/1/24 request for judicial notice of (1) Next Day’s appraisal dated January 11, 2019 (PN-RJN, Exh. 1); and (2) The Bureau of Real Estate Appraisers, State of California, Final Order of Citation, BREA Case No. L20240612-01 dated July 19, 2024 (PN-RJN, Exh. 2), is granted.

 

Meet and Confer

Next Day Defendants’ counsel declares that on July 16, 2024, he attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiffs’ counsel via email.  (Decl. of Campo ¶2.)  Next Day Defendants’ counsel’s declaration fails to state whether he attempted to meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference, and is therefore insufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a).  However, failure to sufficiently meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(4); Dumas v. Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 348, 355; Olson v. Hornbrook Community Services District (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 502, 515.)  Therefore, the Court will consider Next Day Defendants’ demurrer.

 

Summary of Demurrer

Next Day Defendants demur to the 2nd and 4th causes of action in the 5AC on the basis the causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action against Moving Defendants due to the holding in Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239.  (Demurrer Next Day, pg. 3; C.C.P. §430.10(e).)

 

Failure to State a Cause of Action

Negligence & Negligent Misrepresentation (2nd & 4th COAs)

A cause of action for negligence requires the following elements: (1) defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty; (2) breached the duty; and (3) the breach caused (4) damages.  (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)  A complaint which lacks allegations of fact to show that a legal duty of care was owed is fatally defective.  (See Jones v. Grewe (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 950.)  The existence of a legal duty of care is properly challenged by demurrer and is a question of law for the court.  (Hegyes v. Unjian Enterprises, Inc. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1103, 1111.)

“Negligent misrepresentation requires an assertion of fact, falsity of that assertion, and the tortfeasor’s lack of reasonable grounds for believing the assertion to be true. It also requires the tortfeasor’s intent to induce reliance, justifiable reliance by the person to whom the false assertion of fact was made, and damages to that person. An implied assertion of fact is ‘not enough’ to support liability.”  (SI 59 LLC v. Variel Warner Ventures, LLC (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 146, 154, internal citation omitted.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants failed to exercise due care in the investigation, viewing, and opining regarding the market value of the Calabasas Property.  (5AC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants created and distributed an inaccurate and erroneous appraisal report.  (5AC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that the appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and/or entities, such as Plaintiffs, with interest in the Calabasas Property.  (5AC ¶37.) 

Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants owed a duty as licensed real estate appraisers to Plaintiffs as it was known that they would rely on the erroneous appraisal reports.  (5AC ¶38.)  Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants were required to exercise due care and diligence in performing real property appraisal services as a similarly situated licensed real property appraiser would exercise.  (5AC ¶38.)

Plaintiffs allege Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants failed to act carefully and with due diligence in the performance of their appraisal services by failing to investigate and appraise the Calabasas Property carefully and diligently and to not distribute erroneous appraisal reports.  (5AC ¶39.)  Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the negligence of Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶39.)  Plaintiffs allege the negligence of Next Day Defendants and AAMC Defendants was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.  (5AC ¶39.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants represented facts that were true and/or concealed material facts concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property.  (5AC ¶52.)  Plaintiffs allege the representations and/or concealment of Defendants Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property were false.  (5AC ¶53.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants knew or should have known the representations were false and should have disclosed the material facts that were true.  (5AC ¶54.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants made the representations and/or concealments recklessly and without regard for the truth.  (5AC ¶55.)

Plaintiffs allege Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants intended for Plaintiffs to rely on the representations or should have known that Plaintiffs would have relied on the representations.  (5AC ¶56.)

          Plaintiffs allege they reasonably relied on the representations and/or material omissions of Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶57.)

          Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the misrepresentations and/or material omissions of Suddarth, EWL, Next Day Defendants, and AAMC Defendants.  (5AC ¶58.)

Plaintiffs again fail to allege who distributed the appraisal report to whom and who had knowledge of the distribution.  (See 4AC ¶¶37-38.)

An appraiser designated by a lender to value its borrower’s collateral for financing purposes generally owes its duty of care to the lender, not to the borrower.  (Greenwald & Bank, Cal. Practice Guide: Real Property Transactions, Ch. 6-L, 6:701.)  Indeed, “[w]hether the lender conducts the appraisal in house or hires an outside appraiser, the considerations are the same. The appraisal ordered by the lender is for its own protections and the borrower has [their] own means of ascertaining the desirability of the property.”  (Willemsen v. Mitrosilis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 622, 629 [noting borrower should know lender’s appraisal is intended for lender’s benefit, not to ensure success of borrower’s investment]; see also Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1253-1254, [stating lender’s appraiser had no duty of care to borrowers for inaccurate property description as appraisal was intended to support lender’s evaluation of collateral, not borrowers’ decision whether to purchase property].)

Here, Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that Moving Defendants’ appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and entities such as Plaintiffs with interest in the valuation of the Calabasas Property.  (4AC ¶37.)  Moving Defendants’ appraisal report was provided for EWL, not for Defendant Hedger, identified as the Borrower, and most importantly, not for Plaintiffs.  (N-RJN, Exh. A.)  Here, as in Willemsen, there is no indication that Next Day Defendants were aware that Plaintiffs hoped to use the appraisal report at issue as an investigational tool upon which to base their decision to approve or reject the Calabasas Property when it was prepared for EWL.  (Willemsen, 230 Cal.App.4th at pg. 631.)  Unlike in Soderberg v. McKinney (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1760, where an appraiser knew that a particular group or class of persons to which plaintiffs belonged—potential investors contacted by a broker—would rely on his report in the course of a specific type of transaction he contemplated—investing in a deed of trust secured by the appraised property, Next Day Defendants’ appraisal report was prepared for EWL, not for Plaintiffs.  (Tindell, 22 Cal.App.5th at pg. 1254.) 

The applicable contract between Plaintiffs and EWL, the LSA, indicates that the principal-agent relationship began only after the original 2018 loan was in place and as to specified activities set forth in section 1.0, entitled Scope: “Lender retains Broker as Lender’s agent to employ reasonable and prudent practices to collect all scheduled payments on the Loan identified above (the “Loan”), including the protection of the security for the Loan.”  (N-RJN, Exh. D.)

The LSA never discusses the existence of any agency relative to the decision to invest or loan additional funds and retain the appraiser or obtain the appraisal which allegedly induced Plaintiffs to enter into the further loan.

Plaintiffs do not allege that they retained Next Day Defendants and do not deny that the Appraisal was prepared for, and solely given to, EWL.  (5AC ¶26.)  Therefore, Plaintiffs cannot allege Next Day Defendants owed them a legal duty and that Next Day Defendants made any representation to or concealed information from Plaintiffs.

Accordingly, Next Day Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ 2nd and 4th causes of action is sustained without leave to amend as to Next Day and Varon.

 

Conclusion

Next Day Defendants’ demurrer to 2nd and 4th causes of action in Plaintiffs’ 4AC is sustained without leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Conclusion

Next Day Defendants’ demurrer to 2nd and 4th causes of action in Plaintiffs’ 5AC is without leave to amend as to Next Day Defendants.

Moving Party to give notice.
