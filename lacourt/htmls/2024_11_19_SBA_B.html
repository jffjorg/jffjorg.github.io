DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV03335    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

PITT STOP EL SEGUNDO, INC.,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03335

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

HOWARD ITEN, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 19, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants and cross-complainants Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Compel Answers without to Form Interrogatories, Set No. One

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.

Cross-defendant Victor Hooper is ordered to respond without objections to defendants and cross-complainants Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within twenty days.

            The Court orders that cross-defendant Victor Hooper and attorney of record, Richard P. Keavney (SBN 100701), jointly and severally, pay $1,050 in sanctions to defendants, within thirty days.

BACKGROUND

On October 9, 2023, plaintiff Pitt Stop El Segundo, Inc. filed a complaint against Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) fraud and deceit, (4) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, (5) negligent interference with prospective economic advantage, (6) declaratory relief, (7) rescission, and (8) for injunction.

On November 29, 2023, Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten filed a cross-complaint against Frank Perez, Victor Hooper, and Jose Alfredo Lopez for breach of contract.

On February 5, 2024, Frank Perez, Victor Hooper, and Jose Alfredo Lopez filed a cross-complaint against Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) declaratory relief, and (4) rescission.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.  

DISCUSSION

            Defendants and cross-complainants Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten (“Itens”) request that the Court compel cross-defendant Victor Hooper to respond without objections to their initial form interrogatories.

            Itens assert that on May 15, 2024, they served form interrogatories, set one, on Victor Hooper.  Responses were due on June 19, 2024.  On June 19, Hooper’s counsel requested a three-week extension, to July 10, 2024, which was granted.  On July 9, 2024, Hooper’s counsel requested another extension, to July 24, 2024.  On July 17, 2024, Hooper’s counsel requested an extension to July 19, 2024.  On July 31, 2024, cross-defendants Frank Perez and Jose Alfredo Lopez served responses.  On September 4, 2024, Itens’ counsel sent an email to Hooper’s counsel requesting an update on the status of Hooper’s responses but did not receive a response.  On September 18, 2024, Itens’ counsel sent Hooper’s counsel a letter notifying counsel that Hooper’s responses had not been received.  As of the filing date of the motion, Itens’ counsel had not received responses.

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that the form interrogatories were properly served, and that cross-defendant Victor Hooper failed to serve responses and thus has waived objections. 

            The motion is thus GRANTED.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Defendants and cross-complainants Itens request $1,545 against cross-defendant Hooper and his counsel, Richard P. Keavney.  The Court finds that $1,050 ($495/hr. x 2 hrs.; filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be awarded against cross-defendant Victor Hooper and his attorney of records, Richard P. Keavney.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.

Cross-defendant Victor Hooper is ordered to respond without objections to defendants and cross-complainants Howard Iten, Patricia Iten, and Susan Iten’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within twenty days.

            The Court orders that cross-defendant Victor Hooper and attorney of record, Richard P. Keavney (SBN 100701), jointly and severally, pay $1,050 in sanctions to defendants, within thirty days.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

CLEAR RECOVERY CENTER, LLC, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 19, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Clear Recovery Center, LLC

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Jane Doe

Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion to compel arbitration is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On July 17, 2024, plaintiff Jane Doe filed a complaint against Clear Recovery Center, LLC and Kyle Gillies for (1) physical abuse of dependent adult, (2) neglect of dependent adult, (3) IIED, (4) NIED, (5) sexual harassment (Civil Code §51.9), and (6) sexual battery.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Clear Recovery Center, LLC requests that the Court compel arbitration and to stay the case.

The complaint alleges that on July 19, 2022, plaintiff checked into Clear Recovery’s inpatient detox center located at 328 N. Prospect Avenue, Redondo Beach.  Complaint, ¶7.  Plaintiff had previously received services from Clear Recovery for drug addiction issues, but she had recently relapsed, posed a danger to herself, and intended to spend at least two weeks as an inpatient at this facility to address these issues.  She turned in her cell phone when she checked in and became dependent on Clear Recovery to assist her with her addiction-related mental limitations, which restricted her ability to carry out her daily activities, to protect her from herself.  Id., ¶8.

After checking into the facility, plaintiff was approached by Kyle Gillies, one of Clear Recovery’s employees, who attempted to befriend plaintiff.  He claimed to have lived next door to one of plaintiff’s former boyfriends.  Id., ¶9.  After that initial meeting, Gillies approached plaintiff while she was sitting on a couch and typed messages to her on the Notes app of his phone.  Through these messages, he told her that he had watched her when she was with her ex-boyfriend, that he wanted to take her out on a date, and that he wanted to “fool around.”  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff was uncomfortable with Gillies’ advances and told him that she was not interested.  Id., ¶11.

The complaint further alleges that the next day, plaintiff was in the common area when Gillies approached her and tried to kiss her multiple times.  She pulled away and told him no each time.  Undeterred, Gillies forced himself onto plaintiff and kissed her.  Id., ¶12.  The next day, Gillies threatened plaintiff that if she told anyone what he did, she would get kicked out of the facility and he would lose his job, which would cause him to “relapse and die.”  Id., ¶13.  With this threat hanging over plaintiff, Gillies continued his sexual pursuit of her.  Id., ¶14.  Over several nights, defendant came into her room.  She pretended to sleep.  During one night, she woke up in the middle of the night to find defendant sitting on her bed, attempting to touch and kiss her.  She tried fighting him off and told him no.  He did not stop and continued his advances.  Fearful of her wellbeing and completely overwhelmed, plaintiff went into a disassociated state, and defendant sexually abused her.  Id., ¶¶17-23.

The following morning, she told two different Clear Recovery employees what had happened the night before.  Id., ¶24.  The employees attempted to convince her that her encounter was consensual.  When she refused, she was moved into a sober living facility.  Id., ¶25.  While she was there, multiple Clear Recovery employees tried to discuss her situation with Gillies.  Id., ¶26.  Feeling that she would not heal and address her addiction in such an environment, plaintiff decided to leave the sober living facility early.  Id., ¶27.  Since leaving the facility, plaintiff has suffered from severe emotional distress coping with the sexual assaults that occurred while she was at Clear Recovery.  Id., ¶28.

 

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant contends that plaintiff executed an Admission and Financial Agreement on five separate occasions (November 4, 2020, February 3, 2021, July 6, 2022, July 19, 2022, and August 5, 2022) throughout her various admissions between November 4, 2020 and August 5, 2022.  Defendant asserts that the agreements contain an arbitration provision.  See Christopher Mijares decl., Exhs. A-E.  Defendant contends that the documents were executed in front of a Clear Recovery employee to confirm Jane Doe knew and understood what she was signing.

The arbitration provision states:

“25. Dispute Resolution.  In the event that any disagreement, dispute or claim arises among the parties hereto with respect to the enforcement or interpretation of this Agreement or any specific terms and provisions hereof or with respect to whether an alleged breach or default hereof has or has not occurred (collectively, a ‘Dispute’), such Dispute shall be settled in accordance with the following procedures:  25.1. Meet and Confer. . . . 25.2. Mediation. . . . 25.3. Arbitration.  Any Dispute which cannot be resolved by the parties within sixty days following the end of the Meet and Confer Period shall be resolved by final and binding arbitration. . . .”

In the opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant has failed to prove the existence of a valid or enforceable agreement to arbitrate plaintiff’s tort claims.  Plaintiff contends that as to four of the purported agreements attached to Mijares declaration, they have nothing to do with the subject inpatient stay and are irrelevant.  As to the potentially relevant admission agreement (at Exh. D – July 19, 2022), plaintiff asserts that it was never signed by plaintiff and that the signature block is blank.  Plaintiff also points out that the “Dispute Resolution and Jury Waiver” portion of the agreement has a signature line that was never executed, and that plaintiff did not initial the purported waiver.  Plaintiff further argues that the arbitration provision is not triggered because Clear Recovery did not properly initiate the dispute resolution process that it now seeks to enforce.  In any event, plaintiff argues, the Court should deny the motion under “third-party litigation” exception under CCP §1281.2(c) in order avoid the risk of inconsistent rulings on common issues of law or fact.

In reply, defendant argues that plaintiff does not dispute signing the agreements and that plaintiff and her counsel are in possession of unredacted and signed agreements.  Defendant presents the declaration of Jamie Arthur, who states that he is a detox and residential program director with Clear Recovery and that on July 6, 2022, he witnessed Jane Doe complete various forms, including the admission and financial agreement (referring to Exh. C attached to Mijares decl.), which included an arbitration clause.  Jamie Arthur decl., ¶6.  Defendant further argues that there is no possibility of conflicting rulings because the allegations and claims against the defendants differ.

The Court finds that defendant has not met its burden to show the existence of an arbitration provision.  Plaintiff alleges that she checked into Clear Recovery on July 19, 2022.  Defendant has not shown that she executed the Admission and Financial Agreement on July 19, 2022.  Defendant also does not show that plaintiff signed the dispute resolution clause or initial the jury waiver in the July 19, 2022 agreement.  Further, the Jamie Arthur declaration does not address the purported agreement for plaintiff’s admission on July 19, 2022.

Also, defendant has not shown how the other purported admission agreements would cover or be binding as to the admission on July 19, 2022.

 

            Scope of agreement

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s dispute applies to the arbitration provision.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that under the arbitration provision, the arbitrable issues are limited to contractual disputes related to any breach or default of the financial agreements.

In reply, defendant argues that “the language of the agreement is clear that Plaintiff agreed to submit to arbitrate all claims” but does not explain how.

The Court finds that even if an arbitration provision exists, the arbitration provision is narrow and does not encompass plaintiff’s tort claims as they do not arise from “the enforcement or interpretation of” the Admission and Financial Agreement.

The motion is thus DENIED.

ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

TIMOTHY MUTTON,

Hearing Date:                          November 19, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Timothy Mutton

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Marci Bertuzzi

Demurrer to Complaint

           

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first and sixth causes of action.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

On August 14, 2024, plaintiff Marci Bertuzzi, derivatively on behalf of Petra IQ, LLC, filed a complaint against Timothy Mutton for (1) removal of manager, (2) breach of fiduciary duty, (3) conversion, (4) money had and received, (5) unjust enrichment, (6) gross mismanagement, and (7) accounting.  By this demurrer defendant attacks only two of the causes of action.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

A word about advocacy for a demurrer.  This Court must accept as true all well pleaded facts.  It is not free to decide that the Plaintiff’s claims may not be supported by evidence.  Therefore, arguments charging that “Plaintiff’s lawsuit and the ginned up charges contained therein are nothing but a transparent attempt to distract from her own misconduct…”  (Moving Points and Authorities pg. 5:12-13) provide the Court no proper basis for ruling in favor of demurring defendant.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Timothy Mutton demurs to the first and sixth causes of action in the complaint on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that this is a derivative action to redress the past and ongoing egregious misconduct by defendant Timothy Mutton.  In his role as the other 50% owner and managing member of Petra IQ, Mutton improperly enriched himself at the expense of Petra IQ and plaintiff by misappropriating significant sums of money from Petra IQ, therefore depriving plaintiff of her rightful distributions.  Complaint, ¶1.  Petra IQ was formed by plaintiff and defendant on July 24, 2018.  Petra IQ provides data and risk management consultant services to clients in various industries.  Id., ¶15.  Plaintiff and defendant each own 50% of Petra IQ and are the only members of the company.  Id., ¶16.  On July 24, 2018, the parties entered into an operating agreement in connection with the inception of Petra IQ.  Id., ¶17.  In November 2022, plaintiff resigned as manager of Petra IQ.  Id., ¶19.  After plaintiff resigned from Petra IQ, defendant began raiding the company’s bank account in a series of brazen transactions.  Over a few day period, from March 24 to March 30, 2023, defendant transferred over $37,000 from Petra IQ’s Wells Fargo bank account to his personal bank account.  He began treating the company’s assets as his own.  Id., ¶20.

The complaint further alleges that when plaintiff learned that defendant had misappropriated Petra IQ funds, she made numerous Inspection Demands to defendant for the Company’s complete books and records, as was her right under Corp. Code §17704.10 and Petra IQ’s Operating Agreement.  The demands initially went unanswered.  Id., ¶21.  In several instances, plaintiff requested the company’s bank statements, transactional records, and the company’s current accounting statements.  Id., ¶22.  On May 26, 2023, following months of strategic delays and misdirection from defendant, defendant provided March and April 2023 bank statements and two redacted pages from defendant’s personal savings account.  Id., ¶23.  At the document production, defendant and the company were represented by attorneys Yana Komsitsky (defendant’s wife) and Andrew Escobar of Seyfarth Shaw.  During the inspection, Komitsky made incorrect representations regarding the purpose of the transfers and tried to cover up her husband’s “obvious fiduciary breaches and embezzlement of Petra IQ funds.”  Id., ¶24.  Plaintiff never consented to Seyfarth representing both Petra IQ and defendant.  Id., ¶25.  To date, defendant has not provided records responsive to plaintiff’s Inspection Demands, in violation of the operating agreement and Revised Uniform Limited Liability Act §§17704.10 and 17701.13.  Id., ¶26.  Nevertheless, the limited records that defendant did provide demonstrate that defendant embezzled company funds.  Id., ¶27.  See list of withdrawals, deposits, and transactions.  Id., ¶¶27-28, 30. 

The complaint further alleges that defendant has continued looting Petra IQ assets to present day.  On June 21, 2024, defendant wrote himself a check for $50,000.  Defendant continues to use the Petra IQ corporate credit card for personal expenses, including dining, household purchases, and food delivery.  Id., ¶32.  Defendant’s unlawful use of company funds has deprived the company of at least $155,000 in funds.  Id., ¶33.  Defendant began diverting payments that Petra IQ received from its clients to different bank accounts beginning May 2024.  Id., ¶34.  Defendant continues to grossly mismanage Petra IQ’s affairs by neglecting to pay mandatory estimated quarterly tax payments and employment taxes, which exposes the Company to interest and other penalties.  Id., ¶35.  Since plaintiff’s resignation, defendant has failed to issue plaintiff her quarterly share of distributions in breach of the operating agreement.  Id., ¶36.  Defendant has unilaterally paid himself disproportionate distributions in unknown amounts despite neglecting to issue plaintiff equal share, in further breach of his fiduciary duties.  Id., ¶38.

            First cause of action for removal of managing member

Corporations Code §§11704.01 – 17704.10 govern relations of members to each other and to the LLC.  As to “removal,” Corp. Code §17704.07(c)(5) states:  “A manager may be chosen at any time by the consent of a majority of the members and remains a manager until a successor has been chosen, unless the manager at an earlier time resigns, is removed, or dies, or, in the case of a manager that is not an individual, terminates.  A manager may be removed at any time by the consent of a majority of the members without cause, subject to the rights, if any, of the manager under any service contract with the limited liability company.”

Corp. Code §17701.10 states, in part:  “(a) Except as otherwise provided in this section, the operating agreement governs all of the following:  (1) Relations among the members as members and between the members and the limited liability company.  (2) The rights and duties under this title of a person in the capacity of manager.  (3) The activities of the limited liability company and the conduct of those activities.  (4) The means and conditions for amending the operating agreement. 

(b) To the extent the operating agreement does not otherwise provide for a matter described in subdivision (a), this title governs the matter. . . .”

The complaint alleges that since July 2018, defendant has been a managing member of Petra IQ and sole member since November 2022.  Complaint, ¶51.  Defendant, as managing member and 50% owner of Petra IQ, has engaged in dishonest and/or gross abuses of his authority or discretion in connection with the affairs of Petra IQ.  Id., ¶52.  Defendant’s dishonest acts and gross abuse of authority with reference to Petra IQ justify and require the removal of him as manager of Petra IQ and barring him from serving in a managerial position for Petra IQ for a period described by the Court.  Id., ¶53.

Defendant argues that the complaint does not allege facts that would satisfy the “threshold requirement that a majority of the members of Petra IQ must consent to” defendant’s removal.  Rather, the alleged facts are that the members are split 50/50 on his removal.  Further, defendant argues, the complaint does not allege “about what rights [defendant] has under the Petra IQ operating agreement or how the operating agreement treats situations where members are split 50/50 on the removal of a manager.” 

In opposition, plaintiff argues that she has plead sufficient facts justifying defendant’s removal.  Plaintiff contends that she has alleged specific provisions of the operating agreement applicable to defendant’s fiduciary duties, which complement more general fiduciary duties under the Corporations Code.  Plaintiff argues that the Court may enforce the operating agreement by removing defendant as manager to protect Petra IQ from further harm for defendant’s breach of fiduciary duties.

In reply, defendant argues that the allegations are insufficient “despite Plaintiff’s effort to recharacterize it as a breach of contract claim.”

The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action for “removal of manager.”  Plaintiff has not plead allegations that plaintiff is in the majority of the members (see Corp. Code, supra) or any provision in the operating agreement that governs “removal of a manager.”

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first cause of action.  Plaintiff must allege facts showing that she is entitled to have the Court remove Defendant as manager.

 

            Sixth cause of action for gross mismanagement

            The complaint alleges that defendant owed a duty to Petra IQ and plaintiff to act in good faith and to act loyally in the best interests of Petra IQ.  Complaint, ¶92.  Defendant, by his actions or inactions, directly abandoned and abdicated his responsibilities and duties as managing member of Petra IQ.  Id., ¶93.  As a direct and proximate result of defendant’s gross mismanagement and breaches of duties, Petra IQ has sustained and continues to sustain significant damages and injuries.  Id., ¶94.

            Defendant argues that this claim is not a valid cause of action or separate tort, and instead it is a “repackaged version” of the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.  Defendant asserts that there is no CACI instruction for such a claim.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant misstates the breadth of CACIs as they are not all-inclusive or exclusionary.  Plaintiff also argues that California has long recognized a cause of action and an equitable remedy where a defendant has grossly mismanaged an entity under the defendant’s control. 

            In reply, defendant reiterates his arguments that this claim is not a standalone cause of action.

            The Court finds that there is a lack of statutory and/or case authority that “gross mismanagement” is an independent cause of action.  None of the cases cited by plaintiff is persuasive.  Rather, it is a theory that may support a breach of fiduciary duty, which is Plaintiff’s second cause of action.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the sixth cause of action.

 

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first and WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the sixth causes of action in the complaint.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
