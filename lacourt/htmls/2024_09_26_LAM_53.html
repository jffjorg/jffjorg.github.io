[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to strike portions of first amended complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC         

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order striking from the First Amended Complaint filed by plaintiff Art Jr Cervera (“Plaintiff”) (1) the prayer for punitive damages (FAC p. 21, ¶ 3), and (2) the supporting allegations (FAC ¶¶ 34-35).

The court grants Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages and related allegations because (1) Plaintiff has prayed for punitive damages pursuant to Civil Code sections 1708.5, subdivision (b), and 1782, subdivision (2), but (i) Civil Code section 1708.5 authorizes a court to impose punitive damages against “[a] person who commits a sexual battery upon another[,]” but Defendant is not alleged to have committed the sexual battery against Plaintiff (FAC ¶¶ 8-10), and (ii) Civil Code section 1782, which is part of the Consumers Legal Remedies Act, does not authorize a court to impose punitive damages, and (2) Plaintiff has not alleged specific facts establishing (i) that Defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, or (ii) advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification, or act of oppression, fraud, or malice on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Civ. Code, §§ 1708.5, subd. (b), 1782, 3294, subds. (a), (b).)

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  Plaintiff did not file an opposition to this motion, and therefore has not articulated how he could amend his request for punitive damages against Defendant to render it sufficient.  The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion to strike without leave to amend.

ORDER

            The court grants defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC’s motion to strike.

            The court orders that the following portions of the First Amended Complaint filed by plaintiff Art Jr Cervera on December 4, 2023, as alleged against defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC, are stricken: (1) paragraph 34, in its entirety (page 21, lines 4-8), (2) paragraph 35 (page 21, lines 8-15), and (3) paragraph 3 of the prayer for relief (page 21, lines 22-24).

The court orders defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC to file an answer to plaintiff Art Jr Cervera’s First Amended Complaint no later than 10 days from the date of this order.

            The court orders defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, LLC to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:  September 26, 2024

Ex Parte Application for Entry of Default Judgment or Order Setting Hearing on Default

            The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with the ex parte application.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Zenayda Servin (“Plaintiff”) requests that the court enter default judgment against defendant JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors, or, in the alternative, set an Order to Show Cause re entry of default judgment for hearing.

The court notes that the default of JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors appears to have been improperly entered.

Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint in this action on March 12, 2024, against defendants Luis A. Perez d/b/a Alameda Motors and Felipe Perez d/b/a Fix and Go Auto Repair.[1]  On April 16, 2024, those defendants each filed General Denials to the First Amended Complaint.  (April 16, 2024 General Denials.)

Thereafter, on June 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” amending the First Amended Complaint to correct the name of defendant Luis A. Perez, d/b/a Alameda Motors to be JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors.  (June 17, 2024 Amendment to Compl., p. 1.)  The court entered an order approving the amendment on June 17, 2024.  The clerk entered default of JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors on August 2, 2024.  (Aug. 2, 2024 CIV-100, p. 1.)

It appears that the court should not have entered an order approving the Amendment to Complaint lodged by Plaintiff on June 13, 2024.  In requesting to amend the First Amended Complaint, it appears that Plaintiff was attempting to substitute a new entity in as a defendant.  Specifically, Plaintiff, though using the “Amendment to Complaint” form to correct the name of Luis A. Perez, d/b/a Alameda Motors, appears to have been attempting to add an entirely new corporate entity as a defendant (i.e., JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors).  (June 17, 2024 Amendment to Complaint.)  This appears to have been an improper use of the “Amendment to Complaint” form.

Further, even if that were a proper use of the Amendment to Complaint form, in amending its First Amended Complaint on June 17, 2024, Plaintiff merely corrected the name of defendant Luis A. Perez d/b/a Alameda Motors to be JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors.  (June 17, 2024 Amendment to Complaint.)  Plaintiff did not add JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors as a defendant, nor did Plaintiff designate this defendant with a fictitious name (i.e., a Doe defendant) and substitute it for that fictitious defendant.  Instead, the effect of the Amendment to Complaint is the substitution of the true name of the defendant (i.e., JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors) for the incorrect name (i.e., Luis A. Perez d/b/a Alameda Motors) wherever it appears in the First Amended Complaint.

Thus, because defendant JJL Machine Shop, Inc., d/b/a Alameda Motors has already filed a responsive pleading to the First Amended Complaint, it appears that it should not have had its default entered.  (April 16, 2024 General Denial filed by Luis A. Perez d/b/a Alameda Motors.)  Thus, the court denies Plaintiff’s ex parte application.

Based on the analysis set forth above, the court also finds that it is appropriate, and therefore exercises its discretion, to sets an Order to Show Cause (1) why the court should not set aside its June 17, 2024 order approving the Amendment to Complaint, and (2) why the court should not set aside and strike JJL Machine Shop, Inc., dba Alameda Motors’s default, entered on August 2, 2024, for hearing on December 11, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., in Department 53.

Any response to the Order to Show Cause shall be filed and served at least nine court days before the hearing.

            The court orders plaintiff Zenayda Servin (1) to give notice of this ruling to all parties who have appeared in this action, and (2) to file a proof of service of the notice of ruling with the court no later than November 4, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Motion for Monetary Sanctions

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Justin Pagano (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding monetary sanctions in his favor and against defendant FCA US LLC (“Defendant”) in the amount of $7,500 on the ground that Defendant and its counsel have engaged in “unprofessional behavior.”  (Mot., p. 3:15-17.)  It appears that the basis of Plaintiff’s motion is Defendant’s counsel’s failure to respond to certain emails sent by Plaintiff’s counsel. 

The court finds that Plaintiff has not supported his request for monetary sanctions against Defendant because Plaintiff has not (1) identified a statute or other law that would authorize the court to impose monetary sanctions against Defendant, or (2) identified conduct that is subject to sanctions.

The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Justin Pagano’s motion for monetary sanctions.

            The court orders plaintiff Justin Pagano to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

(2)   Ex Parte Application to Vacate September 26, 2024 Hearing

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with the motion for an order staying arbitration or, in the alternative, for leave to withdraw from arbitration.  No opposition papers were filed.

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with the ex parte application.

MOTION FOR ORDER STAYING ARBITRATION OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, FOR LEAVE TO WITHDRAW FROM ARBITRATION

Plaintiff Veronica Martinez (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order staying the arbitration proceedings between herself and defendant Public Health Foundation Enterprises, Inc., d/b/a Heluna Health (“Heluna”) or, alternatively, granting Plaintiff leave to withdraw from the arbitration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.97.

The court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that she served Heluna with the pending motion.  

The proof of service attached to Plaintiff’s motion states that the declarant “caused [the] envelope [with the moving papers] to be delivered for service to the recipient indicated above on the above-mentioned date, by providing same to Signal Attorney Service . . . .”  (Mot., p. 10:20-22 [emphasis added].)  However, this statement is not based on the personal knowledge of the declarant.  Thus, the court finds that this proof of service is defective and insufficient to establish that Plaintiff served Heluna with the pending motion.[1]

The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion, without prejudice to Plaintiff’s filing a new motion and filing a valid proof of service which establishes that the motion has been properly served on Defendant.

EX PARTE APPLICATION TO VACATE SEPTEMBER 26, 2024 HEARING

Specially appearing defendant Heluna has filed an ex parte application requesting that the court vacate the September 26, 2024 hearing on Plaintiff’s motion for an order staying arbitration or, alternatively, granting leave to withdraw from arbitration.  Heluna moves for this relief on the ground that the court lacks jurisdiction over it because Plaintiff has not properly served Heluna with the summons and complaint in this action.

The court has denied Plaintiff’s motion for the reason set forth above.  The court therefore finds that Heluna’s ex parte application is moot and orders that it is taken off calendar.

ORDER

The court denies plaintiff Veronica Martinez’s motion for an order staying arbitration or, in the alternative, for leave to withdraw from arbitration, without prejudice to Plaintiff’s filing a new motion and filing a valid proof of service which establishes that the motion has been properly served on Defendant.

            The court orders that specially appearing defendant Public Health Foundation Enterprises, Inc., d/b/a Heluna Health’s ex parte application to vacate the September 26, 2024 hearing is taken off calendar as moot.  

            The court orders plaintiff Veronica Martinez to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
