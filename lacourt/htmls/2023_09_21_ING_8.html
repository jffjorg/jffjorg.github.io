DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00625    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 September 21, 2023 

¿¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                  21TRCV00625

¿¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Dipu Haque, et al v. Michael Ball, et al.

¿¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiffs, Dipu Haque, et al.

¿¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:       None

¿¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        February 13, 2024

¿¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Terminating Sanctions

                                                (2) Motion for Order of Contempt

¿

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) GRANTED.  The Court strikes Defendant’s Answer for unexcused failure to comply with a Court order compelling discovery, including his failure to appear for and attend his deposition as ordered July 5, 2023

                                                (2) Plaintiffs’ Motion for Order of Contempt is Denied in light of the Court’s granting of the other motion, but without prejudice to being renewed depending on future developments.

¿¿ 

¿¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

This action involves a self-represented defendant, Michael Ball. Plaintiffs are investors in various businesses established by Mr. Ball (“Defendant”) memorialized in written contracts, wherein Defendant was appointed manager of plaintiffs’ investments.  In those contracts, Defendant granted Plaintiffs unfettered access to the businesses’ financial records so that Plaintiffs could monitor their investments at any time. Plaintiffs allege that since 2014, they have invested approximately $20 million in the aforesaid business. However, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has breached his agreements with Plaintiffs by refusing to grant them access to all of the businesses’ financial records and by taking various steps causing Plaintiffs insecurity about the soundness of their investments.

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Plaintiffs have sought discovery from Defendant about their investments, the businesses, Defendant’s relationship with some of the invested businesses, and otherwise.  The Court has been faced with multiple discovery motions by Plaintiffs against Defendant, all or nearly all of which have been granted.  Defendant fails to provide any opposition to these motions.  The Court has issued multiple orders compelling further discovery and ordering payment of monetary sanctions.  On August 9, 2023, after failing to receive any of the monetary sanctions payment, and after Defendant failed to comply with one or more orders compelling further discovery, Plaintiffs filed a Motion for Terminating Sanctions. On August 25, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Motion for Order of Contempt. To date, no responses by Defendant to either pending motion have been filed. Defendant once again did not file an opposition.

¿¿ 

¿II. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A.    Motion for Terminating Sanctions

If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (h) (depositions); § 2030.290, subd. (c) (interrogatories); § 2031.300, subd. (c) (demands for production of documents); § 2033.290, subd. (e) (requests for admission). Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .¿ [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . .” Section 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . .”¿¿ 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].)¿¿¿¿ 

“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” (Id., citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal. App. 4th at pp. 1244-1246 [discussing cases]; see, e.g., Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 [terminating sanctions imposed after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery]; Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 491, disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4 [terminating sanctions imposed against plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes].)¿¿ 

Plaintiffs request that the Court impose monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,913.77 on Defendant Michael Ball. Plaintiffs assert that this instant motion is triggered by Defendant’s refusal to appear for his court-ordered deposition on August 7, 2023. Plaintiffs first noticed Defendant’s deposition for August 22, 2022, however, he failed to appear. Plaintiffs state that they did not seek an Order compelling his appearance since Defendant was pro per, but rather re-noticed the deposition for June 5, 2023. Upon Defendant’s steadfast refusal to appear, Plaintiffs sought and obtained an Order that he do so. The Court’s Order, dated July 5, 2023, compelled Defendant to submit to his deposition no later than August 7, 2023. (Kwong Decl., Exhib. 1.)  Thereafter, Plaintiffs served Defendant with another notice for his deposition, to be conducted remotely via Zoom on August 7, 2023.

Defendant did not object or otherwise respond to the Notice. On August 4, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel forwarded to Defendant the log-in instructions for the deposition. (Kwong Decl., Exhib. 3.) On August 7, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel and Veritext Court Reporter Jahmy Alvarez signed on to the deposition at about 10:00 A.M. and waited for Defendant to appear. Not hearing from Defendant, Plaintiffs emailed him a reminder. Defendant never responded. At around 10:30 A.M., Plaintiffs recorded Defendant's non-appearance. (Kwong Decl., Exhib. 2.)

Defendant has not filed an opposition. The Court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently demonstrated that Defendant abused the discovery process and is entitled to sanctions as he disobeyed a court order to provide discovery, which was attendance at his deposition. Defendant’s continued failure to attend the scheduled deposition warrants a terminating sanction in the form of striking Defendant’s answer to the Complaint and his answer to the First Amended Complaint.

The Court also finds that Plaintiffs are entitled to monetary sanctions for Defendant’s misuse of the discovery process. Plaintiffs’ counsel claims an hourly rate of $150 with 6 hours regarding this instant motion. Plaintiffs also request Court Reporter’s fees in the amount of $952.12 and the $61.65 filing fee with respect to the motion. The Court finds this reasonable and awards $1,913.77 in additional monetary sanctions.

B.     Motion for Order of Contempt

Plaintiffs move for an Order finding Defendant in contempt for disobeying six court orders by failing to: (1) participate in discovery; and (2) pay monetary sanctions totaling the cumulative total of $10,496.65. Plaintiffs further request the imposition of a fine of $1,000 on Defendant payable to the Court or imprisonment of five days for his contempt of court or both. Plaintiffs also request the imposition of monetary sanctions totaling $ 811.65 to reimburse Plaintiffs’ attorney fees and costs incurred in pursuing the herein Motion.

To establish a prima facie case of contempt, it is necessary to establish the following: (1) a lawful order of the court was made; (2) the citee had knowledge of the order; (3) the citee had the ability to comply with the order; and (4) the citee willfully disobeyed the order. (See In re Liu (1969) 273 CA2d 135, 140-141; Board of Supervisors v. Superior Court (1995) 33 CA4th 1724, 1736.)

 

Plaintiff must prepare the OSC, accompanied by an affidavit, for the Court to review. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1211, subd. (a) [“an affidavit shall be presented to the court or judge of the facts constituting the contempt”].) “A contempt proceeding is commenced by the filing of an affidavit and a request for an order to show cause. [Citation.] After notice to the opposing party’s lawyer, the court (if satisfied with the sufficiency of the affidavit) must sign an order to show cause re contempt in which the date and time for a hearing are set forth.” (Cedars-Sinai Imaging Medical Group v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1286.) The OSC acts as a summons to appear in court on a certain day and to show cause why a contempt order should not be issued. (Ibid.) 

 

Here, Defendant was ordered to pay the fees and costs regarding motion to compel responses to discovery. Defendant failed to oppose any of those motions. (Kwong Decl. ¶¶ 1-7.) Plaintiffs’ counsel gave notice of the court-ordered sanctions and emailed Defendant reminding him of his failure to pay said sanctions. Defendant responded by claiming that he lacks funds to pay the sanctions. The court order for December 1, 2022, stated that monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,500.00 were to be paid within 30 days. (Id., Exhib. 1.) The court order for July 5, 2023 set a date of August 4, 2023 by which sanctions in the amount of $1,500.00 were to be paid. (Kwong Decl., Exhib. 7.) However, the remaining court orders of 12/8/22, 1/5/23, 1/12/23, 4/25/23, and 6/15/23 did not set forth a date by which the fees and costs had to be paid. As such, there is no evidence that Defendant willfully disobeyed the following court orders: 12/8/22, 1/5/23, 1/12/23, 4/25/23, and 6/15/23 for payment of monetary sanctions, so those orders do not provide a basis for civil contempt. However, since the 12/1/22 and 7/5/23 court orders did provide a deadline, Plaintiffs sufficiently show that Defendant willfully disobeyed those orders.

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure 1218(a), the Court finds that Plaintiffs are entitled to an award of additional sanctions for fees and costs incurred with the contempt proceeding. Since Plaintiffs have sufficiently shown Defendant’s willful disobedience of the court orders, the Court awards additional monetary sanctions. Plaintiffs’ counsel claims an hourly rate of $150 and claims 5 hours with respect to this motion. The Court reduces the hours to 3, and awards in the amount of $511.65 ($150/hr x 3 hrs + $61.65 filing fee).

In mitigation, the Court notes that Ms. Kwong’s declaration shows that Defendant claimed he lacks the financial ability to pay the monetary sanctions.  Further, the Court is granting a “doomsday” discovery sanction by striking Defendant’s Answer.  On this record, the Court does not find Defendant in contempt and will not impose a fine or jail for his failure to pay money that he may have an inability to pay, but without prejudice to a later contempt motion depending on future developments if any in the case.
