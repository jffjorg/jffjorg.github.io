DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00610    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

Motions for a Protective Order Regarding Apex Depositions

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

RULING

The motion is granted and the notices of deposition as to Jason Craik and David Craik are quashed.

BACKGROUND

On July 21, 2022, plaintiffs Judith Whitesman (in and through her successor-in-interest Robert Whitesman), Robert Whitesman (for himself), Richard Whitesman, and Deena Whitesman

sued defendant Avenir Memory Care Westside, itself and as alias for three other entities: Avenir Senior Living, LLC, AMCLA GP, Inc., and AMCLA LP (collectively “Defendants”). Plaintiffs allege causes of action for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) breach of contract, (4) willful misconduct, and (5) wrongful death.

Plaintiff Judith Whitesman died on June 6, 2022, aged 88 years. (Compl., ¶ 42.) From February 1, 2022 through her death, she resided at Avenir Memory Care Westside, an elder-care facility. (Id., ¶ 28.) During this period, Ms. Whitesman (“Judith” or “Decedent”) suffered from advanced Alzheimer’s disease. (Id., ¶ 28.) Plaintiffs allege Defendant neglected Judith’s care and failed to inform Plaintiffs of multiple adverse health events that occurred between February and June 2022. (Id., ¶¶ 29-34.) Plaintiffs became aware of Defendant’s lapses after Judith died. (Id., ¶ 34). They sued.

The Court refers to Plaintiffs by first names when necessary for clarity, rather than out of familiarity or disrespect.

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiffs noticed the depositions of David and Jason Craik, whom Plaintiffs claim had some degree of operational control over Defendants at relevant times. (Flint Decl., ¶¶ 10-11 and Exhs. 9-10; Opp., 1:22-28.) Plaintiffs selected the morning and afternoon of April 26, 2024, respectively, for each deposition. (Id., Exhs. 9-10.) Plaintiffs requested that the Craiks produce nine (9) categories of documents concurrently with their depositions. (Ibid.)

On April 18, 2024, Defendants served objections to the deposition notices. (Id., Exhs. 11-12.)

On May 3, 2024, Defendants moved for a protective order preventing the Craiks’ depositions.

On May 22, 2024, Plaintiffs opposed.

On May 28, 2024, Defendants replied.

Non-jury trial is set for October 7, 2024.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020 provides that “[t]he court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020(a).) “The court may make this determination pursuant to

a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Id.) “This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Id.)

Where good cause is shown, courts may enter protective orders limiting depositions. Commensurate with its general power under section 2017.020, “[t]he court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420(b).) Courts have considerable discretion in granting and crafting protective orders. (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 588.) A court may quash a subpoena entirely or partially, and issue an order to protect parties, witnesses or consumers from unreasonable or oppressive demands including violations of privacy. (Code Civ. Proc., §1987.1.)

DISCUSSION

Defendants argue the Craiks are “apex” officers for defendant business entities, protected from deposition unless they have “unique or superior personal knowledge” regarding the case, which they do not. (Not. of Mot., 2:3-5.)

Plaintiffs oppose Defendants’ motion based on their contention that the Craiks do possess unique or superior personal knowledge that justifies Plaintiffs’ taking their depositions.

(Plaintiffs qualify Craiks’ status as apex employees several times in their opposition – e.g., by arguing they must sit for deposition “even if” they are apex employees. (Opp., 5:13-14.) But they do not directly argue the point, nor would the Court find such an argument persuasive.)

The “apex doctrine” protects high-ranking government officials and corporate officers from deposition unless the deposing party makes a heightened showing of good cause. (Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1289 (Liberty Mutual); Westly v. Superior Court (2004) 125 Cal.App.4th 907, 910 (Westly).) Per Liberty Mutual, the governing case regarding corporate officers, a party deposing an officer must demonstrate “good cause that the official has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information … .” (Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1288-89.) Put differently: “it amounts to an abuse of discretion to withhold a protective order when a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president, or corporate officer at the apex of the corporate hierarchy, absent a reasonable indication of the officer’s personal knowledge of the case and absent exhaustion of less intrusive means of discovery.” (Id., 1287.)

“An exception will be made to [the apex] rule only when the deposing party makes two showings. First, the deposing party must show that the [proposed deponent] ‘has direct personal factual information pertaining to material issues in the action ... .’ [Citation.] Second, the deposing party must also show ‘the information to be gained from the deposition is not available through any other source.’ [Citation.]” (Contractors’ State License Bd. v. Superior Court (2018) 232 Cal.App.5th 125, 132, quoting Westly, supra, at p. 911.)

A. Prong One: “Direct Personal Factual Information” or “Unique or Superior Personal Knowledge”

a. Material Issues

Defendants argue the Craiks have no knowledge of material issues because “[i]t is undisputed that neither [of the Craiks] had any involvement whatsoever in Ms. Whitesman’s care”. (Mot., 6:1-2.)

Plaintiffs argue Defendants misidentify the relevant material facts.

Plaintiffs do not contend the Craiks were involved with Judith’s care. But because Plaintiffs have brought an elder abuse claim, certain enhanced remedies depend on a showing of ratification by a corporate officer of an employee’s conduct. (Opp., 8:4-17.) Specifically, Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657(c), permitting punitive damages against an employer for elder neglect, adopts the test for punitive damages stated in Civil Code section 3294(b):

“An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages ... based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.

With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

(Civ. Code, § 3294(b), [paragraph break for clarity].)

Plaintiffs contend they must show the Craiks had personal knowledge of certain facts to prove Plaintiffs’ entitlement to punitive damages, by any of these standards. (See Opp., 8:18-9:23 [identifying ratification as Plaintiffs’ primary theory].)

Plaintiffs also contend they must establish a joint venture in order to hold all the defendant entities liable, given Defendants’ anticipated arguments that they are separate entities and the wrongdoing of one cannot be ascribed to any others. (Opp., 9:24-10:23.)

b. David Craik’s Knowledge

Plaintiffs have not shown David Craik has unique and superior knowledge about Defendants’ “operations” or the corporate hierarchy Plaintiff is seeking to prove. At best, Plaintiff asserts that the Craiks are officers or directors of the corporate entities who received licenses from DSS to operate Avenir facilities within California.

In support of the assertion that David Craik has unique or superior knowledge of discoverable information, Plaintiffs point to the deposition of Farooq Moosa, the executive director of the facility where Judith lived, who testified he was “brought in and hired by David Craik to work at the Facility.” (Opp., 13:2-23, citing P.Exh. 6 [Moosa Deposition Transcript], 19:20-20:9.)

Moosa also testified in his deposition:

“Q: ... [W]hat’s your responsibility as President and CFO of Avenir Senior Living, Incorporated when you are made aware of clinical issues?

[Objection interposed.]

A: I would have discussions with the – I’d be made aware of it. I would report it back to Mr. Craik or and, you know, anyone that needs to be made aware of the issue.”

(Moosa Dep., 28:11-22.)

The fact that an executive director of a facility reports to “Mr. Craik” does not show David Craik’s personal involvement with Avenir’s individual facility employees, or the operations of the facility. It merely shows that there was some information that the executive director of the facility provided to senior management of the corporation.

Plaintiffs also contend Moosa directly informed Craik about issues with Judith’s care because of a relevant administrative citation. On this issue, it appears from the transcript that Moosa testified he informed Craik about the issues after Judith’s death. This by itself does not demonstrate Craik had knowledge of material facts or was even in a position to ratify the acts of a facility employee.

c. Jason Craik’s Knowledge

Plaintiffs have not made an equivalent showing for Jason Craik. They only argue he is “at the very least ... a managing agent” and had an ownership interest in on or more defendant. (See Opp., 14:18-15:26.) Plaintiffs have not carried their burden on the first prong of the inquiry as to Jason Craik.

B. Apex Prong Two: Availability from Other Sources

Notwithstanding either Craik’s knowledge, however, Plaintiffs have made no showing the information they seek is not available by less intrusive means. In fact, they waived the argument. Defendants raise this second prong in their motion; Plaintiffs do not discuss it at all in their opposition.

It is Plaintiffs’ burden to satisfy both of the standards demanded under the apex doctrine. They neglected to argue the second. Defendants’ motion is granted based on their uncontested contention that there are less intrusive means via which Plaintiffs can obtain the information they seek.

The operations of the corporation may be appropriately understood by deposing a person most knowledgeable within the corporate structure, and the hierarchy by a request for production of documents. If Plaintiff wants to assert ratification of an employee’s particular act, Plaintiff may use a less intrusive method of discovery than deposition. Defendant’s motion is granted as to David Craik and Jason Craik.

C. Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.250

Defendants also contend that both deponents live in Canada. If that is the case, then Plaintiffs’ deposition notice – which demanded the deponents appear for deposition in San Bernardino – does not satisfy Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.250, requiring natural person deponents to be deposed within seventy-five (75) miles of their residences.

This objection also goes unanswered in Plaintiffs’ opposition.

In support of Defendants’ motion, he declared that he lives in Canada. (J. Craik Decl., ¶ 1.) David Craik also attests to his place of residence in Canda. (D. Craik Decl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff does not respond with any contrary evidence or argument.

Defendants have shown the subpoena fails as to Jason and David Craik on this separate sufficient ground.

RULING

The motion is granted. The Court quashes the outstanding deposition subpoenas for David and Jason Craik.

This order is made without prejudice for Plaintiffs to bring a subsequent motion to compel compliance with a newly issued subpoena, but only if Plaintiffs can show they exhausted all less intrusive means of discovery in the interim and that David or Jason Craik has unique information not otherwise available to Plaintiffs.

Moving Parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling.


Case Number: 23TRCV00110    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

SUBJECT: Motion to Compel Neuropsychological IME of Plaintiff; and Request for Sanctions of $608.00

MOVING PARTY: Defendant William Howard Farris

RESP. PARTY: Plaintiff Wayne Davidson Wu

I. Relief Requested

Defendant Farris seeks an order compelling Plaintiff Wu’s to appear at an independent medical examination within 30 days of notice of this hearing. Defendant Farris also seeks $608.00 in sanctions for bringing this motion.

II. Moving Arguments

· Defendant seeks to compel a neuropsychological examination of Plaintiff Wu to evaluate Plaintiff’s traumatic brain injury. Defendant contends Plaintiff has put is mental functioning at issue. (Motion, 4:17-19.)

· None of the required information under Code of Civ. Proc., § 2032.310(b) apart from Dr. Thorndyke’s name and specialty.

III. Opposing Arguments

· Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s motion lacks the proper information as outlines in Code of Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, as Defendant fails to include the “time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.” (Opposition, 5:6-7.) Plaintiff contends the originally noticed IME was for a neurology examination, however, it appears Dr. Thorndyke’s specialty is neuropsychology, which is a mental examination requiring leave of court. (Id., 4:27-28, 5:1-2.)

· Plaintiff argues Defendant fails to establish good cause for the examination as this would be Plaintiff’s second examination, the first was with Dr. Kaloostian, a neurosurgeon, and Plaintiff is willing to stipulate not to bring a neuropsychology or psychological testimony at trial, pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc., § 2032.320. (Id., 6:11-17.)

IV. Reply Arguments

· No reply brief was filed as of May 31, 2024.

V. Tentative Ruling

The Court DENIES an order compelling Plaintiff’s appearance at an independent medical examination with Dr. Thorndyke. Further, the Court DENIES Defendant’s request for sanctions.

VI. Background

On January 12, 2024, Wayne Davidson Wu (“Plaintiff”) and Ceila Manuelita Wu Contreras filed a complaint against William Howard Farris (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 50 motor vehicle negligence, general negligence and loss of consortium causes of action arising out of a bicycle versus automobile accident occurring on February 21, 2021.

On May 11, 2023, Defendant filed an answer to the complaint.

On June 7, 2023, Plaintiff Ceila Manuelita Wu Contreras and her causes of action were dismissed from the matter.

On May 13, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel the neuropsychological examination of Plaintiff. Plaintiff filed an opposition on May 22, 2024. No reply brief has been filed as of May 31, 2024.

VII. Analysis

Legal Standard

Every defendant in a personal injury case is entitled to take one physical examination of the plaintiff without leave of court. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220.) If a defendant in a personal injury action wishes to take additional physical examinations or a mental examination of the plaintiff, and the plaintiff will not stipulate to the same, the defendant must bring a motion to compel the requested examination. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (a) allows for a mental exam of a party to an action whose mental condition is for good cause shown. Good cause exists when a plaintiff alleges continuing mental anguish and psychological distress stemming from the defendant’s actions. (Vinson v. Super. Ct. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840-841.) Good cause requires a showing of “specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Id. at p. 840, fn. 5 & 6.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310, subdivision (b) provides: “A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” Moreover, Pursuant to Section 2032.320, subdivision (d), the specific diagnostic tests to be performed must be explicitly identified in the court’s order and include the

time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination. (See Carpenter v. Sup. Ct. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 260-262.)

Meet and Confer Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040 states, “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”

A party must make “a serious attempt to obtain ‘an informal resolution of each issue.” (Townsend v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.) “The meet-and-confer rule is designed to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order.” (Sabado v. Moraga (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1, 12.) Even where events in the case reveal great animosity between the attorneys and attempts to resolve the issue may be fruitless, the parties must still make the attempt. (Ibid.) The level of effort that constitutes a reasonable and good faith “meet and confer” on a discovery dispute depends on the circumstances of the dispute, including the complexity of the discovery, the history of litigation, and the nature of the interaction between counsel. (Obregon v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431-433.)

Defendant presents correspondence relating to Plaintiff’s refusal to appear at the noticed IME with Dr. Thorndyke. Exh. B present a meet and confer on Plaintiff’s behalf relating to counsel’s revoking agreement for Plaintiff to appear for the examination by Dr. Thorndyke. Defendant does not show any response made in relation to Plaintiff’s meet and confer or to this motion.

Therefore, the meet and confer requirement is not met as there is no showing of defense counsel’s attempt to informally resolve this issue.

Merits of Motion

Defendant seeks an order compelling Plaintiff’s appearance at an independent medical examination by Dr. Thorndyke.

“A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination.” (Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.310, subd., (b).)

Defendant’s motion lacks the time, place, manner, condition, scope, and nature of the examination. The only information provided by Defendant is the examiner is Dr. Thorndyke and the examination would be a neuropsychology exam within 30 days of this hearing. Defendant does not provide any specific tests to be conducted nor the amount of time the examination should take. Defendant’s Demand for Neurological Examination of Plaintiff only provides the address, date, and time originally requested, but appears to request a neurological exam, which is different than a neuropsychological exam now requested. (Warren Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A.) This demand does not provide a list of tests to be conducted nor the amount of time the examination will take.

The Court finds that Defendant has failed to properly supply the Court with the information required by Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.310, subdivision (b).

Therefore, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s appearance at the independent medical examination, as well as his request for sanctions.

VIII. Conclusion

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the Motion to Compel Neuropsychological IME of Plaintiff. Additionally, the Court DENIES Defendant’s request for sanctions.


Case Number: 23TRCV01785    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

Having considered defendant’s demurrer, plaintiff’s opposition, defendant’s reply, the court rules as follows: the demurrer to the second and third causes of action in the second amended complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff Yuan Fu (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Rita Yu-Mei Chuang alleging causes of action for: (1) Medical Malpractice; and (2) Breach of Contract.

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant Rita Yu-Mei Chuang alleging causes of action for: (1) Medical Malpractice; and (2) Breach of Contract.

On August 8, 2023, Defendant Rita Yu-Mei Chuang, D.D.S. (erroneously sued as Rita Yu-Mei Chuang) (“Defendant”) filed a demurrer to the second cause of action in the FAC.

On February 27, 2024, after hearing, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC with leave to amend.

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Medical Malpractice; (2) Breach of Oral Contract; and (3) Breach of Written Contract.

On April 19, 2024, Defendant filed and served a demurrer to the second and third causes of action in the SAC on the grounds that such causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief to which Defendant replied on May 24, 2024.

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On January 21, 2021, Plaintiff first visited Defendant’s office for an orthodontic treatment to close a gap between two teeth to improve chewing. (SAC, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff was asked to sign a form titled “Agreement of Orthodontic Services” and “Treatment and Insurance Agreement – Further Fee Disclosures” (collectively, the “Agreement”). (SAC, ¶ 7; Exh. A.) For the treatment, Plaintiff was charged a fee of $6,950.00. (SAC, ¶ 8.) At another visit in March of 2021, Defendant negligently damaged one of Plaintiff’s teeth which required emergency dental repair. (SAC, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff was not reimbursed for the fees incurred for the emergency dental work. (SAC, ¶ 9.)

Plaintiff provided full payment for her treatment and alleges that Defendant failed to close the gap between the two teeth Plaintiff initially had visited for. (SAC, ¶ 10.) Plaintiff further alleges that during her treatment by Defendant, Plaintiff suffered severe temporomandibular pains (SAC, ¶ 11), Defendant refused to remove dental attachments (SAC, ¶ 12), and Defendant refused to provide Plaintiff with her treatment records. (SAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff requested to participate in arbitration pursuant to the Agreement, however, Defendant refused. (SAC, ¶ 16.) Accordingly, Plaintiff commenced the instant lawsuit. (SAC, ¶ 17.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153

Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Defendant contends that each cause of action in the SAC fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and that each cause of action is uncertain.

Responding Party’s Opposition

Plaintiff argues that the SAC pleads sufficient facts to constitute causes of action for breach of contract.

Moving Party’s Reply

On reply, Defendant argues that the allegations in the SAC are insufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract against Defendant.

Merits of the Demurrer

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met. Defendant’s counsel, Shirley Carpenter Bridwell (“Bridwell”), presents a declaration stating that the parties engaged in a meet and confer discussion on April 18, 2024 concerning the SAC but were unable to resolve the issues now before the Court. (Bridwell Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendant has satisfied the requirements of CCP § 430.41(a).

B. Uncertainty

Initially, the Court will address the contention of Defendant that the SAC is uncertain. A demurrer for uncertainty will “not be sustained if the allegations are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues that must be met, even if the allegations of the complaint may not be as clear and detailed as might be desired.” (Bacon v. Wahrhaftig (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 599, 606.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will not lie where the ambiguous facts alleged are presumptively within the knowledge of the demurring party.” (Ibid.)

Here, the Court does not find that the SAC is uncertain and therefore OVERRULES the demurrer of Defendant on the grounds of uncertainty. The SAC apprises Defendant of the issues that must be met and sets forth the purported grounds for the alleged breaches of oral and written contract.

C. Second Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) “The elements of a breach of oral contract claim are the same as those for a breach of a written contract: a contract; its performance or excuse for nonperformance; breach; and damages.” (Stockton Mortgage, Inc. v. Tope (2014) 233 Cal.App.4th 437, 453.)

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

Plaintiff alleges that on June 21, 2021, Plaintiff first visited Defendant’s office for a simple orthodontic treatment to close a gap between two teeth to improve chewing and, during such visit, Defendant verbally agreed to provide Plaintiff the treatment to close the single dental gap using the Invisalign technology. (SAC, ¶¶ 24-25, 28.) Plaintiff alleges that she paid Defendant’s fee pursuant to the oral agreement. (SAC, ¶¶ 27-28.) Plaintiff alleges that she has performed all her obligations and duties under the oral agreement with Defendant including making full payment of $6,521.00 for treatment on January 21, 2021. (SAC, ¶ 29.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached the oral agreement by failing to close the single dental gap, refusing to continue treatment to address such issues, and refusing to refund the treatment fees. (SAC, ¶ 32.) Plaintiff alleges that she has been damaged. (SAC, ¶ 33.)

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not stated a cause of action for breach of oral contract. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant promised to provide Plaintiff with treatment to close the single dental gap. Here, Plaintiff indicates that she was provided with treatment, but that the treatment was ineffective, and Defendant refused to continue treatment. Thus, based on the allegations of the SAC, it appears that Plaintiff takes issue with the quality of the purported treatments and Defendant’s cessation of such treatments. In theory, a Plaintiff could allege that a medical care provider agreed to a specified course of conduct and failed to deliver that care. However, that is

not what Plaintiff alleges. Here, there is no assertion that Defendant did not provide Plaintiff with treatment in an attempt to close the single dental gap pursuant to the oral agreement. The allegation that the treatment was unsuccessful is not a breach of contract under the circumstances described.

The Court therefore finds that the second cause of action for breach of oral contact is insufficiently alleged and the demurrer is SUSTAINED.

D. Third Cause of Action Breach of Written Contract

The Court finds that the third cause of action is insufficiently alleged. While Plaintiff has alleged the existence of the Agreement (SAC, ¶ 35), her performance thereunder (SAC, ¶ 37), Defendant’s breach (SAC, ¶ 40), and damages (SAC, ¶ 40), the Court notes that the Agreement does not provide for a particular course of treatment or a particular outcome. Instead, the Agreement sets forth the necessity of payment, the interaction between the provider and an insurer, and general complications that may be expected with orthodontic treatments. Thus, Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant breached the Agreement by failing to close the single dental gap, causing loose spaces, and refusing to refund treatment fees (SAC, ¶ 40), are not explicit obligations contemplated by the Agreement.

Additionally, Plaintiff alleges that the Agreement was breached because Defendant refused to participate in arbitration pursuant to the Agreement. (SAC, ¶ 43.) Plaintiff alleges that pursuant to the arbitration clause in the Agreement, she requested that Defendant participate in arbitration, but Defendant failed to respond to such request. (SAC, ¶ 43.) The arbitration clause in the Agreement provides that “[i]n the event of disagreement regarding the resolution of any treatment or financial dispute, both parties will nominate one arbitrator each, who will then agreement on a third arbitrator, and the third arbitrator alone shall decide the matter so as to be fair to both parties and avoid excessive legal expense. The arbitrator shall also decide the proportionate responsibilities for the arbitration fee.” (SAC, Exh. A.)

Plaintiff, however, has not alleged that she fully performed under the arbitration clause and merely alleges that she “complied with her obligation by making a demand on Defendant to participate in arbitration.” (SAC, ¶ 44.) The arbitration clause does not require a demand but rather requires that each party shall nominate one arbitrator each. Plaintiff does not allege such fact.

The Court therefore finds that the third cause of action for breach of written contract is insufficiently alleged and the demurrer is SUSTAINED.

E. Leave to Amend

The Court finds that there remains the reasonable possibility of successful amendment as to the contractual claims. Plaintiff may allege facts that, if proven, would state a cause of action for breach of written contract for failing to comply with the arbitration clause and that there was a specified course of conduct agreed to and with which Defendant failed to deliver.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer of Defendant to the second and third causes of action in the SAC with 20 days leave to amend.


Case Number: 24TRCV00019    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

Motion: Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

RULING:

The Court considered the moving papers. After review, the Court rules that Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff Shannon Neighbors (“Plaintiff”) filed a form Complaint for general negligence and premises liability against Defendant T.J. Maxx of CA, LLC (erroneously sued as The TJX Companies, Inc.) alleging that on March 4, 2022, she was at TJ Maxx/HomeGoods in Westchester when she was hit by a chair that was falling over. (Complaint, p.4.)

On April 23, 2024, Defendant filed the demurrer without strike.

As of June 3, 2024, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a); see also CCP § 435.5 (imposing similar requirements for a motion to strike).)

On April 18, 2024, I spoke with attorney Avi Muhtar to meet and confer regarding this demurrer. We were unable to reach an agreement. (Thompson Decl. ¶ 4.) Therefore, the requirement is satisfied.

Demurrer

A. Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

The question of plaintiff's ability to prove these allegations is of no concern at the pleadings stage. (See Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp., (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214. [“It is not the ordinary function of a demurrer to test the truth of the plaintiff's allegations or the accuracy with which he describes the defendant's conduct.”])

Analysis

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s first (general negligence) and second (premises liability) causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e).) Defendant also argues that the first (general negligence) and second (premises liability) causes of action are uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(f).)

In this case, Plaintiff’s form complaint alleges two, seemingly identical, causes of action. Both allege one key fact: “Plaintiff was hit by a chair that was falling over.” This fact is insufficient to put Defendant on notice as to the factual basis for their culpability.

In addition, the complaint also alleges a “willful failure to warn” as required by Civil Code section 846. However, no allegation of recreational activity is alleged.

CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
