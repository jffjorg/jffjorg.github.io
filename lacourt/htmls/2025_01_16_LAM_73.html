Case Number: 21STCV43483    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 73

01/16/2025

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf M. Treu, Judge presiding 

 

German Barrios v. Gabriel Angel Albanez, et al. (21STCV43483)

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Plaintiff German Barrios is in pro per

Counsel for Defendant: N/A

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 10/07/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is denied.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff German Barrios (“Plaintiff”), in pro per, filed this action against Defendants Gabriel Angel Albanez, GG Investment Firm, Citadel Escrow, and George Gallardo (collectively “Defendants”) on November 29, 2021. Plaintiff’s complaint alleges breach of contract and fraud causes of action against Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants breached their agreement with Plaintiff to buy Plaintiff’s property so that foreclosure could be avoided. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Citadel Escrow concealed the fact that no monies were received in escrow and induced Plaintiff to sign a blank grant deed. Plaintiff lost the property as a result.

 

After Plaintiff finally served Defendant Citadel Escrow (“Citadel”) and Citadel failed to answer Plaintiff’s complaint, Plaintiff has attempted to obtain default judgment against Citadel several times. Most recently, on October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed another request for entry of default judgment. Plaintiff also filed a Section 585(d) Declaration and a proposed judgment on that date.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.      Application

Plaintiff has not provided a summary of the case as is required by CRC 3.1800. Plaintiff’s Section 585(d) declaration describes Citadel as a now-dismissed defendant in this action, despite Plaintiff trying to obtain the default judgment against Citadel. (Barrios Decl., ¶ 11.) It does not appear that the Doe Defendants that were named in the complaint, nor Defendant George Gallardo, have been dismissed from the case. The damages requested in Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment do not match the requested damages on Plaintiff’s proposed judgment, filed on Form JUD-100. Finally, the evidence submitted with Plaintiff’s declaration in support of the judgment is the same evidence that this Court had previously found to be insufficient.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court cannot enter default judgment against Defendant Citadel Escrow.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is denied.

STEARNS BANK NATIONAL ASSOCIATION v. B. GRIGOROVICH D.D.S., INC., ET AL. (22STCV24560)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Christopher D. Crowell

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Armand Tinkerian

 

motion for attorney fees (filed 12/11/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff Stearns Bank National Association’s motion for attorney fees and costs is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is awarded $66,374.08 in fees and costs payable within 30 days of this order.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 29, 2022, Plaintiff Stearns Bank National Association (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendants B. Grigorovich D.D.S., Inc., Boris Grigorovich (“Defendants”); and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive for (1) breach of equipment finance agreement (no. 1); (2) breach of equipment finance agreement (no. 2); (3) breach of guaranty (no. 1); and (4) breach of guaranty (no. 2).

 

On September 19, 2022, Defendants filed an Answer.

 

On November 7, 2022, this Court sustained in part Plaintiff’s demurrer to answer as to affirmative defenses 2 through 12, 14 through17, 19 through 21, 25 through 28, and 32.

 

On December 6, 2022, Defendants filed an Amended Answer. On December 7, 2022, Defendant Boris Grigorovich filed a Cross-Complaint against Invasix, Inc. (“Cross-Defendant”) for (1) breach of oral contract; (2) fraud; (3) unjust enrichment; (4) violation of Civ. Code §§ 1709-1710; and (5) equitable indemnity.

 

On November 2, 2023, this Court sustained with leave to amend Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to cross-complaint as to its entirety and rendered the motion to strike moot.

 

On January 9, 2024, the Cross-Complaint was stricken for lack of prosecution.

 

On October 1, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment. On October 21, 2024, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff against Defendants, jointly and severally in the amount of $436,475.40 including items allowable as costs under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1032.

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Memorandum of Costs (“MOC”).

 

On December 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Attorney Fees, arguing:

·                Plaintiff reasonably incurred attorney’s fees in the amount of $64,453.50 from 11/9/21 through 10/20/24 to enforce its rights under the Agreements and Guarantees;

·                Plaintiff as the prevailing party in this action is entitled to an award of all these fees and costs pursuant to Civil Code Section 1717, Code of Civil Procedure Sections 1032 and 1033.5, and California Rules of Court, rule 3.1702.

No opposition has been filed.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion for Attorney Fees

 

“In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.” (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).) 

 

The prevailing party has the burden of showing that the requested attorney fees were “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and were reasonable in amount.” (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California Inc.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 817.) The party seeking attorney fees “ ‘is not necessarily entitled to compensation for the value of attorney services according to [his] own notion or to the full extent claimed by [him].’ ” (Levy v. Toyota Motor Sales, USA, Inc.¿(1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 807, 816.)¿¿Therefore, if the “time expended or the monetary charge being made for the time expended are not reasonable under all the circumstances, then the court must take this into account and award attorney fees in a lesser amount.” (Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America¿(1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 104.)¿¿

 

A court may “reduce a fee award based on its reasonable determination that a routine, noncomplex case was overstaffed to a degree that significant inefficiencies and inflated fees resulted.”¿¿(Morris v. Hyundai Motor America¿(2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 39.)¿¿It is also appropriate to reduce an award based on inefficient or duplicative efforts. (Id.¿at p. 38.) However, the analysis must be “reasonably specific” and cannot rely on general notions of fairness. (Kerkeles¿v. City of San Jose¿(2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 88,¿102.)¿¿Moreover, in conducting the analysis, courts are not permitted to tie any reductions in the fee award to some proportion of the buyer’s damages recovery. (Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc.¿(2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 39.)

 

B.      Analysis

 

Plaintiff moves for an order awarding it a total of $66,374.08 in attorney’s fees and costs consisting of (1) $43,173.50 in fees for Frandzel, Robins, Bloom & Csato, L.C. (“FRBC”); (2) $21,280.00 in fees for Hemar, Rousso & Heald, LLP (“HRH”); and (3) $1,920.00 in costs from HRH (requested via Memorandum of Costs).

 

 

1.      Timeliness of Motion

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1702, subdivision (b), provided in pertinent part: “A notice of motion to claim attorney's fees for services up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court--including attorney's fees on an appeal before the rendition of judgment in the trial court--must be served and filed within the time for filing a notice of appeal under rules 8.104 and 8.108 in an unlimited civil case or under rules 8.822 and 8.823 in a limited civil case.” Thus, “[i]n an unlimited civil action such as the case before us, that deadline ordinarily falls 60 days after notice of entry of judgment, or 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first.” (Kaufman v. Diskeeper Corp. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1, 8; Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 8.104.)

 

Here, the entry of judgment on the summary judgment motion’s ruling occurred on October 21, 2024. As such, Plaintiff has until July 16, 2025 to file the present motion. The motion was filed on December 11, 2024. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion is timely.

 

2.      Entitlement to Attorney’s Fees

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff basis for this instant motion is due to the ruling on its summary judgment motion. Specifically, Plaintiff contends it is the prevailing party under the terms of the Agreements and Guarantees because its summary judgment motion was granted by this Court. The Court agrees. On October 1, 2024, this Court granted summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claims of breach of the Agreements and Guarantees. (Min. Order, filed 10/2/24.) Subsequently, this Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendants pursuant to its summary judgment motion ruling. (Judgment, filed 10/21/24.) Moreover, under the terms of the Agreements and Guarantees, the prevailing party would recover reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred in a lawsuit to enforce the contracts. Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees per Civil Code Section 1717.

 

i.                    Attorney Hours

 

Moreover, two separate law firms worked on this case for Plaintiff. Frandzel, Robins, Bloom & Csato, L.C. (“FRBC”) worked on this atter from July 29, 2022 until February 7, 2024. A substitution of attorney was filed on February 7, 2024, and indicated Hemar, Rousso & Heald, LLP (“HRH”) would represent Plaintiff moving forward. HRH represented until the ruling on the summary judgment motion.

 

The hourly rates to be used in computing the lodestar must be “within the range of reasonable rates charged by and judicially awarded comparable attorneys for comparable work.”  (Children's Hospital & Medical Center v. Bonta´ (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 740, 783; PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095  (“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work”).  Particularly where it is difficult to obtain evidence of market based rates for the same type of work, the courts look at fees charged for cases requiring similar skills.  (The Utility Reform Network v. Public Utilities Com. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 522, 536–537; Prison Legal News v. Schwarzenegger (9th Cir.2010) 608 F.3d 446, 454–455 (“all attorneys in the community engaged in ‘equally complex Federal litigation,’ no matter the subject matter”). 

 

In determining the reasonable rate and reasonable hours, the Court looks to that “prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group, Inc., supra, 22 Cal.4th at 1095; Ketchum, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 1132 (“the lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community”).  “A reasonable trial court might determine that the ‘similar work’ or ‘comparable legal services’ related to insurance defense litigation, rather than to civil litigation in general. Were the court to so conclude, it could view the relevant ‘market’ to be that of insurance defense litigation and litigators, rather than general civil litigation. The “market rate” for such services might be limited accordingly. Again, we emphasize that such determinations lie within the broad discretion of the trial court.”  (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 702–703.)

 

The burden is on the fee applicant to produce evidence that the requested rates are in line with those prevailing in the community for similar work.  (ComputerXpress, Inc. v. Jackson (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 993, 1019.)  “Affidavits of the plaintiffs' attorney and other attorneys regarding prevailing fees in the community, and rate determinations in other cases, particularly those setting a rate for the plaintiffs' attorney, are satisfactory evidence of the prevailing market rate.”  (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 1009.) The trial court is not required to adopt counsel’s opinion as to the “market rate” for services of the type performed.  (Syers Properties III, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at 702.)  The trial court may accept the actual rate charged as the reasonable rate.  (Id.)

 

Hal D. Goldflam submits a declaration attesting to FRBC attorneys incurring $43,173.50 in attorney’s fees to represent Plaintiff from November 9, 2021 to February 28, 2024 at the hourly rates of $282.27 to $575.00. (Goldflam Decl., ¶¶2-4, Exs. 1-24.) Specifically, Mr. Goldflam attest to FRBC incurring fees to (1) draft pre-filing demand letters; (2) drafting the Complaint; (3) drafting two sets of written discovery requests to Defendants; (4) litigating a demurrer to Defendants’ Answer to the Complaint; (5) responding to Defendants’ written discovery requests, including making a voluminous document production; (6) addressing issues arising from Cross-Defendant Invasix, Inc.’s involvement in the case; and (7) attending several hearing in this action for a total of 91.9 hours. (Id.) These hours are supported by billing invoices and relate directly to the pre-litigation and litigation of this case. (Id.)

 

Similarly, Christopher D. Crowell submits a declaration attesting to HRH attorneys incurring $21,28.00 in attorney’s fees to represent Plaintiff in this case from January 31, 2024 to October 18, 2024 at an hourly rate of $350.00. (Crowell Decl., ¶¶2-4, Exs. 25-33.) HRH incurred fees for: (1) propounding an additional set of written discovery requests to Defendants; (2) filing six motions to compel discovery responses, which the court granted; and (3) preparing a dispositive motion, which required a lengthy review of Plaintiff’s invoices, loan histories, and other files to explain in detail the calculations giving rise to Plaintiff’s loan balances. These hours are supported by the billing invoices and relate directly to the work performed from time HRH was substituted in as counsel until the granting of the summary judgment motion. (Id.)

 

Both Goldflam and Crowell attest to these rates being the comparable rates charged by attorneys in Los Angeles who practice business and commercial litigation with their level of experience. (Goldflam Decl., ¶5; Crowell Decl., ¶¶5-6.)

 

Therefore, Plaintiff has shown that the time spent by both law firms who represented it in this action were reasonably incurred and necessary to litigate this matter.

3.      Costs

 

“A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.5 or the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first. The memorandum of costs must be verified by a statement of the party, attorney, or agent that to the best of his or her knowledge the items of cost are correct and were necessarily incurred in the case.”(Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1700(a)(1).)

 

To recover a cost, it must be reasonably necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount. (Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 244.) If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-74.)¿ On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.)¿¿¿

 

Here, the items are on the Memorandum of Costs (“MOC”) appear to be proper charges for costs allowable pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 1033.5.  Specifically, Plaintiff seeks $1,920.00 for HRH consisting of: (1) $795.00 – filing and motion fees; (2) $474.98 – service of process fees; and (3) $650.60 – electronic filing or service. Finally, Defendants have not challenged these costs via a Motion to Tax Costs.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff Stearns Bank National Association’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is awarded $66,374.08 in fees and costs payable within 30 days of this order.

MICHAEL RODRIGUEZ v. READY PAC LLC (24STCV08779)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:   David Robert Denis / Law Office of David Robert Denis

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Ann Kathleen Smith / Atkinson, Andelson, Loya, Ruud & Romo

 

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION AND STAY PROCEEDINGS (filed 10/31/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration and stay proceedings is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Michael Rodriguez filed this employment action against defendant Ready PAC LLC on April 8, 2024. He asserts nine (9) causes of action for various violations of the Fair Employment and Housing Act, whistleblower retaliation, and wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

 

Plaintiff alleges he began working for Defendant as an electrician on November 6, 2019. (Compl., ¶ 12.) He was terminated on April 15, 2021. (Ibid.) According to Plaintiff’s complaint, he suffered injuries in the course of his employment, and his injuries caused him to suffer from a physical disability. (Id., ¶ 20.) Rather than accommodating his disability, Defendant terminated him. (Id., ¶¶ 23-24.)

 

On October 31, 2024, Defendant moved to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate his claims and to stay these proceedings pending the outcome of arbitration. Defendant contends Plaintiff signed an “Associate Arbitration Agreement” (“Agreement”) on November 14, 2019, during Defendant’s onboarding procedure at the outset of Plaintiff’s employment. The Agreement purportedly binds Plaintiff to arbitrate the claims he asserts in his complaint.

 

On January 3, 2025, Plaintiff filed his opposition. He denies he signed the Agreement and argues Defendant has submitted no admissible evidence showing otherwise. He also contends the Agreement is unenforceable because it is not part of a contract supported by bargained-for consideration. Finally, he argues his dispute with Defendant is not within the scope of the Agreement because Defendant’s signature does not appear on the Agreement.

 

On January 9, 2025, Defendant replied. It argues Plaintiff cannot credibly disavow his signature because he has not denied that he printed his name on the Agreement and initialed it in a blank on its first page. Nor does he deny he was “provided [with] onboarding documents” including the Agreement. Finally, Defendant points out that the Agreement applies to all the affiliates of “Ready Pac Produce, Inc.”, the signatory, which include Defendant, the signatory’s parent.

 

II. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff’s Objection Nos. 1-3 are overruled.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection No. 4 is sustained for hearsay and/or lack of personal knowledge.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection No. 5 is sustained for lack of personal knowledge and/or improper secondary evidence. The records speak for themselves.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection No. 6 is sustained to the extent the declarant testifies to the fact that Plaintiff signed the documents, which is outside his personal knowledge. The records speak for themselves.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection No. 7 is sustained as improper secondary evidence.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection No. 8 is overruled.

 

III. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

Under California and federal law, public policy favors arbitration as an efficient and less expensive means of resolving private disputes. (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blasé (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 8-9 (Moncharsh); AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339.) Accordingly, whether an agreement is governed by the California Arbitration Act or the Federal Arbitration Act, courts resolve doubt about an arbitration agreement’s scope in favor of arbitration. (Moncharsh, supra, 3 Cal.4th at p. 9; Comedy Club, Inc. v. Improv West Associates (9th Cir. 2009) 553 F.3d 1277, 1284; see also Engalla v. Permanente Med. Grp., Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72 [“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability [citation] and a requirement that an arbitration agreement must be enforced on the basis of state law standards that apply to contracts in general [citation].”].) “[U]nder both the FAA and California law, ‘arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, save upon such grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract.’” (Higgins v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1247.)

 

The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.  (Engalla, supra, 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.)  A petition to compel arbitration must allege both a “written agreement to arbitrate” the controversy, and that a party to that agreement “refuses to arbitrate” the controversy. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) It then becomes plaintiff’s burden, in opposing the motion, to prove by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to her opposition. (Ibid.)

 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 requires a trial court to grant a petition to compel arbitration ‘if the court determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.’”  (Avery v. Integrated Healthcare Holdings, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 50, 59 (Avery), quoting Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2).) Accordingly, “when presented with a petition to compel arbitration, the court’s first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute.” (Id., at p. 59.) Generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues. (Ibid.; see also Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.) “In these summary proceedings the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Avery, supra, 218 Cal.App.4th at p. 59; accord Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)

 

B.      Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

By advancing a copy of the Agreement that appears to carry Plaintiff’s signature, Defendant carried its initial burden.

 

However, in his opposition, Plaintiff unequivocally disavows the signature that appears on the Agreement:

 

“The signature that appears at the end of the Agreement document … is definitely not my signature. I can and do state that I never signed the Associate Arbitration Agreement. … [¶] [¶] I never executed the Agreement or any acknowledgment of the existence of the Agreement and was never informed by Defendant even of the existence of the Associate Arbitration Agreement or of any arbitration agreement.”

 

(Rodriguez Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4.)

 

An examination of the signatures on the documents does not show that it is so “identical” to Defendant’s other exhibits as to render its authenticity beyond question, as Defendant claims. And Defendant has not produced any direct evidence that Plaintiff signed the Agreement; it has only shown that a signature similar to Plaintiff’s appears on the Agreement it keeps in its files.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff has made an “egregious insinuation that Defendant conspired to forge his signature.” (Reply, 4:7-8.) It would be similarly egregious for the Court to adopt Defendant’s express accusation that Plaintiff has perjured himself.

 

Once Plaintiff expressly disavowed his signature, it fell to Defendant to show he signed the document by a preponderance of the evidence. The introduction of several business records with similar signatures do not carry this burden in the face of Plaintiff’s express denial.

 

Because Defendant has not carried its threshold burden, the motion must be denied.

 

/ / /

 

C.      Signatories and Enforceability

 

Because Defendant has not carried its threshold burden, the Court need not decide the remaining issues raised in the papers. It does not matter whether Defendant’s affiliate signed the Agreement, because Defendant has not shown Plaintiff signed it. Nor does the Court need to decide Plaintiff’s argument that the Agreement is not part of the bargained-for terms of his employment.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Because Defendant failed to rebut Plaintiff’s contention that he did not sign the Agreement, Defendant has not carried its threshold burden to show the existence of an arbitration agreement.

 

Defendant’s motion is denied.

DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE (filed 12/13/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

I. BACKGROUND

              7. Trespass,

              8. Private Nuisance,

              9. Quiet Title,

              10. Injunctive Relief, and

              11. Declaratory Relief.

 

As alleged in Plaintiffs’ complaint and accepted as true for demurrer (undesignated paragraph citations to the complaint):

 

Plaintiffs own the property located at 240 S. Carondelet St., Los Angeles CA 90057 (“the Subject Property”). (¶ 17.) Defendants own the adjoining property at 234 S. Carondelet St. (“the Neighboring Property”). (¶¶ 18-19; see also ¶¶ 8-14 [alter ego allegations].) On December 13, 2024, Defendants demurred to all of Plaintiffs’ causes of action except the second, for negligence, and the eleventh, for declaratory relief. They also moved to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint.

 

In support of their demurrer, Defendants argue:

 

·                Elder Abuse: There was no “taking” of property for purposes of a financial elder abuse claim, nor was any purported taking “for a wrongful use and/or with the intent to defraud”;

·                Conversion: Plaintiffs do not allege any property was converted, they only allege property was damaged;

·                Intentional Misrepresentation: Plaintiffs do not allege they relied to their detriment on Defendants’ alleged misrepresentations about “what they would do with respect to repairing the wall that was accidentally damaged”;

·                Trespass: Plaintiffs have not alleged Defendants ever intentionally trespassed; in fact, the complaint concedes “the wall was accidentally knocked down”;

·                Nuisance: Plaintiffs do not allege any substantial, unreasonable interference with their property rights;

·                Quiet Title: Plaintiffs do not allege Defendants assert any interest in their property; and

In support of their motion to strike, Defendants argue Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged malice, oppression, etc. to support their claim for punitive damages.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that:

 

·                Elder Abuse: Raul Rodriguez is an elder, and Defendants took portions of his property (a rear boundary wall) for their own use;

·                Conversion: Plaintiffs allege Defendants exercised dominion over their property;

·                Intentional Misrepresentation: Defendants misrepresented that they were qualified developers and would repair damage to Plaintiffs’ boundary wall, and Plaintiffs allowed them to proceed with construction and did not resort to legal action in reliance on those representations;

·                Trespass: Plaintiffs allege Defendants sent workers onto the Subject Property knowingly and intentionally to demolish their wall;

·                Nuisance: The question of substantial invasion is not for resolution at the pleading stage, and regardless, Plaintiffs have met it;

·                Quiet Title: Plaintiffs allege Defendants are asserting an adverse claim, whether or not Defendants renounce it, because Defendants are occupying a portion of Plaintiffs’ property; and

·                Injunctive Relief: Plaintiffs’ claim is a remedy designed to enjoin and restrain further wrongdoing and require abatement of a nuisance.

 

In opposition to Defendants’ motion to strike, Plaintiffs argue their facts, taken as true, show malice, oppression, or fraud.

 

Defendants filed no reply in support of their demurrer. In support of their motion to strike, they reply that the exhibits to Plaintiff’s complaint show Defendants worked with them in good faith to resolve the dispute and Defendants’ alleged misconduct, if it occurred, was unintentional; thus, punitive damages have not been pled.

 

II. MEETING AND CONFERENCE

 

“Before filing a demurrer ... , the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) An equivalent requirement applies to a motion to strike. (Id., § 435.5.)

 

Defendants’ counsel filed a form declaration establishing the parties met and conferred. (See 12-13-2024 CIV-140.)

 

III. ANALYSIS - DEMURRER

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint alleges facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10; Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220.)

 

When considering a demurrer, a court reads the allegations stated in the challenged pleading liberally and in context, and “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Put differently: for purposes of demurrer, the court treats all facts alleged – but only the facts alleged – in the complaint as true. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School District (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 732.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

B.      First Cause of Action for Financial Elder Abuse

 

A plaintiff claiming elder abuse must allege (1) she is an elder adult and (2) a defendant took, secreted, appropriated, or retained her real or personal property, or assisted another to do so, (3) to a wrongful use, with an intent to defraud, or by undue influence. (Wel. & Inst. Code § 15610.30, subd. (a); Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 174.)

 

As to the first element, Defendants do not deny that plaintiff Raul Rodriguez is an “elder” as defined.

 

As to the second element, “a person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property when an [elder] is deprived of any property right, including by means of an agreement, or testamentary bequest … .” (Id. ¶ 15610.30(c).) Plaintiffs allege Defendants have partly deprived Mr. Rodriguez of his rights in the Subject Property.

 

As to the third element: Plaintiffs allege Defendants falsely claimed Plaintiffs’ boundary wall “collapsed due to the construction” even though Defendants’ workers had “recklessly demolished [it]” at Defendants’ instruction. (¶ 26.) Defendants then falsely promised to rebuild the wall, intending to use it as a necessary retaining wall for their own construction project. (Ibid.) Accepted as true, this course of action amounts to fraud. The third element is pled.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is overruled.

 

C. Third Cause of Action for Conversion

 

“ ‘Conversion is generally described as the wrongful exercise of dominion over the personal property of another.’ ” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119, italics added.) Plaintiffs assert wrongful exercise of dominion over their real property, not their personal property. The demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

D. Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action for Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

The essential elements of fraud, generally, are (1) a misrepresentation (2) knowledge of falsity (3) intent to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. [Citation]. (City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 191, 211.) Similarly, “[t]he elements of negligent misrepresentation are ‘(1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage.’” (National Union Fire Insurance Co. of Pittsburgh, PA v. Cambridge Integrated Services Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal. App. 4th 35, 50, quoting Apollo Capital Fund LLC v. Roth Capital Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 226, 243.) Each element of either tort must be pleaded with particularity to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action. (City of Industry, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at p. 211, citing Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216–217.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs have not pled justifiable reliance on any purported misrepresentation. The Court disagrees. Plaintiffs allege they permitted Defendants to continue nuisance behavior unabated and to adversely occupy Plaintiffs’ property based on Defendants’ false representations that they were working with Plaintiffs in good faith and intended to remedy their wrongful intrusion on Plaintiffs’ property. Plaintiffs have stated a claim for intentional misrepresentation.

 

E. Sixth and Seventh Causes of Action for Trespass

 

“ ‘Trespass is an unlawful interference with possession of property.’ [Citation.] The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant's intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm.” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 261–262.)

 

Defendants argue the complaint “make[s] clear that the Defendants did not intend to make an unauthorized entry on the Plaintiffs’ land.” (Dem., 11:23-24.) On the contrary, Plaintiffs expressly allege Defendants directed their workers to enter Plaintiffs’ property without permission. (See ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs have stated a claim. The demurrer is overruled.

 

F. Eighth Cause of Action for Private Nuisance

 

At the pleading stage, Plaintiffs have stated a claim for nuisance. In order to make a prima facie claim for nuisance, “[f]irst, the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of his property. [Citation.] Second, ‘the invasion of the plaintiff's interest in the use and enjoyment of the land [must be] substantial, i.e., that it cause[s] the plaintiff to suffer “substantial actual damage.” ’ [Citation.] Third, ‘ “[t]he interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, but it must also be unreasonable” [citation], i.e., it must be “of such a nature, duration or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” ’ ” (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263.)

 

“The requirements of substantial damage and unreasonableness are not inconsequential.” (Id. at p. 263, italics in original.) Descriptions of the standard are numerous and nonspecific: as “significant harm”, “harm of importance”, a “real and appreciable invasion of the plaintiffs’ interests,” “definitely offensive, seriously annoying or intolerable,” conduct by which “normal persons in that locality would … be substantially annoyed or disturbed”, and so on. (San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938, citations omitted.)

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants are conducting an unlicensed construction project and have seized a portion of Plaintiffs’ adjoining real property to further that construction. Accepted as true, this is an intolerable invasion of Plaintiffs’ property rights. The claim is pled, and the demurrer is overruled.

 

/ / /

 

G. Ninth Cause of Action to Quiet Title

 

An action for quiet title seeks “to establish title against adverse claims to real or personal property or any interest therein.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 760.020, subd. (a).)  In an action for quiet title, Plaintiff must plead (1) “[a] description of the property that is the subject of the action,” specifically the location of tangible personal property and the legal description and street address or common designation of real property, (2) “[t]he title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title,” (3) “[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought,” (4) “[t]he date as of which the determination is sought,” and (5) “[a] prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.) 

 

Plaintiffs have not alleged the nature of their own title, except as “rightful owners”. (¶ 100.) They have also not alleged what title they claim. This is particularly important here, given that there are two plaintiffs suing in four capacities. They have also not alleged what adverse claim Defendants purtportedly make to the Subject Property. Nor do they allege the date as of which the determination is sought. They have not stated a claim to quiet title. The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.

 

H. Tenth Cause of Action for Injunctive Relief

 

Plaintiffs concede that injunctive relief is a remedy, not a cause of action. The demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

 

IV. ANALYSIS – MOTION TO STRIKE

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Strike

 

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer, such as words, phrases, and prayers for damages. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, and 437.) The proper procedure to attack false allegations in a pleading is a motion to strike. (Id. § 436 (a).)

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435 [notice of motion to strike whole or part of complaint], or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Id. § 436 (a).) Irrelevant matters include immaterial allegations that are not essential to the claim or those not pertinent to or supported by an otherwise sufficient claim. (Id., § 431.10.) The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id. § 436 (b).)

 

A motion to strike is also the proper procedure to attack a purportedly improper remedy such as unjustified punitive damages. (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1547, 1561-1562.) A complaint including a request for punitive damages must also include allegations showing that the plaintiff is entitled to such an award. (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

A plaintiff may seek punitive damages for “oppression, fraud or malice” by the defendant. (Civ. Code, § 3294(a).) “Malice” means conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct that is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(1).)  “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(2).)  “Fraud” means intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact with the intention of depriving a person of property or legal rights, or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(3).)

 

A claim for punitive damages cannot be pleaded generally and allegations that a defendant acted “with oppression, fraud and malice” toward plaintiff are insufficient legal conclusions to show that the plaintiff is entitled to an award of punitive damages. (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) Specific factual allegations are required to support a claim for punitive damages. (Ibid.)

 

As noted above, Plaintiffs have pled a claim for fraud. In fact, they have pled a lengthy fraudulent scheme wherein Defendants pretended to act in good faith while intending to seize Plaintiffs’ real property for use in their own construction project. These allegations are sufficient to show entitlement to punitive damages at the pleading stage. Defendant’s point is well-taken that the exhibits to the complaint suggest Defendants engaged in dialogue with the Plaintiffs regarding the dispute. However, if Plaintiffs’ allegations are accepted as true – which they must be at this stage – all that discussion was an intentional tactic used to deter Plaintiffs from asserting their legal rights. If that is proven by clear and convincing evidence, Plaintiffs might obtain punitive damages.

 

The motion to strike is denied.

 

V. DISPOSITION

 

The demurrer to the tenth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

The demurrer to the third and ninth causes of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

The demurrer is otherwise overruled.

 

The motion to strike is denied.

RICHARD DUANE COLEMAN, JR v. LYNN JOSEPH DORSEY, II, et al. (24STCV28223)

 

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Pro per

Counsel for Plaintiff/ opposing party: N/A

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE/VACATE DEFAULT IS DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s motion to set aside default is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Clerk to provide notice.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On October 29, 2024, Plaintiff Richard Duane Coleman brought this action against Defendant Lynn Joseph Dorsey, II and Dorsey Logistics Inc, alleging causes of action for defamation – slander, defamation – libel, and defamation – pro se.

The complaint alleges that Plaintiff was hired by Defendant Dorsey Logistics as a commercial driver. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant went online on the Los Angeles Police Department website and created a false stolen vehicle police report that Plaintiff was terminated and advised to bring the truck he was driving back to Los Angeles California but refused to. Plaintiff alleges that he was charged with three felony charges: embezzlement, attempted extortion, and malicious injury to property.

On December 16, 2024, the clerk entered default against Defendants.

On December 19, 2024, Defendant Dorsey filed this Motion to set aside default. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 473.5(a) provides: “When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him¿or her¿in¿the¿action, he¿or she¿may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside¿the¿default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action.¿ The¿notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him¿or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him¿or her¿of a written notice that¿the¿default or default judgment has been entered.” (CCP § 473.5(a).)

 

“A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date¿prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that¿the¿party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his¿or her¿avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.”  (CCP § 473.5(b).)   

 

“‘[A]ctual notice’ in section 473.5 ‘means genuine knowledge of the party litigant . . . .’  [Citations.]  ‘[A]ctual knowledge’ has been strictly construed, with the aim of implementing the policy of liberally granting relief so that cases may be resolved on their merits.’  [Citation.]”  (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 547.)  “[A] party can make a motion showing a lack of actual notice not caused by avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect . . .”  (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 180.)  “‘[I]t does not require a showing that plaintiff did anything improper . . . . [T]he defaulting defendant simply asserts that he or she did not have actual notice’.”  (Id.) 

 

“[I]f the court has acquired jurisdiction, i.e., summons has been served, but service of summons has not resulted in actual notice to a defendant, although the defendant has acquired actual knowledge of the action from another source, this does not preclude a defendant from seeking relief under section 473.5.”  (Ellard, 94 Cal.App.4th at 548.)   

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves to set aside entry of default against him, arguing that he was not properly served with notice of this legal action.

 

There is no proof of service showing that the motion has been served on Plaintiff. Failure to give notice of a motion is not only a violation of the statutory requirements but of due process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005; Jones v. Otero (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 757.) As such, the motion is denied without prejudice on this basis.

 

Further, “[a] notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date¿prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that¿the¿party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his¿or her¿avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.”  (CCP § 473.5(b).)   Defendant has failed to accompany his motion with the required declaration. As such, the motion is also denied without prejudice on this basis.

 

Defendant also moves for sanctions and dismissal under CCP section 128.7, which states that a court may impose sanctions on a party or attorney that presents a pleading, petition, motion, or other similar papers in the following circumstances: 

 

1) the document is presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation. 

2) the claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are not warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law. 

3) the allegations and other factual contentions have no evidentiary support; 

4) the denials of factual contentions are not warranted on the evidence. 

 

CCP section 128.7 permits the Court to impose monetary sanctions on an attorney or an unrepresented party that violates any one of these requirements. (Eichenbaum v. Alon (2003) 106 Cal App 4th 967, 976.) 

 

Under section 128.7, a court may impose sanctions if it concludes a pleading was filed for an improper purpose or was indisputably without merit, either legally or factually. (Bucur v. Ahmad (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 175, 189–190.) However, “section 128.7 sanctions should be ‘made with restraint’ [Citation], and are not mandatory even if a claim is frivolous.” (Peake v. Underwood (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 428. at 448.) 

 

After notice and a hearing, a court may impose sanctions on an attorney, law firm, or party that violates Code Civ. Proc., section 128.7(b), subject to the “safe harbor” conditions specified in Code Civ. Proc., section 128.7(c). (Id.; Optimal Markets, Inc. v. Salant (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 912, 920.)

 

The court also cannot grant such relief at this time for the following various reasons. First, a motion for sanctions under Code Civ. Proc., section 128.7 must be made separately from other motions and “shall describe the specific conduct alleged to violate subdivision (b).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(c)(1).) Here, the motion is not made separately from the motion to set aside. Second, the moving party serves notice of the motion for sanctions on the offending party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(c)(1).) Here, there is no proof of service indicating Plaintiff was served with the motion. Next, service of the motion starts a safe harbor period during which the motion cannot be filed with the court. (Id.) If the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation or denial is not withdrawn or corrected within 21 days, the second step is to file or present the motion for sanctions to the court (i.e., presentment). (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(c)(2).)  If Plaintiff has not been served with the motion, then the safe harbor period has not been complied with. Lastly, Defendant has failed to support its arguments in the motion with any evidence.

 

The court notes that Defendant also seeks costs under CCP section 1033.5. However, that section allows for costs to the prevailing party under CCP section 1032. A prevailing party is entitled to recover costs, as a matter of right, in any action or proceeding absent a statute expressly noting otherwise. (Civ. Proc. Code § 1032, subd. (b).)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to set aside is DENIED without prejudice.
