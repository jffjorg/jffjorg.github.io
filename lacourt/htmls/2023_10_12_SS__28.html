DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 20STCV43776    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposing and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On November 13, 2020, Plaintiffs Christopher Thomas and Azariaah Thomas filed this action against Defendants Bashie Brianna Turner (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 for negligence. 

On March 16, 2021, Defendant filed an answer. 

On February 7, 2023, the Court continued the February 14, 2023 trial to June 22, 2023.  The Court ruled that discovery would remain cut off. 

On May 24, 2023, Plaintiff Christopher Thomas (“Plaintiff”) filed a motion to strike Defendant’s expert designation and exclude Defendant’s experts, to be heard on October 12, 2023. On September 28, 2023, Defendant filed an opposition. On October 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed a reply. 

On June 1, 2023, the Court continued the trial to November 30, 2023 and ruled that discovery would remain cut off except for the pending motion to strike Defendant’s expert designation. 

Trial is currently scheduled for November 30, 2023. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Plaintiff requests that the Court strike Defendant's expert designation and exclude Defendant’s experts. 

Defendant requests that the Court deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

“After the setting of the initial trial date for the action, any party may obtain discovery by demanding that all parties simultaneously exchange information concerning each other’s expert trial witnesses” to the extent specified by Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.210.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.210.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.260, subdivision (a), provides: 

“(a) All parties who have appeared in the action shall exchange information concerning expert witnesses in writing on or before the date of exchange specified in the demand. The exchange of information may occur at a meeting of the attorneys for the parties involved or by serving the information on the other party by any method specified in Section 1011 or 1013, on or before the date of exchange.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.260, subd. (a).) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.300 provides: 

“Except as provided in Section 2034.310 and in Articles 4 (commencing with Section 2034.610) and 5 (commencing with Section 2034.710), on objection of any party who has made a complete and timely compliance with Section 2034.260, the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness that is offered by any party who has unreasonably failed to do any of the following: 

“(a) List that witness as an expert under Section 2034.260. 

“(b) Submit an expert witness declaration. 

“(c) Produce reports and writings of expert witnesses under Section 2034.270. 

“(d) Make that expert available for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410).” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.300.) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 provides: 

“(a) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(b) In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following: 

“(1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery. 

“(2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier. 

“(3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party. 

“(4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action. 

“(c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to extend or to reopen discovery, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050.) 

DISCUSSION 

A.   Motion to strike Defendant’s expert designation 

1.    Plaintiff’s argument 

December 6, 2022 was the last day to serve a demand for exchange of expert witnesses.  December 27, 2022 was the final day to exchange expert witness information.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2034.220, 2016.060.) 

On November 22, 2022, using email, Defendant’s counsel served a demand for expert exchange with an exchange date of December 26, 2022. 

Plaintiff served an expert designation on December 29, 2022. In the email serving the designation, Plaintiff’s counsel explained that Plaintiff had not yet received a copy of Defendant’s expert designation. 

On March 24, 2023, Defendant’s counsel served supplemental discovery requests. Plaintiff’s counsel responded that discovery had been closed when the trial date was continued but Defendant’s counsel did not respond. 

On April 6, 2023, Defendant’s counsel served a demand for expert exchange. Plaintiff’s counsel responded that Plaintiff would not provide a new designation because discovery had been closed and Plaintiff had never received Defendant’s designation. Defendant’s counsel asserted that Defendant had timely disclosed on December 26, 2022.  Plaintiff’s counsel requested a copy of the designation.  Defendant’s counsel sent a copy of Defendant’s expert disclosure, which had a proof of service indicating service on December 26, 2022. 

Plaintiff’s counsel examined the metadata in Defendant’s expert disclosure and concluded that the document was created on January 23, 2023. On April 6, 2023, and April 11, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel requested a copy of the email in which Defendant’s counsel had originally served the designation. Defendant’s counsel has not provided this email. 

On May 3, 2023, Defendant’s counsel served a new expert designation. Plaintiff’s counsel again responded that discovery had been closed and was not reopened when the Court continued the trial. 

Plaintiff argues that the Court should strike Defendant’s expert designation and exclude Defendant’s experts because Defendant did not serve a timely expert designation.  Plaintiff relied on Defendant’s failure to serve a timely expert designation and has not had an opportunity to depose Defendant’s two untimely designated experts because discovery has been closed for months. 

2.    Defendant’s argument 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff may not complain of Defendant’s untimely designation because Plaintiff filed his designation two days late. 

“When the objecting party would have been entitled to mandatory exclusion of expert testimony under [Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.300] except for the objecting party’s own noncompliance with the exchange requirements, exclusion of the opponent’s expert testimony is discretionary, not mandatory.”  (R. Fairbank et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Trials and Evidence (Rutter 2022) ¶ 4:274.2, p. 4-71, citing Cottini v. Enloe Medical Center (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 401, 405 (Cottini); see Cottini, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 425 [“We read nothing in section 2034.300 to limit the inherent power of the trial court to exclude from evidence the testimony of Cottini's expert witnesses as a remedy for his unreasonable failure to comply with the expert witness exchange requirements prior to the cutoff date for expert discovery. The statute simply states that when certain conditions are met (i.e., the party seeking to call an expert witness has unreasonably failed to list the witness as an expert under § 2034.260, submit an expert witness declaration, produce reports and writings under § 2034.270, or make the expert available for a deposition), the appropriate remedy is exclusion of that expert's testimony. Where the objecting party has made a complete and timely compliance with section 2034.260, exclusion is mandatory. The statute does not state, as Cottini would have us hold, that where the objecting party's compliance with section 2034.260 is untimely, the trial court is powerless to exclude such testimony”].) 

Defendant relies on Staub v. Kiley (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1437 (Staub), where the Court of Appeal concluded that because defendants’ expert witness exchange demand was premature, they did not comply with the statutory exchange procedures and therefore lacked standing to seek to exclude the opposing party’s experts.  (Staub, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1445-1446.)  The court in Staub did not address whether – as Cottini held – trial courts have discretion apart from Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.300 to exclude expert testimony based on a party’s unreasonable failure to comply with the expert witness exchange requirements.  ““An opinion is not authority for propositions not considered.’”  (Cottini, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 424, quoting Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1182, 1195.) 

Defendant does not dispute Plaintiff’s argument that her expert witness designation was late.  Instead, she argues “there is no evidence of gamesmanship on the part of defendant” and Plaintiff has not shown prejudice because Plaintiff knows who Defendant’s experts are. 

But Defendant does not mention or attempt to refute Plaintiff’s assertion that an examination of the metadata in Defendant’s expert designation, which Plaintiff’s counsel received on April 6, 2023, showed that the document was created on January 23, 2023, almost one month after Defendant’s counsel claimed to have served it. When Plaintiff’s counsel received the document on April 6, 2023, it included a proof of service indicating service on December 26, 2022 and Defendants' counsel stated that Defendant had timely disclosed on December 26, 2022.  

3.    Analysis 

The Court exercises its discretion to strike Defendant’s expert designation and exclude Defendant’s experts from testifying at trial because Defendant’s unreasonable failure to serve a timely expert witness designation prevented Plaintiff from deposing Defendant’s experts before the discovery cut-off.  In addition, Defendant engaged in “gamesmanship” by attempting to mislead Plaintiff about when Defendant served the expert witness designation. 

II.      Motion to continue or reopen discovery 

The Court has considered the factors identified in Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 and finds good cause to re-open discovery for the purpose of allowing the Court to hear Plaintiff’s motion to strike Defendant’s expert designation and exclude Defendant’s late-designated experts. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Christopher Thomas’s motion to strike Defendant Bashie Brianna Turner’s expert designation and exclude Defendant’s experts from trial. 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Christopher Thomas’s motion to reopen discovery for the purpose of hearing the motion to strike. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 21STCV02405    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the documents submitted in support of a default judgment, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On January 21, 2021, Plaintiff Jacob Ali (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Anargyros Lazaridis (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 for motor vehicle tort, general negligence, violation of Vehicle Code section 20001, subdivision (a), and intentional infliction of emotional distress. 

On March 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a proof of service showing personal service on Defendant of the summons, complaint, statement of damages, and other documents on February 24, 2023. 

On April 17, 2023, the clerk entered Defendant’s default. 

On August 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed a request for Court judgment to be heard on October 12, 2023. 

PARTY’S REQUEST 

Plaintiff Jacob Ali requests that the Court enter a default judgment against Defendant Anargyros Lazaridis and award Plaintiff $76,184.94, consisting of $49,278.12 in special damages, $25,000.00 in general damages, $0.00 in prejudgment interest, $0.00 in attorney’s fees, and $1,906.82 in costs. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.      Default judgment 

“[With exceptions that do not apply here,] [a] party seeking a default judgment on declarations must use mandatory Request for Entry of Default (Application to Enter Default) (form CIV-100) . . . The following must be included in the documents filed with the clerk: 

“(1)  Except in unlawful detainer cases, a brief summary of the case identifying the parties and the nature of plaintiff's claim; 

“(2)  Declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; 

“(3)  Interest computations as necessary; 

“(4)  A memorandum of costs and disbursements; 

“(5)  A declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; 

“(6)  A proposed form of judgment; 

“(7)  A dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; 

“(8)  Exhibits as necessary; and 

“(9)  A request for attorney fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.” 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a).) 

B.       Damages 

          On a request for default judgment, “[w]here a cause of action is stated in the complaint, plaintiff merely needs to introduce evidence establishing a prima facie case for damages.”  (L. Edmon & C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2023) ¶ 5:213.1, p. 5-56 (Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure), citing Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361 [trial court erred in applying preponderance of the evidence standard].) 

          The relief granted to a plaintiff upon entry of a defendant's default cannot exceed the amount demanded in the complaint or, for personal injury cases where damages may not be stated in the complaint, the amount listed in the statement of damages. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 580, subd. (a), 585, subd. (b).) “The notice requirement of section 580 was designed to insure fundamental fairness.” (Becker v. S.P.V. Construction Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 489, 494 (Becker).) The statute insures that “defendants in cases which involve a default judgment have adequate notice of the judgments that may be taken against them. [Citation.] ‘If a judgment other than that which is demanded is taken against him, [the defendant] has been deprived of his day in court—a right to a hearing on the matter adjudicated.’ ’’ (Id. at p. 493.) A trial court exceeds its jurisdiction if it awards damages in excess of the amount specified in the complaint or statement of damages. (Id. at p. 494; see Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure, supra, ¶ 5:258, p. 5-70.) 

The court “shall hear the evidence offered by the plaintiff, and shall render judgment in the plaintiff’s favor for that relief, not exceeding the amount stated in the complaint, in the statement required by Section 425.11, or in the statement provided for by Section 425.115, as appears by the evidence to be just. If the taking of an account, or the proof of any fact, is necessary to enable the court to give judgment or to carry the judgment into effect, the court may take the account or hear the proof . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 585, subd. (b).) 

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff has not dismissed Does 1-50.  A default judgment application must include “[a] dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment . . . .” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(7).) 

Plaintiff’s evidence does not support the request for $49,278.12 in special damages. Plaintiff appears to be requesting special damages based on medical expenses. Plaintiff’s billing records list expenses of $45,691.12 and $710.00, which add up to $46,401.12, less than the amount of special damages requested. 

Plaintiff requests $477.50 for “investigation” fees.  Plaintiff should provide additional information about these fees so the Court can determine whether they are recoverable under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, which excludes “investigation expenses in preparing the case for trial.” 

For the reasons stated above, the Court denies the application. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES the application for default judgment filed on August 17, 2023. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 21STCV13947    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposing, reply, and supplemental papers, the Court rules as follows. 

PROCEDURAL HISTORY 

On April 13, 2021, Plaintiff Patricia Avila (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Chris Brown (“Brown”) and Does 1-50 for premises liability, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and strict liability. On October 25, 2021, Plaintiff amended the complaint to include Defendant Black Pyramid, LLC (“Black Pyramid”) as Doe 1. 

On February 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Brown, Black Pyramid, and Does 2-50 for premises liability, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. On May 23, 2022, Brown and Black Pyramid (“Defendants”) filed an answer. 

On June 13, 2022, the Court found that this case (21STCV13947) and case number 21STCV24811 are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 21STCV13947 became the lead case. The Court reminded the parties that related cases are still independent of each other.  The cases have not been consolidated. 

On July 31, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel third party witness Maria Avila (“Third Party”) to comply with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production and for sanctions of $2,256.65, to be heard on October 4, 2023.  On October 3, 2023, Third Party filed an opposition.  Also on October 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed an objection to Third Party’s opposition because it was untimely. 

On August 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Third Party’s appearance at a deposition and for sanctions of $2,904.65, to be heard on October 5, 2023.  On September 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a reply arguing Third Party’s opposition served on September 25, 2023 was untimely.  The Court, however, had not received Third Party’s opposition.  On October 5, 2023, Third Party filed an opposition (or, according to the document’s footer, “amended” opposition), which Third Party apparently had previously filed in the related case (21STCV24811). 

On October 4, 2023, the Court heard argument and continued the hearing on the motion to compel Third Party’s compliance with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production, as well as the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to compel Third Party’s appearance at a deposition (scheduled for October 5, 2023), to October 12, 2023. 

On October 9, 2023, Plaintiff's counsel filed a supplemental declaration. 

Trial is currently set for July 15, 2024. 

PLAINTIFF’S AND THIRD PARTY'S REQUESTS 

Plaintiff requests that the Court order Third Party to produce all documents requested in Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records and document production.  Plaintiff also requests that the Court order Third Party to appear for a deposition.  Plaintiff seeks sanctions on both motions.

Third Party asks the Court to deny the motions and impose sanctions on Plaintiff. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.010.)  A deposition subpoena may command the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.020.) The Court may order a third party to comply with a deposition subpoena upon any terms or condition that the court declares. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1.) 

          Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480 provides in part: 

“(a) If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production. 

“(b) This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(c) Notice of this motion shall be given to all parties and to the deponent either orally at the examination, or by subsequent service in writing. If the notice of the motion is given orally, the deposition officer shall direct the deponent to attend a session of the court at the time specified in the notice. 

* * *

  “(i) If the court determines that the answer or production sought is subject to discovery, it shall order that the answer be given or the production be made on the resumption of the deposition. 

“(j) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel an answer or production, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subds. (a), (b), (c), (i), (j).) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a), provides that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivision (d), provides that misuse of the discovery process includes “making . . . unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or to limit discovery.” 

DISCUSSION 

I.               Motion to compel compliance with deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production 

A.   Plaintiff properly served the motion on Third Party’s counsel 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1346, provides: “A written notice and all moving papers supporting a motion to compel an answer to a deposition question or to compel production of a document or tangible thing from a nonparty deponent must be personally served on the nonparty deponent unless the nonparty deponent agrees to accept service by mail or electronic service at an address or electronic service address specified on the deposition record.” 

Plaintiff served her motion on Third Party’s counsel, not on Third Party personally.  As an attachment to her counsel’s declaration, Plaintiff provided an email from Third Party’s counsel to Plaintiff’s counsel dated July 7, 2023 stating: “[Third Party] is a party to a related action and you are NOT allowed to have any contact with her. All communication must be through me or my office. Moving forward please do NOT have any contact with my client, whether through correspondence or process servers.”  (Murphy Dec. Exh. 5.)  Based on this directive, Plaintiff's service of the motion on Third Party’s counsel was proper under rule 3.1346. 

B.   Third Party is required to comply with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena 

1.    Background 

On June 21, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel issued a deposition subpoena for production of business records to Third Party with a production deadline of July 21, 2023.  On June 29, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel’s attorney service served the deposition subpoena on Third Party at her home.  On July 5, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel served a copy of the deposition subpoena for production of business records and a deposition notice to take Third Party’s deposition on August 3, 2023, on Third Party's counsel's office. 

On July 7, 2023, Third Party’s counsel wrote to Plaintiff’s counsel stating that Third Party was “not available for deposition so it will have to be rescheduled.”  Third Party’s counsel added: “I would prefer not to put [Third Party] through the trauma of a deposition and relieve [sic] this nightmare, and her testimony will be detrimental to your client's case. Please withdraw the deposition notice immediately.” (Murphy Dec. Exh. 5.) 

Plaintiff’s counsel responded that he would not withdraw the subpoena and he requested alternative dates for the deposition. (Murphy Dec. Exh. 5.)  Third Party’s counsel emailed back: “My client will not appear for deposition on the date you have chosen, as I am not available. I am urging to reconsider your position, her testimony will not be favorable to your client and you will confirm your client has no damages, why would you do this? My client has been through enough. Please withdraw the request to depose her. In either event we are not available on the date you chose. I will file a motion for protective order to preclude her deposition but prefer to meet and confer with you further in the hopes of securing your cooperation.” (Murphy Dec. Exh. 5.) 

Plaintiff’s counsel again asked for alternative dates for Third Party’s deposition.  Counsel warned that, unless he received alternative dates, the deposition would go forward and counsel would take a non-appearance and file a motion to compel. 

On July 21, 2023, the deadline for the document production, Third Party did not produce any documents. 

On July 25, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel told Third Party’s counsel: “I’ll give your client until this Friday, July 28, 2023, to produce her documents in response to our subpoena and then we’ll be filing motions to compel and seeking sanctions against your office.”  (Exh. 8.) 

On July 27, 2023, Third Party served objections to the August 3, 2023 deposition stating Third Party and her counsel were not available that day.  (Murphy Dec. ¶ 15 & Exh. 9.)  The objection stated: “[Third Party’s] counsel is willing to meet and confer with Plaintiff Patricia Avila’s counsel. [Third Party] will be produced for a remote deposition on a mutually agreed upon date and time. There is NO refusal to proceed with deposition. There is NO refusal to produce [Third Party] for deposition.”  (Exh. 9.) 

On August 3, 2023, Plaintiff took a certificate of non-appearance. 

Third Party has not provided an alternative date for the deposition and has not produced the documents requested in the deposition subpoena. (Murphy Dec. ¶ 15.)  Third Party’s counsel has not cooperated with Plaintiff’s counsel’s efforts to schedule a new deposition date and obtain the requested documents. 

2.   Analysis 

In her complaint, Plaintiff alleges that she perceived Third Party – Plaintiff’s sister – being attacked and injured by a dog at Defendants’ home, where Plaintiff and Third Party worked. 

Third Party filed a separate lawsuit against Defendants, which remains pending.  Third Party’s lawsuit, however, does not relieve Third Party of her obligation to comply with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena and request for production of documents. 

Third Party argues the Court should deny Plaintiff’s motion because Plaintiff failed to meet and confer before filing the motion.  Based on the email correspondence attached to the motion, the Court finds that Plaintiff's counsel repeatedly attempted to meet and confer with Third Party’s counsel, without success. 

Third Party asserts that Plaintiff should have scheduled an informal discovery conference (IDC) before filing the motion.  The Court requires an IDC before a hearing on a motion to compel further discovery responses.  An IDC was not required here. 

The Court grants the motion to compel Third Party to comply with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production.  Third Party is ordered to provide code-compliant responses to Plaintiff's requests for production numbers 1-21, 24-38, and 40-47 and to produce the documents, electronically stored information, and/or other things requested by within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions of $2,256.65 based on five hours of attorney time to prepare the moving papers and one hour to prepare for and attend the hearing at a rate of $350 per hour, $15.00 to e-file the motion, a filing fee of $61.65, and $80.00 to have paper copies delivered to Department 28. 

The Court grants $561.65 in sanctions based on two hours of attorney time at a reasonable rate of $250 per hour and one filing fee. 

II.            Motion to compel Third Party’s appearance at a deposition 

The Court has already addressed most of the issues raised by this motion in its discussion, above, of the motion to compel compliance with the deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production. 

In addition to the issues addressed above, Third Party argues that “this is not a third party witness situation” because the Court has related this case (21STCV13947) with the case Third Party brought against Defendants (21STCV24811).  Therefore, Third Party argues, Plaintiff was required to notice her deposition and, if Third Party failed to appear, file a motion to compel. 

Third Party’s premise is incorrect.  When the Court related the cases, it reminded the parties that related cases are still independent of each other.  Plaintiff was entitled, and required, to follow the rules that apply to third-party witnesses.  In addition, Plaintiff has provided evidence that her counsel served a copy of the deposition subpoena and deposition notice on Third Party’s counsel on July 5, 2023. 

The Court grants the motion and orders Third Party to appear for a deposition within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

Plaintiff requests sanctions of $2,904.65 based on four hours of attorney time to prepare the moving papers and one hour to prepare for and attend the hearing at a rate of $350 per hour, $15.00 to e-file the motion, a filing fee of $61.65, $80.00 to have paper copies delivered to Department 28, and $498 in court reporter’s fees for the certificate of non-appearance.  Plaintiff also requests that Third Party’s counsel pay $500.00 to Plaintiff's counsel "due to the failure to appear.” 

It is unclear why Plaintiff proceeded with the August 3, 2023 deposition after Third Party served an objection.  Therefore, the Court will not include the court reporter's fee in the sanctions award.  The Court grants $561.65 in sanctions based on two hours of attorney time at a reasonable rate of $250 per hour and one filing fee. 

          The Court denies Third Party’s request for sanctions. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Patricia Avila’s motion to compel Third Party witness Maria Avila’s compliance with Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records and remaining document production and for sanctions.  Third Party Maria Avila is ordered to produce code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s requests for production numbers 1-21, 24-38, and 40-47 and to produce the documents, electronically stored information, and/or other things requested within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Patricia Avila’s motion to compel Third Party witness Maria Avila’s appearance at a deposition and orders Third Party Maria Avila to appear for a deposition within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Patricia Avila’s requests for sanctions and orders Third Party Maria Avila and her attorney to pay Plaintiff $1,123,30 ($561.65 for each motion) within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

The Court DENIES Third Party witness Maria Avila’s request for sanctions. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 22STCV14035    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the documents submitted in support of default judgments, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On April 27, 2022, Plaintiff Douglas Rhee, Jr. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Hilarion Candido Varela (“Varela”), Steve Song (“Song”), and Does 1-50 for motor vehicle and general negligence. 

On March 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed proofs of service showing personal service of a statement of damages on Varela on November 17, 2022, and personal service of a statement of damages on Song on November 12, 2022. 

On April 12, 2023, the clerk entered Varela’s default.  

On May 5 and June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed additional proofs of personal service on Song of a statement of damages on November 12, 2022. 

On June 14, 2023, the clerk entered Song’s default.  

On September 5, 2023, the Court dismissed the Doe defendants without prejudice at Plaintiff’s request. 

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff submitted a request for Court judgment against Varela to be heard on October 12, 2023. On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff submitted a request for Court judgment against Song to be heard on October 12, 2023. 

PARTY'S REQUESTS 

Plaintiff Douglas Rhee, Jr. requests that the Court enter default judgment against Defendant Hilarion Candido Varela and award Plaintiff $9,156,360.92, consisting of $2,150,000.00 in special damages, $7,000,000.00 in general damages, $0.00 in prejudgment interest, $0.00 in attorney’s fees, and $6,360.92 in costs. 

Plaintiff Douglas Rhee, Jr. requests that the Court enter default judgment against Defendant Steve Song and award Plaintiff $9,156,360.92, consisting of $2,150,000.00 in special damages, $7,000,000.00 in general damages, $0.00 in prejudgment interest, $0.00 in attorney’s fees, and $6,360.92 in costs. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.      Default judgment 

“[With exceptions that do not apply here,] [a] party seeking a default judgment on declarations must use mandatory Request for Entry of Default (Application to Enter Default) (form CIV-100) . . . The following must be included in the documents filed with the clerk: 

“(1)  Except in unlawful detainer cases, a brief summary of the case identifying the parties and the nature of plaintiff's claim; 

“(2)  Declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; 

“(3)  Interest computations as necessary; 

“(4)  A memorandum of costs and disbursements; 

“(5)  A declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; 

“(6)  A proposed form of judgment; 

“(7)  A dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; 

“(8)  Exhibits as necessary; and 

“(9)  A request for attorney fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.” 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a).) 

B.       Damages 

          On a request for default judgment, “[w]here a cause of action is stated in the complaint, plaintiff merely needs to introduce evidence establishing a prima facie case for damages.”  (L. Edmon & C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2023) ¶ 5:213.1, p. 5-56 (Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure), citing Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361 [trial court erred in applying preponderance of the evidence standard].) 

          The relief granted to a plaintiff upon entry of a defendant's default cannot exceed the amount demanded in the complaint or, for personal injury cases where damages may not be stated in the complaint, the amount listed in the statement of damages. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 580, subd. (a), 585, subd. (b).) “The notice requirement of section 580 was designed to insure fundamental fairness.” (Becker v. S.P.V. Construction Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 489, 494 (Becker).) The statute insures that “defendants in cases which involve a default judgment have adequate notice of the judgments that may be taken against them. [Citation.] ‘If a judgment other than that which is demanded is taken against him, [the defendant] has been deprived of his day in court—a right to a hearing on the matter adjudicated.’ ’’ (Id. at p. 493.) A trial court exceeds its jurisdiction if it awards damages in excess of the amount specified in the complaint or statement of damages. (Id. at p. 494; see Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure, supra, ¶ 5:258, p. 5-70.) 

The court “shall hear the evidence offered by the plaintiff, and shall render judgment in the plaintiff’s favor for that relief, not exceeding the amount stated in the complaint, in the statement required by Section 425.11, or in the statement provided for by Section 425.115, as appears by the evidence to be just. If the taking of an account, or the proof of any fact, is necessary to enable the court to give judgment or to carry the judgment into effect, the court may take the account or hear the proof . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 585, subd. (b).) 

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff’s declarations do not support the amount of special damages Plaintiff is requesting.  Plaintiff states that he has incurred approximately $600,782.50 in medical expenses to date, but Plaintiff does not address, or provide evidence to support, his request for additional awards of $500,000 for future medical expenses and $1,000,000 for future lost earnings from each defendant. 

Plaintiff’s declaration also provides insufficient information to support his request for $7,000,000 in general damages from each defendant.  Plaintiff suffered serious injuries in the accident and was hospitalized for several weeks.  The attached medical records also show that Plaintiff has experienced mental or emotional symptoms which he attributes to the accident.  Plaintiff is clearly entitled to some amount of general damages.  However, Plaintiff has not provided evidence that justifies an award of $7,000,000 in general damages from each defendant. 

In addition, it appears that Plaintiff is seeking the same damages from each of the two defendants.  The Court will not order two defendants to pay duplicate damage awards.  Plaintiff may wish to consider filing a single default judgment application with a request that the two defendants be found jointly and severally responsible for the damages.

Last, the applications do not contain evidence supporting the request for $5,631 in process server fees for each defendant. 

For the reasons stated above, the Court denies the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES the applications for default judgment filed by Plaintiff Douglas Rhee, Jr. on August 29, 2023, and September 15, 2023. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 22STCV15514    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposing and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On May 10, 2022, Plaintiff Jeffrey Lawrence Newberry (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Felipe Jesus Vazquez (“Vazquez”), HCE LLC, and Does 1-100 for negligence. 

On July 21, 2022, Defendants Vasquez and HCE LLC (“Defendants”) filed an answer. 

On August 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant Felipe Jesus Vazquez’s deposition and for sanctions, to be heard on October 12, 2023. On September 28, 2023, Defendants filed an opposition. On October 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed a reply. 

On August 14, 2023, Defendants filed a motion for a protective order terminating or limiting Vazquez’s deposition and for sanctions, to be heard on November 15, 2023.  On September 11, 2023, Defendants filed a revised motion. 

On September 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion for sanctions for alleged discovery abuse by Vazquez and for sanctions, to be heard on November 13, 2023. 

Trial is currently scheduled for April 19, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Plaintiff requests that the Court compel Vazquez to appear for a deposition and provide further responses to questions. Plaintiff also requests that the Court impose sanctions. 

Defendants request that the Court deny the motion and impose sanctions on Plaintiff. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action … without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) 

A motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following: "(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b).)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (g)(1) provides that if a motion made under 2025.450 is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. 

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).) 

Misuse of the discovery process includes "[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery" and "[m]aking or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or to limit discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d), (h).) 

DISCUSSION  

The Court continues the hearing on this motion to November 15, 2023, so it can be heard at the same time as Defendants’ motion for a protective order terminating or limiting Vazquez’s deposition and for sanctions.  In addition, the Court advances the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions, filed on September 21, 2023, to today and continues the hearing to November 15, 2023. 

The issues raised by these three motions appear to be related.  The Court will address them together. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court CONTINUES the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant Felipe Jesus Vazquez’s deposition and for sanctions to November 15, 2023 at 1:30 p.m. in Department 28 of the Spring Street Courthouse. 

The Court advances the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions filed on September 21, 2023, to today and continues the hearing to November 15, 2023 at 1:30 p.m. in Department 28 of the Spring Street Courthouse. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 22STCV22838    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On July 14, 2022, Plaintiff Rocko Moore (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles Department of Water and Power, Crowley Lake Fish Camp, Inc. (“Crowley”), and Sierra Recreation Associates, Inc. (“Sierra”) for dangerous condition of public property, government liability for acts and omissions of independent contractors, breach of mandatory duty, negligence, and premises liability. Plaintiff later amended the complaint to include Defendant Mono County. 

On January 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint, adding causes of action for assault, battery and parental liability. The First Amended Complaint also added Defendants Albert Cano, Marc Cano, and Jennifer Ziegler. Plaintiff removed Defendant Mono County from the complaint.  On September 29, 2023, Sierra and Crowley filed an answer. 

On September 29, 2023, Defendants and Cross-Complainants Crowley and Sierra filed a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants Roes 1-50 for indemnification, apportionment of fault, equitable implied indemnity, full indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief. 

On September 13, 2023, Crowley and Sierra (“Moving Defendants”) filed a motion to continue the trial to be heard on October 12, 2023.  No opposition has been filed. 

Trial is currently scheduled for January 11, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Moving Defendants request that the Court continue trial to allow the Court to hear a motion for summary judgment prior to trial. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(b), provides that “a party seeking a continuance of the date set for trial, whether contested or uncontested or stipulated to by the parties, must make the request for a continuance by a noticed motion or an ex parte application under the rules in chapter 4 of this division, with supporting declarations. The party must make the motion or application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.” 

          Under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c), the Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance. Circumstances that may indicate good cause include “a party's excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts,” or the unavailability of a party, counsel, or expert due to death, illness or other excusable circumstance. The Court should consider all facts and circumstances relevant to the determination, such as proximity of the trial date, prior continuances, prejudice suffered, whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance, and whether the interests of justice are served. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(d).) 

DISCUSSION 

Moving Defendants ask the Court to continue the trial until after the hearing on their summary judgment motion.  Moving Defendants have reserved a hearing date in June 2024 for a summary judgment motion but they have not yet filed a motion. 

Moving Defendants also request a trial continuance because discovery has not been completed.  

The Court has not granted any previous continuances. In addition, some of the defendants have not appeared.  

The Court finds good cause and grants the request for a continuance. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS the motion to continue the trial filed by Defendants Crowley Lake Fish Camp, Inc. and Sierra Recreation Associates, Inc.. Trial is continued to August 12, 2024, at 1:30 p.m. in Department 28 of the Spring Street Courthouse. The Final Status Conference is July 29, 2024, at 10:00 a.m. in Department 28 of the Spring Street Courthouse. All discovery and related dates are set to trail the new trial date. 

Moving parties are ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving parties are ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.
