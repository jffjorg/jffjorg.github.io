DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV35575    Hearing Date: April 17, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

OSCAR AUSTEN REYES,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

21STCV35575

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

LA SMILES DENTISTRY, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 17, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S. and Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S, Inc.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication          

The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  The motion for summary adjudication is MOOT in light of the ruling on the motion for summary judgment.  Defendants are ordered to lodge a proposed judgment.

BACKGROUND

On September 28, 2021, plaintiff Oscar Austen Reyes (self-represented) filed a complaint against LA Smiles Dentistry, All in One Dental, Moshe Ezidi, Pedram Sooferi, and Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S., Inc. for general negligence.

On November 12, 2021, plaintiff filed a FAC.

On February 23, 2022, the court sustained a demurrer to the FAC.

On December 27, 2022, plaintiff filed a SAC for (1) breach of contract, (2) medical negligence, and (3) bad faith.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” Avivi, 159 Cal. App. 4th at 467; CCP §437c(c).

Discussion

Defendants Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S and Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S., Inc. request that the court enter summary judgment in their favor and against plaintiff pursuant to CCP §437c on the ground that there is no triable issue of material fact and defendants are entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  In the alternative, defendants request summary adjudication as to each of the causes of action on the ground that they have no merit.

In the SAC, plaintiff alleges that on August 15, 2018, plaintiff initially sought treatment for severe tooth pain at defendants for an evaluation and consultation with defendant Sooferi and Moshe Ezidi, D.D.S.  Plaintiff explained the painful symptoms of tooth #25.  Sooferi diagnosed that #25 should be extracted at the earliest possible time.  They advised plaintiff that they would be putting a flipper apparatus (“flipper”), temporary tooth, in place of #25.  Plaintiff explained that he had a flipper before and that it was uncomfortable.  Plaintiff asked if a bonded tooth could be put in instead.  They told plaintiff that a bonded tooth was not possible. Plaintiff requested that they fill the toothless space during the healing process for comfortability, which they explained was not possible.  A mold was taken to create a flipper and an appointment for the extraction of #25 was scheduled for August 29, 2018.  SAC, ¶10.  On August 29, 2018, while under local anesthesia, plaintiff was prepped and awaiting surgery, when Sooferi decided to change the previously agreed upon treatment plan, and advised plaintiff that he should extract tooth #11, which would help additionally with the pain in #25.  Id., ¶11.

The SAC further alleges that on September 1, 2018, #25 continued to cause plaintiff pain, and Sooferi recommended the extraction of #6.  On October 24, 2018, #6 was extracted.  On June 26, 2019, amalgam is removed from #18.  Pain in the area continued.  Plaintiff visited over the next few months for fluoride treatments on #18 as the cure for the persistent pain.  On June 28, 2019, #6 and #11 implants caused complications, which led to severe pain and swelling.  On July 3, 2019, Sooferi sent text message that the #25 and #26 should be removed as soon as possible.  On July 10, 2019, a temporary filing was implanted.  On July 19, 2019, plaintiff continued complaints regarding #25.  On July 25, 2019, Ezidi discussed the situation in depth with plaintiff and offered the option to extract only #25, which he extracted, using mold and flipper from August 15, 2018.  On July 30, 2019, fluoride treatments continued on #18.  Plaintiff incurred additional flipper adjustments due to sharp edges on the flipper.  It was an uncomfortable fit and could come out of place.  Id., ¶12. 

The SAC further alleges that at an appointment on September 11, 2019, plaintiff informed Sooferi that he was leaving for Arizona to visit family and that he “pleaded with Sooferi” to repair the flipper because it was not fitting correctly; he demonstrated the looseness of the flipper and how easily it popped out of place and pointed out the sharp edges.  Sooferi replied that he was too busy to fix the flipper and told plaintiff to call the office to schedule an appointment.  Id.  On September 22, 2019, while taking vitamins, plaintiff unknowingly swallowed the flipper and had trouble breathing.  He went to an urgent care.  He had various appointments with doctors on September 24, 25, and 26 and October 3, 2019.  At an appointment with Dr. Goldman at Southern California Dental, Dr. Goldman bonded #25.  On November 11, 2019, plaintiff received a second opinion as to #6, 11, 18, 25, and 26 and another evaluation on November 22, 2019.  On November 4, 2019, plaintiff had an appointment to discuss flipper swallowing with defendants.  He discussed several items with Ezidi, including swallowing the flipper and hoarse voice, past procedures by defendants, and upcoming appointments.  Id., ¶16.  Plaintiff showed Ezidi the bonded tooth and declined all offers to continue service on the implants or any future procedures.  Id., ¶17.

Statute of limitations

Defendants argues that the SAC is barred by the statute of limitations under CCP §340.5, which states a three-year period after the date of injury “or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.”  Defendants assert that the allegedly tortious conduct took place during plaintiff’s dental treatment with moving defendants from August 18, 2018 to September 11, 2019, but the complaint was not filed until September 28, 2021, almost two years after the alleged conduct occurred.  Defendants point out that plaintiff alleges that on November 4, 2019, plaintiff went to defendants to discuss swallowing his stay plate, and that they discussed possible settlement and that Dr. Ezidi requested a more detailed report to determine “exact settlement figures.”  See SAC, ¶18.  Plaintiff also alleged and testified at his deposition that he sought second opinions, wherein he was informed that his implants on #6 and #11 were improperly placed by Dr. Sooferi.  Plaintiff sought a third opinion on November 22, 2019 from an oral surgeon who informed plaintiff that #18 had been misdiagnosed.  See Katerina Shaller decl., Exh. D (deposition transcript).

Thus, defendants argue, “there is no question Plaintiff was suspicious of and concluded in his mind there was negligence or wrongdoing by Dr. Sooferi and his corporation in connection with his alleged injuries by, at the latest, November 22, 2019” and that he was on inquiry notice.  Defendants assert that with the addition of the tolling period provided by Amended Emergency Rule 9, plaintiff had until May 22, 2021 to file a complaint.  

The court finds that the complaint is barred by the statute of limitations for medical malpractice actions.

 

First cause of action for breach of contract

The SAC alleges that plaintiff sought the professional services of defendants on August 15, 2018.  Defendants maintained their dental office and held themselves out to the general public as competent and skilled dentists and dental surgeons.  Plaintiff relied upon said representations of skill and competency when retaining defendants to examine, advise, and treat him.  SAC, ¶22.  As a proximate result of the breaches of the agreement by defendants, plaintiff was injured.  Id., ¶23.

Defendants argue that this cause of action is based on professional negligence, and that “[a]n action against a doctor arising out of his negligent treatment of a patient is an action sounding in tort and not one based upon a contract.”  Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal. App. 3d 614, 625.  Defendants present evidence that during his deposition, plaintiff admitted that this claim was based upon a contract to render dental services provided by Dr. Sooferi but did not meet the standard of care.  Plaintiff testified that this claim was based upon “contracted services which would have been the providing of the dental flipper” and the implants on #6 and #11, and upon not adjusting his stay plate.  Defendants also contend that plaintiff did not provide any evidence of an express contract.  See Katerina Shaller decl., Exh. D (deposition transcript).

The court finds that defendants have met their burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements for breach of contract, including the existence of an express contract.  The burden shifts to plaintiff who did not file an opposition.  Therefore, plaintiff has not met his burden to show a triable issue of material fact.

 

Second cause of action for gross medical negligence

The SAC alleges that defendants negligently failed to exercise the proper degree of knowledge, skill, and competence in examining, diagnosing, treating, and caring for plaintiff by incompetently and negligently:  changing agreed upon treatment plan; extraction of tooth #6 and #11; amalgam, fluoride treatments and misdiagnosis of #18; extraction of #25; and poorly designed flipper.  SAC, ¶28.

The elements of medical malpractice are:  “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”  Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic (1989) 212 Cal. App. 3d 696, 701-02 (citations omitted).  “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”  Avivi, 159 Cal. App. 4th at 467.

Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”  Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal. App. 3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).  An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.  Starr v. Mooslin (1971) 14 Cal. App. 3d 988, 999.

Defendants argue that they complied with the standard of care and did not cause plaintiff’s alleged injuries. Defendants present the declaration of Alan Shelhamer, D.D.S. who opines that based on is review of plaintiff’s records, as well as his education, training, and experience, Dr. Sooferi and his corporation Pedram Sooferi, D.D.S., Inc. at all times complied with the standard of care in the care and treatment of plaintiff.  Alan Shelhamer, D.D.S. decl., ¶7.  Dr. Sooferi’s treatment planning was appropriate and carried out in compliance with the standard of care.  The treatment plan was not changed; the only modification was in the order in which treatment occurred.  Id., ¶7.a.  The extraction of teeth #6, #11, and #25 were indicated and were performed in a manner compliant with the standard of care.  The records and imaging did not show any complications with the extractions and the areas healed appropriately.  Id., ¶7.b.  Dr. Sooferi’s treatment on tooth #18 was appropriate and within the standard of care.  Placement of a crown was an acceptable course of treatment.  A temporary crown breaking does not mean there was a violation of the standard of care.  Further, fluoride treatments were appropriate and conservative rather than risking the extraction.  Id., ¶7.c.  Dr. Sooferi’s decision to utilize a stay plate rather than a bonded tooth was appropriate and within the standard of care.  Plaintiff had suspected pathology on tooth #25 along with his adjacent teeth which created a concern regarding the stability of those teeth.  A bonded tooth creates hygiene concerns and there was radiographic evidence of mandibular hardware consistent with a previous procedure, which the patient failed to disclose.  Id., ¶7.d.  The stay plate was adjusted appropriately and delivered within the standard of care.  A stay plate is designed for cosmesis and easy removal, not heavy function.  If a stay plate is too tight it can result in pressure necrosis.  Id., ¶7.e.  The placement of dental implants on #6 and #11 met the standard of care.  Dr. Sooferi waited the appropriate amount of time after extractions and bone grafts on the sites to place the implants.  Id., ¶7.g.

Dr. Shelhamer further opines that to a reasonable degree of medical probability, no negligent act or omission on behalf of Dr. Sooferi or his corporation caused or significantly contributed to plaintiff’s claimed injuries or damages.  Specifically, nothing they did or failed to do caused or contributed to the alleged damages plaintiff is claiming.  Id., ¶8.

The court finds that defendants have met their burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements of medical negligence, including breach of standard of care and causation.  The burden shifts to plaintiff who did not file an opposition.  Therefore, plaintiff has not met his burden to show a triable issue of material fact.

 

Third cause of action for bad faith

The SAC alleges the same as the second causes of action.

Defendants argue that this claim is an exact recitation of the second cause of action and that plaintiff’s deposition testimony shows that it is based on professional negligence.  Defendants assert that this claim fails for the same reasons as the second cause of action.

The court finds that defendants have met their burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements of medical negligence, which is what this purported causes of action is based, including breach of standard of care and causation.  The burden shifts to plaintiff who did not file an opposition.  Therefore, plaintiff has not met his burden to show a triable issue of material fact.

The motion for summary judgment is therefore GRANTED.  The motion for summary adjudication is MOOT in light of the ruling on the motion for summary judgment.

 

ORDER

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

The motion for summary adjudication is MOOT in light of the ruling on the motion for summary judgment.

Defendants are ordered to lodge a proposed judgment.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.
