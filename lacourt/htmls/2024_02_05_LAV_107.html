DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02454    Hearing Date: February 5, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

THOVMAS GABRIELIAN,

Plaintiff.

 

v.

 

MERCEDES-BENZ U.S.A., LLC

Defendant.

 

   Case Number             Department

22VECV02454                                   107

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

[TENTATIVE RULING:]

 

The Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the first cause of action (breach of express warranty) is GRANTED with 15 days’ leave to amend.

 

The Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the second cause of action (breach of implied warranty) is DENIED.

 

 

Background

 

This case is a lemon law action filed by Plaintiff Thovmas Gabrielian against Defendant Mercedes-Benz U.S.A., LLC (“MBUSA”). On April 27, 2021, Plaintiff leased a 2021 Mercedes-Benz GLE63 (the “Subject Vehicle”) manufactured by MBUSA pursuant to a motor vehicle lease agreement (the “MVLA”).  The MVLA identifies Plaintiff as the Subject Vehicle’s lessee and Keyes European as the lessor.

 

Plaintiff pleads those defects and non-conformities to warranty manifested themselves in the Subject Vehicle during the applicable warranty period, including malfunctions relating to a defective battery, the check engine light coming on, and a malfunctioning sunroof.  Plaintiff pleads he delivered the Subject Vehicle to Keyes European for repairs, but Keyes European was unable to conform the Subject Vehicle to the applicable express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 8-13.)

 

On December 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed his complaint against MBUSA, alleging breach of express and implied warranties under the Song-Beverly Act. On March 15, 2018, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On November 30, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition.

 

On January 29, 2024, Defendant filed its reply.

 

Motion

 

Defendant argues that the entire Complaint is uncertain, vague, and ambiguous because the Complaint states conclusory language such as the vehicle “exhibited defects, nonconformities, maladjustments, or malfunctions relating to [sic] defective battery; check engine light coming on; noise in vehicle; and malfunction sunroof.” (Mot. for Judg. on the Pleadings p.3; Complaint ¶ 10 (a).) Moreover, Defendant provides other instances of the conclusory allegations and fails to state facts sufficient for a cause of action. (Not. for Mot. p.1; Mot. for Judg. on the Pleadings p.1-2.) Lastly, Defendant requests that the leave to amend be denied. (Mot. for Judg. on the Pleadings p.4.)

 

Opposition

 

Plaintiff argues that the pleadings adequately pled that Defendant failed to replace or repurchase after a reasonable number of repair attempts which is the only required pleading for a Song-Beverly cause of action. (Opp. p.4.) Plaintiff argues that this motion raises issues that Defendant waived by failing to file a special demurrer because Defendant waited a year to raise the issue and no more specificity or particularity is needed. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff further states that precedent case law for this type of cause of action was pled in the same way because a consumer does not need to know the cause of the defect. (Id. at p.5.) However, Plaintiff failed to provide any case law to support this statement. Plaintiff also argues that the Court cannot consider extrinsic evidence in granting this motion because it is not a motion for summary judgment. (Id. at p.6-8.)

 

Reply

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly claims fail as a matter of law because they are inadequately pled. (Reply p.1.) Specifically, Defendant states that Plaintiff fails to provide when the subject vehicle was taken to the authorized dealership for repairs, the alleged issues, how MBUSA failed to conform with the express warranty, and which service visits was the subject vehicle allegedly in repair for 30 days or more (if any). (Id.)

 

Additionally, Defendant argues that it did not waive any of its rights to challenge Plaintiff’s claims because of California Code of Civil Procedure §430.80 (a) and §438. (Id. at p.1-2.) Specifically, these codes of civil procedure, provide relief to Defendants when a pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Id. at 2-3.) Defendant also cites to its July 27, 2023, Answer which contained the affirmative defense regarding the Complaint having insufficient facts to state a cause of action. (Reply p.3.)

 

Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 438 states, in relevant part: “(b)(1) A party may move for judgment on the pleadings. . . . (c)(1) The motion provided for in this section may only be made on one of the following grounds: . . . . (B) If the moving party is a defendant, that either of the following conditions exist: (i) The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint. (ii) The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (emphasis added.)

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings “has the purpose and effect of a general demurrer.” (Smiley v. Citibank (South Dakota), N.A. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 138, 146 (citation omitted).) “[T]he trial court generally confines itself to the complaint and accepts as true all material facts alleged therein.  As appropriate, however, it may extend its consideration to matters that are subject to judicial notice.  In this, it performs essentially the same task that it would undertake in ruling on a general demurrer.”  (Id. (citations omitted).) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party moving for judgment on the pleadings must meet and confer in person or telephonically with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to determine if an agreement can be reached regarding the claims raised in the motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 439, subd. (a).)  The moving party must file a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 439, subd. (a)(3).) 

 

Here, the parties met and conferred via email and were unable to resolve the issues. (Dec. Cowden ¶ 2.) Thus, the meet and confer requirement was satisfied.

 

Discussion

 

Waiver of Issues

 

            Plaintiff’s argument that Defendant waived its rights to raise these issues in a motion for judgment on the pleadings because it did not file a special demurrer, is immaterial because California Code of Civil Procedure § 438 allows this motion for a failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Here, Defendant is seeking the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings because it claims that the Complaint in its entirety fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action. Therefore, the Court will analyze the motion on its merits below.

 

Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

 

Defendant moves for a judgment on the pleadings because the entire Complaint is based on uncertainty, vagueness, and ambiguity. (Mot. for Judg. on Pleadings p.2.) Defendant also argues that Plaintiff failed to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action because of conclusory allegations. (Id.) The Complaint alleges (1) breach of express warranty under the Song-Beverly Act and (2) breach of implied warranty obligations under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

A. First Cause of Action – Breach of Express Warranty

 

A plaintiff pursuing an action under the Act has the burden to prove that (1) the vehicle had a nonconformity covered by the express warranty that substantially impaired the use, value or safety of the vehicle (the nonconformity element); (2) the vehicle was presented to an authorized representative of the manufacturer of the vehicle for repair (the presentation element); and (3) the manufacturer or his representative did not repair the nonconformity after a reasonable number of repair attempts (the failure to repair element). (Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101 [109 Cal.Rptr.2d 583].)

 

Here, the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient for this cause of action because of the conclusory allegations. For instance, the Complaint alleges that “[Plaintiff] returned the SUBJECT VEHICLE to DEALER for repairs under the warranties referenced above because it exhibited defects, nonconformities, maladjustments, or malfunctions relating to [sic] defective battery; check engine light coming on; noise in vehicle; and malfunction sunroof.” (Complaint, ¶ 10(a).) Plaintiff’s Complaint further alleges, “… the Subject Vehicle exhibited further and additional defects, nonconformities, maladjustments, or malfunctions in the same components or systems.” (Complaint, ¶ 10(b).) Plaintiff’s Complaint then states Defendant “…failed to make the Subject Vehicle conform to the applicable warranties, despite a reasonable number of attempts to do so.” (Complaint ¶ 12(b).) Additionally, the Complaint states that “[t]he actions of Defendants, and each of them, in failing to perform the proper repairs, parts replacements, and/or adjustments to make the SUBJECT VEHICLE conform to the applicable express warranties constitute a breach of the express warranties that MANUFACTURER provided to [Plaintiff], thereby breaching Defendants’ obligations under the ACT.” (Complaint ¶ 15.) These allegations are insufficient because they are conclusory and fail to provide any detail as to when the vehicle was presented to the authorized dealer or how many attempts were made to fix the issues.

 

Thus, the Court grants the judgment on the pleadings for this cause of action with leave to amend.

 

B. Second Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Warranty  

 

“The implied warranty of merchantability may be breached by a latent defect undiscoverable at the time of sale.” (Mexia, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 1304.) “To state a claim for breach of implied warranty under California law, a consumer must demonstrate that the good is not “fit for the ordinary purposes for which such goods are used.” Cal. Com. Code § 2314(2)(c). Additionally, the Song-Beverly Act provides that “every sale of consumer goods that are sold at retail in this state shall be accompanied by the manufacturer's and the retail seller's implied warranty that the goods are merchantable.” Cal. Civ. Code § 1792. A plaintiff claiming breach of an implied warranty of merchantability must show that the product “did not possess even the most basic degree of fitness for ordinary use.” Asghari v. Volkswagen Grp. of Am., Inc., 42 F. Supp. 3d 1306, 1338 (C.D. Cal. 2013) (citations omitted). “The basic inquiry, therefore, is whether the vehicle was fit for driving.” [citation omitted.] (Franco v. Ford Motor Co., 644 F. Supp. 3d 672, 682.)

 

            Here, the Complaint states facts sufficient for this cause of action because an implied warranty requires a showing that the new vehicle had a latent defect. Plaintiff alleges that on April 27, 2021, he leased a new 2021 Mercedes-Benz GLE63 AMG 4 (VIN: 4JGFD8KB7MA379098) that came with a “new car warranty.” (Complaint ¶¶ 5-7.) The new car warranty “warranted to perform any repairs or replacement of parts necessary to ensure that the SUBJECT VEHICLE and the components therein were free from all defects in material and workmanship, and to perform any adjustments necessary to maintain the utility of the SUBJECT VEHICLE and the parts, components, and various electrical and/or mechanical systems contained therein.” (Complaint ¶¶ 8, 27.) The Complaint further alleges that “[Plaintiff] returned the SUBJECT VEHICLE to DEALER for repairs under the warranties referenced above because it exhibited defects, nonconformities, maladjustments, or malfunctions relating to [sic] defective battery; check engine light coming on; noise in vehicle; and malfunction sunroof.” (Complaint, ¶ 10(a).)

 

The Franco v. Ford Motor Co. case is instructive. In Franco, defendant Ford moved to dismiss the breach of implied warranty claims under the California Commercial Code and the Song-Beverly Act. The “gravamen of Plaintiffs' complaint is that the exhaust manifolds in Class Vehicles have a propensity to fail prematurely, exposing occupants to toxic fumes and impairing vehicle performance.” (Franco v. Ford Motor Co. (C.D.Cal. 2022) 644 F. Supp. 3d 672, 677.) The court dismissed the claim because “crucially, Plaintiffs do not allege that they have observed or experienced any issues with their exhaust manifolds, or that the alleged defect impaired the use of their vehicles in any way. See Tae Hee Lee v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc., 992 F. Supp. 2d 962, 980 (C.D. Cal. 2014) (“Plaintiffs have not alleged that they stopped using their vehicles.”).” (Franco v. Ford Motor Co., 644 F. Supp. 3d 672, 683 (C.D. Cal. 2022).) Unlike the plaintiffs in Franco, here, among a malfunctioning sunroof and noises the Plaintiff provides that there was a “defective battery” and “the check engine light” which shows that the vehicle was not fit for driving.

 

Thus, the Court denies the judgment on the pleadings for this cause of action.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the first cause of action (breach of express warranty) is GRANTED with 15 days’ leave to amend.

 

The Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the second cause of action (breach of implied warranty) is DENIED.

I. BACKGROUND

 

A. Factual

 

The following facts are drawn from the First Amended Complaint (as defined below). On July 15, 2020, plaintiff Jane JK Doe (“Plaintiff”) was admitted to a hospital with a small bowel obstruction and underwent emergency surgery. Plaintiff remained at the hospital recovering from the surgery for a few days. On July 18, 2020, defendant Divij Sachdeva (“Sachdeva”), a certified radiologic technologist at the hospital, entered Plaintiff’s room and sexually assaulted her without her consent. The following day, Sachdeva again entered Plaintiff’s room at the hospital and sexually assaulted her without her consent. Plaintiff reported Sacheva’s actions to her nurse, who told her he would advise his supervisors. The remaining defendants, West Hills Hospital (the “Hospital”), HCA Health Services of California (“HCA California”), HCA Health Services of Tennessee, Inc. (“HCA Tennessee”), HCA Healthcare, Inc. (“HCA Healthcare”), and C/HCA, Inc. (together with Sachdeva, the Hospital, HCA California, HCA Tennessee, and HCA Healthcare, the “Defendants”), are the entities which own the hospital and employed Sachdeva.

 

B. Procedural

 

On June 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (2) sexual battery, (3) gender violence, (4) negligence, (5) negligent supervision, (6) negligent hiring/retention, and (7) negligent failure to warn, train, or educate. On August 23, 2022, four of the defendants, the Hospital, HCA California, HCA Tennessee (together with the Hospital and HCA California, the “Corporate Defendants”), and Sachdeva, demurred to the Complaint and moved to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages. On October 10, 2022, this court sustained the demurrer, without leave to amend, as to Plaintiff’s fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action and sustained the demurrer, with leave to amend, as to Plaintiff’s first and fourth causes of action as to the Corporate Defendants. This court found the motion to strike as to the Corporate Defendants thereby mooted. This court overruled the demurrer and denied the motion to strike as to Sachdeva.

 

On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “First Amended Complaint” or the “FAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (2) sexual battery, (3) gender violence, (4) negligence, and (5) sexual harassment.

 

On July 10, 2023, the Corporate Defendants filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the FAC with a motion to strike (the “Motion to Strike”). On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike. On January 29, 2024, the Corporate Defendants replied to Plaintiff’s Oppositions.

 

II. MOVING PARTY’S GROUNDS FOR THE DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

           

The Corporate Defendants demur to the first and fourth causes of action on the grounds they are barred by the statute of limitations.  Corporate Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for failure to allege facts sufficient to support the cause of action. The Court notes Corporate Defendants accordingly demur to all causes of action asserted against them, as the second and third causes of action are asserted against Sachdeva only.

 

The Corporate Defendants move to strike the punitive damages as they are sought against the Corporate Defendants, HCA Healthcare, and C/HCA, Inc.

           

III. ANALYSIS

 

The Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

 

A. Statute of Limitations as to the First and Fourth Causes of Action

 

Parties disagree as to what statute of limitations applies to the FAC’s first and fourth causes of action. The Corporate Defendants argue that because they are healthcare providers and Plaintiff’s injury arose “out of medical services” they were providing her, then the Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act of 1975 (“MICRA”) applies, including its Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5, which applies “[i]n an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person’s alleged professional negligence,” sets the statute of limitations at “three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered the injury, whichever occurs first.” The Corporate Defendants observe that Plaintiff admits to having immediately known of her injury and they therefore further argue that her first and fourth causes of action were therefore barred one year after her injury’s occurrence. Plaintiff counters that her injury did not arise from the Corporate Defendants’ professional negligence or medical malpractice as healthcare providers but rather out of their general negligence as employers and maintainers of the hospital premises and therefore that her claims are not barred by MICRA’s Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5.

 

Plaintiff filed her suit against the Defendants on June 10, 2022, just under two years after her injuries on July 18–19, 2020. The Corporate Defendants argue that the filing of the FAC just under three years after Plaintiff’s injuries sets the timing of Plaintiff’s claims for statute of limitations purposes, but this is not correct. (Demurrer p. 7.) “An amended complaint is considered a new action for purposes of the statute of limitations only if the claims do not ‘relate back’ to an earlier, timely-filed complaint. Under the relation-back doctrine, an amendment relates back to the original complaint if the amendment: (1) rests on the same general set of facts; (2) involves the same injury; and (3) refers to the same instrumentality.” (Pointe San Diego Residential Community, L.P. v. Procopio, Corp, Hargreaves & Savitch, LLP et al. (2011) 195 Cal.Appt4th 265, 276. (citing Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 408–09).) Because the FAC’s first and fourth causes of action “relate back” to the Complaint, Plaintiff’s initial filing of the Complaint set the timing of her claims for statute of limitations purposes. So, if Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5 governs her claims, then the first and fourth causes of action are barred by its one-year statute of limitations. If, however, Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5 does not apply, then her claims are governed by the two-year statute of limitations for general negligence set out in Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 335.1 and are timely.

 

“It is settled that additional causes of action may arise out of the same facts as a medical malpractice action that do not trigger MICRA.” (Unruh-Haxton v. Regents of University of California (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 343, 352.) “[W]hen a cause of action is asserted against a health care provider on a legal theory other than medical malpractice, the courts must determine whether it is nevertheless based on the ‘professional negligence’ of the health care provider so as to trigger MICRA.” (Smith v. Ben Bennett, Inc. (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1507, 1514.) “A ‘health care provider’ is any person licensed to provide health care services including a health facility. ‘Professional negligence’ means a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services, which act or omission is the proximate cause of a personal injury or wrongful death, provided that such services are within the scope of services for which the provider is licensed and which are not within any restriction imposed by the licensing agency or licensed hospital.” (Samantha B. v. Aurora Vista Del Mar, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 85, 96 (quoting Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5(1–2)).) Thus, “professional negligence” as used in Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5 does not broadly cover “all conduct directly related to the rendition of professional services.” (Benun v. Superior Court (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 113, 121.) Notably, “MICRA statute of limitations provisions that apply to professional negligence actions against health care providers do not apply to intentional tort claims.” (David M. v. Beverly Hospital (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1278.)

 

Thus, whether MICRA applies turns on whether “the plaintiffs based their intentional tort claims on intentional misconduct [or on] professional negligence.” (Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 336, 348–49.) In Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc., the court distinguishes between intentional torts conducted in the “render[ing] of professional healthcare services” and “intentional torts committed for an ulterior purpose” or “some collateral course of conduct pursued for [the defendant’s] own gain or gratification” (Id. at 351–52.) In Perry v. Shaw, MICRA did not apply to claims arising from a cosmetic surgery done without the patient-plaintiff’s consent because the plaintiff “alleged a battery based on a deliberate decision to ignore the scope of the plaintiff's consent, not a negligent failure” by the physician-defendant. (Perry v. Shaw (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 658, 663–64, 668.) In Delaney v. Baker, MICRA did not apply to claims arising from the abuse of seniors by a nursing home facility and its providers because the Elder Abuse Act provides distinct remedies for willful neglect of seniors so that “neglect” was not merely a product of negligence but rather a willful failure to carry out one’s professional responsibilities. (Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 33–35.) This included the plaintiff’s claims against the nursing home facility that were based on the facility’s ordinary negligence. (Ibid.) By contrast, in David M. v. Beverly Hospital, the plaintiff’s negligence causes of action arising from a hospital’s failure to report a minor patient’s physical abuse was barred by the statute of limitations because “claims for medical malpractice based on ordinary negligence” are subject to MICRA, though the court did note that such would not have been the case “had plaintiff alleged…intentional[] conceal[ment of] his failure to report or [] intentional[] fail[ure] to report known or suspected abuse.” (David M. v. Beverly Hospital, supra, 131 Cal.App.4th at 1278, 1281.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s causes of action arise from the intentional sexual battery committed against her by an employee of the Corporate Defendants, and MICRA therefore does not apply. Plaintiff’s sexual battery had no possible relationship to the rendering of the professional healthcare services for which Plaintiff presented to the hospital but rather was a “collateral course of conduct” pursued for an “ulterior purpose.” Just as the wrongfully done cosmetic surgery of Perry v. Shaw was not subject to MICRA, here, Plaintiff’s claims arise from a battery well outside the scope of her consent, which was deliberately ignored, and are consequently similarly not subject to MICRA. The conduct of an intentional battery, even when done in a medical setting by a medical professional, removes Plaintiff’s claims from MICRA’s scope. Additionally, like in Delaney v. Baker, MICRA does not apply to the Corporate Defendants’ ordinary negligence in preventing the intentional torts of its employees. Unlike in David M. v. Beverly Hospital, where the plaintiff alleged negligence by the defendants because they failed their duty to report abuse of a minor patient, a duty which they bore as healthcare providers; here, Plaintiff does not allege that the Corporate Defendants’ duties to her arose from their statuses as healthcare providers. Rather, she alleges negligence by the Corporate Defendants for their failures to protect her as employers of Sachdeva and as the providers of the hospital facilities. Moreover, Plaintiff alleges that the Corporate Defendants intentionally concealed Sachdeva’s actions, which, following Delaney v. Baker and David M. v. Beverly Hospital, further indicates that MICRA is not appropriately applied here. 

 

The Corporate Defendants state that this court “has previously held that all claims based upon Negligence are time-barred,” but this misstates this court’s prior ruling. This court held that, following So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, all causes of action based on the “negligently hiring, supervising, training and retaining” Sachdeva are barred by MICRA’s Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 340.5.

 

Thus, because Plaintiff’s claims are timely brought within Cal. C. Civ. Pro. § 335.1’s two-year statute of limitations, this court OVERRULES the demurrer to the first and fourth causes of action.

 

B. Failure to State Sufficient Facts as to the Fifth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiff added a new cause of action to the FAC for sexual harassment pursuant to Cal. Civ. C. § 51.9 against all Defendants. Plaintiff asserts that the Corporate Defendants are vicariously liable for Sachdeva’s actions, alleging that Sachdeva sexually harassed her under the supervision of the Corporate Defendants and that the Corporate Defendants are equally liable therefor because they ratified Sachdeva’s wrongful actions. The Corporate Defendants now demur to this cause of action on the grounds that it is “vague, unintelligible, and fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a separate cause of action” against the Corporate Defendants.

 

The parties cite C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp., which holds in part that the corporate owners of a medical facility can be vicariously liable for the sexually harassing actions of its employees under Cal. Civ. C. § 51.9 under a respondeat superior theory where there is evidence that the corporate owners ratified the employee’s actions. (C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp. (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1106–10.) The court in C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp. describes a medical employer’s failure to “investigate or respond to charges that an employee committed an intentional tort, such as assault or battery” as an example of where ratification is “generally applied.” (Id. at 1110 (quoting Baptist v. Robinson (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 151, 169–70).) In that case, the court found that the corporate owners of a medical center ratified the repeated sexually harassing actions of a nurse assistant employee because an agency relationship existed between the owners and the employee, the employee’s supervisors knew of but failed to respond to or “hid” his repeated actions over the course of a couple years and maintained his employment, and the owners “intentionally or negligently spoiled evidence including documents concerning other sexual assaults.” (Id. at 1112 (internal quote marks removed).)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that an agency relationship existed between the Corporate Defendants and Sachdeva and thus it would be possible for the Corporate Defendants to have sexually harassed her by ratifying Sachdeva’s sexual harassment of her, but Plaintiff fails to plead facts sufficient to show that they actually did so. Unlike the defendants in C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp., there are no allegations that Corporate Defendants allowed or covered up repeated sexual abuses by an employee over years, but rather, Plaintiff alleges Corporate Defendants responded with an internal investigation and noted her safety concerns in her medical record. Plaintiff’s allegation that the Corporate Defendants’ investigation report contained incorrect information is not sufficient to show that the Corporate Defendants spoiled evidence or attempted to hide Sachdeva’s actions.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS Corporate Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action, with leave to amend.

 

The Motion to Strike

 

Corporate Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. Specifically, Corporate Defendants move to strike references to Corporate Defendants in Plaintiff’s references to her request for punitive damages set forth in: (1) the Statement of Facts; and (2) the first, fourth, and fifth causes of action.  (Notice of Motion to Strike, p. 2; FAC ¶¶ 22, 42, 86, 99.) The Court notes the request for punitive and exemplary damages set forth in paragraph 6 of the Prayer for Relief is not included in Corporate Defendants Notice of Motion; however, given Corporate Defendants move to strike all supporting allegations for punitive damages, the Court considers it as included.

 

In light of the Court’s ruling sustaining the demurrer as to the fifth cause of action with leave to amend, Corporate Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages set forth in Paragraph 99 is denied as moot.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)

 

The Corporate Defendants rely on Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a), which prohibits the inclusion of punitive damages in “an action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider” without prior leave of the court. Intentional torts may be covered by Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a) if the nature and cause of the plaintiff’s injury are each “directly related to the manner in which professional services were provided.” (Central Pathology Service Medical Clinic Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 181, 192.) Thus, “a cause of action against a health care provider for sexual battery would not, in most instances, fall within the statute because the defendant's conduct would not be directly related to the manner in which professional services were rendered.” (Ibid.)

 

“Plaintiffs must do more than merely rely on the unsubstantiated allegations in their complaints before they may proceed with a punitive damage claim against a health care provider.” (United Western Medical Centers v. Superior Court (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 500, 503–04.) “[A] plaintiff must make an evidentiary showing of a ‘substantial probability’ of prevailing on a punitive damage claim before such a claim may be included in the pleading.” (Id. at 503.) In other words, a plaintiff bears the burden of “both stat[ing] and substantiat[ing] a legitimate, triable punitive damages claim” by demonstrating possession of “competent, admissible evidence,” such as an affidavit, to overcome Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a). (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 709, 719.)

 

The Corporate Defendants cite United Western Medical Centers v. Superior Court as an example of Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a)’s application in the health care context “to protect employers of individuals who are accused of committing [sexual] assault or battery against a patient.” (Motion to Strike p. 5.) The court in United Western Medical Centers v. Superior Court finds the plaintiff in that case barred from seeking punitive damages from the employers because her claim against them was limited to the contention that they “either negligently or intentionally failed to supervise its staff, or hired incompetent staff and thereby allowed real party to be sexually assaulted,” and these actions or inactions related to medical staff are “directly related to its performance of professional services.” (Supra, at 505.) That plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages against the individuals who sexually assaulted her, however, were not barred. (Ibid.) Additionally, in College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court, the court similarly found that Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a) barred the plaintiff’s claims for punitive damages against the corporate defendants who owned medical facilities based on the tortious conduct of their employee. (Supra, at 727). Although the plaintiff in that case alleged that the corporate defendants “knew or should have known” about and therefore “approved or tolerated [the employee’s wrongful actions] by failing to adequately investigate the matter or discipline [the employee],” it held that “[c]orporate ratification in the punitive damages context requires actual knowledge of the conduct and its outrageous nature.” (Id. at 724–26.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s claims for punitive damages against the Corporate Defendants are barred by Cal. Civ. Pro. § 425.13(a) on the face of the pleadings. Although Plaintiff’s claims against the Corporate Defendants are no longer based on their failure to supervise or train Sachdeva but rather on their failure to provide a safe environment at the hospital facilities for Plaintiff’s recovery and for ratifying Sachdeva’s actions, these actions or inactions, in the punitive damages context, are still directly related to the provision of medical services. Moreover, Plaintiff has alleged additional facts to support her punitive damages claim.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED AS MOOT as to the fifth cause of action and GRANTED as to the remaining requests for punitive damages.

 

 

[TENTATIVE RULING:]

 

Demurrer as to the first and fourth causes of action is overruled. Demurrer as to the fifth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike is granted, except as to the fifth cause of action, which it is denied as moot.

[TENTATIVE RULING:]

 

The Court GRANTS the Motion for a Stay of Proceedings pending outcome of People of the State of California v. Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu, case no. LA096033 (Cal. Super. Ct. filed Dec. 21, 2021).

 

 

Background

 

On August 28, 2021, Plaintiff Maribel Greenidge (“Plaintiff”) and Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu (“Defendant”) were involved in a motor vehicle collision. Plaintiff alleges she suffered property damage and bodily injuries as a result of the accident. Plaintiff further alleges that immediately after the motor vehicle collision described above, Defendant Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu approached and assaulted her. Plaintiff then alleges Defendant proceeded to inflict intentional and highly offensive and inappropriate touching, sexually offensive contact, and unreasonable physical force on Plaintiff’s person.

 

Defendant was later charged with sexual penetration by use of force in violation of California Penal Code section 289(a)(1)(A), a felony, in case People of the State of California v. Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu, case no. LA096033 (Cal. Super. Ct. filed Dec. 21, 2021) as a result of the same incident on August 28, 2021. Defendant has pled not guilty and was released on bail. This criminal case is still pending.

 

On April 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant personal injury action alleging (1) motor vehicle negligence, (2) assault, (3) battery, (4) sexual battery, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (6) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

 

On January 10, 2024, Defendant filed this Motion for a Stay of Proceedings.

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed her opposition.

 

On January 25, 2024, Defendant filed his reply.

 

Moving Party

 

Defendant argues that the stay should be granted because he has a right against self-incrimination as he is a defendant in two separate cases arising out of the same incident. (Mot. for Stay of Proceedings p.4.) Additionally, defendant argues that the parties will not be prejudiced if this motion is granted because the stay would still allow the case to be ready for trial in the summer of 2024. (Id. at p.5.)

 

Opposition

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not met his burden to stay proceedings because she would be highly prejudiced by the delay, as she will not be able to gain closure and he has already delayed the proceedings. (Opp. p.4-5.) Moreover, Plaintiff argues that the burden that the civil case may impose on the criminal proceedings is nonexistent, because the Defendant can still exercise his right to plead the 5th amendment in both criminal and civil cases. (Id. at p.5.) Next, Plaintiff argues that denial of the stay promotes the convenience of the court. (Id.) Also, Plaintiff continues to seek and receive treatment stop so both lien providers and health insurance parties who are not parties to the civil litigation would be unfairly prejudiced by this stay. (Id.) Lastly, Defendant has already had more than ten preliminary setting/resetting hearings which prevents the public from finding a resolution in this case. (Id. at p.6.)

 

Reply

Defendant reiterates the same points about his rights against self-incrimination. (Reply p.2-3.) Defendants also argues that the prejudice to him clearly outweighs Plaintiffs’ prejudice because her time delay is not the same as his criminal liability and should therefore be granted. (Id.)

 

Legal Standard

 

When considering whether to grant a motion to stay proceedings pending the outcome of a related criminal case, the trial court shall use its discretion to “assess and balance the nature and substantiality of the injustices claimed on either side.”  (People v. Coleman (1975) 13 Cal.3d 867, 885 (Coleman), quoting Gordon v. Federal Deposit Ins. Corp. (D.D.C. 1970) 427 F.2d 578, 580 (Gordon).)  Factors which the trial court should consider include: “(1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.”  (Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885 (Avant!), quoting Keating v. Office of Thrift Supervision (9th Cir. 1995) 45 F.3d 322, 324-325 (Keating).)

 

Discussion

 

(1) Interest of the Plaintiffs in Proceeding Expeditiously with this Litigation or Any Particular Aspect of It, and the Potential Prejudice to Plaintiffs of a Delay

           

Here, the Plaintiff sets forth no evidence that she stands to suffer actual prejudice in the event of a stay. (Opp. p.6.) Plaintiff only emphasized her preference that this litigation proceed expeditiously because Defendant has previously delayed the trial and she seek closure. (Id.) This alone, without more, is insufficient.

 

(2) Burden which Any Particular Aspect of the Proceedings May Impose on Defendants

           

Here, both the criminal and civil cases arose from the same motor vehicle collision. (Dec. McFarlane ¶¶ 6-13.) Since the civil case includes causes action for (2) assault, (3) battery, (4) sexual battery, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (6) negligent infliction of emotional distress it is likely that discovery questions may be used against Defendant in the criminal case. Thus, Defendant would be burdened by the discovery and would potentially self-incriminate himself in the criminal trial. 

 

(3) Convenience of the Court in the Management of its Cases, and the Efficient Use of Judicial Resources

           

Here, the efficient use of judicial resources would be to stay the trial because Defendant would bring additional motions by not complying with the discovery requests. For instance, Defendant states that he “would be forced to object to each and every question or discovery request by the Plaintiff or the co-defendants individually, wait for these parties to file motions to compel answers to each question or discovery request, oppose those motions, and force the court to rule on each motion.” (Reply p.3.) Thus, it would be more efficient to stay the proceedings.

 

(4) Interests of Persons Not Parties to the Civil Litigation & (5) Interest of the Public In The Pending Civil and Criminal Litigation. 

Although Plaintiff argues that the public, lien providers, and healthcare providers would be unfairly prejudiced by the delay in resolution. (Opp. p.6.) Nevertheless, the Court finds no relevant interests which factor into its analysis of the stay motion in this case.

 

Ultimately, after assessing and balancing the nature and substantiality of the injustices claimed on either side, the Court finds that the relevant factors weigh in favor of granting a stay.  (See Keating, supra, 45 F.3d at pp. 324-325.)  Defendant’s motion for a stay of this entire proceeding will be granted as to the trial date and as to all discovery related to Defendant’s alleged criminal conduct for which he is being prosecuted in People of the State of California v. Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu, case no. LA096033 (Cal. Super. Ct. filed Dec. 21, 2021). The Court intends for the stay to bar Defendant’s discovery in the instant matter.

 

Conclusion

 

Thus, the Court agrees with Defendant that it would be an imposition upon the Court to try this civil case during the pendency of the criminal proceeding as doing so would be plagued with difficulties involving Defendant’s Fifth Amendment Privilege against self-incrimination because both cases relate to the same incident. As such, this Court has discretion to stay this proceeding as against Defendant pending a resolution of the federal criminal case against him. (See Klein v. Superior Court (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 894, 905.)

 

Accordingly, the Court grants the Motion for a Stay of Proceedings pending outcome of People of the State of California v. Alfred Igbokwe Obichukwu, case no. LA096033 (Cal. Super. Ct. filed Dec. 21, 2021).
