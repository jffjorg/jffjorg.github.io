Case Number: 22AVCV00917    Hearing Date: April 9, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a wrongful death action. Plaintiff Maria Pelayo (“Plaintiff”) is the motion of Decedent Jonathan Alfaro (“Decedent”). Plaintiff alleges that on February 18, 2022, in the parking lot at Pete Knight High School and Knight Academy Junior Prep High School (the “Parking Lot”) which is within the supervision, control, and guidance of Defendant Antelope Valley Union High School District (“AVUHSD”), Defendant Rachel Cruz (“Rachel”)[1] and several students were lined up to “burn rubber” (i.e., engaging in sharp turns and driving recklessly around the lot at high speeds) when Decedent was thrown from the back of a truck driven by Rachel, sustaining blunt trauma to his head, feet, and legs, and, ultimately, leading to his death. Plaintiff presents that there were no security officers or school administrators present during the incident.

 

On November 07, 2022, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging two causes of action for Negligence and Wrongful Death against AVUHSD and Doe defendants.

 

On March 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging one cause of action for Negligence (Wrongful Death) against AVUHSD and Doe defendants.

 

On May 04, 2023, AVUHSD filed its Answer and Cross Complaint against Does 11 through 50.

 

On July 28, 2023, AVUHSD changed the fictitious names of Doe Defendants as follows: (1) Doe 11 to Rachel, (2) Doe 12 to Richard Cruz (“Richard”), and (3) Doe 13 to Edelia Cruz (“Edelia” and collectively “the Cruzes”).

 

On August 28, 2023, the Cruzes filed their Answer and a Cross-Complaint against AVUHSD.

 

On October 25, 2023, AVUHSD filed its Answer to the Cruzes’ Cross-Complaint.

 

On November 02, 2023, the Cruzes filed an Application for Determination of Good Faith Settlement.

 

On November 07, 2023, AVUHSD filed this Motion to Contest the Application for Determination of Good Faith Settlement, continued to April 09, 2024.

 

On February 20,2024, AVUHSD filed amendments to the Cross-Complaint changing (1) the fictitious name of Doe 14 to Nancy Torres; (2) the fictitious name of Doe 15 to Eduardo Alfaro, and (3) the fictitious name of Doe 16 to Ingrid Lopez.

 

On March 01, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held.

 

On March 06, 2024, AVUHSD filed this Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses and Produce Documents Without Objections (“Motion to Compel Further”).

 

On March 20, 2024, Rachel filed her Opposition.

 

On March 26, 2024, Ingryd Lopez, erroneously sued as Ingrid Lopez (“Lopez”); Nancy Torres (“Torres”); and Edward Alfaro, erroneously sued as Eduardo Alfaro (“Alfaro”), filed their Answers.

 

On April 02, 2024, AVUHSD filed its Reply.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Further Responses to Requests for Production (“RFPs”) – “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)      

      

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a).)      

     

Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).)     

      

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there is “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”       

      

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup. Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)      

      

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)       

      

Trial courts are vested with “wide discretion” to allow or prohibit discovery. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 540.)

Discussion

 

Application – Requests for Production (“RFPs”) No. 1, 3, 15, 22, and 32 concern a police report.

 

AVUHSD presents the following arguments:

 

(1)   A three-page police report is not the complete report;

(2)   The police report alone is not the entirety of the documents pertaining the underlying incident which gives rise to Rachel’s Cross-Complaint;

(3)   Rachel has complete control over these documents;

(4)   Rachel has not followed the statutory response format under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230; and

(5)   Ali v. Apple, Inc., No. 19CV356117, (Cal.Super. Oct. 14, 2021) 2021 WL 11561428, at *3 (“Ali”) citing A. Farber & Ptnrs., Inc. v. Garber (C.D. Cal. 2006) 234 F.R.D. 186, 189 states: “ ‘There is authority for the proposition that if documents requested from a party are maintained by a third party not a party to the lawsuit, the responding party has an obligation to obtain those documents if there is a contractual relationship between the responding party and the third party.’ ”

 

Rachel’s responses to these arguments are: (1) she has produced whatever document are in her possession as to the police report; (2) she cannot demand such documents from the authorities absent a subpoena, which moving party has admittedly done; (3) she cannot produce documents that she has never had or has not been able to obtain from her former criminal counsel; (4) the information sought is vague; (5) her responses are in good faith; and (5) as to some responses, the responses indicate that she has no such documents.

 

As to the Ali argument, used also in arguments as to RFP Nos. 34-35, this Court is not bound by the decisions of another trial court. A. Farber & Ptnrs., Inc. v. Garber (C.D. Cal. 2006) 234 F.R.D. 186 concerns supplemental production under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure, not Cal. Code Civ. Proc. Thus, this argument is inapposite.

 

The Court directs the parties to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250 for formatting of responses to inspection demands.

 

Response to RFP No. 1: “After a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, we have no documents that be responsive to this request; cross defendant does not believe any such documents ever existed, other than the police report; A copy of that document was previously produced; We have already produced whatever documents are in my possession with respect to the Juvenile Court matter; Neither I nor my attorney have been able to reach my former criminal counsel, Jesse Duran, who I may have additional documents[.]”

 

(Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 1; Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 1.)

 

While this response states that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has ben conducted and no documents are responsive, the response also states that (1) a police report exists and was produced, and (2) Rachel’s former criminal counsel may have additional documents.

 

“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.)

 

Rachel’s response to RFP No. 1 is not compliant with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.

 

Response to RFP No. 3: “After a diligent search we are not aware that any such documents ever existed, other than the police report, which was previously produced.”

 

As with the Response to RFP No. 1, this response indicates that (1) no documents existed, but (2) a police report exists which answers the response. However, the police report is not included as Rachel states that she has already produced it. While the Court understands that production may have occurred previously, the current reference to previously produced documents is not an adequate response. It does not comply with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250 nor does it comply with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.280 which states that “[a]ny documents or category of documents produced in response to a demand...shall be identified with the specific request number to which the documents respond.”

 

(Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 3; Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 3.)

 

Response to RFP No. 15: “After a diligent search, we have not attempted to obtain any of the requested documents other than what has been voluntarily provided to us by cross complainant’s counsel.”

 

(Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 15; Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 15.)

 

On its face, the response to RFP No. 15 does not comply with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250.

 

Response to RFP No. 22: “Other than the Police Report, which has already been produced. After a diligent search we are not aware that any other such documents ever existed; We have no documents responsive to this request.”

 

(Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 22; Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 22.)

 

For the same reasons as the analysis for the response to RFP No. 3, this response is not code compliant.

 

Response to RFP No. 32: “After a diligent search we are not aware that any such documents ever existed; We have no documents responsive to this request other than the police report, which has been produced[.]”

 

(Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 32; Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 32.)

 

For the same reasons as the analysis for the response to RFP No. 3, this response is not code compliant.

 

RFPs No. 24, 25, 26, and 31 concern responses in which this lawsuit is represented as “brought on behalf of Plaintiff.”  

 

RFPs No. 24 through 26 are relatively similar. They request “All DOCUMENTS which IDENTIFY any claims, complaints or actions by PLAINTIFF or anyone acting on behalf of PLAINTIFF or DECEDENT” against Rachel, Richard, and Edelia. (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP Nos. 24-26; Motion, Exh. D at RFP Nos. 24-26.)

 

RFP No. 31 requests “[p]roduce each DOCUMENT[] tangible thing and/or other property which are identified or referenced in the response to Special Interrogatory Number 20, served concurrently with this Request.” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 31; Motion, Exh. D at RFP Nos. 20 and 31.)

 

As to RFPs Nos. 24-26, Rachel has provided: “Objection, ambiguous; While reserving that objection, we have no documents other than this lawsuit which was ‘brought on behalf of Plaintiff.’ ” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP Nos. 24-26; Motion, Exh. D at RFP Nos. 24-26.) Rachel’s ambiguous objections are meritless. These responses are not compliant with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250 nor does it comply with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.280 which states that “[a]ny documents or category of documents produced in response to a demand...shall be identified with the specific request number to which the documents respond.”

 

As to RFP No. 31, Special Interrogatory No. 20 has not been disclosed to the Court. The Court notes that it does not know the context of RFP No. 31; however, the response provided [“After a diligent search we are not aware that any such documents; We have no

documents responsive to this request other than this lawsuit.”] is not code compliant as (1) the statement regarding the documents is incomplete, and (2) the response does not follow the statutory requirements of Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250, 2031.280.

 

RFPs 33 and 34 likewise request production of items that were used to answer special interrogatories. (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 33 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 33 [“Produce each DOCUMENT. tangible thing and/or other property which are

identified or referenced in the response to Special Interrogatory Number 23, served concurrently with this Request.”]; Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 34 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 34 [“Produce each DOCUMENT. tangible thing and/or other property which are identified or referenced in the response to Special Interrogatory Number 24, served concurrently with this Request.”].)

 

Rachel’s response to RFP No. 33 reads: “We have produced Rachel’s letter to Jonathan’s mother. Other than that document, we have performed a diligent search and cannot find any other documents which would be responsive to this request.” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 33 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 33.) This response is not compliant with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.210-2031.250 nor does it comply with Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.280 which states that “[a]ny documents or category of documents produced in response to a demand...shall be identified with the specific request number to which the documents respond.”

 

Rachel’s response to RFP No. 34 reads: “After a diligent search , neither I nor my civil counsel have any documents that would be responsive to this request; My criminal counsel, Jesse Duran might have documents but has not responded to my attorney’s emails or telephone calls[.]” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 34 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 34.) This response is not code compliant under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.

 

RFP No. 35 reads: “Produce each written statement provided by CROSS-DEFENDANT RACHEL CRUZ to any PERSON regarding the SUBJECT INCIDENT.” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 35 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 35.)

 

Rachel’s response reads: “After a diligent search , neither I nor my civil counsel have any documents that would be responsive to this request; My criminal counsel, Jesse Duran might have documents but has not responded to my attorney’s emails or telephone calls[.]” (See ibid.)

 

For the same reasons as RFP No. 34, this response is not code compliant.

 

RFP No. 36 reads: “Produce all reports made by any person or entity concerning the SUBJECT INCIDENT.” (See Response to Separate Statement at RFP No. 36 and Motion, Exh. D at RFP No. 36.)

 

Rachel’s response reads: “Objection, this request is vague, ambiguous and overly broad and seeks information which is irrelevant to the issues presented and is not calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. It also seeks information which may be subject to the attorney-client and work product privileges. While reserving those objections, we have no other documents that would be responsive to this request.” (Ibid.)

 

The objections regarding vague, ambiguous, and overly broad are without merit. To the extent Rachel’s objections are based on privilege, privilege responses “shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log” and Rachel’s responses do not do so. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(b)(c)(1).) Rachel’s statement that “we have no other documents that would be responsive to this request” does not adhere to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Compel Further is GRANTED. Rachel is to Provide code-compliant responses within 30 days of this Court’s Order.

Sanctions

 

While the moving papers do not specifically request sanctions, Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(h) requires sanctions as follows:

 

Except as provided in subdivision (j), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

As presented in the Response to Separate Statement, Rachel believes that her responses are appropriate and in good faith and indicates that Rachel does not have certain documents. However, they are not code-compliant. No justification has been presented for why the responses were not presented in the appropriate format.

 

Accordingly, the Court imposes a sanction of $500.00 on Rachel and her counsel of record, David Arthur Belofsky, jointly and severally.

Conclusion

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant/Cross-Defendant Antelope Valley Union High School’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Produce Documents without Objections is GRANTED.

 

The Court imposes a sanction of $500.00 on Cross-Defendant/Cross-Complainant Rachel Lauren Cruz and her counsel of record, David Arthur Belofsky, jointly and severally. 

 

Cross-Defendants/Cross-Complainants Rachel Lauren Cruz is ordered to provide code-compliant responses within 30 days of this Court Order.
