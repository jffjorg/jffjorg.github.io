Case Number: 19PSCV00420    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: H

 Capital One Bank (USA), N.A. v. Lu, Case No. 19PSCV00420

For the reasons stated below, the hearing on Plaintiff Capital One Bank (USA), Inc.’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment Under Terms of Stipulated Settlement is CONTINUED to November 15, 2024, at 9:30 a.m.

 

Background

This is a credit card collection case. On May 6, 2019, Plaintiff Capital One Bank (USA), Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint, asserting breach of contract and common counts causes of action against Jiazheng Lu (“Lu”).

On October 15, 2019, Plaintiff filed a “Settlement Agreement.” On October 16, 2019, Plaintiff filed a conditional “Notice of Settlement of Entire Case;” that day, an “Order on Settlement Agreement and Stipulation for Judgment” was filed and the case was dismissed, without prejudice.

 

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order, per Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, vacating the dismissal in this case and entering judgment against Lu.

The Court will continue the hearing on the motion to November 15 at 9:30 a.m., inasmuch as there is no indication in the record that Lu was provided notice of the continued hearing date of October 17, 2024.[FN1] Plaintiff’s counsel is ordered to provide notice of the above continued hearing date forthwith and to file a proof of service with the Court reflecting such notice no later than five court days prior to the continued hearing date.

[FN1] The motion was filed on August 27, 2024 (and served via mail on August 26, 2024) and originally set for hearing on September 30, 2024. On September 17, 2024, the Court issued a “Notice Re: Continuance of Hearing and Order,” advising therein that the September 30, 2024 scheduled hearing had been continued to October 17, 2024; notice was given to moving party’s counsel only. There is no indication on the Court’s docket reflecting that notice of the continued hearing date was given to Lu.

Case Number: 22PSCV00430    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: H

Abhari, et al. v. Gordes Hospitality Inc., et al., Case No. 22PSCV00430

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

Defendants/Cross-Complainants Gordes Hospitality Inc. and LVGEM Hotel Corporation’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Cross-Defendant Xicheng Qian is GRANTED. Qian is ordered forthwith to provide Gordes and LVGEM with three dates Qian is available for deposition within the 20-day period from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $1,393.45 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

 

Background 

In this breach of contract case, Plaintiffs Zia Abhari and Amir Shamloo (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

On May 2, 2020, Gordes Hospitality, Inc. (“Gordes”), through Ganyu Huang aka Gary Huang, and Shamloo, with Abhari serving as a silent partner, entered into an “Asset Purchase Agreement” and a “Security Agreement” with Gordes for the transfer of ownership of Hamilton’s Steakhouse (“Hamilton”) from Gordes, as seller, to Plaintiffs, as buyers. On June 18, 2020, LVGEM Hotel Corporation (“LVGEM”), through Huang, and Shamloo, with Abhari serving as a silent partner of Shamloo, entered into a “Lease Agreement” for the lease of the property upon which Hamilton is located. Gordes, LVGEM, and Huang have refused to transfer the existing liquor license for Hamilton to Plaintiffs, failed to provide Plaintiffs with the insurance information and have undertaken efforts to deprive Plaintiffs of their real estate leasehold possessory interest in the premises upon which the Hamilton is located. 

Plaintiffs’ Second Amended Complaint asserts 10 causes of action against Gordes, LVGEM, and others, including for breach of contract, tortious breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, fraud in the inducement, negligent misrepresentation, and fraud by false promise. 

Gordes and LVGEM have filed a Cross-Complaint, asserting 14 causes of action against Abhari, Shamloo, and Xicheng Qian (“Qian”), including for conversion, money had and received, fraud based on intentional misrepresentation, fraud in the inducement, unjust enrichment, and breach of fiduciary duties. 

Pending before the Court is Gordes and LVGEM’s motion to compel Qian to (1) attend his deposition, (2) produce documents responsive to their request for production of documents, and (3) pay sanctions in the amount of $2,533.45. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted, though sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $1,393.45. 

 

Legal Standard 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection . . ., fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) 

A motion to compel deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).) The motion shall also “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) 

A court shall impose monetary sanctions if the motion to compel is granted, “unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) 

 

Discussion 

Gordes and LVGEM move the Court for an order compelling Qian to (1) attend his deposition, (2) produce documents responsive to their request for production of documents, and (3) pay sanctions in the amount of $2,533.45. 

Gordes and LVGEM’s counsel Caixing Ma (“Ma”) represents as follows:

On February 16, 2024, Ma confirmed with Qian’s counsel Matthew Malczynski (“Malczynski”) that Qian would be available for his deposition in late March 2024. (Ma Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) On February 20, 2024, Gordes and LVGEM served Qian with a “Notice of Taking Deposition. . . and Request for Production of Documents,” wherein Qian’s remote deposition was scheduled for March 28, 2024. (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. 2.) 

On March 26, 2024, Malczynski emailed Ma and stated that he had a hearing in Chatsworth that he needed to personally attend on March 28 and thus needed to reschedule the deposition (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. 3); however, when Pasha Vafaei, counsel for Shamloo and Abhari, asked why the March 28, 2024 deposition needed to be rescheduled, Malczynski responded that he “had the flu.” (Id. ¶ 4, Exh. 4.)

On April 16, 2024, Malczynski confirmed that May 17, 2024 would be “perfect” for Qian’s rescheduled deposition (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. 5); as such, Gordes and LVGEM served Qian with the first amended notice of deposition for a May 17, 2024 deposition. (Id., ¶ 5, Exhs. 6 and 7.) On May 16, 2024, Malczynski emailed Ma and said he would “prefer to continue [the deposition] one last time,” which Ma agreed to do, to June 11, 2024. (Id., ¶ 6, Exhs. 7 and 8.) On May 17, 2024, Gordes and LVGEM served Qian with the second amended notice of deposition for a June 11, 2024 deposition. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. 9.)

On June 10, 2024, at 2:52 p.m., Malczynski asked to move the deposition “one FINAL time” from June 11, 2024 to the following Tuesday. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. 10.) Ma agreed; as such, Gordes and LVGEM served Qian with the third amended notice of deposition for a June 18, 2024 deposition. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. 11.)

On June 18, 2024, at 9:24 a.m., 36 minutes prior to the scheduled deposition, Malczynski emailed Ma that Qian “is out of the country.” (Id., ¶ 8, Exh. 12.) Counsel for Gordes and LVGEM thereafter unsuccessfully attempted on multiple occasions to rescheduled Qian’s deposition. (Id., ¶ 9.) Counsel for Gordes and LVGEM filed an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) brief on July 25, 2024 in an attempt to resolve scheduling Qian’s deposition, but the IDC was taken off-calendar when the case was reassigned to Department H. (Id., ¶ 9. Exh. 13.)

On September 5, 2024, Counsel for Gordes and LVGEM emailed Malczynski and asked that he provide her by September 9, 2024 with available dates in late September or early October 2024 for Qian’s deposition; no response was received. (Id., ¶ 10, Exh. 14.) As of the filing date of the motion, Qian has not appeared for his deposition, nor has he provided any objections, responses, or documents to the concurrent document requests. (Id., ¶ 11.)

The motion is granted. Qian is ordered forthwith to provide Gordes and LVGEM with three dates Qian is available for deposition within the 20-day period from the date of the notice of ruling.

As to the request for sanctions, Gordes and LVGEM seek sanctions against Qian in the amount of $2,533.45 [calculated as follows: 0.4 hours preparing IDC form, plus 1.9 hours preparing IDC brief, plus 2.5 hours preparing motion and declaration, plus 1 hour reviewing opposition, drafting reply and preparing for/attending hearing at $300.00 /hour, plus $60.00 filing fee, plus $583.45 in court reporter fees for the last-minute cancellation of deposition].

Utilizing a Lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motion is $1,393.45 (i.e., 2.5 hours at $300.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee, plus $583.45 in court reporter fees). Sanctions are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

Case Number: 23PSCV02704    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: H

Ramirez v.  American Honda Motor Company, Case No. 23PSCV02704

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL

Plaintiff Ana Cecilia Lerma Ramirez’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents is DENIED in part (i.e., as to Nos. 9, 23, 24 and 109) and GRANTED in part (i.e., as to Nos. 27-30, 41, 42, 45, 46, 51-54, 80, 81, 119, 120, 129 and 130, as limited by this order).

 

Background

Plaintiff Ana Cecilia Lerma Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

On April 24, 2021, Plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) regarding a 2021 Honda Civic, VIN No. 2HGFC2F69MH552933 (the “subject vehicle”). The subject vehicle is equipped with a computerized driver-assisting safety system known as “Honda Sensing,” which includes adaptive cruise control, lane departure warnings and steering inputs, and autonomous braking. The Honda Sensing system suffers from a defect that causes the various subsystems within it to malfunction dangerously while the vehicles are driven (the “Sensing Defect”). Defendant knew or should have known about the Sensing Defect before the subject vehicle was sold and failed to disclose same.

 

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting causes of action against Defendant and Does 1-10 for:

 

1.       Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2;

2.       Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2;

3.       Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2;

4.       Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; and

5.       Fraudulent Inducement—Concealment.

 

On February 6, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action, with leave to amend[FN1].

 

The Final Status Conference is set for May 13, 2025. Trial is set for May 27, 2025.



Legal Standard 

“[T]he demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that. . . (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[,] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive [and/or that] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)

The motion must include a meet and confer declaration which “state[s] facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).) “In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(3).)

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents must “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) If the moving party has shown good cause for the production of documents, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.)  

Notice of the motion must be provided “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction. . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

Discussion 

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order striking Defendant’s objections and compelling its further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One (i.e., Nos. 9, 23, 24, 27, 28, 29, 30, 41, 42, 45, 46, 51, 52, 53, 54, 80, 81, 109, 119, 120, 129, and 130).

Plaintiff’s counsel NaQuia Brown (“Brown”) represents, and/or the accompanying documents reflect, as follows:

On December 20, 2023, Plaintiff propounded the subject discovery on Defendant (Brown Decl., ¶ 14, Exh. 3); that day, Brown sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel regarding discovery of electronically stored information, creation of a search protocol and institution of a litigation hold (Id., ¶ 24, Exh. 7.) After obtaining a couple of extensions, Defendant provided responses on March 4, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 17-19, Exhs. 4-6.) On March 21, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel Hannah C. Theophil (“Theophil”) sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel, along with a copy of a proposed Stipulation and Protective Order modeled after the Los Angeles Superior Court Protective Order. (Id., ¶¶ 25-26, Exhs. 8 and 9.) On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a “Request for Informal Discovery Conference.” (Id., ¶ 28, Exh. 10.) On or about May 20, 2024, the parties appeared for an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”), but the judge continued the hearing to May 28, 2024 and ordered counsel to meet and confer telephonically in the interim. (Id., ¶ 29.) On May 28, 2024, counsel met and conferred telephonically, during which time Theophil agreed to limit the documents sought to only those of the same year, make and model as the subject vehicle sold in the State of California and the parties agreed to new definitions for the terms “Sensing Defects” and “Brake Defects.” (Id., ¶ 30, Exh. 11.) On May 28, 2024, the Court continued the IDC to May 30, 2024. (Id., ¶ 31.) During the May 30, 2024, IDC, the Court advised that the instant motion could be filed. (Id., ¶ 32.) 

Plaintiff has categorized the requests in issue as follows: (1) Subject Vehicle Discovery (i.e., No. 9); (2) Internal Knowledge & Investigation Discovery (i.e., Nos.

23, 24, 27-30, 41, 42, 45, 46, and 51-54); (3) Summary Documents and Deposition Testimony Regarding These Documents (i.e., Nos. 80 and 81); (4) Policies and Procedures Discovery (i.e., No. 109); (5) Communications with Governmental Agencies and Suppliers (i.e., Nos. 119 and 120); and (6) Other Documents Related to the Subject Vehicle’s Defects (i.e., Nos. 129 and 130). The Court addresses each of these categories in turn. 

 

Subject Vehicle Discovery (i.e., No. 9) 

No. 9 seeks documents regarding records and data transmitted from the subject vehicle to Defendant through the following remote monitoring and connected systems: “a) Vehicle-to-Manufacturer (V2M) communications or its equivalent, including but not limited to performance data, issue reports, and any other related data. b) Remote Vehicle Diagnostic and Maintenance records or its equivalent, including any instances of remote diagnosis or repair. c) Telematics data, including but not limited to data about the vehicle’s location, speed, fuel consumption, crash notification events, and remote lock/unlock operations. d) Connected Car System records, including any data related to the vehicle’s infotainment system or its equivalent. e) Over-the-Air (OTA) Systems update history or its equivalent, including details of software updates, the reasons for those updates, and the results of those updates.” 

The Court agrees that the request is vague, ambiguous, and overly broad, and seeks irrelevant information unsupported by a showing of good cause. No further response is warranted. 

 

Internal Knowledge & Investigation Discovery (i.e., Nos. 23, 24, 27-30, 41, 42, 45, 46 and 51-54) 

Nos. 23 and 24 seek documents regarding any internal investigation regarding root cause problems, problem-solving efforts, or efforts to identify any actual or potential problems, failures, malfunctions, conditions, and/or defects, regarding the braking system or sensing system[FN2] in Honda Civic vehicles.[FN3]

Nos. 27 and 28 seek documents concerning field reports, dealer contacts, warranty claims, customer complaints, claims, and/or reported failures regarding brake defects[FN4] or sensing defects[FN5] in Honda Civic vehicles.

Nos. 29 and 30 seek documents regarding the brake defects or sensing defects in Honda Civic vehicles equipped with the same braking system or sensing system as the subject vehicle.

Nos. 41 and 42 seek documents to/from Defendant’s agents, representatives, engineers, employees, or part suppliers, concerning common parts failures for the braking system or sensing system in Honda Civic vehicles.

Nos. 45 and 46 seek documents from Defendant’s engineers or suppliers with information relating to suggested repair procedures for commonly observed problems regarding brake defects or sensing defects in Honda Civic vehicles.

Nos. 51 and 52 seek documents regarding Honda Civic vehicles that were repurchased or replaced by Defendant and (1) underwent two or more warranty repair attempts; or (2) were unable to be repaired within 30 days for concerns regarding the brake defects or sensing defects.

Nos. 53 and 54 seek all vehicle warranty history reports for Honda Civic vehicles that Defendant repurchased or replaced with two or more warranty repair attempts to the braking system or sensing system.

At the outset, the Court agrees that the definitions of “sensing defect(s),” “Honda sensing system,” “brake defect(s),” and “Honda Civic vehicles” are impermissibly overbroad. As set forth above, Theophil has since offered to limit the documents Plaintiff seeks to 2021 Honda Civic vehicles sold in the State of California, to refine the term “sensing defect(s)” to mean “(1) malfunctioning warning message(s) to intermittently appear; or (2) vehicles applying brakes although no obstruction is present,” and to redefine the term “brake defect(s)” to mean “squealing, grinding noises, and/or vibration when braking.” (Brown Decl., ¶ 30, Exh. 11.)

The Court will limit the above definitions to the same conditions, defects, or nonconformities which Plaintiffs submitted the subject vehicle to Defendant or Defendant’s authorized facilities for repair on or about April 25, 2022 and on or about August 29, 2022. (Id., ¶¶ 4-6, Exh. 1.) Additionally, every category shall be limited to documents pertaining to vehicles of the same year, make, model, and engine type as Plaintiffs’ vehicle, for vehicles in California. The Song-Beverly Act and its lemon law provisions limits its application to goods sold in California and has certain provisions that afford protections greater than the requirements of the federal Magnuson Moss Act. (See Civil Code §§ 1792, 1792.1; Atkinson v. Elk Corp of Texas (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 212, 231.) As the statute’s application to lemon law is for vehicles “sold in this state,” such phrase is interpreted to restricting the scope to goods sold in California. (California State Electronic Ass’n v. Zeos Int’l Ltd. (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1277.) 

The Court agrees that Defendant properly objected to Plaintiff’s “root cause” discovery (i.e., Nos. 23 and 24) on the basis of relevance. Plaintiff is not required to show what caused a nonconformity. (See Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1102, fn. 8 [“Isuzu also argues it was incumbent on Oregel to prove not only that the car leaked oil but also to show the cause of the leak, and that he failed to meet this burden because he produced no expert testimony proving the cause of the leak. However, the statute requires only that Oregel prove the car did not conform to the express warranty, and proof that there was a persistent leak that Ron Baker could not locate or repair suffices. We do not interpret the statute as depriving a consumer of a remedy if he cannot do what the manufacturer, with its presumably greater expertise, was incapable of doing, i.e. identify the source of the leak”].) No further responses are warranted to Nos. 23 and 24.

 

The Court determines Plaintiff has otherwise established the general relevance of the information sought because the documents, as limited above, are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence regarding whether Defendant knew of the defects at issue, yet failed to repurchase the subject vehicle. In Donlen v. Ford Motor Company (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 143, the appellate court upheld a trial court’s determination that evidence of “the transmission model Ford installed in plaintiff’s truck and other vehicles” should not be excluded from trial as prejudicial in a Song–Beverly Act case brought by a single plaintiff.  (Id. at 154.) Donlen thus provides a basis for permitting discovery to extend beyond Plaintiff’s specific vehicle. A defendant’s knowledge of the defect is relevant to whether Defendant willfully violated the statute.  (Jensen v. BMW of North America, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 136; Kwan v. Mercedes-Benz of North America, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 174, 186 [“A decision made without the use of reasonably available information germane to that decision is not a reasonable, good faith decision.”].)

Defendant also claims, with respect to Nos. 27-29, 41, 42, 45, 46, and 51-54, that documents Plaintiff is seeking “may call for information that is confidential, commercially sensitive, proprietary, and/or trade secret.” Objections based on confidentiality are not proper grounds for withholding responsive information. (See Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 23 [“We know of no case holding that this is a proper ground for objection to an otherwise proper interrogatory. Assuming that the information called for by this interrogatory is of a confidential nature which defendants do not want to have included in a public record, they presumably could have applied for a protective order. . .”].) Defendant, however, has not produced any admissible evidentiary materials showing that any of the documents contain protectable “confidential, commercially sensitive, proprietary, and/or trade secret” information; at any rate, the Court believes that any such concerns may be adequately safeguarded by the protective order and/or redaction of personally identifiable information. 

Defendant claims, with respect to Nos. 27-29, 41, 42, 45, 46, and 51-54, that Plaintiff seeks information “protected by the right to privacy.” Again, the Court believes that any such concerns may be adequately safeguarded by the protective order and/or redaction of personally identifiable information.

Finally, although Defendant objected on the basis of the attorney-client privilege and/or work-product doctrine as to Nos. 51 and 52, Defendant has not met its burden of justifying this objection. Any documents withheld from production on this basis must be accompanied by a privilege log.

The Court determines that further responses are warranted as to 27-30, 41, 42, 45, 46, and 51-54, as limited.

 

Summary Documents and Deposition Testimony Regarding These Documents (i.e., Nos. 80 and 81)

Nos. 80 and 81 seek documents regarding software release notes for all software released for the brake defects or sensing defects in Honda Civic vehicles equipped with the same braking system or sensing system as the subject vehicle.

The Court determines that further responses are warranted as to Nos. 80 and 81, subject to the limitations set forth above.

 

Policies and Procedures Discovery (i.e., No. 109)

No. 109 seeks the user manual for every database where any of the following information is stored: warranty records, customer complaints, field reports, technician line contact, field service reports, and dealer contacts.

Defendant properly objected to No. 109 as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, irrelevant, and seeking “discovery on discovery.” No further response is warranted.

 

Communications with Governmental Agencies and Suppliers (i.e., Nos. 119 and 120)

Nos. 119 and 120 seek communications with and documents provided to or received from the supplier of the braking system or sensing system in Honda Civic vehicles regarding an actual or suspected braking system or sensing system condition, issue, problem, or defect.

The Court determines that further responses are warranted as to Nos. 119 and 120, subject to the limitations set forth above.

 

Other Documents Related to the Subject Vehicle’s Defects (i.e., Nos. 129 and 130)

Nos. 129 and 130 seek documents concerning performance standards relating to the braking system or sensing system in Honda Civic vehicles.

 

The Court determines that further responses are warranted as to Nos. 129 and 130, subject to the limitations set forth above.

 

Conclusion

The motion is denied in part (i.e., as to Nos. 9, 23, 24 and 109) and granted in part (i.e., as to Nos. 27-30, 41, 42, 45, 46, 51-54, 80, 81, 119, 120, 129 and 130, as limited). Defendant is ordered to produce verified, code-compliant responses within 20 days, subject to the limitations contained herein.
