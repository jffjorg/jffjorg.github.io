DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01611    Hearing Date: April 11, 2024    Dept: W

almas & son, llc v. nai capital, inc.

 

DEMURRER to the second amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        April 11, 2024                         Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        19VECV01611

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Patrick Ortiz and Nai Capital Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Almas & Sons LLC    

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (McGarrigle Decl. ¶¶6.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Almas & Son, LLC alleges it entered into an agreement with Defendants Nai Capital, Inc. and Patrick Ortiz where Defendants were to find Plaintiff a suitable tenant for their commercial building. Plaintiff alleges Defendants represented to Plaintiff they found a tenant who would run a legal smoke shop and ran a background check on the tenant; however, these representations were false. Plaintiff alleges Defendants did not run a background check and the tenant operated a cannabis shop in violation of the agreement and as prohibited by the Department of Cannabis Regulation.

 

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint alleging: 1) Breach of Contract; 2) Fraud and Deceit; 3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 4) Violation of Business and Professions Code 17200; and 5) Negligence.  On December 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint.

 

On February 7, 2020, Defendant NAI Capital, Inc. filed Notice of Bankruptcy and Automatic Stay with the court. Matter is only stayed as to Defendant NAI Capital, Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, in part, OVERRULED, in part.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Patrick Ortiz, joined by Nai Capital, Inc. demurs to the Second Amended Complaint on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not on the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.) However, “[i]f facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Mead v. Samoa Bank California (Mead) (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 561, 567-568.) Before filing a demurrer, demurring party is also required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See CCP§ 430.41.) 

 

Express Terms of the Lease Agreement

 

Defendants demur to the SAC on the grounds Plaintiff’s allegations are expressly contradicted by the terms of the Lease entered into by Plaintiff.

 

Defendants first argue the Lease expressly contradicts Plaintiff’s allegations regarding any alleged pre-Lease statements of Defendant regarding the Tenant’s nature, quality, character and financial condition, use and future performance. (See SAC, Exh. 3, ¶¶22, 25, 28, 46.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues they have sufficiently alleged Defendant’s breach of the Lease agreement when Plaintiff specifically asked Ortiz to run a background check, Ortiz informed Plaintiff he followed standard protocol, Plaintiff had communicated to Defendant that it wished the premises to be operated as a legal smoke shop only with proper and required license, and Defendants violated statutory obligations imposed by Civil Code section 2079.2, Business and Professions Code section 10176, Business and Professions Code section 10080, Civil Code section 2079(b), and Business and Professions Code section 10010.5(a)(l) and (2). (See SAC ¶¶11-14, 23.) Plaintiff further argues any attempt to exculpate the agent from liability would be unenforceable and Defendants’ violation of statutory duties cannot be the subject of advance release or waiver.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged a breach of contract claim. What Plaintiff cites as breaches are not express terms of the Lease agreement. Moreover, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged consideration for either breach of express contract or implied contract. Plaintiff has not shown how Defendants’ representation they ran a background check or did a site inspection is a bargained-for exchange. The Lease does refer to the agreed use as a ‘Smoke Shop (tenant not to sell medical marijuana or CBD Products).” However, Plaintiff does not explain how that is Defendants’ breach. Moreover, as noted by Defendants, the Lease provides “The Lease contains all agreements between the Parties with respect to any matter mentioned herein, and no other prior or contemporaneous agreement or understanding shall be effective.” (Lease ¶ 22.) All alleged breaches were apparently made prior to or after the agreement was made.

 

As for Defendants’ contention that the other provisions of the Lease prohibit Plaintiff’s fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, violation of Business Code and Professions section 17200, or negligence claim, the court disagrees. The portions cited to by Defendants in the Lease agreement do not waive or release nor under Civil Code section 1668 could it waive and release Plaintiff’s fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, or violation of Section 17200 claims. The court notes In Bank of America v. Vannini (1956) 140 Cal.App.2d 120, 129 held where there is duty to investigate, plaintiff may be charged with knowledge of facts which would have been disclosed by an investigation. However, “[a] defrauded person … is not barred from maintaining an action merely because he commenced an investigation if it was incomplete or abandoned before discovery of the falsity, particularly if the defendant has a superior knowledge of the facts, or if it is difficult for the plaintiff to ascertain all the facts or he is not competent to judge the facts without expert assistance.” (Hobart v. Hobart Estate Co. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 412, 435.) Although Paragraph 22 provides “Lessor … represents and warrants to the Brokers that it has made, and is relying solely upon, its own Investigation as to the nature, quality, character and financial responsibility of the other Party to this Lease and as to the use, nature, quality and character of the Premises”, a jury may find running the background check and criminal check are something Defendants would have superior knowledge of.

 

Defendants also argue beyond the Lease’s express prohibition of Plaintiff’s claims, Mr. Ortiz is not liable for any opinions (cast by Plaintiff as representations) regarding the future performance, suitability and viability of the tenant under the Lease. The court finds the allegations are not opinions about quality, value, profit and future events are not actionable as a matter of law. Instead, they are representations that the tenant had a 700 credit score, passed the background check, and personally inspected the site.

 

Defendants next argue Plaintiff cannot establish the required element of justifiable reliance or causation with regard to any of its claims and moreover, Plaintiff’s shotgun listing of statutes does not change the result or cure the defects. As noted above, Plaintiff has alleged justifiable reliance as running background checks and credit scores were something within Defendants’ superior knowledge. The court has also previously found the allegations sufficiently alleged causation/damages for the purposes of a demurrer. (See November 16, 2023 Minute Order.)

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendants separately demur to the breach of contract claim. As noted above, the court agrees Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to support a breach of contract claim against Defendants. Plaintiff has not shown a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSATINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. The demurrer to the remainder causes of action is OVERRULED.


Case Number: 22VECV00777    Hearing Date: April 11, 2024    Dept: W

VEDRES FAMILY INVESTMENT PARTNERSHIP, LP v. PAYAM MAROUNI MD, INC.

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        April 11, 2024                                     Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00777

 

Moving Party:             Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP

Responding Party:     Cross-Complainant Payam Marouni MD, Inc.  

Meet and Confer:      Yes.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 8, 2022, Plaintiff Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP filed an unlawful detainer action against Defendant Payam Marouni MD, Inc. Plaintiff’s request for entry of default was entered against All Unknown Occupants in Possession on August 3, 2022. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint naming Defendant Payam Marouni on October 31, 2022; however, Defendant Marouni was dismissed on February 22, 2023.

 

On November 15, 2023, Defendant Payam Marouni MD, Inc. filed a second amended cross-complaint against Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP and Quest Screen for (1) Conspiracy to Interfere with Prospective Economic Advantage (2) Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations and (3) Negligent Interference with Contractual Relations.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, L.P.’s Demurrer to the Second Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, L.P. demurs to the Second Amended Cross-Complaint of Payam Marouni, M.D., Inc. on the grounds the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action.

 

First Cause of Action

 

Cross-Defendant argues the first cause of action fails for two reasons: Cross-Complainant has failed to allege an economic relationship with a cognizable third party that Cross-Defendant interfered with; and Cross-Complainant has failed to allege an independent wrong.

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage in California, a plaintiff must plead and prove (1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant's knowledge of the relationship; (3) the defendant's intentional acts designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the defendant's acts.” (Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1152 fn.6, italics added.)  

 

Cross-Defendant claims Cross-Complainant’s allegations fail to assert a tangible, existing relationship with ascertainable third parties beyond a general class of consumers who might be interested in such tests. Moreover, Cross-Defendant argues Cross-Complainant fails to allege why or how leasing a space it owned to an adjacent tenant is an independent legal wrong.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainant argues the SACC sufficiently identifies the relationship between Cross-Complainant, Cross-Defendant and Quest. Cross-Complainant asserts Cross-Defendants sought to conspire together to hurt Cross-Complainant by allowing Quest to perform COVID-19 testing in the same vicinity as Cross-Complainant who was already, and visibly, performing such services by allowing Quest to erect a tent using the same signage and information from Cross-Complainant’s tent in the parking lot behind the Subject Property to confuse Cross-Complainant’s patients.

 

Civil conspiracy is not an independent tort. (See City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 191, 211.) As previously noted, allegations of conspiracy must be activated by the commission of an actual tort. (Favila v. Katten Muchin Rosenmann LLP (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 189, 206.) As such, Plaintiff needs to allege more than the formation and operation of a conspiracy. Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. Intentional interference with prospective economic advantage requires a relationship between Cross-Complainant and some third party. Allegations regarding interference with potential customers are insufficient to state a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. (See Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1164 (stating that the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage “‘protects the expectation that the relationship eventually will yield the desired benefit, not necessarily the more speculative expectation that a potentially beneficial relationship will arise” (quoting Westside Center Associates v. Safeway Stores 23, Inc. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 507, 524); Roth v. Rhodes (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 530, 546 (finding that an allegation that the defendant interfered with relations with future customers is insufficient to show an existing relationship).)   Moreover, intentional interference with prospective economic advantage requires an independent wrong – one that is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard. (Korea Supply Co., supra, 29 Cal.4th at 1159.) Cross-Complainant has not shown leasing a space is an independent wrong. Even if leasing to another was a breach of the agreement between Cross-Complainant and Cross-Defendant, “an actor's breach of contract, without more, is not ‘wrongful conduct’ capable of supporting a tort[.]” (Drink Tank Ventures LLC v. Real Soda in Real Bottles, Ltd. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 528, 533.)

 

Cross-Complainant has not demonstrated they can there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Second Cause of Action

 

Cross-Defendant demurs to the second cause of action fails because Cross-Complainant has not alleged an economic relationship with a third party or wrongful act committed by a third party.

 

“[T]he tort of interference with contract is merely a species of the broader tort of interference with prospective economic advantage. [Citations.]” (Buckaloo v. Johnson (1975) 14 Cal.3d 815, 823.) To the extent they are distinct, the former refers to disruption of an existing contract, while the latter refers to disruption of a possible future relationship. (Della Penna v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A. Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 376, 392.) “[B]oth … torts protect the public interest in stable economic relationships and both share the same intent requirement.” (Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1152.) 

 

Cross-Defendant argues the only contract alleged in the cross-complaint is the lease between the Cross-Complainant and Cross-Defendant. Accordingly, the cross-complaint omits the most critical element of a claim for contractual interference: a contract which the Cross-Defendant interfered.

 

The court agrees. Intentional interference with contractual relations requires the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party. Cross-Complainant has only alleged a contractual relation with the Cross-Defendant.

 

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Third Cause of Action

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress on the grounds no cause of action exists in California. As previously noted, “[i]n California there is no cause of action for negligent interference with contractual relations. While there exists a cause of action for negligent interference with prospective economic advantage (J'Aire Corp. v. Gregory (1979) 24 Cal.3d 799, 157 Cal.Rptr. 407, 598 P.2d 60), the California Supreme Court in Fifield Manor v. Finston (1960) 54 Cal.2d 632, 7 Cal.Rptr. 377, 354 P.2d 1073, has rejected a cause of action for negligent interference with contract.” (Davis v. Nadrich (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1, 9.)

 

If Cross-Complainant wishes to add a negligent interference with prospective economic advantage, Cross-Complainant must seek leave to do so.


Case Number: 22VECV01907    Hearing Date: April 11, 2024    Dept: W

LEON BARNETT HALL v. PAULINA CRUZ, et al.

 

plaintiff’s MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE AGAINST DEFENDANT ACCESS SERVICES UNDER C.C.P. SECTIONS 2030.290 ET. SEQ. AND REQUEST FOR COSTS, SANCTIONS, AND ATTORNEYS FEES UNDER C.C.P. §§ 2023.010 ET SEQ. AND 2030.290 ET SEQ. IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,636.65

 

Date of Hearing:        April 11, 2024                                    Trial Date:       October 14, 2024 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01907

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Leon Barnett Hall   

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This matter arises out of an automobile accident that occurred on May 2, 2022. Plaintiff alleges they were injured as a result of Defendant Paulina Cruz’ negligence. Plaintiff filed his complaint on November 8, 2022 against Paulina Cruz, Access Services, and MV Transportation, Inc.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One Against Defendant Access Services Under C.C.P. Sections 2030.290 et. seq. and Request for Costs, Sanctions, and Attorney Fees under C.C.P. §§2030.010 et seq. and 2030.290 et. seq. is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Leon Hall moves to compel Defendant Access Services to serve discovery responses, without objections, to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and/or its attorney of record. 

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff propounded their discovery requests including Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One. (Elechyan Decl. ¶5, Exhs. 1, 2.) Responses were due by February 16, 2024. (Elechyan Decl. ¶5.) After no response, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer regarding the overdue discovery responses. (Elechyan Decl. ¶6, Exh. 3.) As of the filing of the instant motion, Plaintiff has still not received any responses to their discovery requests. (Elechyan Decl. ¶7.)

 

The court finds Defendant Access Service has failed to timely respond to Plaintiff’s properly propounded discovery. Plaintiff’s unopposed motion to compel Defendant Service Access’ responses to form interrogatories, set one is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide responses without objection within 20 days.

 

Sanctions

 

The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. (CCP §2023.030.)

 

Plaintiff requests $1,636.65 for 1.5 hours preparing the motion, 2.0 hours reviewing any opposition and preparing a reply, and 1 hour for court appearance at counsel’s hourly rate of $350. (Elechyan Decl. ¶¶12-13.) The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $875.00 per motion.
