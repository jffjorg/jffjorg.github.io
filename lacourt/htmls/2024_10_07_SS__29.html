DEPARTMENT 29 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 29 - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS IMPORTANT  (PLEASE SEND YOUR E-MAIL TO DEPT. 29 NOT DEPT. 2)

Communicating with the Court Staff re the Tentative Ruling 1. Please notify the courtroom staff by email not later than 9:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion. The email address is SSCDEPT29@lacourt.org. Please do not use any other email address. 2. You must include the other parties on the email by "cc." 3. Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the Subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. THE COURT WILL HEAR ARGUMENT UNLESS BOTH SIDES SUBMIT ON THE TENTATIVE.  4. Include the words "SUBMISSION BUT WILL APPEAR" if you submit, but one or both parties will nevertheless appear. 5. For other communications with Court Staff a. OFF-CALENDAR should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed to have a matter placed off-calendar. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) date of proceeding. b. CASE SETTLED should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed that the case has settled for all purposes. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) whether notice of settlement/dismissal documents have been filed; (c) if (b) has not been done, a date one year from the date of your email which will be a date set by the court for an OSC for dismissal of the case. c. STIPULATION should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have stipulated that a matter before the court can be postponed. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) what proceeding is agreed to be postponed e.g. Trial, FSC; (c) the agreed-upon future date; (d) whether all parties waive notice if the Court informs all counsel/parties that the agreed-upon date is satisfactory. This communication should be used only for matters that are agreed to be postponed and not for orders shortening time. 6. PLEASE MAKE SURE THAT ALL COMMUNICATIONS WITH COURT STAFF DEAL ONLY WITH SCHEDULING AND ADMINISTRATIVE MATTERS AND DO NOT DISCUSS THE MERITS OF ANY CASE. (UPDATED 6/17/2020) 
IMPORTANT:  In light of the COVID-19 emergency, the Court encourages all parties to appear remotely.  The capacity in the courtroom is extremely limited.  The Court appreciates the cooperation of counsel and the litigants. 
ALSO NOTE:  If the moving party does not contact the court to submit on the tentative and does not appear (either remotely or in person), the motion will be taken off calendar.  THE TENTATIVE RULING WILL NOT BE THE ORDER OF THE COURT.

Stern v. Vaughn
21STCV26220
Plaintiff’s Motion for Relief from Waiver of Jury Trial

Tentative

The motion is granted.

Background

On July 15, 2021, Dale Stern (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Nathaniel Vaughn (“Vaughn”), Gale Gordon (“Gordon”), Doordash Inc. (“Doordash”), and Does 1 through 20 for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence arising out of an accident occurring on March 30, 2021.

On October 29, 2021, Doordash filed an answer.  On August 29, 2022, Vaughn and Gordon filed an answer and demand for jury trial.

On December 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed and served a notice of posting jury fees.

On February 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).  In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges (among other things): (1) that Vaughn was the driver of the vehicle that struck Plaintiff; (2) that Gordon was the owner of the vehicle; (3) that Vaughn was an employee of Doordash acting within the scope of his employment, making Doordash vicariously liable for Vaughn’s negligence; and (4) in the alternative, if Vaughn was an independent contractor, Doordash was negligent in failing to provide training to Vaughn.

On April 10, 2023, Doordash filed an answer to the FAC.  On the same day, Vaughn and Gordon filed an answer to the FAC and demand for jury trial.

On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for an order granting relief from potential waiver of jury trial. On September 23, 2024, Doordash filed an opposition, and on the same day Vaughn and Gordon filed an opposition. Plaintiff filed a reply on September 30.

 

Trial is set for October 14, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Trial by jury is an inviolate right and shall be secured to all.” (Cal. Const., art. I, § 16.) “In a civil cause a jury may be waived by the consent of the parties expressed as prescribed by statute.” (Ibid.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 631 is the primary statute addressing waiver of a jury trial in a civil case. Under section 631, subdivision(f)(5), a party waives their right to a jury trial by not timely posting jury fees. Jury fees must be paid “on or before the date scheduled for the initial case management conference in the action”; where there is no case management conference, the fees are due “no later than 365 calendar days after the filing of the initial complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 631, subd. (c) & (c)(2).)

 

“The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.” (Id., subd. (g).)

 

The California Supreme Court recently addressed the exercise of this discretion in TriCoast Builders, Inc. v. Fonnegra (2024) 15 Cal.5th 766. In ruling on a request for relief from waiver of jury trial:

 

“[T]he primary consideration is … whether granting relief from waiver would result in any hardship to other parties or to the court, such as delay in rescheduling the trial for a jury or inconvenience to witnesses. But courts have also regularly considered other factors, including the timeliness of the request; whether the requester is willing to comply with applicable requirements for payment of jury fees; and the reasons supporting the request.” (15 Cal.5th at pp. 779-780.)

 

“[T]he presence or absence of hardship is not always dispositive,” and the court may, in its discretion, also consider (among other things) whether the motion for relief from waiver “simply reflects a belated change of heart about trial tactics” or is being used as a “pretext” to obtain a continuance. (Id., at p. 780.) But when a party “has timely given notice that it desires trial by jury” but then loses that right by nonpayment of fees, “lack of hardship to the other parties or the court is generally controlling, absent other factors that weigh against relief.” (Id., at p. 782.)

 

In ruling on request from relief from waiver under section 631, subdivision (g), “courts are mindful of the requirement ‘to resolve doubts in interpreting the waiver provisions of section 631 in favor of a litigant's right to jury trial.’” (Tesoro del Valle Master Homeowners Assn. v. Griffin (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 619, 638, quoting Grafton Partners v. Superior Court (2005) 36 Cal.4th 944, 956.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff’s counsel states that Plaintiff inadvertently failed to post fees and demand a jury trial in a timely manner. (De Simone Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5, & 7.) Plaintiff posted jury fees and filed and served a notice of posting jury fees on December 28, 2022.  Although the posting of fees was untimely by approximately five months, there is no indication that this was a deliberate tactical decision, that Plaintiff is having a belated change of heart, or that this is a pretext for a continuance. Inadvertence is a factor that weighs in support of granting the motion.

 

Although the presence or absence of prejudice is not independently dispositive, the Court notes that Defendants Vaughn and Gordon have twice filed answers with demands for jury trial; that all Defendants have been on notice for approximately 22 months that Plaintiff posted jury fees; and that all parties have been preparing pre-trial documents for a jury trial.  Under these circumstances, the Court does not credit the statements of Defendants’ counsel that they have not been preparing for at least the possibility that the case would be tried to a jury.  The Court finds that granting this relief would not cause any undue or unfair prejudice to Defendants.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff did not timely post jury fees; that in doing so, Plaintiff waived the right to jury trial; that this waiver was inadvertent; and that no party would suffer any undue or unfair prejudice from a court order granting relief from waiver.

 

After considering all of the evidence in the record, the Court exercises its discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 631, subdivision (g), to grant relief from waiver and to allow a trial by jury.

 

In the alternative, Doordash requests a continuance of the trial date.  The Court notes that Doordash filed a motion to continue trial that is set for hearing on October 10, 2024.  As it relates to this motion (only), the Court denies Doordash’s alternative request for a trial continuance; Doordash has not shown good cause for a trial continuance in connection with Plaintiff’s motion for relief from waiver.  The Court expresses no views at this time on the pending motion filed by Doordash for a continuance on other grounds; the denial of the continuance request in Doordash’s opposition to this motion is without prejudice as to the motion to continue set for hearing on October 10.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for relief from waiver of jury trial.

 

Moving party is ORDERED to give notice.

Case Number: 23STCV00599    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: 29

Christian v. City of Los Angeles
23STCV00599
Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment

Tentative

The motion for Summary Judgment filed by Defendants Cara A. Bartlett and Blake Bartlett is granted.

Background

On January 11, 2023, Bridget Christian (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles (“City”) and Does 1 through 100 for premises liability and general negligence arising from a trip and fall occurring on October 15, 2022, on a sidewalk in front of 2630 North Vermont Avenue.

On February 16, 2023, City filed an answer.

On March 30, 2023, Plaintiff amended the complaint to name Blake Bartlett and Cara Bartlett (collectively “the Bartletts”) as Does 51 and 52.

On June 27, 2023, the Bartletts filed an answer.

On May 20, 2024, the Bartletts filed this motion for summary judgment.

Plaintiff filed an opposition and a request for judicial notice on August 13.

The Bartletts filed a reply (and then a corrected reply), along with objections to some of Plaintiff’s evidence, on August 29.

The motion came on for hearing on September 6, 2024. At the close of the hearing, the Court took the matter under submission.

By Minute Order dated September 12, 2024, the Court vacated the submission and set the motion for further briefing on one issue and for further hearing (initially October 3, and then continued to October 7).

Plaintiff filed her supplemental brief on September 26. The Bartletts filed their supplemental brief on September 30. 

Legal Standard

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

As to each cause of action as framed by the complaint, a defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action ... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 850-851; Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 850-851.)

A plaintiff or cross-complainant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to show “that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shift to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

The party opposing a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not simply “rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings” but must instead “set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (p)(1) & (p)(2). To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of California Streets and Highways Code, section 5610 and Los Angeles Municipal Code section 62.104. This request is GRANTED.

Objections to Evidence

The Bartletts raise 7 objections to Plaintiff’s evidence.

Evidence presented in support of, or in opposition to, a motion for summary judgment must be admissible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (d); Perry v. Bakewell Hawthorne LLC (2017) 2 Cal.5th 536, 541-43.) The court must “consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

The Court SUSTAINS Objection No. 6, to paragraph 10 of the Declaration of Phillip Rosescu, as it contains nothing more than legal contentions, which are not the proper subject of expert testimony.

The Court SUSTAINS Objection No. 7, to the City’s interrogatory responses, as discovery responses may be used only against the responding party, and the responses contain nothing more than legal contentions, not facts.  

The Court OVERRULES the other objections.

Discussion

The facts of the accident are largely undisputed for purposes of this motion. The accident occurred on October 15, 2022, on the sidewalk in front of 2630 North Vermont Avenue in Los Angeles. (Defendant’s Statement of Undisputed Material Facts [“DSUMF”], No. 1.) The accident happened at night, and there was a dangerous height differential between the sidewalk slabs that was measured to be between 1¾ and 2¼ inches. (Plaintiff’s Statement of Additional Material Facts [“PSAMF”], Nos. 17-19.) Plaintiff rolled her ankle (according to her deposition testimony) or tripped and fell (according to the complaint) and suffered injuries. (PSAMF, No. 33; Complaint, at p. 4.)

The Bartletts own the abutting private property at 2630 North Vermont Avenue. (DSUMF, No. 3.) The sidewalk is located on a public easement/right of way held by City. (DSUMF, Nos. 2, 5.)

There is evidence that the dangerous condition had existed since April 2009. (PSAMF, No. 22, 32; Rosescu Decl., ¶ 11.) The Bartletts have owned the property abutting the sidewalk since July 31, 2020. (PSAMF, No. 14.)

Plaintiff alleges a single cause of action for negligence against the Bartletts. (This is the Second Cause of Action in the complaint. The First Cause of Action, for premises liability, is asserted only against City and Does 1 through 50, and the Bartletts were named as Does 51 and 52.)

The basic elements of a cause of action for negligence are: (1) the existence of a legal duty; (2) breach of that duty; (3) causation; and (4) resulting damages. (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 213; Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158; Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) The existence and scope of duty are legal questions for the court.¿¿(Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 213; Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 36.)

The general rule governing duty is set forth in Civil Code section 1714: “Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.” (Civ. Code, § 1714, subd. (a).) This establishes what the California Supreme Court has described as the “default rule” that every person has a legal duty “to exercise, in his or her activities, reasonable care for the safety of others.” (Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 214.)

Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm. (Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.) If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for their [customers’] use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.” (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)

The law regarding the liability of landowners with regard to defects or dangerous conditions on sidewalks abutting their property is well established in numerous appellate cases. “Under the common law, a landowner does not have any duty to repair abutting sidewalks along a public street, and does not owe any duty to pedestrians injured as a result of a defect in the sidewalks.” (Jordan v. City of Sacramento (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1490 (citing Schaefer v. Lenahan (1944) 63 Cal.App.2d 324, 325).) By statute, specifically Streets and Highways Code section 5610, abutting property owners have an obligation to repair defects in the sidewalk, regardless of whether they created the defects. (See Jones v. Deeter (1984) 152 Cal.App.3d 798, 803.) Nonetheless, the so-called “Sidewalk Accident Doctrine” provides that abutting property owners have no duty to members of the public in tort law, and are not liable to members of the public, unless the property owner created the defect or exercised dominion or control over the abutting sidewalk. (Lopez v. City of Los Angeles (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 244, 255; Jordan, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1490-91 [Section 5610 “imposes a duty of repair on the abutting property owners for defects in sidewalks, regardless of who created the defects, but does not of itself create tort liability to injured pedestrians or a duty to indemnify municipalities, except where a property owner created the defect or exercised dominion or control over the abutting sidewalk”]; see also Contreras v. Anderson (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 188, 196; Jones, supra, 152 Cal.App.3d at pp. 802-803.) 

Thus, for more than 150 years the general rule has been that “in the absence of a statute, a landowner is under no duty to maintain in a safe condition a public street or sidewalk abutting his property.” (Lopez, supra, 55 Cal.App.5th at p. 255.) “[A] person’s ownership or occupancy of property, without more, is insufficient to impose a duty to maintain abutting, publicly owned property. (Id. at p. 256; see also Isaacs v. Huntington Memorial Hospital (1985) 38 Cal.3d 112, 134 [“A defendant cannot be held liable for the defective or dangerous condition of property which it did not own, possess, or control.”]; Williams v. Foster (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 510, 521 [“we are unwilling to find the duty to maintain the sidewalk established by section 5610 is owed to members of the public in the absence of clear and unambiguous legislative language, especially in view of the long-standing judicial determination that abutters ordinarily have no such duty”].)

This general rule of no duty has an important exception: when the abutting property owner has “exercised control” over the otherwise public property. (Lopez, supra, 55 Cal.App.5th at p. 255.) For this exception to apply, the abutting owner must take some affirmative action regarding the public property, such as when the abutting owner has created the hazard or asserted “dominion and control over the … publicly owned property by effectively treating the property as its own.” (Id. at p. 256.)

Here, the Bartletts argue essentially (1) the accident occurred on a sidewalk that was the City’s public property; (2) as the abutting property owner, they had no duty to Plaintiff or other members of the public to maintain the sidewalk in a safe condition or to warn of the dangerous condition (or at least no such duty that can be enforced through private tort litigation); and (3) they did not cause or contribute to the dangerous condition or exercise control over the sidewalk where the accident occurred.

On the first point, the evidence is undisputed. (DSUMF, No. 2.)

On the second point, the Bartletts are correct on the law. Under the Sidewalk Accident Doctrine, they owed no duty to Plaintiff or other members of the public – unless they caused or contributed to the dangerous condition or exercised control over the sidewalk. To the extent that Plaintiff presents arguments to the contrary (contending, for example, that the Bartletts had a duty, and breached their duty, to Plaintiff to maintain the sidewalk, to make it safe, or to warn of the dangerous condition), those arguments lack merit.

The third point, however, requires a more extended discussion.

On this third point, the Bartletts met their initial burden, as defendants moving for summary judgment, of presenting evidence to show that they did not cause or contribute to the dangerous condition or exercise control over the sidewalk. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The Bartletts showed that from the time that they acquired the property in 2020 to the date of the accident, they did not exclude members of the public from the sidewalk; they did not exercise control over the sidewalk; they did not perform any construction work on or affecting the sidewalk; and they did not otherwise damage, modify, or change the condition of the sidewalk. (DSUMF, Nos. 4, 8.) The Bartletts further presented evidence that the uplift in the sidewalk was caused by root growth from a tree located on public property in front of the Roosevelt Municipal Golf Course. (DSUMF, Nos. 10, 12; Stivers Decl., ¶¶ 2, 5, & 10.)

This initial showing by the moving parties shifts the burden on summary judgment to Plaintiff to present evidence to show that there are triable, disputed issues as to one or more material facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) 

Plaintiff has done so. Plaintiff has presented evidence that the Bartletts entered and exited their property in vehicles using the driveway, and in doing so they naturally crossed the sidewalk. (PSAMF, No. 26; Blake Bartlett Depo., at 19:4-23.) Plaintiff further presented expert testimony that the concrete of sidewalks becomes weak when it has voids beneath it from an offset, and even ordinary vehicle use of a driveway can cause cracking and exacerbate the displacement of the sidewalk slabs. (PSAMF, Nos. 23-24; Greeley Decl., ¶ 7; Rosescu Decl., ¶ 8.) As a result, even though the dangerous condition existed long before the Bartletts became the owners of the abutting property, Plaintiff argues that there is evidence in the record from which a finder of fact could draw a reasonable inference that the Bartletts’ ordinary use of their driveway contributed – in at least some material degree – to the dangerous condition of the sidewalk.

But the Lopez case, supra, is directly to the contrary. In Lopez, a recent case from the Second District Court of Appeal, the plaintiff “tripped and fell in a pothole located on city-owned property where the lip of a driveway and the gutter meet.” (55 Cal.App.5th at p. 250.) Plaintiff brought suit against the City of Los Angeles and Wally’s Wine & Spirits (“Wally’s”) a private company that leased the abutting property. After the jury returned a verdict against both the City and Wally’s, the trial court granted the motion of Wally’s for judgment notwithstanding the verdict. On appeal, the Second District Court of Appeal framed the issue as follows:

“Has the commercial business leasing the property that the driveway services exercised control over the location of the pothole (so as to create a duty of care to passersby) when the business has done no more than put the driveway and gutter to their ‘ordinary and accustomed’ uses?”

(Ibid.)

Notably, the Court of Appeal stated: “The pothole was caused by deterioration of the asphalt due to regular use of the driveway by vehicles and due to water flowing in the gutter.” (Id., at p. 251.) Thus, there was no question in Lopez that the use of the driveway and the gutter by the owner of the private property abutting the street had caused or contributed to the creation of the dangerous condition (the pothole) that led to plaintiff’s injury.

Nonetheless, after reviewing in a comprehensive manner the law relating to the duty of care owed by one who owns or occupies abutting property, the Court of Appeal affirmed the ruling of the trial court. The Court based this ruling, in part, on the absence of any evidence that Wally’s had used the driveway and the gutter for anything other than their “ordinary and accustomed use.” (Id. at pp. 259, 261.) In the absence of such evidence, the Court held that Wally’s owed no duty of care to the plaintiff or other members of the public with regard to the pothole on city-owned property.

Thus, under the Sidewalk Accident Doctrine as interpreted by Lopez, the Bartletts’ ordinary use of their driveway (including crossing the sidewalk with their vehicles as they entered and exited their private, abutting property ) does not constitute “control” of the sidewalk sufficient to impose negligence liability against them.

In their opposition, and in the supplemental brief, Plaintiff argues that the Bartletts’ use of the driveway (including the sidewalk near to where the accident occurred) exceeded the standard for ordinary use; in particular, Plaintiff argues that because of the shape of the driveway, vehicles entering the property likely had to pull forward and then back up several times to make the sharp turn required to travel from the street to the garage (and the same would presumably be true, in reverse, for vehicles exiting the property). (See Rosecue Decl., ¶ 8, Fig. 3, and Photograph 16.) But, as a matter of law, such use is ordinary use of a driveway. Plaintiff presents no evidence of unusual or extraordinary use of the driveway (for example, for transportation or storage of unusually heavy vehicles or any type of construction work or other destructive activity by the Bartletts) or other evidence of control of the sidewalk. Driving back and forth over the driveway, even if it occurs more than once per entry and exit, is ordinary use and cannot provide a basis for negligence liability against an owner of abutting property under Lopez.

In sum, once the Bartletts met their initial burden on summary judgment, the burden shifted to Plaintiff to present evidence to show that there are triable issues as to one or more material facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) Plaintiff has not done so. Accordingly, the Bartletts’ motion for summary judgment is granted.

Conclusion

The Court GRANTS the motion for summary judgment filed by Blake Bartlet and Cara Bartlett.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
