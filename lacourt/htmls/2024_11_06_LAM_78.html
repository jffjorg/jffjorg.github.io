DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV25230    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

Department 78 

 

 

HYPE FACTOR, LLC,

Plaintiff; 

vs. 

JOHN FITZPATRICK, et al.,

Defendants. 

Case No: 19STCV25230

 

Hearing Date: November 6, 2024

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendants’ motion FOR ORDER GRANTING RELIEF FROM ENTRY OF STIPULATED JUDGMENT ON SEPTEMBER 13, 2024

 

















Defendants’ Motion for Order Granting Relief from Entry of Stipulated Judgment on September 13, 2024 is DENIED.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND 

This is a breach of contract matter. The complaint alleges as follows: Plaintiff Hype Factor, LLC (“Hype Factor”), owned by Sean Kilbane (“Kilbane”) is an entertainment company that contracted with Defendant JFF Entertainment, LLC (“JFF”). JFF’s principal is Defendant John Fitzpatrick (“Fitzpatrick”). (Compl. ¶¶ 24-29.) The contract provided that Hype Factor was to ensure that Fabletics, LLC performed their tasks under the contract, and that JFF was to ensure that social media talent Catherine Paiz (“Paiz”) performed her tasks under the contract. (Compl. ¶ 25.) Fitzpatrick/JFF held themselves out to be Paiz’s talent manager. (Compl. ¶ 21.) Paiz failed to perform under the contract because Fitzpatrick and JFF failed to inform Paiz about the agreement because JFF/Fitzpatrick were not Paiz’s talent manager. (Compl. ¶ 54.)

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On July 17, 2019, Plaintiff filed the Complaint asserting five causes of action:

1.     Breach of Contract;

2.     Fraud;

3.     Conversion;

4.     Violation of Business Code § 17200 et seq.; and

5.     Civil Code liability under Penal Code § 496(c).

On October 9, 2019, Defendants filed a joint Answer to the Complaint.

On November 9, 2019, Plaintiff filed an Answer to Defendants’ Cross-Claim.  

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Enforce Settlement, which was opposed by Defendants, and to which Plaintiff filed a reply.

On May 10, 2024, after hearing oral argument and taking the matter under submission, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement.

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Entry of Stipulated Judgment, which sought entry of a stipulated judgment in accordance with the terms of the settlement agreement between Plaintiff and Defendants.

On May 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Legal Fees and Costs seeking fees and costs incurred in enforcing the settlement agreement and entering a stipulated judgment in accordance with the terms of the settlement agreement between the parties.

On June 20, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition to the motion for entry of stipulated judgment, and Plaintiff filed a reply brief on June 25, 2024.

On June 25, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Jill Feeney to the Honorable Michelle C. Kim sitting in Department 78 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse effective July 5, 2024.

On June 27, 2024, Defendants filed a Notice of Appeal, which indicated that Defendants were appealing the Court’s May 10, 2024 order dismissing the case and enforcing the settlement agreement.

On July 2, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for entry of stipulated judgment. (07/02/24 Minute Order.) The motion was denied because the Court had already granted Plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement, which rendered the motion for stipulated judgment unnecessary. (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court opined that the “parties need not relitigate arguments pertaining to whether the settlement agreement was enforceable.” (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court ordered “Plaintiff . . . to file a proposed stipulated judgment by July 9, 2024.” (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court further indicated that “Defendant’s objections to the judgment and request for a stay is to be filed and served by July 23, 2024. Plaintiff’s response is to be filed and served by August 9, 2024.” (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court set a status conference and hearing on proposed judgment/request for stay for September 13, 2024, at 8:30 AM. (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) Notice was waived. (07/02/24 Minute Order at p. 2.)

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a memorandum of points and authorities in support of the entry of stipulated judgment, which indicated on the cover page that the hearing thereon was set for September 13, 2024.

On July 24, 2024, Defendants filed objections to Plaintiff’s July 9, 2024 memorandum. Such objections set forth a September 13, 2024 hearing date on the cover page thereof.

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a response in support of entry of the stipulated judgment, which set forth a September 13, 2024 hearing date on the caption page thereof.

On August 12, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition to the motion for attorneys’ fees, to which Plaintiff replied on August 13, 2024.

On August 20, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the motion for attorneys’ fees to December 18, 2024. (08/20/24 Minute Order at p. 3.) The Court also set a status conference re: appeal for November 18, 2024. (08/20/24 Minute Order at p. 3.)

On September 13, 2024, the Court held a status conference and hearing on proposed judgment /request for stay. (09/13/24 Minute Order.) There were no appearances by or for Defendants. (09/13/24 Minute Order.) The Court and Plaintiff’s counsel conferred regarding the status of the proposed judgment. (09/13/24 Minute Order.) Plaintiff was ordered to file the proposed judgment for the Court’s signature and was also ordered to give notice. (09/13/24 Minute Order.)

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served a Notice of Ruling of the Court’s September 13, 2024 order.

On September 16, 2024, the Court entered a Stipulated Judgment for Fraud and Civil Liability under Penal Code § 496(c). Pursuant to the stipulated judgment, Defendants are to pay Plaintiff, jointly and severally, the sum of $300,000.00, of which $50,000.00 is for attorneys’ fees. (09/16/24 Stipulated Judgment at p. 1:13-16.) The stipulated judgment notes that “[t]he parties have stipulated to the entry of the . . . judgment on the basis of Defendants’ failure to timely pay a settlement according to the terms of the parties’ Settlement Agreement dated April 1, 2024.” (09/16/24 Stipulated Judgment at p. 1:1-3.) Defendants’ Cross-Complaint was dismissed with prejudice. (09/16/24 Stipulated Judgment at p. 8:9-10.)

On September 25, 2024, Defendants filed and served the instant Motion for Order Granting Relief from Entry of Stipulated Judgment on September 13, 2024. The motion is brought on the “grounds that the matter was heard and the order was entered against Defendants in the absence of Defendants and their counsel due to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect of Defendants’ attorney.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 2:2-4.)

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion for an order granting relief, to which Defendants replied on October 29, 2024.

DISCUSSION 

I.                 EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

“The general rule of motion practice . . . is that new evidence is not permitted with the reply papers.” (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537.) “[P]oints raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter that argument.” (Id. at p. 1538.) “[T]he inclusion of additional evidentiary matter with the reply should only be allowed in the exceptional case . . . .” (Id. at p. 1537-1538.)

The Court SUSTAINS Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection number 1 to the declaration of J.D. Stewart in support of the reply brief.

The Court SUSTAINS Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection number 2 to Defendants’ reply brief.

The Court sustains such objections because Defendants are attempting to introduce new evidence and new matter which was not raised in the moving papers. In the moving papers, Defendants contend that their failure to attend the September 13, 2024 hearing was because “counsel mistakenly believed that the Court’s August 20 order setting an appeal status conference for November 18, 2024, vacated and replaced the pending status conference and hearing set for September 13.” (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 3:12-14.) However, for the first time on reply, Defendants argue and present evidence that their counsel did not appear at the hearing because counsel’s “calendaring assistant mistakenly entered the date of the hearing as September 13, 2023, in place of September 13, 2024.” (Reply at p. 3:27-4:1; Stewart Reply Decl., ¶ 7.) It appears that Defendants are attempting to create an alternate basis for relief, which was not raised in the moving papers. If such calendaring error did occur, Defendants should have presented such argument and evidence in the moving papers.

II.               MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM ENTRY OF STIPULATED JUDGMENT

Defendants move for relief from the Court’s September 13, 2024 order entering a stipulated judgment against Defendants.  

“The court may, upon terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other proposed pleading to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) also contains a mandatory relief provision. (SJP Limited Partnership v. City of Los Angeles (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 511, 516.)  “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment; or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Ibid.) “If the prerequisites of the mandatory relief provision of section 473, subdivision (b) exist, the trial court does not have discretion to refuse relief.” (Ibid.)  

“The test for discretionary relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473 requires the party seeking relief to show excusable error.” (Comunidad en Accion v. Los Angeles City Council (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1132.) “A party who seeks relief under section 473 on the basis of mistake or inadvertence of counsel must demonstrate that such mistake, inadvertence, or general neglect was excusable because the negligence of the attorney is imputed to his client and may not be offered by the latter as a basis for relief.” (Ibid., citation omitted, internal quotations omitted.) “In determining whether the attorney’s mistake or inadvertence was excusable, the court inquires whether a reasonably prudent person under the same or similar circumstances might have made the same error.” (Ibid., citation omitted, internal quotations omitted, emphasis in original.) Relief is only permitted under the discretionary provision of CCP § 473 when it involves “mistakes anyone could have made.” (Ibid., citation omitted, internal quotations omitted.) “Conduct falling below the professional standard of care, such as the failure to timely object or to properly advance an argument, is not therefore excusable.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.) “[E]ntering the wrong date in an attorney’s calendar [is] sufficient to warrant relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.” (Id. at p. 1133.)

“Whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, the court shall direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties, in addition to whatever additional fees are authorized by subdivision (c), of Code Civ. Proc. § 473.” (J.A.T. Entertainment, Inc. v. Reed (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 1485, 1491.) Whenever the court grants relief from a dismissal based on any grounds in Code Civ. Proc. § 473, the Court may: (1) impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party; (2) direct that an offending party pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund; or (3) grant other relief as is appropriate.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(1)(A)-(C).)    

A.    Evidence in Support of the Motion

Counsel for Defendants, J.D. Stewart, Esq. (“Stewart”), provides a declaration. Mr. Stewart relevantly states the following: On August 20, 2024, the Court heard arguments for Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees, which was continued to December 18, 2024, and, at the same time, the Court set an appeal status conference for November 18, 2024. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 6.) As a result of such hearing and order, Mr. Stewart reasonably but mistakenly believed that the status conference set for September 13, 2024, was taken off calendar. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 7.) Mr. Stewart did not realize this error until early on Sunday, September 15, when he saw an email from opposing counsel’s office giving notice of the Court’s September 13 order. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 8.) Mr. Stewart states that he made the motion as soon as practicable following his discovery and he has never failed to appear for a hearing in nearly five years of practice. (Stewart Decl. ¶¶ 9-10.)

In his reply declaration, Mr. Stewart states that “[o]n July 2, 2024, the Court scheduled a hearing for September 13, 2024, which was labeled on the Court’s calendar as an appeal status conference.” (Stewart Reply Decl. ¶ 3.) On August 20, 2024, the Court issued an order setting an appeal status conference for November 18, 2024. (Stewart Reply Decl. ¶ 4.) Mr. Stewart indicates that this was the best rationale he could conceive for a slated hearing not being on his office’s master calendar. (Stewart Reply Decl. ¶ 5.) Mr. Stewart indicates that he “deduced this to be the case because [he] could not imagine another reason for [his] calendaring assistant to not calendar the hearing or otherwise take the hearing off calendar.” (Stewart Reply Decl., ¶ 6.)

B.    Evidence in Opposition to the Motion

In opposition to the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel, Thomas Bourke (“Bourke”), provides a declaration. Mr. Bourke presents evidence that Mr. Stewart knew about the September 13, 2024 hearing date because he requested such hearing date. (Bourke Decl. ¶¶ 8-10, Ex. A.) Plaintiff and Defendants each filed documents indicating that the hearing date concerning the stipulated judgment was set for September 13, 2024. (Bourke Decl. ¶¶ 11-13, Exs. B, C, D.) Defendants’ opposition to Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees recognized that the motion for attorneys’ fees was different form the motion to enter stipulated judgment. (Bourke Decl. ¶ 14.) The Court did not mention anything about moving the hearing on the entry of stipulated judgment set for September 13, 2024. (Bourke Decl. ¶ 17.) The August 20, 2024 minute order, which continued the hearing on the motion for attorneys’ fees, said nothing about the September 13, 2024 hearing or moving such hearing. (Bourke Decl. ¶¶ 18-22, Ex. E.) Mr. Bourke attests that Mr. Stewart did not make a showing of excusable neglect or mistake, and that he had actual knowledge of the correct September 13, 2024 hearing date. (Bourke Decl. ¶¶ 30-33.)

C.    Defendants Have Failed to Make a Showing of Excusable Mistake or Excusable Neglect

The Court finds that Defendants have not made a showing of mistake or excusable neglect to warrant relief. Initially, the Court notes that the declaration of Mr. Stewart contains misstatements of fact. Mr. Stewart declares that, on August 20, 2024, the Court heard arguments on Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees, which was continued to December 18, 2024. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 6.) Such statement is inaccurate as the Court did not conduct a hearing on the motion for attorneys’ fees. (See 08/20/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court merely continued the hearing on such motion to December 18, 2024. (08/20/24 Minute Order at p. 1.)

Further, the Court finds that Mr. Stewart’s contention that the Court labeled the September 13, 2024 hearing as an “appeal status conference” is also inaccurate. (Stewart Reply Decl. ¶ 3.) A review of eCourt—which is the Court’s electronic case management system—shows that the September 13, 2024 hearing was calendared as “Status Conference and hearing on proposed judgment/request for a stay.” Thus, the September 13, 2024 hearing was never calendared as an appeal status conference. Moreover, the Court fails to see how counsel thought the September 13, 2024 hearing was taken off calendar when the Court never issued a minute order or any other indication stating that such hearing was taken off calendar. (See 07/20/24 and 08/20/24 Minute Orders.)

The Court finds that it was unreasonable for Defendants’ counsel to believe that the September 13, 2024 hearing was taken off calendar. Given that Defendants’ counsel requested that hearing date, as well as received and effectuated filings showing that such hearing date was still operative and still on calendar, the Court finds that any belief that the hearing was off calendar was not a reasonable belief. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 6.) The Court fails to see how the Court’s August 20, 2024 order—which merely continued the hearing on an attorneys’ fees motion—caused Defendants’ counsel to believe that the September 13, 2024 hearing, which concerned the proposed stipulated judgment, was taken off calendar. A reasonably prudent person would have inquired with the Court or checked the docket to see if the hearing was still on calendar. Thus, the Court finds that Defendants’ counsel failure to appear at the September 13, 2024 hearing was not due to an excusable mistake or excusable neglect.

Accordingly, the Motion for Order Granting Relief from Entry of Stipulated Judgment on September 13, 2024 is DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

On August 21, 2024, the Court granted LG H&H Co., Ltd.’s (“LG”) Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award. 

On September 30, 2024, LG filed the instant motion for attorney fees. LG argues it is entitled to attorney fees because the indemnification clause in the Stock Purchase Agreement (“SPA”) which identifies attorneys’ fees as losses. 

On October 24, 2024, respondents Insil Kim and Sunna Kim (“the Kims”) filed an opposition. They contend that LG this action was not brought under the indemnification clause of the SPA, and that attorneys’ fees as “Losses” covered by an indemnification provision is not a prevailing party fee-shifting clause. 

On October 30, 2024, LG filed a reply. 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

Cal. Civ. Code § 1717(a) provides, “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” 

Here, LG relies on an indemnity provision, which is not the same as an attorney’s fee provision. “A provision including attorney fees as an item of loss in an indemnity clause is not a provision for attorney fees in an action to enforce the contract. Accordingly, such a contractual indemnity provision is not made reciprocal and applicable to the entire contract by…Civil Code section 1717. (Myers Bldg. Indus., Ltd. v. Interface Tech., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal. App. 4th 949, 971; see also Rideau v. Stewart Title of California, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal. App. 4th 1286, 1298 [Generally, the specification of attorney fees as an item of loss in a third party claim indemnity provision does not constitute a provision for the award of attorney fees in an action on the contract which is required to trigger the reciprocal contractual attorney fee statute.].) 
 

III. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, LG’s motion for attorney fees is DENIED.
