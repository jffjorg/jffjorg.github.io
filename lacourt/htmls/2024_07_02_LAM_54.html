            Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

Eileen Carry and Sylvia Merino,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22STCV15060

 

vs.

 

 

Tentative Ruling

 

 

Boys & Girls Clubs of Santa Monica, Inc., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date: July 2, 2024

Department 54, Judge Maurice A. Leiter

Motion for Summary Judgment

Moving Party: Plaintiffs Eileen Carry and Sylvia Merino

Responding Party: Defendants, Boys’ & Girls’ Club of Santa Monica and Robert J. Sullivan Inc.

T/R:     PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED.

            PLAINTIFFS TO GIVE NOTICE.

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative, please email the courtroom at SMCdept54@lacourt.org with notice to opposing counsel (or self-represented party) before 8:00 am on the day of the hearing.

            The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND

            On June 2, 2022, Plaintiffs Eileen Carry and Sylvia Merino filed the operative first amended complaint against Defendants Boys’ & Girls’ Club of Santa Monica and Robert J. Sullivan, Inc. for declaratory relief. Plaintiffs occupy a rental unit owned by Defendants. In 2018, Defendants filed an unlawful detainer action against Plaintiffs; judgment was entered in favor of Defendants in 2019. The appellate court affirmed the judgment. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants did not seek possession of the unit after remittitur and continued to collect rent from Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs seek declaratory relief, stating a new tenancy has been formed.

The unlawful detainer action, filed by Defendant Sullivan Inc. on November 9, 2018, claimed that Carry and Merino had impermissibly installed a lattice fence on the Apartment’s patio. After a bench trial the court found in favor of Defendant Sullivan and against Plaintiffs Carry and Merino. The court subsequently stayed enforcement of its judgment pending appeal, requiring Carry and Merino to pay “rent” as consideration for the stay. The judgment was affirmed on August 12, 2021, and the remittitur was issued on October 15, 2021.

Plaintiffs contend that Defendants did not seek possession of the apartment after issuance of the remittitur. Instead, Sullivan Inc. collected rent for the apartment from Plaintiffs on behalf of the Club for September, October, November, and December 2021, and at least January, February, and March 2022. Plaintiffs argue that by doing so Defendants reinstated Plaintiffs’ tenancy.

On October 24, 2022, this Court overruled Defendants’ demurrer, rejecting the argument that collateral estopped barred Plaintiffs’ claim.  The Court held that Defendants failed to show that the unlawful detainer court, in an ex parte application to vacate the stay on May 23, 2022, intended to fully decide whether a new tenancy had been created. And at least some of the conduct at issue in the present matter occurred after judgment in the UD action was entered.

ANALYSIS

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties' pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Trial judges are required “to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

In a motion for summary judgment, the plaintiff’s burden is to prove each element of the cause of action, not to negate all possible defenses.  (CCP § 437c(o)(1).)  Once the plaintiff has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action.  (CCP § 437c(o)(1).) 

 

 

A. Declaratory Relief—Rental Reinstatement Pursuant to Eo Nomine

Plaintiffs allege that by accepting seven months of consecutive rent payments after the affirmed appeal in favor of Defendants, including five months after the remittitur, Defendants reinstated Plaintiffs’ tenancy. Plaintiffs argue that Jones v. Della Maria, (1920) 48 Cal. App. 171, established that when a landlord or agent accepts rent from a tenant during the eviction process, the landlord has waived its right to continue the eviction and has established the tenancy anew.

“Under section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the landlord, after giving the three days' notice there provided for, has the right to maintain an action for restitution of possession and cancellation of the lease. The attitude of the landlord, after such notice and the commencement of an action to cancel the lease, is that the tenant no longer is entitled to possession by virtue of the lease contract; that the tenant has forfeited his leasehold. Having elected to treat his tenant as no longer entitled to possession or to any right under the lease, the landlord's course must be consistent with this claim in the further progress of the proceeding that he has instituted. And if thereafter he accepts rent accruing subsequently to the demand for possession or accruing subsequently to the commencement of the action and accept it as rent eo nomine--that is, as payment under the original lease contract--he affirms that the lease is still in existence, and thereby waives the forfeiture that he has elected to enforce. By his own acts he admits the continuance of the lease and waives any prior forfeiture. A landlord who thus recognizes a lease as a subsisting, operative contract should not be permitted to insist upon a past forfeiture, nor be permitted to assert that the contract is no longer a subsisting lease affording to the tenant all his contractual rights thereunder.” (Jones, 48 Cal. App. at 173–74.)

            Plaintiffs have met their initial burden of proving their cause of action. The burden now shifts to Defendants to show a triable issue of material fact.  

In opposition, Defendants argue that their acceptance of Plaintiffs’ rent did not reinstate the tenancy. Defendants provide an excerpt from a June 7, 2019, minute order in which the UD court ruled that, because it ordered Plaintiffs to pay rent, Plaintiffs’ tenancy had not been reinstituted. Instead, Defendants contend the rent was consideration for maintaining the stay pending action by the Appellate Division. Defendants also argue that the doctrines of estoppel and res judicata prevent Plaintiffs from obtaining summary judgment here.

Defendants have failed to show a triable issue of material fact. The key issue here is Defendants’ acceptance of rent for seven months after the Appellate Division ruled, including five months after issuance of the remittitur. As the UD court noted in its May 23, 2022 order, “the obligation to pay rent 'going forward’ was part of the Court's stay order and was intended to insure that [the tenants] would pay fair rental value for occupying the premises pending future action by the Appellate Department and for this Court.” Defendants could have sought to enforce their forfeiture order as soon as the remittitur was issued. Instead, they chose to accept rent for several more months. In so doing they waived enforcement of the forfeiture order. Pursuant to Jones, Defendants did not have the option of collecting rent for as long as they wished, and then at a time of their choosing seek to enforce the forfeiture order. Their months-long, post-remittitur acceptance of rent reinstated the tenancy.

The Court addressed Defendants’ res judicata and collateral estoppel arguments in overruling their demurrer. Nothing in this motion warrants a different ruling.

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action for unpaid rent. Plaintiff Kevin Su sued defendants Fengyu Wang, Keyi Wang, and Yuxiao Wang on January 13, 2023 for breach of contract and breach of guaranty. Plaintiff alleges he executed a lease with Defendant Fengyu Wang on or around November 18, 2020. The remaining defendants guaranteed Wang’s obligations under the lease. Plaintiff now moves for an order deeming admitted all matters referred to in his Requests for Admission (Set One) served on defendant Keyi Wang on January 27, 2024.

 

Defendant filed no opposition.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (a), “[i]f a party to whom requests or admission are directed fails to serve a timely response…[t]he party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product[.]”  (Id., § 2033.280(a).)  Where a party fails to respond to requests for admissions, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Id., § 2033.280 (b).)  The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted the matters specified in the requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Id., § 2033.280(c).)

 

A. Deemed Admissions

 

Plaintiff’s counsel attests that he served defendant Wang with RFAs on January 27, 2024, and she failed to timely respond. Defendants filed no opposition contesting these facts. Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming matters admitted.

 

B. Sanctions

 

When deeming matters admitted, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction ... on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitate [a] motion [under section 2033.280].” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)

 

Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions. Counsel attests to a reasonable $900.00 for hours spent working on this motion and $61.65 in costs. The request is granted. The Court imposes sanctions on Defendant in the amount of $961.65, payable to Plaintiff and/or his counsel within sixty (30) days of this order.
