DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation. 



           


Case Number: 19STCV29589    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: June 13, 2024

Case Name: Alaniz, et al. v. Austra Motors, Inc., et al.

Case No.: 19STCV29589

Matter: Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

Moving Party: Plaintiffs David and Rosalie Alaniz

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Motion is granted in part.


Moving party to give notice.


If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly 

encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic. 


This is an action in which Plaintiffs David and Rosalie Alaniz allege they were sold a defective vehicle, which had structural damage from a prior accident.  

Plaintiffs and Austra Motors, Inc. have settled this matter and have agreed that Plaintiffs may request fees and costs by motion.  (Civ. Code §§ 1780(e), 1794(d).)  Plaintiffs now move for $99,469 in attorneys’ fees.

No opposition was filed.

The lodestar figure was calculated as follows: 

 

The Court, in the exercise of its discretion, will reduce the hourly rate of Barry to $600 and the rate of Babbitt to $550.  This is a total deduction of $6,364.

Furthermore, an excessive amount of time was spent on the instant Motion.  Thus, a $3,600 (6 hours at $600 per hour) deduction will be made.

Finally, the Court, in the exercise of its discretion, will make an additional 5% reduction of the remaining lodestar figure.

In sum, the Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is granted in part.  The Court awards $85,029.75 in fees.

Case Number: 23STCV00413    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: June 13, 2024

Case Name: Loomis Armored US, LLC v. Castillo

Case No.: 23STCV00413 

Matter: (1) Motion to Consolidate

(2) Motion for Leave to Amend

Moving Party: Plaintiff Loomis Armored US, LLC

Responding Party: Defendant Rafael Castillo

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Motion to Consolidate is granted.


The Motion for Leave to Amend is granted.


Moving party to give notice.


If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly 

encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic. 


On  January 9, 2023, Plaintiff Loomis Armored US, LLC (“Loomis”) filed the operative Complaint against Defendant Rafael Castillo for (1) fraud and (2) conversion.  Plaintiff alleges that it has a cash delivery service and that its employee, Castillo, stole $36,000 while making a delivery.  


  1. Motion to Consolidate

Loomis seeks to consolidate this matter with Rafael Castillo v. Loomis Armored US, LLC, et al., Case No. 23STCV04436, which is also in Dept. 20.  Loomis contends that there are overlapping questions of law, a common factual nucleus, and similar parties.  

In 23STCV04436, Castillo has brought claims for discrimination, harassment, failure to prevent misconduct, wrongful discharge, and defamation against Loomis and also five of its employees, Jessie Hernandez, Nolan Tate, Ryan Shake, Urias Jimenez, and John Toliano.   Castillo therein alleges that he had been an excellent employee for years, but when Shake was hired, he orchestrated a scheme in which he hired two younger employees, had Castillo completely train them, and then fired Castillo through a false assertion that he stole $36,000.  Castillo alleges that the LAPD and District Attorney found no probable cause for an arrest and that he has not been charged for any crime.  

Castillo opposes consolidation because the issues of fact and law and the identity of the parties are not sufficiently similar.  Castillo also argues that there would be great jury confusion as to the standards for the competing claims. 

Code Civ. Proc. § 1048(a) provides, “[w]hen actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions;¿it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.”  

The pleadings in the subject cases mirror a complaint and cross-complaint.  Given that both cases will closely examine whether Castillo stole the subject money, requiring similar evidence and witnesses, the Court, in the exercise of its discretion, grants the Motion.


  1. Motion for Leave to Amend

Loomis also seeks leave to file a First Amended Complaint adding causes of action for (1) violation of Penal Code § 496 and (2) breach of fiduciary duty.  Loomis further seeks to remove its fraud claim.  

The Court may, in the furtherance of justice, and upon any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 473, 576.)  In general, California courts liberally exercise discretion to permit amendment of pleadings in light of a strong policy favoring resolution of all disputes between parties in the same action.  (Nestle v. Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939; Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)  “[T]here is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.”  (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296.)  Pursuant to this policy, requests for leave to amend generally will be granted unless the party seeking to amend has been dilatory in bringing the proposed amendment before the Court, and the delay in seeking leave to amend will cause prejudice to the opposing party if leave to amend is granted.  (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490; Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565.)  The decision on a motion for leave is directed to the sound discretion of the trial court.  (See generally Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2014) ¶¶ 6:637 et seq.)

Castillo contends that “(1) Loomis fails to establish sufficient facts to allege California Penal Code § 496(a) against Defendant because such a claim requires a conviction—Mr. Castillo was never even charged with a crime and will not be charged; (2) Loomis claims that they are not adding facts to their proposed amended complaint. Mr. Castillo maintained money machines and transported cash—he is not a fiduciary, thus he cannot breach a fiduciary duty. Loomis would need new facts to make this contention; (3) Amending the complaint after Defendant planned, attempted to, and did conduct significant discovery on the existing causes of action, will cause Defendant severe prejudice; (4) The remedies provided by the causes of action in Plaintiff’s proposed amended complaint are significantly different from the remedies in Plaintiff’s initial complaint such that they do not relate back and are barred by the statute of limitations.”

Castillo’s arguments as to a failure to allege a conviction, the application of the statute of limitations, and a failure to allege a fiduciary duty lack merit.  A demurrer or dispositive motion is the proper vehicle by which to hear these assertions.

Further, there is no basis to find sufficient prejudice to preclude an amendment at this juncture.  Indeed, the trial in this matter is currently set for October 2025.  

Because there is not sufficient prejudice, the Motion is granted.

Case Name: Liliana A. v. Universal Background Screening, Inc., et al.

Case No.: 23STCV08447

Matter: Motion to Vacate Stay and Withdraw from Arbitration

Moving Party: Plaintiff Liliana A.

Responding Party: Defendant Universal Background Screening, Inc.

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Motion is granted.  The Court awards reduced sanctions in the 

amount of $3,500.


Moving party to give notice.


If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly 

encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic. 


This is an employment action.  On April 17, 2023, Plaintiff Liliana A. filed a complaint against Defendant Pluto Acquisition Opco LL.C dba Universal Background Screening alleging causes of action regarding her employment with Defendant.  

On October 27, 2023, the Court compelled this matter to arbitration.

Plaintiff Liliana A. now seeks to vacate the stay in this matter and withdraw from arbitration because Defendant Universal Background Screening, Inc. failed to timely pay arbitration fees.  Plaintiff also seeks monetary, evidentiary, and/or terminating sanctions.

Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.97(a)(1) states, “In an employment or consumer arbitration that requires, either expressly or through application of state or federal law or the rules of the arbitration provider, the drafting party to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, if the fees or costs to initiate an arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days after the due date the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement, is in default of the arbitration, and waives its right to compel arbitration under Section 1281.2.”

Defendant does not dispute that fees were not timely tendered.  Rather, Defendant argues that the Motion should be denied because “[i]n Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc., the appellate court specifically addressed whether the Federal Arbitration Act’s (‘FAA’) preempts the Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.97 [ ]. The appellate court found that the FAA does preempt section 1281.97.”

In Reply, Plaintiff contends that the Court “could side with Second Appellate District Division 1 (Espinoza v. Superior Court), Second Appellate District, Division 2 (Gallo v. Wood Ranch USA, Inc), Second Appellate District, Division 3 (De Leon v. Juanita’s Foods), Second Appellate District, Division 8 (Hohenshelt v. Superior Court), Fourth Appellate District, Division 1 (Suarez v. Superior Court) or it can side with the sole Second Appellate District, Division 5 (Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises) which was truly fragmented as Presiding Justice Baker gave a blistering dissent.”

“When there are conflicting court of appeal decisions on point, the trial court can choose to follow either of them; it can even adopt the position taken by another district, notwithstanding a conflicting decision emanating from the trial court's own district. (As a practical matter, however, trial courts usually adhere to the decisions from their own districts.)”  (Eisenberg et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Appeals & Writs (The Rutter Group 2005) ¶ 14:195, p. 14-72.)

The Court chooses to follow Espinoza, et al., and finds that the FAA does not preempt Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.97.

Further, as discussed, it appears undisputed that fees were not timely tendered.  Therefore, Plaintiff has a right to reinstate the action.  

As to sanctions, the Court will only award monetary sanctions.  Plaintiff seeks $14,170 in sanctions; this represents the time associated with the instant Motion.

The Court will award reduced sanctions in the amount of $3,500.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1281.97(d), 1281.99.)

The Motion is granted.  The objections are overruled.  The Requests for Judicial Notice are granted.
