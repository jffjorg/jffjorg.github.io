DEPARTMENT 76 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV20484    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 76

Pursuant to California Rule of Court 3.1308(a)(1), the Court does not desire oral argument on the motion addressed herein.  Counsel must contact the staff in Department 76 to inform the Court whether they wish to submit on the tentative, or to argue the matter.  As required by Rule 3.1308(a), any party seeking oral argument must notify ALL OTHER PARTIES and the staff of Department 76 of their intent to appear and argue.

Notice to Department 76 may be sent by email to smcdept76@lacourt.org or telephonically at 213-830-0776.

Per Rule of Court 3.1308, if notice of intention to appear is not given, the Court may adopt the tentative ruling as the final ruling.


Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Wayne Baldwin’s anti-SLAPP special motion to strike the Cross-Complaint is GRANTED as to counts, one, two, five and six, and GRANTED IN PART as specified in the ruling as to counts three and four.

Cross-Defendant’s request for an award of attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $10,139.31 pursuant to Civ. Proc. Code, § 425.16(c)(1) is GRANTED. 

            Defendants motion for an order declaring Defendant/Cross-Complainant Connie Villafan a vexatious litigant is DENIED without prejudice to a renewed motion later in the proceedings. 

ANALYSIS

Anti-SLAPP Special Motion To Strike

Discussion

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Wayne Baldwin d brings an anti-SLAPP special motion to strike as to the First Amended Cross-Complaint.

In ruling on a defendant’s special motion to strike, the trial court uses a “summary-judgment-like procedure at an early stage of the litigation.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 192.)  This is a two-step process.  First, the defendant must show that the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken “in furtherance of the [defendant]’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b)(1).) Second, if the defendant carries that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b)(3).)  The defendant has the burden on the first issue, and the plaintiff on the second. (Kajima Engineering & Construction, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 921, 928; Rivero v. American Federation of State, County and Municipal Employees, AFL-CIO (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 913, 919.) In making both determinations the trial court considers “the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §425.16(b)(2); Equilon Enterprises, LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.)

The Defendant’s act underlying the cause of action must itself have been in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech. (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal. 4th 69, 76-78.)  The defendant’s acts are protected activity – that is, made in furtherance of protected petition or free speech in connection with a public issue – if they fit into one of the following categories under the section 425.16, subdivision (e) categories: (1) oral or written statements made before a legislative, executive, judicial or any other official proceeding; (2) oral or written statements made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; (3) written or oral statements made in a place open to the public or in a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest; and (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional rights of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(e).)

            If such a showing is made, the burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show a probability of prevailing on the claim.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b)(1).)  To establish a probability of prevailing on the merits, the Plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited. (Matson v. Dvorak (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 539, 548.) In making this assessment it is the court’s responsibility to accept as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff. (HMS Capital, Inc. v. Lawyers Title Co. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 204, 212.) The Complaint needs only to establish that his or her claim has minimal merit (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89) to avoid being stricken as a SLAPP. (Jarrow Formulas, Inc. v. LaMarche (2003) 31 Cal.4th 728, 738.)

“For purposes of this inquiry, ‘the trial court considers the pleadings and evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant (§ 425.16, subd. (b)(2)); though the court does not weigh the credibility or comparative probative strength of competing evidence, it should grant the motion if, as a matter of law, the defendant's evidence supporting the motion defeats the plaintiff's attempt to establish evidentiary support for the claim.’ (Citation omitted.)” (Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 291.) 

1.         Re: Whether the Causes of Action Are Subject To Being Stricken Pursuant to CCP § 425.16.

A.        Count One – Policy to Abuse Process, Fraudulent Use of Process, Misuse of Process, Harassment. 

          Count One is based on the following allegations:

17. This case was filed with the intent to deprive Villafan of her property through an intentional and well-orchestrated practice to set up Villafan, in advance, by becoming a plaintiff, in this case, Baldwin, engage in intentional, outlandish activity and conduct during his tenancy, and, following his orchestrated "eviction", then retained counsel to, draft and file the complaint filled with untruths and innuendo but made with plausible allegations.

 

18. This scheme is similar to the law governing situations where an individual or a company makes repeat frivolous filings. Sanctions can be issued including fines and citations.

 

19. An individual who files legal complaint, must have a degree of certainty that their claim has merit as abuse of the legal process is a very serious matter with harsh consequences for violators.

 

20. Cross-Defendant Baldwin manufactured the alternative facts alleged in the Complaint.

 

(First Amended Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 17 – 20.)

 

This count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.

 

Section 425.16, subdivision (b)(1) defines the types of claims that are subject to the anti-SLAPP procedures. These claims include causes of action "arising from" an "act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition . . . under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue." (§ 425.16, subd. (b)(1), italics added.)

It is well established that filing a lawsuit is an exercise of a party's constitutional right of petition. (Citations omitted.) " ' "[T]he constitutional right to petition . . . includes the basic act of filing litigation or otherwise seeking administrative action." ' " (Citations omitted.) Further, the filing of a judicial complaint satisfies the "in connection with a public issue" component of section 425.16, subdivision (b)(1) because it pertains to an official proceeding. (Citations omitted.)

Under these accepted principles, a cause of action arising from a defendant's alleged improper filing of a lawsuit may appropriately be the subject of a section 425.16 motion to strike. (Citation  omitted.) The essence of the  [*1088]  Chavezes' malicious prosecution claim is that the plaintiff in the underlying action (Mendoza) filed litigation that was improper because it was allegedly filed with a malicious motive and without probable cause. This claim "aris[es] from" the defendant's constitutionally protected petitioning activity, and therefore is subject to the anti-SLAPP statute. (§ 425.16, subd. (b)(1).)

(Chavez v. Mendoza (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 1083, 1087-88.) 

“[A]n abuse of process claim . . . involves protected activity under section 425.16. Therefore, the first anti-SLAPP prong is satisfied.” (S.A. v. Maiden (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 27, 42.)

            Accordingly, count one is subject to being stricken.

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this count against Cross-Defendant.

 B.       Count Two – Abuse of Process, Fraudulent Use of Process, Misuse of Process, Harassment

            Count two is based upon the following allegations: 

22. The Cross-Defendant's brought this lawsuit for reasons other than resolving a dispute. There was no dispute. Baldwin intentionally caused all of the problems set forth in his Complaint with the expectation of subsequently filing this lawsuit. The ulterior motive for the Cross-Defendant is monetary gain. Villafan already has suffered damages, not only emotionally but pecuniary, as well. The willful actions include not only Baldwin's intentional misconduct but also includes the filing of the Complaint. This case is clearly an improper use of legal process.

 

23. This abuse of process also was used to harass Villafan as it was a course of conduct that was knowing and willful, was directed at Villafan causing her serious emotional harm, which improper use of process served no legitimate purpose.

 

24. Cross-Defendant Baldwin engaged in the malicious perversion of regularly issued process by this court, without reasonable or probable cause. He did so in a manner not contemplated by the law, to effect a purpose for which the law was not intended.

 

25. Cross-Defendant Baldwin engaged in willful and intentional abuse of process or misuse of process, to attain an objective which is unlawful itself and beyond the purpose for which process may be legally employed.

 

26. Cross-Defendant Baldwin used process for fraudulent purposes by creating fictitious claims.

 

27. Cross-Defendant Baldwin used process to harass Villafan.

 

28. Cross-Defendant Baldwin engaged in perversion of legal remedy.

     (First Amended Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 22 – 28.)

This count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.

            Accordingly, count two is subject to being stricken. 

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this count against Cross-Defendant.

C.        Count Three – Fraud, Deceit, Concealment

            Count three is based on the following allegations:

30. The Complaint filed in this case contained allegations which were known by Baldwin to be false and contrived. Baldwin initially met with Villafan and moved into her residence, all the while, deceiving Villafan while gaining her trust and apparent justifiable reliance which has resulted in damages and emotional distress to Villafan.

 

31. The knowingly false allegations contained in the Complaint and filed with the Court are intended to mislead the Court and the jury into making a decision in favor of Baldwin.

 

32. The Complaint intentionally conceals material facts that Baldwin had a duty to disclose and made with the intent to defraud Villafan and the Court. Villafan would not have rented the room to Baldwin had there been truthful disclosure. As a result of Villafan being "duped" by the Baldwin, she has suffered damages and emotional distress.

 

33. Baldwin made material misrepresentations as to the past and to current facts, with knowledge or belief of its falsity, and with the intention that the court would rely on them. There was reasonable reliance on the material misrepresentations as to material facts. Baldwin lied and concealed the evidence, thereby engaging in fraudulent concealment, fraud and deceit.

 

(First Amended Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 30 – 33 [bold emphasis added].)

            This count is a mixture of conduct which is not subject to being stricken—alleged misrepresentations by Baldwin to induce Villafan to renting a room to Baldwin—and conduct which is subject to being stricken—an attempted fraud upon this Court based upon the allegations in the Complaint.

            As such, only those portions of count three based upon an attempted fraud upon this Court in making the allegations in the Complaint are subject to being stricken. An anti-SLAPP special motion to strike may be brought to strike allegations of protected activity even if it is alleged alongside unprotected activity. (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 396.)

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on these portions of this count against Cross-Defendant.

D.        Count Four – Unclean Hands

            Count four is based upon the following allegations:

35. Baldwin violated the unclean hands doctrine. The unclean hands doctrine bars relief to a party who has engaged in inequitable behavior related to the subject matter of that party's claim. Here, Baldwin acted unethically through the filing of untruthful statements in the Complaint. Such untruthful statements were, in actuality, the result of intentional actions made by Baldwin to "create" a legal cause of action, to which he is now trying to collect, as damages. The filing of untrue allegations is such a clear abuse of process that equity demands dismissal of Baldwin' s Complaint.

 

36. Baldwin misled Villafan as to his true intentions when he applied to move into Villafan' s residence and begged her to move into her garage. He should not be able to collect damages as his willful and intentional misconduct should not be rewarded.

 

37. The doctrine of Unclean Hands bar all Cross-Defendants from obtaining any relief.

 

     (First Amended Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 35 – 37 [bold emphasis added].)

            As with the third count, this count is a mixture of conduct which is not subject to being stricken—alleged misrepresentations by Baldwin to induce Villafan to renting a room to Baldwin—and conduct which is subject to being stricken—unclean hands in  filing the Complaint based upon inequitable behavior.

            As such, only those portions of count four based upon alleged inequitable behavior in making the allegations in the Complaint are subject to being stricken.

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on these portions of this count against Cross-Defendant.

E.         Count Five – Unjust Enrichment

39. Baldwin, in advance of litigation, as a matter of his practice and policy, engineered his scheme to deploy prefabricated claims as a means to unjustly enrich himself. He is attempting to become enriched through the filing of Baldwin' s frivolous law suit by receiving a benefit at Villafan's expense, under circumstances that makes it unjust for him to receive such a Court award.

 

40. Baldwin and others to be named following discovery unjustly enriched himself.

This count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.

            Accordingly, count five is subject to being stricken. 

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this count against Cross-Defendant.

F.         Count Six – Equitable Relief

            Count six is based upon the following allegations:

42. Baldwin and others to be named following discovery, should be enjoined from filing any claims involving landlords and tenants. Baldwin intentionally engineered his scheme to enable him to a file prefabricated complaint. Baldwin should be refrained from engaging in any future filing of untrue, prefabricated complaints.

This count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.            Accordingly, count six is subject to being stricken. 

            Accordingly, count six is subject to being stricken. 

The burden shifts to Cross-Complainant to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this count against Cross-Defendant. 

2.         Re: Whether Plaintiff Has Established That There Is A Probability She Will Prevail On The Claims – CCP ¶ 425.16(b)(1).

            On this second prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis, the burden shifts to Plaintiffs to show a probability of prevailing on the claim. (Civ. Pro. Code, § 425.16(b)(1).) 

A.        Count One – Policy to Abuse Process, Fraudulent Use of Process, Misuse of Process, Harassment. 

The common law tort of abuse of process arises when one uses the court's process for a purpose other than that for which the process was designed. (Citations  [*1057] omitted.) It has been “interpreted broadly to encompass the entire range of ‘procedures’ incident to litigation.” (Citation omitted.)

 

“[T]he essence of the tort [is] … misuse of the power of the court; it is an act done in the name of the court and under its authority for the purpose of perpetrating an injustice.” (Citation omitted.) To succeed in an action for abuse of process, a litigant must establish that the defendant (1) contemplated an ulterior motive in using the process, and (2) committed a willful act in the use of the process not proper in the regular conduct of the proceedings. (Citation omitted.)

(Rusheen v. Cohen (2006) 37 Cal.4th 1048, 1056-57.)

Here, Cross-Complainant submitted a “Declaration” which is not made under the penalty of perjury, as required by Civ. Proc. Code, § 2015.5. 

Moreover, as noted by Cross-Defendant in the reply, the litigation privilege bars the tort of abuse of process when it is based upon filing a lawsuit. 

The tort of abuse of process is barred by the litigation privilege, which protects a “publication or broadcast … . [¶] … [¶] (b) In any … (2) judicial  [*1430]  proceeding … .” (Civ. Code, § 47, subd. (b)(2), italics added.) Section 47, subdivision (b)(2) is a defense to an abuse of process action. (Citations omitted.) “The privilege is broadly applied to protect most publications within lawsuits provided there is some connection between the lawsuit and the publication. [Citation.]” (Citations omitted.) Doubts about the privilege’s applicability are resolved in favor of its use. (Citation omitted.)

 

This court held the Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b)(2) privilege applied to bar a claim for abuse of process for the filing of an allegedly false document valuing a husband’s medical practice in a dissolution action. In Carden v. Getzoff, supra, 190 Cal. App. 3d at page 913, we stated, “While courts have argued about the scope of the privilege [citation], it is clear that ‘the privilege has been applied to publications which were private communications between parties and which communications were related not only to actual but potential court actions.’ [Citation.]” Thus, we held that publications made in preparing documents for submission to the court in a dissolution proceeding, as well as the defendant’s testimony at the trial, were covered by the absolute privilege of former section 47, subdivision (b)(2), because they were part of a judicial proceeding, where the dissolution action was pending, and the defendant had been hired as an expert witness for the appellant’s wife. “This situation is clearly part of a judicial proceeding … .” (Citation omitted.)

 

(Pollock v. University of Southern California (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1416, 1429-30.)

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action. The special motion to strike count one is GRANTED. 

B.        Count Two – Abuse of Process, Fraudulent Use of Process, Misuse of Process, Harassment.

            The same analysis applicable to count one also applies to count two.

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action. The special motion to strike count two is GRANTED.

C.        Count Three – Fraud, Deceit, Concealment.

            “To establish a claim for deceit based on intentional misrepresentation, the plaintiff must prove seven essential elements: (1) the defendant represented to the plaintiff that an important fact was true; (2) that representation was false; (3) the defendant knew that the representation was false when the defendant made it, or the defendant made the representation recklessly and without regard for its truth; (4) the defendant intended that the plaintiff rely on the representation; (5) the plaintiff reasonably relied on the representation; (6) the plaintiff was harmed; and (7) the plaintiff's reliance on the defendant's representation was a substantial factor in causing that harm to the plaintiff. (Citations omitted.)” (Manderville v. PCG&S Group, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1486, 1498 [italics omitted].)

            As this Court has concluded re: the first prong, the common law fraud claim based on Cross-Defendant Baldwin making misrepresentations to Cross-Complainant Villafan is not subject to being stricken under Civ. Proc. Code, § 425.16. 

As discussed above, to the extent this count is based upon an alleged attempted fraud upon this Court based upon the allegations in the Complaint, for the reasons discussed above re: count one, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a probability of prevailing on count three.

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action, to the extent it based upon an alleged attempted fraud upon this Court based upon the allegations in the Complaint. The special motion to strike count three is GRANTED in part.

D.        Count Four – Unclean Hands.

            As this Court has concluded, this count is a mixture of conduct which is not subject to being stricken—alleged misrepresentations by Baldwin to induce Villafan to renting a room to Baldwin—and conduct which is subject to being stricken—unclean hands in  filing the Complaint based upon inequitable behavior.

As discussed above, to the extent this count is based upon an alleged attempted fraud upon this Court based upon the allegations in the Complaint, for the reasons discussed above re: count one, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a probability of prevailing on count three.

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action, to the extent it based upon an alleged attempted fraud upon this Court based upon the allegations in the Complaint. The special motion to strike count four is GRANTED in part.

E.         Count Five – Unjust Enrichment.

As this Court has concluded re: the first prong, this count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.

            The same analysis applicable to count one also applies to count five.

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action. The special motion to strike count five is GRANTED.

F.         Count Six – Equitable Relief.

As this Court has concluded re: the first prong, this count is based upon Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filing the Complaint in this action.

            The same analysis applicable to count one also applies to count six.

As such, Cross-Complainant cannot demonstrate a probability of prevailing on this cause of action. The special motion to strike count six is GRANTED.

            Because Cross-Defendant’s anti-SLAPP motion, although denied in part, had a practical effect of eliminating practically all of the First Amended Cross-Complaint, Cross-Defendant’s request for an award of attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $10,139.31 pursuant to Civ. Proc. Code, § 425.16(c)(1) is GRANTED. (See Declarations of David M. Saldana and Genevieve Jenkins.)

 

A defendant prevailing on a special motion to strike is entitled to recover its attorney fees and costs. (§ 425.16, subd. (c)(1); Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131 [104 Cal. Rptr. 2d 377, 17 P.3d 735].) The defendant can recover only its fees and costs in connection with the motion, not the entire action. (Citations omitted.)

 

A defendant need not succeed in striking every challenged claim to be considered a prevailing defendant entitled to recover attorney fees and costs under the statute. Instead, a defendant is entitled to recover fees and costs in connection with a partially successful motion, unless the results obtained are insignificant and of no practical benefit to the defendant. (Citation omitted.) A court awarding fees to the prevailing defendant on a partially successful special motion to strike must exercise its discretion in determining the amount of fees and costs to award in light of the defendant's relative success in achieving its litigation objectives. (Citation omitted].)


(City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 191, 218 [bold emphasis and underlining added].)

Motion To Declare Vexatious Litigant

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Wayne Baldwin  brings s a motion to declare Defendant/Cross-Complainant Connie Villafan a vexatious litigant and to require that she deposit with the Court a security of no less than $5,000, or such amount as the Court sees fit, each time she files any further motions in the instant litigation, pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §391, et seq.

            Civ. Proc,. Code, § 391 provides in pertinent part:

As used in this title, the following terms have the following meanings:

 (a) "Litigation" means any civil action or proceeding, commenced, maintained or pending in any state or federal court.

 (b) "Vexatious litigant" means a person who does any of the following:

. . .

   (3) In any litigation while acting in propria persona, repeatedly files unmeritorious motions, pleadings, or other papers, conducts unnecessary discovery, or engages in other tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.

. . .

 

 (c) "Security" means an undertaking to assure payment, to the party for whose benefit the undertaking is required to be furnished, of the party's reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees and not limited to taxable costs, incurred in or in connection with a litigation instituted, caused to be instituted, or maintained or caused to be maintained by a vexatious litigant.

 (d) "Plaintiff" means the person who commences, institutes or maintains a litigation or causes it to be commenced, instituted or maintained, including an attorney at law acting in propria persona.

 (e) "Defendant" means a person (including corporation, association, partnership and firm or governmental entity) against whom a litigation is brought or maintained or sought to be brought or maintained.

(Civ. Proc. Code, § 391 [bold emphasis added].)

            Civ. Proc. Code, § 391.1(a) provides:

 

In any litigation pending in any court of this state, at any time until final judgment is entered, a defendant may move the court, upon notice and hearing, for an order requiring the plaintiff to furnish security or for an order dismissing the litigation pursuant to subdivision (b) of Section 391.3. The motion for an order requiring the plaintiff to furnish security shall be based upon the ground, and supported by a showing, that the plaintiff is a vexatious litigant and that there is not a reasonable probability that he or she will prevail in the litigation against the moving defendant.

     (Civ Proc. Code, § 391.1(a) [bold emphasis and underlining added].)

            Here, the Court only examines Villafan’s actions as a Cross-Complainant, not as the defendant in the underlying action. Thus, only Villafan’s actions post-March 24, 2023—when she filed the Cross-Complaint—and in her capacity as Cros-Complainant will be considered. (Civ. Proc. Code, § 391.1(a).)

            Moving party basically argues that Defendant filing a Cross-Complaint and refusing to dismiss it— justifies the vexatious litigant declaration. However, the anti-SLAPP motion was only partially successful.

Further, moving party has not demonstrated that Villafan has repeatedly filed unmeritorious motions, pleadings, or other papers, conducts unnecessary discovery, or engages in other tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay (Civ. Proc. Code, § 391(b)(3).) Cross-Complainant successfully obtaining entry of Cross-Defendant’s default does not qualify. Nor does Villafan’s requests for a continuance to obtain assistance of counsel, her request for attorney’s fees as a pro per litigant, and an ex parte request for an extension of time to respond to the anti-SLAPP motion.

            “A court exercises its discretion in determining whether a person is a vexatious litigant. [Citation.]” (Holcomb v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1498.)

            The Court exercises its discretion in finding that the foregoing is insufficient to rise to the level of  “repeatedly fil[ing] unmeritorious motions, pleadings, or other papers” for purposes of Civ. Proc. Code § 391(b)(3).

            As such, the Court declines to declare Defendant/Cross-Complainant Connie Villafan a vexatious litigant at this time. 

            Defendants motion for an order declaring Defendant/Cross-Complainant Connie Villafan a vexatious litigant is DENIED without prejudice to a renewed motion later in the proceedings.
