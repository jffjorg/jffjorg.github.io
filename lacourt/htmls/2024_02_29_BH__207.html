DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV00182    Hearing Date: February 29, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

February 29, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21SMCV00182

MOTION

Demurrer to Answer of Chicago Title Company

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Keyway Pride Limited LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Chicago Title Company

 

MOTIONS

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Keyway Pride Limited LLC (“Plaintiff”) demurs to the general denial and all thirty-three affirmative defenses alleged in Defendant Chicago Title Company’s (“Defendant”) Answer to Plaintiff’s verified First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) for failure to state facts sufficient to cause a defense, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.20, subdivision (a).  

 

Defendant opposes the demurrer and Plaintiff replies. 

 

MEET AND CONFER REQUIREMENT

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a) requires that “Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” 

 

The statute further requires “As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(1).)  “The party who filed the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer shall provide legal support for its position that the pleading is legally sufficient or, in the alternative, how the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer could be amended to cure any legal insufficiency.”  (Ibid.)

 

“The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either” the means by which the parties met and conferred, or that the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

 

Here, Defendant asserts that when Plaintiff’s counsel contacted Defendant’s counsel to meet and confer prior to filing the instant demurrer, Plaintiff’s counsel demanded additional factual detail underlying each of Defendant’s affirmative defenses.  (Canepa Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. A.)  In response, Defendant’s counsel indicated that they had only been recently retained, did not yet know all the facts underlying the various multi-million dollar real estate transactions, and had filed a general denial and thirty-three affirmative defenses to retain Defendant’s rights while they got up to speed on the facts of the case.  (Ibid.)  Defense counsel also granted Plaintiff an extension of time to demur to the answer, and requested additional time to get up to speed on the facts of the case and meaningfully meet and confer before the demurrer was filed.  (Ibid.)  Apparently skeptical of the legal force of a 120-day extension to demur to the answer, Plaintiff filed.  (Ibid.)

 

Under the circumstances, the Court does not find that further meet and confer efforts would be fruitful.

 

LEGAL STANDARD - DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a [pleading].”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the [pleading] a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction” means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the drafter, not the demurring party.  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the [pleading] is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a [defense], the rule is that if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the [pleader] is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the [demurring party] the [pleading] will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the [defense] shown, or although the [pleader] may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      DEMURRER TO ANSWER

 

            As a threshold matter, the Court finds defects with the form of the Answer.  First, a verified complaint may not be answered by way of a general denial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.30, subd. (d); City of Hollister v. Monterey Ins. Co. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 455, 476.)

 

Second, “Where a complaint is verified, […] the answer also must be verified.”  (French v. Smith Booth Usher Co. (1942) 56 Cal.App.2d 23, 29.)  “If an answer is not sufficiently verified in such a case, it may be stricken out[.]”  (Ibid.; see also Hearst v. Hart (1900) 128 Cal. 327, 328.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff filed a verified First Amended Complaint, but Defendant responded via an unverified Answer, with only a general denial.  Therefore, the Court, on its own motion, strikes the Answer as improper.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd (b) [“The court may, […] at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper […] [s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court”].)

 

            Because the Court strikes the Answer as improper, it does not address the parties’ arguments regarding the level of factual detail underlying the general denial and affirmative defenses.

 

2.      LEAVE TO AMEND

 

The pleading party has the burden of showing in what manner the pleading could be amended and how the amendment would change the legal effect of the pleading, i.e., state an affirmative defense. (See The Inland Oversight Committee v City of San Bernardino (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 771, 779; PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 156, 189.) The pleading party must not only state the legal basis for the amendment, but also the factual allegations sufficient to state a denial or affirmative defense. (See PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.App.5th at p. 189.) Moreover, a pleading party does not meet his or her burden by merely stating in the opposition to a demurrer or motion to strike that “if the Court finds the operative [pleading] deficient, [the pleading party] respectfully requests leave to amend.” (See Major Clients Agency v Diemer (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1133; Graham v Bank of America (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 618 [asserting an abstract right to amend does not satisfy the burden].)

 

Here, Defendant has failed to meet this burden, as Defendant merely cites to Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318 (hereinafter Blank), and requests leave to amend.  (Opposition at p. 3.)  But Blank reiterates “[t]he burden of proving such reasonable possibility [that the defect can be cured by amendment] is squarely on the plaintiff.”  (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)

 

Nonetheless, because the Court strikes the Answer due to improper form only, the Court grants Defendant leave to file a code-compliant Answer. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court strikes the Answer for improper form, and overrules as moot Plaintiff’s Demurrer to the Answer. 

 

Defendant shall have up to and including March 20, 2024 to file and serve a code-compliant Answer to Plaintiff’s verified First Amended Complaint. 

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

LEGAL STANDARDS – SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.  Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law. If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”  (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839 [cleaned up].)  Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.  Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.”  (Id. at p. 840 [cleaned up]; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty if that party contends that there is no merit to the cause of action, defense, or claim for damages, or if the party contends that there is no duty owed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)  A cause of action has no merit if: (1) one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded, or (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (n); Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 583.)  Once the defendant has shown that a cause of action has no merit, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(2); Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 583.)

DISCUSSION

 

A.    ISSUE 1: SECOND CAUSE OF ACTION FOR FRAUD/INTENTIONAL MISREPRESENTATION/DECEIT

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

Sound Equity moves for summary adjudication as to issue 1 on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish the first element – that Sound Equity made or authorized any misrepresentation to her. 

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges Sound Equity “by and through its authorized officers, managing agents and designated agents/employees, Curtis Boyer and Ken Hanson, authorized and directed defendant WEGNER on that date to misrepresent to Plaintiff that SOUND EQUITY would "assist" her in "refinancing" her home[.]”  (Complaint ¶ 38.)

 

1.      Sound Equity’s Evidence

 

Sound Equity has provided evidence that (1) Defendant Andre Wegner was not ever an agent of Sound Equity (UMF 2); (2) Plaintiff did not ever meet or communicate with Ken Hansen or Curtis Boyer prior to the funding of the loan (UMF 3-4); and (3) Sound Equity did not authorize or ratify any communications between Palms and Plaintiff, Plaintiff’s execution of any documents referencing the subject property, or any action related to the transfer of title of the subject property, other than to fund the loan (UMF 5-7.)

 

Therefore, Sound Equity has met its burden to establish a prima facie case that Plaintiff cannot prove it made or authorized any misrepresentation.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

Plaintiff argues that Sound Equity was in on the scheme to defraud Plaintiff.  In support, Plaintiff has provided the following evidence:

 

·         Excerpts of the Drinkwater deposition, indicating:

 

o   Defendant Wegner unexpectedly came to her house late one evening with a stack of paperwork, urging her that she did not have time to read it, and just needed to quickly sign it. (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 87:18-88:21; 94:8-19; 96:8-97:8.) 

 

o   Plaintiff did not recognize the signatures on various documents as hers, or the name of the notary on various real estate documents, nor had any notary been present when she signed documents.  (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 97:15-98-2; 102:17-25; 128:9-130:12; 146:21-147:12; 148:3-18)

 

o   Plaintiff had never heard of Dee Brothers or Gold Constructors, even though loan proceeds in excess of $1.1 million had been paid to those entities for renovations to the subject property.  (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 143:1-144:1; 149:25-150:7; 151:4-9.)

 

·         Excerpts of the Hansen deposition, indicating:

 

o   Sound Equity began doing business with companies owned by Defendants Wegner and Sanchez in mid-2018, when Sound Equity employee Curtis Boyer brought them on as clients. (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at pp. 33:3-10, 33:20-34:2.)

 

o   By January of 2020, Sound Equity had provided approximately 25 loans to companies owned by Wegner and Sanchez, most notably Top Flight Investments, LLC, where Wegner and Sanchez were guarantors on the loans.  By January of 2020, approximately 10 of those loans had been paid off, and 12 to 15 of the loans remained outstanding, with a total outstanding loan balance of approximately $25 million. (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at pp. 35:21-25; 46:9-16; 65:16-23; 68:16-19; 69:10-18.)

 

o   At one point in time, Wegner and Sanchez were not in default on any of the loans, and at another point in time, they were in default on all of them.  (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at p. 81:7-9.)

 

o   Sound Equity did not do a background check on Defendant Wegner because its practice is to only vet client company owners or members that have over 20 percent equity in the entity, and Wegner did not have 20 percent equity in Palms Boulevard, Venice Beach, LLC, the entity Wegner and Sanchez created to purchase and renovate the subject property.  (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at pp. 32:10-19; 33:3-10; 33:20-24; 35:21-25; 36:7-37:3; 46:9-16.)

 

o   In January 2020, Ken Hansen of Sound Equity was unaware that Andre Wegner had a criminal background.  (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at p. 37:24.)

 

o   With his criminal background, Wegner would not have met Sound Equity’s standard for lending.  (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at p. 37:19-24.)

 

o   Sound Equity no longer lends money to any entity in which Defendants Wegner or Sanchez are involved because “they did not pay their bill and they lost their properties over default.”  (Ex. D. to Novak Decl. at p. 40:7-13.)

 

o   It is the responsibility of the escrow company to verify that the down payment indicated in the documents was actually paid.  (Ex. D to Novak Decl. at pp. 59:10-60:5.)

 

·         Excerpts from the Murray declaration indicating:

 

o   “Andre Wegner, or someone from Top Flight, had been forging the draw requests, sending us the forged draw requests, and had basically been pocketing the money themselves.”  (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at p. 16:17-22.)

 

o   “Andrew basically let it slip that he was using an appraiser called Jed Wiltchik, I believe his name was, who was basically appraising these properties above the contract value; i.e., we buy a property – we have a property and a contract for $2 million.  You would expect the appraisals [sic] come in at $2 million. 

You don’t appraise the property at $2.2 million.  That’s not how it’s done.  Right?  All you’re asking is if the property is worth what you have it under contract for, not if it's worth more.  Is it worth the contract value? 

What I believe Jed was doing was writing appraisals for over contract value.  So, again, using the $2 million example, we had a contract for $2 million.  You expect an appraisal to come in at $2 million.  Jed was writing contracts at two-two, three-three.

Now, you’re asking, well, what’s the benefits of that?  To my understanding, what was then happening was Andre was giving these inflated appraisals to an underwriter at Sound Equity, the underwriter being Curtis Boyer, who we discussed earlier.

Curtis was then pushing the appraisal through not at the contract value of $2 million but to the increased appraisal value – or the inflated appraisal value of, say, 2.2 million.

The benefits of this was effectively to defraud Sound Equity out of money by having them overleverage the properties.”  (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at pp. 19:4-20:9.)

 

o   “When I got hold of Ken Hansen, I had this conversation that I thought something like this was happening.  The next day Ken put Curtis Boyer on leave and stopped all payment to Top Flight.” (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at p. 22:5-8.)

 

o   Wegner told Murray that Jed Wiltchik did all of Wegner and Sanchez’s appraisals.  (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at p. 27:11-12.)

 

o   Curtis Boyer would push through the inflated appraisals he was getting from Jed Wiltchik.  (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at pp. 28:10-29:12.)

 

o   Wegner and Dee Brothers set up unlicensed shell LLCs, like Gold Contractors and Think Greener, to bill Sound Equity for construction work for the properties. (Ex. G to Novak Dec. at pp. 50:13-51:23.)

 

·         Excerpts from the deposition of Cassandra Cameron, Sound Equity’s person most qualified, indicating:

 

o   It was Sound Equity’s guidelines and practice were to run a background check and credit report on anyone owning 20% or more of an entity Sound Equity lent to.  (Ex. F to Novak Dec. at pp. 79:3-80:7)

 

o   Sound Equity’s guidelines and practice were not to lend to a company if that owner had unexplained felony offenses, such as fraud, or pending litigation related to the subject property.  (Ex. F to Novak Dec. at pp. 80:8-81:6.)

 

o   The application submitted to Sound Equity for Top Flight, which listed only Doug Sanchez as owner, with the owner percentage redacted, should have raised further questions regarding the applicant’s ability to borrow.  (Ex. F to Novak Dec. at p. 98:6-19.)

 

o   If Cameron were presented with an application showing Sanchez and Wegner owning 30% and 60% of the entity, Cameron would likely have run a background check and credit report on both Sanchez and Wegner.  (Ex. F to Novak Dec. at p. 123:3-15.)

 

·         The Top Flight Investments LLC Articles of Organization, indicating that Defendant Wegner owns 60%, Sanchez owns 30%, and Dee Brothers own 10% of Top Flight (Ex. O to Novak Decl. at p. 14.)

 

·         The Scope of Work provided by Wegner’s construction company, Think Greener, to Sound Equity, outlining $198,150.00 in estimated construction costs.  (Ex. K to Novak Decl.)

 

·         Gold Constructors’ license was revoked in June 2021 for hiring unlicensed contractors to perform work, failing to carry worker’s compensation insurance, and submitting false certificates of completion to receive payment for work not done.  (Ex. Y to Novak Decl.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has provided evidence that  

 

(1)   underwriter Curtis Boyer or someone else at Sound Equity either ran the standard background check on Wegner, as 60% owner of Top Flight, and repeatedly underwrote loans for his companies anyway, knowing Wegner was a felon and did not meet Sound Equity’s lending standards, or underwrote the loans without running Sound Equity’s standard background checks on Wegner, and therefore knew or should have known they were doing business with a convicted felon;

 

(2)   Curtis Boyer, or someone else on behalf of Sound Equity, hired Wegner’s appraiser, Jed Wiltchek, who routinely overvalued the property appraisals he did for Wegner;

 

(3)   Curtis Boyer underwrote the loans, knowingly using Jed Wiltchek’s inflated appraisal values as opposed to the contract price; and

 

(4)   Sound Equity helped Wegner launder over $1 million of the loan proceeds through shell construction companies by approving disbursements in excess of five times the estimated construction costs of approximately $200k. 

 

Therefore, there remain disputed issues of material fact as to whether Sound Equity knew or should have known that Wegner was not a qualified borrower, and whether Wegner was acting on behalf of Sound Equity when he fraudulently transferred title to the subject property out of Plaintiff’s name and granted Sound Equity foreclosure rights, in exchange for Sound Equity (through its underwriter Curtis Boyer) paying Wegner inflated loan disbursements and laundering a large portion of that money through Wegner’s shell construction companies.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 1.

 

B.     ISSUE 2: FIRST CAUSE OF ACTION FOR ELDER FINANCIAL ABUSE

 

“Financial abuse” of an elder or dependent adult occurs when a person or entity does any of the following:

 

(1)   Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(2)   Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(3)   Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.

 

(Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.30, subd. (a).)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish Sound Equity’s wrongful use or intent to defraud. 

 

1.      Defendant’s Evidence

 

In support, Defendant has provided evidence that (1) it procured an interest in the subject property via a Promissory Note secured by a Deed of Trust executed by Wegner on behalf of Palms (UMF 9-12); (2) a grant deed purporting to convey the subject property to Palms was recorded (UMF 13); (3) Sound Equity did not take, secret [sic], appropriate, obtain, or retain any of the net monetary proceeds from the sale of the subject property from Plaintiff to Palms (UMF 14); (4) Palms defaulted on its repayment obligations to Sound Equity (UMF 15); (5) so Sound Equity foreclosed on the subject property, pursuant to the power of sale in the Deed of Trust (UMF 16); and (6) the subject property was sold at public auction on March 30, 2022 (UMF 17.)

 

Therefore, Defendant has met its burden of establishing a prima facie case that Plaintiff cannot establish Sound Equity’s wrongful use or intent to defraud.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has provided evidence creating a disputed issue of material fact as to whether, and to what extent, Sound Equity’s underwriter Curtis Boyer was in on the scheme.  Plaintiff further contends Sound Equity profited from (1) the origination, underwriting, and other various fees for its services as lender; and (2) the windfall associated with the sale of the property that was financed for 80% of the inflated appraisal value.

 

As for the origination and other fees, Sound Equity charged approximately $52,000, which Palms paid Sound Equity via wire transfer on January 9, 2020 (Exs. Q & R to Novak Decl.) 

 

As for the windfall from the foreclosure sale of the property, the purchase price was $2,534,000 (Ex. L to Novak Decl.), the inflated Wiltchik appraisal came in at $2,850,000 (Ex. J to Novak Decl.), and the loan amount from Sound Equity was $2,279,700.00 (Ex. J to Novak Decl.), representing 80% of the appraisal value.  The property sold at public auction for $3,194,128.26 on March 30, 2022.  (RJN Ex. 3.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has raised disputed issues of material fact as to Sound Equity’s wrongful use and intent to defraud – namely, whether Curtis Boyer, on behalf of Sound Equity, underwrote the loan for Wegner, who Boyer knew or should have known was a convicted felon who did not meet Sound Equity’s lending criteria, knowingly using the inflated Wiltchik appraisal to underwrite a larger loan for Wegner, and helped Wegner launder a large portion of the loan proceeds through his shell construction companies, in exchange for Wegner fraudulently transferring the subject property out of Plaintiff’s name and signing a deed of trust to benefit Sound Equity upon foreclosure sale of the property.

 

Defendant argues in reply that (1) as indicated in the Murray deposition testimony, Wegner’s scheme was designed to defraud Sound Equity, not benefit it (Ex. G to Novak Decl. at p. 20:7-9); (2) Sound Equity has produced a copy of a complaint it has filed against Wegner, Sanchez, Palms, and TFI., as evidence that Sound Equity did not benefit from Wegner’s scheme (Supp. RJN 4); (3) all of Wegner’s loans went into default in August 2020, thus, at the time Sound Equity processed the loan for the subject property, Wegner had not defaulted on any of the loans; and (4) there is no evidence that Think Green’s $200k construction estimate was ever sent to or relied upon by Sound Equity in processing the loan (Ex. K to Novak Decl.)

 

The Court notes that Sound Equity seeks to foreclose on the subject properties at issue in its complaint against Wegner, Sanchez, Palms, and TFI, and if successful, Sound Equity may recoup all its loan amounts, interest, and other fees, from the foreclosure sales of those properties.  Moreover, the $200k construction estimate bears Sound Equity’s logo at the top.

 

Ultimately, Defendant’s arguments only underscore the factual dispute over whether Curtis Boyer was working with Wegner to defraud Sound Equity, or was working on behalf of Sound Equity with Wegner to defraud Plaintiff.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 2.

 

C.     ISSUE 3: FOURTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR CONVERSION

 

The elements of a conversion claim are “(1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) the defendant's disposition of the property in a manner that is inconsistent with the plaintiff's property rights; and (3) resulting damages.”  (Regent Alliance Ltd. v. Rabizadeh (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1181.)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Sound Equity did not wrongfully exercise dominion over Plaintiff’s personal property.

 

1.      Defendant’s Evidence

 

In support, Defendant has provided evidence that Sound Equity did not take, acquire, or control any of Plaintiff’s personal property or any of the net monetary proceeds from the sale of the subject property from Plaintiff to Palms. (UMF 21-22.)

 

Defendant did acquire an interest in the subject property by virtue of that sale via the Deed of Trust Palms executed, securing the Promissory Note for the purchase of the property.  (RJN 1-2.)  However, “conversion is a tort that may be committed only with relation to personal property and not real property.”  (Munger v. Moore (1970) 11 Cal.App.3d 1, 7.)

 

Therefore, Defendant has met its burden that Plaintiff cannot establish a case of conversion against Sound Equity.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues (1) Sound Equity issued the loan to Palms based on false representations; (2) disbursed $1.2 million for construction work estimated to cost only $200,000; and (3) did not investigate Wegner, a known convicted felon, who owns a 60% interest in Top Flight, an entity Sound Equity repeatedly loaned money to.  (Novak Decl. Exs. A, D, F, J, K.)

 

However, Plaintiff has not created any disputed issue of material fact that Sound Equity ever took, acquired, or controlled any of Plaintiff’s personal property, including any of the net monetary proceeds from the sale of the subject property to Palms.

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 3.

 

D.    ISSUE 4: FIFTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR CONSTRUCTIVE TRUST

 

A constructive trust “is not an independent cause of action but merely a type of remedy for some categories of underlying wrong. (Glue-Fold, Inc. v. Slautterback Corp. (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023, fn. 3 [citing 5 Witkin, Cal. Procedure (4th ed. 1997) Pleading, § 796, p. 252.])

 

Defendant argues that because Plaintiff’s first four causes of action fail, its fifth cause of action for constructive trust must also fail.  Because the Court denies summary adjudication as to the first and second causes of action for fraud and elder financial abuse, it similarly denies summary adjudication as to Issue 4.

 

E.     ISSUE 5: SIXTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR INTENTIONAL INFLICTION OF EMOTIONAL DISTRESS

 

To prevail on the Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”) cause of action, a plaintiff must prove: “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051.) A defendant’s conduct is outrageous when “it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Ibid. [cleaned up].) Further, the defendant’s conduct must be “intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Id. at p. 1051 [cleaned up].)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish that Sound Equity engaged in extreme outrageous conduct directed at Plaintiff.

 

1.      Defendant’s Evidence

 

In support, Defendant cites to the same evidence listed above, that Sound Equity merely executed a foreclosure sale as it had a right to do, pursuant to the Deed of Trust.  Therefore, Defendant has met its burden that Plaintiff cannot establish it engaged in extreme or outrageous conduct.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

In response, Plaintiff similarly cites to the same evidence that Curtis Boyer executed the underwriting and loan disbursements in furtherance of the fraudulent scheme.

 

While Plaintiff has created a disputed issue of material fact as to whether Sound Equity was involved in the fraudulent scheme, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Sound Equity, or any of its agents or employees, engaged in conduct “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 5.

 

F.     ISSUE 6: SEVENTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR NEGLIGENCE

 

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.”  (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.)

 

Defendant requests summary judgment on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish Sound Equity owed her a duty.

 

1.      Defendant’s Evidence

 

In support, Defendant has provided evidence that (1) it loaned Palms $2,279,700 for the purpose of acquiring, refinancing, and/or renovating the subject property (UMF 47); (2) Sound Equity did not enter into any agreement with Plaintiff at any time (UMF 48); (3) Sound Equity did not authorize or ratify any action related to the transfer of title of the subject property, other than to fund the loan (UMF 49); (4)  Plaintiff never met with Ken Hansen or Curtis Boyer of Sound Equity prior to the funding of the loan (UMF 50-51); (5) Andre Wegner was never an agent of Sound Equity (UMF 53); and (6) Sound Equity never authorized or ratified any communications between Palms and Plaintiff (UMF 52.)

 

Thus, Defendant has met its burden of demonstrating that Plaintiff cannot establish Sound Equity owed her a duty of care.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

In opposition, Plaintiff has provided evidence discussed above that (1) Sound Equity conducted several transactions with known convicted felon, Wegner, notwithstanding its policy to investigate anyone owning more than a 20% interest in any borrowing entity; (2) Sound Equity willingly distributed funds for construction that was never done in an amount five times the amount estimated for construction when the loan issued; and (3) the loan from Sound Equity to Palms was based on multiple false representations.  Plaintiff also argues that Sound Equity ratified the fraudulent transfer by accepting the subject property as collateral for the loan.

 

            In regard to the first three arguments, Plaintiff’s evidence does not demonstrate that Sound Equity owed Plaintiff any duty of care to not issue a loan to Wegner/Palms or authorize loan disbursements to Wegner’s closely-held construction companies.

 

As for ratification, as discussed above, Plaintiff has provided evidence that Wegner may have been acting for the ultimate benefit of Sound Equity when he fraudulently transferred the subject property from Plaintiff to Palms, and then transferred foreclosure rights to Sound Equity in exchange for Sound Equity overpaying and laundering loan proceeds for Wegner’s benefit.  Although Ken Hansen placed Curtis Boyer on leave as soon as he discovered what was going on ((Ex. G to Novak Decl. at p. 22:5-8), Sound Equity nonetheless retained the benefit of the bargain by initiating a foreclosure sale of the property and retaining the sale proceeds (RJN 3.) 

 

Thus, to the extent Wegner owed and breached a duty of care to Plaintiff, there remains a disputed issue of material fact as to whether Wegner’s negligence can be imputed to Sound Equity under a theory of ratification, by virtue of Curtis Boyer’s conduct.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 6.

 

G.    ISSUES 7 AND 8: EIGHTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR INJUNCTIVE RELIEF AND NINTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR DECLARATORY RELIEF

 

Defendant argues that summary adjudication is appropriate as to the eighth cause of action for injunctive relief because Plaintiff cannot establish Sound Equity engaged in unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practices.  Defendant also argues that summary adjudication is appropriate as to the ninth cause of action for declaratory relief because Plaintiff cannot establish any wrongful conduct by Sound Equity.

 

As discussed above, the Court denies summary adjudication as to Issues 1 and 2, finding disputed issues of material fact as to whether Sound Equity engaged in fraudulent conduct or committed elder financial abuse.  Therefore, the Court similarly denies summary adjudication as to Issues 7 and 8.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated above, the Court grants in part and denies in part Sound Equity’s motion for summary adjudication. 

 

Sound Equity’s motion for summary adjudication is granted as to Issue 3 (Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for conversion) and Issue 5 (Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress).

 

Sound Equity’s motion for summary adjudication is denied as to Issue 1 (Plaintiff’s second cause of action for fraud); Issue 2 (Plaintiff’s first cause of action for elder financial abuse); Issue 4 (Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for constructive trust); Issue 6 (Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for negligence); Issue 7 (Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for injunctive relief); and Issue 8 (Plaintiff’s ninth cause of action for declaratory relief.) 

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

 

The Court orders Sound Equity to give notice of the Court’s ruling, and to file a proof of service of such.

LEGAL STANDARDS – SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.  Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law. If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”  (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839 [cleaned up].)  Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.  Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.”  (Id. at p. 840 [cleaned up]; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty if that party contends that there is no merit to the cause of action, defense, or claim for damages, or if the party contends that there is no duty owed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)  A cause of action has no merit if: (1) one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded, or (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (n); Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 583.)  Once the defendant has shown that a cause of action has no merit, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(2); Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 583.)

DISCUSSION

 

A.    ISSUE 1: FIRST CAUSE OF ACTION FOR ELDER FINANCIAL ABUSE

 

“Financial abuse” of an elder or dependent adult occurs when a person or entity does any of the following:

 

(1)   Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(2)   Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(3)   Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.

 

(Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.30, subd. (a).)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish that Stewart Title took, hid, appropriated, obtained, or retained Plaintiff’s property for a wrongful use or with the intent to defraud Plaintiff.

 

1.      Defendant’s Evidence

 

In support, Defendant has provided evidence that

 

(1)   On December 26, 2019, it received a signed purchase agreement for the subject property, identifying Palms as the buyer and Plaintiff as the seller (UMF 15-16);

 

(2)   On January 6, 2020, Palms and Plaintiff signed and submitted their First Amendment to California Residential Purchase Agreement and Joint Escrow Instructions to Stewart Title, indicating, “Buyer(s) will cause Escrow Holder to be handed $2,534,000.00 of which $254,000.00 has been (POC) deposited with Seller’s with or prior to delivery of the within executed instruction and buyer shall deposit any additional funds and instruments required from Buyer(s) to enable Escrow Holder to comply with these instructions, which Escrow Holder is to use on or before January 10, 2020 and record any documents called for in this escrow all of which Escrow Holder may use when Escrow Holder holds for Buyer(s) a Grant Deed executed by Ginger Drinkwater, an unmarried woman, and when Escrow Holder can obtain assurance of title in the form of an Owner's Policy of Title Insurance on said property with liability limited to $2,534,000.00 on real property in the City of Los Angeles.” (UMF 17-18);

 

(3)   On January 7, the parties signed and submitted an amendment to the escrow instructions to Stewart Title (UMF 19);

 

(4)   On January 8, Stewart Title prepared an estimated master settlement statement (UMF 20);

 

(5)   Also on January 8, Stewart Title was provided with copies of a notarized grant deed, and the owner’s affidavit and indemnity (UMF 21);

 

(6)   Stewart Title also received signed instructions by Plaintiff to pay Gold Constructors $1,100,000.00 and Dee Brothers $27,380.22 at the close of escrow (UMF 22);

 

(7)   Stewart Title provided Palms and Plaintiff with its Amended Preliminary Report dated January 8, 2020, and the parties submitted the “Acknowledgment of Receipt, Understanding and Approval of the Preliminary Report” dated January 8, 2020 (UMF 23);

 

(8)   On January 13, 2020, Stewart Title provided notice to Palms and Plaintiff that it completed escrow in accordance with the escrow instructions and escrow closed on January 10, 2020 (UMF 24);

 

Therefore, Defendant has met its burden of establishing a prima facie case that it merely acted as escrow holder for the transaction, following the signed escrow instructions, and Plaintiff cannot establish that Stewart Title took, hid, appropriated, obtained, or retained Plaintiff’s property for a wrongful use or with the intent to defraud Plaintiff.

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Evidence

 

Plaintiff has provided evidence that

 

(1)   Plaintiff never intended to sell the subject property (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 63:22-25; 64:24-65:6; 127:20-128:1);

 

(2)   There was no notary the night Wegner came to Plaintiff’s home, urgently asking her to sign documents without reading them (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 87:18-88:21; 94:8-19; 96:8-97:8; 97:15-98:2);

 

(3)    Plaintiff never spoke to anyone at an escrow company as part of the transaction (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at p. 102:17-25);

 

(4)   Plaintiff never received any notices about the transaction (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 127:20-128:1);

 

(5)   Plaintiff had not seen the purchase agreement, and Plaintiff’s initials at the bottom of the purchase agreement do not match her signature (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 128:9-130:12);

 

(6)   Plaintiff never saw the escrow instructions directing payments to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 143-144);

 

(7)   Plaintiff did not sign the estimated settlement statement (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 146:21-147:12);

 

(8)   Plaintiff never met notary Nancy Rico, and no notary had been to Plaintiff’s house in the preceding four years (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at p. 148:3-18); and

 

(9)   Plaintiff does not recall ever having seen the settlement statement from Noe Gonzalez at Stewart Title to Plaintiff (Ex. A to Novak Decl. at p. 148:23-149:2).

 

The Court notes that the shapes of Plaintiff’s initials “G” and “D” as signed at the bottom of the original purchase agreement do not match the shapes of the capital “G” and “D” in Plaintiff’s signature on Plaintiff’s driver’s license or Plaintiff’s signatures on the other escrow documents.  (Compare UMF 15-16 [purchase agreement] with Ex. W to Novak Decl. [Plaintiff’s driver’s license] and UMF 17-19, 21-23 [other escrow documents].)  Thus, Plaintiff has produced evidence that Stewart Title knew or should have noticed that Plaintiff’s signature at the bottom of the original Purchase Agreement differs from Plaintiff’s signature on all other escrow documents. 

 

There is also conflicting evidence as to whether notary Nancy Rico was actually present when Plaintiff purportedly signed the grant deed.  (Compare UMF 21 [notarized grant deed] and Ex. E to Novak Decl. at pp. 21:10-24:15 [Nancy Rico testimony that she was present at Plaintiff’s home with Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s son when Plaintiff signed the notarized grant deed and other “seller’s package” escrow documents] with Ex. A to Novak Decl. at p. 148:3-18) [Plaintiff’s testimony that she had never met Nancy Rico and no other notary had been to her house in the preceding four years].) 

 

There is further conflicting evidence as to whether Stewart Title escrow officer Noe Gonzalez ever communicated with Plaintiff.  (Compare Ex. B to Novak Decl. at pp. 45:25-2; 67:10-19; 71:21-72:22 with Ex. A to Novak Decl. at pp. 102:17-25; 127:20-128:1; 146:21-147:12; 148:3-18; and 148:23-149:2.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has met her burden of production in establishing disputed issues of material fact as to whether Defendant wrongfully granted the subject property to Palms and diverted the loan proceeds to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors with the intent to defraud Plaintiff, with the assistance of Defendant.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 1.

 

B.     ISSUE 2: SECOND CAUSE OF ACTION FOR FRAUD/INTENTIONAL MISREPRESENTATION/DECEIT

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to issue 2 on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title made any representation to Plaintiff; that Stewart Title knowingly made a false representation to Plaintiff; that Plaintiff justifiably relied on any such misrepresentation; or that such reliance caused Plaintiff’s damages. 

 

As discussed above, there is a disputed issue of material fact as to whether Stewart Title’s notary Nancy Rico, or another Stewart Title employee fabricated the purportedly notarized grant deed and other “seller’s package” escrow documents.  Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s only proof is her own self-serving statements, but provide no binding legal authority for the Court to grant summary adjudication on this basis.  Ultimately, it is for the trier of fact to weigh conflicting evidence.

 

However, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Stewart Title made any other misrepresentations to Plaintiff or any evidence that Plaintiff relied on any such representations or misrepresentations.  Plaintiff’s deposition testimony indicates Plaintiff never met or communicated with anyone at Stewart Title or saw any of the escrow documents purportedly bearing Plaintiff’s signature prior to the close of escrow. 

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 2.

 

C.     ISSUE 3: THIRD CAUSE OF ACTION FOR BREACH OF THE DUTY OF GOOD FAITH AND FAIR DEALING

 

Every contract “imposes upon each party a duty of good faith and fair dealing in its performance and its enforcement.”  (McClain v. Octagon Plaza, LLC (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 784, 798.)  “A third party should not be permitted to enforce covenants made not for his benefit, but rather for others . . . .”  (Gulf Ins. Co. v. TIG Ins. Co. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 422, 430.)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Plaintiff does not have and cannot establish that Stewart Title engaged in any wrongful conduct.

 

As discussed above, there remain disputed issues of material fact as to whether Stewart Title’s notary Nancy Rico, or another Stewart Title employee fabricated the purportedly notarized grant deed and other “seller’s package” escrow documents, and whether Noe Gonzalez actually communicated with Plaintiff or sent Plaintiff any of the escrow documents.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 3.

 

D.    ISSUE 4: FOURTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR CONVERSION

 

The elements of a conversion claim are “(1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) the defendant's disposition of the property in a manner that is inconsistent with the plaintiff's property rights; and (3) resulting damages.”  (Regent Alliance Ltd. v. Rabizadeh (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 1177, 1181.)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title substantially interfered with Plaintiff’s property.

 

As discussed above, there remain disputed issues as to whether Stewart Title’s notary Nancy Rico, or another Stewart Title employee substantially interfered with Plaintiff’s property by fabricating the purportedly notarized grant deed and other “seller’s package” escrow documents, resulting in the deed to the subject property being fraudulently transferred to Palms and the substantial portions of the loan proceeds being disbursed to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 4.

 

E.     ISSUE 5: FIFTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR CONSTRUCTIVE TRUST

 

“Under Civ. Code, §§ 2223, 2224, and the case law applying them, a constructive trust may only be imposed where the following three conditions are satisfied: (1) the existence of a res (property or some interest in property); (2) the right of a complaining party to that res; and (3) some wrongful acquisition or detention of the res by another party who is not entitled to it.”  (Communist Party v. 522 Valencia, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 980, 981.)

 

Civil Code section 2224 provides: “One who gains a thing by fraud, accident, mistake, undue influence, the violation of a trust, or other wrongful act, is, unless he or she has some other and better right thereto, an involuntary trustee of the thing gained, for the benefit of the person who would otherwise have had it.”

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication on the basis that Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title took possession of her property by fraud, accident, mistake, undue influence, or any other wrongful act.

 

But as discussed above, there remain disputed issues of material fact as to whether Stewart Title’s notary Nancy Rico, or another Stewart Title employee fabricated the purportedly notarized grant deed and other “seller’s package” escrow documents, resulting in the fraudulent transfer of Plaintiff’s home to Palms and the fraudulent disbursement of loan funds to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 5.

 

F.     ISSUE 6: SIXTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR INTENTIONAL INFLICTION OF EMOTIONAL DISTRESS

 

To prevail on the Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”) cause of action, a plaintiff must prove: “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051.) A defendant’s conduct is outrageous when “it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Ibid. [cleaned up].) Further, the defendant’s conduct must be “intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Id. at p. 1051 [cleaned up].)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title engaged in outrageous conduct, or that Stewart Title’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s severe emotional distress.

 

As discussed above, although Plaintiff has created a disputed issue of material fact as to whether notary Nancy Rico or another Stewart Title employee fabricated escrow documents, or whether Stewart Title escrow officer Noe Gonzalez actually communicated with or provided escrow documents to Plaintiff, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Stewart Title engaged in conduct “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” 

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 6.

 

G.    ISSUE 7: SEVENTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR NEGLIGENCE

 

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.”  (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title breached a duty, if any, owed to Plaintiff or that such breach caused Plaintiff’s damages.

 

“An escrow agent is a fiduciary of the parties to the escrow.”  (Farmers & Merchants Trust Co. v. Vanetik (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 638, 656, as modified (Mar. 27, 2019).)  Thus, Stewart Title owed Plaintiff a duty of care, as a party to the escrow.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has created a disputed issue of material fact as to whether notary Nancy Rico or another Stewart Title employee fabricated escrow documents, and whether Stewart Title escrow officer ever communicated with Plaintiff or sent Plaintiff escrow documents, resulting in the deed to Plaintiff’s property being fraudulently transferred to Palms and loan proceeds being improperly disbursed to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors without Plaintiff’s knowledge or consent.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 7.

 

H.    ISSUE 8: EIGHTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR INJUNCTIVE RELIEF

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title engaged in unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practices.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has created a disputed issue of material fact as to whether notary Nancy Rico or another Stewart Title employee engaged in unlawful, unfair, and/or fraudulent business practices by fabricating purportedly notarized escrow documents, including the grant deed to Palms, and whether Stewart Title escrow officer Noe Gonzalez withheld escrow documents from Plaintiff and processed the funds disbursement without ever communicating with Plaintiff.

 

Therefore, the Court cannot grant summary adjudication as to Issue 8.

 

I.       ISSUE 9: NINTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR DECLARATORY RELIEF

 

Plaintiff’s ninth cause of action seeks “a declaration of rights as to her ownership interest in the Subject Property.”

 

“When seeking summary judgment on a claim for declaratory relief, the defendant must show that the plaintiff is not entitled to a declaration in its favor by establishing “(1) the sought-after declaration is legally incorrect; (2)[the] undisputed facts do not support the premise for the sought-after declaration; or (3) the issue is otherwise not one that is appropriate for declaratory relief.”  (Cates v. California Gambling Control Com. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1302, 1307.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish Stewart Title claims any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest to the subject real property.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant.  Although Plaintiff has provided some evidence that Stewart Title may have participated in the fraudulent transfer of Plaintiff’s property to Palms and the improper disbursement of loan proceeds to Dee Brothers and Gold Constructors, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Stewart Title claims any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest to the subject property.

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 9.

 

J.      ISSUE 10: TENTH CAUSE OF ACTION FOR WRONGFUL FORECLOSURE

 

“The basic elements of a tort cause of action for wrongful foreclosure […] are: (1) the trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property pursuant to a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust; (2) the party attacking the sale (usually but not always the trustor or mortgagor) was prejudiced or harmed; and (3) in cases where the trustor or mortgagor challenges the sale, the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from tendering.”  (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 408.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish standing or that Stewart Title caused a foreclosure sale of Plaintiff’s home under a power of sale in a mortgage/deed of trust.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant.  Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that Stewart Title caused the foreclosure sale of Plaintiff’s home under the power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust.

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 10.

 

K.    ISSUE 11: PLAINTIFF’S CLAIM FOR PUNITIVE DAMAGES

 

Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a), provides for punitive damages “where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice[.]”  “Malice” is “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Id., subd. (b)(1).)  “Oppression” is “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”  (Id., subd. (b)(2).)  “Fraud” is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”

 

Additionally, Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657.5, subdivision (c) requires the standards set forth in subdivision (b) of Section 3294 of the Civil Code to be satisfied before punitive damages may be imposed against an employer found liable for financial abuse.  Civil Code Section 3294, subdivision (b) provides:

 

An employer shall not be liable for [punitive damages] based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  With respect to an employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish by clear and convincing evidence that Stewart Title acted with malice, oppression, fraud, or in conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others in the commission of a tort.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has created a triable issue of material fact as to whether Stewart Title employees acted fraudulently or in conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights. 

 

However, Plaintiff has not produced any evidence that an officer, director, or managing agent of Stewart Title committed any oppression, fraud, or malice or had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employees who committed such oppression, fraud, or malice.  The only evidence Plaintiff has produced in this regard is deposition testimony of Stewart Title escrow officer Noe Gonzalez indicating that manager approval was required (and obtained) prior to disbursing funds in excess of one million dollars to Gold Constructors.  (Ex. B to Novak Decl. at pp. 100:9-19; 101:3-13.)

 

For purposes of punitive damages, “[t]he term ‘managing agent’ includes only those corporate employees who exercise substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decisionmaking so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy.”    (Powerhouse Motorsports Group, Inc. v. Yamaha Motor Corp., U.S.A. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 867, 886.) 

 

Plaintiff has provided no evidence that the manager who approved the loan disbursement to Gold Constructors had the power or authority to determine corporate policy at Stewart Title.

 

Therefore, the Court grants summary adjudication as to Issue 11.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated above, the Court grants in part and denies in part Stewart Title’s motion for summary adjudication. 

 

Sound Equity’s motion for summary adjudication is granted as to Issue 2 (Plaintiff’s second cause of action for intentional misrepresentation); Issue 6 (Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress); Issue 9 (Plaintiff’s ninth cause of action for declaratory relief); Issue 10 (Plaintiff’s tenth cause of action for wrongful foreclosure; and Issue 11 (Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.)

 

Sound Equity’s motion for summary adjudication is denied as to Issue 1 (Plaintiff’s first cause of action for elder financial abuse); Issue 3 (Plaintiff’s third cause of action for breach of the duty of good faith and fair dealing; Issue 4 (Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for conversion); Issue 5 (Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for constructive trust); Issue 7 (Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for negligence); and Issue 8 (Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for injunctive relief.)

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

 

The Court orders Stewart Title to give notice of the Court’s ruling, and to file a proof of service of such.

Ex Parte Application for TRO and OSC re: Preliminary Injunction

Plaintiff Christian Taylor

OPPOSING PARTIES

EX PARTE MOTION

 

This case arises from a dispute concerning Plaintiff Christian Taylor’s (“Plaintiff”) failure to make certain payments and ultimate default on the mortgage on his property located at 311 Market Street.  Plaintiff contends he relied on his then-live-in boyfriend to make the payments (using Plaintiff’s money), including the final payoff payment, but later discovered the payments were not actually sent to pay the mortgage.

 

Plaintiff challenges many of the amounts itemized in the outstanding balance statement, but offered to pay the balance of the amounts.  Defendants did not accept Plaintiff’s offer to tender payment.

 

Plaintiff now moves ex parte for a temporary restraining order (TRO) to enjoin the nonjudicial foreclosure sale of his property, currently scheduled for March 12, 2024, and for an order to show cause why a preliminary injunction should not issue.

 

Defendants Investment Management Company LLC and Brian Boren oppose Plaintiff’s ex parte application. 

 

LEGAL STANDARDS – TRO AND PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

A party seeking a TRO must establish “(1) irreparable injury to the moving party without the TRO; (2) no harm to the public interest; (3) no substantial harm to other interested parties;  and (4) a likelihood of prevailing on the merits.”  (Sarale v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 225, 243.)

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 527, subdivision (a), “[a] preliminary injunction may be granted at any time before judgment upon a verified complaint, or upon affidavits if the complaint in the one case, or the affidavits in the other, show satisfactorily that sufficient grounds exist therefor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 527, subd. (a).) “The purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending final resolution upon a trial.” (Grothe v. Cortlandt Corp. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1316.) The status quo has been defined to mean the last actual peaceable, uncontested status which preceded the pending controversy. (14859 Moorpark Homeowner’s Assn. v. VRT Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1396. 1402.) Preliminary injunctive relief requires the use of competent evidence to create a sufficient factual showing on the grounds for relief. (See, e.g., ReadyLink Healthcare v. Cotton (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016; Ancora-Citronelle Corp. v. Green (1974) 41 Cal.App.3d 146, 150.)

 

The trial court considers two factors in determining whether to issue a preliminary injunction: (1) the likelihood the plaintiff will prevail on the merits of its case at trial, and (2) the interim harm the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied as compared to the harm the defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants a preliminary injunction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 526, subd. (a).) The balancing of harm between the parties “involves consideration of such things as the inadequacy of other remedies, the degree of irreparable harm, and the necessity of preserving the status quo.” (Husain v. McDonald’s Corp. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 860, 866-67.)

 

“The decision to grant a preliminary injunction rests in the sound discretion of the trial court ... before the trial court can exercise its discretion the applicant must make a prima facie showing of entitlement to injunctive relief. The applicant must demonstrate a real threat of immediate and irreparable injury.” (Triple A Machine Shop, Inc. v. State of Cal. (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 131, 138.) “[A]n injunction is an unusual or extraordinary equitable remedy which will not be granted if the remedy at law (usually damages) will adequately compensate the injured plaintiff,” and the party seeking injunctive relief bears the burden to prove its absence. (Department of Fish & Game v. Anderson-Cottonwood Irrigation Dist. (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 1554, 1564-1565.)

           

ANALYSIS

 

A.    PROBABILITY OF SUCCESS ON THE MERITS

 

A TRO or preliminary injunction may not issue unless it is “reasonably probable that the moving party will prevail on the merits. (San Francisco Newspaper Printing Co., Inc. v. Superior Court (1985) 170 Cal.App.3d 438, 442; see Costa Mesa City Employees’ Association v. City of Costa Mesa (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 298, 309 [no injunction may issue unless there is at least “some possibility” of success].)

 

Plaintiff makes the following arguments as to why the requested TRO should issue: (1) Defendants are improperly charging late charges based upon a percentage of the total unpaid balance, in violation of California law; (2) Defendants improperly charge the increased 18% interest from the inception of the loan, instead of from the date the notice of default was recorded; (3) Defendants’ statement balance mischaracterizes foreclosure and other fees as “advances” and charges more than the statutorily-permitted amounts; (4) Defendants improperly seek to foreclose on both of Plaintiff’s properties, 311 Market Street and 313 Market Street, despite the fact that the Deed of Trust granted a power of sale only as to the 311 Market Street property; and (5) when these improper amounts are deducted from the outstanding balance, Plaintiff has tendered payment in full.  The Court analyzes each in turn.

 

a.      Improper Late Charges

 

Plaintiff first contends that the late charges Defendant is charging, which amount to an additional 6% interest rate on the full unpaid balance, violates California law.  In support, Plaintiff cites to Honchariw v. FJM Private Mortgage Fund, LLC (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 893, 905-906; Garrett v. Coast and Southern Federal Savings and Loan Association (1973) 9 Cal.3d 731; and Civil Code section 1671.

 

Civil Code section 1671 provides, in relevant portion, “a provision in a contract liquidating the damages for the breach of the contract is valid unless the party seeking to invalidate the provision establishes that the provision was unreasonable under the circumstances existing at the time the contract was made.”  (Civ. Code, § 1671, subd. (b).)

 

Honchariw and Garrett both stand for the proposition that a late fee charged for making a single late payment, as a percentage of the total outstanding loan balance, is unreasonable.  However, Honcariw specifically notes that, “[D]efault interest following note maturity has long been allowed in California without resort to a liquidated damages analysis.”  (83 Cal.Ap.5th at 905, fn. 8.)

 

Paragraph 6 of Plaintiff’s mortgage note provides:

 

IF BORROWER IS IN DEFAULT UNDER THIS NOTE…, AND THE NOTE HOLDER HAS RECORDED A NOTICE OF DEFAULT …, THEN THE ENTIRE UNPAID PRINCIPAL BALANCE SHALL AUTOMATICALLY BEAR AN ANNUAL INTEREST RATE (INSTEAD OF THE RATE SPECIFIED IN PARAGRAPH 3) EQUAL TO THE LESSER OF (A) SIX PERCENTAGE POINTS (6%) PER ANNUM ABOVE THE RATE OF INTEREST OTHERWISE APPLICABLE THEREUNDER, OR (B) THE MAXIMUM INTEREST RATE ALLOWED BY LAW (THE “DEFAULT RATE”).

 

(Ex. A to Taylor Decl.)

 

            Thus, the additional 6% being charged is default interest, not a late charge for a single missed payment.  Thus, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits that the additional 6% interest is prohibited by California law.

 

b.      Charges In Excess of Those Agreed

 

Plaintiff next contends that the charges exceed those agreed to in the note because Defendants are charging the increased interest on all outstanding payments, from the inception of the loan, not just those payments after the notice of default.

 

The note provides:

 

IF BORROWER IS IN DEFAULT UNDER THIS NOTE…, AND THE NOTE HOLDER HAS RECORDED A NOTICE OF DEFAULT…, THEN THE ENTIRE UNPAID PRINCIPAL BALANCE SHALL AUTHOMATICALLY BEAR AN ANNUAL INTEREST RATE (INSTEAD OF THE RATE SPECIFIED IN PARAGRAPH 3) EQUAL TO THE LESSER OF (A) SIX PERCENTAGE POITNS (6%) PER ANNUM ABOVE THE RATE OF INTEREST OTHERWISE APPLICABLE THEREUNDER, OR (B) THE MAXIMUM INTEREST RATE ALLOWED BY LAW (THE “DEFAULT RATE”)

 

Thus, pursuant to the note, the increased interest rate applies to “the entire unpaid principal balance” not just the payments due after the notice of default is recorded.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of prevailing on the merits of his argument that the increased interest rate exceeds the terms of the note.

 

c.       Advances

 

Plaintiff next argues that Defendants’ ledger claims $18,758.11 for “Advance[s]” owed on the loan that relate to foreclosure, forbearance, processing, and regulation fees and costs that exceed those allowable pursuant to Civil Code sections 2924c-2924d.

 

Those sections limit the types of foreclosure costs that can be recovered, and limit the amount of trustee’s or attorney’s fees that may be recovered. 

 

However, Plaintiff has not demonstrated that the requested $18,758.11 includes types of costs that are not allowable or include trustee’s or attorney’s fees in excess of those allowed by statute.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits that the $18,758.11 in “Advance[s]” are improper.

 

d.      Foreclosure on Both of Plaintiff’s Properties

 

Plaintiff contends the Deed of Trust granted Defendants the power of sale with respect to the 311 Market property only, yet Defendants now seek to improperly foreclose on both 311 Market and the adjacent property, 313 Market, which Plaintiff also owns.

 

The Deed of Trust, dated July 5, 2018, grants a power of sale in the property associated with APN: 4238-009-006 “which has the address of 311 Market Street (Venice area) Los Angeles CA 90291.  (Ex. B to Taylor Decl.)  The Notice of Default and Notice of Trustee’s Sale reference “APN: OLD: 4238-009-006 NEW: 4238-009-024[.]”  (Exs. C-D to Taylor Decl.)  Thus, it appears the parcel number for the property in which Plaintiff granted the power of sale has changed since the Deed of Trust was executed.  But there is nothing on the face of the Notice of Default or Notice of Trustee’s Sale or any other evidence demonstrating that Defendants are improperly foreclosing on property they are not entitled to foreclose upon.

 

Therefore Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits that Defendants have improperly noticed a trustee’s sale on a property they are not entitled to foreclose upon.

 

e.       Plaintiff Tendered the Outstanding Balance

 

Plaintiff contends that although Defendants demand a payoff amount of $192,440.58, the outstanding amount Defendants may lawfully demand does not exceed $60,000, once the deductions are made in accordance with Plaintiff’s arguments above.  Plaintiff further contends that Plaintiff tendered $60,000 to Defendants, thus Plaintiff has tendered the outstanding balance in full and Defendants may not lawfully foreclose upon the property.

 

Because the Court finds Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits that the outstanding balance owed exceeds that which Defendants may lawfully collect, Plaintiff has similarly not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits that Plaintiff’s tendering of $60,000 paid off the lawfully-allowed outstanding balance in full. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, because the Court finds that Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for a temporary restraining order and order to show cause re preliminary injunction enjoining the March 12, 2024 foreclosure sale of Plaintiff’s property.
