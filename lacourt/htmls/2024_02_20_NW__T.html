23VECV04042 ANZHELA HONCHARENKO, AN IND... vs ALEK ZEVEN

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Defendants Alek Zeven and dba Zeven Law Firm’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay the Action is DENIED.  Defendants to respond to the First Amended Complaint within 30 days.

Defendants Alek Zeven and dba Zeven Law Firm’s Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED. 

Introduction

Defendants Alek Zeven and dba Zeven Law Firm (Defendant) moved to compel arbitration and stay Plaintiff Anzhela Honcharenko’s (Plaintiff) First Amended Complaint (FAC).

Discussion 

Defendant argued that Plaintiff should be compelled to arbitration and the action be stayed based upon the Retainer Agreement’s arbitration provision which states: “Client agrees that all disputes between attorney and client, including costs, fees, quality of service, claims of negligence, malpractice, and or fraud shall be submitted to binding arbitration in accordance with section 1280, et sec. California Code of Civil Procedure and said parties further agree to use the arbitration services of the Los Angeles County Bar Association, or if unavailable, or unwilling, JAMS, and in so agreeing client waives their right to a trial by judge or jury, as well as rights to appeal.”

Before the burden shifts to Plaintiff to oppose the motion, Defendant must initially prove by a preponderance of evidence the existence of the arbitration agreement and that the dispute is covered by the agreement (i.e., arbitrability).  (Valsan Partners Ltd. Partnership v. Calcor Space Facility, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 809, 817.)  Defendant argued that arbitrability of the claims is a question for the arbitrator and not the court due to the arbitration agreement’s reference to the rules of the American Arbitration Association (AAA) (i.e., a delegation clause is included in the arbitration agreement).  Defendant asserted that the Commercial Arbitration Rules apply and require the arbitrator to determine arbitrability.  However, a plain reading of the arbitration agreement showed that no reference to AAA rules is made, instead the arbitration agreement referenced JAMS as being a choice of arbitrator if the Los Angeles County Bar Association was unavailable or unwilling to arbitrate the matter.  The arbitration agreement stated: 

“Client agrees that all disputes between attorney and client, including costs, fees, quality of service, claims of negligence, malpractice, and or fraud shall be submitted to binding arbitration in accordance with section 1280, et sec. [sic] California Code of Civil Procedure and said parties further agree to use the arbitration services of the Los Angeles County Bar Association, or if unavailable, or unwilling, JAMS, and in so agreeing client waives their right to a trial by judge or jury, as well as rights to appeal.”  [Bolding added.]

The “Notice of Arbitration” showed that the references to the AAA rules did not specify that the Commercial Arbitration Rules applied.  The notices of the arbitration agreement stated:

“If they do not reach such solution within a period of 60-days, then, upon notice by either party to the other, all disputes, claims, questions, or differences from the aforementioned agreement shall be finally settled by arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association in accordance with the provisions of its Arbitration Rules.”  [Bolding added.]

Again, nothing in the Notice of Arbitration made any specific reference to or required the use of the Commercial Arbitration Rules.  As stated above, the it is upon the Court to determine arbitrability (i.e., the scope of the arbitration agreement).  (See also, First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan (1995) 514 U.S. 938, 944; Howsam v. Dean Witter Reynolds, Inc. (2002) 537 U.S. 79, 83-84; BG Group PLC v. Republic of Argentina (2014) 572 U.S. 25, 34; Jackpot Harvesting, Inc. v. Applied Underwriters, Inc. (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 719, 730.)  The Court relinquishes its authority to determine arbitrability when the parties “clearly and unmistakably” provide otherwise.  (Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson (2010) 561 US 63, 66, 68-69, fn. 1; B.D v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc. (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 931, 957-958.) 

Where the agreement does not “clearly and unmistakably” confer such power on the arbitrator, courts—rather than the arbitrator—must determine such matters.  (First Options of Chicago, Inc. v. Kaplan (1995) 514 U.S. 938, 944; Hartley v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1249, 1251-1252; Ajamian v. CantorCO2e, L.P. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 771, 781-782.)  As reviewed above, there is no clear and unmistakable provision in the arbitration agreement requiring the use of AAA’s Commercial Rules.  Because there is no clear and unmistakable delegation clause requiring use of AAA’s Commercial Rules, the Court is authorized to determine arbitrability.  The Court does not find Defendant’s argument as to AAA’s Commercial Rules to be persuasive.

Defendant argued that the claims in the instant action fall within the scope of the arbitration provision due to the broad language in the provision.  However, the scope of the retainer agreement obligated Defendant to provide legal services and obligated Plaintiff to pay for legal services.  The allegations in the FAC do not involve disputes over the legal services provided or the amounts to be paid by Plaintiff.  The allegations in the FAC are for sexual assault and battery.  Despite Plaintiff’s first cause of action (COA) being titled negligence, the gravamen of the negligence claim is for unconsented sexual and threatening comments and touching.  (FAC par. 28.)  The second through eighth COAs are also based upon unconsented sexual and threatening comments and touching.  Nothing in the FAC’s eight COAs is there any allegation that Defendant’s legal services were negligent or that malpractice is being alleged or that a dispute over fees existed.  Without claims based upon the actual legal services being negligent or the fees to be paid as being improper, there is insufficient showing that Plaintiff’s claim “arose from or are related to” the retainer agreement. 

The allegations regarding the retainer agreement and the attorney-client relationship provided background facts to support how and why Defendant had access to Plaintiff.  The attorney-client relationship is used in this action to provide context as to how the parties were at the same time and place and/or that Plaintiff was lured into certain locations by Defendant to effectuate the assault and battery.  The fact that the attorney-client relationship brought the two parties together is insufficient to present a nexus between the alleged assault and battery and the retainer agreement’s arbitration provision.  Despite broad wording, an arbitration clause does not apply to disputes unrelated to matters covered in the contract.  (Lawrence v. Walzer & Gabrielson (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 1501, 1506.) 

There are two arbitration provisions, one in the Attorney-Client Employment Agreement and one in the Notice of Arbitration.  Between the two of them, they confusingly identify AAA, LA County Bar, ARC, Judicate West, JAMS, Orange County Bar Association as the arbitration venue.  The wording varies between the two.  Also, although this is a consumer relationship between an attorney and a client, the reply mentions the AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules. 

Because the allegations in the FAC do not arise from or relate to the retainer agreement, the Court finds that Defendant did not meet his initial burden to show that the claims are within the scope of the retainer agreement or the arbitration provision.  The burden did not shift to Plaintiff.

The court also notes that H.R. 4445, the “Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021” expresses a public policy against arbitration of sexual assault cases.  H.R. 4445 amends the FAA by exempting sexual harassment and assault claims from arbitration agreements. 

Finally, the retainer agreement specifically mentions the types of claims that would be subject to arbitration (including costs, fees, quality of service, claims of negligence, malpractice, and or fraud) and the separate arbitration provision uses similar language ((such as, for example, any claim brought by you for legal malpractice and/or legal fees and cost disagreements).  There is no provision or suggestion that a claim for sexual assault would be included.

The motion to compel arbitration and stay the action is DENIED.

IT IS SO ORDERED, CLERK TO GIVE NOTICE.
