DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02294    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: W

SUSAN NAIMI v. AZUL VENTURES, LLC, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of ron rozillio and production of documents

 

Date of Hearing:        October 28, 2023                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        23VECV02294

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Susan Naimi

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 24, 2023, Plaintiff Susan Naimi filed a complaint against Defendants Azul Ventures, LLC, Ron Rozillio, and The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners’ Association for 1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 2. Violation of CC&Rs and Architectural Guidelines; 3. Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 4. Negligence; 5. Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6. Violation of CC&Rs and Architectural Guidelines; 7. Nuisance; and 8. Declaratory Relief. Plaintiff alleges this lawsuit arises out of the failure of the HOA to enforce its Declaration of Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions (“CC&Rs”) and other governing documents. Plaintiff alleges by its failure to enforce its governing documents, the HOA has permitted Rozillio to engage in a pattern of abusive conduct towards Plaintiff since acquiring the neighboring property.

 

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint.

 

Defendant Rozillio filed a Cross-Complaint against Roes 1-20 for trespass to personal property, negligence, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Rozillio dismissed the Cross-Complaint on April 30, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Ron Rozillio and Production of Documents is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Susan Naimi moves the court for an order compelling the deposition of Ron Rozillio and production of documents as well as requests sanctions against Defendant Rozillio in the amount of $3,595.00. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds Defendant Rozillio did not appear at his duly noticed deposition, refuses to appear for his deposition, and has failed to produce records in response to the Requests for Production contained in the notice of deposition.

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production . . . of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”  (CCP § 2025.450(a).) Where a motion to compel a party’s appearance and testimony at deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent, unless the court finds the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  (CCP § 2025.450(g)(1).)

 

On August 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel requested dates from Defendant to take Rozillio’s deposition and if counsel did not hear back by September 4, 2024, Plaintiff would unilaterally notice the deposition. (Shenian Decl. ¶2.) Opposing counsel responded stating that the deposition of Rozillio and Azul should be taken together and he would furnish deposition dates for the two later that week. (Shenian Decl. ¶2.) However, nothing was heard back. (Shenian Decl. ¶2.) As a result, Plaintiff unilaterally set the deposition for October 1, 2024. (Shenian Decl. ¶2.) Prior to the deposition, on September 25, 2024, Rozillio complained of service on his PO Box and claimed he would be unavailable October 1, 2024. (Shenian Decl. ¶3.) Plaintiff’s counsel invited Rozillio to furnish alternative dates to consider for rescheduling the deposition; however, Rozillio did not give dates until 2.5 months later, the earliest being mid-December. (Shenian Decl. ¶4.) Plaintiff kept the October 1, 2024 deposition on calendar. (Shenian Decl. ¶¶3-4.) Rozillio did not appear for deposition or produce demanded documents on October 1, 2024. (Shenian Decl. ¶4.) Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to further meet and confer with Rozillio regarding his discovery obligations. (Shenian Decl. ¶5.) Rozillio did not cooperate. (Shenian Decl. ¶5.)

 

Plaintiff asserts good cause exists to compel the deposition of Rozillio because he has failed to appear for his deposition without justification or produce the demanded documents. Although Rozillio gave dates in mid-December, Plaintiff argues discovery cut off is in March and mediation in February and accordingly, Rozillio’s refusal to appear within a reasonable time compromises Plaintiff ability to conduct follow-up discovery before mediation and trial.

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition of Rozillio and production of documents. The parties cannot come to an agreement. Although Rozillio gave alternative dates, Rozillio has failed to oppose this motion and explain why the deposition cannot occur for another two months. Currently, trial is set for April 28, 2025.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Ron Rozillio and Production of Documents is GRANTED. At the hearing, the parties are ordered to appear with dates they are available for the deposition of Rozillio.

 

The court DENIES the request for sanctions.


Case Number: 23VECV04438    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: W

PAUL PINCKARD vs LYFT, INC.

 

defendant lyft, inc.’s motion to strike portions from plaintiff paul pinckard’s first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 28, 2024                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04438

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Lyft, Inc.   

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Paul Pinckard   

Meet and Confer:      Yes (Hughes Decl. ¶¶10-14.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 5, 2023, Plaintiff Paul Pinckard filed a complaint against Defendant Lyft, Inc. for 1) General Negligence; 2) Negligent Hiring, Supervision and Retention; 3) Common Carrier Negligence; 4) Vicarious liability for Torts; 5) Vicarious liability for Assault and Battery; and 6) NIED. Plaintiff alleges on October 12, 2021, Plaintiff was battered by a Lyft driver while the driver was driving for Lyft. Moreover, Plaintiff alleges the driver was impaired while driving.

 

Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint on July 8, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions from Plaintiff Paul Pinckard’s First Amended Complaint is GRANTED, in part.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of Plaintiff Paul Pinckard’s First Amended Complaint, this court’s order sustaining Lyft’s Demurrer to Plaintiff Paul Pinckard’s Complaint, and granting Lyft’s Motion to Strike with leave to amend, and several other cases’ orders granting motion to strike punitive damages. (RJN, Exh. A-H.)

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Lyft, Inc. moves to strike portions of the FAC on the ground that the complaint contains allegations that are irrelevant, improperly asserted, and not drawn in conformity with the laws of this state. Plaintiff seeks to strike: (1) Page 10, Paragraph 52; (2) Page 10-11, Paragraphs 54-56; (3) Page 15-16, Paragraphs 93-94; (4) Page 16, Paragraph 95; (5) Page 18, Paragraphs 109-110; (6) entire Seventh Cause of Action at pages 20-21, Paragraphs 124-133; and (7) Page 21, Paragraph 3 of the prayer for relief.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.) Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)¿ A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿ (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.).)¿¿ 

 

Defendant seeks to strike Plaintiff’s improper amendments to the vicarious liability claims as well as Plaintiff’s allegations relating to punitive damages. First, Defendant argues Plaintiff exceeded the scope of this Court’s Order granting him leave to amend the complaint by adding new allegations to the FAC in support of causes of action that were never the subject of Lyft’s Demurrer. Moreover, Plaintiff’s added “allegation” is one of vicarious liability for assault and does not pertain to or support Plaintiff’s negligence theories of liability. Notwithstanding that Plaintiff’s addition is not a factual allegation, it pertains to Plaintiff’s Fourth or Fifth Cause of Action for vicarious liability for intentional torts, which the court did not grant leave to amend.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues they did not exceed the scope of the court’s order. Rather, the FAC’s amendments are consistent with the court’s instructions to clarify the basis of Plaintiff’s claims. The allegations of vicarious liability for assault stem directly from Lyft’s responsibility to ensure passenger safety and the risks inherent in their business model, which involves interactions between drivers and passengers.

 

The court finds the allegations in Paragraphs 52, 54, 93, 109, 124-133, and prayer for relief within the court’s order and sufficiently plead (see discussion below). While the court did not explicitly give leave to amend the fourth and fifth causes of action, the court ordered Plaintiff to clearly plead whether Plaintiff was attempting to hold Defendant liable under a respondeat superior theory for assault or a negligent hiring or supervision theory. This in turn directly affected Plaintiff’s fourth and fifth causes of action for vicarious liability.

 

However, the court agrees Paragraphs 55, 56, 94, 95 (“Pursuant to the holding in Flores”) and 110 improper. The addition of legal citations exceeds the court order. Those paragraphs are stricken. 

 

Next, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations remain insufficient because: (1) Plaintiff does not allege any facts rising to the level of oppression, fraud, or malice required to give rise to punitive damages; as well as that (2) Plaintiff cannot recover punitive damages for conduct which Lyft did not know about or ratify. Defendant contends Plaintiff does not identify any detailed allegations of Lyft’s alleged knowledge to implement adequate safety measures and merely asserting that unspecified “corporate leadership” was aware of undisclosed information regarding risks to passengers isn’t enough to show oppression, fraud or malice.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the complaint adequately alleges Lyft’s corporate leadership knowingly failed to implement adequate safety measures, despite being aware of the risks to passengers and that this decision was motivated by profit, placing passengers like Plaintiff at risk. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges the “decision to not take appropriate safety precautions was a business decision ratified by the managing agents of LYFT” and “the that the managing agents failed to implement safety precautions, despite being on notice of its drivers committing assaults on its passengers, was motivated by profit margins and the desire to employ as many LYFT drivers as possible.” (FAC ¶¶127, 128.)

 

The court finds the allegations sufficient for the purposes of a motion to strike. Plaintiff alleges managing agents of Lyft decided not to take safety precautions even though they were fully aware of the issue of assaults committed by Lyft drivers and moreover, such conduct amounts more than just mere negligence. Plaintiff alleges managing agents of Lyft willfully ignored this information in order to maximize profits.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Strike Portions from Plaintiff Paul Pinckard’s First Amended Complaint is GRANTED, in part.
