Case Number: 21AVCV00302    Hearing Date: May 21, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Larry Lee Limones, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Monique Limones (“Plaintiff”), alleges that on October 22, 2020, Plaintiff, who was 68-years old at the time, was a passenger on a bus operated by Defendants Antelope Valley Transit Authority (“AVTA”) and Transdev Services (“Transdev”) and driven by bus driver Defendant Luna (“Luna”). Plaintiff further alleges that he was sitting near the front of the bus entrance along with a young female passenger, Doe 2, when an elderly female passenger boarded the bus. Plaintiff states that he asked Doe 2 to accommodate the new elderly female passenger, Doe 2 verbally and aggressively refused then proceeded to call an unknown individual to complain about the event. Plaintiff alleges that, shortly thereafter, an unknown individual intentionally parked a car in front of the bus to prevent it from leaving the bus stop, individuals identified by Plaintiff as Doe 3 and Doe 4 boarded the bus and physically attacked Plaintiff, punching him multiple times in the head and face, before leaving with Doe 2. Plaintiff contends that Luna did not instruct Doe 3 and Doe 4 to leave the bus, did nothing to intervene or protect Plaintiff from the imminent danger of Doe 3 and Doe 4, did not notify law enforcement before the attack, and, after the attack, neither provided any assistance or First Aid to Plaintiff nor notified law enforcement or his supervisors of the attack on Plaintiff. Plaintiff suffered damages, including a traumatic brain injury.

 

On April 28, 2021, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging three causes of action for: (1) General Negligence against AVTA and Transdev; (2) Assault and Battery against Does 2 through 10, inclusive; and (3) Elder Abuse against AVTA and Transdev.

 

On July 13, 2021, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging six (6) causes of action for: (1) Assault against Does 2 through 10; (2) Battery against Does 2 through 10, (3) Elder Abuse against Does 2 through 10; (4) Negligence against AVTA, Transdev, Luna, and Does 11 through 20; (5) Elder Abuse against AVTA, Transdev, Luna, and Does 11 through 20; and (6) Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision against AVTA, Transdev, and Does 11 through 20.

 

On July 20, 2021, Plaintiff Amended the fictitious/incorrect names to the true names of several Doe defendants: Doe 2 was amended to Raven Britt; Doe 3 was amended to Raymond Britt, Jr.; and Doe 4 was amended to Breanna Britt.

 

On August 16, 2021, AVTA and Transdev brought a Demurrer to the Complaint, demurring to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action in the FAC, Elder Abuse against AVTA, Transdev, Luna, and Does 11 through 20, subsequently sustained with 14 days’ leave to amend.

 

On February 02, 2022, Plaintiff filed his Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”), alleging six causes of action for: (1) Assault against Rayvyn Britt (“Rayvyn”)[1], Raymond Britt Jr. (“Raymond Jr.”)[2], Breanna Britt (“Breanna”), and Raymond Britt Sr. (“Raymond Sr.”); (2) Battery against Rayvyn, Raymond Jr., Breanna, and Raymond Sr.; (3) Elder Abuse against Rayvyn, Raymond Jr., Breanna, and Raymond Sr.; (4) Negligence against AVTA, Transdev, and Luna; (5) Elder Abuse against AVTA, Transdev, and Luna; and (6) Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision against AVTA and Transdev.

 

On March 07, 2022, Transdev, AVTA, and Luna brought a Demurrer to the SAC, subsequently sustained without leave to amend on April 07, 2022. The Demurrer concerned the Fifth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse) against AVTA, Transdev, and Luna.

Analysis

 

Standard for Summary Judgment – The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc.¿§437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿“The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67 (“Juge”), citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Cal.¿Code Civ.¿Proc.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna are that there is no duty placed on common carriers to prevent attacks on passengers for which there was no notice. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna concede that there is a duty on common carriers to prevent reasonably foreseeable assaults by others. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna highlight City and County of San Francisco v. Superior Court (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 45 (“City and County of S. F”) described by Transdev, AVTA, and Luna as: (1) a man boarded a bus wearing soiled clothing and the bus driver did not otherwise notice anything unusual about the man; (2) seconds later, the new passenger stabbed the plaintiff; and (3) the appellate court determined that there was an absence of evidence that the driver had warning of the attack and summary judgment could not be entered. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna draw a parallel between City and County of S. F and the action at hand, citing to the events that unfolded in their separate statement. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna believe that Luna would have had to have known the following in order for the assault to be foreseeable: (1) when Rayvyn talked to an unknown person on her phone and told that person that Plaintiff had touched her, that statement would be interpreted by the unknown individual as a request for that unknown individual to assault the Plaintiff; (2) the unknown individual would agree to assault the Plaintiff or to recruit another individual to assault the Plaintiff; (3) the unknown individual that agreed to assault the Plaintiff would appear at a bus stop prior to Plaintiff exiting the bus; (4) that Raymond Jr. was the unknown individual who would appear to assault Plaintiff before Rayvyn left the bus; (5) that it was Raymond Jr. who appeared at the bus stop (i.e., Luna would have needed to know who Raymond Jr. was and that Rayvyn was speaking with Raymond Jr.) where the assault occurred; and (6) that Raymond Jr. had either agreed or decided himself to assault the Plaintiff.

 

The City and County of S. F court emphasizes that the factual allegations underlying the event were not in dispute and “[t]here was nothing about [the assailant] or his appearance to raise any suspicion.” (City and County of S. F, supra, 32C Cal.App.4th at 47.) The City and County of S. F provided the following legal standard:

 

Common carriers are not, however, insurers of their passengers' safety. Rather, the degree of care and diligence which they must exercise is only such as can reasonably be exercised consistent with the character and mode of conveyance adopted and the practical operation of the business of the carrier. [Citations.]” (Lopez, supra, 40 Cal. 3d at pp. 785-786.) Perhaps for this reason, the duty  to protect passengers from assault lies only when the common carrier knows or should know that an assault is about to occur. The Lopez court cited with approval Terrell v. Key System (1945) 69 Cal. App. 2d 682, 686-688 [159 P.2d 704], in which a train operator was held liable for one passenger's assault upon another only because the train conductor had “ ‘notice of . . . boisterous and vituperative conduct of . . . drunken crap shooters’ ” and failed to take steps to prevent the situation from escalating to physical assault. (Lopez, supra, 40 Cal. 3d at p. 786, quoting Terrell, supra, 69 Cal. App. 2d at p. 688.)

 

No California case has held a common carrier liable for a sudden assault which occurs with no warning. Such liability would literally require an armed presence on every common carrier, which is precisely what Lopez decried: “Finding such a duty [to protect passengers from assault] to exist is not the functional equivalent of finding a duty to provide an armed security guard on every bus. There are a number of actions a carrier might take short of placing an armed guard on each bus which, in a given case, might be sufficient to meet the duty imposed by Civil Code section 2100. For instance, where the disorderly conduct of certain passengers threatens the safety of others, the bus driver . . . might warn the unruly passengers to quiet down or get off the bus [citations]; alert the police and summon their assistance [citations]; or, if necessary, eject the unruly passengers [citation].” (40 Cal. 3d at p. 787.) At least one California court has noted, albeit in dictum, that there is no duty to prevent criminal assailants from boarding buses in the first place. ( Taeleifi v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1982) 130 Cal. App. 3d 366, 369 [181 Cal. Rptr. 697], overruled on other grounds in Lopez, supra, 40 Cal. 3d at p. 787, fn. 4.)

 

(Id. at 48-49.)

The focus of Transdev, AVTA, and Luna’s argument is (1) duty of a common carrier based on (2) foreseeability.

 

As a general rule one has no duty to control the conduct of another, and no duty to warn those who may be endangered by such conduct. (Rest.2d Torts, § 315; Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197, 203; Thompson v. County of Alameda, supra, 27 Cal.3d 741, 751; Tarasoff v. Regents of University of California (1976) 17 Cal.3d 425, 435.) A duty may arise, however, where "(a) a special relation exists between the actor and the third person which imposes a duty upon the actor to control the third person's conduct, or (b) a special relation exists between the actor and the other which gives the other a right to protection." (Rest.2d Torts, § 315; Davidson v. City of Westminster, supra, at p. 203; Thompson v. County of Alameda, supra, at pp. 751-752; Tarasoff v. Regents of University of California, supra, at p. 435.) Among the commonly recognized special relationships are that between a common carrier and its passengers, that between an innkeeper and his or her guests, and that between a possessor of land and members of the public who enter in response to the landowner's invitation. (Rest.2d Torts, § 314A.) The duty of common carriers was discussed, ante.

      ii.            Sixth Cause of Action (Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision)

 

Transdev, AVTA, and Luna argue that as Luna was operating the bus in the course and scope of his employment and that factual allegation has been admitted, they are entitled to summary adjudication of this claim under Diaz v. Carcamo (2011) 51 Cal.4th 1148 (“Diaz”). Transdev, AVTA, and Luna further argue that there is no evidence for key elements of this claim as a plaintiff must show “ ‘[t]he principal . . . has reason to know that the servant or other agent, because of his qualities, is likely to harm others in view of the work or instrumentalities entrusted to him . . . Liability results under the rule stated in this Section, not because of the relation of the parties, but because the employer antecedently had reason to believe that an undue risk of harm would exist because of the employment[]’ ” and so Plaintiff must show that Transdev was negligent in its hiring or training processes. (Motion for Summary Judgment 10:2-6 [citing Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th, 842-43.)

 

Diaz concerns negligent entrustment. Specifically, the Diaz court states:

 

1.      “If, as here, a plaintiff asserts both theories, and the employer admits vicarious liability for any negligent driving by its employee, can the plaintiff still pursue the negligent entrustment claim? The answer is “no,” as we held in Armenta v. Churchill (1954) 42 Cal.2d 448 [267 P.2d 303] (Armenta).” (Diaz, supra, 51 Cal.5th 1148, 1152.)

2.      “In 2005, the Court of Appeal in Jeld-Wen, supra, 131 Cal.App.4th 853, rejected a similar argument. Negligent entrustment, that court reasoned, may establish an employer's own fault but “ ‘should not impose additional liability’ ”; instead, “ ‘the employer's liability cannot exceed [that] of the employee.’ ” (Id. at p. 871, italics added by Jeld-Wen.) We agree. As Jeld-Wen noted, if an employer admits vicarious liability for its employee's negligent driving in the scope of employment, “the damages attributable to both employer and employee will be coextensive … .” (Ibid.) Thus, when a plaintiff alleges a negligent entrustment or hiring cause of action against the employer and the employer admits vicarious liability for its employee's negligent driving, the universe of defendants who can be held responsible for the plaintiff's damages is reduced by one—the employer—for purposes of apportioning fault under Proposition 51. Consequently, the employer would not be mentioned on the special verdict form. The jury must divide fault for the accident among the listed tortfeasors, and the employer is liable only for whatever share of fault the jury assigns to the employee.” (Id. at 1159.)

 

Here, there is no claim for negligent entrustment. Rather, there is a claim for Negligence based on a common carrier’s duty and Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision. There is no holding in Diaz that shows that if an employer admits that the employee was acting in the course and scope of his employment at the time of the assault, a plaintiff cannot pursue a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, and supervision, nor can the plaintiff introduce evidence related to negligent hiring, entrustment, or retention.

 

“California case law recognizes the theory that an employer can be liable to a third person for negligently hiring, supervising, or retaining an unfit employee.” (Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1054.) “Negligence liability will be imposed on an employer if it ‘knew or should have known that hiring the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm materializes.’ ” (Phillips v. TLC Plumbing, Inc. (2009) 172Cal.App.4th 1133, 1139.)

 

Transdev, AVTA, and Luna point only to UMF No. 26 in support of their argument. It is unclear how Transdev, AVTA, Luna’s UMFs No. 1-25 impact the argument for Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision as no argument is provided. The Court notes that Transdev admits that Luna is its employee in Transdev, AVTA, and Luna’s UMF No. 27. The Court further notes that UMF No. 28 directs the parties and the Court to UMF Nos. 1-27.

 

Transdev, AVTA, and Luna state: “For all the reasons already laid out, Plaintiff, based on indisputable facts, cannot carry the required burden of showing that Luna behaved negligently in not preventing Raymond Jr.’s assault on Plaintiff. California law does not require bus drivers to guess about what they must do to protect passengers.” (Motion for Summary Judgment 10:14-17.) Transdev concedes that “warn[ing] the unruly passengers to quiet down or get off the bus; alert the police and summon their assistance; or, if necessary, eject the unruly passengers” are examples that the California Supreme Court has provided in Lopez v. So. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780 (“Lopez”). Here, Luna did not take any action as outlined in Lopez. Transdev, AVTA, and Luna state: “As has been previously discussed, none of these actions would have prevented Raymond Jr. from assaulting Plaintiff.” (Motion for Summary Judgment, 10:20-21.) However, (1) the Court has discussed the issues of the evidence presented, ante; and (2) it is unsupported that none of these actions would have prevented Raymond Jr. from assaulting Plaintiff.

 

Transdev, AVTA, and Luna did not meet the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense for this cause of action.

 

The Court need not address the Opposition as the burden has not shifted.

 

Accordingly, summary judgment and summary adjudication are DENIED.

Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified Dec. 23, 2003.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

 

-----¿¿¿¿ 

 

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.) The Court notes that this requirement has been met. (See Demurrer by AVHD, Decl. Ryan S. Riddles ¶¶ 3-5.)

Discussion

 

Application – AVHD demurs to the Third, Fourth, and Fifth Causes of Action.

 

        i.            Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Negligent/Abuse (Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15657)

 

AVHD argues that the TAC does not adequately allege that Decedent was a dependent adult. Specifically, AVHD highlights that the alleged symptoms continue to be connected to fentanyl use and the TAC is devoid of particularized allegations regarding Decedent’s physical or mental limitations, or that Decedent was an inpatient. AVHD presents the current allegations in paragraph 64: “Upon information and belief, a question of fact exists as to whether [Decedent] was administratively admitted as an inpatient, but there is no evidence to suggest that [Decedent] would not have been admitted as an inpatient at [Defendant Hospital] in light of his medical condition including chest pains, bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia and derangement.” AVHD argues that this is not particularized as needed for a claim under the Elder Abuse Act. AVHD further argues that the TAC fails to adequately allege caretaking or custodial relationship, including a duty to prevent Decedent’s suicide; invokes the sham pleading doctrine; and does not adequately allege neglect or abuse. AVHD contends that it is subject to the immunities provided in Cal. Gov. Code §§ 855.6 and 855.8.

 

Plaintiff argues that she has sufficiently pled her Third Cause of Action because Decedent was an inpatient admitted at a general acute care hospital, directing the Court and the parties to paragraphs 30 to 41 and 61 to 70 in the TAC. Plaintiff presents that AVHD had notice of Decedent’s psychotic episode prior to transport and whether a caretaking or custodial relationship exists is a question of fact and is reserved for the jury. Plaintiff further argues (1) the sham pleading doctrine does not apply if Plaintiff is able to offer a plausible explanation for the amendment and show that a mistake was made in drafting the original pleading, Plaintiff erred in stating that only intake was occurring at the time of the underlying incident, and that reports indicate that Decedent was checked in at the time of the underlying incident; and (2) statutory immunity does not apply as Hall Ambulance already diagnosed Decedent’s mental condition prior to Decedent’s arrival at the hospital and AVHD’s failure to place Decedent on a 5150 hold falls under Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6(d).

 

On Reply, AVHD reiterates arguments within its moving papers: (1) Plaintiff’s allegations fail to satisfy the heightened pleading standard for an elder abuse claim, (2) the TAC does not adequately allege that Decedent was a dependent adult; (3) the TAC does not adequately allege that AVHD had a custodial relationship with Decedent; (4) there has been no factual allegations regarding recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice; (5) the TAC implicates; and (6) AVHD did not have a duty prevent Decedent’s suicide.

 

The Court first addresses the sham pleading doctrine. Plaintiff concedes in her current Opposition, Plaintiff stated that only intake was occurring in her Opposition to the demurrer to the SAC. (See Opp. 6:7-9.) The TAC states, in relevant part:

 

Upon information and belief, after MR. BARDIN was triaged and admitted to DEFENDANT HOSPITAL, DEFENDANT ANTELOPE VALLEY when they failed to conduct an assessment of MR. BARDIN’s mental state, and either put the bag containing the gun BARDIN used to commit suicide on his chest or allowed the bag to be placed there.

 

[. . .]

 

Upon information and belief, MR. BARDIN required urgent care due to his chest pains, bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia, and derangement, such that MR. BARDIN was transported to ANTELOPE VALLEY to be admitted as an inpatient.

 

Upon information and belief, a question of fact exists as to whether MR. BARDIN was administratively admitted as an inpatient, but there is no evidence to suggest that MR. BARDIN would not have been admitted as an inpatient at DEFENDANT HOSPITAL in light of his medical condition including chest pains bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia and derangement.

 

At all times relevant, DEFENDANTS knew of MR. BARDIN’s chest pains, bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia, derangement, and fentanyl use that “messed with his head.” MR. BARDIN’s condition, need for treatment, and inability to care for himself was comparable to the condition of persons who are compelled to live in health care facilities

 

At all times relevant, MR. BARDIN was a dependent adult pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.23(a) as he was between the age of 18 and 64 and had physical or mental limitations that restricted his or her ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his own rights.

 

At all times relevant, ANTELOPE VALLEY, HALL AMBULANCE and DOES 1 through 50 were care custodians as defined in Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.17. DEFENDANTS and each of them, had a custodial relationship with MR. BARDIN while under their care, custody, and control of MR. BARDIN.

 

At all times relevant, the actions of DEFENDANTS and each of them rose to the level of abuse when they deprived him of care and services necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering while he was in imminent danger of harming himself (as defined by Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.39).

 

At all times relevant, the actions of DEFENDANTS and each of them rose to the level of reckless neglect when they failed to protect him from health and safety hazards, including the danger of harming himself.

 

DEFENDANTS’ failures and neglect of MR. BARDIN led to the foreseeable result of MR. BARDIN hurting himself and sustaining severe and ongoing injuries

 

(TAC ¶¶ 63-70.)

 

Rancher’s declaration directs to the Court to Exhibit A for evidence of mistake. Such evidence is not considered on a Demurrer. (See ante.)

 

The Court takes judicial notice of the SAC for the purpose of the sham pleading doctrine.  

 

The SAC states, in relevant part:

 

At all times relevant herein MR. BARDIN was in the medical and/or custodial care of DEFENDANTS.       

 

[. . .]

 

DEFENDANTS, and each of them, undertook custodial care of Mr. Bardin breached their legal, professional, and custodial duties by allowing the abuse and neglect of MR. BARDIN and proximately causing injuries, as set forth more fully hereafter. . .

 

[. . .]

 

Upon arrival and intake to ANTELOPE VALLEY, DEFENDANTS failed to secure MR. BARDIN and/or search his person for illicit drugs, weapons, and/or other items which could cause harm to himself and others.

 

[. . .]

 

DEFENDANTS failed to ensure that MR. BARDIN, while in their custodial care, possessed no objects that could harm himself of others which led to his death.

 

(SAC ¶¶ 4, 17, 34, 47.)

 

There are no allegations within the SAC that intake did not occur. AVHD’s support for imputing the argument is: “Under the sham pleading doctrine, a pleader cannot circumvent prior admissions by the easy device of amending a pleading without explanation. Womack v. Lovell, 237 Cal. App. 4th 772, 787 (2015).” Plaintiff’s previous arguments are not within any filed complaint. The sham pleading doctrine is not at issue.

 

The issue of intake versus admission, which establishes a hospital-patient relationship is a question of fact and, as such, would be in the purview of the jury. However, this a demurrer and the determination of triable issues of material fact are not at issue. The Court directs the parties to its legal standard, ante, and highlights that a demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. In the TAC, admission is addressed by Plaintiff.

 

Statutory interpretation is a matter of law for the Court to decide. (See Christensen v. Lightbourne (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1251 [“'On questions of law arising in mandate proceedings, [the court] exercise[s] independent judgment.' …. Interpretation of a statute or regulation is a question of law.”]; In re J. W. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 200, 209 [“If there is no ambiguity in the language of the statute, ‘then the Legislature is presumed to have meant what it said, and the plain meaning of the language governs.’ [Citation.] ‘Where the statute is clear, courts will not “interpret away clear language in favor of an ambiguity that does not exist.” [Citation.]’ [Citation.]”]; Hernandez v. Modesto Portuguese Pentecost Assn. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1274, 1280 [“Because liability in this case turns upon a question of statutory interpretation, it is a question of law for the court to decide in a summary judgment motion.”].) 

 

Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23 provides: 

 

(a) “Dependent adult” means a person, regardless of whether the person lives independently, between the ages of 18 and 64 years who resides in this state and who has physical or mental limitations that restrict his or her ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his or her rights, including, but not limited to, persons who have physical or developmental disabilities, or whose physical or mental abilities have diminished because of age. 

 

(b) “Dependent adult” includes any person between the ages of 18 and 64 years who is admitted as an inpatient to a 24-hour health facility, as defined in Sections 1250, 1250.2, and 1250.3 of the Health and Safety Code. 

 

The Complaint alleges that Decedent’s symptoms were connected to fentanyl use, not a physical or mental limitation that restricted his ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his rights. (See TAC ¶¶ 31.) As such, Decedent does not fall under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(a). The following definitions are applicable to Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(b) and this action:

 

·         “ ‘General acute care hospital’ means a health facility having a duly constituted governing body with overall administrative and professional responsibility and an organized medical staff that provides 24-hour inpatient care, including the following basic services: medical, nursing, surgical, anesthesia, laboratory, radiology, pharmacy, and dietary services. A general acute care hospital may include more than one physical plant maintained and operated on separate premises as provided in Section 1250.8. A general acute care hospital that exclusively provides acute medical rehabilitation center services, including at least physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy, may provide for the required surgical and anesthesia services through a contract with another acute care hospital. In addition, a general acute care hospital that, on July 1, 1983, provided required surgical and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with another acute care hospital may continue to provide these surgical and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with an acute care hospital. The general acute care hospital operated by the State Department of Developmental Services at Agnews Developmental Center may, until June 30, 2007, provide surgery and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with another acute care hospital. Notwithstanding the requirements of this subdivision, a general acute care hospital operated by the Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation or the Department of Veterans Affairs may provide surgery and anesthesia services during normal weekday working hours, and not provide these services during other hours of the weekday or on weekends or holidays, if the general acute care hospital otherwise meets the requirements of this section. . .” (Cal. Health & Saf. Code § 1250(a).)  

·         "Inpatient" means a person who has been admitted to a hospital, skilled nursing facility, or intermediate care facility for bed occupancy for purposes of receiving inpatient services. A person is considered an inpatient when he is formally admitted as an inpatient with the expectation that he will remain at least overnight and occupy a bed, even though it later develops that he can be discharged or that he is transferred to another facility and does not actually use a bed overnight. (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 22, § 51108 (Lexis Advance through Register 2024, No. 3, January 19, 2024).) 

 

Plaintiff has attempted to rectify the previous deficiency regarding Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(b) by adding:

 

Upon information and belief, MR. BARDIN required urgent care due to his chest pains, bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia, and derangement, such that MR. BARDIN was transported to ANTELOPE VALLEY to be admitted as an inpatient.

 

Upon information and belief, a question of fact exists as to whether MR. BARDIN was administratively admitted as an inpatient, but there is no evidence to suggest that MR. BARDIN would not have been admitted as an inpatient at DEFENDANT HOSPITAL in light of his medical condition including chest pains bounding radial pulse, sinus tachycardia and derangement.

 

(TAC ¶¶ 63-64.)

 

The SAC was previously silent as to inpatient status or how the symptoms would affect potential inpatient care. Rather, the SAC focused on a comparison to others who are “compelled to live in health care facilities.” As alleged currently, a reasonable inference can be drawn that Decedent would need inpatient care due to the symptoms alleged.

 

“It is true that statutory elder abuse includes ‘neglect as defined in Section15610.57,’ which in turn includes negligent failure of an elder custodian ‘to provide medical care for [the elder’s] physical and mental health needs.’ . . .‘[N]eglect’ within the meaning of Welfare and Institutions Code section15610.57 covers an area of misconduct distinct from ‘professional negligence. As used in the Act, neglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the ‘failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.’ Thus, the statutory definition of ‘neglect’ speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 783.) “[T]he Act does not apply unless the defendant health care provider had a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship, involving ongoing responsibility for one or more basic needs, with the elder patient. It is the nature of the elder or dependent adult’s relationship with the defendant - not the defendant’s professional standing - that makes the defendant potentially liable for neglect.” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 152.)

 

Here, the alleged duty that was violated is: “At all times relevant, the actions of DEFENDANTS and each of them rose to the level of abuse when they deprived him of care and services necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering while he was in imminent danger of harming himself (as defined by Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.39). At all times relevant, the actions of DEFENDANTS and each of them rose to the level of reckless neglect when they failed to protect him from health and safety hazards, including the danger of harming himself.” (TAC ¶ 68-69.) Plaintiff’s allegations of a custodial relationship in paragraph 67 are conclusory as it is without a factual allegation on which an inference that a caretaking or custodial relationship can be made. As alleged, the duty breached is not one that shows failure to attend to Decedent’s basic needs and comforts and is more akin to professional negligence.

 

Further, Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6 reads:

 

Except for an examination or diagnosis for the purpose of treatment, neither a public entity nor a public employee acting within the scope of his employment is liable for injury caused by the failure to make a physical or mental examination, or to make an adequate physical or mental examination, of any person for the purpose of determining whether such person has a disease or physical or mental condition that would constitute a hazard to the health or safety of himself or others.

 

It appears that Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6 acts as a bar to Plaintiff’s cause of action. While Plaintiff argues that it does not apply as “Defendant Hospital’s immunity argument fails because Decedent’s mental condition was already diagnosed by Defendant Hall Ambulance prior to his arrival at the hospital” and “Defendant Hospital’s failure to place Mr. Bardin on a 5150-hold after being notified that Decedent would need psychiatric medical treatment upon his arrival falls under the exception provided in Cal. Gov. Code §855.6(d), which states, ‘Nothing in this section exonerates a public employee from liability for injury proximately caused by his negligent or wrongful act or omission in administering any treatment prescribed for mental illness or addiction.’ ” (Opp. 7:6-7, 7:10-14). Plaintiff does not provide any statute or case precedent allowing the imputation of Hall Ambulance’s diagnosis on AVHD to replace AVHD’s own diagnosis. In fact, there are no allegations to an administration or treatment by AVHD to Decedent.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED.

 

      ii.            Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action)

 

AVHD argues that the Fourth Cause of action is not a cause of action and is derivative of the Third and Fifth Causes of Action. The Court directs the parties infra to section iii for discussion of the Fifth Cause of Action.

 

Plaintiff argues that she has standing to bring a survival cause of action, all requirements have been met, and no other person has the right to commence the instant action as successor in interest to Decedent. Plaintiff believes that her survival action is sufficiently pled.

 

AVHD, in Reply, presents that survivorship is not a cause of action, and, even if the Court finds that survivorship is a cause of action, the claim is derivative and must fail.

 

In a survival action, the damages recoverable by a decedent's representative or successor in interest on the decedent's Cause of Action are limited to the loss or damage that the decedent sustained or incurred before death. Code of Civil Procedure Section 377.34. “The survival statutes do not create a cause of action. Rather, ‘[t]hey merely prevent the abatement of the cause of action of the injured person, and provide for its enforcement by or against the personal representative of the deceased.’ (Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1264.) 

 

A survival action is not a cause of action.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED.

 

 

    iii.            Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death)

 

AVHD presents that the claim for wrongful death is uncertain as to what purported wrongful acts are attributable to AVHD. AVHD argues that it did not have a duty to prevent Decedent’s suicide as one is ordinarily not liable for the actions of another and under no duty to protect another from harm, in the absence of a special relationship of custody or control. AVHD further argues that Decedent’s actions were not foreseeable based on the allegations of the TAC.

 

Plaintiff argues that she has standing to bring a survival cause of action, all requirements have been met, and no other person has the right to commence the instant action as successor in interest to Decedent. Plaintiff believes that her wrongful death claim is sufficiently pled.

 

In Reply, AVHD reiterates the case law cited in its moving papers, emphasizing foreseeability.

 

The elements of the cause of action for wrongful death are the tort (negligence or other wrongful act), the resulting death, and the damages, consisting of the pecuniary loss suffered by the heirs.” (Lattimore v. Dickey, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) “In any action for wrongful death resulting from negligence, the complaint must contain allegations as to all the elements of actionable negligence.” (Novak v. Continental Tire North America (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 189, 195.)  

 

Plaintiff’s TAC alleges the following negligence for the Fifth Cause of Action:

 

On or about June 11, 2022, Decedent consulted DEFENDANTS, and each of them, for the purpose of obtaining diagnosis, care, and treatment in connection with chest pains reactions or other problems leading to breathing impairment and other medical conditions and employed said DEFENDANTS, and each of them, to examine, diagnose, treat, and care for Decedent for compensation, which PLAINTIFF agreed to pay.

 

DEFENDANTS, and each of them, undertook, individually and by and through their agents, servants, and employees, to examine, diagnose, treat, prescribe for, and care for Decedent, including but not limited to examining, diagnosing, providing to and prescribing for and administering various devices, drugs, and medications, and securing decedent’s person for dangerous items. Said DEFENDANTS, and each of them did examine, treat, prescribe care for or treat, did fail to prescribe and care for Decedent by means of various procedures, including but not limited to physical examinations, searching for dangerous objects, failure to secure and observe Decedent properly, and failure to administer certain drugs and medications.

 

(TAC ¶¶ 82-83.)

 

The TAC is not uncertain as it imputes all alleged actions of negligence on both defendants.

 

AVHD’s argument centers on foreseeability.

 

In an action for wrongful death of an individual who has committed suicide, the intentional act of suicide “is a superseding cause of harm and relieves the original actor of liability unless such act was reasonably foreseeable or the failure to foresee such act was a factor in the original negligence. [Citation.]” (Lucas v. City of Long Beach (1976) 60 Cal.App.3d 341, 351.) “In general, if the risk of injury is reasonably foreseeable, the defendant is liable. An independent intervening act is a superseding cause relieving the actor of liability for his negligence only if the intervening act is highly unusual or extraordinary and hence not reasonably foreseeable. [Citations.] Reasonable foreseeability in this context is a question for the trier of fact.” (Cline v. Watkins (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 174, 178.)

 

As foreseeability is within the province of a jury. AVHD’s cited cases, Meier v. Ross General Hospital (1968) 69 Cal.2d 420 and Vistica v. Presbyterian Hospital (1967) 67 Cal.2d 465, concern judgment entered after a jury trial and are not applicable to this stage of the pleadings. AVHD does not argue the alleged factual allegations concerning their negligence for this cause of action with this Demurrer.

 

As pled, the Fifth Cause of Action contains the elements required of a wrongful death claim. (See TAC P 83-84 [allegations of causation] and Prayer for Relief for damages.)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿While under California law¿leave¿to amend¿is liberally granted, “leave¿to amend¿should not be granted¿where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.”¿(Vaillette¿v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co.¿(1993),¿18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿“A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿

 

As to the Third Cause of Action, it does not appear from the facts alleged that an amendment can rectify the deficiencies. That is, Plaintiff has tried three times to amend its allegations related to this cause of action, the Court has ruled on it twice and, based on the allegations, Decedent was at the initial intake or recently admitted and had not even entered the hospital, so a custodial or caretaking relationship could not ensure. (See SAC ¶ 38 [“Not only did DEFENDANTS fail to carry out their duties to prevent the gun BARDIN used to kill himself from entering the hospital, they put the bag on his chest.”].) Plaintiff appears to seek to change this allegation, now stating that Decedent was not checked “while he lay on a gurney in the hospital” and “[a]s a result of said breach, HALL AMBULANCE actually and proximately caused MR. BARDIN to possess a pistol, which he ultimately used to shoot himself in the chest while in the custody of the DEFENDANTS” (TAC ¶ 22, 52.) Plaintiff cannot disregard her original factual allegations without triggering the sham pleading doctrine. While Plaintiff has included an argument for the sham pleading doctrine and an explanation or intake versus admission, no explanation is given as to how Decedent suddenly went from committing suicide prior to entering the hospital to lying in the hospital. Should Plaintiff seek to amend the pleadings regarding Decedent’s whereabouts which may rectify the deficiencies, Plaintiff is requested to file a formal leave to amend motion in which the issue of the sham pleading doctrine can be addressed.

 

As to the Fourth Cause of Action, a survival action is not a cause of action and Plaintiff cannot rectify this deficiency with an amendment.

 

Accordingly, the Court does not allow leave to amend.

Conclusion

 

Defendant Antelope Valley’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in part as to the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Negligent/Abuse (Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15657) and Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action) and OVERRULED as to the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death).
