DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV01059    Hearing Date: April 8, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

   Chelato Chacon,

 

     Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

   Zeyad Elalami, et al.

 

          Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

 21VECV01059                  107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

 

(1) Leave to Amend Complaint

(2) Compel Responses to Requests for Production

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

I. BACKGROUND

This is a landlord-tenant case. The following factual allegations are drawn from the Complaint (as defined below).

 

  1. Factual

 

In November 2014, plaintiff Chelato Chacon (“Plaintiff”) entered a lease agreement (the “Lease”) regarding a certain residential real property (the “Property”) with its prior owner (the “Original Owner”). (Complaint p. 2.) Between then and 2021, Plaintiff and the Original Owner made verbal alterations to the Lease, including rent increases; Plaintiff made timely rent payments, performed under the Lease, and made repairs to the Property without any rent offsets; and many habitability issues arose at the Property, including pest infestations and problems with heating. (Id. at pp. 2–3.)

 

On January 20, 2021, Plaintiff learned that defendant Zeyad Elalami (“Elalami”) and the corporation in which Elalami holds multiple officer positions, defendant Luxor Funding Group, Inc. (“Luxor” and, together with Elalami, “Defendants”), had acquired the Property from the Original Owner, making Defendants the new owners of the Property. (Complaint pp. 1, 3.) The same day, Elalami informed Plaintiff that he was immediately increasing Plaintiff’s monthly rent by approximately 47.14% although Elalami did not provide Plaintiff with proper notice of any rent increase. (Complaint p. 3.) Plaintiff protested that such a rent increase violated rent control and stabilization laws governing the Property and continued to timely pay rent at his previous rate. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami accepted such payment for the months of February 2021, March 2021, and April 2021, but thereafter returned Plaintiff’s attempted payments to him. (Ibid.) Elalami made verbal and written implied threats to damage Plaintiff’s FICO credit score. (Id. at p. 3.) On February 1, 2021, Elalami attempted to issue Plaintiff a three-day notice to pay or quit via text message, alleging that Plaintiff had failed to make timely rent payments. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami told Plaintiff that he has “no lease for the Property” and Plaintiff received notice that an unlawful detainer action had been filed. (Id. at p. 4.)

 

  1. Procedural

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the warranty of habitability, (3) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (5) retaliatory eviction in violation of Civil Code section 1942.5, (6) declaratory judgment, and (7) injunctive relief.

 

On September 20, 2021, Defendants answered the Complaint.

 

On August 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion (the “Motion to Amend”) for leave to amend the Complaint.

 

On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion (the “Motion to Compel” and, together with the Motion to Amend, the “Motions”) to compel responses to requests for production from Elalami and seeking sanctions in the amount of $5,911.65.

 

On March 25, 2024, Elalami filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to each of the Motions, and on March 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed replies (the “Replies”) to each of the Oppositions.

 

II. PARTIES’ POSITIONS

 

  1. The Motion to Amend

 

In the Motion to Amend, Plaintiff seeks leave to file a first amended complaint (the “FAC”) and attaches to the Motion to Amend the proposed FAC. (Motion p. 4.) Plaintiff contends that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the filing of the FAC because it primarily alters or adds legal theories based on facts already pled in the Complaint and that denial of the Motion to Amend will prejudice Plaintiff by resulting in duplicative lawsuits based on substantially similar facts. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that he only confirmed that he had the cause of action he seeks to add after his deposition of Elalami on August 3, 2023. (Id. at p. 5.) Plaintiff contends that the Motion to Amend is timely sought because Plaintiff “took action to seek leave to amend” “immediately” after the August 3 deposition. (Id. at p. 10.) Plaintiff argues that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the filing of the FAC because the new facts it learned at the deposition arise from the same transactions and occurrences as the Complaint, this is “not an ‘eve of trial’ type scenario,” and even if trial must be delayed, Defendants would not be prejudiced as Elalami wants to move trial to accommodate vacation plans anyway. (Ibid.) Plaintiff argues that the FAC will not cause Defendanst unfair surprise or additional costs and that it is permissible for an amended complaint to expand the scope of litigation while discovery is ongoing. (Id. at pp. 10–11.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that all additional allegations and the additional cause of action that the FAC seeks to add are protected litigation activity “and would therefore be subject to a SLAPP Motion to Strike.” (Opposition p. 2.) Defendants contend that the new cause of action in the FAC does not specify what allegations violate the ordinance and that “the proposed amendment itself alleges the purpose of the entry [by Defendants into Plaintiff’s residence at the Property] was… clearly [for] a ‘lawful purpose.’” (Id. at p. 3.) Finally, Defendants state that if the Motion to Amend is granted, Defendants intend to file both a “SLAPP Motion to Strike” and a demurrer, intend to conduct additional discovery, “and potentially move for summary judgment.” (Id. at p. 4.) Defendants therefore request that if the Motion to Amend is granted, the currently set trial date be vacated. (Ibid.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff requests that this court decline to consider the Opposition to the Motion to Amend because Plaintiff was not served with a copy of such. (Reply p. 3.) Plaintiff only became aware of the Opposition to the Motion to Compel on March 29, 2024 when he searched the case number on the court’s docket and was therefore unable to respond to the substance of the Opposition to the Motion to Amend. (Ibid.)

 

  1. The Motion to Compel

 

In the Motion to Compel, Plaintiff alleges that he served a notice of deposition of Elalami on Defendants on May 3, 2023 that included certain requests for production. (Motion p. 1.) Plaintiff alleges that Parties agreed to a deposition date of May 30, 2023 in response thereto, but Plaintiff cancelled that deposition on May 26, 2023. (Id. at p. 2.) On July 14, 2023, Plaintiff served a second notice of deposition of Elalami on Defendants that included the same certain requests for production, and on August 3, 2023, Plaintiff deposed Elalami. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges he inquired as to Elalami’s response to the requests for production a couple times thereafter but as of the filing of the Motion to Compel, has not received any such production nor a reason or excuse for Elalami’s failure to produce discovery responses. (Id. at pp. 2–4.) Plaintiff argues that he properly served Elalami with the notice of deposition that included requests for production and that by failing to respond to such, Elalami has waived any right to object to those requests. (Id. at p. 4.) Plaintiff observes that he may move for an order to compel discovery responses when none are produced by the discovery recipient and that he may seek an order compelling Elalami to attend a second deposition. (Id. at pp. 5–6.) Plaintiff contends that this court must monetary sanctions in the amount of Plaintiff’s reasonable expenses in bringing the Motion to Compel and in taking a second deposition of Elalami. (Id. at pp. 7–8.)

 

In opposition, Elalami alleges that he responded to Plaintiff’s written discovery, including a request for production of documents, which Elalami attaches to the Opposition to the Motion to Compel, on May 3, 2022. (Opposition pp. 2–3.) Elalami further alleges that the notice for deposition “does not state any specific time or place to deliver the requested documents.” (Id. at p. 3.) Elalami contends that he was not required to produce the requested documents before the scheduled deposition. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami argues that the Motion to Compel does not show good cause for the production of documents and that “documents that would have been responsive to the document request in the deposition notice were already produced in response to [the May 3] written request for documents.” (Id. at p. 4.) Elalami argues that there is no factual showing that additional documents not already produced would be relevant and that the request for documents is too broad. (Id. at p. 5.) Finally, Elalami contends that Plaintiff’s efforts to meet and confer before the filing of the Motion to Compel were insufficient and that Defendants never refused a second deposition. (Ibid.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff alleges further incidents of harassment that have occurred since Plaintiff filed the Motion to Compel. (Reply pp. 1–2.) Plaintiff contends that Elalami’s argument that he has already produced the requested documents in response to a prior discovery request is irrelevant because the prior discovery request was directed at his co-defendant Luxor. (Id. at p. 2.) Plaintiff also alleges that her has served new discovery requests on Defendants, to which Defendants have failed to timely respond. (Id. at pp. 2–3.) Plaintiff states that the deposition notice was clear that it was to be taken virtually over Zoom and that the requested documents should be produced simultaneously and could have been sent by e-mail. (Id. at p. 3.) Plaintiff argues that any objections Elalami might have made were waived three days before the scheduled deposition. (Id at pp. 4–5.) Plaintiff contends argues that he does not need to show good cause for his document requests because that requirement only applies to motions to compel further and not in cases where the deponent has failed to produce any responses. (Id. at p. 5.) Plaintiff further contends that Parties were not required to meet and confer prior to the filing of the motion to compel because that requirement only applies to motions to compel further. (Id. at p. 6.) Plaintiff then reiterates his request for monetary sanctions against Elelani, which he argues is mandatory and should be considered in the context of Defendants’ other failures to respond to discovery requests and previously alleged continued harassment of Plaintiff. (Id. at pp .7–8.) 

 

III. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Opposition to the Motion to Amend

 

Along with the Oppositions, Defendants filed two identical proofs of service, which purport on the first page to confirm service of the Opposition to the Motion to Amend but which contradictorily purport on the second page to confirm service of the Opposition to the Motion to Compel and the accompanying McCone Declaration. There is no proof of service attached to the Opposition to the Motion to Amend. Plaintiff filed with his Reply to the Opposition to the Motion to Amend a declaration by his attorney (the “Brockmeier Declaration”), which states that the Opposition to the Motion to Amend was never served on Plaintiff and never received by either of his attorneys. (Brockmeier Decl. ¶¶ 4, 6, 8, 10.) Plaintiff only learned of the Opposition to the Motion to Amend on March 29, 2024, which is five court days before the hearing on April 8, 2024 and therefore the date by which Plaintiff’s Reply to the Opposition to the Motion to Amend was due. (Id. at ¶ 8.) Plaintiff was consequently unable to file a timely reply that addressed the substance of the Opposition to the Motion to Amend. (Reply p. 3.) Plaintiff therefore requests that this court decline to consider the Opposition to the Motion to Amend, construe Defendants’ consequent lack of an opposition an admission that the Motion to Amend is meritorious, and grant the Motion to amend without a hearing on its merits. (Notice of Reply p. 2; Reply p. 4.)

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1005, subdivisions (b) and (c), opposition papers to a motion should be filed at least nine court days before the motion is set for hearing, and the same should be served on the other parties to the lawsuit “not later than the close of the next business day after the time the opposing papers… are filed.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿1005, subds. (b)–(c).) Nine court days before the Motion to Amend’s April 8, 2024 hearing was March 25, 2024. Because Elalami timely filed the Opposition to the Motion to Amend on that day, he should have also served it on Plaintiff by the close of the next business day, March 26, 2024. Furthermore, per California Rules of Court Rule 8.817, subdivision (a)(1), “[b]efore filing any document, a party must serve… one copy of the document on the attorney for each party separately represented.” (Cal. Rules Court Rule 8.817, subd. (a)(1).) Thus, Elalami should have served Plaintiff with the Opposition to the Motion to Amend before filing it. “A trial court has broad discretion under rule 3.1300(d) of the Rules of Court to refuse to consider papers served and filed beyond the deadline without a prior court order finding good cause for late submission.” (Bozzi v. Nordstrom, Inc. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 755, 765.)

 

Based on the sworn statements in the Brockmeier Declaration and the error in the list of documents served in the proofs of service filed by Plaintiff, we believe that although the Opposition to the Motion to Amend was timely filed with this court, it was never served on Plaintiff and Plaintiff thereby remained unaware of its filing until a few days later, on March 29, 2024. We additionally believe that Plaintiff was thereby deprived of a fair chance to consider and respond to the substance of the Opposition to the Motion to Amend in a timely manner. We therefore decline to consider the Opposition to the Motion to Amend.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel document production requested in a deposition notice “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration” that “state[s] facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040, § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) While Plaintiff asserts that no meet and confer requirement exists in this case because this is a motion to compel, not a motion to compel further, and no responsive documents were produced by Elalami at his deposition, California Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.450, subdivision (b)(2) only excuses the moving party of a motion to compel from the meet and confer requirement when the deponent has failed to make any appearance at the scheduled deposition. In this case, while Elalami did not produce any of the requested documents, he did appear at his deposition, and therefore Plaintiff was required to meet and confer with Defendants in good faith before the filing of the Motion to Compel.

 

Plaintiff does not attach to or file concurrently with the Motion to Compel any meet and confer declaration. We remind Plaintiff of his obligation to meet and confer with Defendants in good faith.

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Motion to Amend

 

“The court may…, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding….” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿473, subd. (a)(1).) “A trial court has wide discretion to allow the amendment of¿pleadings, and generally courts will liberally allow amendments at any stage of the proceeding.” (Falcon v. Long Beach Genetics, Inc. (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1280.) “The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) “Time and knowledge are important factors to be considered when granting or denying a¿motion¿to¿amend.” (Stockton v. Ortiz (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 183, 194.) “If the¿motion¿to¿amend¿is timely made and the granting of the¿motion¿will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to¿amend¿and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court of Cal. In and For Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) A court may grant leave to amend to add new causes of action, except an amendment may not be permitted where “an attempt is made to state facts which give rise to a wholly distinct and different legal obligation against the defendant” and “a change is made in the liability sought to be enforced against the defendant.” (Klopstock v. Superior Court in and for the City and County of San Francisco (1941) 17 Cal.2d 13, 20.) 

 

Plaintiff seeks to add new background facts about events that took place between August 10, 2021 and August 4, 2023. Facts that occurred after the filing of the Complaint on August 11, 2021 should be filed in a supplemental pleading under California Code of Civil Procedure section 464, subdivision (a), not an amended pleading. However, “a judgment will not be reversed for technical defects in the pleadings” and “courts regard substance and not labels in determining the sufficiency of a pleading.” (Starkweather v. Eddy (1927) 87 Cal.App. 92, 96; Hutnick v. U.S. Fidelity & Guaranty Co. (1988) 47 Cal.3d 456, 464, fn. 6.) Thus, this court may treat that which is labelled as an amended complaint as a supplemental complaint. Like with amended pleadings, “[i]t is the general policy that courts should exercise liberality in permitting the filing of supplemental pleadings when the alleged ‘occurring-after’ facts are pertinent to the case.” (Flood v. Simpson (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 644, 647; see also Louie Queriolo Trucking, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County (1967) 252 Cal.App.2d 194, 297.) As the facts which Plaintiff seeks to add relate to existing causes of action, this court has broad discretion both to liberally grant leave to amend the Complaint for those facts which occurred before the filing of the Complaint, and to liberally grant leave to file a supplemental pleading to the Complaint for those facts which occurred after the filing of the Complaint.

 

Plaintiff also seeks to add a new cause of action for violation of Los Angeles Anti-Tenant Harassment Ordinance (Ord. no. 187109 sec. 45.30, et seq.) based on the same set of facts already pled in the Complaint. While an amended complaint may add a new cause of action, a supplemental complaint may not. (Flood v. Simpson, supra, 45 Cal.App.3d 644, 647.) Thus, to the extent the new cause of action relies on new facts pled in the supplemental complaint, it is not permissible. However, the new cause of action largely relies on general facts already pled that will not “give rise to a wholly distinct and different legal obligation” against Defendants, and is thus permissible. Moreover, Plaintiff pleads that he only became aware of the facts underlying this cause of action after he deposed Elalami, after which he promptly moved to amend the Complaint. Thus, there is minimal delay in Plaintiff’s seeking to add the new cause of action.

 

Finally, Plaintiff seeks to change the phrasing of its request for attorney’s fees in the Complaint’s prayer for relief and to add a sentence to the beginning of each of the Complaint’s causes of action re-alleging all previously asserted paragraphs. These edits are largely clerical and do not change the nature of the legal obligations being brought against Defendants.

 

This court has broad discretion to liberally grant leave to amend or leave to supplement so long as such amendment or supplement does not prejudice the other party. Here, the case remains several months from the set trial date and the changes that Plaintiff seeks to make to the Complaint do not change the nature of the legal obligations being brought against Defendants. We find that no prejudice to the other party will result from the proposed amendments in this case. 

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion to Amend.

 

  1. The Motion to Compel

 

A motion to compel document production requested in a deposition notice may be brought when a party fails to produce for inspection any document described in a deposition notice following service of the deposition notice without that party having served a valid objection under California Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (a).) Any such motion that seeks document production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production,” and if production of electronically stored information is sought, the burden falls on the party opposing the motion to show that “the information is from a source that is not reasonably accessible because of the undue burden or expense.” (§ 2025.450, subds. (b)–(c).)

 

Plaintiff properly bring the Motion under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (a) because the Motion to Compel follows Elalami’s failure to produce documents requested in the deposition notice at his deposition, which followed service of the deposition notices without Elalami having served any valid objections under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.410. However, Plaintiff does not include any specific facts or argument showing good cause to justify such production.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Compel is DENIED.

 

  1. Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory in favor of the moving party and against the deponent, or the party affiliated with the deponent or both, whose failure to produce documents at the deposition necessitated the motion to compel “unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [The court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.].) “If the party seeking a monetary sanction meets its burden of proof, the burden shifts to the opposing party attempting to avoid a monetary sanction to show that it acted with ‘substantial justification.’” (Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1436.) “‘[S]ubstantial justification’ as used in the above statutes means a justification that is ‘well-grounded in both law and fact.’” (Diepenbrock v. Brown (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 743, 747.) Behavior during discovery that is intended “for purposes of delay or harassment, or [is] otherwise unreasonable,” for example, would point against a finding of substantial justification. (Union Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1978) 80 Cal.App.3d 1, 15.) “As to the amount of sanctions, Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing ‘the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result’ of a party's misuse of the discovery process. (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.)

 

Here, however, since we deny the Motion to Compel, we do not believe that it was necessary and therefore sanctions are not warranted.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request for sanctions against Plaintiffs is DENIED.

I. BACKGROUND

A.   Factual

 

The following factual background consists of allegations drawn from the SAC (as defined below).

 

In June 2019, plaintiff Darryl MacDonald (“Plaintiff”) and non-party Sarkis Minasyan (“Minasyan”) entered into an agreement, brokered by defendant Legions Capital, Inc. (“Legions”), whereby Plaintiff would lend Minasyan $40,000.00, which loan (the “Loan”) would be secured by a second deed of trust on Minasyan’s real property (the “Property”). (SAC ¶ 9.) Accordingly, shortly thereafter, defendant Precision Escrow Inc. (“Precision”) prepared a note secured by a second deed of trust (the “Note”) and a short form deed of trust and assignment of rents (the “Deed”), which identified Plaintiff as the beneficiary. (Id. at ¶¶ 10–11.) Minasyan executed the Note on June 7, 2019 and the Deed on June 11, 2019. (Id. at ¶¶ 12–13.) Plaintiff wire transferred $40,000 to Precision sometime in June 2019. (Id. at ¶ 14.) Precision orally promised to record the Note and Deed with the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder and defendant Isaac Nouri (“Nouri” and, together with Legions, the “Demurring Defendants” and the Demurring Defendants, together with Precision, “Defendants” and Defendants, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”), an employee of Legions, assured Plaintiff that the Note and Deed would be recorded with the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder. (Id. at ¶ 15.) However, the Note and Deed were never recorded. (Id. at ¶ 19.) Nonetheless, in late June 2019, Nouri represented to Plaintiff that the Note and Deed had been recorded with the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder. (Id. at ¶ 20.)

 

On April 20, 2020, the holder of the first deed of trust on the Property completed a foreclosure sale of the Property, which was sold below its fair market value. (SAC ¶ 16.) The foreclosure sale extinguished Plaintiff’s junior lien. (Id. at ¶ 23.) Plaintiff did not receive notices of default and sale before the April 20 foreclosure sale, and did not learn about the foreclosure sale until he was told about it by Minasyan in August 2020. (Id. at ¶¶ 17–18.) Minasyan promised to pay back the Loan throughout 2020 but failed to do so by 2021. (Id. at ¶ 19.) Plaintiff first learned that the Note and Deed had never been recorded when his attorney ran a title search in November 2021. (Ibid.)

 

B.   Procedural

 

On September 16, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”). On September 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) negligence, (3) constructive fraud, (4) breach of oral agreement, (5) breach of implied agreement, (6) violations of California Business and Professions Code section 17200, et seq. The Demurring Defendants demurred to the FAC, and their demurrer was sustained as to the second and fifth causes of action on January 3, 2024 on the grounds that these causes of action were barred by each’s applicable statute of limitations. On January 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (the “SAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) negligence, (3) constructive fraud, (4) breach of oral agreement, and (5) violations of California Business and Professions Code section 17200, et seq.

 

On March 1 or 5, 2024, the Demurring Defendants filed the instant demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (the “Motion”). On March 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to the Demurrer and the Motion, and on April 2, 2024, the Demurring Defendants replied to the Oppositions.

 

II. PARTIES’ POSITIONS

 

A.   The Demurrer

 

The Demurring Defendants demur to the second and fourth causes of action of the SAC on the grounds that they each fail to state a cause of action against the Demurring Defendants. (Notice of Demurrer p. 3.)

 

The Demurrer observes that this court agreed with the Demurring Defendants in its January 3 ruling on their first demurrer to the FAC that the statute of limitations began to run in July 2019. (Demurrer p. 2.) The Demurring Defendants argue that the additional allegations pled in the SAC over the FAC are insufficient to show Plaintiff’s delayed discovery of his claims. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) The Demurrer argues that conclusions of law are subject to a demurrer and observes that a pleading must state the ultimate facts of the case to withstand a demurrer. (Id. at pp. 4–5.) The Demurring Defendants state that the statute of limitations period for negligence is three years and is two years for breach of an oral contract. (Id. at p. 5.) The Demurring Defendants state that the delayed discovery rule only delays accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff has, or should have, notice of its elements of wrongdoing, causation, and harm, and that a plaintiff must conduct a reasonable investigation to become aware of an injury. (Id. at p. 6.) Thus, the Demurring Defendants argue that a plaintiff relying on the discovery rule to bring a cause of action must specifically plead facts showing both the time and manner of discovery and its inability to make an earlier discovery of the facts. (Id. at pp. 6–7.) The Demurring Defendants contend that the second and fourth causes of action in the SAC “contain little to no information regarding the delayed discovery.” (Id. at p. 7.) They observe that the Deed was to be returned to Plaintiff after recording but that he took no action when he did not receive it. (Ibid.) They also argue that Plaintiff waited over a year and a half after learning from Minasyan that the property had been sold to bring this lawsuit. (Ibid.) Thus, the Demurring Defendants contend that all of Plaintiff’s causes of action accrued more than three years before the filing of this lawsuit. (Ibid.) Finally, the Demurring Defendants argue that leave to amend should not be granted. (Id. at pp. 7–8.)

 

In the Opposition to the Demurrer, Plaintiff agrees that the statute of limitations period for negligence is three years. (Opposition p. 3.) Plaintiff argues that his claim for negligence falls within this three-year time period because it takes the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder up to three months to process and return recorded instruments, Plaintiff “could not have known prior to the end of September 2019 that his instruments were not recorded.” (Ibid.) In the alternative, Plaintiff argues that, in the alternative, his claim for negligence is not barred by the statute of limitations because it was tolled for his claim for 180 days by the Covid-19 Emergency Rule 9. (Ibid.) Plaintiff acknowledges that the Deed provides that the instrument is to be mailed to him after recording but argues again that given that he could not know how diligent the Demurring Defendants would be in doing so and the months-long delays in recording, he could not have expected to receive the Deed for months after June 2019. (Id. at p. 4.) Plaintiff further argues that he was entitled to rely on Nouri’s assurances that the Deed had been recorded because Nouri is a loan broker. (Id. at pp. 4–5.) Plaintiff therefore contends that he has alleged his inability to make an earlier discovery of his claims. (Id. at p. 5.)

 

In reply to the Opposition to the Demurrer, the Demurring Defendants argue that the SAC’s cause of action for negligence is barred by the statute of limitations because Plaintiff was on notice of his claim when he failed to receive any loan payments from Minasyan in July 2019, when he failed to receive written confirmation that the Note and the Deed had been recorded, and when he was notified of the foreclosure on the Property. (Reply p. 3.) The Demurring Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s argument that the Covid-19 Emergency Rule 9 tolled the statute of limitations fails because the case Plaintiff cites does not make such analysis in its ruling and Plaintiff did not proffer this argument in response to the first demurrer. (Ibid.) The Demurring Defendants argue that Plaintiff failed to investigate the recording of the Note and the Deed and that the SAC “does not allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action for negligence as timely filed.” (Id. at p. 4.) “Moreover, MacDonald apparently does not contest the two-year Statute of Limitations for the Oral Agreement fourth Cause of Action has lapsed.” (Ibid.) Additionally, the Demurring Defendants content that the case Plaintiff cites to support his contention that he was entitled to rely of Nouri’s representations that the Note and the Deed had been filed does not support his position. (Id. at pp. 4–5.) “[Plaintiff] does not dispute that he must state the basis for his alleged delayed discovery and the factual basis for the same, including what reasonable diligence he undertook to discover his alleged claims.” (Id. at p. 5.) The Demurring Defendants argue that facts about what Plaintiff did to discover that neither the Note nor the Deed were recorded “between June 2019 and late 2021” are “completely absent” from the SAC. (Ibid.) “Plaintiff was able to easily discover all the facts from which the basis for all his claims against Defendants by merely performing a search of the subject property's title history….” (Ibid., emphasis original.) Finally, the Demurring Defendants argue that leave to amend should not be granted because “[Plaintiff] has already had two attempts to cure these defects.” (Id. at p. 6.)

 

B.   The Motion to Strike

 

Demurring Defendants move to strike from the FAC (1) the lines constituting the entire second cause of action on the grounds that it is not drawn in conformity with the law and that it is an irrelevant, false, or improper matter; (2) the lines constituting the entire fourth cause of action on the grounds that it is not drawn in conformity with the law and that it is an irrelevant, false, or improper matter; (3) two instances of the sentence “[i]n breaching their fiduciary duty to Plaintiff, [] Precision, [] Legions and [] Nouri acted with malice and oppression and with a conscious disregard of Plaintiffs rights, making them liable for punitive damages” on the grounds that it is not drawn in conformity with the law and that it is an irrelevant, false, or improper matter; and (4) Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive or exemplary damages on the grounds that it is not drawn in conformity with the law and that it is an irrelevant, false, or improper matter. In the Motion to Strike, Demurring Defendants first observe that this court has the power to grant a motion to strike and that it “is the proper procedure to strike the conclusionary and descriptive adjectives.” (Motion pp. 2–3.) They argue that they “seek to strike several conclusions of law, hyperbole, and rhetoric that are pled in a generalized, legalese nature and baseless.” (Id. at p. 3.) Demurring Defendants argue that the cause of action for negligence fails as a matter of law because they did not owe Plaintiff a duty of care. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) In the Opposition to the Motion to Strike, Plaintiff argues that a duty to care did exist between Demurring Defendants and Plaintiff as Demurring Defendants brokered the loan between Plaintiff and Minasyan and made representations that the instruments had been recorded. (Opposition pp. 2–3.) Plaintiff argues that by failing to argue their motion to strike as to the fourth cause of action, Demurring Defendants waive the issue. (Id. at p. 3.) Plaintiff argues further that Demurring Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages should be denied because this court previously denied a similar request by Precision. (Id. at p. 3.) “In addition, it is indisputable that punitive damages are recoverable in breach of fiduciary duty and constructive fraud cases – both of which have been asserted against [Demurring] Defendants.” (Id. at p. 4.) Plaintiff contends that Demurring Defendants’ request to strike attorney’s fees should be denied because it was not included in the Notice of Motion and because it is recoverable under Business & Professions Code § 17200, et seq. (Id. at p. 4.)

 

In the Reply to the Opposition to the Motion to Strike, Demurring Defendants argue that the second cause of action must be stricken because there is no duty between Demurring Defendants and Plaintiff and because it is barred by the statute of limitations. (Reply p. 3.) They further argue that the fourth cause of action must be stricken because it is also barred by the statute of limitations. (Ibid.) Demurring Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to plead in the SAC facts showing “malice, fraud, or oppression” so as to support a claim for punitive damages. (Id. at p. 4.) Finally, Demurring Defendants contend that because this is the second amended complaint being challenged, leave to amend should be denied. (Id. at p. 5.)

 

III. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

A.   The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41(a) and §¿435.5(a) require parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. The Demurring Defendants submit with the Demurrer a declaration by their attorney stating that Parties met and conferred over the phone but were unable to resolve the issues raised in the Demurrer. (Borchard Decl. ¶¶ 6–7.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

 

B.   Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40(a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint.

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005(b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).) We are therefore satisfied that all filings in this matter were timely.

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

A.   The Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a] statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

1.     The Second Cause of Action for Negligence

 

The only argument raised on demurrer against the second cause of action relates to the statute of limitations. Parties agree that the applicable statute of limitations period for the SAC’s second cause of action for negligence is three years. Parties disagree as to when Plaintiff’s cause of action accrued, triggering the statute of limitations period to begin to run, and as to whether the statute of limitations period was ever tolled since it accrued.

 

Emergency¿Rule¿9¿was adopted by the Judicial Council during the COVID-19 pandemic and, as amended, reads: “(a) Tolling statutes of limitations over 180 days [¶] Notwithstanding any other law, the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.” (People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc.¿(2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 879, 885.) “The Advisory Committee comment explains the application of the¿rule: ‘Emergency¿rule¿9¿is intended to apply broadly to toll any statute of limitations on the filing of a pleading in court asserting a civil cause of action.’” (Ibid.) “The Judicial Council [further] explained the background of¿Emergency¿rule¿9, and the reasons for amending it, in a¿Circulating¿Order.” (People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc.¿(2021) 73 Cal.App.5th 33, 39.) The Circulating Order begins with a sentence that explains that Emergency Rule 9 was adopted “[t]o protect parties who have a civil cause of action that accrued before or during the state of emergency related to the COVID-19 pandemic.” (Circulating Order No: CO-20-09, available at https://www.alameda.courts.ca.gov/system/files?file=co-20-09.pdf p. 1.) Under a heading about clarifying the scope of the rule, the Circulating Order declares that “the rule applies to all statutory limitation periods for a civil cause of action.” (Id., p. 6.) Thus, the Circulating Order clarifies that the application of Emergency Rule 9 is not meant to be limited to only some litigants but rather that it should “broadly apply” to all litigants who had not yet made their initial filings on claims for which the statute of limitations period was running during the COVID-19 pandemic. 

¿ 

Thus, Emergency Rule 9 tolled the statutes of limitations for Plaintiff’s causes of action between April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020, affording him 178 extra days to file his causes of action. Therefore, the second cause of action for negligence is timely so long as it is filed within two years and 178 days of the date such cause of action accrued. Even assuming that the cause of action accrued at the earliest possible date either of the Parties suggest, on June 11, 2019, when Minasyan executed the Deed after having already executed the Note, Emergency Rule 9 therefore makes Plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence timely so long as it was filed by December 6, 2022. As the Complaint was filed on September 16, 2022, this cause of action is timely.

 

The Demurring Defendants argue that People v. Financial Casualty & Surety, Inc.¿(2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 879 (hereafter, People) “does nothing to support Plaintiff’s contention that the negligence claim is tolled 180 days in this matter and the court in People does not make any such analysis in its ruling.” (Reply p. 3.) However, we rely on People neither for its holding that Emergency Rule 9 does not toll the appearance period for vacating forfeitures of bail bonds nor for our finding that Emergency Rule 9 does toll the statute of limitations period for negligence claims. Rather, we cite People merely for the background context it provides for Emergency Rule 9 and the Circulating Order, the texts of which we rely on in making our finding. The Demurring Defendants additionally raise that “this is the second [d]emurrer filed against Plaintiff and the negligence cause of action, yet this is the first time this argument is proffered by Plaintiff.” (Ibid.) However, as this is a different demurrer to a different amended complaint than the Demurring Defendants’ prior demurrer to the FAC, Plaintiff is not here beholden to the arguments he raised in opposition to that demurrer and this observation by the Demurring Defendants is irrelevant to our finding.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the demurrer to the SAC’s second cause of action for negligence.

 

2.     The Fourth Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

 

Plaintiff does not challenge the Demurring Defendants’ contention that the statute of limitations period for breach of oral contract is two years. Plaintiff argues that his discovery of his claim was delayed because (1) it takes the Los Angeles County Registrar-Recorder up to three months to process and return recorded instruments; and (2) Nouri made affirmative representations to Plaintiff that the Note and Deed had been recorded, which reassurances Plaintiff was entitled to rely on because Nouri is an expert. Additionally, Emergency Rule 9 would have tolled the statute of limitations period for 180 days.

 

Even taking both the three-month delay for registration and the 180-day tolling period of Emergency Rule 9 into account, the two-year statute of limitations period would have expired by mid-March 2022, before the filing of the Complaint and thus barring this cause of action. Thus, to overcome the Demurrer, Plaintiff must show that he was entitled to rely on Nouri’s representations that the Note and Deed had been recorded.

 

“Under the discovery rule, suspicion of one or more of the elements of a cause of action, coupled with knowledge of any remaining elements, will generally trigger the statute of limitations period.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 807.) “The discovery rule does not encourage dilatory tactics because plaintiffs are charged with presumptive knowledge of an injury if they have information of circumstances to put them on inquiry or if they have the opportunity to obtain knowledge from sources open to their investigation. In other words, plaintiffs are required to conduct a reasonable investigation after becoming aware of an injury, and are charged with knowledge of the information that would have been revealed by such an investigation.” (Id. at 807–08, emphasis original, internal citations omitted.) However, “[a] defendant's fraud in concealing a cause of action against him will toll the statute of limitations, and that tolling will last as long as a plaintiff's reliance on the misrepresentations is reasonable.” (Grisham v. Philip Morris U.S.A., Inc. (2007) 40 Cal.4th 623, 637.) “The recipient of a fraudulent misrepresentation of fact is justified in relying upon its truth, although he might have ascertained the falsity of the representation had he undertaken an investigation.” (Weatherly v. Universal Music Publishing Group (2004) 125 Cal.App.4th 913, 919 (hereafter, Weatherly), quoting El Pollo Loco, Inc. v. Hashim (9th Cir. 2003) 316 F.3d 1032, 1039 [applying California law].) Moreover, “[w]here no duty is imposed by law to make inquiry, and where, under the circumstances, a prudent man would not be put on inquiry, the mere fact that means of knowledge are open and not availed of does not operate to give constructive notice of the facts.” (Baker v. Beech Aircraft Corp. (1974) 39 Cal.App.3d 315, 321–22.) For example, plaintiff’s right to conduct an audit of defendant’s records was not sufficient to put plaintiff on inquiry, at least where defendant's misrepresentations “hindered” plaintiff's discovery of facts. (Weatherly, supra, 125 Cal.App.4th at 920.)

 

Demurring Defendants argue that because Plaintiff could have conducted a title search at any time and discovered that the Note and Deed had never been recorded, he was on constructive notice from the time they should have been recorded, immediately after they were executed by Minasyan in June 2019. However, Plaintiff alleges that one of the Demurring Defendants, Nouri, an employee of the other Demurring Defendant, Legions, made an affirmative representation to him in his capacity as a broker that the Note and Deed had been recorded. Like the defendant’s misrepresentations in Weatherly, this misrepresentation of fact by the Demurring Defendants hindered Plaintiff’s discovery of his injury and prevented him from being put on inquiry. Although Plaintiff could have done a title search at any time, he had no reason to because of the misrepresentations made to him by Demurring Defendants.

 

Demurring Defendants also argue that Plaintiff was on constructive notice that the Note and Deed had not been recorded because Minasyan did not make his payments due to Plaintiff on time. However, while this certainly would have put Plaintiff on notice of his injury by Minasyan, which is the failure to pay back the money owed, it would not necessarily have put him on notice of his injury by Demurring Defendants, which is the failure to record the Note and the Deed.

 

Demurring Defendants also argue that Plaintiff should have been put on notice by the time Minasyan informed him that the Property had been sold in August 2020. Given that Plaintiff had not received notice of the sale before the Property was sold, we agree with Demurring Defendants. Thus, we find that Plaintiff was first put on constructive notice of his cause of action and the two-year statute of limitations period began to run in August 2020.

 

As we found above, Emergency Rule 9 applies to the causes of action in the instant lawsuit and would have tolled the statute of limitations for 180 days. Thus, Plaintiff’s cause of action is timely so long as it was filed within 2 years and 180 days of August 2020. Assuming that Plaintiff learned of the Property’s sale on August 1, 2020, the statute of limitations period for this cause of action expired on January 28, 2023. Because this lawsuit was filed on September 23, 2022, and this cause of action relates back to the original causes of action, it was therefore timely filed.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the demurrer to the SAC’s fourth cause of action for breach of an oral contract.

 

B.   The Motion to Strike

 

1.     The Second Cause of Action for Negligence

 

Parties dispute whether Demurring Defendants owed Plaintiff a duty as a matter of law and whether the second cause of action for negligence is barred by the statute of limitations. As addressed above in our ruling on the demurrer to the same cause of action, we find that the second cause of action is not barred by the statute of limitations. Plaintiff does not allege that Demurring Defendants, as brokers, owed them any statutory duty of care, but only that they owed him a common law duty of care arising from the general law of agency. Demurring Defendants deny owing Plaintiff any fiduciary duties.

 

“Under common law, generally any person who performs professional services owes a duty of care to all persons within the area of foreseeable risk.” (Greif v. Sanin (2022) 74 Cal.App5th 412, 428.) “The standard of care imposed on a [] broker thus is higher than that applicable to a layperson. The broker is subject to a duty of skill, care, and diligence commensurate with the professional standards that the [] industry has held out to the public and that the public reasonably can expect.” (Ibid.) This includes a duty that runs to third parties in a transaction where the broker does not have privity with the third party or owe fiduciary duties to the third party. (Id. at 429.)

 

Thus, while we agree with Demurring Defendants that Plaintiff does not plead any fiduciary duties owed by Demurring Defendants to Plaintiff merely because Plaintiff was involved in a transaction brokered by Demurring Defendants, we do believe that Demurring Defendants, as professionals brokering a transaction to which Plaintiff was a party, nonetheless owed Plaintiff the duty of care to act with the skill, care, and diligence commensurate with the professional standards of the industry. We believe that the filing and misrepresenting the filing of the Note and the Deed could be found to fall within that duty of care.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to Strike as to the second cause of action for negligence.

 

2.     The Fourth Cause of Action for Breach of Oral Contract

 

Demurring Defendants move to strike the fourth cause of action on the grounds that it is barred by the statute of limitations. As addressed above in our ruling on the demurrer to the same cause of action, we find that the fourth cause of action is not barred by the statute of limitations.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to Strike as to the fourth cause of action for breach of an oral contract.

 

3.     Punitive Damages

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.) 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)  

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves, supra at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].) 

 

Plaintiff’s SAC alleges facts sufficient to support that Demurring Defendants misrepresented that Precision recorded the Note and Deed as orally instructed by Plaintiff. Furthermore, the SAC alleges facts to support that Demurring Defendants concealed the fact that the Note and Deed had not been recorded as Plaintiff alleges that he only discovered they were not recorded in late 2021. Therefore, these allegations in the SAC taken together support the potential imposition of punitive damages, at least on the basis of fraud. Moreover, these facts support the inference that Demurring Defendants acted willfully in failing to file the Note and Deed and then subsequently making affirmative representations to Plaintiff to the contrary, while their professional status as brokers gives rise to the inference that they were aware of the probable dangerous consequences of this conduct for Plaintiff. Thus, these allegations in the SAC also support the potential imposition of punitive damages on the basis of malice.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to Strike as to the lines related to and the prayer for punitive damages.
