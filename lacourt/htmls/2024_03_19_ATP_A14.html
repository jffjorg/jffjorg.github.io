Case Number: 19AVCV00259    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is an action based in fraud. Plaintiff Soroush Janamian (“Plaintiff”) alleges that, commencing April 2013, Defendants Raman Refaei (“Raman”)[1], Hamid Refai (“Refai”), and R & A, LLC (“R&A” and, collectively, “Defendants”) (1) rented real property to Plaintiff located at 2711 E. Avenue I, Lancaster, CA 93535 (“Property No. 1”); (2) a property management agreement for 2703 E. Avenue I, Lancaster, CA 93535 (“Property No. 2” and, collectively, the “Properties”); and (3) a property management agreement for 2707 E. Avenue I, Lancaster, CA 93535 (“Property No. 3”). The residential agreement was set at the rate of $300.00 per month. Plaintiff presents:

 

  1. He had accepted the rental agreement and, beginning April 2013, had occupied the apartment on the second floor of Property No. 1, and paid a total of $11,400.00 ($300.00/month) in rent for the period of April 2013 through May 2016.
  2. He entered into a two-year commercial lease agreement with Defendants to rent a mechanic shop located on the ground floor of Property No. 1 on June 01, 2016 for a security deposit of $2,000.00 and three-months’ rent, totaling $4,800.00. Plaintiff also alleges $7,000.00 was used for miscellaneous start up expenses to establish the mechanic shop.
  3. He was employed by Defendants to manage Properties Nos. 2 and 3 and would be paid $800.00 per month for his services and that Plaintiff would be paid when Defendants sold the Properties.

 

Plaintiff further alleges that, in July 2016, an inspector from the City of Lancaster informed him (1) that there was no certificate of occupancy for the apartment, the apartment was illegal, the apartment lacked proper permits, and the apartment was not habitable, and (2) Property No. 1 was not zoned for a mechanic shop, had no on-site parking for automobiles, and had not been zoned commercial for 13 years. Regarding the management agreement, Plaintiff presents that Defendants knew the representations were false and have not sold the Properties. Plaintiff contends that he has suffered damages in the amount of $30,400.00.

 

On April 02, 2019, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging one cause of action for Fraud and Deceit.

 

On September 10, 2019, Raman filed his Answer.

 

On September 17, 2019, Hamid and R&A, LLC filed its Answer. 

 

On January 24, 2020, Plaintiff retained counsel and filed his Substitution of Attorney on January 27, 2020.

 

Defendants former counsel was relieved on December 05, 2019 for R&A, LLC; January 07, 2020 for Hamid Refai, and substituted on March 04, 2022 for Raman.

 

On June 24, 2022, Raman filed a Substitution of Attorney, retaining Andrew Davis (“Davis”) as counsel.

 

On June 24, 2022, Raman filed his Motion for Sanctions Against Plaintiff and his Attorney Bruce T. McIntosh (“McIntosh”) (“Motion for Sanctions”).

 

On June 28, 2022, Raman filed his Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (“MJOP”).

 

No Oppositions were filed to either motion.

 

On August 11, 2022, the Court held a hearing on the matter. The Court granted the MJOP and denied the Motion for Sanctions. Plaintiff was given 30 days’ leave to amend the Complaint.

 

On September 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On December 15, 2022, Raman filed two motions, (1) Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, and (2) Motion for Sanctions, set for hearing on January 10, 2023.

 

On January 06, 2023, two court days before the hearing, Plaintiff filed his Oppositions. The Court, in its discretion, did not consider the untimely Opposition as doing so would deprive Raman a chance to reply and indicated such in its Order and Statement of Decision. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1300(d).)

 

On January 10, 2023, a hearing on the motions was held. McIntosh and Davis presented oral argument for their respective clients. The Court took the matter under submission.

 

On January 13, 2023, the Court issued its ruling on the submitted matter. The Court determined the Motion for Judgment on the pleadings moot as the FAC was untimely filed. That is, the FAC was filed after the time allowed by the Court’s August 11, 2022 Order. The FAC was stricken pursuant to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 438(h)(4)(A). The Court imposed Sanctions of $999.99.

 

On April 04, 2023, the Court clarified that the sanctions imposed were to be against Plaintiff and McIntosh jointly and severally. The Court ordered the sanctions to be paid no later than April 15, 2023.

 

On May 01, 2023, the Court dismissed the Complaint filed by Plaintiff on April 02, 2019 with prejudice.

 

On November 07, 2023, Plaintiff filed this Motion for an Order Setting Aside Dismissal of Case and Reinstating Case to Civil Active List (“Motion to Set Aside”).

 

On January 16, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to Set Aside to March 19, 2024 at 08:30 AM in Department A14 at Michael Antonovich Antelope Valley Courthouse.

 

No Opposition has been filed. “All papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days. . .before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Ibid.) The hearing is set for March 19, 2024. Accordingly, an Opposition was due by March 06, 2024. Should an Opposition be filed, it is now untimely.

 

On March 08, 2024, McIntosh filed a Supplemental Declaration in Support of the Motion to Set Aside.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard to Set Aside – ¿“ ‘ A motion for relief under section 473 is addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court and an appellate court will not interfere unless there is a clear showing of an abuse.’ ” (Burnete¿v. La Casa Dana Apartments¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1262, 1266.)¿ “Although a trial court has discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief, and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.” (Cruz v.¿Fagor¿America, Inc.¿(2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿¿

Additionally, the California Supreme Court has held the following:¿¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿¿

Section 473 is often applied liberally where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and the party opposing the motion will not suffer prejudice if relief is granted. [Citations.] In such situations “very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.” [Citations.] [¶] Moreover, because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default. [Citations.] Therefore, a trial court order denying relief is scrutinized more carefully than an order permitting trial on the merits. [Citations.]¿¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿¿

(Elston¿v. City of Turlock¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 233-34.)¿¿¿¿

¿¿¿¿

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) permits the Court to “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.”¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b).) “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Id.)¿¿¿

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – Plaintiff presents that his case is similar to Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975 (“Rappleyea”). Plaintiff presents the following as to the action:

 

·         No final Judgment has ever been entered by the Court, despite the fact that the Court ordered Defendant’s counsel to prepare such a judgment;

·         The Court issued an Order to Show Cause to the Defendant that the Defendant submit a proposed Judgment to finalize this case;

·         This case has no final judgment;

·         The Court’s Order of dismissal is, in essence, the entry of a default as to the Plaintiff’s Amended Complaint;

·         Plaintiff’s counsel has stated under oath the nature of the fact that he routinely failed to receive notices from the court’s clerk regarding the results of court hearings and actions taken by the court independently;

·         Plaintiff’s counsel had no notice from the Court clerk, or opposing counsel, that the Court’s request for a time estimate for the filing of the Amended Complaint would be strictly adhered to;

·         Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings was set for a date after the August 9, 2022, and so the Motion was not properly before the Court at that time;

·         When Defendant’s counsel failed to timely do so, the Court set an Order to Show Cause why Defendant’s counsel should not be sanctioned for failing to submit a Judgment for the Court to sign;

·         Defendant’s counsel failed to comply with the Court’s Order a second time, confirming the Defendant’s complete and total disinterest in obtaining such a Judgment;

·         When the Defendant failed to respond to the continued OSC [Order to Show Cause], the court reversed its decision to award sanctions to the Defendant because of the lack of diligence on the part of their counsel; and

·         Beginning with the August 09, 2022 hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel did not waive notice and requested Defendant’s counsel give notice of what transpired.

 

McIntosh’s declaration presents the following as to the record:

 

·         [After Davis filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings which was set for August 11, 2022], On June 29, 2022, the Court, on its own Motion and without notice, continued the scheduled Jury Trial date to August 19, 2022, a date well after the scheduled trial date of July 9, 2022 (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 14);

·         At the hearing on August 9, 2022, the Court on its own Motion, raised the issue of Defendant’s pending Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, a discussion was held, The Court then asked McIntosh to revise the Complaint so that it would fully and clearly state the facts underlying the Plaintiff’s claims in this case, McIntosh stated he would be happy to do so, the Court inquired as to when McIntosh would be in the position to file the FAC, McIntosh stated 30 days, and the Court stated that would be fine (Decl. McIntosh ¶¶ 16-22);

·         McIntosh requested notice of what occurred and no notice was given (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 23);

·         The Court did not state that it was granting Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, so McIntosh states that, in his mind, an informal agreement was reached between himself and the Court (Decl. McIntosh ¶¶ 25-26);

·         Defendant refiled his Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings, but McIntosh was never served (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 28);

·         A hearing was held in which the Court took the matter under submission and subsequently issued a minute order (Decl. McIntosh ¶¶ 29-32);

·         McIntosh never received the Minute Order and informed the Court, no method of communication with the Court described, that he had not received any prior Minute Orders from the Court (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 33);

·         Defendant never filed a proposed Judgment, the Court set an Order to Show Cause (“OSC”) set for March 29, 2023 as to the failure to submit a proposed judgment, Davis neither filed papers nor appeared, and the Court continued the matter (Decl. McIntosh ¶¶ 34-37);

·         At the continued hearing on May 01, 2023, the Court orally indicated that it intended to dismiss the action and strike its prior award of sanctions (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 39);

·         McIntosh was never notified of a final order (Decl. McIntosh ¶ 40).

 

McIntosh’s declaration also presents that personal matters arose during this action: (1) his sister’s passing; (2) McIntosh’s own cancer diagnosis; (3) McIntosh’s relocation to Texas, and (4) McIntosh’s removal of the glad causing the cancer which McIntosh believes decreased some of his mental acuity. (Decl. McIntosh ¶¶ 49-53.)

 

Numerous courts have found no abuse of discretion in granting relief where the motions for relief under Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b) were filed five to ten weeks after entry of judgment. (See, e.g., Minick v. City of Petaluma (2016) 3 Cal. App. 5th 15, 33-34 [5-week delay acceptable when attorney did not appreciate effects of medication on ability to practice law]; Freeman v. Goldberg (1961) 55 Cal.2d 622, 625 [10-week delay acceptable when plaintiff showed evidence that the mail box used to file opposition was not serviced on time by the USPS]; Hallett v. Slaughter (1943) 22 Cal.2d 552, 554, 557 [7-week delay acceptable when plaintiff filed opposition that was never delivered by the USPS]; Outdoor Imports, Inc. v. Stanoff (1970) 7 Cal.App.3d 518, 523–524 [9-week delay acceptable when defendant reasonably believed the lawsuit was unfiled].) A delay is unreasonable as a matter of law only when it exceeds three months and there is no evidence to explain the delay. ( See Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 532; see also Stafford v. Mach (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1184, as modified on denial of reh'g July 8, 1998 [stating that the “three-month unofficial ‘standard’” established in Benjamin “remains true today”]; see also Huh v. Wang (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 1406, 1420–1425, as modified Jan. 16, 2008 [the court denied relief to a default to a summary judgment when the plaintiff made a series of errors including the failure to respond to discovery, failure to oppose summary judgment, and a two-month delay in seeking to overturn the judgment based solely on the attorney being “overwhelmed and disorganized.” The court reasoned that the attorney's declaration failed to show either a clerical mistake made by a clerk or legal assistant or extraordinary circumstances such as one or more attorneys leaving a law firm.]). Rappleyea provides that “relief under section 473 [is] unavailable” when “more than six months had elapsed from the entry of default.” (Rappleyea, supra, 8 Cal. 4th 975, 980.)

 

Plaintiff appears to be arguing the circumstances surrounding both the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings and the Court’s dismissal of the action.

 

Regarding the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, the Court provides the following: (1) there was no hearing on August 09, 2022; (2) there was a hearing on the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings to the Complaint, filed June 28, 2022 and continued in open court with McIntosh present on June 29, 2022, on August 11, 2022 (see 06/29/2022 Minute Order); (3) the Court granted the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings to the Complaint (see 08/11/2022 Minute Order); (4) McIntosh was present for the hearing (see ibid.); and (5) the clerk mailed the Minute Order from the 08/11/2022 hearing, including the ruling on submitted matters, to McIntosh at 1055 E Colorado Blvd, Ste 500, Pasadena, CA 91106, McIntosh’s then address of record (see 08/11/2022 Certificate of Mailing). The Court does not enter into informal agreements. Court action is reflected in either Minute Orders or Statements of Decision. As explained in Rappleyea, the general rule is that “relief under section 473 [is] unavailable” when “more than six months had elapsed from the entry of default.” (Rappleyea, supra, 8 Cal. 4th 975, 980.) Thus, to the extent that this motion pertains to the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, this motion is untimely.

 

Regarding the Court’s dismissal of the action, the Court clarifies the following:

 

·         It is not the Court’s practice to request an abstract of judgment for dismissals under these circumstances;

·         The May 01, 2023 Minute Order reflects that sanctions were vacated and the action was dismissed with prejudice;

·         McIntosh was present for this hearing (see 05/01/2023 Minute Order);

·         No Minute Order from the ruling on the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings to the FAC to the present reflects a request for Raman to file a proposed judgment; and

·         All Minute Orders from August 11, 2022 to present show McIntosh’s presence at the hearings and, where notice was not provided by a party, the relevant Minute Orders are accompanied by Certificates of Mailing that show the Minute Order was served to McIntosh at 1055 E Colorado Blvd, Ste 500, Pasadena, CA 91106, McIntosh’s then address of record.

 

McIntosh’s Supplemental Declaration states that on January 16, 2024, the Court “needed to know why no mention was made in those Declarations of the events of August 9, 2022, and requested that Plaintiff’s counsel provide a response to that request.” (Supp. Decl. McIntosh ¶ 3.) McIntosh summarizes the events of the alleged August 09, 2022 hearing from his previous declaration.

 

On January 16, 2024, the Court inquired as to (1) why no mention was made as to the August 11, 2022 hearing; and (2) why the Motion to Set Aside was not served upon Hamid and R&A, LLC.

 

The dismissal in this action was entered on May 01, 2023. (See 05/01/2023 Minute Order.) Six Months from May 01, 2023 is Wednesday, November 01, 2023. The motion is, likewise, untimely as it pertains to the order for dismissal as it was filed on November 07, 2023, over six months since the dismissal was entered.

 

The Court does not have authority to relieve Plaintiff from the dismissal under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) as this motion has been brought past the time set out by statute.

 

An exception to this general rule that relief under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 is unavailable when more than six months have elapsed may be available through the Court’s use of its equitable powers to grant relief from extrinsic mistake, such relief, however, “may be given only in exceptional circumstances.” (Rappleyea, supra, 8 Cal. 4th 975, 981.) An extrinsic mistake giving rise to the use of equitable relief comes from, among other things, “a mistake [leading] a court to do what it never intended.” (Id. at 981 [quoting Kulchar v. Kulchar (1969) 1 Cal.3d 467, 471-472]; see also Sullivan v. Lumsden (1897) 118 Cal. 664, 669 [referees’ use of wrong map was extrinsic mistake and equitable relief was available because “‘it is not a mistake of the law, or an inadvertent conclusion as to what the law is, but a mistake or inadvertence in doing something not intended to be done.’”]).

 

Plaintiff was present through his attorney, McIntosh, at both the hearing on the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings on August 11, 2022 and the was present at the May 01, 2023 hearing in which the case was dismissed with prejudice. (See 08/11/2022 Minute Orders and May 01, 2023 Minute Order). Further, as to the Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, service is presumed if the party serving the document satisfies the formal requirements of section 1013. (See, e.g., Sharp v. Union Pacific R.R. Co. (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 357, 360 [9 Cal. Rptr. 2d 925] [service of notice of appeal complete when deposited in the mail].)” (Jones v. Catholic Healthcare West (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 300, 308.) Thus, there is indication that no mistake occurred as McIntosh was aware that the case was dismissed, but did not act until after the statutory deadline.

 

Further, Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603 (“Leader”) explained that “the ‘day in court’ envisioned by [prior case law] is not a guaranteed trial on the merits, but merely the opportunity to appear and present evidence and argument in opposition” to a motion. (See Leader, supra, 89 Cal.App.4th at 621.) While the motions in Leader’s cited case discussed motions to dismiss, Leader discussed how their plaintiffs had a day in court in opposition to the demurrers, availed themselves of the opportunity, had an opportunity to amend, and belatedly attempted to do so. (See ibid.) That is, the Leader court held that a trial court acted within its discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 58(f)(2), in granting the defendant's motion to dismiss for failure to file the amendment in time. This is directly applicable to the case at hand as, though Plaintiff did not file Oppositions[2], Plaintiff appeared in court, presented oral argument, and had an opportunity to amend, but did not do so.

 

The Court notes that had it been able to relieve Plaintiff under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b), there is no proposed pleading to accompany this motion. The statute states clearly, in relevant part: “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) [emphasis added].)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Set Aside is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Soroush Janamian’s Motion for an Order Setting Aside Dismissal of Case and Reinstating Case to Civil Active List is DENIED.

Case Number: 23AVCV00835    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Beverly D. Owens (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on September 11, 2021, she was walking around Defendants National CORE dba Whispering Palms Apartments (“Whispering Palms”); and The Housing Partnership Network, Inc. (“HPN”); City of Palmdale (“Palmdale”); and Housing Authority of the City of Palmdale (“Palmdale Housing Authority”)’s parking lot when Plaintiff slipped and fell due to a black oily substance on the ground, sustaining injury.

 

On August 01, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging three causes of action: (1) Premises Liability against Whispering Palms, HPN, Palmdale, and Palmdale Housing Authority; (2) General Negligence against Whispering Palms, HPN, Palmdale, Palmdale Housing Authority; and (3) Government Tort Liability (Cal. Gov. Code §§ 815.2, 815.4, 820, 835, and 840.2.

 

On February 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Petition for Relief to File a Late Claim for Beverly D. Owens.

 

On March 06, 2024, Palmdale filed its Opposition.

 

No Reply has been filed.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Judicial Relief from Bar to Sue Because of Nonpresentation of Claim– Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6 provides that “[i]f an application for leave to present a claim is denied or deemed to be denied pursuant to Section 911.6, a petition may be made to the court for an order relieving the petitioner from Section 945.4 [requiring a claim to be presented to a public entity before an action may be brought against the public entity].” (Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(a).) The petition must be filed within six months after the application to the board is denied. (Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(b).) The court shall relieve the petitioner from the requirements of Section 945.4 if the court finds (1) the application to the board was made within a reasonable time not to exceed that specified in Section 911.4(b); (2) was denied or deemed denied pursuant to Section 911.6; and (3) that one or more of the enumerated reasons applies. (Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(c).) The enumerated reasons include (1) the failure to present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect unless the public entity establishes that it would be prejudiced in the defense of the claim if relief is granted; (2) the person who sustained the alleged injury or loss was a minor during the time the claim was to be presented; (3) the person who sustained the alleged injury or loss was physically or mentally incapacitated during the time the claim was to be presented; and (4) the person who sustained the alleged injury or loss died before the expiration of the time to present the claim. (Ibid.) Where no testimonial evidence is offered with respect to a delay in filing a claim against a county, the court may decide whether to grant relief on the basis of the petition and declarations offered in support of, or in opposition to, the petition. (Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(e).) 

 

Cal. Gov. Code § 911.4(b) provides that an application to the board to present a late claim must be made within one year after the accrual of the cause of action.  

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that there may be an issue with notice. Plaintiff’s notice states, in relevant part: “Petitioner Beverly D. Owens by & through their Attorney Vincent J. Quigg, Esq. hereby by Petition this Court for Order Granting Leave to pursue a Late Government Claim for Beverly D. Owens, Alternatively Plaintiffs Attorney seeks for Leave to File a Late Claim Pursuant to C.C.P. § 473(b).” (Petition 1:22-25 [emphasis in original].) The Memorandum and Points of Authorities includes an argument related to Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(b), which was not disclosed in the Notice as a ground upon which the motion would be made. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1010.)

 

However, Palmdale has waived any objection to defects in notice as it has filed an Opposition on the merits. (See Tate v. Superior Court (1975) 45 Cal. App. 3d 925, 930; Alliance Bank v. Murray (1984) 161 Cal. App. 3d 1, 7; Eliceche v. Federal Land Bank Assn. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 1349, 1375; Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1288-89.)

 

Plaintiff presents a timeline that includes the following: (1) the alleged incident occurred on September 11, 2021; (2) on October 05, 2021, the Law Office of Vincent J. Quigg was hired to represent counsel; (3) from October 18, 2021 to August 01, 2023, Plaintiff attempted to informally resolve the claim with Broadspire, presented to be the insurance entity for this accident; (4) on August 01, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint; (5) on July 28, 2023, Vincent J. Quigg (“Quigg”), submitted an Application for Relief to File a Late Claim by U.S. Post Express Mail; (6) on August 24, 2023 Palmdale denied to Application for Relief to File a Late Claim by U.S. Post Express Mail; and (7) on February 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion. This presentation is supported by Quigg’s attached declaration.

 

Cal. Gov. Code § 911.4(b) provides that an application to the board to present a late claim must be made within one year after the accrual of the cause of action.  One year from September 11, 2021 is September 11, 2022. Plaintiff has presented that she did not present the Application for Relief to File a Late Claim by September 11, 2022. Rather, she presented the claim on July 28, 2023. (Decl. Quigg ¶ 9.) July 28, 2023 is approximately 10 months from September 11, 2022.

 

Plaintiff only moves under Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(b).[1] Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(b) which  provides that a petition shall show that application was made to the board under Section 911.4 and was denied or deemed denied, the reason for failure to present the claim within the time limit specified in Section 911.2, the information required by Section 910, and the petition shall be filed within six months after the application to the board is denied or deemed to be denied pursuant to Section 911.6. Relief by the Court, however, is conditioned upon the requirements set out in 946.6(c). The Court cannot provide relief as Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(c) states that the application to the board was made within a reasonable time not to exceed that specified in Cal. Gov. Code 911.4(b), Cal. Gov. Code § 911.4(b) provides that an application to the board to present a late claim must be made within one year after the accrual of the cause of action, and, as described ante, the Application for Relief to File a Late Claim exceeds that specified in Cal. Gov. Code § 911.4(b). The California Supreme Court reiterates the importance of the statutory timeline in Draper v. City of L.A. (1990) 52 Cal.3d 502, 504 [reiteration of statutory timeline prior to discussion of Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(c)(3)].) Further, Plaintiff has not addressed any subdivision of Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(c). Rather, Plaintiff presents mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 as a separate argument.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) does not provide for relief for failing to meet the statutory deadline set out in Cal. Gov. Code §§ 946.6 as such an action does not constitute a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” Plaintiff as contemplated by the statute.

 

The Court notes, should the moving papers be read liberally so that Plaintiff’s mistake inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect argument was applied to Cal. Gov. Code § 946.6(c)(1), the statutory time limit prevents a bar to Plaintiff’s relief.

 

Accordingly, the Petition for Relief to File a Late Claim is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Beverly D. Owens’ Petition for Relief to File a Late Claim for Beverly D. Owens is DENIED.
