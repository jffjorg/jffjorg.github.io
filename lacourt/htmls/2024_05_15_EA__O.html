DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 22PSCV01402    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

PLAINTIFF/CROSS-DEFENDANT WEN WEN JIANG’S NOTICE OF MOTION AND MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE AGAINST DEFENDANT TOMAS DELGADO; AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,461.65 AGAINST DEFENDANT TOMAS DELGADO AND SEMPER LAW GROUP LLP is GRANTED; monetary sanctions are imposed in the requested amount.[1]

 

Background

 

This is a fraud case. Plaintiff WEN WEN JIANG alleges the following against Defendant TOMAS DELGADO: Plaintiff is the owner of two vehicles, a Tesla and Prius. Plaintiff’s husband, Antonio Wong (Wong), “gave Delgado an envelope full of titles which were intended to be the titles of the business vehicles” (Complaint P11) wherein Wong pledged various vehicles as collateral for but Wong inadvertently gave vehicle titles to Plaintiff’s two vehicles. Plaintiff asserts that based thereon, Delgado has unlawful possession of the two vehicles.[2]

 

On February 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed suit for: (1) Fraudulent Concealment (2) Conversion (3) Negligent Misrepresentation (4) Unjust Enrichment (5) Intentional Misrepresentation (6) Intentional Infliction Of Emotional Distress.

 

On January 3, 2023, Defendant filed his answer.

 

On January 4, 2023, Defendant filed a cross-complaint for Express Indemnity; Equitable Indemnity; Breach Of Contract; Fraud; Conspiracy To Commit Fraud; Declaratory Relief.

 

On February 28, 2023, Defendant filed a first amended cross complaint.

 

On August 28, 2023, the court denied Plaintiff’s MTS portions of Defendant’s answer (re: attorney fees).

 

On November 29, 2023, Defendant filed a second amended cross-complaint (2ACC).

 

On January 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed her answer to the 2ACC.

 

On January 24, 2024, the parties attended an IDC. The minute order provides, in pertinent part, “Plaintiff's Counsel is to provide the Court with a Stipulation and Order due on or before Monday, January 29, 2024, of the issues discussed in today's Informal Discovery Conference (IDC). Counsel is to provide the agreed upon discovery due on or before February 23, 2024.”

 

On February 26, 2024, the parties attended a CMC wherein the court stated, in pertinent part: “[T]he Court notes that it previously ordered Plaintiff's Counsel to provide the Court with a Stipulation and Order on or before Monday, January 29, 2024, of the issues discussed in Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) held on January 24, 2024; no such Stipulation and Order is on file. Parties are ordered to meet and confer this week regarding drafting of the Stipulation and Order; if unsuccessful in drafting a Stipulation and Order, a Motion to Compel may be filed.”[3]

 

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion.

 

On May 1, 2024, Defendant filed his opposition.

 

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed her reply.

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff filed the instant motion seeking to compel in full with Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set One, that was propounded on April 14, 2023. The specific discovery at issue are the following:

 

1) text messages between cross-Defendant Wong and Defendant Delgado,

2) titles delivered to Defendant Delgado by Cross-Defendant Wong, and

3) text messages from OC Sheriff Deputy Orlando Salinas. (Motion p. 1.)

 

Additionally, Defendant failed to provide documents that are in usable form (as required by CCP § 2031.280(d)(1)) because 5 of the 13 PDF files submitted are illegible (either blurry, did not contain the entire document, and/or contained stray marks).

 

In opposition, Defendant maintains he has produced the discovery.

 

Here, however, a review of the exhibits and Plaintiff’s opposition corroborates Plaintiff’s point that Defendnat has not fully and properly responded to discovery.

 

1.     Labeling

 

First, according to CCP § 2031.280(a), a party is to sort and label documents with categories in the document demand no matter how many documents are produced. Here, however, Defendant’s documents are merely named after Defendant. Therefore, Defendant has not complied with CCP section 2031.280’s labeling requirements.[4]

 

2.     Texts between Plaintiff and Defendant

 

Second, as for the texts, Plaintiff argues that there may be more in the text message chain because the photo somewhat cuts off the complete text.[5] Therefore, should there be more messages, then Defendant has not produced all text messages between Plaintiff and Defendant.

 

3.     Title Copies

 

Third, Defendant failed to provide legible copies of the title documents (called pink slips in the opposition) as required by CCP § 2031.280(d)(1). Despite Defendant’s contention in opposition, the court agrees with Plaintiff that they are not legible copies. It is unclear why legible copies cannot be produced if Defendant maintains possession of the title copies. (And in fact, according to Plaintiff, Defendant and his counsel claimed that they would provide “cleaner copies” as far back as July 2023.) Therefore, Defendant is to produce complete and legible documents.

 

4.     Texts between Defendant and Salinas

 

Fourth, though the court does not have access to the bates stamped documents produced, Plaintiff maintains that Defendant has not produced text message(s) from Deputy Salinas because what was produced is an email and not a text message. Therefore, as Defendant’s verified discovery responses claimed the existence of text messages (not emails) which Defendant has not produced, Defendant is ordered to produce the text messages.

 

5.     Sanctions

 

As for sanctions, though this court generally refrains from imposing monetary sanctions, here, the court does so as it has been one year that Plaintiff is waiting for discovery.

Plaintiff seeks $4,461.65 against Defendant Tomas Delgado and her attorneys of record, Semper Law Group LLP ($425/hour, 5.5 hours drafting motion, 1 hours reviewing opposition, 1.5 hours drafting reply and filing fee).[6]

 

Here, utilizing a Lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the court determines that Plaintiff’s requested sanctions are reasonable as is.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the motion is granted, and monetary sanctions are imposed in the total amount of $4,461.65, which is payable within 20 days of this order.

Case Number: 23PSCV00979    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

Defendant Ven-Chee Koh dba Win Way Academy’s MOTTON FOR RECONSIDERATION OF COURT'S ORDER OF JANUARY 30, 2024 DENYING DEFENDANTS' MOTION TO VACATE DEFAULT JUDGMENT is DENIED as no new evidence has been provided.

 

Background

 

This is a contracts case arising from the alleged breach of a commercial rental agreement.

 

On April 4, 2023, Plaintiff RKW & MLW Fmy Lmt Prtsp I, a CA LP DBA Walnut Commercentre filed suit against Defendant Ven-Ghee Koh, an individual DBA Win Way Academy alleging damages of $37,862.48, with interesting continuing to accure on the past due rent.

 

On May 18, 2023, default was entered against Defendant.

 

On July 18, 2023, default judgment was entered against Defendant in the amount of $48,039.08.

 

On January 5, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to set aside default and default judgment, which the court on January 30, 2024, the court denied without prejudice.

 

On February 9, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion.

 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.

 

On April 17, 2024, Defendant filed a Reply.

 

 

 

Legal Standard

 

A formal notice of ruling is required to set the time limit running to file a motion for reconsideration. The 10-day time limit runs from service of notice of entry of the order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1008: 

 

When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1008) (emphasis added)

 

Discussion

 

The court denied Defendant’s motion to set aside default and default judgment because Defendant failed to produce evidence to overcome the presumption that service was proper.

 

Now, Defendant brings forth the motion on the grounds of new evidence. The new evidence is (1) the declaration of the parent of the individual with whom Defendant was tutoring and (2) the declaration of Cesar Olivia, a neighbor who lives on Defendant’s street stating that Defendant lives alone.

 

However, neither is new evidence.

 

As noted by Plaintiff in opposition, the burden is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence; that is, the information must be such that the moving party could not with reasonable diligence have discovered or produced it at trial. (Opp. p. 5, citing New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213, emphasis added.)[1]

 

Here, Defendant could have provided the declarations at some during the pendency of the motion (i.e., in the motion, in the reply, or during the hearing).[2] Instead, Defendant presented the white robe during the hearing stating that a person was not served but that the robe, hanging behind the front door, essentially gave the illusion that someone was home. (See also Reply p. 4:3-4 [“Defendant's robe hanging on the back of the door for more privacy due to the glass structure of the door for privacy purposes.”].) As stated in the court’s ruling on the motion to set aside default judgment, a registered process server’s declaration of service establishes a presumption that the facts stated in the declaration are true. The photograph submitted from the process server and their declaration shows an individual behind the glass door and this was the same individual who accepted service of process. The robe did not accept service.

 

To the extent that in Reply Defendant attempts to characterize Plaintiff’s Counsel as fraudulently claiming service in another case (22WCUD00369),[3] Defendant again misconstrues service. (Reply p. 4.) Plaintiff neither in this case nor the other case is asserting that Defendant was directly served but that Defendant was served via substituted service (i.e., member of the household or someone authorized to accept service). Here, the individual who accepted service identified themselves as an occupant but did not provide their name; and that the process server was able to list all characteristic traits of the individual who accepted service of process on behalf of Defendant. CCP section 415.20 provides no requirement that the name of the individual must be stated to effectuate valid substituted service. Therefore, Plaintiff’s registered process server fully complied with California Code of Civil Procedure Section 415.20(b) which defeats any claim made by Defendant that California Code of Civil Procedure Section 473.5 was not properly complied with. (See Opp. p. 3.) 

 

Therefore, as stated before, the burden shifted to Defendant to present evidence to overcome the presumption of valid service—service of which results in actual notice to Defendant[4]—but Defendant failed to meet his burden.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, absent new evidence, the motion for reconsideration is denied.
