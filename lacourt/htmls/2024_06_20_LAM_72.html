DEPARTMENT 72 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV13243    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MARIA CRISTINA SOTO,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

REAL TIME STAFFING SERVICES, LLC., et al.,

 

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  22STCV13243

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  June 20, 2024

 Calendar Number:  1

 

 

 

Plaintiff Maria Cristina Soto (“Plaintiff”) moves to quash the subpoena served by Defendant St. Clair Plastics, Inc. (“St. Clair”) on Pirate California Holdings, LLC (“Pirate Holdings”) seeking production of the confidential settlement agreement between Plaintiff and Pirate Holdings.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion.

 

The Court orders, however, that the parties keep the settlement agreement strictly confidential and disclose it and its terms only to the parties, their attorneys and experts in this case and the Court.  If the parties wish to file any portion of the settlement agreement or a quote from the settlement agreement in a filed document, they must do so under seal.  The parties may use the settlement agreement only for purposes of this litigation and for no other purpose.   

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed this action on April 20, 2022 against Defendant Real Time Staffing Servies, LLC d/b/a Select Staffing (“Select Staffing”), St. Clair, Novel Alamilla, Pirate Staffing (“Pirate”), Pirate Holdings, and Commerce Temporary Staffing Services, LLC (“Commerce”).

 

Select Staffing employed Plaintiff as a temporary employee company. Select Staffing entered an agreement with St. Clair, whereby Select Staffing provided its employees to work at St. Clair’s warehouse. Plaintiff was one such employee.

 

While Plaintiff worked at the St. Clair Warehouse, another temporary employee, Novel Alamilla, allegedly harassed and retaliated against Plaintiff. Alamilla was not employed by Select Staffing, but by Pirate Holdings.

 

The operative complaint is now the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), which raises claims for (1) gender based harassment; (2) sexual harassment; (3) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment; (4) failure to correct and remedy discrimination and harassment; (5) retaliation for complaints of discrimination and harassment; (6) violation of whistle-blowing law; (7) wrongful termination; (8) hostile work environment; (9) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (10) negligent infliction of emotional distress; (11) failure to permit inspection of personnel and payroll records in violation of Labor Code section 1198.5; (12) failure to provide meal and rest breaks; and (13) violation of Business & Professions Code, sections 17200, et seq.

 

Around November 2023, Plaintiff reached a settlement agreement with Alamilla and Pirate Holdings. On December 5, 2023, Alamilla, Pirate, Pirate Holdings, and Commerce were dismissed from this action pursuant to Plaintiff’s request.

 

On February 28, 2024, St. Clair issued a deposition subpoena to Pirate Holdings, seeking production of the settlement agreement. Counsel for Pirate Holdings indicated that they would not produce the agreement, pending Plaintiff’s motion to quash.

 

Plaintiff filed this motion on March 21, 2024. St. Clair filed an opposition. Plaintiff did not file a reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of documents, the court may, upon motion reasonably made, make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  

 

In making an order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, “the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)  

 

Discussion

 

St. Clair argues that it needs to be able to view the settlement agreement in order to determine whether the settlement was made in good faith and whether it properly apportioned liability between the settling defendants.

 

Plaintiff argues that the settlement agreement is subject to the privacy protections of the Article I, Section 1 of the California Constitution.

 

Confidential settlement agreements are indeed entitled to privacy, as a general rule. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531.) However, “an implicit waiver of a party's constitutional rights [to privacy] encompasses only discovery directly relevant to the plaintiff's claim and essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit.” (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 842.)

 

The Court agrees that St. Clair faces a practical litigation need to determine whether the settlement was entered into in good faith by the settling parties and whether it fairly apportions liability.

 

Plaintiff’s privacy concerns can be resolved by a protective order.  The Court will consider any formal protective order proposed by the parties.  Moreover, the Court has made a confidentiality order governing the settlement agreement at the beginning of this order.

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

EBONY ELAINE MOSS,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

PROPERTY I.D. CORPORATION, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  22STCV29253

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  June 20, 2024

 Calendar Number:  2

 

 

 

Defendant Property I.D. Corporation (“Defendant”) moves for terminating sanctions, issue sanctions, $14,659.00 in monetary sanctions, and a jury instruction on destruction of evidence against Plaintiff Ebony Elain Moss (“Plaintiff”) for spoliation of evidence and failure to comply with a discovery order.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $12,459.00. Plaintiff shall pay this amount to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of the issuance of this order.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed this action on September 8, 2022 against Defendant, her former employer, alleging (1) discrimination under FEHA; (2) retaliation under FEHA; (3) failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation; and (4) wrongful termination.

 

On October 26, 2023, Defendant answered and filed a Cross-Complaint, raising cross-claims for (1) breach of fiduciary duty of loyalty; and (2) violation of Comprehensive Computer Data and Access Fraud Act.

 

On August 24, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with the discovery at issue. From September 27, 2023 to October 30, 2023, counsel for Plaintiff and Defendant met and conferred, resolving a substantial portion of their disputes.

 

On October 13, 2023, Plaintiff produced documents including emails that she had forwarded from Defendant to her personal email account.  Those emails, however, were missing their attachments. 

 

On January 16, 2024, the Court ordered Plaintiff to provide, within 30 days, (1) full and complete further responses and responsive documents to Defendant’s Requests for Production (“RFP”) Nos. 1 and 11 and (2) full and complete answers to Form Interrogatory – Employment (“FIE”) Nos. 202.1, 203.1, 205.1, and 217.1 (the “January 16 Order”). The RFPs in question included emails sent from Plaintiff’s work email to her personal emails, including certain attachments. The attachments are at issue in this case because Defendant contends that Plaintiff illegally sent herself confidential client data.

 

On April 4, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for monetary sanctions (the “First Sanctions Motion”) against Plaintiff for failure to comply with the January 16 Order, but denied Defendant’s request for issue sanctions without prejudice (the “April 4 Order”).

 

On March 5, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel forensic inspection of Plaintiff’s electronic devices.  On April 16, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel, ordering the production of Plaintiff’s laptop and cell phone for inspection (the “April 16 Order”). Plaintiff turned over her laptop and cell phone to a neutral third-party forensic examiner, which searched the devices and produced the resulting data to both sides. (Corpuz Decl., ¶ 13.)

 

Defendant filed this motion on May 28, 2024, contending that Plaintiff intentionally destroyed electronically stored documents subject to the January 16 Order during the pendency of its motion to compel a forensic examination of the laptop. Plaintiff filed an opposition and Defendant filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where a party misuses the discovery process, courts have discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidence, or monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2023.010(g), 2030.290(c); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.) Misuse of the discovery process includes failure to respond to an authorized method of discovery or disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010(d), (g).)      

 

Monetary sanctions may be imposed “ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct…unless [the Court] finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a).) 

 

Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.” (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.) Further, preventing parties from presenting their cases on the merits is a drastic measure; terminating sanctions should only be ordered when there has been previous noncompliance with a rule or order and it appears a less severe sanction would not be effective. (Link v. Cater (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1326.) 

 

Before any sanctions may be imposed the court must make an express finding that there has been a willful failure of the party to serve the required answers. (Fairfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 113, 118.) Lack of diligence may be deemed willful where the party understood its obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply. (Deyo v. Killbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 787.) The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful. (Id. at 788.) 

 

Discussion

 

Terminating Sanctions

 

The central issue on this motion involves Plaintiff’s intentional destruction of documents relevant to this lawsuit.  Plaintiff intentionally destroyed these documents in March 2024, deleting them from her computer.  She conducted word searches for documents relevant to the case and when she found those documents she deleted them. Plaintiff does not dispute that this occurred.  This destruction of evidence took place just a few months ago, long after Plaintiff filed the litigation and had a duty to preserve evidence.  The documents she destroyed included those the Court had ordered her to produce on January 16, 2024.  She destroyed the documents that were on her computer at a time that Defendant had pending before the Court a motion to examine Plaintiff’s computer.

 

Plaintiff’s only excuse for this conduct makes little sense and does not exonerate this behavior in any event.  She claims she destroyed these documents because she had printed them out and given them to her attorney.  But her attorney has not produced documents with their original attachments, which would not have printed out automatically if Plaintiff had printed the emails.  Moreover, printing out copies of documents does not permit Plaintiff to destroy the originals.  The original electronic documents have information like metadata that is relevant to this case.  Moreover, destroying the original documents plunges this case into chaos, making it impossible to clearly verify what documents did or did not exist at any given time.

 

The Court finds that there is no other option but to issue terminating sanctions.  Lesser sanctions have not worked.  This recent conduct by Plaintiff is the worst violation thus far.  Moreover, Defendant cannot get a fair trial where Plaintiff intentionally searched for and deleted centrally relevant documents—for example, documents mentioning Defendant’s president who Plaintiff accuses of misconduct, and documents mention the person who allegedly told Plaintiff about the bad acts at issue in this lawsuit.  Terminating sanctions are necessary here. 

 

            Although this latest conduct is the most serious, this is not the first time that Plaintiff has not complied with her discovery obligations.  As the Court found in the April 4 Order granting sanctions, a number of Plaintiff’s discovery responses have not been forthcoming and honest.  For example, Plaintiff previously stated that her first post-termination job was in May 2023, Defendant later learned from a third-party subpoena that Plaintiff had represented in a job application that she had worked for Uber from December 2020 through October 2023. (Supp. Corpuz Decl. Re First Sanctions Motion ¶¶ 5-6.) Although Defendant then requested the production of Plaintiff’s pay records from Uber, Plaintiff only produced records for part of the period where she purportedly worked there. (Corpuz Decl. Re First Sanctions Motion ¶ 7.) Defendant also provided evidence that Plaintiff misrepresented her hourly pay at her current employer as being $18.00 rather than $18.50 as demonstrated by her pay stubs, and that Plaintiff had accepted a job with the County of Los Angeles on October 2, 2023, several days before her initial discovery responses were due, which she did not disclose in her responses. (Corpuz Decl. Re First Sanctions Motion ¶ 8.)

 

The documents that Plaintiff recently destroyed on her own system were documents that she had previously destroyed on Defendant’s computer system when she had been working at Defendant.  In Plaintiff’s May 9, 2024 deposition, Defendant questioned her on the electronically stored information. (Corpuz Decl., Ex. 3 (“Moss Depo.”) at p. 196:18-197:1.) Plaintiff admitted that she had sent those emails from her work email to her personal email to use as future evidence against Defendant. (Moss Depo at pp. 196:18-197:1.) Plaintiff admitted that she had ‘double-deleted’ the forwarded emails – deleting them from her work ‘sent’ folder, and then from her work ‘trash folder’ – resulting in those emails no longer existing in Defendant’s system. (Moss Depo. At p. 229:7-23; Corpuz Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

            The emails included a number of attachments. (Corpuz Decl., Ex. 2.) Defendant requested production of both the emails and their attachments, leading to the January 16 Order. Plaintiff declared, under penalty of perjury, that she had not produced those attachments because they ‘never existed in [Plaintiff’s] custody, or control, or in my possession.’ (Corpuz Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 1.) Plaintiff later represented to the Court in a Joint IDC statement that there were no attachments. (Corpuz Decl., Ex. 5.)

 

The Court found in the January 16 Order that this contention was implausible, and ordered Plaintiff to produce the emails and their attachments. The Court ruled that “Defendant’s request for the email attachments is eminently reasonable.” (1/16/2024 Minute Order at p. 6.) 

 

On March 5, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel forensic inspection of Plaintiff’s electronic devices.

 

            In March, 2024, apparently while Defendant’s motion to review Plaintiff’s electronic devices were pending, Plaintiff destroyed relevant documents that were on her personal computer.  Plaintiff testified at her deposition that she intentionally deleted a large number of the emails from her personal devices and email accounts in 2024 – several months after the Court had issued its January 16, 2024 Order.  (Moss Depo. At pp. 220:1-22; 222:18-223:10.) Plaintiff declares that she provided her attorney with hard copies of the emails. (Moss Decl. ¶ 5.) Plaintiff testified that she then searched the keywords “Property I.D. Title”; “Denis Laboy”; “Ani”; “Sami Braszel”; “Giovannni”; and “Norma Melgoza” and deleted every email that she saw that had those keywords in it. (Moss Depo. pp. 225:10-226:18.) Each of the foregoing names are key witnesses or actors in Plaintiff’s case alleging discrimination. For example, “Ani” is the person at Defendant whom Plaintiff accuses of misconduct.  “Denise Laboy” is the person who allegedly told Plaintiff about the misconduct and whose deposition and emails play a key role in Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendant’s summary judgment motion.

 

 Further, the neutral forensic examiner retained to examine Plaintiff’s devices was unable to locate the emails with the attachments at issue. (Corpuz Decl. ¶ 15.) The examiner did discover evidence that Plaintiff forwarded hundreds of emails to her personal account – more than double the amount Plaintiff stated she forwarded. (Corpuz Reply Decl. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff does not contest any of these facts.

 

            These emails, and their attachments, still have not been produced. (Corpuz Decl. ¶ 15.) Plaintiff contends that she provided her attorney with printed copies of the emails and that “once he had a copy of … the emails, I didn’t need to have it.” (Moss Depo. at p. 223:16-21.)

 

Even assuming for the sake of argument that a copy of every single email destroyed by Plaintiff exists in printed form—an assertion that seems unlikely—printout of the emails would likely not include the attachments and would certainly not have included any metadata associated with the emails.  Those would have been destroyed when Plaintiff destroyed the original documents on her computer. 

 

            There are additional reasons to believe that Plaintiff was not being truthful when she declared that she turned over all evidence to her attorney. Plaintiff admitted in her deposition that she was in possession of a video that Defendant demanded in discovery, but never gave that video to her counsel. (Reply Corpuz Decl. Ex. 12, Moss Depo. at pp. 400:17-403:16.) Plaintiff also failed to turn over an email she sent to a witness containing copious amounts of explicit language. (Reply Corpuz Decl., Ex. 13.) The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful. (Deyo v. Killbourne, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 788.) (Id. at 788.) Plaintiff has not made such a showing.

 

            “Spoliation occurs when evidence is destroyed or significantly altered or when there is a failure to preserve property for another's use as evidence in current or future litigation.” (Strong v. State of California (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1439, 1458, disapproved of on other grounds by Leon v. County of Riverside (2023) 14 Cal.5th 910.) The duty to preserve evidence arises when litigation is “reasonably foreseeable”. (Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1144.) Spoliation of evidence is considered a “serious form of discovery abuse”. (Id. at p. 1139.)

 

“[A] party moving for discovery sanctions based on the spoliation of evidence must make an initial prima facie showing that the responding party in fact destroyed evidence that had a substantial probability of damaging the moving party's ability to establish an essential element of his claim or defense.” (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1227.)

 

            The Court of Appeal has upheld terminating sanctions for spoliation of evidence even where the content of the spoliated evidence is unclear. (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1227, citing Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 14.) “Without knowing the content and weight of the spoliated evidence, it would be impossible for the jury to meaningfully assess what role the missing evidence would have played in the determination of the underlying action. The jury could only speculate as to what the nature of the spoliated evidence was and what effect it might have had on the outcome of the underlying litigation.” (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center, 18 Cal.4th at p. 14.) A moving party therefore does not need to show prejudice in order to obtain terminating sanctions for spoliation, because “[the responding party’s] own actions make that showing difficult, if not impossible.” (Electronic Funds Solutions, LLC v. Murphy (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1184.)

 

            Here, terminating sanctions are warranted not only by the extensive nature of the spoliation, but by its timing. Plaintiff deleted her electronically stored information after the Court had already granted a motion to compel its production and while Defendant’s motion to compel production of Plaintiff’s devices was being briefed. In other words, Plaintiff knew that she was under an obligation to produce that electronically stored information and knew that there was a live issue as to whether she would have to produce her devices for the specific purpose of allowing Defendant to obtain that electronically stored information. The presence of both a discovery order and a pending motion regarding the electronically stored information renders any innocent motive on Plaintiff’s part implausible.

 

            The Court has attempted other discovery remedies. The Court has issued monetary sanctions on multiple occasions and has ordered a neutral third-party investigation of Plaintiff’s devices. Plaintiff has not only deleted a significant quantity of electronically stored information in spite of these orders, but appears to have deleted the information for the purpose of avoiding the inspection order. The Court’s attempts at lesser sanctions have not only failed, but appear to have spurred on even worse conduct. Moreover, Defendant cannot fairly defend the case, given that the spoliation involved documents relating to the key players relating to Plaintiff’s claims.  Terminating sanctions are therefore necessary.

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant improperly subpoenaed Plaintiff’s other employers for information on Plaintiff. This argument is irrelevant to the spoliation issue and the Court does not consider it. The same is true of Plaintiff’s attempts to relitigate the April 16 Order, which was issued after the spoliation occurred.

 

            The Court grants Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions.

 

Monetary Sanctions

 

Defendant requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $14,659.00, consisting of the costs of the neutral forensic examiner and the attorney fees and costs associated with this motion.

 

The forensic examiner has incurred $9,204.00 in fees in connection with the examination of Plaintiff’s devices. The Court finds this amount to be reasonable. Defendant requests an additional $2,000.00 in fees not yet incurred. The Court does not award this additional amount.

 

            Defendant requests an hourly rate of $355.00 for Olatomiwa Aina. The Court finds this rate to be reasonable in light of Aina’s four and a half years of experience.

 

            Defendant requests a total of 9 hours for Aina. The Court finds this time to be reasonable. The Court awards an attorney fee of $3,195.00.

 

 

            Defendant also reasonably requests its $60 filing fee.

 

The Court therefore awards $12,459.00 in sanctions.


Case Number: 22STCV29610    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

TENTATIVE RULING

 

HEATHER ALTEPETER,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

ASH PATEL, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  22STCV29610

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  April 25, 2024

 Calendar Number:  7

 

 

 

Defendants Ash Patel, Shahzad Khan, Houri Simon, Neel Pinge, Alex Meruelo, Thomas McCullough, John Friedemann, and Commercial Bank of California (“Commercial Bank”) (collectively, “Defendants”) move for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

The Court GRANTS the motion for summary judgment as to Defendants Meruelo, McCullough, and Pinge.

 

The Court DENIES the motion for summary judgment as to the remaining defendants.

 

The Court GRANTS the motion for summary adjudication as to the fifth claim.

 

The Court DENIES the motion for summary adjudication as to the remaining claims.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff is the owner and CEO of National Merchants Association, Inc. (“NMA”). NMA provides electronic payment processing services to businesses. CBC served as NMA’s sponsor bank. Plaintiff alleges that CBC misled her into a business relationship whereby CBC learned and usurped proprietary information, industry connections, trade secrets, and business strategies from Plaintiff.

 

Around March 1, 2017, CBC and NMA entered into a Merchant Marketing and Processing Agreements (“Marketing Agreement”)—the first of three. (Additional Material Fact (“AMF”) 11.) The parties experienced some difficulties in the process of rolling out their relationship, and although the parties continued to move forward, NMA also sought out other banking partners to facilitate a smoother relationship. (AMF 12.)

 

In March 2019, MUFG Union Bank (“Union Bank”) filed a lawsuit against NMA, Plaintiff, and her other business entities. (Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) 8.) Union Bank obtained an order appointing a receiver to oversee NMA’s operation. That receiver, Howard Grobstein, terminated Plaintiff’s role and employment as CEO of NMA in October 2019.

 

Following a settlement with Union Bank, Grobstein was removed as the receiver and Plaintiff regained her prior position in NMA. However, CBC provided Plaintiff with a ‘take it or leave it’ Amended Temporary Marketing Agreement. (AMF 29.) Plaintiff contends that CBC coerced her into entering the agreement. The new Marketing Agreement was executed on April 14, 2020. (AMF 29.) On April 27, 2020, Plaintiff received compensation form NMA for the first time since her removal as CEO.

 

Plaintiff did not receive access to NMA’s company data and merchant activity records until May 5, 2020. (AMF 30.) When she obtained access, Plaintiff discovered that, during her absence, CBC had attempted to steer merchant accounts with NMA to other sponsored payment processing organizations. (AMF 31.)

 

NMA eventually identified Evolve Bank as its new sponsor bank. CBC ported NMA’s merchant portfolio to Evolve by the beginning of November 2020. (UMF 17.) Neither Plaintiff nor NMA have held any business relationship with CBC since at least January 1, 2021. (UMF 18.) Plaintiff has not held any depository account at CBC in her own name since at least 2019. (UMF 19.)

 

In December 2021, NMA filed an action (the “NMA Action”) against CBC in the Los Angeles County Superior Court, Case No. 21STCV44674. (UMF 26.) NMA raised claims for breach of written agreement, breach of contract, breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, unjust enrichment, unfair business practices, promissory fraud, and rescission of the April 14, 2020 Marketing Agreement. (UMF 26.) The court ordered the NMA Action to a judicial reference on March 8, 2022. (UMF 27.) Shortly before trial in the NMA Action, NMA filed a request for dismissal, and the NMA Action was dismissed on February 8, 2023. (UMF 28.)

 

Plaintiff filed this action on April 29, 2022. The operative complaint is now the First Amended Complaint, which raises claims for (1) unfair business practices; (2) intentional interference with prospective economic relations; (3) negligent interference with prospective economic relations; (4) intentional interference with prospective contractual relations; (5) inducing breach of contract; and (6) negligence.

 

Plaintiff names as Defendants CBC; Patel, as CBC’s President and Chairman (FAC ¶ 80); Khan, as its Executive Vice President, Chief Growth and Strategy Officer (FAC ¶ 81); Simon, as Executive Vice President, Payment Solutions (FAC ¶ 82); Friedemann, as CBC’s General Counsel (FAC ¶ 83); Pinge, as Executive Vice President, Chief Risk Officer of CB Cal (FAC ¶ 84); Mr. Meruelo, as the founder of CBC and a member of its Board of Directors (FAC ¶ 85); and McCullough, as CBC’s Executive Vice President, Executive Administration (CBC ¶ 86).

 

Plaintiff moved for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint joining NMA as a party, but the Court denied the motion on September 12, 2023. (UMF 29.)

 

Defendants filed this motion on April 4, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition and Defendants filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“In ruling on the motion, the court must consider all of the evidence and all of the inferences reasonably drawn therefrom [citation] and must view such evidence [citations] and such inferences [citations] in the light most favorable to the opposing party.” (Aguilar, supra, at pp. 844-845 [quotation marks omitted].) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Discussion

 

Damage to NMA

 

As a preliminary matter, Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not have standing to recover damages that inured to NMA, rather than to her.

 

“[A] stockholder may sue as an individual [i.e., not in a derivative suit] where he is directly and individually injured although the corporation may also have a cause of action for the same wrong.”  (Sutter v. General Petroleum Corp. (1946) 28 Cal.2d 525, 530.) “[A]n individual cause of action exists only the damages were not incidental to an injury to the corporation … only where it appears that the injury resulted from the violation of some special duty owed the stockholder by the wrongdoer and having its origin in circumstances independent of the plaintiff's status as a shareholder.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 112 [citations and quotation marks omitted].) “In other words, it is the gravamen of the wrong alleged in the pleadings, not simply the resulting injury, which determines whether an individual action lies.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiff contends that Defendants not only stole NMA information, but also stole Plaintiff’s trade secret strategies and industry insights, resulting in the destruction of Plaintiff’s personal reputation in the payment processing industry. This harm goes beyond mere damage incurred in Plaintiff’s capacity as a shareholder, and Plaintiff can thus state claims for it.

 

Unfair business practices – First Claim

 

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

 

Defendants argue that a plaintiff may only obtain injunctive relief under the UCL, and that Plaintiff cannot raise a claim for injunctive relief because the parties no longer do business with each other.  Plaintiff has admitted that her relationship with Defendants ended more than three years ago.  (UMF ¶¶ 18-19.)

 

“[T]he remedies provided [under the UCL] are limited. While any member of the public can bring suit under the act to enjoin a business from engaging in unfair competition, it is well established that individuals may not recover damages.” (Madrid v. Perot Systems Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 440, 452.) Restitution is also available in certain cases – “in the UCL context ... restitution means the return of money to those persons from whom it was taken or who had an ownership interest in it.” (Shersher v. Superior Court (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1491, 1497 [citation and quotation marks omitted].)

 

The Court agrees that Defendant have met their initial burden showing no triable issue of fact on any possible relief to Plaintiff under the UCL.  Plaintiff, however, has not demonstrated any triable issue of material fact. Plaintiff argues that CBC withheld the transfer of funds from NMA’s settlement account to its operating account, effectively taking possession of money that it never returned, and that she is therefore entitled to restitution. This is a harm that inures to NMA, however, and not Plaintiff in a manner separately from her role as a shareholder. Thus, NMA is the proper plaintiff for this unjust enrichment claim, not Plaintiff.

 

The Court therefore grants summary adjudication as to this claim.

 

Statute of Limitations – Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Claims

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s second, third, and fourth claims – for intentional and negligent interference with prospective economic relations, and intentional interference with prospective contractual relations – are time-barred.

 

“[A] cause of action for interference with prospective economic advantage, an action involving a property right, is governed by the two-year limitations period of section 339, subdivision 1[.]” (Augusta v. United Service Automobile Assn. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 4, 10.) Negligence similarly uses a two-year statute of limitations. (Cyr v. McGovran (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 645, 652.)

 

“[Courts] have recognized that in some instances, the accrual of a cause of action in tort is delayed until the plaintiff discovered (or reasonably should have discovered or suspected) the factual basis for his or her claim.” (Shively v. Bozanich (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1230, 1248.) “[T]he discovery rule most frequently applies when it is particularly difficult for the plaintiff to observe or understand the breach of duty, or when the injury itself (or its cause) is hidden or beyond what the ordinary person could be expected to understand.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has provided evidence that she was not given access to NMA’s company data and merchant activity records upon her return until May 5, 2020. (AMF 30.) This is within the two-year timeframe prior to the filing date of this lawsuit. Because there are disputed facts, the Court does not grant summary adjudication on this basis.

 

Factual Duplication – Second, Third, and Fourth Claims

 

Defendants argue that these claims are founded on the same allegations, “save for the element of intent.” (Motion at p. 16:8-9.) Intent is a critical element, however – a failure to prove the requisite intent level would result in Plaintiff losing at trial on the claim. Thus, pleading claims with different levels of intent is not duplicative.

 

Causation

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot prove causation because the interruption in her relationship with NMA resulted from the third-party receiver. However, Plaintiff contends that Defendants committed misconduct while Plaintiff was absent, using her absence. This is different from a claim that Defendants procured her absence from NMA.

 

Inducing Breach of Contract – Fifth Claim

 

Defendants argue that there was no contract between Plaintiff and NMA to breach. The undisputed facts do not show that there was no contract, however. Plaintiff served as NMA’s CEO for most of its lifetime, and in fact returned.

 

However, here, causation is a problem for Plaintiff. The alleged contractual relationship was between Plaintiff and NMA. Defendants have demonstrated undisputed facts that that relationship (as opposed to any relationships involving Plaintiff and third parties in the industry) was disrupted by the court-appointed receiver, not any other party. Further, Grobstein testified that neither CBC nor CBC’s lawyer influenced his decision to remove Plaintiff from the position of CEO. (UMF 12-13.) Thus, Defendants have met their initial burden.

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff, therefore, to demonstrate a disputed issue of fact.  Plaintiff has not done so.  Although Plaintiff points to evidence showing that Defendants desired to have her removed as CEO, that does not show that Defendants actually influenced the decision.

 

Plaintiff therefore cannot show causation.

 

The Court grants summary adjudication on this claim.

 

Negligence – Sixth Claim

 

As discussed above, there is sufficient evidence for a jury to find that CBC’s conduct damaged Plaintiff’s professional reputation in a way not merely connected to her status as a shareholder. Similarly, the delayed discovery rule prevents a finding that this claim is time-barred.

 

Defendants Meruelo, McCullough, and Pinge

 

Defendants argue that Defendants Meruelo, McCullough, and Pinge should be dismissed because there is no evidence that any of them participated in the alleged conduct by CBC against Plaintiff. The sum total of Plaintiff’s allegations against them is that (1) they live in California, and (2) each used his power and authority with CBC to “effectuate the harms done onto [Plaintiff].” (FAC ¶¶ 7-9; 84-86.) When asked in discovery to identify the wrongs that Plaintiff claims each of these three committed, Plaintiff had no answer. (UMF 23-24.) Defendants have therefore met their initial burden to show a lack of triable issues of fact as to Defendants Meruelo, McCullough, and Pinge.

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff, who has not met her burden.  Plaintiff argues that these defendants “all held positions where they would have likely been privy to the intentional conduct as alleged in the FAC and discussed further herein.” (Opposition at p. 15:20-24.) Plaintiff additionally notes that discovery is ongoing and that none of these three defendants have affirmatively exculpated themselves. This is not the standard, however. Summary judgment looks to whether there is a material dispute of fact showing that the defendant in question may be liable. Defendant has shown that Plaintiff is not able to provide evidence to dispute these three defendants’ culpability, or lack thereof. It is insufficient to contend that they were high-ranking officers – such a rule would allow parties to routinely drag an organization’s upper officers through trial merely by virtue of the fact that they held positions of power within the organization.

 

There is no triable issue of fact that Meruelo, McCullough, or Pinge committed a wrongful act against Plaintiff. The Court therefore grants summary judgment as to those three defendants.


Case Number: 23STCV13611    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

EMANUELE GIACOMETTI,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

GIORGIO BALDI, INC., et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  23STCV13611

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  June 20, 2024

 Calendar Number:  4

 

 

 

Defendants Giorgio Baldi, Inc. (“GBI”) and Elena Baldi (collectively, “Defendants”) move for judgment on the pleadings as to the Complaint filed by Plaintiff Emanuele Giacometti (“Plaintiff”).

 

The Court GRANTS the motion as to Plaintiff’s first five claims and DENIES the motion as to Plaintiff’s sixth claim.

 

Background

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff’s employment with GBI.

 

GBI terminated Plaintiff from his job as a waiter on March 16, 2020. (Complaint ¶ 23.)

 

Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants on June 13, 2023, raising claims for (1) failure to provide rest periods; (2) failure to provide meal periods; (3) failure to pay wages due upon termination; (4) conversion; (5) retaliation; and (6) unfair competition.

 

Defendants filed an Answer on September 14, 2023.

 

Defendants moved for judgment on the pleadings on May 28, 2024. Plaintiff filed a response and Defendants filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

Either prior to trial, but after the time to answer or demur has passed, or at the trial, the plaintiff or the defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings and that the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense. (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791; See also Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586 [The non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for a general demurrer are never waived.], see also Code Civ. Proc., §438(f).)  

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. (See, e.g., Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (TRG 1998) §§ 7:275, 7:322; Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.) Both a demurrer and a motion for judgment on the pleadings accept as true all material factual allegations of the challenged pleading, unless contrary to law or to facts of which a court may take judicial notice. (Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677; Edwards v. Centex Real Estate Corp, (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 15, 27.) 

 

The motion may be made only after one of the following conditions has occurred: (1) If the moving party is a plaintiff, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired; (2) If the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(f).) The motion provided for in Code of Civil Procedure section 438 may be made even though either of the following conditions exist:  (1) The moving party has already demurred to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section and the demurrer has been overruled, provided that there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer; (2) The moving party did not demur to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(g).) No motion may be made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438 if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(e).)

 

Discussion

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s first five claims must be dismissed because Plaintiff brought them outside of their three-year statutes of limitations. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 338 [3- year period for liability created by statute]; Lab. Code, § 226.7 [statute creating claim for failure to provide meal and rest periods]; Lab. Code § 203, subd. (b) [suit penalties for failure to pay wages subject to same statute of limitations as an action on the wages in question]; AmerUS Life Ins. Co. v. Bank of Am., N.A. (2006) 143 Cal. App. 4th 631, 639 [actions for taking goods governed by Code Civ. Proc., § 338(c)’s three-year statute of limitations]; Lab. Code, § 1102.6 [statute creating claim for retaliation].)

 

Plaintiff does not dispute Defendants’ contention that his first five claims are untimely. Plaintiff does argue that his sixth and final claim, for unfair business practices, is timely.

 

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

 

The UCL has a four-year statute of limitations. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17208.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s UCL claim is time-barred because it simply reasserts the facts of Plaintiff’s other claims, which are themselves time-barred. The court disagrees. The UCL creates a separate cause of action with a separate statute of limitations. Defendants have provided no law to indicate that the UCL simply imports the statutes of limitations from other causes of action pertaining to the violation of law that forms the basis for the claim. This is especially salient because the UCL is not, as discussed above, a mere substitute for other torts.

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s UCL claim is impermissibly vague. Defendants do not contend that Plaintiff has failed to allege that Defendants engaged in unlawful acts. Defendants’ contention therefore appears to assume that Plaintiff’s factual allegations regarding time-barred claims must be stricken because his claims must be stricken. As discussed above, that is not the law, because the UCL creates a standalone claim.

 

The Court therefore grants the motion as to Plaintiff’s first five claims and denies the motion as to Plaintiff’s sixth claim.


Case Number: 24STCV07473    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

LINDA GREIF, TRUSTEE OF THE GREIF FAMILY TRUST,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

T.D. TRAUTH,

 

                                  Defendant.

 

 Case No:  24STCV07473

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  June 20, 2024

 Calendar Number:  10

 

 

 

Defendant T.D. Trauth (“Defendant”) moves for summary judgment as to the Complaint filed by Plaintiff Linda Greif, Trustee of the Greif Family Trust (“Plaintiff”).

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

 

Background

 

            This is an unlawful detainer case. The following facts are taken from the Complaint and supporting documents.

 

            Defendant is a tenant at the residential property located at 725 N. Sweetzer Avenue, Unit 102, Los Angeles, California 90069 (the “Property”). Plaintiff is the landlord and owner of the Property. Plaintiff agreed to rent the property month-to-month and pay rent of $2,100.00 per month.

 

            On March 12, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with a three-day notice to pay rent or quit (the “Notice”) by posting it on the premises. The Notice stated that Defendant owed $29,400.00 in unpaid rent for the period of February 15, 2022 through April 14, 2023.

 

            Plaintiff filed this action on March 22, 2022, seeking past-due rent of $29,400.00, attorney’s fees, forfeiture of the agreement, and damages.

 

            Defendant filed this motion on June 5, 2024, with an original calendar date of July 16, 2024.

 

            On June 7, 2024, the Court granted, over Plaintiff’s opposition, Defendant’s ex parte application for an order shortening the time for the hearing to be held and set the hearing for June 20, 2024. In that order, the Court continued the jury trial scheduled for June 17, 2024 to July 1, 2024.

 

            Plaintiff has not filed an opposition to the motion for summary judgment.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“In ruling on the motion, the court must consider all of the evidence and all of the inferences reasonably drawn therefrom [citation] and must view such evidence [citations] and such inferences [citations] in the light most favorable to the opposing party.” (Aguilar, supra, at pp. 844-845 [quotation marks omitted].) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Discussion

 

            A three-day notice to pay rent or quit “may be served at any time within one year after the rent becomes due.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1161, subd. (2).) “If a landlord waits over one year to sue for unpaid rent, the landlord is limited to collecting such rent in an ordinary breach of contract action, with all the delays that remedy normally involves and without restitution of the demised property.” (Bevill v. Zoura (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 694, 697 [citation and quotation marks omitted].)

 

            On March 12, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with a three-day notice to pay rent or quit (the “Notice”) by posting it on the premises. Plaintiff’s Notice stated that Defendant owed $29,400.00 in unpaid rent for the period of February 15, 2022 through April 14, 2023. (Complaint, Ex. 2.) The Notice was served on March 12, 2024. (Complaint, Ex. 2.) The Notice therefore facially seeks rent that was more than a year overdue at the time that it was served. The Notice is therefore defective.

 

            Defendant preemptively responds to a number of arguments Plaintiff might make regarding the Covid-19 eviction moratorium. Because Plaintiff does not raise these arguments, the Court does not address them.

 

            The Court grants Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.
