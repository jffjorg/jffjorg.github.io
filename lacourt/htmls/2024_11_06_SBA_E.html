Case Number: 21STCV20321    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

THELIDRIA CALHOUN, in and through her Successors-In-Interest DIMITRIA CALHOUN, an individual; DARREN CALHOUN; an individual; and DWAYLON CALHOUN, an individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

21STCV20321

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Sustained without leave to amend

 

KND DEVELOPMENT 53, LLC dba KINDRED HOSPITAL SOUTHBAY (ERRONEOUSLY SUED AND SERVED AS KINDRED HOSPITAL SOUTHBAY); and DOES 1 through 50,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             November 6, 2024

 

Moving Party:             Defendant KND Development 53

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiffs Thelidria Calhoun, Dimitria Calhoun, Darren Calhoun, And Dwaylon Calhoun

HEARING: Demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

The Court sustains the Demurrer as to the first cause of action for elder abuse without leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

           

On May 28, 2021, Plaintiffs Thelidria Calhoun, Dimitria Calhoun, Darren Calhoun, And Dwaylon Calhoun filed the Complaint.

 

            On September 25, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint.

 

            On May 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Second Amended Complaint.

 

            On September 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (TAC) against Defendant KND Development 53, alleging causes of action for elder abuse and wrongful death.

 

            On October 10, 2024, Defendant KND Development 53 filed the Demurrer to the TAC.

 

            On October 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 30, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

           

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

DISCUSSION

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for elder abuse.

 

Meet and confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

 

Defendant’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter on September 26, 2024, regarding the demurrer to the TAC. Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant has met the meet and confer requirement.

 

 

“Every pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper shall be signed by at least one attorney of record in the attorney’s individual name, or, if the party is not represented by an attorney, shall be signed by the party. Each paper shall state the signer’s address and telephone number, if any. Except when otherwise provided by law, pleadings need not be verified or accompanied by affidavit. An unsigned paper shall be stricken unless omission of the signature is corrected promptly after being called to the attention of the attorney or party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7 subd. (a).

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s unsigned Demurrer should be stricken in its entirety. However, Defendant filed a notice of errata rectifying the issue. Therefore, the Court uses its discretion to rule on the merits of the Demurrer.

 

Timeliness

 

The Court may dismiss the Complaint after a demurrer to the Compliant is sustained with leave to amend if Plaintiff fails to amend within the time allowed by the Court and either party moves for dismissal. (Code Civ. Proc., § 581 subd. (f)(1).)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs have consistently missed court deadlines and failed to adequately amend their complaints despite multiple opportunities to do so. Plaintiffs filed their TAC eight days late, following a similar delay with their Second Amended Complaint. This tardiness is part of a broader pattern, including an 18-month period of inactivity that led to a prior dismissal. Plaintiffs’ repeated amendments are nearly identical, failing to address the court’s feedback. Defendant requests that the court sustain their demurrer to the TAC without leave to amend and dismiss the Elder Abuse claim due to Plaintiffs' non-compliance.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that, per the court’s ruling, they were given 20 days from the notice date to amend their complaint. Since Defendant’s counsel served notice on August 21, 2024, and under Code of Civil Procedure §1010.6, electronic service allows an extension of two court days, the amendment period began on August 23, 2024. Consequently, Plaintiffs assert that the TAC, filed on September 12, 2024, was within the allotted time and thus timely.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that although Plaintiffs assert that the filing deadline was extended due to a Notice of Ruling filed by Defendant on August 21, Plaintiffs’ counsel attended the August 15 hearing where parties waived the need for notice. (Reply Exhibit 2, Pg. 12.) Additionally, the Court’s minute order and mailing certificate confirm the ruling was served on August 15, making the TAC due by September 4. (Reply Exhibit 3, Pg. 1.)

 

Parties waived notice during the August 15 hearing, and the Court granted 20 days leave to amend the elder abuse cause of action. (8/15/2024 Minute Order.) Therefore, the Court finds that the TAC is untimely. However, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the motion on its merits.

 

Cause of Action 1 – Elder Abuse (Welfare & Institutions Code § 15600)

           

            In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789.) There must be a showing of recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression. (Id. at 783.)  

 

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).) “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.” (Id. at 410 (citation omitted).) “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.” (Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).) Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code §15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.) “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.” (Id. at 159.) 

 

“If some care is provided, that will not necessarily absolve a care facility of [elder or] dependent abuse liability. For example, if a care facility knows it must provide a certain type of care on a daily basis but provides that care sporadically or is supposed to provide multiple types of care but only provides some of those types of care, withholding of care has occurred. In those cases, the trier of fact must determine whether there is a significant pattern of withholding portions or types of care. A significant pattern is one that involves repeated withholding of care and leads to the conclusion that the pattern was the result of choice or deliberate indifference.” (Sababin v. Superior Court¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 90.) 

 

Civil Code §3294 subdivision (b) provides, “An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

           

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs cannot pursue claims under California’s Elder and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act for professional negligence, as such claims require more than ordinary negligence. Defendant explains that elder abuse claims must involve custodial neglect or intentional misconduct beyond standard medical negligence. (Benun v. Superior Court (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 113, 121.) Plaintiffs' allegations, focused on inadequate wound care, do not meet this threshold since wound care is considered professional, not custodial, care. Furthermore, Defendant contends that Plaintiffs failed to provide specific facts showing recklessness or malice and simply repeated prior allegations without addressing the court’s concerns. (Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 31.) Defendant requests dismissal of the elder abuse claim as inadequately pleaded.

 

            In opposition, Defendant points to paragraphs 49-51 regarding specific and particular allegations and 53, 54, and 56 regarding authorization or ratification. (TAC ¶¶ 49-51, 53, 54, and 56.)

           

           

49. On or about January 27, 2019, DECEDENT was admitted to KINDRED with pre-existing bed sores and wounds on her back and other serious medical conditions. The bed sores and wounds required careful and regular repositioning, hygiene, and wound care to allow them to heal, and to prevent further deterioration, infection, and new bed sores and wounds.

 

50. From the time of DECEDENT’s admission in 2019 until her death on DECEDENT’s daughter Dimitria Calhoun repeatedly requested KINDRED to reposition DECEDENT frequently and to provide appropriate care for her bed sores and wounds

 

51. Despite the request, KINDRED and its nursing staff violated California’s Welfare & Institutions’ Code Sections 15600 et.seq. in the following manner: (a) Repeatedly failed to reposition DECEDENT regularly, allowing her to remain in positions that exacerbated her bed sores and wounds, and caused the breakout of new bed sores and wounds; (b) Repeatedly failed to timely and adequately change DECEDENT’s soiled linens and garments, allowing her wounds to come into contact with feces and urine; (c) Repeatedly failed to clean and care for DECEDENT’s wounds in a proper manner, resulting in infections and severe pain and discomfort; (d) Repeatedly failed to provide basic hygiene, including bathing and oral care, leaving DECEDENT in unsanitary conditions with foul odors; and (e) Repeatedly failed to provide adequate nutrition and hydration, and sometimes refusing to provide food and water.

           

53. In April 2019, DECEDENT’s daughter, Plaintiff DIMITRIA, requested and had a meeting with KINDRED’s CEO and Heads of Departments. At the meeting DIMITRIA complained about the problems alleged in paragraph 50 hereinabove, and pleaded for transfer to another facility, KINDRED ORANGE, or a significant improvement in the care provided to DECEDENT, to resolve all the problems mentioned above in paragraph 50. KINDRED denied the transfer request and failed to improve the inhumane treatment of DECEDENT.

 

54. The actions and omissions of Defendant KINDRED and its staff were reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, and malicious, as defined under California law, demonstrating a conscious disregard for Decedent’s rights, safety, and wellbeing, warranting punitive damages under Welfare and Institutions Code §15657.

 

56. The actions of the employees of KINDRED and DOES 1-50 were ratified by KINDRED’s CEO and other Department Heads when they decided not to take action against the employees or take remedial action to provide proper care to DECEDENT. The CEO failed to remediate the care of DECEDENT and Defendants allowed KINDRED staff to maintain the poor and substandard care rendered to DECEDENT.

(TAC ¶¶ 49-51 and 53-56.)

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs' Opposition merely reiterates the general elements of Elder Abuse without addressing specific deficiencies identified by the Court in previous rulings. The TAC lacks concrete details, such as responsible individuals, specific times, and actions, that are necessary to establish an Elder Abuse claim. Plaintiffs rely on broad allegations of inadequate care but do not specify facts supporting claims of willful or malicious conduct. Additionally, Plaintiffs' assertions of ratification are nearly identical to those in the prior complaint, failing to add any substantive detail. (Exhibit 8; Pg. 6.) Defendant contends this repetition shows a lack of genuine effort to meet the Court’s requirements, and therefore, the demurrer to the elder abuse cause of action should be sustained.

 

Previously, the Court sustained the Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint as to the cause of action for elder abuse because the pleaded facts did not “rise to the level of oppression, fraud, or malice or a conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (unstageable bed sore).” (8/15/2024 Minute Order.) Furthermore, the Court found that the Second Amended Complaint failed to plead any specific facts that show an officer, director, or managing agent authorized the Defendant’s employees’ conduct. (8/15/2024 Minute Order.)

 

Plaintiff has failed to plead new facts that show an officer, director, or managing agent authorized Defendant’s employees' conduct. In the Opposition, Defendant pointed to paragraphs 53, 54, and 56 of the TAC to prove that the TAC alleged particular facts showing that an officer, director, or managing agent authorized the misconduct. However, paragraphs 53 and 54 of the TAC are nearly identical to paragraph 31 of the Second Amended Complaint, which the Court found to be defective. (Second Amended Complaint ¶ 31; 8/15/2024 Minute Order.) 

 

Furthermore, paragraph 56 merely concludes that Defendant's CEO and other department heads ratified the misconduct, and paragraph 54 merely concludes that Defendant was reckless, fraudulent, and malicious. (TAC ¶¶ 54, 56.) Therefore, the Court finds that TAC does not plead sufficient facts alleging that a managing agent of Defendant authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct. Furthermore, Plaintiff’s continuous late filing and repeated failure to adequately plead facts to support a claim for elder abuse shows that an amendment cannot be made to remedy this issue.

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer as to the first cause of action for elder abuse without leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

JUDICIAL NOTICE

           

The Court may take judicial notice of records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (d).) The Court may also take judicial notice of “Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.” (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (c).)

 

Defendant requests that the Court take notice of the existence and contents of the Verified Petition for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief Ordering Compliance with California Public Records Act, Government Code § 6258, the existence and contents of the Defendant’s Answer to the Petition for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief filed in LASC Case No. 22TRCP00251, the existence and contents of the Declaration of Esther Kim in support of the Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment filed in LASC Case No. 22TRCP00251, and the fact that according to the Torrance Online Citizen Portal for the City of Torrance, the status of the Building Permit Application for the Property identified as BLD24-000649 is “out for corrections” and was “picked up by Esse Kim.”

 

The Court grants these requests.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

 

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to the California Public Records Act and the California Constitution, Petitioner seeks to make a copy of a “revised set of records” for the Minor Hillside Exemption Permit Application pertaining to the real property located at 139 Via Alameda, Redondo Beach, CA (Property) identified by the CITY as MHE 23-00021:

A0.0 – Coversheet & Site Plan

A2.1 – 1st Floor Plan – Date 10:20:23

A2.3 – 2nd Floor Plan – Date 10:20:23

A2.5 – Roof Plan – Date 10:2:23

A3.1 – Elevations – Date 1-14-24.

(See Petition, ¶¶1, 6 – 17, 23, 32.) Petitioner also seeks to make copies of documents contained in the Building Permit Applications for the property identified as BLD24-00649 and BLD00650. (Petition ¶ 25.)

 

Defendant demurs to the TAC on the grounds that Petitioner has failed to join a necessary party.

 

Meet and confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41 subd. (a).)

 

On September 5, 2024, Defendant’s Counsel spoke with Petitioner regarding the demurrer. (Guerrero Decl. ¶ 2.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Joinder

 

The Code of Civil Procedure section 389 requires that a person be joined as a party “if (1) in his absence complete relief cannot be accorded among those already parties or (2) he claims an interest relating to the subject of the action and is so situated that the disposition of the action in his absence may (i) as a practical matter impair or impede his ability to protect that interest or (ii) leave any of the persons already parties subject to a substantial risk of incurring double, multiple, or otherwise inconsistent obligations by reason of his claimed interest.” (Code Civ. Proc § 389, subd. (a).) A person meeting these requirements is often referred to as a “necessary party.” (Bowles v. Superior Court (1955) 44 Cal.2d 574, 583, 283 P.2d 704; Quantification Settlement Agreement Cases (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 758, 848, 134 Cal.Rptr.3d 274.)

 

Defendant argues that complete relief cannot be granted to the Petitioner without including the design professionals and owner of the property, such as Esther Kim Design, as indispensable parties. (Code Civ. Proc. § 389 subd. (a)(b); In re Marriage of Ramirez (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 336, 345.) These parties are essential because they hold property rights and monetary interests in the project plans and drawings (see e.g., Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 61503.)  Given that the building plans are "out for correction," meaning they are currently with Esther Kim Design for revisions, any order regarding the plans would directly impact their interests. Therefore, Defendant contends that Esther Kim Design and other relevant professionals must be joined.

 

In opposition, Petitioner argues that complete relief can be given to Petitioner if Petitioner is allowed to use his own equipment to copy the documents at issue. Furthermore, Petitioner states that in his First Amended Complaint, which has not been filed with the Court, Petitioner only requests to make copies of the documents Defendant currently has possession of.

 

            In reply, Defendant argues that Petitioner's First Amended Verified Petition, referenced in his Opposition, is not on the court’s docket, making it unclear if it was officially submitted. Petitioner does not contest the distinct property and pecuniary interests of design professionals in the plans, nor that these interests differ from Defendant’s and are not adequately represented. Instead, Petitioner suggests design professionals should file a separate lawsuit to protect their rights but incorrectly claims this must be in federal court. (Opposition pp. 6-7.) Defendant counters that federal jurisdiction only applies if a federal issue appears on the petition's face. (Scholastic Entm’t, Inc. v. Fox Entm’t Group, Inc. (9th Cir. 2003) 336 F.3d 982, 986.) However, because federal copyright law is not an essential element in proving Petitioner’s argument under the California Public Records Act, California Government Code section 7920.000, or the California Constitution, the petition does not arise out of federal copyright law, and therefore, the court would not lose jurisdiction if Esther Kim Designs was joined.

 

            The plans that the Petition seeks to copy are currently in the possession of Esther Kim Designs, and Esther Kim Designs holds property rights to the plans. Therefore, Esther Kim Designs is a necessary party because the Court cannot grant relief to Petitioner as the plans are held by a third party. Furthermore, Esther Kim Designs holds proprietary rights to the plans, and not joining them to this suit may open up Defendant to multiple inconsistent obligations. (Request for Judicial Notice Exhibit D ¶ 8.) While Petitioner argues that in the FAC, he clarifies that he only seeks documents Defendant currently possesses, Petitioner has not filed the FAC. Furthermore, Petitioner has not rebutted that failing to join Esther Kim Designs may lead to multiple inconsistent obligations for Defendant or impair Esther Kim Designs' ability to protect its interest. Last, the Court finds that joining Esther Kim Designs would not cause the Court to lose jurisdiction over the case as this case does not arise out of federal copyright law. (Scholastic Entm’t, Inc., supra, 336 F.3d 982, 986.)

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer with leave to amend.
