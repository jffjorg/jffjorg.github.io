Case Number: 20STCV28343    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

Hearing Date: 6/6/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV28343 SIMON MAJDIPOUR vs MARC I. ZUSSMAN

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendants

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION IS GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART

 

Background 

 

On July 29, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present dog bite case against Defendants Marc Zussman and Tami Zussman, asserting causes of action for (1) general negligence and (2) strict liability pursuant to California Civil Code Section 3342 (“the Dog Bite Statute”). Plaintiff, owner of a carpet store, alleges that when he went to Defendants’ house to pick up carpets for cleaning, he was attacked by Defendants’ two dogs. Plaintiff now moves the Court for summary adjudication on the following affirmative defenses: 1st affirmative defense for Plaintiff’s comparative negligence; 4th affirmative defense for Plaintiff’s failure to mitigate; 5th affirmative defense that Defendants possessed no knowledge of the dogs' vicious propensity as to the strict liability cause of action; and 6th affirmative defense that Plaintiff trespassed, asserting that Plaintiff was not authorized to be on Defendants’ property at the time of the incident.

Evidentiary Rulings

        Plaintiff’s objections to the Declaration of Hilary Patton are overruled.

        Defendant’s objections to the exhibits attached to the Vafa Declaration exhibits are overruled.  Defendant’s objection no. 13 is sustained. 

The Court did not otherwise rely on evidence to which an objection was made, and thus the Court does not rule on those objections.

Legal Standard

 

Summary Judgment

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.  While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. [Citation.] Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.  If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true. [Citation.] Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

The Dog Bite Statute

Civil Code §3342(a) provides that, “The owner of any dog is liable for the damages suffered by any person who is bitten by the dog while in a public place or lawfully in a private place, including the property of the owners of the dog, regardless of the former viciousness of the dog or the owner’s knowledge of such viciousness…” Civil Code §3342(a), known as the “dog bite” statute, allows one to recover without having to show fault. “Subdivision (a) of section 3342 has been recognized as imposing a duty of care on every dog owner to prevent his or her dog from biting persons in a public place or lawfully in a private place. (Davis v. Gaschler (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1392, 1399.) Simply put, the statute is designed “to prevent dogs from becoming a hazard to the community” (Ibid.) by holding dog owners to such a standard of care, and assigning strict liability for its breach.” (Priebe v. Nelson (2006) 39 Cal.4th 1112, 1120.)

To prevail under the dog bite statute in the present case, Plaintiff needs to prove that Defendants owned the dog, Plaintiff was harmed, Defendants’ dog was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm and the dog bit Plaintiff while he was lawfully on private property. Plaintiff is considered lawfully on private property of the owner if he was on the property at the invitation, express or implied, of the owner. (CACI No. 463.)

 

Affirmative Defenses

 

Comparative Negligence

 

Plaintiff’s total damages are not recoverable to the extent his or her own negligence contributed to the injuries. Rather, awardable damages must be proportionately reduced to reflect the percentage of plaintiff’s “fault.” (Li v. Yellow Cab Co. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 804, 828–829.) The trier of fact determines what percentage of the fault that caused the injuries was attributable to plaintiff and then reduces gross damages by that percentage. Thus, some amount of damages is recoverable even if plaintiff's negligence is equal to or greater than defendant’s. (See also Zavala v. Regents of Univ. of Calif. (1981) 125 Cal.App.3d 646; CACI 405, 3960; BAJI 14.90, 14.91.)  Comparative negligence “does not lend itself to the exact measurements of a micrometer-caliper.” The trier of fact has broad discretion in allocating fault and may consider all relevant evidence on the question in order to arrive at an “equitable apportionment or allocation of loss.” (Brandon G. v. Gray (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 29, 40-41.) Appellate courts will not disturb the jury's apportionment of negligence so long as its decision is supported by substantial evidence. (Id.) Plaintiff's negligence or other culpable conduct need not be a “proximate” (“legal”) cause of the injury. Rather, comparative fault principles are properly invoked whenever plaintiff's conduct contributes to the overall harm emanating from the injury. (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1011.) 

 

Contrary to Plaintiff’s past practice of ringing the bell and waiting for Defendants to open the door, evidence presented by Plaintiff shows that on this occasion, Plaintiff entered Defendant's property unannounced by opening both the gate and the door. Furthermore, upon entering, Plaintiff was seen swatting at the dogs and shortly thereafter, Plaintiff displayed to Tami Zussman an inch-long open bloody wound on his forearm. (Tami Zussman Depo. 116:12-21.) Despite the injury, Plaintiff remained at Defendants' residence for an additional 45 minutes to measure carpets, then traveled back to his store with Tami Zussman and subsequently returned to the house with the selected carpets. (Tami Depo. 123:1-14.)

 

Based solely on the evidence presented by Plaintiff, reasonable minds could differ whether Plaintiff's actions of entering the house without ringing the bell and swatting at the dogs contributed to the incident. Plaintiff's sudden and unexpected entry could have startled the dogs, who may have perceived Plaintiff as an intruder. Plaintiff's further action of swatting at the dogs may have further aggravated the dogs, leading to their attack. Furthermore, it is unclear how long Plaintiff delayed seeking medical attention. Evidence shows that Plaintiff waited at least 45 minutes after the incident to make measurements, then made trips to his store and back to the house before pursuing medical help. This delay in seeking medical attention could have potentially worsened his injuries consisting of an inch-long open bloody wound. (Tami Zussman Depo. 116:12-21; 123:1-14.) These decisions and actions by Plaintiff could be contributing factors to the incident and the overall harm emanating from the injury. Thus, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden and summary adjudication as to Defendants’ first affirmative defense of comparative negligence is DENIED.

 

Mitigation

 

As to the affirmative defense of mitigation, a plaintiff who suffers damage as a result a tort has a duty to take reasonable steps to mitigate those damages and will not be able to recover for any losses that could have been thus avoided. (Shaffer v. Debbas (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 33, 41.) This rule of mitigation of damages comes into play when the event producing injury or damage has already occurred and it then has become the obligation of the injured or damaged party to avoid continuing or enhanced damages through reasonable efforts. (Valle de Oro Bank v. Gamboa (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 1686, 1691.)  

 

Plaintiff’s only argument against Defendant’s affirmative defense of mitigation is that “[Plaintiff] had the right to treat outside his plan.” (Motion at p. 10.) Plaintiff misses the glaring issue that he was bitten by two dogs, resulting in an inch-long bloody open wound, but he did not seek medical attention promptly. Evidence introduced by Plaintiff showed that he spent at least 45 minutes taking measurements at Defendants’ house, then went to his store, and then returned to Defendants' house with two carpets before seeking medical help. Reasonable minds can differ on whether, by not promptly seeking medical attention, Plaintiff failed to avoid continuing or enhanced damages through reasonable efforts. Thus, Plaintiff failed to meet his initial burden and summary adjudication as to Defendants’ fourth affirmative defense of mitigation is DENIED.

 

Knowledge of Dogs’ Vicious Propensity

 

As a matter of law, an owner's knowledge of the dog’s viciousness is not required under Civil Code § 3342(a). The Dog Bite Statute imposes strict liability on dog owners for damages suffered by any person bitten by their dog while lawfully on private property, regardless of the dog's former viciousness or the owner's knowledge of such viciousness. This statute allows one to recover without having to show fault. Because Plaintiff argues that the dog's “prior violent propensities are irrelevant to prevail on the strict liability cause of action” (Motion at p. 10) and is seeking summary adjudication solely on the strict liability claim, Defendants’ argument regarding the owner's knowledge being relevant to the negligence cause of action is immaterial. Therefore, Plaintiff's motion for summary adjudication on Defendants’ fifth affirmative defense against Plaintiff’s strict liability cause of action only is GRANTED.

Trespass 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff was not lawfully upon their private property at the time of the dog bite, thereby negating an element Plaintiff must prove for strict liability.  Whether Plaintiff was lawfully upon Defendants’ property depends on the scope of Defendants’ invitation.

 

Plaintiff presented evidence that Tami Zuckerman expected him on the date of the incident to pick up carpets for cleaning. Her husband informed her that Plaintiff would be arriving sometime in the morning (Plf’s Ex. 4, Tami Zussman Deposition 112:21-113:2; Plf’s Ex. 5, Marc Zussman Deposition 20:7-15). The evidence thus shows that Defendants had invited Plaintiff to their property.

 

Plaintiff’s evidence also shows that on previous occasions when he visited Defendants’ property for carpet-related business, he would ring the bell, Defendants would open the door, and he would enter (Plf’s Ex. 3, Plf’s Depo. 51:5-9). However, on the date of the incident, Plaintiff did not ring the bell and wait for Defendants to open the door. Tami Zussman stated that she did not give permission for Plaintiff to just walk in the house without ringing the bell or knocking on the door. Specifically, she noted, "normal person would ring the doorbell. He bypassed twice. He had twice opportunity to ring the bell and let someone let him come in. He did not do that. He just opened the door and he just walked right in the gate and then he opened ... the front door voluntarily and just walked in." (Plf’s Ex. 4, Tami Zussman Depo. 160:17-23). Furthermore, Marc Zussman’s testimony introduced by Plaintiff indicated that Tami complained to Plaintiff, "Why did you come into my house on your own?" (Plf’s Ex. 5, Marc Zussman Depo. 62:23-63:3) “Plaintiff walked into our house on his own. No one let him in.” (Id.)

 

Fullerton v. Conan (1948) 87 Cal.App.2d 354, 357-358 addresses the scope of an invitation. The court held that “It should be noted at the outset that gates and doors have been the generally accepted means of indicating privacy for centuries; their use for such a purpose is traditional and is still sanctioned by universal custom. A gate is a barrier just as a door to a home; both in effect are notifications that what is beyond them is private. And it must be assumed that when gates and doors are a part of the property involved they represent such a purpose. Nor is an invitation without limitation. It would be presumptuous indeed to assume that a social¿invitation includes authorization or permission to ignore the customary formalities and establish by judicial decision that such an invitation includes the right, as a matter of law, to enter without observing the customary formalities.” Fullerton v. Conan (1948) 87 Cal.App.2d 354, 357-358. 

 

Based on the evidence presented by Plaintiff, in prior instances he would ring the bell and Defendants would open the door to let him in. However, on the date of the incident, Plaintiff did not ring the bell or wait for Defendants to open the door; instead, he opened the gate, then the door, and walked straight into the house. This conduct is contrary to Plaintiff’s past interactions with Defendants and the universal customs of knocking or ringing the bell before entering someone’s house.  Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his initial burden to establish that no triable issue exists as to Defendants’ sixth affirmative defense of trespass.  Accordingly, summary adjudication as to Defendants’ sixth affirmative defense of trespass is DENIED.

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Court grants summary adjudication on the fifth affirmative defense as to the dog bite cause of action and otherwise denies the motion for summary adjudication.

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 6/6/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV04346 ROGELIO AGUILAR ZAMORA, et al. vs JUAN CALDERON

Moving Party: Defendant

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.  (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).)  The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.  (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).) The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial.  (See generally CRC Rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).)  Additional factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.  (CRC Rule 3.1332(c), (d).)

 

On February 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present motor accident case. On January 18, 2024, Defendant Juan Calderon answered the complaint. The current trial is set for August 26, 2024. Defendant now moves the Court to continue the trial by five months to allow more time for discovery. Considering the limited amount of time Defendant had to conduct discovery, Defendant’s request is reasonable. Given the recent filing of the case, no prior requests for continuances, the current request for a continuance of only 5 months, and no opposition arguing for prejudice, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to continue the trial.

Background

 

On September 14, 2023, Defendants propounded their first set of special interrogatories on Plaintiff who is appearing in pro per. On March 4, 2024, Plaintiff served her discovery responses, which were untimely. Defendants attempted to meet and confer but received no responses. On April 19, 2024, Defendants moved the Court to compel further responses. On May 31, 2024, Defendants attended an informal discovery conference (IDC) concerning the discovery responses at issue; however, Plaintiff did not attend the IDC and the Court allowed Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further to be heard.

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff served her responses on March 4, 2024 through electronic service, and Defendants filed and served the present motion on April 19, 2024, within the statutory deadline. Defendants attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff but received no responses, thereby fulfilling its meet and confer obligations. Defendants attended an informal discovery conference (IDC), but Plaintiff did not attend, and the Court allowed Defendants’ motion to be heard. All the preliminary requirements for the Court to hear the present motion have been met.

 

Special Interrogatories 12, 24, 26, 27:

 

GRANT. Plaintiff’s responses are unintelligible or non-responsive, e.g., “[Blank.]”. A party has a duty to answer the interrogatories as completely and straightforwardly as possible given the information available to it. (C.C.P. § 2030.220.) Plaintiff needs to provide full and complete responses that are as straightforward as possible to the questions asked, including all subparts. If a responding party does not know the answer to a particular discovery response, the party must make a reasonable inquiry and investigation to acquire the knowledge or information from all sources under the responding party’s control. (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at 782.) If the responding party lacks personal knowledge sufficient to respond, he or she may so state, but only after making a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other persons or organizations (Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court, (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504.)

 

Special Interrogatories 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 20, 21, 22, 23, 25, 28, 29, 30, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35:

 

GRANT. Plaintiff did not timely respond to Defendants’ discovery requests and therefore has waived all of her objections. (CCP § 2030.290(a)). Plaintiff needs to provide full and complete responses to the requests at issue without objections, including all subparts.

 

 

SANCTIONS

 

Defendants have requested sanctions of $3200 in relation to the present motion. Given that the Court is granting the present motion, sanctions are mandatory. However, because no opposition was filed and attending the hearing will most likely not take two hours, the Court exercises its discretion and lowers the amount of sanctions to $2000. Thus, the Court grants sanctions against Plaintiff in amount of $2000, to be paid within 30 days of today’s date.
