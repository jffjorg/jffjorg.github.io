Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling.

Case Number: 22AHCV00356    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: X


Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted and Order to Show Cause Hearing

 

Order to Show Cause Hearing re: Striking Defendant Mile High’s Answer and Cross-Complaint

 

The Court notes that a substitution of attorney has been filed as to Defendant Mile High Cure Corp.  However, no substitution of attorney has been filed on behalf of Cross-Complainant Mile High Cure Corp.  In the absence of any such substitution filed prior to the OSC hearing, the latter will remain on calendar.

 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted; Request for Sanctions

 

Plaintiff moves for an order that the truth of the matters specified in the requests for admission be deemed admitted against Defendant Mile High.  As responses have been provided, the Motion is denied as moot.  However, sanctions are granted in the amount of $598.50.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (b), provides that if a party fails to respond to a request for admission, “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted”.  Unless the court determines that the responding party “has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220, it must order the RFAs deemed admitted… But a responding party’s service, prior to the hearing on the ‘deemed admitted’ motion, of substantially compliant responses, will defeat a propounding party’s attempt under section 2033.280 to have the RFAs deemed admitted.”  (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 775–776, citations and footnote omitted.)

 

Plaintiff served its first set of requests for admissions on December 6, 2023, which were due January 8, 2024 (Liberzon Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1).  Plaintiff provided at least three extensions to Mile High.  (Liberzon Decl., ¶¶ 5-6.)  Plaintiff provides that Mile High failed to produce any responses as of the filing of this motion (August 27, 2024).  (Liberzon Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

In opposition, Defendant Mile High provides it served objection-free responses on October 7, 2024.  (Murrin Decl., ¶ 4.)  Because Defendant has served responses prior to the hearing on this motion, Plaintiff’s motion to deem admitted is denied as moot.

 

Due to the necessity in having to file the instant motion to secure responses, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $598.50 is granted.  Sanctions are payable by Defendant Mile High to Plaintiff’s counsel and due within 30 days of mailing of a notice of ruling.

 

Moving Party is to give notice.


Case Number: 22AHCV00421    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: X


Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney Fees

 

On June 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors, LLC which included a claim for Violation of Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act-Civil Code §1790 et seq.  Plaintiff, having accepted Defendant’s CCP Section 998 Offer to Compromise, now moves for an order that determines the reasonable attorneys’ fees payable to Plaintiff by Defendant General Motors.  Plaintiff states its attorneys’ fees are in the amount of $42,091.50, a 1.35 multiplier enhancement on the attorney fees ($14,732.03), costs in the amount of $3,484.68, and an additional $3,500 for Plaintiff’s counsel to review the opposition, draft a reply, and attend the hearing on this motion. 
 

The Court GRANTS the motion in the amount of $29,835.00 and costs in the amount of $3,484.68.  In total, Plaintiff is awarded $33,319.68 in attorney fees and costs.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

In Plaintiff’s reply papers, Plaintiff’s counsel Castro filed evidentiary objections to the Pappas Declaration.  The objections are overruled.

 

Legal Analysis

 

Generally, each party is responsible for their attorney fees unless a statute or contract provides otherwise for the prevailing party.  (Eden Township Healthcare Dist. v. Eden Medical Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 418, 425.)  In determining which party is the prevailing party, trial courts may consider all factors reasonably indicating success in the litigation but may not abuse their discretion and deny fees arbitrarily.  (Sears v. Baccaglio (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1136, 1149, 1155, 1158.)  A court abuses its discretion by denying fees where the party obtained a “‘simple, unqualified win,’” and the results were not mixed.  (Pacific Custom Pools, Inc. v. Turner Construction Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1254, 1268-1271.)

 

“[T]he starting point of every fee award…must be a calculation of the attorney’s services in terms of the time he has expended on the case.  Anchoring the analysis to this concept is the only way of approaching the problem that can claim objectivity, a claim which is obviously vital to the prestige of the bar and the courts.”  (In re Vitamin Cases (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1058.)  “[T]he primary method for establishing the amount of ‘reasonable’ attorney fees is the lodestar method.  The lodestar (or touchstone) is produced by multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by counsel by a reasonable hourly rate.”  (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2001) 92Cal.App.4th 819, 833.)  Under this approach, a base amount is calculated from a compilation of time reasonably spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney.  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 639.)  While “a fee request ordinarily should be documented in great detail,” the absence of time records and billing statements did not deprive the court of “substantial evidence” to support an award when the attorney provided a declaration under the penalty of perjury which described the work and permitted the trial court to make its own evaluation of the reasonableness of the work done in light of the nature of the case and on the credibility of counsel’s declaration.  (Weber v. Langholz (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1578, 1587.)

 

Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff is the prevailing party.  Further, Section 1794, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of damages and other legal and equitable relief when the action involves a buyer of consumer goods that is damaged by an implied or express warranty of a contract.  Section 1794, subdivision (d) further authorizes the recovery of attorney’s fees “based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.”  (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Payam Shahian, declares the rates of attorneys at his firm Strategic Legal Practices, APC, are reasonable.  In Shahian’s Declaration, he lists numerous cases where the courts approved the plaintiff’s requested lodestar and hourly rates, ranging from $325 to $695 per hour.

 

The opposition complains the requested fees and costs are unreasonably excessive and cites three main reasons to significantly reduce Plaintiff’s counsel’s fee requests:

 

1.     Counsel’s repeated use of templated filings shows that this was a routine lemon law case, and the counsel’s litigation-by-template strategy was evident from the first filing to the last, which is incongruent with the claimed fees.

2.     Counsel fails to show that a multiplier is warranted in this straightforward single-vehicle lemon law case, as fee multipliers are reserved only for truly exceptional situations.  The counsel recycled templated discovery, correspondence, and in-limine motions used in every Song-Beverly Act against GM, despite billing this case as if the work were original or unique.

3.     Counsel should not benefit from inefficiencies that resulted from the excessive number of billing timekeepers.  If the counsel’s fee motion were granted in full, with the requested enhancement, they would be awarded a total of $60,323.53, which GM argues is unreasonably excessive for this case.  The opposition argues a non-complex lemon law matter such as this, where not a single deposition was taken, which never went to trial, and settled within statutory limits, does not command such a fee recovery.

 

A reasonable hourly rate is determined by the prevailing rate charged to attorneys of similar skill and experience in the relevant community.  (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)  However, the court may also consider the attorney’s skill and expertise, the nature of the work performed, the relevant area of expertise and the attorney’s customary billing rates.  (Flannery v. California Highway Patrol (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 629, 632.)  The court is the best judge of the value of professional services provided and may use its discretion to apply rates in line with the market rates for the services provided.  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)  Based on the Court’s knowledge and experience, it determines that $450.00 represents a reasonable hourly rate.

 

As to the number of hours expended in prosecuting this action, Plaintiff attached the billing records to the Shahian Declaration as Exhibit 20.  Plaintiff’s motion argues his victory was obtained after two years of litigation which would not have been possible without the efforts of counsel, which included propounding and responding to discovery requests, filing and opposing motions, and responding to Defendant’s demurrers.

 

A court “must carefully review attorney documentation of hours expended” to determine whether the time reported was reasonable.  (See Ketchum, supra, 24 Cal.4th at p. 1132 [quoting Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48].)  Thus, evidence provided by the fee applicant “should allow the court to consider whether the case was overstaffed, how much time the attorneys spent on particular claims, and whether the hours were reasonably expended.”  (Christian Research Inst. v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1320.  The court must exclude “duplicative or excessive” time from its fee award.  (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 161; see also Ketchum, supra, at p. 1132 [stating “inefficient or duplicative efforts [are] not subject to compensation.”].)

 

The billing records submitted by Plaintiff’s counsel indicate that the attorneys expended 78.8 hours.  (Shahian Decl., Exh. 20.)  In reply, co-counsel Christian R. Castro declares he has spent over 9.3 hours preparing the reply brief, evidentiary objections, his declaration, and the response to Defendant’s time entry objections; his rate is $475 per hour.  (Castro Decl. (Reply), ¶ 6.)

 

The opposition argues Plaintiff’s fee demand should be reduced by at least 44.5 hours ($23,474.14) and reduced further by 20% to remove the padding caused by overstaffing the case.  Further, Defendant argues the entire amount of $3,484.68 in claimed costs should be disallowed because Plaintiff’s counsel has not filed a memorandum of costs nor provided evidence to support the claimed costs beyond the billing records in Exhibit 20 of the Shahian Declaration.  Thus, Defendant concludes Plaintiff should only be awarded attorney’s fees in the amount of $14,893.89.

 

The court has discretion to apply a reduction in hours for duplicative and or excessive billing. Proper factors to consider in applying a negative reduction are the lack of complexity, that the matter did not go to trial, that named partners were doing work that could have been done by lower-billing attorneys, and that all the attorneys were doing work that could have been done by paralegals, thus applying a 39% reduction in the lodestar.  (Morris v. Hyundai Motor America (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 41.)  The Court finds that the reasonable hours spent by counsel are 66.3.  In making this determination, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s counsel billed for some clerical tasks and that certain billings were excessive.  This includes excessive billing for telephonic/remote appearances and drafting boilerplate documents.  Attorney fees are awarded in the amount of $29,835.00.

 

Finally, on the issue of costs, Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) states, “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses,…, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.  The Court awards $3,484.68 for recoverable costs.
