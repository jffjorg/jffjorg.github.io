DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00165    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Michael Schutz and Nicole Schutz, Trustees of the Michael & Nicole Schutz Family Trust Dated July 21, 2004 and ALI J LLC filed this action against Defendants, WNDM Properties, LLC, QNDM Distribution, LLC, John Barrett, Jr., Mark A. Bruno, and Kelly Marie Dooley for unlawful detainer.  They filed their original complaint on 4/12/22, but once possession was no longer in issue, the case was converted to a civil action, and they filed their FAC for unpaid rent on 8/05/22. 

 

Cross-complaints are pending amongst the defendants, and the moving defendants also have a cross-complaint pending against Luis Castro and Charnel James, neither of whom would otherwise be parties to the action. 

 

2.     Motion for Leave to File a Cross-Complaint

a.     Parties’ Positions

The two entity defendants and Dooley move for leave to file a cross-complaint against Plaintiffs, pursuant to which they seek to assert claims for breach of contract, promissory estoppel, fraudulent misrepresentation, unjust enrichment, violation of unfair competition law, and breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.  Defendants contend Plaintiffs made representations that caused Plaintiffs to invest substantial sums in the premises, then breached their promises by filing the UD complaint.   

 

Plaintiffs oppose the motion, arguing (a) there is no showing of lack of bad faith with the moving papers, and (b) the facts of the case show bad faith.    

 

Defendants, in reply, contend the facts set forth in the opposition show delay, but do not show bad faith.  They therefore contend leave to file the cross-complaint is compulsory. 

 

b.     Law Governing Leave to File a Compulsory Cross-Complaint

CCP §426.30 defines a cross-complaint as “compulsory” if it is pled against the plaintiff and includes claims related to the subject matter of the complaint.  Defendants’ proposed cross-complaint clearly meets this test, and Plaintiffs concede as much in opposition to the motion.  CCP §426.50 makes clear that the Court MUST grant a motion for leave to file a compulsory cross-complaint unless it finds the moving party is acting in bad faith. 

 

c.     Initial Note

Defendants originally filed their cross-complaint on 9/01/23.  They did so without leave of court, which was improper.  Plaintiffs therefore filed a motion to strike the unauthorized complaint, which is set for hearing on 2/22/24.  Defendants then filed this motion for leave to file the cross-complaint on 1/02/24. 

 

d.     Analysis

Findings of bad faith based on substantial evidence are required to justify denial of a motion for leave to file a cross-complaint.  See Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98-99.  Plaintiffs made a detailed showing of delay, but did not make any showing at all of bad faith.  The motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is therefore granted. 

 

The Court notes, however, that trial is scheduled to go forward on 3/25/24.  The Court wishes to hear from the parties, at the time of the hearing, concerning whether trial will be going forward on 3/25/24 or not.  The Court notes that there is a demurrer to one of the other cross-complaints in the action scheduled for hearing on 5/09/24.  It is possible that the action could be bifurcated to permit trial on the main complaint to go forward as scheduled, with trial on the cross-complaint(s) going forward at a later date.  No party has, to date, requested bifurcation.  If the parties do not appear at the hearing (submit on the tentative), the Court will leave the 3/25/24 trial date scheduled and will assume the parties will be prepared to go to trial on the complaint and all pending cross-complaints, including this one, at that time.

 

Defendants are ordered to file a separate copy of their cross-complaint within ten days. 

 

The 2/22/24 hearing on Plaintiffs’ motion to strike is taken off calendar as moot in light of this ruling. 

 

Moving Defendants are ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 22LBCV00356    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Suzanne Yorgason filed this action against Defendant, Ken Lamphear for elder abuse, fraud, and related claims arising out of a real estate transaction.  The crux of the complaint is that Lamphear fraudulently induced Plaintiff to sell her home, worth approximately $850,000, to him for $250,000 plus the agreement that Plaintiff’s sister could retain a life estate in the property.    

 

            The case proceeded to jury trial, and the jury ultimately returned a verdict in the amount of $1,659,456 (compensatory) plus $3,000,000 (punitive).  On 12/15/23, the Court heard Defendant’s unopposed motion for a new trial.  The Court granted the motion in part, reducing the punitive damages award from $3 million to $939,669. 

 

2.     Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

a.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff seeks an attorneys’ fees award in the amount of $1,659,805.  Defendant opposes the motion, arguing (a) all fees billed by Justin Torres should be disallowed because Torres is a disbarred attorney, and (b) no lodestar multiplier should be awarded.  Plaintiff, in reply, concedes Torres’s fees should not be awarded, but argues the requested multiplier is appropriate.  Thus, the sole issue before the Court is whether it should award a lodestar multiplier on top of the actual fees incurred. 

 

b.     Fees Not in Dispute

The amount of fees not in dispute is $487,172.  Those fees are billed at rates ranging from $350/hour to $650/hour.  Notably, the vast majority of the fees were billed at the rate of $650/hour (662.2 hours, vs. approximately 115 total hours by all other attorneys). 

 

c.     Law Governing Lodestar Multipliers

The unadorned lodestar reflects the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community. It may be adjusted based on various factors, including:

•       the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved;

•       the skill displayed in presenting them;

•       the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys;

•       the contingent nature of the fee award (except as mitigated by the client's agreement to pay some portion of the fee regardless of the outcome);

•       the success achieved (including all positive results from litigation—i.e., changes in company policy, dollar value of settlement benefits); and

•           in class actions, the rate of acceptance of the benefit offered to class members (measures one aspect of overall success—i.e., the degree to which the settlement benefits were of interest to class members).   Serrano v. Priest (“Serrano III”) (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49. 

 

The purpose of a fee enhancement is “primarily to compensate the attorney for the prevailing party at a rate reflecting the risk of nonpayment in contingency cases as a class.”  Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1138.  The aim is to compensate attorneys for their services at fair market value as an inducement to accept such matters. (“Fair market value” typically includes a premium for the risk of nonpayment or delay in payment of attorney fees.)   Id. at 1138, 104 CR2d at 389.  The party seeking the fee enhancement bears the burden of proof.  Id. 

 

The court must not consider extraordinary skill and the other Serrano factors to the extent these are already included within the lodestar. Id. at 1139.  In particular, extraordinary skill and the difficulty of the question involved are usually already encompassed in the lodestar.  “A trial court should award a multiplier for exceptional representation only when the quality of representation far exceeds the quality of representation that would have been provided by an attorney of comparable skill and experience billing at the hourly rate used in the lodestar calculation. Otherwise, the fee award will result in unfair double counting and be unreasonable.” Id. 

 

d.     Analysis

Plaintiff argues a lodestar multiplier of 2.2 is appropriate in this case because (a) the fee was contingent and therefore the risk of nonpayment was high, and (b) Counsel’s skill justifies a multiplier.  The Court finds Counsel’s skill is adequately represented by the relatively high billing rate of $650/hour on the vast majority of the work performed.  Thus, the sole remaining issue is whether a multiplier is appropriate because the fee was contingent.

Defendant argues a multiplier is not necessary despite the contingent fee because (a) Plaintiff has already paid $207,623.50 of the bill, meaning the fees were not entirely contingent, (b) a favorable judgment was virtually certain where Plaintiff was elderly and disabled, and (c) collection has never been at issue because Defendant is wealthy. 

 

Notably, in Sonoma v. Thompson (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 978, the Court of Appeals held the fact that a fee is only partially contingent does not preclude awarding a multiplier.  Thus, the fact that about half of the fees at issue have already been paid does not preclude a multiplier.  Notably, however, the multiplier in that case was 1.4, not the 2.2 sought in this case. 

 

The Court finds Defendant’s argument that a favorable judgment was virtually certain to be of questionable good faith in light of Defendant’s vigorous defense of the case.  Additionally, with respect to collection, Plaintiff provides evidence in the reply that Defendant has threatened bankruptcy, such that collection has never been certain.  Plaintiff also correctly notes that much of Defendant’s wealth is in real estate, which will make collection more difficult. 

 

On balance, the Court is inclined to award a modest fee multiplier.  Again, the Court will not award a multiplier based on the skill of the attorneys working on the case, as that skill is reflected in the billing rate itself.  The Court will award a 1.3 multiplier.  The Court thereafter awards a total of $633,323.60 (the agreed-upon lodestar of $487,172 x 1.3).

  1. Demurrer to Complaint (Thunder Studios, Inc.)

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

The Court continued the hearing to require the parties to meet and confer.  The details of the meet and confer attempts will be discussed below, to the extent they are relevant to this ruling.  The Court finds the parties satisfied the meet and confer requirement, and will rule on the demurrers on their merits at this time.

c.     First Cause of Action, Breach of Contract

Defendant demurs to the breach of contract cause of action, contending (a) Defendant cannot meaningfully ascertain the terms of any purported contract running between the parties, (b) Defendant cannot ascertain whether any such contract is written, oral, or implied in conduct, (c) any alleged contract violates the statute of frauds, and (d) any agreement set forth in the complaint is merely an “agreement to agree” and not an actual agreement.

 

The parties, during meet and confer, agreed that Plaintiffs could clarify the terms of the contract(s), whether there are multiple contracts, how they were breached, etc.  Notably, Defendant expressly indicates that it does not agree the claims could be properly pled, but concedes Plaintiffs could attempt to do so.  The Court, therefore, will focus on the unresolved issues, which are whether the agreements are barred by the statute of frauds and whether they set forth nothing more than an “agreement to agree.”

 

i.              Statute of Frauds

Defendant’s first argument is that the parties’ agreement is barred by the statute of frauds.  Defendant notes that the complaint alleges the term of the MOU and NDA is alleged to have been two years from the commencement date, with a one-year option.  Specifically, at ¶10 the complaint alleges the parties entered into, “orally and by conduct with the written terms drafted and accepted by Thunder and Mr. David partly as follows”; Plaintiff then goes on to allege that the parties’ agreement had a two-year term with a one-year renewal option. 

 

Pursuant to Civil Code §1624(a)(1), an agreement that, by its terms, cannot be performed within a year is invalid unless the contract is in writing and subscribed by the party to be charged or the party’s agent. 

Plaintiffs argue (a) the contract could have been performed within one year, and (b) the agreement was executed, such that the statute of frauds does not apply.

 

Plaintiffs’ first argument is that, pursuant to Foley v. Interactive Data Corp. (1988) 47 Cal.3d 653, 673, the parties’ agreement could have been performed within one year, and therefore the statute of frauds is not implicated.  Specifically, Plaintiffs contend either party could have acted in a manner that caused the contract to terminate during the course of its first year, which is sufficient to overcome the statute of frauds bar.  In Foley, the Court considered whether an employment contract for a term in excess of a year that only permits termination of the contract in the event of “good cause” is outside the statute of frauds because the employee could be discharged for cause, the employee could retire, die, or voluntarily leave employment, or there could be general layoffs that affect the employee. 

 

Defendant argues Foley is not on point because the parties’ relationship in this case was not an employment relationship.  Plaintiffs allege, with respect to termination of the contract, “Either Party may terminate the Relationship and this MOU for cause upon thirty (30) days’ notice of breach to the other party.  During such notice period, the party alleged o be in breach will have the opportunity to cure such breach.” 

 

Defendant fails to cite any case contrary to Foley.  The Court finds the analysis of Foley, which is a CA Supreme Court case, to be controlling.  Plaintiffs allege the express terms of the parties’ agreement permit either of them to declare a breach and terminate the agreement, which could occur within the first year of the contract’s term.  The demurrer on the ground that the oral contract is barred by the statute of frauds is therefore overruled.  The Court declines to consider the parole evidence argument, as doing so is not necessary to a resolution of the merits of the pleading challenge.

 

ii.             Agreement to Agree

Defendant’s next argument is that Plaintiffs have pled nothing more than an unenforceable “agreement to agree.”  Defendant notes that ¶10 of the complaint, which alleges the terms of the parties’ MOU, includes, under the “SCOPE” section, a statement that “Following the acceptance and commencement of this MOU, the parties intend to negotiate a deeper integration of the relationship via an Asset Purchase Agreement (APA), Employment Agreement for Irfan Merchant and a SAFE (Simple Agreement for Equity) that will require the parties to enter into a formal Agreement (the “Agreement”) encompassing these extra components.  It is intended that DPS will sell its assets to Thunder for a to be agreed valuation sum via an APA for equity in Thunder via a SAFE connected to a long-term employment contract between Thunder and Irfan Merchant.”

 

At most, this clause would bar Plaintiffs from alleging any breach of any APA, Employment Agreement, or SAFE agreement, but it would not bar Plaintiffs from alleging a breach of the terms of the MOU itself.  As noted above, Plaintiffs will be amending their complaint to clarify whether there are multiple agreements alleged to have been breached, and to set forth each purported agreement in a separate cause of action for clarity.  The Court will defer ruling on this issue until Plaintiffs file their amended complaint.  The Court asks Plaintiffs to clearly plead, in the amended complaint, the existence of each contract allegedly running between the parties, the terms of such contract, and how such contract was breached.

  1. Second Cause of Action, Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, Fourth Cause of Action, Concealment, and Tenth, Eleventh, and Twelfth Causes of Action, Breach of Fiduciary Duty

  1. Seventh Cause of Action, Conversion

Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action is pled against Thunder and David.  Notably, Thunder did NOT demur to the seventh cause of action.  This cause of action alleges Defendants converted Plaintiff’s property by taking possession of his property, failing to permit him to access his property, and refusing to return his property upon demand. 

 

Defendant argues the cause of action is subject to demurrer because Plaintiff’s complaint admits “Thunder” possesses his property, not David.  Additionally, Defendant argues the cause of action is preempted by the CUTSA (California Uniform Trade Secrets Act). 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition, argues he alleges David directed Thunder to convert his property, and therefore David is liable for the conversion personally.  Plaintiff argues there is no preemption of the claim. 

 

The first issue is whether David can be held liable for conversion if he is not personally alleged to have taken the property or to currently possess the property (and where, as here, the entity defendant, Thunder, is expressly alleged to have possession of the property).  Neither party cites authority directly on point.  Plaintiffs rely on general authority holding that principals are liable for their own torts committed on behalf of their entities.  Defendant relies on the general law re: conversion holding that someone is liable for conversion if and only if the person wrongfully takes and retains the subject property.  The Court will not further research this issue in light of the parties’ failure to brief the specific issue – whether a principal can be liable for conversion if the principal does not personally take or retain the goods.  If Plaintiffs re-plead this claim against David, and if David challenges it, the parties must meet and confer and brief this specific issue in connection with any pleading challenge.

 

The bigger issue is whether the conversion claim is preempted by the CUTSA.  David argues the property Plaintiffs allege was converted is the same as the property that is the subject of Plaintiffs’ misappropriation of trade secrets cause of action, and therefore David concludes the claim is preempted by CUTSA.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, concede their claims relating to intangible property are likely preempted and offer to remove those allegations.  Defendant, in reply, focuses only on the intangible property claims in this regard.  Plaintiffs must amend their complaint accordingly. 

 

  1. False Promise

The demurrer to the false promise cause of action is sustained with leave to amend for the reasons set forth above in connection with Thunder’s demurrer.  To the extent David also demurs on the ground that he is not personally liable for fraud because any misrepresentation he made was on behalf of Thunder, his argument fails.  As set forth in the opposition papers, an individual defendant is personally liable for his own tortious conduct, even if that conduct is done on behalf of the principal.  See Frances T. v. Village Green Owners Assn (1986) 42 Cal.3d 490, 505.  Note that this cause of action is distinct from the conversion cause of action, because Plaintiffs allege David personally committed all elements of the tort.  Thus, the demurrer on this additional ground is overruled.

 

  1. Thirteenth Cause of Action, Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

Plaintiffs’ thirteenth cause of action is pled against Thunder and David.  Thunder does not demur to this cause of action.  David demurs, arguing Plaintiffs have not pled with sufficient specificity what it is that Defendants took that is subject to trade secret protection.  David relies on Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 244, 250, which held that the complaint must allege the existence of subject matter capable of protection as a trade secret, and must allege specific facts so showing. 

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argues Diodes is no longer good law, and instead the specifics of the claim can be determined through discovery.  Plaintiffs argue CCP §2019.210, which was enacted in 1984, supercedes Diodes.  Plaintiffs cite Advanced Modular Sputtering, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 826, 833-834 in support of their position.  Even the portion of Advanced Modular that Plaintiffs cite, however, supports Defendant’s position, not Plaintiffs’ position.  The Court therein held that §2019.210 “codifies Diodes,” and that it requires the complainant to describe the subject matter of the trade secret with particularity before commencing discovery.  Plaintiffs appear to take the position that there is now some sort of pre-discovery identification of the specifics of the trade secrets that should occur.  A reasonable reading of the statute and case law, however, is that the plaintiff remains obligated to specify the trade secret having been misappropriated at the pleading stage, as doing so is a necessary prerequisite to the right to litigate and conduct discovery. 

 

The demurrer is therefore sustained with leave to amend.

While David demurs to the eighth cause of action, it is only mentioned in the introduction and the conclusion of the points and authorities.  David makes no argument in his points and authorities showing the cause of action is deficiently pled.  The demurrer is therefore overruled.

Case Number: 23LBCV00974    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jesus and Diana Renteria filed this action against Defendants, Pinner Construction Co., Inc.., et al. on 6/06/23.  Plaintiffs’ operative complaint is their First Amended Complaint, which they filed before any pleading challenge was heard, on 6/09/23.  The FAC includes causes of action for general negligence, premises liability, and loss of consortium.  The FAC alleges the defendants, and each of them, provided negligent drawings, plans, specifications, safety instructions, and designs for Plaintiff to use in constructing a building.  As a result, the building Plaintiff was working on collapsed, causing him to sustain grave injuries.  His wife sues for loss of consortium. 

 

Plaintiffs have added numerous doe defendants to the action, including CH2M Hill Engineers, Inc., which it named as Doe One on 9/07/23.  Many defendants have pending cross-complaints against the other defendants. 

 

2.     Demurrer (City of Long Beach)

  1. Legal Standard

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares Counsel met and conferred extensively, but were unable to come to agreement concerning the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The demurrer will therefore be heard on its merits. 

 

  1. Scope of Demurrer

The City demurs to the first cause of action for general negligence only.  It does not demur to the second cause of action for premises liability, which is pled on a dangerous condition of public property theory, or the third cause of action for loss of consortium. 

 

  1. Law Governing Liability Against a Public Entity

Gov Code §815 makes clear that all causes of action against a public entity must be grounded in statute.  Additionally, claims against public entities must be pleaded with specificity.  Ramsey v. City of Lake Elsinore (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 1530, 1541-42. 

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

The City argues Plaintiff has not pled which statute he bases his claim for liability on.  Plaintiff argues he has adequately set forth a claim pursuant to Gov Code §815.2.  The City, in reply, argues Plaintiff’s opposition fails to show how his FAC can survive demurrer, and the demurrer should be sustained. 

 

  1. Gove Code §815.2

Gov Code §815.2 provides:

(a) A public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee or his personal representative.

(b) Except as otherwise provided by statute, a public entity is not liable for an injury resulting from an act or omission of an employee of the public entity where the employee is immune from liability. 

 

Plaintiff argues, in opposition to the demurrer, that he does not have to identify the specific employee whose negligence caused his injuries in order to allege vicarious liability pursuant to §815.2.  Plaintiff cites to C.A. v. William S. Hat Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872 to support this position. 

 

Defendant, in reply, contends Plaintiff’s argument fails for two reasons.  First, Defendant contends the complaint does not identify Gov Code §815.2 as the statutory basis for the claims against the City, and therefore does not satisfy the requirements of §815 and Ramsey.  The Court tends to agree.  The FAC does not make clear that §815.2 forms the basis of the claim.  See also Searcy v. Hemet Unified School District (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 802, holding that the statute that forms the basis of liability must be pled in the operative complaint.

 

Second, Defendant argues Plaintiff does not set forth any facts in the FAC making clear that he is pleading his claim based on vicarious liability.  In C.A., the plaintiff clearly alleged that the school district was vicariously liable for its employees’ negligent hiring, training, supervision, and/or retention of an unfit teacher.  Defendant argues no such facts are present here, and the FAC appears to be pled as one for direct, rather than vicarious liability.  Defendant focuses on the fact that the FAC alleges that “Defendants” engaged in the subject acts, not that any public employee engaged in the acts.  Defendant is, again, technically correct.  Plaintiff has alleged that “Defendants” engaged in the acts.  While it is clear the entity defendants could have only acted through their employees, because of the requirement that the complaint be pleaded with specificity, the Court will require Plaintiff to amend the FAC to clarify the allegations.

 

  1. Conclusion

The City’s demurrer to the first cause of action for general negligence in the FAC is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff must file a Second Amended Complaint clarifying his allegations against the City and their statutory basis within twenty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.

 

3.     Demurrer (CH2M Hill Engineers, Inc.)

a.     Initial Note

This demurrer was filed by Defendants, CH2M Hill Engineers, Inc. and Jacobs Solutions, Inc.  On 9/07/23, Plaintiffs dismissed Jacobs as a defendant in the action, and therefore the demurrer remains pending as to CH2M only.

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares Counsel met and conferred prior to the filing of the demurrer, but were unable to resolve the dispute.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for determination at this time.

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

CH2M demurs to the FAC, contending it lacks any specific facts to show it, as opposed to the other myriad defendants, is liable for Plaintiff’s injuries.  Plaintiff opposes the demurrer, arguing the FAC is sufficient to meet the relaxed pleading standard in a negligence action, and also arguing Defendant has failed to respond to discovery, which would be necessary if Defendant wanted Plaintiff to plead with more specificity. 

 

d.     Law Governing Pleading Negligence Against a Private Entity Defendant

Pursuant to Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527, a plaintiff can plead negligence against a defendant in general terms.  While the plaintiff cannot simply allege “the defendant’s negligence caused the plaintiff injury,” substantial detail is not required.  Id.  In this case, Plaintiff alleges the various defendants negligently provided the subject drawings, plans, specifications, safety instructions, and designs (negligence cause of action) and owned, maintained, managed, and/or operated the subject premises (premises liability cause of action).  At the pleading stage, this is sufficient.  The parties can resolve issues relating to the specifics of the claims through discovery.  The demurrer is overruled. 

 

4.     Conclusion

The City’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  CH2M’s demurrer is overruled.  Plaintiff must file a Second Amended Complaint within twenty days.  Any defendant who wishes to file a responsive pleading to the SAC may do so within the statutory time thereafter.  Any defendant who answered the FAC but does not timely respond to the SAC will have its answer to the FAC deemed its answer to the SAC.   

 

5.     OSC and CMC

The parties are reminded that there is an OSC re: proof of service and Case Management Conference on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the above demurrers.  The Court asks Counsel to make arrangements to appear remotely at the OSC, CMC, and hearing on the demurrers.
