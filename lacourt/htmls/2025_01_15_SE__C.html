DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22NWCV00740    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: C

PEREZ-MORALES V. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00740

HEARING: 01/15/2025

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.             Plaintiff Jose Perez-Morales’s motion to compel deposition attendance of Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc’s person most qualified and custodian of records is GRANTED.

 

II.            Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Plaintiff Jose Perez-Morales (Plaintiff) moves for an order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Defendant) to produce a Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records (PMQ) pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2025.450 and 2025.230.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed this lemon law action on August 23, 2022. On September 18, 2019, Plaintiff purchased a new 2019 Honda Odyssey, VIN: 5FNRL6H51KB130309 (Subject Vehicle). (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges that the vehicle had electrical and transmission defects. (Complaint, ¶ 24.) The complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Act – breach of express warranty, (2) violation of the Song-Beverly Act – breach of implied warranty, and (3) fraudulent inducement – concealment.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450 subd. (a), a party may move to compel a deponent’s attendance and production for inspection of documents if after serving the deposition notice the responding party fails to serve valid objections and fails to appear for the deposition or produce any document described in the deposition notice. A motion under Code of Civil Procedure section¿2025.450 subd. (a) must (1) provide specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document and (2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450 subd. (b).)

 

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450 subd. (g)(1).)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040, “[a] meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” “[W]hen the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (b)(2).)

 

Here, the parties have not adequately met and conferred. For each notice of deposition Plaintiff served, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding Defendant’s attendance and objections. (Tran Decl., ¶¶ 7, 12.) On both occasions, Defendant’s counsel responded that it would follow up with alternative deposition dates. (Dixon Decl., ¶¶ 10, 15.) While the parties exchanged emails regarding scheduling a deposition date, these efforts do not demonstrate a good faith resolution of the issues.

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order compelling Defendant to produce a PMQ on the grounds that Plaintiff has properly served and noticed two depositions of Defendant’s PMQ. In opposition, Defendant argues it properly and timely opposed both notices of deposition.

 

On November 20, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with a notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMQ for a deposition on December 11, 2023. (Tran Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) The notice of deposition identified 53 matters for examination and 53 requests for document production related to the Subject Vehicle, repairs, warranty claims, and information related to the alleged defective torque converter lock-up clutch and transmission. (Tran Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff requested Defendant provide alternative dates by November 27, 2023. (Tran Decl., ¶ 4.) On December 1, 2023, Defendant served a response stating it would not produce a PMQ for the deposition and did not provide alternative dates. (Tran Decl., ¶ 6.) On the noticed deposition date, December 11, 2023, Plaintiff sent Defendant a video link to enable Defendant’s PMQ to participate in the deposition. (Tran Decl., ¶ 8.) Defendant did not appear and did not provide alternative dates. (Tran Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff served an amended notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMQ noticed for May 6, 2024. (Tran Decl., ¶ 10.) The same sequence of events occurred. (Tran Decl., ¶¶ 10-15.) On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff requested additional dates and Defendant proposed February 7, 2025. (Tran Decl., ¶ 16.) Plaintiff responded inquiring whether Defendant would respond to all matters for examination. (Tran Decl., ¶ 17.) Defendant did not respond, and to date, no deposition date has been agreed on. (Tran Decl., ¶¶ 17-18.)

 

On November 5, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a cross-notice of the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ, advising Plaintiff of the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ on November 20, 2024 in a separate matter. (Dixon Decl., ¶ 22, Exh. R.) The matters for examination in that matter included transmission issues in 2011-2019 Honda Odyssey vehicles. (Dixon Decl., Exh. R.) Plaintiff objected to this cross-notice. (Dixon Decl., ¶ 23.)

 

Upon review of Defendant’s opposition, the Court notes that Defendant does not oppose producing its PMQ. Rather, Defendant argues that its PMQ has limited availability.

 

          Objections to Matters for Examination

 

Plaintiff’s separate statement identifies issues with matters for examinations, nos. 1-4, 6-54.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230 provides, in relevant part, that “[i]f the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

 

Defendant objects to these requests on the basis they are vague, ambiguous, broad, unduly burdensome, harassing, argumentative, seek information protected by attorney-client privilege, and seek confidential information. (Separate Statement.)

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230, Plaintiff’s matters for examination are intended to guide Defendant in selecting a PMQ for deposition who is knowledgeable on the matters. The Court finds that Plaintiff’s matters for examination are reasonably particularized. Defendant does not provide objections as to any error or irregularity in the deposition notice. 

 

Requests for Production

 

Defendant objects to the requests for production on the basis they are vague, ambiguous, broad, unduly burdensome, harassing, argumentative, seek information protected by attorney-client privilege, and seek confidential information. (Separate Statement.)

 

A request for documents in conjunction with a deposition notice is not the equivalent of a request for production of documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010 et seq. A request for production of documents in a deposition notice is intended to specify with reasonable particularity the materials or category of materials to be produced by the Defendant. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.220 subd. (a)(4).) “Objections to the competency of the deponent, or to the relevancy, materiality, or admissibility at trial of the testimony or of the materials produced are unnecessary and are not waived by failure to make them before or during the deposition.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.460 subd. (c).)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s request for production are reasonably particularized. Defendant does not provide objections as to any error or irregularity in the deposition notice. 

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel Defendant’s PMQ is GRANTED. Defendant’s PMQ is ORDERED to appear for a deposition and produce the documents contained within the notice of deposition, without objection. Defendant has waived its objections to the document requests because it failed to object to them pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.410. The parties shall meet and confer to determine the date, time, and location of the depositions, which shall take place no later than 45 days from the date of the Court’s issuance of this Order. The date may be extended by agreement of the parties.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff’s counsel requests the Court order monetary sanctions against Defendant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subd. (g)(1), “the court shall impose a monetary sanction… in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Here, the Court finds that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. Defendant proposed an alternative date, which Plaintiff refused, and served a cross-notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMQ. Additionally, the parties have failed to adequately meet and confer. Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

 


Case Number: 22NWCV00913    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: C

LOPEZ V. SUBWAY RESTAURANTS, LLC, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00913 

HEARING: 01-15-2025

 

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Subway Restaurants, LLC’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Defendant Subway Restaurants, LLC (Defendant or SRL) moves the Court for an order granting summary judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c.

 

Background

 

This product liability action was filed by Plaintiff Karina Lopez (Plaintiff) on September 29, 2022. On October 31, 2023, a first amended complaint (FAC) was filed. On August 12, 2024, a second amended complaint (SAC) was filed.

 

The SAC alleges that on May 31, 2021, Plaintiff was dining at Subway Restaurant, located at 4070 Tweedy Boulevard, Suite A, South Gate, CA 90280 (Subject Premises). Plaintiff alleges that when she went to sit on a chair, it collapsed and she fell onto the floor. (SAC ¶ 11.) As a result, Plaintiff sustained injuries. (SAC ¶ 11.) Defendant Foldcraft Co. manufactured the chair. The SAC asserts causes of action for (1) strict products liability; (2) breach of express and implied warranties; (3) negligence based on defective product; (4) negligence and premises liability; and (5) negligence based on violation of OSHA Regulations. Specifically, against SRL, the SAC alleges the first cause of action for strict liability - count three failure to warn, and the fourth cause of action for negligence and premises liability.

 

SRL filed the instant motion for summary judgment on April 9, 2024. On August 8, 2024, the Court continued this motion because it found good cause to conduct essential discovery relevant to the opposition.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendants object to Paragraphs 1 through 29 in Plaintiff’s undisputed material facts.

          Paragraphs 1 through 15: overruled.

          Paragraph 16 through 29: overruled.

         

Plaintiff objects to portions of the Declaration of Kristine Zaniewski.

         

Page 2, Paragraph 3, Lines 3-4: overruled.

Page 2, Paragraph 5, Lines 9-11: overruled.

Page 2, Paragraph 6, Lines 12-14: overruled.

Page 2, Paragraph 7, Lines 15-16: overruled.

Page 2, Paragraph 8, Lines 17-19: overruled.

 

On November 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a supplemental opposition to SRL’s motion for summary judgment in which she argues that Kristine Zaniewski does not have personal knowledge because she does not work for SRL, but rather for Defendant Franchise World Headquarters. However, there is no requirement that an individual must be a direct employee to have personal knowledge regarding a subject matter.

 

Undisputed Material Facts

 

On September 22, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against SRL seeking compensatory damages for her injuries sustained when her chair collapsed while dining at the Subject Premises. (UMF, ¶ 1.) On September 26, 2023, the Court granted SRL’s demurrer dismissing four causes of action against SRL. (UMF, ¶ 2.) The FAC alleged a single cause of action against SRL, Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action. (UMF, ¶ 3.)

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subd. (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

 

In moving for summary judgment, a defendant has the “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (p)(2).)

 

Once a moving party has satisfied the initial burden of proof, the burden “shifts to the opposing party to show, by responsive separate statement and admissible evidence, that triable issues of fact exist.” (Ostayan v. Serrano Reconveyance Co. (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1411, 1418, disapproved on other grounds by Black Sky Cap., LLC v. Cobb (2019) 7 Cal.5th 156, 165. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

An issue of damages must dispose of an entire cause of action. (DeCastro West Chodorow & Burns, Inc. v. Superior Court (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 410, 422.) Summary adjudication as to punitive damages is proper only if a reasonable jury could not find clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression. (Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 762.)

 

Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

Discussion

 

SRL moves for summary judgment on the grounds that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that it is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.

 

SRL argues Plaintiff’s negligence and premises liability cause of action against Defendant must fail because (1) SRL did not own, operate, maintain, or control the Subject Premises and (2) SRL did not owe a duty to Plaintiff.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.)

 

SRL argues that Plaintiff cannot prove the duty element of negligence. SRL asserts it did not owe a duty to Plaintiff to maintain or repair the chair in the Subject Premises because it did not own, lease, occupy, or control the Subject Premises. SRL references “Exhibit D, Declaration of Anne Jasorkowski” in support of its arguments. However, the Court does not find any Declaration of Anne Jasorkowski.

 

Here, because Defendant only relies on this Declaration of Anne Jasorkowski and the pleadings as evidence, Defendant has not met its evidentiary burden under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subd. (p)(2). The burden does not shift to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact does exist.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

HEARING: 01/15/2025

 

#8

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff U.S. Bank National Association’s motion to vacate default judgment and dismiss case without prejudice is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Plaintiff U.S. Bank National Association (Plaintiff) moves this Court for an order vacating the default judgment entered in Plaintiff’s favor on September 13, 2023.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed this complaint for replevin against Defendant Jose Ontiveros (Defendant) on January 11, 2023. This action concerns a written retail installment sale contract (Contract) entered into between the parties for the purchase of a 2018 Jeep Cheroke, VIN No. 1C4RJFN95JC399616 (Subject Vehicle). (Complaint, ¶ 5.) Plaintiff alleged Defendant defaulted on the Contract by failing and refusing to make payments as they became due. (Complaint, ¶ 6.)

 

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff requested the Court enter default against Defendant. On September 13, 2023, judgment was entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant, entitling Plaintiff to immediate possession of the Subject Vehicle.

 

Legal Standard

 

Discretionary and mandatory relief is available to parties or legal representatives from “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473 subd. (b).) “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any…resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves this Court for an order vacating the default judgment entered in Plaintiff’s favor on September 13, 2023.

 

Plaintiff states that “[a]fter the aforementioned default judgment was granted, Plaintiff became aware that the contract was fraudulently brought about by a third party unknown to Defendant.” (Motion.) However, Plaintiff fails to include any grounds for the motion or notice of the motion. Written notice is required for motions to set aside default pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473.5 and 585.5. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1005.) Additionally, under Code of Civil Procedure section 663a, “[a] party intending to make a motion to set aside and vacate a judgment… shall file with the clerk and serve upon the adverse party a notice of his or her intention, designating the grounds upon which the motion will be made, and specifying the particulars in which the legal basis for the decision is not consistent with or supported by the facts.” Here, without a legal basis for the motion and without notice of the motion, the Court cannot find in favor of Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to vacate default judgment and dismiss case without prejudice is DENIED without prejudice.

Case Number: 24NWCV00630    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: C

BARBARINI-COBIAN, ET AL. v. GARCIA, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00630

HEARING: 01/15/2025

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Marelin Rodriguez’s unopposed demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Defendant Marelin Rodriquez (Rodriguez) demurs to the second cause of action in Plaintiffs Jose Ricardo Barbarini-Cobian and Brenda Osornio’s (Plaintiffs) complaint on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Background

 

On February 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this action arising out of a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs allege two causes of action: (1) negligence against Defendants Andrew Darren Garcia (Garcia) and Stacy Quoshena (Quoshena) and (2) declaratory relief against Rodriguez. Plaintiffs allege that on February 14, 2023, Defendants Garcia and Quoshena, owners and drivers of a black 2021 Nissan Rogue, collided into Plaintiffs’ vehicle on Alondra Blvd, Bellflower, California. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs allege Rodriquez was a passenger in Garcia and Quoshena’s vehicle. (Complaint, ¶ 15.) Plaintiffs sustained injuries, severe fright, shock, and pain as a result of the collision. (Complaint, ¶ 8.)

 

On April 3, 2024, Rodriguez filed a cross-complaint for negligence against Jorge Barbarini, Cindy Cruz, Andrew Darren Garcia, and Stacy Quoshena.

 

Meet and Confer

 

 “Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41 subd. (a).)

 

Rodriguez’s counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel on March 20, 2024, her intentions to demur to the complaint and legal basis for doing so. (Hoonanian Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Hoonanian Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, at p. 747.) A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50.) 

 

“Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)

 

Discussion

 

Rodriguez demurs to the cause of action for declaratory relief asserted against her on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.) A cause of action for declaratory relief should not be used as a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues raised in another cause of action. (General of America Insurance Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.) “The availability of another form of relief that is adequate will usually justify refusal to grant declaratory relief.” (California Insurance Guarantee Association v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624.)

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for declaratory relief fails to allege a controversy relating to Plaintiffs’ rights or obligations. The complaint alleges Rodriquez was a passenger in the vehicle driven by Garcia and Quoshena. (Complaint, ¶ 15.) The complaint alleges that Rodriguez asserts a claim for damages against Garcia and Quoshena and that a controversy has arisen between Plaintiffs and Rodriguez over the monetary recovery from Garcia and Quoshena. (Complaint, ¶¶ 16-17.) However, as Rodriguez argues, Plaintiffs do not provide any facts demonstrating a controversy between Plaintiffs and Rodriguez over the monetary recovery. Additionally, there are no separate allegations that Rodriguez caused Plaintiffs’ injuries.

 

Moreover, Plaintiffs request the Court determine Plaintiffs’ “damages based on their injuries and incurred medical expenses as a result of this incident.” (Complaint, ¶ 15.) This request for declaratory relief seeks the same evaluation of damages as the negligence cause of action against Garcia and Quoshena. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ second cause of action does not allege an independent basis to sustain a cause of action for declaratory relief against Rodriguez.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Marelin Rodriguez’s unopposed demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days LEAVE TO AMEND.

Plaintiff alleges four causes of action: (1) forgery of handwriting, (2) forgery by passing or attempting to use forged documents, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff states that in the previous action, Defendants attached a copy of an arbitration contract with Plaintiff’s name on it to file a demand for arbitration. (FAC, ¶ 68.) Plaintiff alleges that she did not enter into an arbitration agreement, and that Defendants’ forged her name on to the document. (FAC, ¶ 152.)

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 permits the court to strike causes of action arising from an act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of free speech or petition unless the plaintiff establishes that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.

In assessing a defendant’s Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 special motion to strike (Anti-Slaap Motion), the Court must engage in a two-step process. (Shekhter v. Financial Indem. Co. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.) First, the court must decide whether the defendant has met the threshold burden of showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s constitutional rights of free speech or petition for redress of grievances. (Id.) This burden may be met by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff’s cause of action was an act that falls within one of the four categories of conduct set forth in section 425.16(e).

Once a defendant has met its initial burden and established that the anti-SLAPP statute applies, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate a “probability” of success on the merits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b); Equilon Enters. LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.) “[T]he plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.” (Matson v. Dvorak (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 539, 548 (internal quotations omitted).) At this “second stage of an anti-SLAPP hearing, the court may consider affidavits, declarations, and their equivalents if it is reasonably possible the proffered evidence set out in those statements will be admissible at trial. Conversely, if the evidence relied upon cannot be admitted at trial, because it is categorically barred or undisputed factual circumstances show inadmissibility, the court may not consider it in the face of an objection. If an evidentiary objection is made, the plaintiff may attempt to cure the asserted defect or demonstrate the defect is curable.” (Sweetwater Union High School Dist. v. Gilbane Building Co. (2019) 6 Cal.5th 931, 949.)

The trial court properly considers the evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant, but it may not weigh the credibility or comparative strength of the evidence and must instead simply determine whether the plaintiff's evidence would, if believed by the trier of fact, be sufficient to result in a judgment for plaintiff. (McGarry v. Univ. of San Diego (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 97, 108-09.) The Court “accepts as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff and evaluates the defendant’s evidence only to determine if it has defeated that submitted by the plaintiff as a matter of law.” (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 326.) Further, whether or not the evidence is in conflict in the context of a motion to strike under the Anti-SLAPP statute, if the plaintiff has presented a sufficient pleading and has presented evidence showing that a prima facie case will be established at trial, the plaintiff is entitled to proceed. (Moore v. Shaw (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 182, 193.) Only a minimal showing of merit is required. (Robinzine v. Vicory (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1416, 1421.)

Discussion

First Prong: Protected Activity

Defendants argue that the entire FAC relies on a pending lawsuit which has been ordered into arbitration. Therefore, Defendants argue the FAC’s causes of actions arise out of Defendants’ protected activities in demanding arbitration.

A moving party can satisfy its burden by showing: (1) statements were made before legislative, executive, or judicial proceedings, or made in connection with matters being considered in such proceedings; or (2) statements were made in a public forum, or other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional rights of petition or free speech, in connection with issues of public interest. (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 subd. (e); Equillon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66.)

Here, Defendants argue that the causes of action are protected because they arise out of statements made in a judicial proceeding. The act of initiating a private arbitration is not a protected activity under the Anti-SLAPP. (Century 21 Chamberlain & Associates v. Haberman (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1, 8-9, (“Arbitration claims ... are not filed in courts and they do not initiate judicial proceedings.”).) However, here, the activity at issue is Defendants’ demand for arbitration pursuant to a Court’s order.

In the parties’ proceedings in the Superior Court of Orange County, on August 1, 2022, the Court initially denied Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration without prejudice. (RJN, Exh. 2.) However, on April 4, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ renewed motion to compel arbitration. (RJN, Exh. 3, Minute Order, 4/4/2023.) Plaintiff then filed a peremptory challenge, which was denied as untimely, and then filed various appeals of the order denying the peremptory peremptory challenge. (RJN, Exh. 6-8.) Plaintiff additionally filed a motion to vacate a void order, which was denied. (RJN, Exh. 9.)

Plaintiff’s first cause of action is for “forgery of handwriting – violation of Cal. Penal Code § 470(b). Plaintiff alleges Defendants forged her handwriting on the arbitration contract. (FAC, ¶ 152.) The second cause of action is for “forgery by passing or attempting to use forged document” and alleges Defendants used the arbitration contract to defraud Plaintiff of her right to a trial. (FAC, ¶ 159.)

Plaintiff’s third cause of action is for intentional infliction of emotional distress and alleges Defendants’ acts in committing forgery caused her emotional distress. (FAC, ¶ 167.) Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is for negligent infliction of emotion distress and alleges the same. (FAC, ¶ 181.)

A review of Plaintiff’s allegations demonstrate that Plaintiff filed the instant lawsuit as a result of Defendants’ filing a demand for arbitration with the American

Arbitration Association (AAA) on August 23, 2024. Plaintiff states “[o]n or about August 23, 2024…Ms.Cooper was mentally, emotionally, and physically injured by Defendants when Ms. Cooper received a copy of Defendant’s Demand for Arbitration via email.” (FAC, ¶ 137.) Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff brings these causes of actions alleging forgery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligent infliction of emotional distress in response to Defendants’ demand for arbitration made pursuant to a Court order. Accordingly, Defendants have met their burden of demonstrating that the FAC arises from a protected activity in demanding arbitration pursuant to a Court order.

Second Prong: Probability of Prevailing

As Defendant has met the burden of demonstrating that at least portions of the first through fourth causes of action arise from protected activity, the burden now shifts to Plaintiff to establish a probability of succeeding on the merits.

The absolute litigation privilege bars a civil action for damages for communications made in any judicial proceeding. (Civ. Code § 47.) As indicated, the allegations in the FAC arise out of Defendants’ demand for arbitration pursuant to a Court order. Statements made during or in connection with a judicial or official proceeding are privileged, whether or not the statements were made with malice, or other bad motive. (Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205.) “The litigation privilege is also relevant to the second step in the anti-SLAPP analysis in that it may present a substantive defense a plaintiff must overcome to demonstrate a probability of prevailing.” (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 323.) Plaintiff is therefore barred from seeking civil damages arising out of Defendants’ protected actions in following a Court order.

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s FAC is entirely precluded by collateral estoppel because the allegations of forgery were addressed in the April 4, 2023, order by Judge Nathan Vu in the Orange County Superior Court. The Court notes that Plaintiff’s forgery allegations were already heard and ruled on by Judge Vu of the Orange County Superior Court. (RJN, Exh. 3.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that this Court lacks subject matter jurisdiction to rule on this motion because of Plaintiff’s appeal. Plaintiff filed a notice of appeal on October 9, 2024, seeking to appeal this Court’s order denying her ex parte application for a temporary restraining order and order to show cause re: preliminary injunction to prevent the arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims. (Minute Order, 10/8/2024.) On December 19, 2024, the Court of Appeals granted Defendants’ motion to dismiss. On December 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a petition for rehearing in the Court of Appeal, which the Court rejected. (Cooper Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff intends to file a motion to vacate order of dismissal and reinstate appeal. (Cooper Decl., ¶ 6.)

“An appeal from an order denying a preliminary injunction does not deprive the trial court of jurisdiction to proceed to try the case on the merits.” (MaJor v. Miraverde Homeowners Assn. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 618, 623.) “If the moving party desires that the trial be stayed pending appeal, the party may first ask the trial court in its discretion to continue or stay the underlying proceedings until the appeal is decided…If the trial court denies a request to stay the underlying proceedings, the moving party may request the appellate court to stay them.” (Reed v. Superior Court (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 448, 455, citing MaJor v. Miraverde Homeowners Assn., supra, 7 Cal.App.4th at 623.)

Here, Plaintiff’s notice of appeal of the ex parte application did not automatically stay proceedings in this Court. Further, as Defendants argue in reply, Plaintiff has not moved for a stay in the Court pending appeal. Therefore, this Court has subject matter jurisdiction to hear this matter.

Accordingly, Defendants’ special motion to strike the FAC pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s FAC is DISMISSED with PREJUDICE.

(Id.) “Any SLAPP defendant who brings a successful motion to strike is entitled to mandatory attorney fees. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131.)
