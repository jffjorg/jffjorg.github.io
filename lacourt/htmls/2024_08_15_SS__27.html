DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STVC40804    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Hearing Date: 8/15/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV40804 Karen Alfaro vs. California Department of General Services et al.

Moving Party: Defendant State of California

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED.

 

Background

On September 23, 2019, at approximately 2:00 p.m., Plaintiff filed the instant case concerning an incident involving Defendant Luther Lawson, who was driving behind Plaintiff. The parties suspected there was a rear-end collision. Lawson exited his vehicle, and as Plaintiff was about to exit hers, with one foot on the ground, Lawson allegedly closed the car door on Plaintiff’s legs, causing injuries. Lawson was operating a vehicle owned by the California Department of General Services at the time of the incident. Plaintiff then followed Lawson and observed him entering Yoshinoya, a fast- food restaurant, shortly after the incident.

Plaintiff brought two causes of action, intentional tort and general negligence, against Lawson and his employer, the State of California. Defendant State of California now moves for summary judgment, claiming that the incident occurred while Lawson was on his break and therefore was not within the scope of employment.

Legal Standard

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)¿ 

¿ 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)¿ 

¿ 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)¿ 

¿ 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)¿ 

¿ 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)¿ 

 

Going and Coming Rule

In order for a public entity to be liable for an act or omission of its employee, that act or omission must have been undertaken in the “course and scope” of employment. (Hoff v. Vacaville Unified School Dist. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 925, 932.) California Government Code section 815.2, subdivision (a) provides that “a public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee.” Plaintiff has the burden to demonstrate that the employee acted in the scope of his employment. (Ducey v. Argo Sales Co. (1979) 25 Cal.3d 707, 721.)

Generally, an employee is not acting within the scope and course of employment when commuting to or from the workplace at the beginning or end of a work day. (Sumrall v. Modern Alloys, Inc. (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 961, 967-968.) Thus, accidents caused during the employee's normal transit between home and work cannot support respondeat superior liability. (This is known as the  “going and coming” rule.) This principle is based on the theory that the employment relationship is suspended from the time employees leave their jobs until they return and that during normal commutes, employees are not rendering services directly or indirectly to their employers. (Pierson v. Helmerich & Payne Int'l Drilling Co. (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 608, 618.)  

The going and coming rule applies to conduct even when an employee leaves for an authorized break. (Tryer v. Ojai Valley Sch. (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 1476, 1483 [affirming grant of summary judgment where employee was involved in car accident after leaving work for lunch]; see also Caldwell v. A.R.B., Inc. (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 1028, 1039 [employee’s departure from work “that was merely taken at an earlier time in the day than [her] regular commute” was squarely within the going and coming rule].)

The Court in Sunderland v. Lockheed Martin Aero. Sys. Support (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1, explains that an employer is vicariously liable only if the employee committed the tortious act within the scope of his employment, and the act is within the scope of employment when “ ‘the risk of such an act is typical of or broadly incidental to the employer's enterprise’ ” or “ ‘inherent in, typical of or created by the work so that it is a foreseeable risk of the particular employment.’ ” The court continued: “If an employee's act is purely personal, it is not ‘typical of or broadly incidental to the employer's enterprise.’ [Citation.] If the main purpose of the injury-producing activity ‘was the pursuit of the employee's personal ends, the employer is not liable.’ [Citations.]” (Id. at p. 11.) The court concluded the employee's trip to a fast food restaurant was personal and not related to his employment or his employer. The purpose of the employee's trip to buy fast food “was a purely private and personal activity, ‘a substantial personal deviation from his employment duties' sufficient to make it unfair to hold [the employer] vicariously liable. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 12.) This activity did not create a risk typical of or broadly incidental to his employment. Consequently, the employee was not acting within the scope of his employment. The court affirmed summary judgment in favor of the employer.  (Ibid.) The going and coming rule equally applies when an employee is traveling to or from lunch, even in the employer's vehicle. ( Halliburton Energy Servs., Inc. v. Dep't of Transp. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 87, 100-1.)

Undisputed Facts

On September 23, 2020, at approximately 2:00 p.m., Plaintiff was driving on Exposition Boulevard in Los Angeles. Plaintiff attempted to exit her vehicle to see if the van, driven by Defendant Lawson, that stopped behind her had rear-ended her car. Lawson’s van did not hit Plaintiff’s car. However, because Plaintiff contended that Lawson closed her car door on her legs, Plaintiff phoned the police and began following Lawson. Plaintiff followed Lawson and advised police over the phone that Lawson entered a Yoshinoya restaurant after leaving the scene of the incident. Plaintiff never met Lawson before the incident. Plaintiff did not know where Lawson worked before the incident.

Discussion

Defendant has met its prima facie burden. It has provided undisputed evidence that Lawson was in the midst of an outing to a fast food restaurant at the time of the incident, albeit in a car owned by his employer.  The going and coming rule would thus apply.  (See Halliburton Energy Servs., Inc., supra.)

Although Plaintiff argues that Defendant did not meet its burden, Plaintiff did not present any argument on how Defendant failed to meet its initial burden. While Plaintiff cited various cases, it did not apply the law to the facts of this case. (Opp. at 7.)

Plaintiff's main argument is that there is a triable issue of fact regarding whether Lawson was acting within the scope of employment at the time of the incident. Plaintiff points to various excerpts from Lawson's deposition to support this claim. Lawson testified that on the date of the incident, his shift started at 9:00 a.m. and ended at 3:30 p.m. (Lawson Depo. 21:25-22:4). He drove to the DMV for air conditioning maintenance (Lawson Depo. 22:5-16) and testified that he has a morning break at 9:00 a.m., a lunch break at 11:00 a.m., and an afternoon break at 2:00 p.m. (Lawson Depo. 24:23-25:3). He finished his work at the DMV around 1:30 to 2:00 p.m. (Lawson Depo. 25:24), and the incident occurred around 2:00 p.m. after he completed his tasks (Lawson Depo. 24:6; 29:1-5). Lawson was on his way to get food during his afternoon break when the incident occurred. He stopped at Yoshinoya, ordered food, and intended to take his break there before returning to the office. He was at the restaurant for 15-20 minutes (Lawson Depo. 35:24-36:8) and indicated that it was the norm for him to go somewhere to grab something to eat during his second break in the afternoon (Lawson Depo. 70:7-15).

Evidence presented by Plaintiff indicates that the incident occurred while Lawson was en route to Yoshinoya to eat, contradicting Plaintiff's assertion that it happened within Lawson's scope of employment. Plaintiff suggests that the incident took place before Lawson's scheduled afternoon break; however, this is unsupported by evidence, as Lawson's break typically starts at 2:00 p.m., coinciding with the time of the incident. Significantly, Lawson had completed his tasks at the DMV and was traveling to Yoshinoya to take his break when the incident occurred. Therefore, the incident transpired while Lawson was engaged in and in pursuit of a personal activity.

Plaintiff contends that even during his paid afternoon break, Lawson is still on the clock and therefore within the scope of employment, yet Plaintiff presented no legal authority to support this contention. While the court recognizes differences between a lunch period and a mere break, particularly for hourly employees who must clock out for lunches but not for breaks, where the employee is on the clock during the break and off the clock during lunch; nevertheless, the employment nexus is similar in both the lunch and break contexts when the employee travels during these periods merely to obtain something to eat. (See Bailey v. Filco, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1552, 1566-67.) The key factor is not whether Lawson was on the clock, rather than whether the incident occurred in pursuit of a purely private and personal activity unrelated to Lawson’s work function.

Plaintiff further argues that Lawson was driving a government vehicle at the time of the incident, and that he was en route to return the vehicle to the office in Van Nuys while performing duties mandated by Defendant CDGS. However, the going and coming rule applies, even when the employee is driving a company car. (Halliburton Energy Servs., Inc., supra.) Further, if the primary purpose of the injury-producing activity was the pursuit of the employee’s personal ends, the employer is not liable. (Sunderland, supra, 130 Cal.App.4th at p. 11.) Lawson’s deposition makes it clear that he had finished work at the DMV and was headed to Yoshinoya to take his afternoon break and eat before returning to the office in Van Nuys. Lawson testified that the incident occurred while he was en route to Yoshinoya, intending to and indeed did take a personal break of 15-20 minutes there. The Court finds this excursion to be a full departure from Lawson’s employee duties, thereby taking his  conduct out of the scope of employment. Consequently, Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact, and thus, summary judgment is GRANTED.

MOTION FOR ORDER TO SEAL PRIVILEGED DOCUMENTS

Hearing Date: 8/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV37769 JUAN PABLO FERRUFINO MEJIA vs MICHELLE JO FINKELSTEIN. 

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendant Michelle Finkelstein

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO SEAL GRANTED 

 

Background

On July 28, 2020, a staff member in Plaintiff's counsel's office apparently included an email communication from Plaintiff to “Monica,” his case manager at PARRIS Law Firm, in information provided to Dr. Fishman, Plaintiff’s doctor.

Plaintiff remained unaware of this disclosure until June 18, 2024, when Dr. Fishman was deposed. During this deposition, defense counsel introduced this email as Exhibit 7, following Dr Fisherman’s production of Plaintiff’s entire medical file on June 17, 2024. In response, Plaintiff requested that defense counsel either return the privileged information or agree to seal Exhibit 7 and any related testimony. Plaintiff also sought a stipulation to prevent the disclosure of the letter's contents during the ongoing litigation and trial. Defendant refused these requests.

Plaintiff now moves the Court for the following orders (1) an order prohibiting Defendant and her attorneys from disclosing any privileged documents or communications; (2) an order compelling Defendant and her attorneys to either return, destroy, or redact all privileged documents and information in their possession; (3) an order to seal any court records containing privileged information; and (4) an order requiring Defendant to disclose the names and contact information of any individuals or entities who received copies of the privileged documents or information derived from such documents, along with the dates of such disclosures.

Legal Standard

Generally, attorney-client privilege “is waived with respect to a communication protected by the privilege if any holder of the privilege, without coercion, has disclosed a significant part of the communication or has consented to disclosure made by anyone.” Evid. Code § 912. Nonetheless, “‘waiver’ does not include accidental, inadvertent disclosure of privileged information by the attorney.” State Comp. Ins. Fund v. WPS, Inc., 70 Cal. App. 4th 644, 654 (1999); accord Newark Unified School Dist. v. Superior Court, 245 Cal. App. 4th 887, 900 (2015).  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.285(a) provides that “[i]f electronically stored information produced in discovery is subject to a claim of privilege or of protection as attorney work product, the party making the claim may notify any party that received the information of the claim and the basis for the claim.” After receiving such notice, “a party that received the information shall immediately sequester the information and either return the specified information and any copies that may exist or present the information to the court conditionally under seal for a determination of the claim.” Civ. Proc. Code. § 2031.285(b). At least one Court of Appeal decision has stated that “by enacting Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.285 in 2009, the Legislature codified the rule [that inadvertent disclosures do not constitute waivers of attorney-client privilege], at least as to electronically stored materials produced during discovery.” Newark Unified School Dist., 245 Cal. App. 4th at 900 (citations omitted). Thus, a party may claw back privileged electronically stored information inadvertently produced in discovery, and the opposing party may challenge that claim of privilege in a noticed motion.  

 

Discussion

Defendant contends that the email exchanges between Plaintiff and the case manager at his attorney's office do not qualify for attorney-client privilege. It is true that not all communications between a client and their attorney are privileged. (Los Angeles County Bd. of Supervisors v. Superior Court (2016) 2 Cal.5th 282, 296.) Nonetheless, the specifics of the communication, as described by both Plaintiff and Defendant, reveal that the nature of the communication appears to center on discussing case evidence, seeking to receive legal advice and instructing counsel on managing a specific aspect of the case. Further, these communications occurred after Plaintiff had retained counsel, indicating that they were made within the context of legal representation.

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s counsel may have intentionally disclosed the communication to Dr. Fisherman to influence his professional judgment. Though attorney-client privilege is only waivable by the privilege holder and is not negated by unauthorized disclosures by the client's agent (DP Pham, LLC v. Cheadle (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 653, 668), Defendant seems to suggest that that Plaintiff authorized his attorney to disclose the email to Dr. Fisherman in attempt to have Dr. Fisherman alter his medical report.  However, this suggestion appears to be entirely speculative.

In any event, Defendant here has not followed the protocol of CCP § 2031.285(b).  That section describes what Defendant should have done to use the contested information.  Defendant did not do that.  Accordingly, the Motion is granted in an effort to place the parties in the position intended by that CCP section.

¿ 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL MENTAL EXAMINATION

Hearing Date: 8/15//24

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV30916 HORACIO GALVAN vs CAESAR VALIENTE, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Valiente

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL MENTAL EXAMINATION IS CONTINUED

 

Legal Standard

¿ 

Except for defense physicals in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and exams arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing, and for “good cause shown.” (CCP §2032.320(a).)

¿ 

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action. (CCP §2032.020(a).) This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation. Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337. Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.” See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer. Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged.

¿ 

Where the plaintiff's injuries are complex, several exams may be necessary by specialists in different fields. There is no limit on the number of physical or mental exams that may be ordered on a showing of good cause. The good cause requirement checks any potential harassment of the plaintiff. See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.

 

The burden is on the moving party to show (by declarations or other evidence) that the examinee's condition is “in controversy” in the action. The moving party must also establish good cause for the examination(s) sought. A court order for physical or mental examination must be based on a showing of “good cause” (CCP § 2032.320(a)): (1) relevancy to the subject matter; and (2) specific facts justifying discovery: i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) The purpose is to protect an examinee's privacy by preventing annoying “fishing expeditions” - i.e., one party may not compel another to undergo psychiatric testing “solely on the basis of speculation that something of interest may surface.” (Id.)

 

The notice of motion must state the time, place, identity and specialty of the examiner, and the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination.” (CCP § 2032.310(b).) The requirement for specification of the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination” apparently requires disclosure of whatever diagnostic tests and procedures will be utilized (x-rays, blood and urine samples, etc.). (See CCP § 2032.220(c).) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2032.310(b).)¿¿ 

 

Discussion

 

On September 21, 2022, Plaintiff Horacio Galvan filed the present case, alleging he was attacked by Defendant Caesar Valiente, an off-duty police officer, while Plaintiff was trying to prevent Defendant's dog from attacking his dog. Plaintiff claims he was diagnosed with PTSD and severe mental distress as a result of the incident. On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff designated Amy Childers as a non-retained medical expert to opine on Plaintiff's mental condition and emotional distress. On July 16, 2024, Defendant requested Plaintiff’s mental examination. Subsequently, on July 17, 2024, Defendant moved the court to compel Plaintiff’s mental examination.

 

Defendant has demonstrated that Plaintiff's mental condition is in controversy and that there is good cause for the examination, as Plaintiff was diagnosed with PTSD and severe emotional distress as a result of the incident. Additionally, Plaintiff has recently designated an expert who will opine on his mental condition. Plaintiff does not dispute that Plaintiff's mental condition is in controversy or that there is good cause for the mental examination in his opposition.

 

Defendant opposes the present motion on several procedural grounds. First, Plaintiff argues that the notice fails to specify the identity of the psychologist who will conduct the examination, as it states, "The psychological evaluation will be performed by Dr. Rad and a testing associate." However, Dr. Rad filed a declaration clarifying that she "will personally conduct both the clinical interview of Plaintiff and the aforementioned battery of psychological and psychometric testing and will score and tabulate the data it produces by herself without the assistance of 'a testing associate.'" (Dr. Heleya Rad, PSY.D’s Declaration, ¶10). Thus, Defendant has identified Dr. Rad as the psychologist who will be conducting the examination, in compliance with CCP § 2032.320(d).

 

Second, Plaintiff argues that Defendant did not specify the diagnostic tests to be performed in accordance with CCP § 2032.320(d). Defendant’s demand states that all measures that will be administered to the Plaintiff will be drawn from the test list provided below: TEST LIST Beck Anxiety Inventory (BAI) Beck Depression Inventory-2 (BDI-2) Beck Hopelessness Scale (BHS) Clinician Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5 (CAPS-5) Detailed Assessment of Posttraumatic Stress (DAPS) Mini Mental Status Exam- Brief or Standard (MMSE-2-BV/SV) Minnesota Multiphasic Personality Inventory-3 (MMPI-3) Personality Assessment Inventory (PAI) PTSD Checklist for DSM-5 (PCL-5) Trauma Symptom Inventory-2 (TSI-2) Victoria (VSVT) Wide Range Achievement Test (WRAT-4) Ex. "7".

 

The nature of a mental examination often requires a certain degree of flexibility, as the examiner may find certain tests unnecessary or new ones pertinent as he/she conducts the interview and evaluations. At the time of the present motion, the psychologist is only aware that Plaintiff has been diagnosed with PTSD and severe emotional distress. Without a prior testing and interview, predicting the precise tests to be conducted in advance is impractical. As the examination unfolds and more information is gathered, the relevance of certain tests may change. Consequently, it is reasonable for Defendant to choose potential tests from a list based on the evolving specifics of the case as the assessment progresses. Carpenter v. Superior Court, 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 267 (Cal. Ct. App. 2006), supports this approach by allowing Plaintiff to request the identification of "potential tests and procedures" in the moving papers.  The Court acknowledges that providing a list of tests could potentially lead to abuse, where Defendant might include an exhaustive array of tests, obscuring the relevant ones within a sea of options and thereby failing to give Plaintiff adequate notice of the actual tests to be performed. This is not the situation here. The tests listed by Defendant are related to Plaintiff's alleged PTSD and severe emotional distress, and the list is concise enough to provide Plaintiff with adequate notice, allowing him to object to any specific tests he deems inappropriate, irrelevant, or abusive.

 

Third, the Court agrees with the Defendant that a separate statement is not necessary in this instance. California Rule of Court 3.1345 states that a separate statement is required “[f]or medical examination over objection.” (Rule 3.1345(6)). Since no objection to the mental examination was served, the requirement for a separate statement does not apply. The purpose of the separate statement is to allow the court to review objections and responses side by side for each request, for the court’s convenience. This requirement is inapplicable in the present motion as no objections were raised.

 

Having said that, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that no meet and confer was conducted by Defendant prior to the filing of the present motion. Defendant sent a request to stipulate to a mental examination on July 16, and the very next day, served a demand for the mental examination and filed a motion to compel. Defendant argues that based on past meet and confer experiences with Plaintiff's counsel where counsel does not respond, a more modest effort is sufficient under the circumstances. However, even if the Court were to agree with that position, it does not perceive the effort here to be even “modest.” Typically, meet and confer efforts involve a phone call or an in-person meeting.  Serving a demand for a mental examination and then filing a motion to compel on the very same day does not constitute a modest effort but rather no effort at all. This approach does not provide Plaintiff with a reasonable amount of time to respond or to engage in meet and confer.

 

Overall, the Court finds that there is good cause for the mental examination and that the demand for the mental examination has met the requirements of CCP § 2032. The court will continue the present motion to _____, 2024, and ORDERS the parties to meet and confer on Plaintiff’s mental examination based on the Court’s inclination as stated in the foregoing analysis.
