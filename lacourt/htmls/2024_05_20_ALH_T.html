DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV40992    Hearing Date: May 20, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

Moving Party:                         Defendant Ritz Carlton Hotel Company

Responding Party:                   Plaintiff Vivian Powers

Tentative Ruling:                    Denied

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Vivian E. Powers (“Plaintiff”) and Chris V. Powers filed this action against Defendants, including Defendant Ritz-Carlton Hotel Company LLC (“Defendant” or “Ritz”) for injuries Plaintiff sustained after falling at Defendant’s hotel property located at 900 W. Olympic Boulevard, Los Angeles, California 90015 (“premises”), because of an alleged dangerous condition.  Plaintiff contends that on October 29, 2018, Plaintiff began walking from her vehicle in the valet area towards the front door entrance to the lobby of the hotel when she tripped in the driveway due to an uneven tile that was of disproportionate height to the remainder of the tiles.  (Complaint ¶ 15.)  Plaintiff alleges the disproportionate tile created a dangerous condition, causing her to trip and sustain injuries.  (Id.)  Plaintiff filed the operative complaint on October 26, 2020, alleging causes of action for (1) general negligence; (2) premises liability; and (3) loss of consortium. (Id.)

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            Defendant Ritz now moves for summary judgment and on October 23, 2024, filed the motion, along with a proposed order, a compendium of evidence, an original flash drive of the incident surveillance video (“Exhibit N”), and a separate statement.  Plaintiff opposed the motion on May 6, 2024, along with filing a response to Defendant’s separate statement, evidentiary objections, and the declaration of Plaintiff’s attorney, Tom Schultz.  On May 15, 2024, Defendant filed their reply.

Moving Argument

Defendant argues that it is entitled to summary judgment because Plaintiff has failed to establish any triable issue of fact as to whether the alleged defect was anything more than trivial.  (MSJ, pg. 17.) 

Defendant first argues it is undisputed that the tile was misleveled by no more than ¼” and thus, the trivial defect doctrine applies.  Under this doctrine, a property owner is not liable for damages caused by a minor, trivial, or insignificant defect in property unless a plaintiff can plead and prove additional factors bearing on whether the defect presented a substantial risk of injury.  (Motion for Summary Judgment “MSJ”, pgs.12-14.)  

Next, Defendant contends that even if the misleveled tile constituted a “dangerous condition,” Defendant had no notice.  (MSJ, pg. 19.)  Further, the California State Supreme Court has emphasized hotels, like landlords, “are subject to liability. . .  only for conditions of which he is aware, or of which he could have known in the exercise of reasonable care.  (Peterson v. Sup. Ct. (Palm Springs Marquis Hotel) (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1185, 1197-1198.)  And it is not enough to be actually or constructively aware of a condition; the landlord must be on notice of unreasonable dangerousness.”  (MSJ, pg. 19.)  Defendant argues that it is undisputed that Defendant Ritz had no actual notice of such condition and Plaintiff presented no evidence that Defendant had even constructive notice of any unreasonable level of danger from the tile.  (MSJ, pgs. 19-20.)  Further, Defendant argues that landlords are not liable for a plaintiff’s injury arising from a trivial defect, even if the Defendant had actual notice.  (MSJ, pg. 19.)

Lastly, Defendant argues that the American Disability Act (ADA) is not applicable to the instant claim.  (MSJ, pg. 20.)  Although the ADA provides that paving or tiles which deviate in level between ¼” to ½” must be beveled, such rule only applies to a property’s designated and accessible route, not to the whole property.  (Id.)  Given Defendant has a designated ADA passenger drop-off (which is marked by yellow and black stripes), the rule applies to Defendant’s designated route and not to the valet driveway where Plaintiff fell.   (MSJ, pgs. 20-21.) 

Opposing Argument

In Opposition, Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment should be denied because there are several disputed material facts concerning the dangerousness of Defendant’s valet area where Plaintiff tripped.  (Opposition (“Opp.”), pg. 13.) 

Plaintiff represents there is a dispute as to whether there was a loose paving stone which contributed to Plaintiff’s injury and caused her to lose her balance and fall.  Plaintiff relies on the testimony of Defendant’s doorman, William Madrid, who was working for Defendant’s contractor, Classic Parking, on the date of the incident.  (Opp., pg. 11.)  Mr. Madrid later testified that there was a tile or paver that was like a “floorboard that was loose, and there was like—like a crack where it—if you—if you were a female and you were wearing heels and you stepped there, you would—chances are you would probably fall. (PUMF No. 16).”  (Opp., pg. 14.)

 Next, Plaintiff states that the size and dangerousness of the misleveling is disputed.  (Id.)  Although Defendant maintains the misleveling was less than ¼”, Defendant’s own designated witness said it was larger (PUMF No. 32) and Plaintiff’s safety experts measured the misleveling to be 3/8”, making the defect unreasonably dangerous. (PUMF No. 33.) (Opp., pg. 14.). Additionally, Plaintiff asserts that a “tape measure alone cannot be used to determine whether the defect was trivial.”  (Fajardo v. Dailey, (2022) 85 Cal. App. 5th 221, 227.)  “A party seeking summary judgment must offer admissible evidence about the surrounding context that would allow the factfinder to know if the defect is more dangerous than the tape measure would suggest.”  (Id.). Plaintiff contends that Plaintiff has provided contextual evidence by establishing the valet was busier than usual which required Plaintiff to walk away from the designated pedestrian path she typically used and expert testimony established the location of the stone, its dark color, and the limits of human perception all contributed to the danger.  (Opp., pg. 15.)  Although Defendant discussed context, such evidence constitutes unsworn statements of counsel which are not evidence because in summary adjudication, facts must be set forth in the separate statement otherwise the Court may not permit them.  (Id.)

Third, Plaintiff argues that by failing to comply with building codes and industry standards, including 2008 Los Angeles Building Code Section 1003.4, (requiring flush walkways), breached Defendant’s duty.

Lastly, Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s negligence is disputed because (a) Defendant had a non-delegable duty to keep the valet parking area safe, as California holds landowners liable for the negligence of their agents, regardless of whether the agent was an employee or independent contractor.  (Opp., pg. 16.)  Next, (b) Defendant’s failure to conduct inspections of the valet area is negligent because Defendant had a duty to exercise ordinary care in the management of their premises.  (Opp., pg. 18.)  Finally, (c) Defendant had constructive notice of the defect because constructive notice can be established by demonstrating a defendant’s failure to make reasonably regular inspections and Defendant had actual notice because Mr. Madrid testified he had actual knowledge of the misleveling.  (Opp., 19.)

 Reply Argument

Defendant further argues that building codes, industry standards, and defendant’s own internal height standards do not raise a triable issue of fact on a trivial defect MSJ according to the courts because in the instant case, it is not at issue whether there is a defect, but instead, whether it is substantially dangerous. (Reply, pg. 5.)  Defendant cites to appellate case, Caloroso v. Hathaway which stated that “elevations ranging from ¾” to 1 ½” were noted to commonly indicate a trivial defect” and “the court properly found no foundation for Avrit’s opinion that that non-compliance of certain building codes and standards made the crack dangerous.”  (Caloroso v. Hathaway, (2004) 122 Cal.App.44th 922, 928.)

 

Defendant also addresses Plaintiff’s argument concerning contextual evidence of substantial dangerousness and states that Defendant’s trivial defect MSJ was granted and affirmed in Nunez v. City of Redondo Beach, (2002) 81 Cal.App.5th 749 on 11/16” misleveling whereby plaintiff's expert opined that "there was no color or texture differentiation between the concrete slabs forming the height differential (Id. at p. 754).”  (Reply, pg. 8.)  Lastly, Defendant maintains that notice does not create liability when a defect is considered minor or trivial.  (Reply, pgs. 7-8.)

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

Motion for Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Id. at §437c(p).) A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option, he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence....” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal. 4th at 865-66.)

 

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.) Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.) In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the

cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.) (Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.)

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action. (Id. at §437c(p)(2).) On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion. (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

Premises Liability 

“The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury.  Premises liability ‘ “is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises” ’; accordingly, ‘ “mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act.” ’ But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases. (Kesner v. Superior Court, (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159 [210 Cal.Rptr.3d 283, 384 P.3d 283.])

Application to Facts

Defendant is the moving party in the instant case so Defendant must show either that (1) one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established, or (2) there is a complete defense to that cause of action.

 

Here, Defendant states that although the trivial defect doctrine does not serve as an affirmative defense, it is an aspect of duty that plaintiff must plead and prove.  Thus, Defendant must demonstrate that Plaintiff is unable to establish that Defendant (1) had a duty to exercise a legal duty of care; (2) breached that care; or (3) was the proximate cause resulting in the jury.

Defendant contends that when the size of a defect in a premises liability case falls within the definition of triviality under the appellate course case, Huckey v. City of Temecula, Plaintiff must prove additional factors bearing on whether the defect presented a substantial risk. (MSJ, pg. 14.)  Such additional factors include whether there were any broken pieces or jagged edges in the area of the defect, whether any dirt, debris or other material obscured a pedestrian’s view of the defect, the plaintiff’s knowledge of the area, whether the accident occurred at night or in an unlighted area, the weather at the time of the accident and whether the defect has caused any other accident.”  (MSJ, pg. 15.) 

Defendant cites to case law declaring that misleveling ranging between ¼” to 1 ½” is considered a trivial defect. Although Defendant initially claims in its moving papers that it is undisputed that the misleveling constituted no more than ¼ inches, Defendant quotes 3/8” in its Reply, as Plaintiff asserted the evidence pointed to 3/8” and not ¼”.  Assuming, arguendo, that the misleveling of 3/8” in this case is considered trivial, Defendant must then negate Plaintiff’s evidence that such additional Huckey factors did not contribute to making the trivial defect substantially dangerous.

Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not demonstrate these factors because the partial crack depicted is not the paver Plaintiff fell on and even if it was, a crack does not constitute a “broken piece” because the partial crack is not all the way through.  (Id.)  Defendant states there are no broken pieces of jagged edges. (Id.)  However, Plaintiff alleges that the tile was dark in color and that Plaintiff was walking along a pathway she did not usually walk around because she had to navigate through the valet drive on the busy night.  Although Defendant argues in its reply that the court in Nunez granted its MSJ despite plaintiff’s expert stating there was no color or texture differentiation, Defendant’s argument is inapposite. The Huckey factors do specify that the color of the tile and the pedestrian’s view is a relevant factor and Defendant does not assert that the Nunez court found the paving color irrelevant in their determination.  Moreover, the Huckey factors are factors to be weighed on balance. There is no assertion that any one factor is dispositive or that the factors provided are even an exclusive list. Additionally, Plaintiff asserted the tile was loose and provided supporting testimony from Mr. Martin.  Finally, the Court finds Defendant has merely asserted its opinion and is not a binding authority in distinguishing the risk or danger due to “partially cracked tile” from the risk of a fully cracked or loose tile, especially considering that Mr. Martin testified the tile was loose.

Given that the Huckey factors determine whether a trivial defect constitutes a substantial risk which create a legal duty of care to Plaintiff which Defendant breached, the facts giving rise to such factors are material facts in this case.  It is disputed whether the color of the stone, the location of incident, the nature of the crack, and Plaintiff’s familiarity with the walkway was a trivial misleveling that created a substantial risk.  It is also disputed whether the tile was loose. Further, Plaintiff contends that Defendant had a duty to regularly inspect and maintain the premises which it breached. Thus, the facts that would create a substantial risk are in dispute, and Defendant has not negated Plaintiff’s arguments or evidence demonstrating so.

A defendant moving for MSJ may discharge its burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.   In this case, Plaintiff has not conceded in discovery that it lacks evidence to establish an essential element of premises liability. Defendant has proffered arguments and evidence attempting to negate facts that can establish premises liability but such evidence must be affirmative.  Here, the Court finds Defendant’s evidence is not.

  Moreover, even if such evidence was affirmative, the burden would then shift to Plaintiff to give Plaintiff the opportunity to defeat the motion through producing evidence demonstrating a triable issue of material fact. Here, Plaintiff asserted material facts in its opposition relating to the Huckey factors, thereby, creating a triable issue of material fact.  Defendant has been unable to negate such facts in its reply.  It remains that the moving party’s supporting documents are to be strictly construed on a motion for summary judgment, its opponent’s documents are to be liberally construed, and doubts are to be resolved against granting the motion.

Thus, the Court hereby denies Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment. 

CONCLUSION

            The Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On November 5, 2020, Plaintiff Adrianne Pearson filed this trip and fall negligence action against Defendant Chipotle Mexican Grill, Inc. 

On February 21, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion to Compel Nonparty David Vesco, M.D. to Comply with the Subpoena. On February 27, 2024, Dr. Vesco responded to the Subpoena with an Affidavit of No Records.

On April 4, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses to Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records Issued to Non-Party David Vesco, M.D. No opposition has been filed. 

LEGAL STANDARD

             Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies. (CCP § 2020.220(c).)

CCP § 2025.480(a) provides that “[i]f a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information,¿or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” Unlike CCP § 1987.1, CCP § 2025.480 also includes a time limit. If a nonparty disobeys a deposition subpoena, the subpoenaing party may seek a court order compelling the nonparty to comply with the subpoena within¿60 days¿after completion of the¿deposition record. (CCP § 2025.480(b); Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 CA4th 123, 127.) The deposition record is “complete” either on the date documents are specified for production or on the date objections are served. (Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson (2021) 59 CA5th 1011, 1032-1033.)  

A motion to compel production of documents described in a deposition notice must be accompanied by a showing of “good cause”—i.e., declarations containing specific facts justifying inspection of the documents described in the notice. (CCP § 2025.450(b)(1)).

“A written notice and all moving papers supporting a motion to compel an answer to a deposition question or to compel production of a document or tangible thing from a nonparty deponent must be personally served on the nonparty deponent unless the nonparty deponent agrees to accept service by mail or electronic service at an address or electronic service address specified on the deposition record.” (CRC Rule 3.1346.) 

A separate statement is required for motions to compel further responses to a demand for inspection of documents or tangible things. (CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(3).)

DISCUSSION

Defendant moves to compel further responses to the deposition subpoena for production of business records issued to non-party David Vesco, M.D.

            As Dr. Vesco is a non-party, personal service of the motion is required. (CRC Rule 3.1346.) The proof of service attached to the motion indicates service by mail only. Further, it is unclear if Dr. Vesco was even personally served with the deposition subpoena or only served by mail based on the proof of service of deposition subpoena. (Lopez Decl., ¶5, Exh. 2A.) Lastly, there is no separate statement as required by CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(3).

Based on the foregoing, the motion is DENIED.
