DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.

                                       Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.  This does not excuse a moving party's need to do one of the following: appear; submit; or take a matter off calendar by canceling the motion in the case reservation system before issuance of the tentative ruling if the matter moving party does not intend to proceed.    
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.

Having considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On January 25, 2022, Plaintiff Insurance Company of the West (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Angel Granado (“Granado”), Priority Workforce, LLC (“Workforce”), and Does 1-10 for general negligence. 

On February 2, 2024, Workforce filed a demurrer and request for judicial notice to be heard on May 8, 2024. On April 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On May 1, 2024, Workforce filed a reply.  The Court continued the hearing to June 4, 2024. 

No trial date is currently scheduled. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Workforce asks the Court to sustain the demurrer to the complaint. 

Plaintiff asks the Court to overrule the demurrer. 

WORKFORCE’S REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE 

          Granted 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.   Demurrer 

“The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object, by demurrer or answer as provided in Section 430.30, to the pleading on any one or more of the following grounds:

B.   Negligence 

The elements of a negligence claim are (1) a legal duty of care, (2) breach of that duty, and (3) proximate cause resulting in injury.  (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.) 

DISCUSSION 

A.   The complaint 

The complaint alleges the following: 

On February 3, 2020, at 3851 S. Santa Fe Avenue, Vernon, CA 90058, Fernando Garduno (“Garduno”) was operating a forklift in the course and scope of his employment with Hannibal Industries, Inc. (“Hannibal”).  Plaintiff was Hannibal’s workers’ compensation insurer. 

Angel Granado was operating a separate forklift at the same location in the course and scope of his employment with Workforce.  Granado failed to use reasonable care in the operation of the forklift, causing a collision between the two forklifts. As a result, Garduno was injured, requiring Plaintiff to provide worker’s compensation benefits to Garduno. 

Workforce is vicariously liable for damages as Granado’s employer.  In addition, Workforce failed to use reasonable care in its hiring, training and/or supervision of Granado.  Defendants’ negligence caused Garduno’s injury and Plaintiff’s damages. 

Under Labor Code sections 3850, et seq., Plaintiff seeks damages in subrogation for worker’s compensation benefits paid to and on behalf of Garduno for sums according to proof.  Benefits are continuing. 

B.   Workforce’s demurrer 

Workforce argues the complaint’s allegations are conclusory and fail to allege facts showing that Workforce owed Plaintiff a duty of care, breached that duty, or caused Plaintiff’s damages.  Workforce also contends the complaint is vague, ambiguous and uncertain. 

Plaintiff opposes the demurrer, arguing the complaint sufficiently pleads a negligence claim. 

C.   Analysis 

A complaint must contain “[a] statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a)(1).)  “The essential elements to be pleaded to state a cause of action are determined by the substantive law.”  (L. Edmon & C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2023) ¶ 6:121, p. 6-37 (Cal. Practice Guide).) 

“The ‘facts’ to be pleaded are those upon which liability depends—i.e., ‘the facts constituting the cause of action.’  These are commonly referred to as ‘ultimate facts.’”  (Cal. Practice Guide, supra, ¶ 6:123, p. 6-39.)  “ ‘The complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.’ ”  (Ibid., quoting C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 54 Cal.4th 861, 872.) 

“Lawyers and judges have struggled with these distinctions.  ‘Ultimate facts’ are those that raise the issues on which the right to recover depends – i.e., the essential elements of the cause of action.  All the facts that are material to the cause of action – i.e., the facts that make a difference to the outcome of the case – must be alleged [citation].  [Citations.]  By contrast, allegations of unnecessary detail and generalized argument may be objectionable as ‘evidentiary’ pleading and ‘legal conclusion,’ respectively.”  (Cal. Practice Guide, supra, ¶ 6:124, pp. 6-39 to 6-40; see id., ¶ 6:125, p. 6-40 [allegation that defendant drove while under the influence of alcohol pleads an ultimate fact; allegation that defendant drove immediately after consuming a fifth of vodka pleads evidentiary matters; allegation that defendant drove in violation of California drunk driving laws pleads a legal conclusion].) 

A leading treatise concludes that “[t]he distinction between ‘ultimate facts’ and ‘evidentiary’ matters is of diminishing importance because a complaint will be upheld if it provides the defendant with ‘notice of the issues sufficient to enable preparation of a defense.' "  (Cal. Practice Guide, supra, ¶ 6:128, pp. 6-43 to 6-44, quoting Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-550 (Doe).)  “Thus, plaintiff need only plead such facts as are necessary ‘to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source and extent of his claims.’ ”  (Id., ¶ 6:128, p. 6-44, quoting Doe, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.) 

“Ordinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms, without stating the acts constituting negligence or detailing the particular manner in which plaintiff’s injury occurred.”  (Cal. Practice Guide, supra, ¶ 6:129, p. 6-44.)  “On the other hand, a bare allegation that ‘defendant’s negligence caused plaintiff’s injury’ is not sufficient.  Plaintiff must allege the acts or omissions that are claimed to have constituted the negligence.”  (Ibid.) 

The Court has evaluated the complaint in light of these authorities and concludes it pleads ultimate facts that state a cause of action for negligence under theories of vicarious liability and negligent hiring, training, and supervision.  The complaint acquaints Workforce with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s claims.  It is not vague, ambiguous, or uncertain.  The Court overrules the demurrer. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court OVERRULES Defendant Priority Workforce, LLC’S demurrer to Plaintiff Insurance Company of the West’s complaint.
