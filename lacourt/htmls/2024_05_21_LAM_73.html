Case Number: 22STCV35995    Hearing Date: May 21, 2024    Dept: 73

05/21/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

NORRIS v. BRENTWOOD DERMATOLOGY MEDICAL GROUP, INC., et al. (22STCV35995)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Leonard Sansanowicz (Sansanowicz Law Group, P.C.)

Counsel for Defendants:  Lauren Katunich (Raines Feldman Littrell LLP)

 

(1) PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (filed by Plaintiff on 12/13/23)

 

(2) DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT (filed by Defendants on 12/14/23)

 

(3) PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER FOR DEFENDANTS TO PRODUCE EVIDENCE OF THEIR

FINANCIAL CONDITION SUBJECT TO PROTECTIVE ORDER (filed by Plaintiff on 3/20/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED as to Plaintiff’s first, second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action, and as to punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to Issue Nos. 1-4 and GRANTED as to Issue No. 5.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Order for Defendants to Produce Evidence of their Financial Condition Subject to Protective Order.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 14, 2022, Plaintiff Madison Norris (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendants Brentwood Dermatology Medical Group, Inc. (“BDMG”) and Jack Hakimian Silvers, M.D. (“Dr. Silvers”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) sexual harassment in violation of Government Code § 12940(j); (2) gender discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(a); (3) FEHA retaliation in violation of Government Code § 12940(h); (4) retaliation in violation of Labor Code § 1102.5(c); and (5) wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. BDMG offers medical, procedural and cosmetic dermatology services. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges upon information and belief, Dr. Silvers is the sole practicing physician of BDMG. (Id., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff began working for Defendants in June 2020 as an office assistant. (Id., ¶ 15.) Plaintiff worked at the front desk and her job description included duties such as scheduling, stocking supplies, answering phone calls, processing payments, managing the company’s social media posts, and greeting visitors. (Id., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff alleges that Dr. Silvers tried to present Plaintiff as a medical assistant to the patients and wanted her to perform medical assistant duties, such as taking notes during patient examinations and intakes, removing stitches from patients, and removing actinic keratoses – rough, scaly patches of skin – from patients. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, Plaintiff alleges she refused to perform some of these duties because she had no training as a medical assistant. (Ibid.)

 

One of the services offered by BDMG is platelet-rich plasma (“PRP”) therapy, which involves injecting a concentration of platelets from the patient’s own blood back into the body after having been separated from the patient’s blood with a centrifuge. (Id., ¶ 16.) BDMG also offers a form of PRP therapy known as the Priapus shot or “P-Shot” which is typically used to improve sexual function or treat erectile dysfunction in men. (Id., ¶ 16.) Plaintiff alleges Dr. Silvers insisted Plaintiff be present in the room on several occasions while he administered PRP injections to women, although Plaintiff did not request nor wish to observe the procedure. (Id., ¶ 18.) Shortly after she began working at BDMG, Dr. Silvers also requested that Plaintiff edit videos of him injecting patients with the P-Shot to be uploaded on the BDMG website and to be used to show patients the effects of PRP therapy. (Id., ¶ 19.) Plaintiff alleges she did not want to see videos of genitals but complied with Dr. Silvers’ request as she was new to BDMG and did not want to lose her job. (Ibid.) In or around September 2020, Dr. Silvers asked Plaintiff to film him injecting himself with the P-Shot. (Id., ¶ 20.) Plaintiff alleges she was shocked by the request and did not answer right away. II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motions for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

Motions for summary adjudication are procedurally identical to motions for summary judgment. (Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 859.) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code of Civ. Proc., §¿437c,¿subd. (f)(1).)¿

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

Defendants move for summary judgment of Plaintiff’s first, second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action in the Complaint.

 

a.       First Cause of Action for Sexual Harassment

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for sexual harassment in violation of the FEHA fails because Plaintiff was not subject to conduct that qualifies as sexual harassment and the conduct complained of was not severe or pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment.

 

The Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”) makes it unlawful for an employer to “harass an employee because of the employee’s ‘sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, . . . [or] sexual orientation.’” (Taylor v. Nabors Drilling USA, LP (2014) 222 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1235-36 (quoting Govt. Code, § 12940(j)(1)).) “A hostile work environment sexual harassment claim requires a plaintiff employee to show: (1) he or she was subjected to unwelcome sexual advances, conduct or comments; (2) the harassment was based on sex; and (3) the harassment was sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment.” (Lewis v. City of Benicia (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1524; see also Taylor, supra, 222 Cal.App.4th at 1236.) Unwelcome conduct under the hostile work environment standard need not be motivated by sexual desire. (Miller v. Dept. of Corrections (2005) 36 Cal.4th 446, 469.) Verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature “that has the purpose or effect of unreasonably interfering with an individual’s work performance or creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment” is sufficient. (Id. at 463.)

 

“There is no recovery ‘for harassment that is occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial.’” (Fuentes v. AutoZone, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1221, 1226 (quoting Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1043).) “A single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment.”  (Gov. Code, § 12923(b).)  “Harassment cases are rarely appropriate for disposition on summary judgment. In that regard, the Legislature affirms the decision in Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243 and its observation that hostile working environment cases involve issues ‘not determinable on paper.’” (Gov. Code, § 12923(e).) 

 

The Complaint alleges that Dr. Silvers’ sexually harassing conduct includes: (1) requesting Plaintiff to edit videos of his medical procedures involving genitalia; (2) sexually harassing comments; and (3) asking Plaintiff to film him injecting himself with the P-Shot.

 

                                                               i.      Requesting Plaintiff to Edit Videos of Medical Procedures Involving Genitalia

 

Defendants argue that requesting Plaintiff to edit videos of Dr. Silvers’ medical procedures involving genitalia is not conduct that qualifies as sexual harassment because it was legitimate, work-related conduct.

 

The Complaint alleges shortly after she began working at BDMG, Dr. Silvers requested that Plaintiff edit videos of him injecting patients with the P-Shot to be uploaded on the BDMG website and to be used to show patients the effects of PRP therapy. (Complaint, ¶ 19.) Plaintiff alleges she did not want to see videos of genitals but complied with Dr. Silvers’ request as she was new to BDMG and did not want to lose her job. (Ibid.)

 

Defendants assert that asking Plaintiff to edit videos of his medical procedures involving genitalia is legitimate, work-related conduct that a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would expect to do while working in a medical office that performs medical procedures involving genitalia. “In determining whether or not a hostile environment existed, courts should only consider the nature of the workplace when engaging in or witnessing prurient conduct and commentary is integral to the performance of the job duties.” (Cal. Gov. Code Section 12923(d).) Defendants rely on Lyle v. Warner Bros (2006) 38 Cal. 4th 264, 287 where the Court considered the nature of the creative work environment and the legitimate need for discussions of sex in the workplace when determining whether summary judgment for the employer was appropriate. (Lyle v. Warner Bros (2006) 38 Cal. 4th 264, 287.)

 

Here, Defendants contend the PRP genital treatments were part of the of medical services offered by BDMG and thus editing videos of the procedures for marketing and educational purposes was a legitimate need of the business. In Dr. Silvers’ declaration, he stated the videos were intended to allow potential patients the ability to learn about and observe given procedures and their results before deciding to get the procedure. (Silvers Decl., ¶ 31.) Defendants also note that Plaintiff testified there was nothing sexual about the PRP genital procedure itself or Dr. Silvers’ conduct during the procedures. (Norris Depo., 89:19-91:9.) Thus, since Defendants have shown that the videos had a marketing and educational purpose, and that the Plaintiff did not find the procedures themselves had sexual connotations, the Court finds that taking into account Plaintiff’s workplace, a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would not have been surprised or offended by having to observe, record, or edit videos of the PRP genital procedures.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that when she first interviewed for the job, she was not told there would be an expectation or requirement to assist with medical procedures involving genitalia or edit videos showing genitalia. Plaintiff argues that in Lyle, the plaintiff was told before she accepted the position that there may be sexual commentary in the course of her work. (Lyle, supra, 38 Cal. 4th at 287.) However, in Lyle, the conduct and statements were overtly sexual and sexist, but not harassing because they were a necessary part of the writing process for a TV show. Here, the complained of conduct was not sexual and involved sex organs and nudity that was medically necessary for the procedure. Therefore, the Court finds that a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would not find exposure to nudity in a medical practice that offers procedures on genitalia to be unexpected or offensive. Thus, Plaintiff has not shown that Dr. Silvers’ request for her to edit videos involving genitalia qualifies as sexual harassment because it was legitimate, work-related conduct.

 

                                                             ii.      Dr. Silvers’ Comments

 

Defendants argue the allegedly sexually harassing comments made by Dr. Silvers were not severe or pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment.   

 

The Complaint alleges that on more than one occasion, Dr. Silvers made comments to Plaintiff to the effect that it did not matter how good his medical procedure work was as it was not going to be effective with his patients, several of whom were women in their sixties, because they were “aging poorly.” (Id., ¶ 22.) Dr. Silvers also commented to some of his female patients on multiple occasions while in Plaintiff’s presence, that women getting divorced these days was causing anxiety with their children. (Id., ¶ 23.) Plaintiff alleges upon information and belief, Dr. Silvers did not make similar comments to his male patients. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that on multiple occasions, Dr. Silvers would talk to Plaintiff and her female co-worker about his dating attempts and kept his dating apps on the iPad that Plaintiff used to record treatments, which made Plaintiff uncomfortable. (Id., ¶ 24.)

 

Defendants argue these occasional comments regarding his female patients and his personal life were not severe or pervasive and fail to demonstrate any alteration of Plaintiff’s employment conditions or any impediment to her job performance.

 

Although it appears that the comments are based on gender, and Plaintiff alleges they made her uncomfortable, the Court finds these statements do not rise to the level of severe or pervasive. The comments made by Dr. Silvers do not appear severe, for example, they do not include epithets, derogatory comments or slurs. The comments also do not appear to have been pervasive, as Plaintiff must show harassment of a repeated, routine or generalized nature, especially when harassing conduct is not severe. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1043; see McCoy v. Pacific Maritime Assn. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 283, 294 [finding that, over the four months plaintiff worked in the office, at the most nine comments were made to plaintiff, and this was insufficient to show that the workplace was permeated with harassment].) Harassment that is occasional, isolated, or sporadic is insufficient. Plaintiff does not specify the number of times Dr. Silvers made these comments, but stating they were made on “multiple occasions” is not sufficient in showing the workplace was permeated with harassment. Thus, Plaintiff has not shown that Dr. Silvers’ comments regarding female patients and his dating life qualifies as sexual harassment.

 

                                                           iii.      Dr. Silvers’ Request for Plaintiff to Film his PRP Procedure

 

Defendants also argue Dr. Silvers’ request for Plaintiff to film his PRP procedure was legitimate, work-related conduct that would not constitute sexual harassment, and it was not severe or pervasive. 

 

The Complaint alleges in or around September 2020, Dr. Silvers asked Plaintiff to film him injecting himself with the P-Shot. (Complaint, ¶ 20.) Plaintiff alleges she was shocked by the request and did not answer right away. (Ibid.) About a week later, Dr. Silvers asked Plaintiff again to film him and Plaintiff refused. (Ibid.) After she refused to film Dr. Silvers, Plaintiff alleges that she did not receive any bonuses in her last four pay periods, except for a bonus of $7.39 on her last paycheck, which was a fraction of what she had been paid in bonuses before. (Id., ¶ 21.) Plaintiff’s compensation was supposed to include a bonus based on the number of patient compliments she received and documented, and Plaintiff claims she received multiple patient compliments during those last four pay periods. (Ibid.)

 

Defendants argue that Dr. Silvers’ request for Plaintiff to film him injecting himself with the P-Shot was legitimate, work-related conduct that would not constitute sexual harassment. However, while Defendants assert that viewing procedures involving patients nudity is expected, Defendants do not demonstrate how assisting Dr. Silvers inject the PRP procedure on himself would be expected, even if the video was made for educational purposes. “In determining whether or not a hostile environment existed, courts should only consider the nature of the work when [the complained-of conduct] is integral to the performance of job duties.” (See Cal. Gov’t Code § 12923(d).) It does not appear that the complained of conduct, filming Dr. Silvers, was integral to the performance of Plaintiff’s duties. Defendants have not shown that a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would expect to be exposed to nudity from her supervisor as opposed to nudity from patients. Since the Court does not find Defendants have shown this request would be work-related conduct, the Court next turns to whether this request was severe or pervasive such that it would qualify as sexual harassment.

 

Defendants assert the request only occurred twice and was not pervasive. Defendants also contend it did not rise to the level of sufficiently severe conduct because it did not alter Plaintiff’s employment conditions or impede her job performance. According to the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that after she refused to film Dr. Silvers, she did not receive any bonuses in her last four pay periods, except for a bonus of $7.39 on her last paycheck, which was a fraction of what she had been paid in bonuses before. (Compl., ¶ 21.) Plaintiff’s compensation was supposed to include a bonus based on the number of patient compliments she received and documented, and Plaintiff claims she received multiple patient compliments during those last four pay periods. (Ibid.) Although the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff’s bonus was reduced after refusing to film Dr. Silvers’, Defendants present evidence that the decrease in Plaintiff’s bonus was not due to her refusal to film Dr. Silvers. In Dr. Silvers’ declaration, he states that Plaintiff received a nominal $7.39 incentive bonus on her final paycheck on December 1, 2020 and no other employee that month received an incentive bonus. (Silvers Decl., ¶ 47.) Dr. Silvers testified that COVID was a factor in calculating the incentive bonus and that the incentive bonus was based on the monthly profit of the office and how happy patients were with Plaintiff. (Silvers Depo. 162: 9-20.) Since Defendants provided other reasons for Plaintiff’s decrease in her bonus, the Court finds Defendants have sufficiently established that Dr. Silvers’ request was not severe such that it altered her employment conditions.

 

The burden then shifts to Plaintiff, who asserts that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude this particular incident was sufficiently severe enough to create a work environment that was hostile to Plaintiff in violation of the FEHA. “A single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment if the harassing conduct unreasonably interfered with the plaintiff’s work performance or created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.” (Cal. Gov. Code § 12923(b).) In support, Plaintiff cites to Dr. Silvers’ deposition testimony in which he states that he noticed a change in Plaintiff after his request. (Silvers Depo. 101: 13-102:1.) Dr. Silvers felt that Plaintiff was no longer happy at the job but could not tell why she was unhappy. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds Dr. Silvers’ testimony to be sufficient in raising a triable issue of fact as to Plaintiff’s harassment claim. A reasonable juror could conclude that Dr. Silvers’ request for Plaintiff to film him while being nude sufficiently severe to create a hostile work environment for Plaintiff. Sexual harassment involving the creation of a hostile work environment need not have anything to do with sexual advances. (Accardi v. Superior Ct. (1993) 17 Cal. App. 4th 341, 348–49.) “Sexual harassment creates a hostile, offensive, oppressive, or intimidating work environment and deprives its victim of her statutory right to work in a place free of discrimination, when the sexually harassing conduct sufficiently offends, humiliates, distresses or intrudes upon its victim, so as to disrupt her emotional tranquility in the workplace, affect her ability to perform her job as usual, or otherwise interferes with and undermines her personal sense of well-being.’ [Citation.]” (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 608.) Thus, drawing inferences in the light most favorable to the nonmoving party, as required on summary judgment, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated a triable issue of fact in this respect. The Court finds Plaintiff has met her burden.

 

As such, summary adjudication for the first cause of action in the Complaint for sexual harassment is DENIED.

 

b.      Second Cause of Action for Gender Discrimination

 

Defendants move for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action on the ground that

Plaintiff is unable to establish a prima facie claim for discrimination under the FEHA. In any case, Defendants assert that they had a legitimate business reason for the actions they took against Plaintiff.

 

A plaintiff alleging discrimination must allege “that (1) he was a member of a protected class, (2) he was qualified for the position he sought or was performing competently in the position he held, (3) he suffered an adverse employment action, such as termination, demotion, or denial of an available job, and (4) some other circumstance suggests discriminatory motive.” (Guz v. Bechtel National Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 355.) To satisfy its initial summary judgment burden, moving party employer must either undermine an element of plaintiff's prima facie case by affirmatively negating it or showing plaintiff cannot prove it, or provide a legitimate nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse employment action. (McGrory v. Applied Signal Tech., Inc.¿(2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1523.) 

 

The Complaint alleges that Dr. Silvers’ comments about women and to women reflected his objectification of women and his refusal to treat them as equals to men. Specifically, the Complaint alleges Dr. Silvers made comments to Plaintiff to the effect that it did not matter how good his medical procedure work was as it was not going to be effective with his patients, several of whom were women in their sixties, because they were “aging poorly.” (Id., ¶ 22.) Dr. Silvers also commented to some of his female patients on multiple occasions while in Plaintiff’s presence, that women getting divorced these days was causing anxiety with their children. (Id., ¶ 23.) Plaintiff alleges upon information and belief, Dr. Silvers did not make similar comments to his male patients. (Ibid.)

 

Defendants argue that they had legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons for terminating Plaintiff that were not motivated by Plaintiff’s gender. Dr. Silvers terminated Plaintiff not because she was a woman but because he did not think she was a good fit for the Practice, was not showing the same enthusiasm for the job as she initially showed, and that she did not want to do the job for which she was hired. (Silvers Decl., ¶¶ 42-43.) Thus, Defendants have met their burden of showing an element of Plaintiff’s case is missing by showing a non-discriminatory motive for her termination, which shifts the burden to Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff argues that she was terminated, in substantial part, due to her gender. Plaintiff contends she was engaged in protected activity under the FEHA for opposing sexual harassment, which itself is a form of gender discrimination. (Meritor Sav. Bank, FSB v. Vinson (1986) 106 S.Ct. 2399, 2404; Government Code section 12940(h).) Plaintiff asserts that her refusal to film Dr. Silvers was sexually harassing conduct that she opposed. Approximately three months after Plaintiff refused Dr. Silvers’ request, she was terminated. (Norris Depo., 146:5-12.) Plaintiff asserts that the timing of her termination sufficiently infers pretext.

 

The Court agrees that a reasonable juror could conclude that timing infers pretext. “Pretext may…be inferred from the timing of the company’s termination decision, by the identity of the person making the decision, and by the terminated employee’s job performance before the termination.” (Flait v. N. Am. Watch Corp. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 467, 479.) The Ninth Circuit has found that “causation can be inferred from timing alone where an adverse employment action follows on the heels of protected activity.” (Villiarimo v. Aloha Island Air, Inc. (9th Cir. 2002) 281 F.3d 1054, 1065; Matthews v. Trilogy Comm’ns, Inc., 143 F.3d 1160, 1166 (8th Cir. 1998) (a gap of only two months between the protected activity and the employee’s discharge may create an inference of a retaliatory motive).) Here, Plaintiff’s termination was only a few months after opposing the alleged sexual harassment. The person making the decision to terminate Plaintiff was Dr. Silvers, the same person she refused to film. This is a sufficient showing to meet Plaintiff’s shifted burden.

 

As such, summary adjudication for the second cause of action in the Complaint for gender discrimination is DENIED.

 

c.       Third Cause of Action for Retaliation in violation of FEHA

 

Defendants argue that the third cause of action in the FAC for retaliation fails because (1) Plaintiff cannot show a prima facie case of retaliation because she cannot show a causal link between any protected activity and any adverse employment action; and (2) Defendants have shown a legitimate, non-retaliatory reasons for their actions. 

 

To establish retaliation under FEHA, a plaintiff must show that “(1) he or she engaged in a ‘protected activity,’ (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer’s action.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.) A retaliation claim can be brought by an employee who has complained of conduct reasonably believed to be discriminatory. (Id. at 1043; see also Kelley v. Conco Companies (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 191, 209-10.) “[A]lthough an adverse employment action must materially affect the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment to be actionable, the determination of whether a particular action or course of conduct rises to the level of actionable conduct should take into account the unique circumstances of the affected employee as well as the workplace context of the claim.” (Yanowitz, supra, 36 Cal.4th at 1052.)  

 

Defendants submit the same evidence to show that they had legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons for terminating Plaintiff that were not motivated by any protected activity. As discussed above, this evidence was sufficient to meet Defendants’ burden, however, Plaintiff also presented evidence of pretext and triable issues of material facts. The Court finds that it is sufficient to meet Plaintiff’s burden for this cause of action as well.

 

As such, summary adjudication as to the third cause of action in the FAC for retaliation is DENIED.

 

d.      Fourth Cause of Action for Retaliation in violation of Labor Code 1102.5

 

Defendants move for summary adjudication as to the fourth cause of action on the ground that

Plaintiff is unable to establish a prima facie claim for retaliation in violation of Labor Code 1102.5(c).  

 

Section 1102.5(c) states, “An employer…shall not retaliate against an employee for refusing to participate in an activity that would result in a violation of state or federal statute, or a violation of or noncompliance with a local, state, or federal rule or regulation.” (Cal. Lab. Code § 1102.5(c).) Where a plaintiff alleges violation of 1102.5(c), she must establish that an actual violation of law would result from an activity to prove retaliation under § 1102.5(c). (Ibid.) An employee’s reasonable but incorrect suspicion of illegality is not sufficient; the activity must actually result in the violation of a statute, rule, or regulation. (Nejadian v. County of Los Angeles, 40 Cal. App. 5th 703, 719 (2019).) The employee must identify the specific activity in which the employee refused to participate, and what specific statute, rule, or regulation would have been violated. (Id.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff did not refuse to perform an act that would result in the violation of law. Plaintiff alleges one activity she refused to perform: refusing to remove sutures (stitches) from patients because she was not a trained medical professional in violation of Business and Professions Code § 2069. (Norris Depo., , 76:9-77:9.) That statute states in pertinent part:

 

(a)(1) Notwithstanding any other law, a medical assistant may administer medication only by intradermal, subcutaneous, or intramuscular injections and perform skin tests and additional technical supportive services upon the specific authorization and supervision of a licensed physician…

[(b)](1) “Medical assistant” means a person who may be unlicensed, who performs basic administrative, clerical, and technical supportive services in compliance with this section and Section 2070 for a licensed physician….

(3) “Supervision” means the supervision of procedures authorized by this section by the following practitioners, within the scope of their respective practices, who shall be physically present in the treatment facility during the performance of those procedures: (A) A licensed physician and surgeon….

(4)(A) “Technical supportive services” means simple routine medical tasks and procedures that may be safely performed by a medical assistant who has limited training and who functions under the supervision of a licensed physician….

(Bus. & Prof. Code § 2069.)

 

Medical assistants are specifically permitted to remove sutures from patients after “receiving such training as, in the judgment of the supervising physician[,] is necessary to assure the medical assistant’s competence in performing that service[.]” (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 16, §§ 1366(b)(4) (sutures), 1366.2 (training).)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admits that Dr. Silvers trained her on how to remove sutures. (Norris Depo., p. 83:9-25.) In Plaintiff’s deposition, she states that “When he [Dr. Silvers] was showing me things, or, I guess, for lack of a better term, training me on things, almost all the time it was with patients in the room. So it wasn’t before – it wasn’t like we had a training session before I performed it. It was at that point in time he would walk me through it or showed me.” (Ibid.) Dr. Silvers attested that he trained Plaintiff on how to perform any technical supportive services and any service or task she did was under his supervision. (Silvers Decl., ¶¶ 17-20.) The Court finds that Defendants have sufficiently carried its burden in showing that the removal of sutures by Plaintiff would not have resulted in the violation of law to prove retaliation under Labor Code section 1102.5(c).  

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Dr. Silvers satisfied all the requirements necessary for Plaintiff to have completed the training required for removing sutures.  

 

Plaintiff asserts that to be eligible to perform such this service, at least four conditions must all be met, including that:

 

“(3) The medical assistant has completed the training specified in Sections 1366.2, 1366.3, and 1366.4 and has demonstrated competence in the performance of the service;” and

“(4) A record shall be made in the patient chart or other record…of each technical supportive service performed by the medical assistant, indicating the name, initials or other identifier of the medical assistant, the date and time, a description of the service performed, and the name of the physician or podiatrist who gave the medical assistant patient-specific authorization to perform the task....”

 

(16 C.C.R. § 1366(a).)

 

Under Section 1366.2, “Training to Perform Additional Technical Supportive Services” provides that:

“Such training shall be administered pursuant to either subsection (a)(1) or (a)(2) of Section 1366.3.” (16 C.C.R. § 1366.2.) Subsection (a)(1) provides that training may be administered “Under a licensed physician or podiatrist, who shall ascertain the proficiency of the medical assistant.” However, “The supervising physician or podiatrist, pursuant to subsection (a)(1)…shall certify in writing the place and date such training was administered, the content and duration of the training, and that the medical assistant was observed by the certifying physician, podiatrist, or instructor to demonstrate competence in the performance of each such task or service, and shall sign the certification.” (16 C.C.R. § 1366.3(b)(1).)

 

Plaintiffs assert that Defendants never kept any records on any of the alleged training he provided to Plaintiff, in violation of 16 C.C.R. § 1366(a). In support, Plaintiff cites to the deposition of Dr. Silvers. When asked whether Plaintiff’s training on removing sutures was documented anywhere, in writing or some other recorded form, Dr. Silvers states “No.” (Silvers Depo. 10/26/23, 39:24-40:7.) Thus, Dr. Silvers’ failure to certify in writing the date, place, content, or duration of any training he provided to Plaintiff would have made it a violation of 16 C.C.R. § 1366(a) for Plaintiff to perform the suture removal procedure. The Court finds this is sufficient to create a triable issue of material fact to prove retaliation under Labor Code section 1102.5(c).  

 

As such, summary adjudication for the fourth cause of action in the Complaint for retaliation in violation of Labor Code 1102.5 is DENIED.

 

e.      Fifth Cause of Action for Wrongful Termination

 

An employee has a common law right to sue for wrongful termination “when he or she is discharged for performing an act that public policy would encourage, or for refusing to do something that public policy would condemn.”  (Gantt v. Sentry Insurance (1992) 1 Cal.4th 1083, 1090 (overruled on other grounds).)  “‘The elements of a claim for wrongful discharge in violation of public policy are (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employer terminated the plaintiff’s employment, (3) the termination was substantially motivated by a violation of public policy, and (4) the discharge caused the plaintiff harm.’”  (Nosal-Tabor v. Sharp Chula Vista Medical Center (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1224, 1234-35 (quoting Yau v. Allen (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 154).)  “A discharge is actionable as against public policy if it violates a policy that is: ‘(1) delineated in either constitutional or statutory provisions; (2) ‘public’ in the sense that it ‘inures to the benefit of the public’ rather than serving merely the interests of the individual; (3) well established at the time of discharge; and (4) ‘substantial’ and ‘fundamental.’’”  (Id. at 1238-39 [quoting Carter v. Escondido Union High School District (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 922, 929].)   

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot establish that she was wrongfully terminated in violation of public policy because her termination was a direct result of her inadequate job performance. However, since triable issues of fact exist with regard to Plaintiff’s harassment and gender discrimination claims, triable issues of fact also exist with Plaintiff’s wrongful termination in violation of public policy claim. 

 

Thus, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the fifth cause of action. 

 

f.        Punitive Damages

 

A recovery of punitive damages under Civil Code § 3294 requires clear and convincing evidence that that an officer, director, or managing agent of the defendant acted with "oppression, malice, or fraud." (Code Civ. Proc., § 3294(b); Lacker v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210.) Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1) defines “malice” as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2) defines “oppression” as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” 

 

A managing agent is not merely a supervisory employee, but rather is limited to those “who exercise[] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal. 4th 563, 577.) Corporate policies are “the general principles which guide a corporation, or rules intended to be followed consistently over time in corporate operations” and a managing agent is “one with substantial authority over decisions that set these general principles and rules.” (Cruz v. Homebase (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 160, 167-68.) Under CCP § 437c(f)(l), a party may move for summary adjudication if the party contends that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code. (Catalano v. Superior Ct. (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 91, 96-97.) 

 

The FAC seeks punitive damages for each of its causes of action. Defendants argue that since Plaintiff’s claims fail, her punitive damages claim must also fail.

 

However, as discussed above, the Court finds triable issues of fact exist with regard to Plaintiff’s claims. Courts have found that evidence of pretext in a FEHA discrimination or retaliation case creates an inference of intent, meeting the punitive standard for both malice and oppression. (Cloud v. Casey (1999) 76 Cal. App.4th 895, 911-912 (intentional retaliation itself consists of a conscious disregard of another’s rights, thus is malicious, oppressive, and despicable conduct easily supporting punitive damages). Further, Plaintiff states Dr. Silvers has been the owner, CEO, and every corporate officer of BDMG since its incorporation. (AUMF 14.) Thus, since the Court finds triable issues of fact exist with regard to Plaintiff’s discrimination and retaliation claims, she has established a prima facie case for punitive damages.

 

As such, the Court DENIES the motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.

 

E.      Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

a.       Procedural Objections

 

As an initial matter, Plaintiff asserts that the Court should strike Defendants Separate Statement because it was served late.

 

The Court notes that the deadline for Defendants to file an opposition was February 13, 2024, i.e., 14 days before the hearing on February 27, 2024. (CCP § 437c(b)(2).) Defendants filed the Separate Statement in support of their opposition to Plaintiff’s MSA on February 15, 2024, and it is therefore late. The Court has the discretion to refuse to consider any evidence not timely submitted. (Mackey v. Bd. of Trustees of California State Univ. (2019) 31 Cal. App. 5th 640, 657.) Despite the Separate Statement being filed late, the Court will not exercise its discretion to strike and will consider it. 

 

b.      Issue Nos. 1-3: Defendants’ Affirmative Defenses

 

Plaintiff argues there is no merit to Defendants’ Seventeenth Affirmative Defense (Consent), Twentieth Affirmative Defense (Good faith), and Twenty-First Affirmative Defense (Essential Lawful Part of Business Operation).

 

Defendants’ Seventeenth Affirmative Defense states, in relevant part, “Defendant asserts that Plaintiff is barred on the ground that at all times alleged in the Complaint, Plaintiff expressly or impliedly assented to, ratified, or concurred with the conduct alleged to be unlawful.”

 

Defendants’ Twentieth Affirmative Defense states, in relevant part, “Defendant asserts that all actions taken by Defendant with respect to Plaintiff, at all times relevant to this action, were taken in good faith for legitimate non-discriminatory reasons.”

 

Defendants’ Twenty-First Affirmative Defense states, in relevant part, “Defendant asserts that Plaintiff is barred because the alleged conduct, if true, would be an essential lawful part of the Defendant’s business operations and/or consistent with industry practice.”

 

Plaintiff argues she has established as a matter of law that there is no merit to Defendants’ Seventeenth, Twentieth and Twenty-First Affirmative Defenses specifically as to the Plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action for Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code section 1102.5

 

However, “[a] motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 437(c)(f)(1). Here, Plaintiff’s Notice of MSA moves for a judicial determination of these affirmative defense only as to the Fourth Cause of Action for Retaliation. Plaintiff’s Notice of MSA would have needed to move for a judicial determination that the affirmative defenses can be resolved as to all causes of action alleged.  Because Plaintiff’s MSA would not completely dispose of the seventeenth, twentieth and twenty-first affirmative defenses, her MSA as to these affirmative defenses must therefore be denied.

 

As such, the Court DENIES the motion for summary adjudication as to Issue Nos. 1-3.

 

c.       Issue No. 4: Duty to Comply with California Law by Providing Plaintiff Training

 

Plaintiff seeks an order adjudicating that “Defendants owed her a duty to comply with California law by providing her with the required training, including documenting such training, before asking her to perform technical supportive services on patients.” (Notice of MSA, p. 2.)

 

Plaintiff asserts that “although the word “duty” does not appear in the relevant Code sections or regulations, Civil Code section 1714 imposes a general duty of care on a defendant only when it is the defendant who has ‘“created a risk”’ of harm to the plaintiff, including when ‘“the defendant is responsible for making the plaintiff's position worse.”’ (Brown v. USA Taekwando (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 214.)

 

Plaintiff performs an analysis of the factors from Rowland v. Christian, 69 Cal.2d 108, 113. However, Rowland only applies in situations that justify a departure from Civ. Code, section 1714. In Cabral v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2011) 51 Cal.4th 764, the Supreme Court summarized the so-called Rowland factors: “In the Rowland decision, this court identified several considerations that, when balanced together, may justify a departure from the fundamental principle embodied in Civil Code section 1714.” Rowland’s multifactor test “was not designed as a freestanding means of establishing duty, but instead as a means for deciding whether to limit a duty derived from other sources,” like Civil Code section 1714. (Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 217.)

 

In Kuciemba v. Victory Woodworks, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal. 5th 993, the court stated that Rowland’s multifactor test “was not designed as a freestanding means of establishing duty, but instead as a means for deciding whether to limit a duty derived from other sources,” like Civil Code section 1714. (Kuciemba, supra, 14 Cal. 5th at 1021.)  “As we have also explained, however, in the absence of a statutory provision establishing an exception to the general rule of Civil Code section 1714, courts should create one only where ‘clearly supported by public policy.’ ”[Citations].” (Ibid.) “The most important factor to consider in determining whether to create an exception to the general duty to exercise ordinary care articulated by [Civil Code] section 1714 is whether the injury in question was foreseeable.” (Ibid.) In making this assessment, the court must focus not on particularities of the defendant's conduct and the plaintiff's injury, but on “whether the category of negligent conduct at issue is sufficiently likely to result in the kind of harm experienced that liability may appropriately be imposed ....” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Plaintiff appears to argue that Civil Code § 1714 supports the proposition that Defendants owed Plaintiff the duty to train her properly and document that training before asking her to act as a medical assistant. However, Plaintiff does not point to a statutory provision establishing such a duty. Further, based on the Rowland factors, Plaintiff does not specify the injury in question that was foreseeable. Plaintiff merely states “it is extremely foreseeable that if Plaintiff were not properly trained, it could cause harm to her or others.” The Court does not find that Plaintiff has established Defendants owed Plaintiff the duty to train her properly and document that training before asking her to act as a medical assistant.

 

As such, the Court DENIES the motion for summary adjudication as to Issue No. 4.

 

d.      Issue No. 5: Duty to Not Retaliate

 

Plaintiff also seeks an order adjudicating that “Defendants owed Plaintiff a duty to not retaliate against her for refusing to participate in practices which would result in violations of California law.” (Notice of MSA, p. 2.)

 

Plaintiff argues that the duty to not retaliate is a statutory duty. Plaintiff cites to Labor Code section 1102.5, subdivision (c), which states that neither an employer nor anyone acting on the employer’s behalf may retaliate against an employee for refusing to participate in illegal conduct. Whistleblower protections reflect “the broad public policy interest in encouraging workplace whistle-blowers to report unlawful acts without fearing retaliation.” (Green v. Ralee Eng’g Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 66, 77.) Since Plaintiff cites to the statutory provision of Labor Code 1102.5(c) establishing that Defendants owed a duty to not retaliate, Plaintiff has met her burden.

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s MSA as to this duty must be denied because it would not completely dispose of any duty at issue or cause of action in this case. However, Plaintiff is only requesting the Court to grant summary adjudication as to the issue of duty only. Finding such a duty would completely dispose of the issue of whether the duty exists and should have been applied in this instance. Plaintiff still would need to prove the merits of her retaliation claims to prevail on those causes of action. Thus, the Court grants summary adjudication as to the issue of duty only.

 

As such, the Court GRANTS the motion for summary adjudication as to Issue No. 5.

 

F.       Plaintiff’s Motion for Order for Defendants to Produce Evidence of their Financial Condition Subject to Protective Order

 

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order that Defendants produce their current financial information consisting of their net worth and wealth subject to a protective order limiting the use of such information to the second phase of trial, pursuant to California Civil Code section 3295(c).

 

California Civil Code section 3295, subdivision (c), provides in pertinent part:

 

“[T]he court may at any time enter an order permitting the discovery otherwise prohibited by this subdivision if the court finds, on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294.”

 

Finding a substantial probability of success does “not require a weighing of evidence by the trial judge, but only a determination as to whether sufficient evidence ha[s] been presented to demonstrate a prima facie case [for punitive damages].” (Looney v. Super. Ct. (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 521, 538.)

 

Plaintiff argues that she has demonstrated a prima facie case for punitive damages. Plaintiff must present evidence of Defendants’ financial condition to provide a basis for an award of punitive damages. Thus, Plaintiff requests Defendants produce their current financial information consisting of their net worth and wealth subject to a protective order limiting the use of such information to the second phase of trial.

 

Defendants do not oppose the motion.  “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion or demurrer is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.”¿(Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.”¿(Id.) 

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff has demonstrated a prima facie case for punitive damages. In order for Plaintiff to base an award for punitive damages, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion subject to a protective order limiting the use of such information to the second phase of trial.

 

As such, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Order for Defendants to Produce Evidence of their Financial Condition Subject to Protective Order.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED as to Plaintiff’s first, second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action, and as to punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to Issue Nos. 1-4 and GRANTED as to Issue No. 5.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Order for Defendants to Produce Evidence of their Financial Condition Subject to Protective Order.

KIMI KIM v. JACOB WOOHER (23STCV26561)

Defendant’s Application for Order for Appearance and Examination – Enforcement of judgment Against Judgment Debtor is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Modification of the Amount of Attorneys Fees is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 31, 2023, Plaintiff Kimi Kim filed this lawsuit against Defendant Jacob Woocher alleging causes of action for:

C/A 2: Conversion Theft 

C/A 3: Legal Malpractice

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant lied, committed perjury and submitted falsified documents in his representation of an unlawful detainer action. The unlawful detainer action was filed by Plaintiff and Defendant provided legal representation to the defendants in that underlying action.

 

On December 20, 2023, Defendant filed a Special Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s Complaint pursuant to C.C.P Section 425.16 (Anti-Slapp).

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a request for dismissal of the entire action without prejudice.

 

On March 21, 2024, the Court granted the Motion for Attorneys Fees filed by Defendant. The Court stated in its Minute Order: “The court finds that Woocher would have prevailed on his anti-SLAPP motion as to the fraud cause of action. Woocher is therefore entitled to attorneys’ fees pursuant to section 415.16, subdivision (c).” (Court’s 03/21/24 Minute Order, p. 1.)

 

On April 11, 2024, the Court entered Judgment for Defendant against Plaintiff in the total amount of $5,565.00. 

 

On April 2, 2024, Defendant (now Judgment Creditor) filed an Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Plaintiff (now Judgment Debtor). 

 

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Modification of the Amount of Attorneys Fees.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Defendant’s Application and Order for Appearance and Examination

 

a.     Legal Standard

 

“The judgment creditor may apply to the proper court for an order requiring the judgment debtor to appear before the court, or before a referee appointed by the court, at a time and place specified in the order, to furnish information to aid in enforcement of the money judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (a).) 

 

“If the judgment creditor has not caused the judgment debtor to be examined under this section during the preceding 120 days, the court shall make the order upon ex parte application of the judgment creditor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (b).) 

 

“If the judgment creditor has caused the judgment debtor to be examined under this section during the preceding 120 days, the court shall make the order if the judgment creditor by affidavit or otherwise shows good cause for the order. The application shall be made on noticed motion if the court so directs or a court rule so requires. Otherwise, it may be made ex parte.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (c).) 

 

“The judgment creditor shall personally serve a copy of the order on the judgment debtor not less than 10 days before the date set for the examination. Service shall be made in the manner specified in Section 415.10. Service of the order creates a lien on the personal property of the judgment debtor for a period of one year from the date of the order unless extended or sooner terminated by the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (d).) 

 

“The order shall contain the following statement in 14-point boldface type if printed or in capital letters if typed: ‘NOTICE TO JUDGMENT DEBTOR. If you fail to appear at the time and place specified in this order, you may be subject to arrest and punishment for contempt of court and the court may make an order requiring you to pay the reasonable attorney’s fees incurred by the judgment creditor in this proceeding.’” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (e).)

 

b.     Discussion

 

Judgment Creditor applies for an order for Judgment Debtor to appear for examination. (Application, Items 1.a., 4, 5.a.) The Application form used is Judicial Council Form AT-138/EJ-125, and it includes the statutorily-required notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (e).) Judgment Creditor has a judgment against Judgment Debtor, and Judgment Creditor has not applied to have Judgment Debtor appear within the last 120 days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

The Court GRANTS Judgment Creditor’s Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Judgment Debtor.  

 

Judgment Creditor shall file proof of service no less than 72 hours in advance of the next hearing.  ¿ 

 

B.     Plaintiff’s Motion for Modification of Attorneys Fees

 

Plaintiff seeks an order modifying the amount of attorneys fees granted to Defendant on March 21, 2024. However, the Court finds the instant motion to modify attorneys fees is an improper motion for reconsideration.  (CCP § 1008.)   

 

The moving party must present new facts, circumstances, or law to meet its burden on a motion for reconsideration.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); see also Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.)  The party seeking reconsideration of an order shall state by affidavit what application was made before, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts or circumstances are claimed to be shown.  (Code Civ. Proc, § 1008.)  Further, “…the party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”  (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457.)  The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier.  (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)  A disagreement with a ruling is not a new fact that will support the granting of a motion for reconsideration.  (Id.) 

 

Here, the Court has already heard Defendant’s motion for attorneys fees. Plaintiff filed an opposition on March 19, 2024. After the motion was heard and argued, the Court granted Defendant’s motion on March 21, 2024. In the instant motion, Plaintiff does not present any new laws, facts, or circumstances that would justify reconsidering the Court's previous holding. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to modify attorneys fees is DENIED.

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

Judgment Creditor’s Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Judgment Debtor is GRANTED.  Judgment Creditor shall file proof of service no less than 72 hours in advance of the next hearing.

 

The Motion for Modification of the Amount of Attorneys Fees is DENIED.

RUBIO, et al. v. B.V. GENERAL, INC., et al. (23STCV29280)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Tom Allen (Allen Saltzman LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party: Thomas McAndrews (Reback, McAndrews, Blessey, LLP)

 

Demurrer to complaint (filed by Defendants Azmy Fouad Ghaly, M.D. and Senior Care Clinic on 02/09/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The demurrer to Plaintiffs’ second, third and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The motion to strike is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 6, 2023, Plaintiffs Anna E. Rubio (“Anna”), a deceased dependent adult, by and through her Successor in Interest, Ruby Pacheco; and Ruby Pacheco, individually, filed this action against Defendants B.V. General, Inc., and East Los Angeles Healthcare, LLC jointly doing business as Costa Del Sol Healthcare, Healthcare Management Services, LLC, Providence Group, Inc., Azmy Fouad Ghaly, M.D., and Senior Care Clinic.

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff Anna was harmed by Defendants during her residency at Costa Del Sol Healthcare, a nursing facility, which led to her wrongful death. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

            C/A 1: Non-Covid Dependent Adult Abuse/Neglect (First Count)

            C/A 2: Non-Covid Dependent Adult Abuse/Neglect (Second Count)

            C/A 3: Dependent Adult Abuse/Physical Abuse (Non-Covid Related)

            C/A 4: Non-Covid Negligence

            C/A 5: Dependent Adult Abuse/Financial (Wrongful Advantage)

            C/A 6: Violation of Resident’s Rights

            C/A 7: Wrongful Death

 

On May 13, 2024, Defendants Costa Del Sol Healthcare, Providence Group, Inc., B.V. General Inc., East Los Angeles Healthcare, LLC and Healthcare Management Services filed a motion to determine good faith settlement stating that they reached a settlement with Plaintiffs.

 

On February 9, 2024, Defendants Azmy Fouad Ghaly, M.D. and Senior Care Clinic filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike portions of the Complaint, arguing:

·       Defendants demur to Plaintiffs’ Second Cause of Action for Neglect on the grounds that Plaintiffs fail to state facts sufficient to constitute said cause of action against these Defendants

·       Defendants demur to Plaintiffs’ Third Cause of Action for Physical Abuse on the grounds that Plaintiffs fail to state facts sufficient to constitute said cause of action against these Defendants

·       Defendants demur to Plaintiffs’ Fifth Cause of Action for Financial Abuse on the grounds that Plaintiffs fail to state facts sufficient to constitute said cause of action against these Defendants.

·       Defendants move to strike any claim for punitive damages as well as all language supporting the same from Plaintiffs’ Complaint

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The Complaint adequately alleges Neglect

o   Plaintiffs allege that defendants failed to use the degree of care that a reasonable person in the same situation would have used

o   Defendants argue they are not liable for DA abuse absent a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship

§  However, Winn v. Pioneer Healthcare (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148 stated , it is the defendant’s relationship with the elder…-not the defendant’s professional standing or expertise – that makes the defendant potentially liable for neglect. Here, Plaintiffs allege defendants rendered and controlled her care in Costa del Sol's residential setting. While Anna was in Costa del Sol, defendants were required to initiate and control Anna's medical care precisely because she was an in-patient at Costa del Sol. Winn supports Anna's claims for elder abuse against defendants because they had the type of custodial control contemplated by §§ 15657 and 15610.57.

·       The Complaint adequately alleges Physical Abuse

o   Plaintiffs allege Anna was provided only between one quarter and 30% of her fluid needs and on several days she was provided no fluids at all. (Compl., ¶ 238.) She also received less than half of her food needs 69% of the time, and that she had no daily meals 39% of the time. (Compl., ¶ 239.)

·       The Complaint adequately alleges Financial Abuse

o   Plaintiffs allege defendants injured and damaged Anna and were overpaid by Medicare despite having done so. The injuries and damages caused by defendants required otherwise unnecessary additional care, for which Anna's Medicare account was charged. This allowed defendants to profit financially by obtaining Anna’s property in the form of her Medicare benefits despite damaging her, and defendants continue to retain those ill-acquired funds. Third, it is Anna, not defendants, who is forced by federal law to reimburse Medicare for the payments made to and kept by defendants.

·       Defendants’ Motion to Strike must be denied because it violates Rule 3.1322. Defendants’ motion fails to specify/quote the portions of the Complaint it seeks to strike.

Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs fail to provide facts showing oppression, fraud or malice to support their cause of action for Elder Abuse

·       Dr. Ghaly was not a care custodian, thus he cannot be held liable

o   In the case of Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc., supra, 63 Cal.4th 148, the California Supreme Court held that a physician cannot be held liable for elder abuse “unless the defendant healthcare provider has a caretaking or custodial relationship with the elder or dependent adult.” (Id. at 165.) Merely treating a patient in a hospital or skilled nursing facility does not give rise to such a relationship. There are no factual allegations showing that Dr. Ghaly stepped outside his role as a medical care provider and rendered or was responsible for rendering custodial care.

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he attempted to call Plaintiffs’ counsel to meet and confer regarding the instant motion. (Sverdlov Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) He states Plaintiffs’ counsel returned his call, but they were not able to meet and confer. (Ibid.) Counsel for Defendants then sent an email to Plaintiffs’ counsel and although Plaintiffs’ counsel responded, the parties did not meet and confer. (Ibid.) Here, the Court finds the moving party did not satisfy its obligation to meet and confer. While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

C.     Second and Third Causes of Action: Neglect and Physical Abuse

 

“The Elder Abuse Act's heightened remedies are available only in limited circumstances. A plaintiff must prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse under section 15610.63 or neglect under¿section 15610.57, and that the defendant committed the abuse with ‘recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.’ ” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 156.) “To recover the enhanced remedies available under the Elder Abuse Act from a health care provider, a plaintiff must prove more than simple or even gross negligence in the provider's care or custody of the elder.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 405 (“Carter”.) “In order to obtain the Act's heightened remedies, a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 789.) 

 

“Neglect” is defined as “[t]he negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.” (Welf. & Inst. Code section 15610.57, subd. (a)(1).)  “Neglect includes, but is not limited to, all of the following: [¶] (1) Failure to assist in personal hygiene, or in the provision of food, clothing, or shelter. [¶] (2) Failure to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs.... [¶] (3) Failure to protect from health and safety hazards. [¶] (4) Failure to prevent malnutrition or dehydration.” (Welf. & Inst. Code section 15610.57, subd. (b).) 

 

The Act defines “Physical Abuse”, in pertinent part, as follows: (d) Unreasonable physical constraint, or prolonged or continual deprivation of food or water. … or (f) Use of a physical or chemical restraint or psychotropic medication under any of the following conditions: (1) For punishment; (2) For a period beyond that for which the medication was ordered pursuant to the instructions of a physician and surgeon licensed in the State of California, who is providing medical care to the elder or dependent adult at the time the instructions are given; (3) For any purpose not authorized by the physician and surgeon. (Wels. & Inst. §15610.63)

Here, the Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiffs allege during the course of Anna’s admission at Costa Del Sol, Defendants knowingly deprived Anna of sufficient food and water to meet her nutritional and hydration needs for a period of months, thus allowing her to become profoundly dehydrated and malnourished. (Compl., ¶ 237.) Anna was provided only between one quarter and 30% of her fluid needs and on several days she was provided no fluids at all. (Compl., ¶ 238.) She also received less than half of her food needs 69% of the time, and that she had no daily meals 39% of the time. (Compl., ¶ 239.)

“The plaintiff must allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care [citations]; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs [citations]; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services¿necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult's basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury¿(if the plaintiff alleges recklessness) [citations]. The plaintiff must also allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at pp. 406-07.) 

 

The Court finds that the second and third causes of action in the Complaint against Defendants are insufficiently alleged. Plaintiffs failed to allege specific facts showing that Defendants acted with recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice. (Covenant Care, supra, 32 Cal.4th 771, 779.) Plaintiffs’ allegations that Defendants conduct is more than negligence is based solely on conclusions. “ ‘Facts, not conclusions, must be pleaded.’ [Citation.] Further, where, as here, statutory remedies are invoked, the facts ‘must be pleaded with particularity.’ ” (Carter, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at p. 410.) The Complaint concludes, without supporting factual allegations, that Defendants “acted with recklessness in physically abusing Anna.” (Compl., ¶ 234.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ second and third causes of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

D.     Fifth Cause of Action: Financial Abuse

 

“[F]inancial abuse of an elder . . . occurs when a person or entity does the following:”

 

(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.

 

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action is not adequately pled because the Complaint does not allege the wrongful use Plaintiffs’ property by Defendants.

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendants committed financial dependent adult abuse against Anna through reckless neglect, physical abuse, and/or negligent conduct which caused her injury. (Compl., ¶ 262.) These injuries increased both the required level and cost of medical and nursing care she required. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs are informed and believes that Defendants having increased the need for and level of Anna's care and treatment, then sought higher and additional reimbursement rates from Medicare for the enhanced level of care and treatment they and each of them claimed to have provided her. (Ibid.) Those higher reimbursement rates were paid to, received by, and retained exclusively by Defendants. (Ibid.)

Generally, to plead a cause of action, the complaint must contain a “statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 425.10(a).)¿ Although pleading conclusions of law does not fulfill this requirement, it has long been recognized that “[t]he distinction between conclusions of law and ultimate facts is not at all clear and involves at most a matter of degree.¿ For example, the courts have permitted allegations which obviously included conclusions of law and have termed them ‘ultimate facts' or ‘conclusions of fact.” ’ (Burks v. Poppy-Construction Co. (1962) 57 Cal.2d 463, 473.)¿ However, because causes of action for elder and dependent abuse are governed by a statute, the elements must be alleged with particularity. (See Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15600, et seq.; Intrieri v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 72, 82 [elder abuse elements are statutory]; Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790 [in general statutory claims must be alleged with particularity].) 

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ allegations are vague and fail to meet the required specificity requirement when alleging fraud. The Complaint alleges broadly that Defendants increased the cost of Anna’s treatments and sought higher reimbursement rates from Medicare, these reimbursements were then kept by Defendants. This fails to allege any detail regarding when this occurred, what the services were, or how much was allegedly fraudulently charged.  

 

Thus, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

E.     Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike a pleading or any part thereof.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)¿ The court¿may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)¿ The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)¿ An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., 431.10, subd. (b).)¿ The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿¿ 

 

Defendants move to strike “any claim for punitive damages as well as all language supporting the same from Plaintiffs’ Complaint.” (Motion, p. 2.)

 

However, Defendants fail to quote the portions of the Complaint they are moving to strike. Motions to strike are governed by Rule 3.1322 of the California Rules of Court. Rule 3.1322 subpart (a) states: "A notice of motion to strike a portion of a pleading must quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count, or defense." Since Defendants' motion fails to comply with this mandatory requirement, the Court denies Defendants motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, the motion to strike is denied.

 

F.      Conclusion

 

The demurrer to Plaintiffs’ second, third and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The motion to strike is DENIED.

APPLICATION AND ORDER FOR APPEARANCE AND EXAMINATION – ENFORCEMENT OF JUDGMENT AGAINST JUDGMENT DEBTOR (filed on 01/18/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

GRANTED

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 27, 2018, Plaintiffs Kimheng “Sam” Lao and Lily Chang filed this lawsuit against Defendant Kim Sharma alleging causes of action for:

C/A 1: Breach of Written Contract 

C/A 2: Breach of Oral Contract 

C/A 3: Promissory Fraud and Deceit 

 

Plaintiffs are married and own a produce distribution company.  Defendant Kim Sharma is the owner of an agricultural business called “Ms. Asia Fresh” or “Agrorincon S. De P.R. DE R.L.” On October 5, 2017, Plaintiffs and Defendant entered a promissory note agreement for Plaintiffs to lend Defendant $100,000 (and to allow Defendant to borrow further funds) in exchange for Defendant to ship quality produce and other sellable goods to Plaintiff’s company per separate invoices (deducting 30% of the total invoice as payment to the agreement).  In the event of default, Plaintiffs could terminate the agreement and demand payment of monies owed due within 7 business days. Interest on unpaid principal balance accrues at the rate of 1.5% per annum.  Between September 2017 through January 2018, Plaintiffs made 9 wire transfers to Defendant, totaling $155,000.  Between December 5, 2017 and January 22, 2018, Defendant made approximately 6 ships to Defendant, but nearly all goods were of such low quality that they were unsellable (thus in violation of the agreement).  No further shipments were made after January 22, 2018. On May 10, 2018, Plaintiffs sent a letter to Defendant, notifying her of the breach and demanding monies due and payable. 

 

On August 23, 2019, the Court entered Judgment for Plaintiffs and against Defendant in the total amount of $107,004.95. 

 

On January 18, 2024, Plaintiffs (now Judgment Creditor) filed the instant Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Defendant Sharma (now Judgment Debtor). 

 

No opposition or other response has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

“The judgment creditor may apply to the proper court for an order requiring the judgment debtor to appear before the court, or before a referee appointed by the court, at a time and place specified in the order, to furnish information to aid in enforcement of the money judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (a).) 

 

“If the judgment creditor has not caused the judgment debtor to be examined under this section during the preceding 120 days, the court shall make the order upon ex parte application of the judgment creditor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (b).) 

 

“If the judgment creditor has caused the judgment debtor to be examined under this section during the preceding 120 days, the court shall make the order if the judgment creditor by affidavit or otherwise shows good cause for the order. The application shall be made on noticed motion if the court so directs or a court rule so requires. Otherwise, it may be made ex parte.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (c).) 

 

“The judgment creditor shall personally serve a copy of the order on the judgment debtor not less than 10 days before the date set for the examination. Service shall be made in the manner specified in Section 415.10. Service of the order creates a lien on the personal property of the judgment debtor for a period of one year from the date of the order unless extended or sooner terminated by the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (d).) 

 

“The order shall contain the following statement in 14-point boldface type if printed or in capital letters if typed: ‘NOTICE TO JUDGMENT DEBTOR. If you fail to appear at the time and place specified in this order, you may be subject to arrest and punishment for contempt of court and the court may make an order requiring you to pay the reasonable attorney’s fees incurred by the judgment creditor in this proceeding.’” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (e).)

 

B.     Discussion

 

Judgment Creditor applies for an order for Judgment Debtor to appear for examination. (Application, Items 1.a., 4, 5.a.) The Application form used is Judicial Council Form AT-138/EJ-125, and it includes the statutorily-required notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (e).) Judgment Creditor has a judgment against Judgment Debtor, and Judgment Creditor has not applied to have Judgment Debtor appear within the last 120 days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

The Court GRANTS Judgment Creditor’s Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Judgment Debtor.  

 

Judgment Creditor shall file proof of service no less than 72 hours in advance of the next hearing.  ¿ 

 

C.     Conclusion

 

Judgment Creditor’s Application and Order for Appearance and Examination — Enforcement of Judgment against Judgment Debtor is GRANTED.  

 

Judgment Creditor shall file proof of service no less than 72 hours in advance of the next hearing.
