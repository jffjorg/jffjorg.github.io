Case Number: 22AVCV00548    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a motor vehicle personal injury action. Plaintiffs Jonathan Arevalo (“Arevalo”) and Kevin Arevalo Castillo (“Castillo” and collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that on or about November 16, 2020, they were passengers in a truck loaned/leased/rented by Defendant DTLA Trucks Center, Inc. (“DTLA Trucks”) operated by Defendant Duane Shawndell Butts (“Butts”) who worked for Defendant Hermes Moving and Storage dba CalState Moving and Storage (“Hermes”) when Driver fell asleep and collided with the guardrail located to the east of the highway twice. Plaintiffs present that Castillo was ejected thorough the front windshield and Averalo was partially ejected, causing both Plaintiffs injury to their body and emotional pain and suffering.  

 

On August 01, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging four causes of action for: (1) Negligence/Negligence Per Se (CCR § 1214; VC §§ 14601.1 and 21702) against Butts; (2) Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision/Training against Hermes; (3) Vicarious Liability against Hermes; and (4) Negligent Entrustment against DTLA Trucks.  

 

On September 12, 2022, Butts filed his Answer.  

 

On October 11, 2022, Plaintiffs amended the fictious name of Doe 1 to LA Moving Centre Inc., a California Corporation dba Calstate Moving and Storage (“LAMC”). The causes of action against Doe 1 are:  Negligence/Negligence Per Se (CCR § 1214; VC §§ 14601.1 and 21702), Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision/Training, Vicarious Liability, and Negligent Entrustment. 

 

On October 28, 2022, Hermes and LAMC and filed its Answer. 

 

On July 13, 2023, Plaintiffs amended the ficticious name of Doe 2 to Jamoliddin Sharifovich Yuldashev dba Calstate Moving & Storage (“Yuldashev”). The causes of action against Doe 2 are:  Negligence/Negligence Per Se (CCR § 1214; VC §§ 14601.1 and 21702), Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision/Training, Vicarious Liability, and Negligent Entrustment. 

 

On July 28, 2023, Yuldashev filed a Demurrer, subsequently overruled.

 

On September 06, 2023, Yuldashev filed his Answer.

 

On December 08, 2024, Hermes filed a Substitution of Attorney, substituting Miles Kavaller with James D. Lyon.

 

On February 08, 2024, Butts filed a Substitution of Attorney substituting himself, in pro per, with Daniel Alderman.

 

On February 13, 2024, the trial date set for March 08, 2024 was continued to September 13, 2024; the Final Status Conference (“FSC”) was continued to September 04, 2024; and all dates and deadlines imposed by the setting of trial, including the discovery and motion cut-off dates, became calculated by the new trial date.

 

May 16, 2024, LAMC and Yuldashev filed a Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

On May 21, 2024, Hermes filed its Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

On May 29, 2024, Butts filed his Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

The motion at hand is Hermes’ Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

On October 07, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition.

 

On October 08, 2024, Plaintiffs filed another Opposition, including a supplemental declaration from defense counsel Pavel Sterin indicating that there were errors in the previously filed documents.

 

On October 15, 2024, Hermes filed its Reply.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Summary Judgment – The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc.¿§437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67 (“Juge”), citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Cal.¿Code Civ.¿Proc.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v.¿Paetkau¿(1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿¿¿¿¿¿

Discussion

 

Application – The causes of action brought against Hermes are: (1) Second Cause of Action (Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision); and (2) Third Cause of Action (Vicarious Liability).

 

The gravamen of Hermes’ moving arguments is that Butts is not an employee of Hermes, but LAMC. That is, the alleged incident occurred when Butts was in the course and scope of his employment with LAMC and, as such, there can be no liability placed on Hermes.

 

Hermes relies on the following evidence to show that Butts was LAMC’s employee: Exhibit "2" - Trip Computer Printout, Yuldashev’s declaration at paragraphs four through six, and eight through nine, and Edward J. Morales (“Morales”)’ declaration at paragraph 8. (See Hermes’ Separate Statement at No. 1.)

 

Exhibit 2 shows order information for a LAMC job on November 15, 2020. It details the number of trucks, price, customer information, gas fees, distances, and other pertinent information for the order. It does not detail Hermes’ employees or the driver. Exhibit 2 is inapposite as it does not provide any information on Butts’ employment. 

 

Yuldashev’s declaration describes his ownership in both Hermes and LAMC, details the business information of both Hermes and LAMC, states that Hermes was not involved, and presents that Butts and Plaintiffs were employees of LAMC. (See Decl. Yuldashev, ¶¶ 4-6, 8-9.) Yuldashev’s declaration is signed under the penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California.

 

Morales is counsel of record for Hermes. He declares at paragraph eight that Butts was employed by LAMC and this fact is established by Yuldashev’s declaration.

 

Yuldashev’s declaration is sufficient to meet Hermes’ initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense as both causes of action directed against Hermes requires an employer-employee or agent-principal relationship. (See Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons (1986) 41 Cal.3d 962, 967-970; Roman Catholic Bishop v. Superior Court (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1556, 1564–1565; Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1054; Lisa M. v. Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital (1995) 12 Cal.4th 291, 296-297.)  The burden shifts to Plaintiffs.

 

Plaintiffs present that Yuldashev owns, manages, and controls both Hermes and LAMC. Specifically, Yuldashev is the Chief Financial Officer of LAMC and the sole shareholder, officer, and director of Hermes. Plaintiffs present that the companies use their names interchangeably. (See Plaintiffs’ UMF No. 28 [LAMC’s Special Interrogatory No. 21 response states that no one uses the Hermes name to do business, but both Hermes and LAMC use the name Calstate Moving and Storage; U.S. Department of Transportation (“DOT”) Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration Licensing and Insurance page shows that Hermes does business as Calstate Moving and Storage; California’s Public Utility Commission details for LAMC show that the carrier’s DBA is Hermes].) Plaintiffs also dispute Yuldashev’s presentation of the business information provided (i.e., intra state v. interstate services); however, this does not impact the analysis of Butts’ employment. Because of the presented facts, Plaintiffs argue that Butts was under the direct control of Yuldashev. Plaintiffs further argue that (1) Butts admitted that he was an employee of Hermes in his Answer and it is a settled law that any statements in plaintiff’s complaint that were not denied by defendant will be taken as true for the purposes of the case; (2) Butts’ Application for Employment does not distinguish an employer name (Plaintiffs’ UMF No. 20), (3) Yuldashev sought liability coverage from Hermes’ liability insurer (Plaintiff’s UMF Nos. 31-32), and (4) Yuldashev admitted that Butts was an employee of Hermes in his Reply to Plaintiffs’ Opposition to the Demurrer.

 

The Court notes that the argument that Butts was under the direct control of Yuldashev is attached to a statement that reads, “Now Defendant BUTTS deceitfully misrepresents that he was employed by LAMC.” The Court clarifies that this is Hermes’ Motion for Summary Judgment and Hermes’ arguments, not Butts’.

 

The Court also notes that the legal standard in a demurrer is distinguished from a motion for summary judgment. Yuldashev’s Reply, which was untimely and not considered by the Court, followed the legal standard for a demurrer by admitting the truth of all factual, material allegations; acknowledged Plaintiffs’ own pleadings which allege that all defendants are alter-egos or successors of each other and were to be treated as a single entity; and provided argument under that assumption.

 

Regarding Butts’ Answer, Butts has admitted all allegations within the Complaint from paragraphs 30 through 37. This includes the allegation that “PLAINTIFFS incorporate herein by reference each and every allegation contained in the preceding paragraphs as though the same were set forth in full herein.” (See Complaint ¶ 30.) As such, Butts has admitted that all defendants are alter-egos and/or successors of each other. (See Owens v. White Memorial Hospital (1956) 138 Cal.App.2d 634, 641-42 [citing to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 462.].)

 

As such, Plaintiffs have shown that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists (i.e., Butts’ employment based on Butts’ own admission; and whether Hermes, LAMC, and Yuldashev are to be treated as one entity for the purposes of the determination of Butts’ employment) as to the causes of action brought against Hermes.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

-----

 

Reply

 

Hermes’ Reply states that Yuldashev’s Reply to the Opposition to the Demurrer does state what Plaintiffs were presenting; however, it directly quotes from Plaintiffs’ Opposition and, as such, Yuldashev’s Reply was addressing Plaintiffs’ statements and there was no agreement or stipulation between Butts and Plaintiffs as to the employer of Butts. Next, Hermes presents that Plaintiffs have submitted an objectionable supplementary declaration admitting their erroneous understanding of the matter and Paragraphs 30 through 37 of the Complaint that Butts’ has admitted do not contain any allegations other than negligence. Hermes argues that Plaintiff’s evidence is based on a lack of evidence (i.e., application does not state name of employer) and does not meet the standard, Plaintiffs cannot rely on their own pleadings, and Butts’ Answer is not a judicial admission as Paragraphs 31 through 37 contain nothing relating to employment and Paragraph 30, which incorporates Paragraphs One through 29, alleges only a belief that Butts was employed by Hermes. Further, Hermes argues that the truth of the incorporation of allegation in paragraphs One through 29 is not considered a waiver.

 

The Court believes that it has addressed a majority of Hermes’ Reply arguments in its analysis, ante. As to whether Butts’ admission of Paragraph 30, which incorporates Paragraphs One through 29, Hermes cites to the legal standard regarding waiver, but does not provide an argument as to why “the truth of the incorporated allegations. . .cannot be treated [as] a waiver of a proof of fact and the concession of its truth in this motion.” (See Reply 6:4-5.)


Accordingly, the Reply does not impact the Court’s analysis, ante, and the Court’s opinion remains unchanged.

Conclusion

 

Defendant Hermes Moving and Storage’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

Case Number: 22AVCV01032    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This action arises from an incident that occurred on or about September 08, 2022 when Minor Claimant Jane JE Doe (“Minor Claimant”) and her classmates were left unsupervised on the campus of Sunnydale in an outdoor area. Defendant Lancaster School District (“Defendant”) is alleged to be responsible for the supervision and safety of the students comprising their student population.

 

On December 12, 2022, Minor Claimant, through her guardian ad litem, Jane BE Doe, filed a complaint against Defendant alleging three causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Supervision; and (3) Negligent Failure to Warn, Train, or Educate.

 

On September 13, 2024, Petitioner Jane BE Doe (“Petitioner”) filed a petition to approve the compromise of pending action on behalf of Minor Claimant.

 

Analysis

 

Standard for Approving Minor’s Compromises – Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim or that of a person lacking the capacity to make decisions.  (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372; see Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1337.)

 

“[T]he protective role the court generally assumes in cases involving minors, [is] a role to assure that whatever is done is in the minor’s best interests . . . .  [I]ts primary concern is whether the compromise is sufficient to provide for the minor’s injuries, care and treatment.”  (Goldberg v. Superior Court (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1382.) 

 

A petition for court approval of a compromise or covenant not to sue under Code of Civil Procedure section 372 must comply with California Rules of Court Rules 7.950, 7.951, and 7.952.  The petition must be verified by the petitioner and contain a full disclosure of all information that has “any bearing upon the reasonableness” of the compromise or the covenant.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.)  The person compromising the claim on behalf of the minor or person who lacks capacity, and the represented person, must attend the hearing on compromise of the claim unless the court for good cause dispenses with their personal appearance.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.952(a).)

 

An order for deposit of funds of a minor or person lacking decision-making capacity and a petition for the withdrawal of such funds must comply with California Rules of Court Rules 7.953 and 7.954.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1384; see also Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rules 4.115-4.118.)

 

-----

 

The petition shows that Minor Claimant settled with Defendant for a total amount of $75,000. $0.00 will be used to pay medical expenses, $18,475.62 will be used to pay attorney’s fees, and $6,524.38 will be used to pay non-medical expenses, leaving a balance of $50,000.00 for Minor Claimant. Petitioner proposes the net proceeds be deposited into blocked accounts on behalf of the minor claimants.

 

The Court finds the settlement to be fair and reasonable.  The Court also finds the requested attorney’s fees, which constitute 25% of the gross settlement amount to be reasonable.

 

Conclusion

 

The petition to approve compromise of pending action on behalf of Minor Claiamnt Jane JE Doe is GRANTED.
