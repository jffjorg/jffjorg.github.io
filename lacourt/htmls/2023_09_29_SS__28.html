DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 20STCV04316    Hearing Date: September 29, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposing and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On February 3, 2020, Plaintiff Eusebio Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Bigge Crane and Rigging Co. (“Bigge”) and Does 1-50 for negligence. 

On March 25, 2020, Bigge filed an answer. 

On June 24, 2020, Plaintiff amended the complaint to include Defendant Mr. Crane, Inc. as Doe 21 (“Mr. Crane”). 

On July 27, 2020, Mr. Crane filed an answer. 

On July 13, 2023, Mr. Crane filed a motion for a protective order to be heard on September 29, 2023.  On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On September 25, 2023, Mr. Crane filed a reply. 

Trial is currently scheduled for January 19, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Mr. Crane requests that the Court grant a protective order preventing the deposition of Mr. Crane’s executives, Robert Schiller (“Schiller”) and Andy Steinberg (“Steinberg”). 

Plaintiff requests that the Court deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

          Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420 provides in part: 

“(a) Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order.  The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(b) The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: 

“(1) That the deposition not be taken at all. 

* * *

 (g) If the motion for a protective order is denied in whole or in part, the court may order that the deponent provide or permit the discovery against which protection was sought on those terms and conditions that are just. 

(h) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subds. (a), (b)(1), (g), (h).) 

“[W]hen a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president or other official at the highest level of corporate management, and that official moves for a protective order to prohibit the deposition, the trial court should first determine whether the plaintiff has shown good cause that the official has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information. If not, as will presumably often be the case in the instance of a large national or international corporation, the trial court should issue the protective order and first require the plaintiff to obtain the necessary discovery through less intrusive methods. These would include interrogatories directed to the high-level official to explore the state of his or her knowledge or involvement in plaintiff's case; the deposition of lower level employees with appropriate knowledge and involvement in the subject matter of the litigation; and the organizational deposition of the corporation itself, which will require the corporation to produce for deposition the most qualified officer or employee to testify on its behalf as to the specified matters to be raised at the deposition. [Citation.] Should these avenues be exhausted, and the plaintiff make a colorable showing of good cause that the high-level official possesses necessary information to the case, the trial court may then lift the protective order and allow the deposition to proceed.”  (Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1289 (“Liberty Mutual”).)  

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff alleges that he fell while working at a construction site as an employee of Earthscapes, which provided services to Mr. Crane on site.  At the time of the accident, Plaintiff was assigned to assist Mr. Crane’s certified rigger and signalman, Mike Dillard (“Dillard”), by hooking large bags of soil to the crane rigging so they could be hoisted to upper floors. Plaintiff contends that he lacked the qualifications to provide this assistance because he was not a certified rigger.  He alleges that one of the bags knocked him off a flatbed trailer onto a metal swing stage, injuring him. 

Plaintiff took the deposition of Matthew Fruhling, Mr. Crane’s “person most qualified” regarding the company’s policies for hiring, using and/or recruiting uncertified persons to assist with rigging operations.  Fruhling testified “I don’t know if they [Mr. Crane] have any policy” saying non-qualified people are not allowed to help a qualified rigger at a site.  Asked, “Do you know who would have knowledge of the policies and practices there at Mr. Crane?,” Fruhling answered that he did not know for sure, but “Robert Schiller would be someone I would take a good guess on.”  In response to the question, “Anybody else?,” Fruhling answered, “Andy Steinberg.” 

Mr. Crane now asks the Court to grant a protective order to prevent Plaintiff from deposing Schiller and Steinberg.  Mr. Crane argues Plaintiff has not shown that Schiller and Steinberg have unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information as required by Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at page 1289.

Fruhling’s deposition testimony that he had a “good guess” that Schiller and Steinberg knew about Mr. Crane’s policies and practices satisfies Plaintiff’s burden under Liberty Mutual, entitling Plaintiff to propound discovery to Schiller and Steinberg on the issue of policies and practices at Mr. Crane. 

Plaintiff may not, however, depose Schiller and Steinberg without first exhausting less intrusive discovery methods such as interrogatories.  If and when these less restrictive avenues are exhausted, and Plaintiff makes “a colorable showing of good cause that the high-level official possesses necessary information to the case,” the Court will lift the protective order and allow the depositions to proceed.  (Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at p. 1289.) 

The Court grants the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Mr. Crane, Inc.’s motion for a protective order and orders that Plaintiff Eusebio Hernandez may not take the depositions of Defendant’s executives Robert Schiller or Andy Steinberg without first (1) exhausting less intrusive discovery methods and (2) making a colorable showing that Schiller and/or Steinberg possess information necessary to Plaintiff’s case. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 20STCV36707    Hearing Date: September 29, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the petitioning papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On September 24, 2020, Plaintiff Caleb Chevy Bardling-Velasco, by and through his guardian ad litem Allena Velasco (“Plaintiff”), filed a complaint against Defendants Bandini Street Elementary School, Los Angeles Unified School District, LA’s Best, and Does 1-100. The complaint alleged (1) vicarious liability under Government Code sections 815, 815.2, 815.4, 815.6 and 820, (2) breach of mandatory duty under Government Code section 815.6, and (3) general negligence. 

On March 24, 2021, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint against Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 alleging liability under Government Code sections 815, 815.2, 815.4, 815.6 and 820. 

On April 26, 2021, Defendant filed an answer. 

On July 18, 2022, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication. 

On May 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement. 

On September 6, 2023, Petitioner Allena Velasco (“Petitioner”) filed a petition to approve minor’s compromise to be heard on September 29, 2023. 

PARTY’S REQUEST 

Petitioner asks the Court approve the petition to compromise Plaintiff Caleb Chevy Bardling-Velasco’s disputed claim. 

DISCUSSION 

Section 12a of the petition states that Plaintiff incurred $1,565.37 in medical expenses, none of which has been paid or subject to reduction.  Section 12a also states that these medical expenses will not be paid or reimbursed using the settlement proceeds.  Section 12a does not list any liens. 

However, Section 12b(5) of the petition states that the $1,565.37 in medical expenses have been paid.  The petition does not explain who paid these expenses or whether settlement funds will be used to reimburse the person or entity that paid this amount. 

The Court cannot approve a minor’s compromise unless it reimburses all medical expenses that have not been reduced or waived.  Petitioner should revise the petition to address the discrepancies noted above and to make sure that all liens are resolved prior to seeking Court approval. 

For the reasons stated above, the Court denies the petition. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES the petition to approve the compromise of minor Caleb Chevy Bardling-Velasco’s claims, filed by Petitioner Allena Velasco. 

Petitioner is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Petitioner is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 21STCV26659    Hearing Date: September 29, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On July 20, 2021, Plaintiff Maricel Pitcher (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Joseph Fahr, D.D.S., doing business as Beautiful Smith Dental Center, and Does 1-10 for dental malpractice. 

On September 7, 2021, Defendant, Joseph Peyman Fahr, D.D.S. (erroneously named and sued as Joseph Fahr, DDS, doing business as Beautiful Smile Dental Center) (“Defendant”) filed an answer. 

On April 28, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s motions to compel further discovery responses.  The Court ordered Plaintiff to serve further discovery responses and pay Defendant $2,600 in sanctions by May 26, 2022. 

On July 6, 2022, the Court found that Plaintiff had not fully complied with the Court’s April 28, 2022, order.  The Court ordered Plaintiff to provide verified responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 12 and 14 and code-compliant responses to Requests for Production Nos. 1, 3, 4, and 11 (indicating that Plaintiff has made a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry to locate the items requested (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230)) by July 21, 2022.  In addition, the Court ordered Plaintiff and her counsel to pay Defendant $2,510 in sanctions by August 6, 2022. 

On August 29, 2023, Defendant filed a motion for monetary sanctions to be heard on September 29, 2023.  Plaintiff has not filed an opposition. 

Trial is currently set for March 28, 2024. 

PARTY’S REQUEST 

Defendant requests that the Court impose sanctions of $1,500.00 on Plaintiff based on Plaintiff’s failure to pay previous sanction awards. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure section 177.5 provides: 

“A judicial officer shall have the power to impose reasonable money sanctions, not to exceed fifteen hundred dollars ($1,500), notwithstanding any other provision of law, payable to the court, for any violation of a lawful court order by a person, done without good cause or substantial justification. This power shall not apply to advocacy of counsel before the court. For the purposes of this section, the term ‘person’ includes a witness, a party, a party’s attorney, or both.” 

“Sanctions pursuant to this section shall not be imposed except on notice contained in a party’s moving or responding papers; or on the court’s own motion, after notice and opportunity to be heard. An order imposing sanctions shall be in writing and shall recite in detail the conduct or circumstances justifying the order.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 177.5.) 

In 20th Century Ins. Co. v. Choong (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1274, the Court of Appeal rejected the argument that the trial court abused its discretion by assessing $250 in sanctions against a party for failing to pay a previously-issued sanctions award.  The appellate court explained that “the purpose of the second sanction was not to collect the first, but to penalize [the party] for ignoring lawful orders of the court. The court had every right to impose a monetary sanction to compel obedience to its lawful orders, or to punish disobedience and disrespect of the court's processes [citation].”  (Id. at p. 1278.) 

DISCUSSION 

On April 28, 2022, the Court ordered Plaintiff to pay Defendant $2,600.00 in sanctions by May 26, 2022. Plaintiff has not paid these sanctions and has not shown good cause or substantial justification for the failure to pay these amounts.  Defendant requests that the Court impose additional sanctions to penalize Plaintiff for disobeying the Court’s April 28, 2022 order. 

The Court grants the request and orders Plaintiff to pay the Court $500 in sanctions by October 30, 2023. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Joseph Fahr D.D.S.’s motion for sanctions and orders Plaintiff Maricel Pitcher to pay $500 in sanctions to the Los Angeles County Superior Court by October 30, 2023. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 21STCV44910    Hearing Date: September 29, 2023    Dept: 28

Having considered the documents submitted in support of a default judgment, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On December 9, 2021, Plaintiff State Farm General Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Nabu Hernandez (“Defendant”) and Does 1-100 for subrogation and indebtedness.  The complaint requested $129,301.85 in damages. 

On November 29, 2022, the clerk entered default against Defendant. 

On March 13, 2023, the Court dismissed the Doe defendants without prejudice at Plaintiff’s request. 

On September 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed a request for Court judgment against Defendant to be heard on September 29, 2023. 

PARTY’S REQUEST 

Plaintiff requests that the Court enter a default judgment against Defendant Nabu Hernandez and award Plaintiff $129,833.35, consisting of $129,301.85 as the demand of the complaint, $0.00 in prejudgment interest, $0.00 in attorney’s fees, and $531.50 in costs. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.      Default judgment 

“[With exceptions that do not apply here,] [a] party seeking a default judgment on declarations must use mandatory Request for Entry of Default (Application to Enter Default) (form CIV-100) . . . The following must be included in the documents filed with the clerk: 

“(1)  Except in unlawful detainer cases, a brief summary of the case identifying the parties and the nature of plaintiff's claim; 

“(2)  Declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; 

“(3)  Interest computations as necessary; 

“(4)  A memorandum of costs and disbursements; 

“(5)  A declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; 

“(6)  A proposed form of judgment; 

“(7)  A dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; 

“(8)  Exhibits as necessary; and 

“(9)  A request for attorney fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.” 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a).) 

B.       Damages 

          On a request for default judgment, “[w]here a cause of action is stated in the complaint, plaintiff merely needs to introduce evidence establishing a prima facie case for damages.”  (L. Edmon & C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2023) ¶ 5:213.1, p. 5-56 (Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure), citing Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361 [trial court erred in applying preponderance of the evidence standard].) 

          The relief granted to a plaintiff upon entry of a defendant's default cannot exceed the amount demanded in the complaint or, for personal injury cases where damages may not be stated in the complaint, the amount listed in the statement of damages. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 580, subd. (a), 585, subd. (b).) “The notice requirement of section 580 was designed to insure fundamental fairness.” (Becker v. S.P.V. Construction Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 489, 494 (Becker).) The statute insures that “defendants in cases which involve a default judgment have adequate notice of the judgments that may be taken against them. [Citation.] ‘If a judgment other than that which is demanded is taken against him, [the defendant] has been deprived of his day in court—a right to a hearing on the matter adjudicated.’ ’’ (Id. at p. 493.) A trial court exceeds its jurisdiction if it awards damages in excess of the amount specified in the complaint or statement of damages. (Id. at p. 494; see Cal. Practice Guide: Procedure, supra, ¶ 5:258, p. 5-70.) 

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff has submitted a completed packet with all necessary evidence. The Court grants the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff State Farm General Insurance Company’s application for default judgment filed on September 18, 2023.  The Court enters judgment of $129,833.35 against Defendant Nabu Hernandez. 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
