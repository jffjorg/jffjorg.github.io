DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01158    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: W

FAUSTINO PACHECO MORALES V. RAVIT OHANA, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS’ PETITION TO RELEASE STALE MECHANICS’ LIEN AND REQUEST FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

Date of Hearing:        May 14, 2024                                                Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        22VECV01159

 

Moving Party:            Defendants/Cross-Complainants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales and Defendants Ravit and Sharon Ohana entered into an agreement whereby Plaintiff was to transport dirt from one of Defendants’ properties to another. Plaintiff submitted invoices for payments; however, Defendants have failed to pay.

 

On August 15, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for breach of contract, common counts, and quantum meruit. 

 

Request for entry of default was entered February 14, 2023. The court then denied Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment. Plaintiff then sought request for entry of default again on June 12, 2023.

 

On April 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. Request for entry of default was again entered June 12, 2023 for Ravit Ohana and June 15, 2023 for Sharon Ohana. The court entered default judgment on November 9, 2023. On March 13, 2024, the court set aside the entry of default and default judgment.

 

Ravit and Sharon Ohana filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for violation of Business and Professions Code §7031 on March 25, 2024.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Petition to Release Stale Mechanics’ Lien and Request for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana will petition the Court for: (1) an order releasing the stale mechanics lien recorded by Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales; and (2) an order awarding Defendants $4,380.00 in attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Civil Code § 8488.

 

After a mechanic’s lien has been recorded, “[t]he owner of property or the owner of any interest in property subject to a claim of lien may petition the court for an order to release the property from the claim of lien if the claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the lien within the time provided in Section 8460.”¿ (Civ. Code, § 8480(a).)¿ A claimant must commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days of recording the lien, otherwise, “the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.”¿ (Civ. Code, § 8460(a).)¿ However, the 90-day time limit to commence an action to enforce a lien does not apply if there was an agreement to extend credit and a notice of that fact was recorded within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien or more than 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien but before a purchaser or encumbrancer for value and in good faith acquires rights in the property.¿ (Civ. Code, § 8460(b).)¿ 

 

Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana seeks an order releasing the Mechanics’ Lien from real property located at 4806 Topeka Avenue, Tarzana, California 91356 (the “Property”).

 

Plaintiff recorded the Mechanics’ Lien against the Property on February 3, 2022.  (See Petition, Exh. 3.)  Plaintiff thereafter failed to bring an action to enforce the Mechanics’ Lien within 90 days, as required under Civil Code section 8460.

 

On August 12, 2022, Defendants sent Plaintiff a written demand to release the Mechanics’ Lien.  (See Petition, Exhs. 1-2.)  Following a discussion with Plaintiff’s counsel, Plaintiff’s filed their complaint on August 15, 2022. This is six months from the recordation of the claim lien, well over the 90 day requirement.   

 

As Plaintiff failed to commence an action to enforce the Mechanics’ Lien within the mandatory 90-day window set forth under Civil Code section 8460, Defendants are entitled to an order releasing the Mechanics’ Lien from the Property.  (See Civ. Code, § 8460 (a).)  The Petition will be granted, and the Mechanics’ Lien will be released.

 

Petitioner is entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys' fees.  (See Civ. Code, § 8488 (c) (prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees); see PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1096 (court awards reasonable attorneys’ fees according to the lodestar).)

 

Here, counsel for Defendants states that their rate is $450.00/hour and that she spent 6.1 hours researching and drafting the petition, anticipates 2.5 hours reviewing and analyzing Plaintiff’s opposition, 1 hour appearing for the hearing, and spent $60 on filing fees, for a total of $4,380.00.

 

The court award the attorney fees in the reduced amount of, $3,255.00. No opposition has been filed.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ petition for release of mechanics’ lien from real property located at 4806 Topeka Avenue, Tarzana, California 91356 recorded on February 3, 2022, by Plaintiff is GRANTED and the mechanics’ lien is hereby RELEASED.

 

Defendants are AWARDED its costs and reasonable attorneys’ fees against Plaintiff in the total amount of $3,255.00.  These sanctions are payable within 30 days of this order.

FAUSTINO PACHECO MORALES V. RAVIT OHANA, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS’ PETITION TO RELEASE STALE MECHANICS’ LIEN AND REQUEST FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

Date of Hearing:        May 14, 2024                                                Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        22VECV01159

 

Moving Party:            Defendants/Cross-Complainants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales and Defendants Ravit and Sharon Ohana entered into an agreement whereby Plaintiff was to transport dirt from one of Defendants’ properties to another. Plaintiff submitted invoices for payments; however, Defendants have failed to pay.

 

On August 15, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for breach of contract, common counts, and quantum meruit. 

 

Request for entry of default was entered February 14, 2023. The court then denied Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment. Plaintiff then sought request for entry of default again on June 12, 2023.

 

On April 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. Request for entry of default was again entered June 12, 2023 for Ravit Ohana and June 15, 2023 for Sharon Ohana. The court entered default judgment on November 9, 2023. On March 13, 2024, the court set aside the entry of default and default judgment.

 

Ravit and Sharon Ohana filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for violation of Business and Professions Code §7031 on March 25, 2024.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Petition to Release Stale Mechanics’ Lien and Request for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana will petition the Court for: (1) an order releasing the stale mechanics lien recorded by Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales; and (2) an order awarding Defendants $4,380.00 in attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Civil Code § 8488.

 

After a mechanic’s lien has been recorded, “[t]he owner of property or the owner of any interest in property subject to a claim of lien may petition the court for an order to release the property from the claim of lien if the claimant has not commenced an action to enforce the lien within the time provided in Section 8460.”¿ (Civ. Code, § 8480(a).)¿ A claimant must commence an action to enforce a lien within 90 days of recording the lien, otherwise, “the claim of lien expires and is unenforceable.”¿ (Civ. Code, § 8460(a).)¿ However, the 90-day time limit to commence an action to enforce a lien does not apply if there was an agreement to extend credit and a notice of that fact was recorded within 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien or more than 90 days after recordation of the claim of lien but before a purchaser or encumbrancer for value and in good faith acquires rights in the property.¿ (Civ. Code, § 8460(b).)¿ 

 

Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana seeks an order releasing the Mechanics’ Lien from real property located at 4806 Topeka Avenue, Tarzana, California 91356 (the “Property”).

 

Plaintiff recorded the Mechanics’ Lien against the Property on February 3, 2022.  (See Petition, Exh. 3.)  Plaintiff thereafter failed to bring an action to enforce the Mechanics’ Lien within 90 days, as required under Civil Code section 8460.

 

On August 12, 2022, Defendants sent Plaintiff a written demand to release the Mechanics’ Lien.  (See Petition, Exhs. 1-2.)  Following a discussion with Plaintiff’s counsel, Plaintiff’s filed their complaint on August 15, 2022. This is six months from the recordation of the claim lien, well over the 90 day requirement.   

 

As Plaintiff failed to commence an action to enforce the Mechanics’ Lien within the mandatory 90-day window set forth under Civil Code section 8460, Defendants are entitled to an order releasing the Mechanics’ Lien from the Property.  (See Civ. Code, § 8460 (a).)  The Petition will be granted, and the Mechanics’ Lien will be released.

 

Petitioner is entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys' fees.  (See Civ. Code, § 8488 (c) (prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees); see PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1096 (court awards reasonable attorneys’ fees according to the lodestar).)

 

Here, counsel for Defendants states that their rate is $450.00/hour and that she spent 6.1 hours researching and drafting the petition, anticipates 2.5 hours reviewing and analyzing Plaintiff’s opposition, 1 hour appearing for the hearing, and spent $60 on filing fees, for a total of $4,380.00.

 

The court award the attorney fees in the reduced amount of, $3,255.00. No opposition has been filed.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ petition for release of mechanics’ lien from real property located at 4806 Topeka Avenue, Tarzana, California 91356 recorded on February 3, 2022, by Plaintiff is GRANTED and the mechanics’ lien is hereby RELEASED.

 

Defendants are AWARDED its costs and reasonable attorneys’ fees against Plaintiff in the total amount of $3,255.00.  These sanctions are payable within 30 days of this order.

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV05660    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: W

CONNIE BROCKMAN A/K/A CONSTANCE BROCKMAN, INDIVIDUALLY AND AS TRUSTEE OF THE BROCKMAN TRUST DATED OCTOBER 24, 2000, AND AS TR v. KRAVETZ CAPITAL FUNDING, LLC, et al.

 

plaintiff’s WRIT OF ATTACHMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        May 14, 2024                         Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV05660

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Connie Brockman, The Brockman Trust Dated October 24, 2000, Provident Trust Group, LLC FBO: Connie Brockman Solo 401k, and Adam and Charity Brockman Trust Dated 9-28-07

Responding Party:     Defendants Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC, Norman J. Kravetz and Diversified Business Operations

BACKGROUND

 

On December 22, 2023, Plaintiffs Connie Brockman a/k/a Constance Brockman (“Mrs. Brockman”), an individual and trustee of The Brockman Trust Dated October 24, 2000 (“Brockman Trust”), and trustee of the Provident Trust Group, LLC FBO: Connie Brockman Solo 401K (“Provident”); and Adam Brockman as trustee of the Adam and Charity Brockman Trust Dated 9-28-07 (“ACBT”) filed a complaint against Defendants Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC, Norman J. Kravetz, Douglas Jacobsen and Diversified Business Operations (“DBO”) for breach of written contract, fraudulent inducement – misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, breach of oral contract, and quantum meruit. Plaintiffs allege they provided Defendant Kravetz with $1,110,000.00 to be repaid by Defendants who made interest payments on each of the Promissory Notes. After Defendants defaulted on the Notes in 2022, Defendants made repeated assurances to Plaintiffs that they would repay them. Plaintiffs allege to date, they have not.

 

On February 14, 2024, Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC, Norman J. Kravetz, Douglas Jacobsen filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiffs for usury.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

 Plaintiff’s Writ of Attachment is GRANTED.

 

OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiffs submit evidentiary objections to the declaration of Norman J. Kravetz. Objection Nos 1-3 are OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiffs request the court grant the Application and issue a Right to Attach Order and a Writ of Attachment against Defendants Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC, Norman J. Kravetz, Douglas Jacobsen on the grounds Plaintiffs’ Applications meet the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 483.010.

 

A writ of attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500). (CCP §483.010(a).) A claim is “readily ascertainable” where the amount due may be clearly ascertained from the contract and calculated by evidence; the fact that damages are unliquidated is not determinative. (CIT Group/Equipment Financing, Inc. v. Super DVD, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 537, 540-41 [attachment appropriate for claim based on rent calculation for lease of commercial equipment].)

 

The application shall be executed under oath and must include: (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based;  (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101 et seq.); and  (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment. (CCP § 484.020.) Code Civil Procedure section 487.010(a) provides that “[w]here the defendant is a corporation, all corporate property for which a method of levy is provided” is subject to attachment.

 

“The declarations in the moving papers must contain evidentiary facts, stated ‘with particularity,’ and based on actual personal knowledge with all documentary evidence properly identified and authenticated.” (Hobbs v. Weiss (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 76, 79-80 [citing CCP § 482.040].) “In contested applications, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties and make a determination of¿the probable outcome of the litigation.” (Id. at p. 80.) 

 

Procedural Issues

 

In reply, Plaintiffs note Defendant’s opposition is untimely. The court agrees the opposition has been untimely filed. However, given Plaintiff’s ability to respond to the opposition, the court continues to hear the opposition.

 

Probable Validity of Plaintiff’s Claim

 

As noted above, the court may issue an attachment on a claim for money arising under contract, so long as the amount claimed by the party seeking attachment is reasonably ascertainable in an amount greater than $500.00.  (CCP § 483.010 (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege a breach of written contract claim against each of the Defendants. Plaintiffs provide copies of each Note entered into: October 1, 2015 Promissory Note between Defendant Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC (“Kravetz Capital”) and the Brockman Trust (the “First Note”); October 29, 2015 Promissory Note between ACBT and Kravetz Capital (the “Second Note”); December 1, 2015 Promissory Note between Provident and Kravetz Capital (the “Third Note”); and March 22, 2017 Promissory Note between Linda R. Asher and Kravetz Capital (the “Fourth Note”) along with the Commercial Guaranty’s. (Exhs. 1-8.) For Plaintiffs’ oral agreement with DBO, Plaintiff presents the declaration of Connie Brockman, trustee of Plaintiff The Brockman Trust Dated October 24, 2000 (the “Brockman Trust”) and Plaintiff Provident Trust Group, LLC FBO: the Connie Brockman Solo 401k (“Provident”). Ms. Brockman testifies she agreed to provide DBO with accounting services as an independent contractor at the request of Defendants Norman Kravetz and Douglas Jacobsen (the “Individual Defendants”), in their capacities as a principals of DBO, in exchange for DBO’s promise to pay me for my services. (C. Brockman Decl. ¶21.) Accordingly, Ms. Brockman prepared and sent DBO monthly invoices for her accounting services and initially, DBO paid her the amounts owed. (Brockman ¶22, Exh. 16.) Ms. Brockman testifies Defendants breached the agreements and guarantees by failing to make payments thereunder, which caused Plaintiffs to suffer damages in at least the amount of $1,674,297.39, or in the alternative, $1,484,630.94 resulting from Defendant Kravetz, DBO and the Individual Defendants’ breach of the Notes and guarantees thereof, and DBO’s breach of the Oral Contract. (C. Brockman Decl. ¶¶24-25; A. Brockman ¶18; COE Exh. 19; Koczara Decl. ¶7.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claim is of a type for which attachment may be issued. (See CCP § 483.010 (a).  Additionally, Plaintiff’s submitted evidence is sufficient to establish the probable validity of Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim. (See Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178 (necessary elements for breach of contract are: (1) valid contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance obligations thereunder; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) damages).)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue Plaintiffs have not demonstrated “probable validity” of their claims. According to Defendants, because each of the Notes provides for interest at a rate of 12% per annum, Plaintiffs are only entitled to repayment of the principal amount. All payments made by Defendants are required to be credited against the principal balance. As Plaintiffs have not taken into account the impact of such usurious interest on their claims, Plaintiffs have not established that it is more likely than not that they will obtain judgment on their claims.

 

Defendants in their intro point out Plaintiffs have not submitted an Application for Right to Attach Order and Order for Issuance of Writ of Attachment against defendant Douglas Jacobsen (“Jacobsen”). In reply, Plaintiffs state that’s wrong and Plaintiffs filed and served the Application as to Defendant Jacobsen on April 17, 2024 and received a conformed copy of this Application on same day with a timestamp of 10:48 a.m. The applications shows up on eCourt on May 3, 2024.

 

The parties dispute whether Defendants may use the defense of usury. Plaintiffs argue Defendant Kravetz and the Individual Parties – sophisticated business parties - authored the Notes and the Guaranties to include the interest rates set forth therein. Based on these terms, Plaintiffs provided the requested funds. Defendants are therefore estopped from citing the very interest rate they drafted as a usury defense to avoid attachment altogether for their failure to adhere to their contractual obligations. (See Lakeview Meadows Ranch v. Bintliff (1973) 36 Cal.App.3d 418, 420; see also Fitch v. Pacific Fid. Life Ins. Co. (1975) 54 Cal.App.3d 140, 148.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue mere “authorship” of a note by a borrower does not give rise to a defense of estoppel. (Martin v. Ajaz Const. Co. (1954) 124 Cal.App.2d 425, 431 (“The findings that [borrower and guarantors] solicited the money and they prepared the contract for its repayment . . . do not diminish the rigor of usury.”)

 

The court agrees case law supports a finding that a borrower who suggested and initiated loan terms and the interest rate is not always estopped from claiming that loan is usurious. In the cases cited by Plaintiff, the court found estoppel of the usury defense may exist when the plaintiff either relied on the drafter of the agreement and believed the defendant would draft the agreement according to law, when the borrower misrepresented to an inexperienced lender his intention to repay and value of property securing loan, or when the drafter fraudulently insert a usurious rate of interest for the purpose of defeating an action for interest on the note. (See Lakeview Meadows Ranch, supra, 36 Cal.App.3d at p. 425; Buck v. Dahlgren (1972) 23 Cal.App.3d 779, 789; Paillet v. Vroman (1942) 52 Cal.App.2d 297.) Here, Plaintiff does not argue they relied on Defendant when drafting the agreement or that Defendant fraudulently inserted the usurious rate.

 

However, based on Defendants admitted default on the Notes, Plaintiffs satisfy their initial burden to establish the probable validity of their claims.  In order to defeat this showing, Defendants must present evidence that it is more likely than not or equally likely that they will defeat Plaintiffs claims based on their defense of usury.  They have not.

 

Amount to be Secured

 

As noted above, “an attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500) exclusive of costs, interest, and attorney's fees.”  (CCP § 483.010(a).)  “An attachment may not be issued on a claim which is secured by any interest in real property arising from agreement ….”  (CCP § 483.010(b).)  

 

“It is a well-recognized rule of law in this state that an attachment will lie upon a cause of action for damages for a breach of contract where the damages are readily ascertainable by reference to the contract and the basis of the computation of damages appears to be reasonable and definite. [Citations.] The fact that the damages are unliquidated is not determinative. [Citations.] But the contract sued on must furnish a standard by which the amount due may be clearly ascertained and there must exist a basis upon which the damages can be determined by proof.’¿” (See CIT Group/Equipment Financing, Inc. v. Super DVD, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App. 4th 537, 541.)   

 

Plaintiffs argue the amounts owed are readily ascertainable from the contracts attached to Plaintiff's Declaration including the Notes, Guaranties, and Invoices. Plaintiffs contend Defendant Kravetz is liable for its default of the First Note of $400,000.00, and monthly interest and late fees as of October 2022 totaling $75,600.00. (C. Brockman Dec. ¿ 24.) Defendant Kravetz is liable under the Second Note in the amount of $200,000.00, plus monthly interest and late fees as of October 2022 totaling $37,800.00. (C. Brockman Dec. ¿ 24.) Under the Third Note, Defendant Kravetz liable is in the amount of $325,000, plus monthly interest and late fees as of October 2022 totaling $61,425.00. (C. Brockman Dec. ¿ 24.) Under the Fourth Note, Defendant Kravetz is liable in the amount of $185,000, plus monthly interest and late fees as of October 2022 totaling $34,965, for a total of $1,110,000.00 owing in principal amounts and $209,790.00 in interest and late fees under all Notes. (C. Brockman Dec. ¿ 24.) The Individual Defendants are liable for each of these amounts pursuant to the Guaranties. Plaintiffs have also modified the interest and late fee amounts due per the express provision of the Notes is as follows: $63,000.00 pursuant to the First Note; $31,500.06 pursuant to the Second Note; $51,187.44 pursuant the Third Note; $29,137.56 pursuant to the Fourth Note, for a total of $174,825.06. (C. Brockman Dec. ¿ 24.) In sum, Plaintiffs are each entitled attachment as follows: $408,333.06 on the First Note, $398,263.11 on the Second and Fourth Notes, $194,096.11 on the Third Note, and $33,498.50 on the DBO Oral Breach. Plaintiffs also seek $321,681.89in attorney fees and costs. (Koczara Dec. ¶ 7.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue several years of interest at a usurious rate have been paid by Defendants to Plaintiffs. Furthermore, Defendants are entitled to recover all usurious interest payments made in the 2 years preceding the date of Plaintiff’s Complaint. (Civ. Code § 1916-3(a).) As such, this needs to be taken into account when determining the amount of Plaintiffs’ claims, making the amount of the claims not readily ascertainable. Furthermore, while Defendants concede “usury savings clause” is contained in the promissory notes submitted by Plaintiffs, Defendants dispute that such usury savings clause is valid. Defendants contend they do not appear until the third and fourth notes and therefore, it does not affect any of the Loans before it first appears in the applicable Notes, and all payments on such Loans before the addition of the usury savings clause language are deemed to be payments of principal.

 

The court finds the amount readily ascertainable. As noted by Plaintiffs, it is not required to fix the amount of its damages with certainty at this juncture. (See Bringas v. Sullivan (1954) 126 Cal.App.2d 693, 699 (the attachment statute “does not suggest that the amount owing must be fixed with absolute certainty”) (internal quotation marks and citation omitted).) Rather, “an attachment is proper although the exact amount due is unknown to the attaching party.” (Id.) Here, we know Defendants’ payment obligations under the Note and DBO Contract and what the reduced interest rate is.

 

Reduced Amount

 

Defendants argue the amount of any RTAO must be reduced by Defendants’ cross-claims. (CCP § 483.015(b).) Specifically, Defendants have established cross-claims totaling not less than $36,000.00 on the Brockman Trust Note, $29,250.00 on the Provident Note, $18,000 on the ACBT Note and $16,560 on the Asher Note).

 

The amount to be secured by an attachment shall be reduced by the sum of (1) the amount of indebtedness that the defendant has in a money judgment against plaintiff, (2) the amount claimed in a cross-complaint or affirmative defense and shown would be subject to attachment against the plaintiff, and (3) the value of any security interest held by the plaintiff in the defendant’s property, together with the amount by which the acts of the plaintiff (or a prior holder of the security interest) have decreased that security interest’s value.  (CCP §483.015(b).) A defendant claiming that the amount to be secured should be reduced because of a cross-claim or affirmative defense must make a prima facie showing that the claim would result in an attachment against the plaintiff. (Lydig Construction, Inc. v. Martinez Steel (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 937; Pos-A-Traction, Inc. v. Kelly Springfield (C.D. Cal. (1999) 112 F.Supp.2d 1178, 1183.)

 

Defendants have failed to present a prima facie claim of offset. Defendants do not provide any documentation supporting Defendants’ contention they overpaid interest. Defendants do not provide any documentation reconciling the overpaid interest with the 10% interest.

 

Moreover, Defendants argue the amount of legal fees and costs secured by the RTAO must be reduced. Pursuant to Los Angeles Superior Court Rule 3.214(a), when a promissory note provides for the recovery of attorneys’ fees the amount allowed (in cases where the damages are in excess of $100,000, is $5,270 plus 2% of the excess over $100,000. Accordingly, Defendants argue that based on that formula Plaintiffs are, at most, entitled to attorneys’ fees of $29,665.80.

 

“In the discretion of the court, the amount to be secured by the attachment may include an estimated amount for costs and allowable attorney's fees.” (CCP § 482.110.) The court agrees that under this provision Plaintiff will be entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees if it prevails.  However, Plaintiff’s estimated attorneys’ fees of $76,809.02 are not reasonable.  Local Rule 3.214(a) states that “[w]hen a promissory note, contract, or statute provides for the recovery of reasonable attorneys’ fees,” the following formula will be used to calculate fees in a contested case over $100,000: $2,890 plus 1% of the excess over $100,000. Accordingly, the court limits Plaintiff’s estimated attorney’s fees to $30,000.00.  The court also limits Plaintiff’s estimated costs to $2,672.87 in incurred costs and $3,000 in estimated costs (Koczara Decl. ¶¶5-6.)

 

Exemption

 

Plaintiffs argue Kravetz’s claims of exemption should be denied. Specifically, Kravetz is not entitled to the homestead exemption of the entire value of the property. At most, he is entitled to an exemption in the maximum amount of $600,000.00. Moreover, Kravetz generally claims the motor vehicles exemption under Code of Civil Procedure § 704.010 (for his Bentley and Cadillac Escalade), which authorizes up to $7,500.00 of the aggregate value in motor vehicles as exempt. However, the Kravetz Statement of Financial Condition self-reports his net worth much higher. Lastly, with respect to Kravetz’s “necessities of life” exemption, he fails to show how the amounts Plaintiffs are seeking to levy on are necessary for his or his family’s support.

 

As for the homestead exemption, the amount of the homestead exemption is limited to either $300,00.00 or $600,000.00, depending on the median countywide home price.  (See CCP § 704.730 (a).)  Accordingly, the court applies the exemption in the amount of $600,000.00.

 

As for the ‘necessities of life’ exemption, the court finds Defendants have not shown that his income and liquid assets are insufficient to cover the reasonable expenses necessary to support him and his family. (CCP §703.115.) The exemption is denied.


Case Number: 24VECV00867    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: W

PARY AL ZAHAWI vs JOURIY GRKIKIAN

 

DEFENDANTs’ demurrer with motion to strike the complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        May 14, 2024                                     Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV00867

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Jouriy Grkikian, DDS and Y2K Dentistry

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Pary Al Zahawi

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Harrison Decl. ¶8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff Pary Al Zahawi filed a complaint against Defendants Jouriy Grkikian, DDS and Y2K Dentistry for medical malpractice. Plaintiff alleges Defendants failed to act within the standard of care when performing multiple dental treatments on Plaintiff.  

 

Plaintiff previously filed an action against Defendants in Pary al Zahawi v. Jouriy Grkikian, et al. (Case No. 23VECV03478) on August 10, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer with Motion to Strike the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of 1. Plaintiff Pary Al Zahawi’s Complaint for Damages filed in another case, Pary al Zahawi v. Jouriy Grkikian, et al., in the Los Angeles County Superior Court, Department O, Case No. 23VECV03478 and 2. Judge Michael R. Amerian’s Minute Order in this referenced matter, dated January 30, 2024.

 

The court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Jouriy Grkikian, DDS and Y2K Dentistry demur to Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds Plaintiff’s cause of action for medical malpractice is uncertain and time barred.

 

A cause of action arising out of the alleged professional negligence of a healthcare provider is governed by the statute of limitations set forth in section 340.5 of the Code of Civil Procedure. (Drexler v. Petersen (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 1181, 1189.) Except under certain exceptions not applicable here, such a cause of action must be brought either (1) within one year of the date plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, or (2) within three years of the injury, whichever comes first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5.) Under this ‘discovery rule,’ “the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff suspects or should suspect that her injury was caused by wrongdoing, that someone has done something wrong to her.” (Jolly v. Eli Lilly & Co. (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1103, 1110.) The rule contains “two alternate tests for triggering the limitations period,” one subjective, and the other objective. (Kitzig v. Nordquist (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 1384, 1391.) Under the subjective test, the one-year limitations period begins to run when “the plaintiff discovers both his or her injury and its negligent cause.” (Drexler, supra, 4 Cal.App.5th at p. 1189.) Under the objective test, the limitations period begins to run when the plaintiff “has notice or information of circumstances to put a reasonable person on inquiry.” (Jolly, supra, 44 Cal.3d at p. 1110-11.) It is not necessary under either test, however, that the plaintiff be aware of the “specific facts necessary to establish the claim.” (Id. at p. 1111.) 

 

Defendants argue the “general negligence” cause of action is vague and insufficient on the grounds Plaintiff’s conclusory allegations that Defendant failed to act within the standard of care provides zero insight as to what standard of care was allegedly breached and how any such breach occurred. While the complaint might be bare, demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and should only be sustained where the complaint is so uncertain that the demurring defendant cannot reasonably respond thereto. (See Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Plaintiff's Complaint is not so uncertain that Defendant cannot reasonably respond thereto. 

 

Defendants next argue Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the statute of limitations. According to Defendants, Plaintiff clearly confirms her understanding on each of the dates identified in 2019, 2020, 2021 and 2022 that she had sustained injury/damage as a result of the alleged negligent rendering of dental services by the Defendants. She also specifically states that between July 30, 2020 and June 9, 2022, no less than 7 dentists had reportedly informed her that the dental work done by the Defendants was a “bad job.” As such she was clearly aware of the alleged negligence (“bad job”) of the Defendants more than 1 year prior to the filing of the Complaint, on February 27, 2024. Defendants contend the alleged discovery in 2023 of additional alleged breaches of the standard of care by the Defendants does not negate that the Plaintiff was placed on inquiry notice of dental malpractice well more than a year prior to when suit was filed.

 

Plaintiff argues they have a five-year warranty and Defendants have refused to honor it. This argument does not counter Defendants’ contention that the medical malpractice claim is barred. As noted above, under the subjective test, the one-year limitations period begins to run when “the plaintiff discovers both his or her injury and its negligent cause.” (Drexler, supra, 4 Cal.App.5th at p. 1189.) Under the objective test, the limitations period begins to run when the plaintiff “has notice or information of circumstances to put a reasonable person on inquiry.” (Jolly, supra, 44 Cal.3d at p. 1110-11.) It is not necessary under either test, however, that the plaintiff be aware of the “specific facts necessary to establish the claim.” (Id. at p. 1111.) As alleged, Plaintiff was aware of the alleged negligence well before one year of the filing of this complaint. Plaintiff continuing to seek help in fixing the alleged injury does not extend Plaintiff’s inquiry notice.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages. As the court has sustained without leave Defendants’ demurrer, the motion to strike is MOOT. The court notes Plaintiff also improperly added a prayer for punitive damages pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.13.
