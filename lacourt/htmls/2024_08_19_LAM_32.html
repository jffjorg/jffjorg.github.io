DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV11618    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: 32

 

MICHAEL BOYD,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

MEGAN MICHELLE ST. CLAIR, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV11618

  Hearing Date:  August 16, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant halo behavioral health, inc.’s demurrer and motion to strike

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 23, 2023, Plaintiff Michael Boyd filed this action against Defendants Megan St. Clair, Peter Burra, HALO Behavioral Health, Inc., Brandon St. Clair, and Paul Meyers. Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint on June 11, 2024, asserting 15 causes of action arising from the following facts.

            Plaintiff alleges that he was engaged to Kathleen St. Clair (Kathleen) until her death in May 2022. Kathleen’s two children, Defendants Megan St. Clair (Megan) and Brandon St. Clair (Brandon), were allegedly hostile towards Kathleen and resentful of the relationship between Plaintiff and Kathleen. When Kathleen was hospitalized for a heart attack, Megan allegedly used a California Advanced Health Care Directive (CAHCD) to place Kathleen in hospice, exclude Plaintiff from visiting, and prohibit Kathleen’s healthcare providers from communicating with Plaintiff.

The complaint alleges that Megan and her husband, Defendant Bryan Burra (Burra), falsely accused Plaintiff of having a criminal record as a pretext to exclude him from Kathleen’s care. Megan and Burra allegedly embarked on a scheme to investigate Plaintiff and fabricate and disseminate false information on Plaintiff. This scheme allegedly involved resources and employees of their business, Defendant HALO Behavioral Health, Inc. (HALO). Megan allegedly filed for a temporary restraining order (TRO) against Plaintiff based on false information.

In November 2022, Plaintiff was hospitalized. Defendant Paul Meyers (Meyers), Kathleen’s brother, allegedly discovered this by calling the hospital and then informed Brandon. Brandon then allegedly took the opportunity to break into Plaintiff’s home, taking furniture and other property. Plaintiff alleges that Megan, Burra, and Meyers were either present at or assisted in the burglary and that Megan and Burra took possession of the stolen property. Plaintiff alleges that Megan and Burra burglarized his home again in January 2023.

On July 24, 2024, HALO filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike against the SAC. Plaintiff filed his late opposition on August 8, 2024. HALO filed its reply on August 12, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Martinez Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Vicarious Liability

            a. Legal Principles

“Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, an employer is vicariously liable for his employee's torts committed within the scope of the employment.” (Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons (1986) 41 Cal.3d 962, 967.) Wrongdoing is considered within the scope of employment if “in the context of the particular enterprise an employee's conduct is not so unusual or startling that it would seem unfair to include the loss resulting from it among other costs of the employer's business.” (Rodgers v. Kemper Constr. Co. (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 608, 619.)

            “In California, the scope of employment has been interpreted broadly under the respondeat superior doctrine.” (Farmers Ins. Group v. County of Santa Clara (1995) 11 Cal.4th 992, 1004.) Thus, “[t]he fact that an employee is not engaged in the ultimate object of his employment at the time of his wrongful act does not preclude attribution of liability to an employer.” (Alma W. v. Oakland Unified School Dist. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 133, 139.) “For example, acts necessary to the comfort, convenience, health, and welfare of the employee while at work, though strictly personal to himself and not acts of service, do not take him outside the scope of his employment.” (Ibid.)

“However, that is not to say, that employers are strictly liable for all actions of their employees during working hours. If an employee substantially deviates from his duties for personal purposes, the employer is not vicariously liable for the employee's actions.” (Alma W., supra, 123 Cal.App.3d at p. 139.) “Thus, if the employee ‘inflicts an injury out of personal malice, not engendered by the employment’ [citation] or acts out of ‘personal malice unconnected with the employment’ [citation], or if the misconduct is not an ‘outgrowth’ of the employment [citation], the employee is not acting within the scope of employment.” (Farmers, supra, 11 Cal.4th at p. 1005.) In such a case, “the mere fact that an employee has an opportunity to abuse facilities or authority necessary to the performance of his or her duties does not render the employer vicariously liable.” (Id. at p. 1006.) “If an employee's tort is personal in nature, mere presence at the place of employment and attendance to occupational duties prior or subsequent to the offense will not give rise to a cause of action against the employer under the doctrine of respondeat superior.” (Alma W., supra, 123 Cal.App.3d at p. 140.)   

b. Application

“Ordinarily, the determination whether an employee has acted within the scope of employment presents a question of fact.” (Farmers, supra, 11 Cal.4th at p. 1019.) Here, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to support an inference that Megan’s and Burra’s actions were connected to their employment at HALO. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ actions were in retaliation for Plaintiff’s filing of a complaint to the Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB). (SAC ¶¶ 90-93.) The SAC alleges that HALO operates under Megan’s BACB certification as a Behavior Analyst. (Id., ¶ 87.) In Spring 2022, Plaintiff allegedly reported HALO, Megan, and Burra for failure to comply with BACB certification and licensing requirements. (Id., ¶ 91.) The conduct detailed in the SAC was allegedly carried out to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff for his complaint to the BACB. (Id., ¶ 93.)

HALO argues that the addition of allegations regarding Plaintiff’s BACB complaint does not suddenly render HALO liable for every cause of action in the SAC. HALO argues that Plaintiff fails to articulate which specific acts were actually connected to the BACB issue. HALO maintains that the allegations only demonstrate acts arising from personal malice. However, “where the employee is combining his own business with that of his employer, or attending to both at substantially the same time, no nice inquiry will be made as to which business he was actually engaged in at the time of injury, unless it clearly appears that neither directly nor indirectly could he have been serving his employer.” (Farmers, supra, 11 Cal.4th at p. 1004.)

While the allegations in the SAC do suggest personal malice based on the individual defendants’ resentment of Plaintiff’s relationship with Kathleen, that does not preclude Megan and Burra from also acting in the interests of HALO and their employment therein. For demurrer purposes, it must be assumed true that Megan and Burra engaged in a course of action to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff for his BACB complaint. (SAC ¶¶ 90-93.) It may be reasonably inferred that Megan and Burra engaged in this course of action because the BACB complaint threatened HALO’s ability to operate. Thus, the alleged acts may be “fairly attributable to work-related events or conditions.” (See Lisa M. v. Henry Mayo Newhall Memorial Hospital (1995) 12 Cal.4th 291, 301.) At the pleading stage, the parties need not engage in a granular dissection of which precise acts were actually in service of HALO. The SAC sufficiently establishes that the overall course of conduct was done for this purpose. 

II. Factual Sufficiency of Each Claim

            HALO also demurs to each individual cause of action for failure to state sufficient facts, purportedly on grounds beyond the vicarious liability issue. However, HALO’s arguments largely fall back on its contention that the alleged acts can only be attributed to the individual defendants rather than HALO. HALO additionally reiterates arguments that the Court already rejected in a prior demurrer.

            a. Defamation

            HALO argues that statements made as part of the TRO proceedings are privileged under Civil Code section 47. However, as the Court held previously, the litigation privilege does not cover the other alleged acts of defamation. For example, the alleged crafting and publishing of a false criminal record may be reasonably considered as part of the scheme to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff after his BACB complaint. (See SAC ¶¶ 42-47.)

            HALO also argues that merely conducting “internet searches” does not constitute defamation. Plaintiff does not base the defamation claim on internet searches. HALO acknowledges that the SAC alleges publications made by Megan and Burra. Thus, the SAC alleges publications, not mere research. Again, whether these publications were sufficiently linked to the employment with HALO is a question of fact.

            b. Malicious Prosecution

            HALO argues that the excerpted segments of Megan’s declaration show probable cause for filing the TRO, i.e., she was concerned for her safety and believed Plaintiff had a criminal record. On a demurrer, the Court cannot take judicial notice of facts asserted in a court filing, even if the filing itself is judicially noticeable. (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) Thus, the Court cannot assume the truth of Megan’s motivations for filing the TRO. Instead, the Court assumes Plaintiff’s allegations as true, i.e., Megan did not have probable cause for filing the TRO. Moreover, Megan’s genuine concern for her safety relates to her subjective intent, whereas probable cause is objective. (See Area 55, supra, 61 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) And as discussed above, whether the false filing of a TRO is sufficiently connected to HALO is a question for the trier of fact.

           

 

c. Theft, Conversion, and Trespass

            HALO reiterates the rejected argument that most of the property allegedly stolen belonged to Kathleen and that the home being burglarized belonged to Kathleen. As the Court held previously, Plaintiff alleges that his personal property was stolen, particularly computer drives. (See SAC ¶ 83.) The Court has also already found that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged ownership or control of the property that was burglarized. (See May 22, 2024 Order re Dem. 4:23-5:4.)

Plaintiff alleges that the stolen drives contained information relating to his BACB complaint. (SAC ¶ 83.) Therefore, the alleged theft, conversion, and trespass may reasonably be considered as part of the scheme to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff for his BACB complaint. This establishes a sufficient connection to HALO for purposes of vicarious liability.

d. Invasion of Privacy

As HALO acknowledges, the invasion of privacy claims are premised on the alleged defamation and theft/trespass. As discussed above, the defamation, theft, and trespass claims are adequately pled. The Court has already held that the allegations are sufficiently clear for pleading purposes and demonstrate an invasion of privacy. (May 22, 2024 Order re Dem. 5:15-23.) And as discussed above, stealing computer drivers containing information on Plaintiff’s BACB complaint sufficiently establishes a connection to HALO.

e. Stalking

As the Court held previously, the allegations sufficiently establish stalking. (May 22, 2024 Order re Dem. 6:19-24.) It is a factual matter whether the alleged surveillance of Plaintiff sufficiently furthered the scheme to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff for his BACB complaint or otherwise served HALO’s interests.

 

 

f. Intentional and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

To state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must establish: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.) “For conduct to be outrageous, it must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated by a civilized community.” (Faunce v. Cate (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 166, 172.) “Severe emotional distress [is] emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.” (Fletcher v. Western Life Insurance Co. (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 376, 397.)

Plaintiff fails to allege severe emotional distress. “[D]iscomfort, worry, anxiety, upset stomach, concern, and agitation” do not satisfy the severity standard. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051.) Plaintiff makes generic allegations such as “suffering, anguish, fright, horror, nervousness, grief, anxiety, worry, shock, humiliation, and shame.” (See SAC ¶ 299.) This does not sufficiently demonstrate severe emotional distress.

Additionally, “[t]here is no independent tort of negligent infliction of emotional distress; rather, ‘[t]he tort is negligence, a cause of action in which a duty to the plaintiff is an essential element.’” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 205, quoting Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984.) Plaintiff has failed to allege any duty of care owed by HALO towards him. Therefore, the NIED claim fails for this additional reason.

 

 

g. Negligent Hiring and Civil Conspiracy

“The plaintiff may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so, unless the new cause of action is within the scope of the order granting leave to amend.” (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.) Plaintiff was not given leave to assert a new cause of action. Therefore, the negligent hiring and conspiracy claims are stricken.

III. Punitive Damages

            As the Court held previously, the alleged conduct is sufficiently despicable, cruel, or unjust for pleading purposes. (May 22, 2024 Order re Dem. 8:20-24.) Defendants allegedly sought to discredit and retaliate against Plaintiff because he complained to the BACB. This sufficiently shows malice.

CONCLUSION

            HALO’s demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the tenth, eleventh, twelfth, thirteenth, and fifteenth causes of action and OVERRULED in all other respects. HALO’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

 

MICHAEL BOYD,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

MEGAN MICHELLE ST. CLAIR, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV11618

  Hearing Date:  August 19, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant paul meyers’ demurrer to second amended complaint

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 23, 2023, Plaintiff Michael Boyd filed this action against Defendants Megan St. Clair, Peter Burra, HALO Behavioral Health, Inc., Brandon St. Clair, and Paul Meyers. Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint on June 11, 2024, asserting 15 causes of action arising from the following facts.

            Plaintiff alleges that he was engaged to Kathleen St. Clair (Kathleen) until her death in May 2022. Kathleen’s two children, Defendants Megan St. Clair (Megan) and Brandon St. Clair (Brandon), were allegedly hostile towards Kathleen and resentful of the relationship between Plaintiff and Kathleen. When Kathleen was hospitalized for a heart attack, Megan allegedly used a California Advanced Health Care Directive (CAHCD) to place Kathleen in hospice, exclude Plaintiff from visiting, and prohibit Kathleen’s healthcare providers from communicating with Plaintiff.

The complaint alleges that Megan and her husband, Defendant Bryan Burra (Burra), falsely accused Plaintiff of having a criminal record as a pretext to exclude him from Kathleen’s care. Megan and Burra allegedly embarked on a scheme to investigate Plaintiff and fabricate and disseminate false information on Plaintiff. This scheme allegedly involved resources and employees of their business, Defendant HALO Behavioral Health, Inc. (HALO). Megan allegedly filed for a temporary restraining order (TRO) against Plaintiff based on false information.

In November 2022, Plaintiff was hospitalized. Defendant Paul Meyers (Meyers), Kathleen’s brother, allegedly discovered this by calling the hospital and then informed Brandon. Brandon then allegedly took the opportunity to break into Plaintiff’s home, taking furniture and other property. Plaintiff alleges that Megan, Burra, and Meyers were either present at or assisted in the burglary and that Megan and Burra took possession of the stolen property. Plaintiff alleges that Megan and Burra burglarized his home again in January 2023.

On July 16, 2024, Meyers filed the instant demurrer to the first, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action in the SAC. Plaintiff filed his late opposition on August 6, 2024. Meyers filed his reply on August 9, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Ibid.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.) The Court notes that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Manaa Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Defamation and False Light (First and Eighth COAs)

            The elements of a defamation claim are: (1) publication that is (2) false, (3) defamatory, (4) unprivileged, and (5) has a natural tendency to injure or cause special damage. (Wong v. Jing (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1354, 1369.) False statements charging the commission of crime, or tending directly to injure a plaintiff in respect to his or her profession by imputing dishonesty or questionable professional conduct are defamatory per se. (Burrill v. Nair (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 357, 383.) “[Where] the language is ambiguous and an explanation is necessary to establish the defamatory meaning, the pleader must do two things: (1) Allege his interpretation of the defamatory meaning of the language (the ‘innuendo,’ . . .); (2) support that interpretation by alleging facts showing that the readers or hearers to whom it was published would understand it in that defamatory sense (the ‘inducement’).” (Barnes-Hind, Inc. v. Superior Court (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 377, 387.)

            Here, the defamation claim as against Meyers is based on the allegation that Meyers falsely claimed that Plaintiff was living in Kathleen’s house “for free.” (SAC ¶¶ 50-51.) A statement that someone is living in a home for free is not defamatory per se. Plaintiff has failed to allege the defamatory meaning of the statement, as well as the defamatory effect on a listener. (See Barnes-Hind, supra, 181 Cal.App.3d at p. 387.) Therefore, the defamation claim fails as a matter of law.

            “When a false light claim is coupled with a defamation claim, the false light claim is essentially superfluous, and stands or falls on whether it meets the same requirements as the defamation cause of action.” (Jackson v. Mayweather (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 1240, 1264.) Because the defamation claim fails, so does the derivative false light claim.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the first and eighth causes of action.  

II. Trespass, Theft, Conversion, and Invasion of Privacy (Third, Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Seventh COAs)

            Meyers argues that there are no allegations he directly participated in the alleged breaking into Plaintiff’s property or stealing Plaintiff’s personal belongings. However, Meyers does not challenge the civil conspiracy claim. “The elements of an action for civil conspiracy are (1) formation and operation of the conspiracy and (2) damage resulting to plaintiff (3) from a wrongful act done in furtherance of the common design.” (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1136.) “The significance of a conspiracy theory of liability is that each member may be held jointly liable as a tortfeasor, even though he or she may not have participated directly in the underlying tort.” (Ibid.)

            The SAC alleges that Meyers assisted in the breaking and theft by informing Brandon that Plaintiff had been hospitalized and was away from home. (SAC ¶ 193.) The SAC further alleges that Meyers was “present and participated” in the trespass onto Plaintiff’s property. (Id., ¶ 222.) These facts sufficiently support liability against Meyers under civil conspiracy. Therefore, Plaintiff has adequately pled claims for trespass, theft, conversion, and invasion of privacy against Meyers. 

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action.

III. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (Tenth COA)

To state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must establish: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.) “For conduct to be outrageous, it must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated by a civilized community.” (Faunce v. Cate (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 166, 172.) “Severe emotional distress [is] emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.” (Fletcher v. Western Life Insurance Co. (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 376, 397.)

Plaintiff fails to allege severe emotional distress. “[D]iscomfort, worry, anxiety, upset stomach, concern, and agitation” do not satisfy the severity standard. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051.) Plaintiff makes generic allegations such as “suffering, anguish, fright, horror, nervousness, grief, anxiety, worry, shock, humiliation, and shame.” (See SAC ¶ 299.) This does not sufficiently demonstrate severe emotional distress.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the tenth cause of action.

IV. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (Eleventh COA)

            “There is no independent tort of negligent infliction of emotional distress; rather, ‘[t]he tort is negligence, a cause of action in which a duty to the plaintiff is an essential element.’” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 205, quoting Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984.)

            Plaintiff has failed to allege any duty of care owed by Meyers towards him. Therefore, the NIED claim fails for this additional reason.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the eleventh cause of action.  

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Paul Meyers’ demurrer is SUSTAINED in part as set forth above without leave to amend.

Case Number: 24STCV12145    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: 32

 

DAISY SOLANO,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

HAWAIIAN GARDENS CASINO, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV12145

  Hearing Date:  August 19, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ motion to compel arbitration

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 14, 2024, Plaintiff Daisy Solano filed this employment discrimination action against Defendants Hawaiian Gardens Casino and Nicholas Bolger. Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint on May 31, 2024.

            On July 25, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to compel arbitration. Plaintiff filed her opposition on August 6, 2024. Defendants filed their reply on August 14, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists….” (Code Civ. Proc, § 1281.2.) “The party seeking arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement, and the party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving any defense, such as unconscionability.” (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236.)

DISCUSSION

I. Proof of Agreement

            “The moving party ‘can meet its initial burden by attaching to the motion or petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the opposing party's signature.’” (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165.)

            Here, Plaintiff signed an “Agreement to Arbitrate” on June 15, 2020, covering “any and all disputes, claims or controversies arising out of or relating to this Agreement, the employment relationship between the parties, or the termination of the employment relationship.” (Fausto Decl., Ex. A.) The agreement specifically identifies wrongful termination, discrimination, retaliation, and harassment as the types of claims within its coverage. (Ibid.) The agreement covers “any claims the Company may have against Employee, or that Employee may have against the Company or any of its officers, directors, employees, agents, parents, subsidiaries, or affiliated entities.” (Ibid.)

            Plaintiff does not dispute the existence of the agreement, its terms, or her signature on it. Thus, Defendants have satisfied their initial burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement covering the claims at issue.

II. Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act (EFAA)

            a. Statutory Framework

The EFAA provides that “no predispute arbitration agreement or predispute joint-action waiver shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.” (9 U.S.C. § 402.) “The term ‘predispute arbitration agreement’ means any agreement to arbitrate a dispute that had not yet arisen at the time of the making of the agreement.” (Id., § 401(1).) “The term ‘sexual harassment dispute’ means a dispute relating to conduct that is alleged to constitute sexual harassment under applicable Federal, Tribal, or State law.” (Id., § 401(4).)

            b. Application to “Disputes” Arising After March 3, 2022

The EFAA applies “to any dispute or claim that arises or accrues on or after March 3, 2022.” (9 U.S.C. § 401.) “[T]he date that a dispute has arisen for purposes of the Act depends on the unique facts of each case, but a dispute does not arise merely from the fact of injury” or “solely from the alleged sexual conduct.” (Kader v. Southern California Medical Center, Inc. (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 214, 218, 222.) Instead, “[a] dispute arises when one party asserts a right, claim, or demand, and the other side expresses disagreement or takes an adversarial posture.” (Id. at p. 222.) This is unlike a claim, which “arises for the first time when the plaintiff suffers an injury.” (Id. at p. 223.) Thus, “a claim may arise or accrue before a dispute arises.” (Id. at pp. 224-25.)

In Kader, despite the alleged sexually harassing conduct beginning in 2018, there was no evidence that the plaintiff ever reported the harassment to his employer or that the employer disputed any claim before March 3, 2022. (Kader, supra, 99 Cal.App.5th at pp. 218-19, 224.) A dispute did not arise until the plaintiff filed charges with the Department of Fair Employment and Housing[1] in May 2022. (Id. at p. 224.) Because the dispute arose after March 3, 2022, “the Act applied to invalidate the predispute arbitration agreement.” (Id. at p. 225.)    

Here, Defendants argue that the EFAA does not apply because “Plaintiff’s claims . . . arose and accrued during her employment, beginning in 2018.” (Mtn. 10:14-16.) Defendants further argue that “Plaintiff does not allege any acts of sexual harassment that occurred on or after March 3, 2022.” (Reply 9:9-10.) However, as discussed above, the date that wrongdoing is committed or a claim accrues is not dispositive, because the EFAA operates in relation to “the sexual harassment dispute.” (See 9 U.S.C. §§ 401, 402; Kader, supra, 99 Cal.App.5th at pp. 222-25.) In this case, as in Kader, there is no indication that a dispute arose until Plaintiff filed charges with the CRD in May 2024. (See Odell Decl., Ex. C.) Even assuming a dispute arose when Plaintiff reported the harassment to Defendants, that allegedly occurred in September 2023. (FAC ¶ 21.) Either way, the dispute arose after March 3, 2022. Defendants identify no other date when a dispute could have arisen.

Defendants’ reliance on the unpublished opinion in Martinez v. Simplified Labor Staffing Solutions (May 15, 2024, No. B331667) 2024 Cal. App. Unpub. LEXIS 3021 is unavailing. It is true that the court in Martinez held the EFAA applied because the plaintiff “alleged acts that arose after EFASASH was signed into law.” (Id., at *6.) However, the court did not hold that the EFAA only applies if the alleged acts occur after the EFAA’s enactment. Martinez did not concern a situation where the alleged conduct occurred before March 3, 2022, but a dispute arose afterwards. “A court's opinion is not authority for a proposition not considered in it.” (People v. Anderson (2015) 232 Cal.App.4th 1259, 1275.) Kader is therefore more on point and also binding, unlike Martinez. Because the sexual harassment dispute in this case arose after March 3, 2022, the EFAA applies.    

c. Continuing Violation

Even if the EFAA only applied to claims that accrued before March 3, 2022, Plaintiff alleges sexual harassment “throughout her employment,” which lasted until March 2024. (FAC ¶¶ 20, 27.) Under the continuing violation doctrine, a claim based on a “pattern of reasonably frequent and similar acts” accrues on the date of the last act. (Willis v. City of Carlsbad (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 1104, 1124.) “[H]ostile work environment claims . . . by their very nature involve repeated conduct and thus cannot be said to occur on any particular day.” (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1057.) “[A] harassment claim is composed of a series of separate acts that collectively constitute one ‘unlawful employment practice.’” (Ibid.) Thus, “it is appropriate to apply the continuing violation doctrine to . . . harassment claims.” (Id. at p. 1058.)

Here, the allegations in the FAC sufficiently establish that the sexual harassment, even if it began before March 3, 2022, continued through that date. Thus, the sexual harassment claim accrued after March 3, 2022. As a result, applying Defendants’ legal standard (in contravention of Kader) leads to the same conclusion. 

d. Application to Claims Other Than Sexual Harassment

Defendants request the alternative remedy of ordering arbitration for all claims except the sexual harassment claim, arguing that the EFAA only bars arbitration of the harassment claim. The EFAA provides that “no predispute arbitration agreement or predispute joint-action waiver shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.” (9 U.S.C. § 402.)

Defendants argue that the remaining claims do not stem from conduct fitting the EFAA’s definition of “sexual harassment.” (See 9 U.S.C. § 401.) However, those claims, even if not for sexual harassment, sufficiently “relate to” the sexual harassment dispute. Plaintiff alleges discrimination based on the same mistreatment. Plaintiff alleges retaliation for her reporting of the alleged mistreatment. Plaintiff alleges disability discrimination and failure to accommodate based on the anxiety and stress that she suffered as a result of the mistreatment. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to prevent or address the mistreatment. Plaintiff has not alleged distinct claims such as wage and hour, for example. (See Doe v. Indus Invs., 2023 Cal. Super. LEXIS 48876, *12-13 [distinguishing claims based on “other forms of discrimination and retaliation” from “unrelated . . . wage and hour claims” in applying the EFAA]; see also Johnson v. Everyrealm, Inc. (S.D.N.Y. 2023) 657 F.Supp.3d 535, 559 [EFAA “extends to the entirety of the case relating to the sexual harassment dispute, not merely the discrete claims in that case that themselves either allege such harassment or relate to a sexual harassment dispute”].) Therefore, the claims sufficiently “relate to” the sexual harassment dispute to fall under the EFAA. Accordingly, all of the claims are barred from arbitration.  

CONCLUSION

            Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration is DENIED.
