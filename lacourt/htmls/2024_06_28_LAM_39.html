DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV21799A    Hearing Date: June 28, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 28, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV21799A

MOTION

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Steven C. Kim

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiff 3800 West Sixth Street, LLC

 

MOTION

Defendant Steven C. Kim (“Defendant”) moves to compel further responses from Plaintiff 3800 West Sixth Street, LLC (“Plaintiff”) to Special Interrogatories, set one (“SROG”).  Plaintiff opposes the motion. Defendant filed his separate statement on March 5, 2024, pursuant to this court’s December 13, 2023 order.  

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300.) 

For the SROG at issue, Plaintiff is asked to state the facts and identify the documents that support allegations in its complaint.  Defendant contends Plaintiff’s responses do not state facts that support the allegations.  Defendant cannot compel Plaintiff to state certain facts, even if Plaintiff necessarily knows such facts.  (Wimberly v. Derby Cycle Corp. (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 618, 634.) Defendant’s remedy is to file a motion in limine to exclude Plaintiff from producing evidence at trial that Plaintiff did not disclose in discovery.  (Mitchell v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 269, 272.)

In response to SROG Nos. 2, 5, 11, 14, 17, 20, 26, 41, 44, 47, 50 and 53, Plaintiff states it has not completed reviewing documents to permit it to respond.  Such a response is improper.  Plaintiff must provide “complete and straightforward” responses to the SROG.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220, subds. (a)-(b).)  Plaintiff cannot unilaterally extend its deadline to complete responses to Defendant’s discovery.  It must, at minimum, identify and summarize responsive documents.  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783-784.)  If Plaintiff contends documents are in the possession of Defendant or others, Plaintiff must so state and specify the efforts Plaintiff has made to obtain the documents.  (Id., at p. 782.)  The court grants the motion to compel further responses to SROG Nos. 2, 5, 11, 14, 17, 20, 26, 41, 44, 47, 50 and 53.  Plaintiff is to serve the further responses to these SROG within 30 days.

The court declines to award sanctions to Defendant.  In its December 13, 2023 order, the court permitted Defendant to file this motion without attempting to informally resolve this dispute with Plaintiff so that Defendant could meet the jurisdictional deadline for the filing.  Under these circumstances, an award of sanctions would be unjust.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d) [monetary sanction not to be imposed on party who unsuccessfully opposes motion to compel when “circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust”].) 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and to file a proof of service concerning it.

ANALYSIS

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320, the court may compel a party to comply with its agreement to comply with a request for production of documents.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (a).)  In its December 13, 2023 order, the court ordered Plaintiffs to produce all documents in response to the RPD.  (See December 13, 2023 Minute Order.)  As such, the court has already entered the order Defendant seeks.  Defendant cites no authority for the proposition once again the court should compel Plaintiffs to comply with its December 13, 2023 order.  Rather, under the circumstances, Defendant’s remedy is to seek an issue, evidence, or monetary sanction.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (c).)  The court, therefore, denies Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiffs to produce responsive documents as moot. 

In opposition, Plaintiffs make various arguments regarding the burden of responding to the RPD and Defendant’s failure to comply with his discovery obligations.  These arguments are irrelevant.  The court has already ordered Plaintiffs to respond to the discovery, and any issue of Defendant’s lack of compliance is not before the court.  Further, Plaintiffs’ contention the court’s December 13, 2023 order did not require it to produce private financial information is incorrect.  The court ordered Plaintiffs to produce “all responsive confidential documents” after entry of a stipulated protective order.  The court entered the parties’ stipulated protective order on January 2, 2024.  As such, Plaintiffs were required to produce confidential documents under that protective order, including private financial information. 

Plaintiffs concede they produced responsive documents in batches, which were Bates- stamped consecutively.  (Declaration of Zoe M. Vallier, ¶ 12.)  This is improper.  Plaintiffs must sort and label the responsive documents to correspond to the categories of Defendant’s RPD.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.280, subd. (a).)  If Plaintiffs contend they do not have access to responsive documents, Plaintiffs must provide the name and address of anyone Plaintiffs believe might have such documents.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)  Plaintiffs apparently have not done so.  (Opposition to Motion to Compel Compliance with Court’s Order, p. 10.) 

The court finds Plaintiffs’ failure to properly produce responsive documents in compliance with this court’s December 13, 2023 order is an abuse of the discovery process and imposes sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010, subd. (g), 2031.320, subd. (c).)  The court imposes sanctions against Plaintiffs and their counsel of record, Ervin Cohen & Jessup, LLP, jointly and severally, in the amount of $2,260, which represents four hours of attorney time for Defendant to prepare this motion at $550 per hour, plus the filing fee.  Plaintiffs and their counsel of record are to pay sanctions in that amount to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 30 days of notice of this order.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
