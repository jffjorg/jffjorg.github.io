DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22LBCV00165    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: S25

Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant John Barrett, Jr.’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

Mr. John Cha, counsel for Defendant, Cross-Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant John Barrett, Jr., moves to be relieved as counsel.

Legal Standard

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. (2)). California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

1. Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2. A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under CCP §284(2), is brought instead of filing a consent under CCP §284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3. Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4. The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).) The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceedings or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court, (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case. (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

Tentative Ruling

The motion to be relieved as counsel complies with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a), (c), (d) and (e). The upcoming hearings scheduled are: (1) November 14, 2024 Case Management Conference, (2) February 21, 2025 Final Status Conference, (3) March 3, 2025 Trial Date. Exercising its discretion and finding no prejudice to Barrett, the court grants the motion.

3. Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4. The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).) The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court, (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case. (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

Tentative Ruling

The motion to be relieved as counsel complies with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a), (c), (d) and (e). The upcoming hearings scheduled are: (1) November 14, 2024 Case Management Conference, (2) February 21, 2025 Final Status Conference, (3) March 3, 2025 Trial Date. Exercising its discretion and finding no prejudice to Castro, the court grants the motion.

Case Number: 23LBCV02502    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On January 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Old Navy, LLC (“Old Navy”), GAP, LLC (“GAP”) and Does 1 to 20, alleging the following causes of action:

1. Discrimination on the Basis of Disability [Gov. Code §§ 12940 (a)];

2. Disability Discrimination – Reasonable Accommodation – Failure to Engage in Good Faith Interactive Process [GOV. CODE § 12940(n)];

3. Disability Discrimination – Failure to Make Reasonable Accommodation [Gov. Code § 12940(m)(1)];

4. Retaliation on the Basis of Request for Accommodation [Gov. Code § 12940(m) (2)]; and

5. Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy.

Plaintiff alleges that she was hired by Old Navy on July 6, 2016 and was employed by Old Navy for over 7 years until her termination on July 14, 2023, also alleging that she believes Old Navy and GAP to be her co-employers. (Compl., ¶¶ 11, 12.) Plaintiff further alleges that she was diagnosed with anxiety and depression and that in March of 2023, Old Navy learned of Plaintiff’s anxiety issues. (Compl., ¶¶ 13-14.) Plaintiff then alleges that in May of 2023, she experienced anxiety issues at work. (Compl., ¶ 15.) After Plaintiff had an anxiety attack on June 6, 2023 in her car that was observed by one of Plaintiff’s co-workers, Plaintiff was not allowed to return to work until she reached out to one of Old Navy’s online counselors for therapy; eventually, Plaintiff was terminated on July 14, 2023 because she violated company police when she went outside during her anxiety attack. (Compl., ¶¶ 16-22.) On February 20, 2024, Defendant Old Navy, LLC filed an answer. That same day, Defendant GAP, Inc. (erroneously sued and served as GAP, LLC) filed an answer.

On August 9, 2024, Defendant Old Navy filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant Old Navy’s Requests for Production (Set One). On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a response stating she does not oppose the instant motion.

Legal Standard

If a party to whom document demands are directed fails to respond at all, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a court order compelling answers thereto. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) All that needs to be shown is that the discovery was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. The moving party is not required to show a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the matter informally before filing this motion. A motion to compel initial discovery responses need not show good cause, meeting and conferring, or timely filing, and need not be accompanied by a separate statement. (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pac. Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The failure to timely respond also waives all objections.¿¿

On motion, a party may be relieved from its waiver of objections if: (1) the party subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance “with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280” and (2) “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a)(1)-(2).)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (c), provides that, "[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust." Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010, subd. (d), provides that a misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010, subd. (h), states that a misuse of the discovery process includes making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery. A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct under Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771.)

Analysis

In its motion, Defendant Old Navy provides that Requests for Production of Documents (Set One) was propounded on Plaintiff on March 29, 2024. (Deyoung Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s responses were due April 28, 2024 and the parties agreed to extend the deadline to respond to July 8, 2024; however, Plaintiff’s counsel still failed to serve any response to all of the Requests for Production of Documents except Request No. 28. (Deyoung Decl., ¶¶ 2-5.) Defendant Old Navy argues for Plaintiff to provide objection-less responses to Defendant Old Navy’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One.

Plaintiff filed a response stating that she does not oppose the instant motion. Plaintiff counsel asserts that he has been unable to provide responses as of this date because he was engaged in trial for another case entitled, Danny Salas v. Smart & Final Stores, LLC, et al., LASC Case No. BC724445. (Olivo Decl., ¶ 4.) Expecting the presentation of evidence from both sides in the Salas matter to likely conclude by September 3, 2024, Plaintiff counsel states that he will endeavor to have the responses provided during any down time for the trial or immediately after its conclusion. (Olivo Decl., ¶ 5.)

Tentative Ruling

The instant motion is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide objection-less responses to the Defendants’ Requests for Production of Documents (Set One), within 20 days from the date of the hearing.

The Court will not award monetary sanctions at this time based upon counsel’s representations; however, continued failure to respond to discovery by Plaintiff may result in future monetary sanctions.

Case Number: 24LBCV00239    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

Plaintiff filed his complaint against Defendants Roseann De Luca and Does 1 through 20 on February 6, 2024. On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an first amended complaint (“FAC”). On May 21, 2024, the Court overruled the Defendant’s Demurrer as to the 1st, 3rd, 4th and 5th causes of action in the FAC and sustained with leave to amend as to 2nd cause of action; the Court also granted in part Defendant’s request to strike portions of the FAC. (May 21, 2024 Minute Order.) On June 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (“SAC”), alleging the following causes of action:

1. Violation of Cal. Code. Regs. tit. 2 § 12176;

2. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress;

3. Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act;

4. Disability Discrimination – Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation; and

5. Disability Discrimination – Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process.

On or about October 5, 2023, Plaintiff Albert Simic (“Plaintiff”) responded to a rental advertisement on Zillow.com for an apartment located on 1337 South Gaffey Street #8, San Pedro, CA 90731. (SAC ¶ 17.) Defendant Roseann Rosman (“Defendant”) was the owner and property manager for the apartment. (SAC ¶ 18.) On October 6, 20023, Defendant responded to Plaintiff’s inquiry and informed Plaintiff of her no pets and no smoking policy. (SAC ¶ 19.) The following day, Plaintiff view the apartment alone. Subsequently, during a phone call, Plaintiff allegedly informed Defendant of his emotional support animal. (SAC ¶ 20.) On October 9, 2023, Defendant emailed the rental application to Plaintiff. In the email, Defendant stated “Please know I do NOT accept pets or other occupants other than you.” (SAC ¶ 21.) That same day, Plaintiff submitted a rental application and indicated to Defendant his difficulties in obtaining an extended credit report as required by Defendant. These difficulties were communicated to Defendant and Plaintiff offered to reimburse Defendant of any costs associated with obtaining an extended credit report. Defendant insisted on an extended credit report to allegedly obtain criminal background check on Plaintiff; Plaintiff ultimately obtained the extended credit report and provided the document to Defendant. Defendant acknowledged receipt of the extended report. (SAC ¶¶ 22-26.) In the evening of October 9, Defendant informed Plaintiff that a relative needed the apartment and asked Plaintiff to “wait.” The next day, using a “disguised” email address, Plaintiff contacted Defendant to ask if the apartment was available; Defendant responded that the apartment was available. Further, on or about November 5, Plaintiff received a Zillow notification that the apartment remained available, and the rental amount was reduced. (SAC ¶¶ 27-28.) Consequently, Plaintiff claims Defendant would not rent the apartment to him because of Plaintiff’s emotional support animal. In late November, Plaintiff informed Defendant of the “perceived discrimination” to which Defendant responded she “did not owe him any explanations” and was allegedly advised against creating unnecessary stress. (SAC ¶ 30.)

On July 5, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer with motion to strike. On August 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On August 28, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

Meet and Confer

The requirements are satisfied. Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).

Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice of Exhibit 1 is granted.

Tentative Ruling

Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc., § 430.1, Defendant demurs to the 1st through 5th causes of action on the ground that each cause of action is not sufficiently pleaded. Defendant also challenges the FAC on the grounds there are inconsistent allegations that violate the “sham pleading” doctrine.

Sham Pleading Doctrine

Regarding the same pleading doctrine, “[t]he general rule … is that material factual allegations in a verified pleading that are omitted in a subsequent amended pleading without adequate explanation will be considered by the court in ruling on a demurrer to the later pleading.” (Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 12-13.) Although a court generally must take the allegations in a complaint as true when ruling on a demurrer, “an exception exists where a party files an amended complaint and seeks to avoid the defects of a prior complaint either by omitting the facts that rendered the complaint defective or by pleading facts inconsistent with the allegations of prior pleadings.” (Owens v. Kings

Supermarket (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 379, 383-384.) “In these circumstances, the policy against sham pleading permits the court to take judicial notice of the prior pleadings and requires that the pleader explain the inconsistency. If he fails to do so the court may disregard the inconsistent allegations and read into the amended complaint the allegations of the superseded complaint.” (Id.) “The exception is reserved, however, for the extreme case, and it may not be indiscriminately applied; it ‘must be taken together with its purpose, which is to prevent amended pleading which is only a sham, when it is apparent that no cause of action can be stated truthfully.’ [Citations.]” (Amarel v. Connell (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 137, 144.)

Plaintiff previously alleged that Plaintiff’s ESA was a “dog” but now pleads in the SAC that Plaintiff’s ESA is a “cat”. Unlike in Amid v. Hawthorne Community Medical Group, Inc. (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1383, 1384-1385, wherein plaintiff in the fourth amended complaint alleged a sham allegation of an express oral nondisclosure agreement after the trial court properly sustained the defendant’s demurrer to the physician’s cause of action for intentional breach of confidential relationship on the ground that the plaintiff alleged no basis for a confidential relationship, and Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 751, wherein the omission of alternate allegation that defendant did not tell plaintiff she did not have breast cancer alleged in the second amended complaint did not render the third amended complaint a sham pleading vulnerable to demurrer in the plaintiff’s action against physicians for negligence and fraudulent concealment, here, the change of the ESA from the “dog” to “cat” does not materially alter the fundamental nature of the claims asserted. The Court finds Defendant’s “sham pleading” argument unpersuasive and the demurrer to the entire complaint is OVERRULED on that ground.

1st Cause of Action – Violation of Cal. Code. Regs. Title 2 § 12176

An individual with a disability seeking reasonable accommodation or modification must make a request for such accommodation or modification. (See CCR 2 § 12176, subd. (f)(1)). “A request for a reasonable accommodation or modification need not be made in a particular manner or at a particular time. An individual makes a reasonable accommodation or modification request at the time they request orally or in writing… because of a disability, regardless of whether the phrase “reasonable accommodation” or “reasonable modification” is used as part of the request. A request for a reasonable accommodation or reasonable modification may be made at any time, including during the inquiry or application process….” (CCR 2, § 12176 subd.(f)(1)(3).

The Court recognizes that case law is unclear whether a defendant may properly demur to an amended complaint on grounds overruled in a prior demurrer. (See Bennett v. Suncloud (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 91, 96-97 [holding that a trial judge was “foreclosed from rendering a new determination on the viability of” causes of action which had been the subject of a previous demurrer on the same grounds where the demurrer had been

overruled, “unless some new facts or circumstances were brought to his attention.”]; contra, Carlton v. Dr. Pepper Snapple Group, Inc. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1200, 1211 [plaintiff’s filing of a second amended complaint permitted renewed demurrer to entire complaint despite prior unsuccessful demurrer to same cause of action]; Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2024) ¶¶ 7:140-7:142.)

As stated above, Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff’s new allegation that he has a cat as an ESA does not substantially change the allegations as to the first cause of action. As Plaintiff points out, the Court previously overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC on May 21, 2024, finding that sufficient facts have been pled. (RJN ¶ 1 [May 21, 2024 Minute Order].) Because Defendant has not demonstrated that any of Plaintiff’s earlier allegations set forth in the FAC has been omitted or changed in the SAC, aside from the ESA fact, the demurrer as to the 1st cause of action is OVERRULED.

2nd Cause of Action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

IIED occurs under conditions where there is: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.) A defendant's conduct is “outrageous” when it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. And the defendant's conduct must be intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result. (Ibid.)

Here, Plaintiff has added new allegations as to Defendant’s refusal to accommodate Plaintiff’s disability and need for an Emotional Support Animal (“ESA”), despite Plaintiff’s explicit notification of his need, and Defendant’s subsequent acts of misleading Plaintiff about the apartment’s availability. (See SAC ¶¶ 40-43.) While alleged communications between the parties could be described as “untrue,” “deceitful” “evasive,” Defendant’s alleged conduct does not rise to the level of “extreme and outrageous” conduct that exceeds all bounds tolerated in a civilized society. Based upon the facts previously alleged and Plaintiff’s opposition, it does not appear that Plaintiff can cure this defect. The demurrer is sustained WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND to the 2nd cause of action.

3rd Cause of Action – Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act

Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff’s new allegation that he has a cat as an ESA does not substantially change the allegations as to the first cause of action. As Plaintiff points out, the Court previously overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC on May 21, 2024, finding that sufficient facts have been pled. (RJN ¶ 1 [May 21, 2024 Minute Order].) Moreover, the Unruh Civil Rights Act applies to businesses engaged in the selling or

leasing of residential housing, including owners, property managers, developers, and real estate brokers. (CCR 2 § 12005, subd. (f); see Marina Point, Ltd. v. Wolfson (1982) 30 Cal. 3d 721, 730-731; O'Connor v. Village Green Owners Association (1983) 33 Cal.3d 790, 794-795.) The demurrer as to the 3rd cause of action is OVERRULED.

4th and 5th Causes of Action – Disability Discrimination: Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation and Disability Discrimination: Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process

Plaintiff’s new allegation that he has a cat as an ESA does not substantially change the allegations as to the first cause of action. As Plaintiff points out, the Court previously overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC on May 21, 2024, finding that sufficient facts have been pled. (RJN ¶ 1 [May 21, 2024 Minute Order].) Because Defendant has not demonstrated that any of Plaintiff’s earlier allegations set forth in the FAC has been omitted or changed in the SAC, aside from the ESA fact, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 4th and 5th causes of action.
