DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV06977    Hearing Date: October 29, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MICHELLE RAMOS

                          

         vs.

 

CAROLINE RAMOS, et al.

 

 Case No.: 23STCV06977

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 29, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication is DENIED.

 

On 3/29/2023, Plaintiff Michelle Ramos (Plaintiff) filed suit. On 9/8/2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC) against Caroline Ramos, Katherine Ramos, and Rituel De Fille, Inc. (collectively, Defendants), alleging: (1) breach of license agreement; (2) breach of license agreement; (3) breach of fiduciary duty (partnership); (4) breach of fiduciary duty (corporate); (5) breach of employment agreement/wrongful termination; (6) misappropriation of trade secret; (7) accounting; and (8) unfair competition.

 

            On 7/25/2024, Plaintiff moved for summary adjudication of her Complaint.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moves this Court to summarily adjudicate the following: that (1) Michelle, Defendants and Cross-Complainants Katherine Ramos (Katherine) and Caroline Ramos (Caroline) formed a partnership for the manufacture and sale of cosmetics; (2) they agreed to incorporate a company in which they would be equal owners and to which they would license the IP they had created; (3) a lawful agreement to license the IP was fully performed by Michelle, Katherine, and Caroline on the one hand and the company (i.e., Defendants and Cross-Complainant Rituel de Fille, Inc. (RDF) they formed on the other; (4) that the unilateral and unjustified cancellation by RDF, Katherine and Caroline of the License Agreement and their continued use of the IP constituted misappropriation of the IP; and (5) Michelle was damaged because after cancellation of the license agreement she was no longer remunerated for the company’s use of the IP although Katherine and Caroline continued to be.

 

            In support, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-         In approximately 2012, Michelle, Caroline, and Katherine created a partnership for the purpose of developing and selling cosmetics. This included creating tangible and intangible assets, including proprietary formulas, packaging layout and design, testing, color and shade creation, scent development, and proprietary marketing systems (IP). They continued this work together for approximately ten years. (SS 1.)

 

-         In approximately the fall of 2013, they decided to form Rituel de Fille, Inc. (RDF) as a “C” corporation in which they would be equal shareholders. (SS 2.)

 

-         On August 28, 2013, they filed articles of incorporation with the State of California. Michelle was listed as the Treasurer. Katherine has been the President and “run the company” since 2013. (SS 3.)

 

-         Michelle, Katherine and Caroline signed a “LICENSE AGREEMENT” which bears the date August 28, 2013. (SS 4.)

 

-         The LICENSE AGREEMENT defines the Licensors as Michelle, Katherine, and Caroline, and the Licensee as RDF. It further states: “It is understood and agreed that the Licensor is the sole and exclusive owner of all rights, title and interest in and to the Licensed Property, defined as “intangible and tangible assets to include, proprietary formulas, packaging layout & design, testing, color & shade creation, scents development, and proprietary marketing systems for the sale of cosmetics to the public.” (SS 5.)

 

-         Pursuant to the LICENSE AGREEMENT, the “Licensee agrees to pay the Licensor the Royalty recited in Schedule A attached hereto based on the Licensee’s net Sales (the “Actual Royalty” in exchange for an exclusive right to utilize the Licensed Property. (SS 6.)

 

-         Beginning as early as April 21, 2016 and continuing through July 19, 2022 Katherine, Caroline and Michelle characterized the payments they received from RDF as royalties. (SS 7.)

 

-         In approximately 2018, the IRS initiated an audit of RDF’s 2017 tax return, during the course of which RDF asserted to the IRS that it paid RDF’s owners a royalty pursuant to the LICENSE AGREEMENT. (SS 8.)

 

-         After the IRS concluded the audit in 2019, Michelle, Katherine and Caroline continued to receive royalty payments from RDF through July of 2022. (SS 9.)

 

-         In August of 2022, Katherine and Caroline terminated the LICENSE AGREEMENT. (SS 10.)

 

-         The LICENSE AGREEMENT states: “If this Agreement is terminated . . . the Licensee . . . shall have not right to offer for sale, sell, advertise, promote, or use the Licensed Property or to use in any way any Promotional Material relating to Licensed Property.” It states further: “After the expiration of termination of this Agreement, all rights granted to the Licensee shall forthwith revert to the Licensor…” (SS 11.)

 

-         In July 2022, Katherine and Caroline removed Michelle Ramos as an officer and board member of RDF, and terminated her access to the company server. (SS 12.)

 

-         RDF continued to formulate and sell cosmetics after the termination of the License Agreement and the removal of Michelle Ramos as an officer and board member of RDF. (SS 13.)

 

-         Katherine Ramos and Caroline Ramos have received a salary from RDF on a monthly basis for the period, and holiday bonus, beginning July 2022. (SS 14.)

 

-         None of the founders of RDF ever received any salary, bonus or a W-2 from RDF prior to 2022. (SS 15.)

 

-         Michelle Ramos has received no salary, dividend, royalty or other compensation from RDF since August 2022. (SS 16.)

 

Plaintiff’s evidence supports a reasonable inference as to the five findings of fact sought in her motion. 

 

            However, as noted by Defendant, a motion for summary adjudication can only be granted “if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (CCP § 437c(f)(1).) Here, Plaintiff does not state what causes of action, affirmative defenses, damages, or issues of duty are the subject of her motion. Rather, she asks the Court to make five findings of fact. Such is not proper for a summary adjudication motion. Second, Plaintiff’s motion does not specifically state who she is moving against. As such, it is unclear from her motion whether she is moving against one Defendant, or all three.

 

Moreover, even setting aside these deficiencies, Defendants have set forth evidence sufficient to disclose a triable issue of material fact. More specifically, Defendants submitted evidence that:

 

-         Plaintiff did not join RDF until June 10, 2013 in exchange for her small financial investment. (SS 1.)

 

-         Prior to Michelle’s involvement, Katherine and Caroline developed the concept that became RDF and began developing all product formulas in May 2010. Prior to Michelle joining RDF, on the product development side, Katherine and Caroline developed extensive intellectual property including the RDF brand itself, product names, formulas, coloring, and packaging, which is is all owned by RDF. (Ibid.) 

 

-         The first time a draft of the 2019 License Agreement was provided to the Ramos Sisters was on December 12, 2018. Michelle admits that she backdated this agreement to August 28, 2013. (SS 29.)

 

-         The CPA Plaintiff hired to handle RDF’s taxes and financials is her family member, a convicted felon who lost his accounting license due to being convicted in 2009 of a felony for tax evasion (SS 26.)

 

-         Michelle intentionally misrepresented to the Ramos Sisters that the purpose of the 2019 Fraudulent License Agreement was only for tax purposes and that the business could not survive without this agreement. (AF 30.) Michelle backdated the 2019 Fraudulent License Agreement to 2013 because that was the date of RDF’s incorporation.

 

-         Michelle never owned any of RDF’s intellectual property. (SS 31.)

 

As such, Defendants submitted evidence that they were fraudulently induced to sign the License Agreement based on knowingly false representations by Plaintiff. “[A] contract induced by fraud renders the entire agreement voidable, permitting the aggrieved party to defend a suit on the contract by objecting to its enforcement because procured or induced by fraud.” (Filet Menu, Inc. v. C.C.L. & G, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 852, 861.) Defendants also submitted evidence that even assuming the License Agreement was valid, they would still be able to re-license property back to RDF, via, paragraph 13 (c) of the Agreement which notes that the rights shall “revert to Licensor who shall be free to license others to use the Property.” (AF 30.)

 

Taken together, Defendants’ evidence supports a reasonable inference that the License Agreement is invalid, and that Defendants did not breach it even if it were valid. Plaintiff’s motion did not adequately address her trade secret, unfair competition, wrongful termination, and accounting claims and thus did not meet her burden with respect to those claims. Moreover, there are procedural issues with Plaintiff’s motion which made adjudication inappropriate at this time.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 24STCV16493    Hearing Date: October 29, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MARIA MERCEES FAJARDO

 

 

         vs.

 

ALAL, LLC d/b/a KEI-AI LOS ANGELES HEALTHCARE CENTER

 

 Case No.:  24STCV16493

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: October 29, 2024

 

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. This action is stayed pending the completion of arbitration.

 

            On 7/2/2024, Plaintiff Maria Mercedes Fajardo (Plaintiff) filed suit against ALAL, LLC d/b/a KEI-AI Los Angeles Healthcare Center (Defendant), alleging: (1) disability discrimination; (2) failure to accommodate; (3) failure to engage in interactive process; (4) retaliation in violation of FEHA; (5) age discrimination; (6) failure to prevent discrimination and retaliation; and (7) wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

 

            On 8/30/2024, Defendant moved to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate her Complaint, and stay this action.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Where the Court has determined that an agreement to arbitrate a controversy exists, the Court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy …unless it determines that…  grounds exist for rescission of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) Among the grounds which can support rescission are fraud, duress, and unconscionability. (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 239.) The Court may also decline to compel arbitration wherein there is possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 (c).)

 

Discussion

 

1.      Defendants’ Burden

 

The party moving to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving [the] existence [of an arbitration agreement] by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) The moving party also bears the burden of demonstrating that the claims fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

           

A.    Existing Agreement

 

Defendant submitted evidence that Plaintiff entered into an Arbitration Agreement with Defendant on 4/292/2024. (Flores Decl., Exh. A.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that at the time the agreement was presented to her, she was unable to fully comprehend a contract presented only in English.

 

However, Defendant submitted evidence that because Plaintiff’s role is client-facing, the job description for Certified Nursing Assistant (Plaintiff’s position) specifically required as an essential function of her job, that the person “must be able to read, write, speak and understand English language.” (Flores Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. D.) Relatedly, Plaintiff signed a “Languages in the Workplace Acknowledgement” that similarly confirmed, as follows: “I understand that for the respect of our patients/residents, efficiency of communication, and the inclusion of colleagues while clocked in for work and in the common or patient areas of the facility, English is the language that must be spoken.” (Flores Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. E.)

 

As such, the preponderance of evidence does not support a conclusion that Plaintiff was unable to comprehend the English-language contract. Moreover, Defendant submitted evidence that a Spanish version of the arbitration agreement was also available for any employees who requested or preferred to review it in Spanish. (Flores Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

There was no deadline associated with the signing of the agreement, and thus Plaintiff had the opportunity to ask for clarification if there were aspects of the Agreement she was unfamiliar with, or was confused by. Plaintiff does not indicate that she ever availed herself of this opportunity.

 

Given that Defendant has established by a preponderance of the evidence that an arbitration agreement exists, and that Plaintiff’s claims are covered by that agreement, the burden shifts to the Plaintiff to establish that the arbitration clause should not be enforced. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236. (Pinnacle).)

 

2.      Plaintiff’s Burden

 

The party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence any defense, such as unconscionability or duress. (Pinnacle, supra, 55 Cal.4th at p. 236.)

 

“Unconscionability has both procedural and substantive elements. Although both must appear for a court to invalidate a contract or one of its individual terms, they need not be present in the same degree: ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’”  (Roman v. Superior Court (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469. (“Roman”).) Where the degree of procedural unconscionability is low, “the agreement will be enforceable unless the degree of substantive unconscionability is high.” (Ajamian v. CantorCO2e (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 771, 796 (Ajamian).)

 

A.    Procedural Unconscionability

 

“Procedural unconscionability focuses on the elements of oppression and surprise. ‘Oppression arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and an absence of meaningful choice … Surprise involves the extent to which the terms of the bargain are hidden in a ‘prolix printed form’ drafted by a party in a superior bargaining position.’ ” (Roman, supra, 172 Cal.App.4th at p. 1469.)

 

Plaintiff argues that the agreement is procedurally unconscionable because (1) it is a contract of adhesion, and (2) a copy of the applicable AAA rules were not attached to the agreement.

 

I.       Adhesion Contract

 

Where pre-employment adhesion contracts are involved, a degree of procedural unconscionability is always present. This is because, “the arbitration agreement stands between the employee and necessary employment, and few employees are in a position to refuse a job because of an arbitration agreement.” (Little v. Auto Stiegler, Inc. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1064, 1071 (Little).)  However, where “there is no other indication of oppression or surprise, the degree of procedural unconscionability of an adhesion agreement is low, and the agreement will be enforceable unless the degree of substantive unconscionability is high.” (Ajamian, supra, 203 Cal.App.4th at p. 796.)

 

Therefore, while Plaintiff is correct that including the arbitration clause within a contract of adhesion indicates a degree of procedural unconscionability, there must be other indications of oppression or surprise to render the agreement unenforceable. (Little, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p.1071; Ajamian, supra, 203 Cal.App.4th at p. 796.)

 

II.               Copy of AAA-Rules

 

As additional evidence of procedural unconscionability, Plaintiff points to Defendant’s failure to attach a copy of the applicable JAMS-rules to the Arbitration Agreement. However, courts have held that a failure to attach arbitration rules is insufficient, by itself, to sustain a finding of procedural unconscionability. (Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) Furthermore, the applicable rules are available online.

 

The Court concludes that Defendants’ failure to directly attach a copy of the applicable JAMS rules does not amount to procedural unconscionability. Because Plaintiff has shown no other indication of oppression or surprise outside of the existence of an employment adhesion agreement, the agreement will be enforceable unless Plaintiff can demonstrate a high degree of substantive unconscionability. (Ajamian, supra, Cal.App.4th at p. 796.)

 

B.    Substantive Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the Arbitration Agreement is substantively unconscionable because (1) it has a carve-out for preliminary/injunctive relief; (2) fails to guarantee an award of attorney fees to the Plaintiff if she is the prevailing party; (3) it has non-mutual provisions; and (4) it prohibits representative PAGA actions.

 

“Substantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create ‘overly harsh’ or ‘‘one-sided’ results,’ that is, whether contractual provisions reallocate risks in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.” (Roman, supra, 172 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1469-1470.) Substantive unconscionability ‘may take various forms,’ but typically is found in the employment context when the arbitration agreement is ‘one-sided,’ in favor of the employer without sufficient justification. (Ibid.) Common examples of substantive unconscionability include “when ‘the employee's claims against the employer, but not the employer's claims against the employee, are subject to arbitration’” (Ibid.), or when employees are required to pay excessive fees to gain access to the administrative forum. (Armendariz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at p. 90-91.)

 

“Where appropriate, courts have discretion to sever or limit the application of unconscionable provisions and enforce the remainder of an arbitration agreement under Civil Code section 1670.5, subdivision (a).” (Ramos, supra, 28 Cal.App.5th at p. 1065.) Circumstances in which severability can be appropriate include where the unconscionability can be cured by striking the offending clause or clauses. (Id. at p. 1069.)

 

As for the first contention, the carve-out for preliminary and/or injunctive relief applies equally to employer and employee. Thus, while Plaintiff may contend that Defendant is more likely to avail itself of this provision, there are a number of injunctive relief claims an employee may pursue. As such, the Court is unpersuaded that this is an indication of substantive unconscionability.

 

As for the second contention, the agreement provides that “[i]f any party prevails on a statutory claim that affords the prevailing party attorneys’ fees and costs . . . the Arbitrator may award reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs to the prevailing party.” (Flores Decl., Exh. A.) As such, the Agreement does not limit the ability to recover attorney fees or any type of relief otherwise available in court because the Arbitration Agreement states that the Arbitrator “has the authority to award the Parties all form[s] of relief that would otherwise be available to the Parties in a court of law …” (Id.) As such, the Court interprets the Agreement’s use of “may award” as affirming the arbitrator’s ability to award any fees and costs Plaintiff would otherwise be entitled to as prevailing party. The Court is therefore unpersuaded that this is an indication of substantive unconscionability.

 

As for the third contention, Plaintiff argues that there is non-mutuality as to the confidentiality provision and the covered parties impacted by the Arbitration Agreement. However, Plaintiff offers no substantive explanation as to how the provision “JAMS and the Arbitrator shall maintain the confidential nature of the Arbitration proceeding and the Award, including the Hearing, . . . .” is nonmutual, nor did Plaintiff submit any caselaw which could show a similar provision has been found to be substantively unconscionable. As to coverage scope, Plaintiff argues that:

 

The Agreement required Ms. Fajardo to arbitrate any claims she “may have against the Company or against its officers, directors, supervisors, managers, employees, or agents in their capacity as such . . . .” Thus, while the Agreement requires Ms. Fajardo to arbitrate her claims against anyone affiliated with Defendant,” it only requires “the Company” (defined narrowly as Kei-Ai) to arbitrate its claims against Ms. Fajardo.

 

            (Opp., 14: 6-9.)

 

            However, tellingly, Plaintiff did not cite any analogous caselaw which could show that Defendant’s inclusion of its officers, directors, supervisors, managers, employees, or agents was overly broad or an indication of unconscionability.

 

Finally, Section 5(b) of the Agreement purports to waive the employee’s right to bring or participate in any representative action “in arbitration or otherwise.” Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint does not contain a representative PAGA action. Therefore, while the arbitration agreement’s PAGA waiver is unenforceable as a matter of law, it can easily be severed from the Complaint without fundamentally altering the parties’ agreement regarding the scope of arbitration and the powers of the arbitrators to provide relief. (Ramos, supra, 28 Cal.App.5th at p. 1065.)

 

The Court concludes that Plaintiff has failed to show any evidence of substantive unconscionability. While Plaintiff has demonstrated a degree of procedural unconscionability, he has not demonstrated any degree of substantive unconscionability. As a result, the Court finds that the arbitration agreement is enforceable. (Ajamian, supra, Cal.App.4th at p. 796.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is granted. This action is stayed pending the completion of arbitration.

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC587400    Hearing Date: October 29, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

TIMOTHY JANG, M.D.

                          

         vs.

 

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES dba HARBOR-UCLA MEDICAL CENTER, et al.

 

                                         

 Case No.:  BC587400

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 29, 2024

 

 

The Court GRANTS IN PART, DENIES IN PART Defendant’s motion to tax costs. The Court taxes $100 from Item 2, $395 from Item 4, and $37,394.11 from Item 16, for a total of $37,889.11.

 

On 7/7/205, Plaintiff Timothy Jang M.D. (Plaintiff) filed suit against the County of Los Angeles d/b/a Harbor UCLA Medical Center and Roger Lewis M.D., alleging: (1) Health and Safety Code section 1278.5; (2) Labor Code section 98.6 and 1102.5; (3) Business and Professions Code section 2056; (4) discrimination; (5) retaliation; (6) harassment; and (7) failure to prevent harassment, discrimination, and/or retaliation.

 

On 8/13/2024, Defendant County of Los Angeles (Defendant) moved to tax costs.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant moves to tax the following costs:

 

-         Item 1: Filing & Motion Fees

-         Item 2: Jury Fees

-         Item 4: Deposition Costs

-         Item 5: Service of Process

-         Item 8: Expert Witness Fees

-         Item 11: Court Reporter Fees

-         Item 12: Models, enlargements, and photocopies

-         Item 14: E-Filing or Service Fees

-         Item 16: Other, Miscellaneous

 

As for Item 1, Defendant argues that “[s]pecifically, plaintiff is claiming $645.00 for CourtCall Remote appearance fees, including $90.00 in late fees. These are neither a filing nor motion fee within the meaning of CCP §1033.5. These remote appearances were not mandatory, but rather a “convenience” to plaintiff’s counsel and therefore should be disallowed…. Further, plaintiff is claiming $61.65 in filing fees in connection with a post-trial motion on an equitable relief claim, which ultimately denied, and therefore the County, not plaintiff is the prevailing party on that claim. Lastly, plaintiff is claiming $8.00 in retrieval fees for retrieving documents from the Court’s website, which are not filing or motion fees within the meaning of CCP §1033.5.” (Motion, 5: 28-6:9.)

 

            However, a cost is not disallowed merely because it is not listed in the statute as recoverable. (Applegate v. St. Francis Lutheran Church (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 361 363-364.) Under the FEHA, the award of costs is left to the discretion of the Court.

 

            Remote appearances create a number of efficiencies, and Plaintiff’s associated attorney fee motion would have been much higher had Plaintiff sent an attorney to appear in-person to every case management conference or status conference. Defendant did not cite any authority to show that Plaintiff cannot recover the filing fee for the motion for equitable relief because the Court denied it. Plaintiff is entitled as a matter of right to recovery of costs for filing fees under CCP section 1033.5 because he is the prevailing party in the action. The Court does not evaluate the prevailing party on the basis of who prevailed on each underlying motion. Retrieval fees are not listed as a disallowed cost under CCP section 1033.5, and those costs were reasonable and necessary to the litigation of this action. As such, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that the claimed costs are recoverable.

 

            As for Item 2, Defendant argues that Plaintiff miscalculated the total claimed cost for jury fees, and moves to strike a portion of claimed costs. In opposition, Plaintiff agreed:

 

Plaintiff inadvertently omitted the receipt for jury fees paid on August 14, 2024, in the amount of $156.73. It is included as an exhibit in the declaration of Kelsey Ciarimboli, filed concurrently with this motion. (Ciarimboli Dec., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff does not dispute that his Memorandum of Costs included a typographical error which resulted in incorrectly reporting incurred charges of $607.99; in fact, the charges were for $507.99, a difference of $100. Plaintiff withdraws the $100 overstatement charge.

 

            Accordingly, the Court strikes $100 from the claimed costs.

 

            As for Item 4, Defendant argues that Plaintiff miscalculated the total claimed cost for deposition costs, and seeks to strike $1,475.10 of the $11,011.77 in claimed costs. In opposition, Plaintiff agreed to withdraw the costs associated with the cancellation of Dr. Jang’s deposition, $384.60. However, Plaintiff argues that the Court should award the $395 cancellation fee for the deposition of Victoria Wilkerson, Defendant’s retained expert, contending:

 

This cancellation was necessitated because Defendant failed to produce the documents in Plaintiff’s deposition notice of Wilkerson. Defendant only produced the witness’s CV and report the day before the deposition, even though the documents must be produced three business days in advance as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.415. (Ciarimboli Dec., ¶ 5.) Defendant did not comply at all with several of the document inspection requests. Accordingly, because Defendant had only partially and belatedly complied with the document inspection demands in the deposition notice, Plaintiff was forced to reschedule the deposition and met and conferred with Defendant regarding the non-compliance. (Ciarimboli Dec., ¶ 6.)

 

            (Opp., 10: 7-16.)

 

            The Court strikes $384.60 in costs but awards the remaining costs. CCP section 1033.5, subdivision (b) does not list deposition cancellation fees as a disallowed cost, and the Court finds that, in light of the circumstances, it was reasonable for Plaintiff to reschedule the deposition and meet and confer.

 

            As for Item 5, Defendant challenges $2,035.48 for service of process costs. However, after review, the Court finds the costs actually and reasonably incurred.

 

            As for Item 8, Defendant argues that Plaintiff impermissibly seeks to recover expert witness fees. Costs such as expert witness fees not ordered by the court, or attorney fees that require a court determination, may not be “immediately enter [ed]” by the clerk, and instead necessitate a decision by the trial court, exercising its discretion. (Anthony v. City of Los Angeles (2008) 166 Cal. App. 4th 1011, 1016.) Defendant did not advance any substantive argument as to why the Court should not, in its discretion, award these fees. Plaintiff prevailed on his FEHA retaliation claim, with the jury finding that Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff based on his engaging in FEHA protected activities.  As such, these expert witness fees were reasonably necessary and contributed to Plaintiff prevailing on his claim.  Accordingly, the Court finds these fees to be recoverable.

 

            As for Item 11, Defendant argues that Plaintiff should not recover for $3,442.29 for costs associated with models and enlargements to aid the jury at trial. Models, the enlargements of exhibits and photocopies of exhibits, and the electronic presentation of exhibits, including costs of rental equipment and electronic formatting, may be allowed if they were reasonably helpful to aid the trier of fact. (CCP § 1033 5(a)(13).) In order to secure recovery, models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits must have actually been used at trial to aid the trier of fact. (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal. 4th 761, 774.) Here, Plaintiff submitted expenses associated with printing trial exhibits, exhibit binders, jury questionnaires for use during voir dire, and Post-It Note Boards, which Mr. Bohm used frequently during Plaintiff’s case in chief for demonstrative purposes. (Ciarimboli Dec., ¶ 9.) The Court finds these costs to be actually and reasonably incurred.

 

            As for Item 12, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot recover for non-Court ordered transcripts and reporter fees citing CCP section 1033.5, subdivision (b). However, Costs under the FEHA are governed by Government Code section 12965, subdivision (b), which does not disallow recovery of daily transcripts. The Court has discretion to award this cost under the FEHA. As set forth in Plaintiff’s opposition, daily transcripts were necessary in order to better prepare the case against Defendant. (See Opp., 12: 12-14.) Accordingly, the Court awards these costs in its discretion.

 

            As for Item 14, electronic filing fees are an expressly allowable cost where electronic filing is required by local rules. (CCP § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14).) Since January 2, 2019, the Superior Court for the County of Los Angeles has required electronic filing. (See Los Angeles County Local Rule 3.4(a).) Plaintiff submitted the receipts associated with these costs, which were all associated with this case. (Ciarimboli Dec., ¶ 11.) Thus, these costs are reasonable and necessary on their face.

 

            As for Item 16, Plaintiff seeks $37,384.11 for “other” unspecified costs. As set forth in Defendant’s motion, this sum include such costs as:

 

-         7/31/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $149.02

-         8/01/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $170.54

-         8/1/23 Dinner at Mercado for $145.47

-         8/2/23 Dinner at Sushi Roku for $395.29

-         8/2/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $170.54

-         8/03/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $196.44

-         8/08 Lunch at Agua Viva for $333.61

-         8/9/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $334.70

-         8/10/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $280.71

-         8/10/23 Dinner at Café Santorini for $243.70

-         8/11/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $279.62

-         8/15/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $364.89

-         8/16/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $346.56

-         8/18/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $516.60

-         8/22/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $468.11

-         8/24/23 Dinner at Nick’s for $159.95

-         8/24 Dinner at Nick’s for $163.36

-         8/25/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $313.42

-         8/28/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $678.50

-         8/29/23 Dinner at Café Santorini for $356.25

-         8/29/23 Lunch at Agua Viva for $324.47

-         8/30/23 Dinner at Terra at Eataly for $924.66

-         AirBnb Receipt for $1,395.00

-         Target Receipt for groceries for $118.41

-         Dinner at Sushi Roku for $299.95

-         Whole Foods Groceries for $36.58

-         Hotel Dena for $901.68

-         Dinner at The Arbour for $174.29

-         Airfare for $218.98

-         Airfare for $276.99

-         Airfare for $263.98

-         Hotel Dena for $214.96

-         Travel Expenses for Jack Brouwer, including a $147.82 Uber Ride

-         Airfare for $218.98

-         AirBnB Receipt for $1,943.42

-         AirBnB Receipt for $4,380.10

 

As noted by Plaintiff, these costs are discretionary under FEHA and the CCP. After review, the Court finds that all of Plaintiff’s “Other” costs were convenient, rather than necessary, to this litigation. The Court taxes the entire amount of “Other” costs totaling $37,384.11.

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court grants in part, denies in part Defendant’s motion to tax costs. The Court taxes $100 from Item 2, $395 from Item 4, and $37,394.11 from Item 16, for a total of $37,889.11.

 

 

It is so ordered.
