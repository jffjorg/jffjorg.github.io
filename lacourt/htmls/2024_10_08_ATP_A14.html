Case Number: 20AVCV00801    Hearing Date: October 8, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury – motor vehicle action. Plaintiff Ashley Cabrera, by and through her guardian ad litem, Carol Leon (“Plaintiff”), alleges that Defendants Scott Ali Fishback (“Fishback”) and Antelope Valley Schools Transportation Agency (“AVSTA” and collectively “Defendants”) were negligent and their acts were the legal and proximate cause of injuries and damages to Plaintiff. Plaintiff further alleges that the acts led to a motor vehicle accident that occurred on April 26, 2019 at the northbound 101 Freeway near Los Osos Valley Road.

 

On November 09, 2020, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging one cause of action for Motor Vehicle.

 

On February 11, 2021, AVTA filed its Answer.

 

On October 10, 2022, Plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney, substituting Lane Grey Friedman for Gary A. Laff.

 

On December 29, 2022, Gregory E. Stone was associated as co-counsel in this matter for Plaintiff.

 

On January 05, 2024, the Vartazarian Law Firm, APC was associated in on behalf of Plaintiff in this matter.

 

On August 07, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held.

 

On August 12, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories from Plaintiff Ashley Cabrera (“Motion to Compel Further Special Interrogatories”).

 

On August 22, 2024, Defendants filed an Ex Parte Application for Order Continuing the Trial Date and all Related Dates, subsequently granted on August 26, 2024.

 

On September 11, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Complete the Defense Medical Examination by Joshua Prager, M.D., M.S. (“Motion to Compel Medical Examination”).

 

On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition to the Motion to Compel Further Special Interrogatories only.

 

On October 01, 2024, Defendants filed their Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to the Motion to Compel Further Special Interrogatories and a Notice of Non-Opposition to the Motion to Compel Medical Examination.

 

On October 02, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition to the Motion to Compel Medical Examination. An Opposition “shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days. . .before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) The hearing on this matter is set for October 08, 2024. The Court notes that September 27, 2024 was a holiday. Accordingly, an Opposition was due by September 24, 2024. However, “No paper may be rejected for filing on the ground that it was untimely submitted for filing. If the court, in its discretion, refuses to consider a late filed paper, the minutes or order must so indicate.” (California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1300(d).)

 

On October 02, 2024, after the Opposition to the Motion to Compel Medical Examination was filed, Defendants filed a Sur-Reply to the aforementioned Opposition.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Further Interrogatories – Under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, a court may order a party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the court finds that any of the following apply:¿¿(1) an answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete; (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate; and (3) an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(a).)¿Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).)¿¿“Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220(a).) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible and, if the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party. (Id. at (b)-(c).) “If only a part of an interrogatory is objectionable, the remainder of the interrogatory shall be answered. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc § 2030.240(a).) If an objection is made to an interrogatory or to a part of an interrogatory, the specific ground for the objection shall be set forth clearly in the response. If an objection is based on a claim of privilege, the particular privilege invoked shall be clearly stated. If an objection is based on a claim that the information sought is protected work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010), that claim shall be expressly asserted. (Id. at (b).)¿

Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal. App. 4th 249, 258.) 

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(a) states, “[i]n any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff, if both of the following conditions are satisfied:¿(1) The examination does not include any diagnostic test¿or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive.¿ (2) The examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.”¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2032.220(a).) Subdivision (b) provides, “[a]¿defendant may make a demand under this article without leave of court after that defendant has been served or has appeared in the action, whichever occurs first.”¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2032.220(b).)¿

This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration…”

Discussion

 

Judicial Notice – Defendants request judicial notice of the following:

 

·         Exhibit D – Food and Drug Administration Warning Label for Accutane;

·         Exhibit E – Food and Drug Administration Information Page for Accutane;

·         Exhibit F – Food and Drug Administration “Isotretinoin Capsule Information;” and

·         Exhibit G – Publication by American Academy of Dermatology Association Listing of Known Side Effects of Accutane

 

 

All requests for judicial notice are GRANTED under Cal. Evid. Code §§ 452(c) for U.S. Food and Drug Administration publications, and 452(h) for the publication by the American Academy of Dermatology Association.

 

Application – Both motions concern Plaintiff’s responses to inquiries regarding Accutane. Specifically, the special interrogatory at issue, Special Interrogatory No. 11, asks if Plaintiff took Accutane, and the Motion to Compel Medical Examination pertains to questions asked by Joshua Prager, M.D., M.S. (“Dr. Prager”) regarding medication Plaintiff is taking or has taken and follow-up questions.

 

Regarding Special Interrogatory No. 11, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s objection on the basis of privacy is meritless as Accutane is known for side effects, including those that overlap with Plaintiff’s claimed symptomology and damages, and so the Accutane question is necessary to evaluate Plaintiff’s injury claims and Plaintiff is the only person who knows when she took the medication. Defendants further present that Plaintiff has waived her right to privacy by making injury contentions on the same subject matter.

 

Plaintiff argues that her use of prescription acne medication is not discoverable because it is irrelevant to this case. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that defense counsel does not demonstrate any medical evidence or diagnosis that prescription acne medication is related to Plaintiff’s injuries. Plaintiff presents that her acne medication is protected by the California constitution. Plaintiff believes that, if the acne treatment is related to Plaintiff’s symptomology, the Court should order Dr. Prager to submit an expert declaration and presents that Dr. Prager will not want or be able to testify because it will open the subject to cross-examination and be disproved by Plaintiff’s expert.

 

Defendants’ Reply reiterates their moving arguments: (1) the information is relevant as it goes to Plaintff’s claims, (2) the information can only be discovered from the Plaintiff; (3) Plaintiff’s privacy objections are without merit as the medication needs to be explored as to the causation of the claimed injuries; and (4) the question is narrowly tailored.

 

The constitutional right of privacy is not absolute; it may be abridged to accommodate a compelling public interest. One such interest, as evidenced by California's broad discovery statutes, is “the historically important state interest of facilitating the ascertainment of truth in connection with legal proceedings.” (Moskowitz v. Superior Court (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 313, 316 [overruled on other grounds], citing Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 13 Cal.3d 844, 857.) Moreover, an appropriate protective order can make an invasion of privacy non-serious. (TBG Ins. Services v. Superior Court (Zieminski) (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 454; Britt, supra, 20 Cal. 3d at 849.) No partial limitation is required here as it appears the privacy claim was waived. That is, when a plaintiff places their medical conditions at issue by the allegations in their Complaint, they must expect that discovery will include relevant medical history which relates to his claimed injuries and, here, (1) Plaintiff has alleged fibromyalgia and migraines, hills sachs lesion as a result of the incident in her response to Special Interrogatory No. 2. (see Motion to Compel Further Special Interrogatory, Exh. C); and (2) Defendants have highlighted that headaches and musculoskeletal symptoms are known side effects of Accutane (see id. at Exh. D). (See Britt, supra, 20 Cal.3d at 864; see also Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 842.) Plaintiff has placed conditions such as headaches and musculoskeletal injuries at issue for this case and Defendants have shown that Accutane may affect such injuries or damages.

 

The Motion to Compel Further Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

Regarding the Motion to Compel Further Examination, Defendants present that the statutory scheme does not bar an inquiry into medical history and cite to case precedent to show that courts have allowed doctors at medical examinations to ask questions in order to form opinions. Defendants contend that the questions Dr. Prager wanted to ask concern Plaintiff’s claimed injuries, treatments, and are needed to determine any interactions between medication and symptomology. Defendants highlight Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatory responses (Motion to Compel Medical Examination, Exh. B); and Plaintiff’s own expert Laura Edinger, D.O., was able to opine about the subject matter during her examination (id. at Exh. C).

 

Plaintiff presents that Defendants are not entitled to an oral medical interrogation of Plaintiff and that they are only allowed to interview Plaintiff at her deposition. Plaintiff argues that she provided her medical history and prescription usage in the full medical records that have been produced.

 

The Court is not swayed by Plaintiff’s arguments. Defendants have shown that there is overlap between Plaintiff’s medication and her claimed symptomology resulting from the incident that underlies this case. (See analysis ante.) The Court’s previous analysis applies as Plaintiff has claimed migraines and hills sachs lesions (see Motion to Compel Medical Examination, Exh. B.) and, for the evidence shows Plaintiff’s own expert felt the need to opine on headaches, migraines, nerve blocks, etc. (id. at Exh. C.) It appears that good cause exists for some inquiry into Plaintiff’s medical history and if the medications affect any of Plaintiff’s presented symptomology. However, Defendants have not provided a scope. The moving papers simply discuss questions regarding medication. The Court is not inclined to allow this medical examination to become a fishing expedition. The Court inquires at the hearing as to scope.

 

Plaintiff has also provided case precedent that a plaintiff should be permitted to have the assistance and protection of an attorney at a medical examination.

 

Defendants’ Sur-Reply is inapposite as it simply requests the Court to exercise its discretion with respect to its consideration of Plaintiff’s belated Opposition.

 

The Motion to Compel Medical Examination is GRANTED subject to a scope to be determined at the hearing and an attorney’s presence.

Conclusion

 

Defendants Antelope Valley Schools Transportation Agency and Scott Fishback’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories from Plaintiff Ashley Cabrera is GRANTED.

 

Defendants Antelope Valley Schools Transportation Agency and Scott Fishback’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Complete the Defense Medical Examination by Joshua Prager, M.D., M.S., is GRANTED subject to a scope to be determined at the hearing.

Case Number: 22AVCV00005    Hearing Date: October 8, 2024    Dept: A14

The Motion to Quash is DENIED. Defendants moved under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10 to quash the service of the summons. Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10 provides, in relevant part:

(a) A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:

(1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.

[. . .]

(d) No default may be entered against the defendant before expiration of his or her time to plead, and no motion under this section, or under Section 473 or 473.5 when joined with a motion under this section, or application to the court or stipulation of the parties for an extension of the time to plead, shall be deemed a general appearance by the defendant.

(e) A defendant or cross-defendant may make a motion under this section and simultaneously answer, demur, or move to strike the complaint or cross-complaint.

[. . .]

(3) Failure to make a motion under this section at the time of filing a demurrer or motion to strike constitutes a waiver of the issues of lack of personal jurisdiction, inadequacy of process, inadequacy of service of process, inconvenient forum, and delay in prosecution.

Here, the time for Defendants to respond to the Complaint has passed. Defendants moved to set aside their defaults which was subsequently denied. Only upon a denial of their Motion to Set Aside did Defendants move to quash the service of the Summons and Complaint. That is, Defendants did not collaterally bring a Motion to Quash with the Motion to Set Aside as contemplated by Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(d). The Court believes that Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(e)(3)’s waiver is necessarily applicable to this motion as Defendants attempted to file a pleading to set aside their defaults without disputing issues of lack of personal jurisdiction, inadequacy of process, inadequacy of service of process, inconvenient forum, and delay in prosecution.

Defendants have also filed a document titled “Response to Application for Default Judgment.” The Court, in its discretion, STRIKES this document as a pleading “pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) This document is not premised on any statute nor is it a noticed motion. The Court further notes that this document is inapposite as there is no request for default judgment that has not been ruled upon. (See infra.)

For clarity, the Court’s records show that Plaintiffs’ Default Judgment was denied on July 05, 2024. Plaintiffs’ complaint does not include an amount for damages. (See Complaint, Prayer for Relief.) Plaintiffs attempted to rectify this via the filing of two Statement of Damages on June 13, 2024. However, Defendants were placed in default on March 13, 2024, over a year before the service of a Statement of Damages. Further, Statement of Damages are used for personal injury, wrongful death cases, or cases seeking punitive damages. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 425.11 and 425.115.) Here, the case is marked as a wrongful eviction case and the causes of action suggest breach of contract, habitability, negligence, and harassment among other things. The Court notes that there are requests for treble and punitive damages; however, the June 13, 2024 filed Statements of Damages do not request punitive damages. Thus, it appears that the filed Statements of Damages were not used for the purposes contemplated by Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 425.11 and 425.115, but instead were used as an attempt to circumvent the notice issue that constitutes a deficiency for a potential default judgment.

Demurrer

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿ A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified (Dec. 23, 2003).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

-----¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Meet and Confer Requirement– Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41 provides that a declaration may be filed where he party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith. (Id. at (a)(3)(B).) Defense counsel Sam M. Muriella (“Muriella”) has filed a declaration stating that he attempted to contact Frank; however, a response was not received. “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Id. at (a)(4).) Accordingly, the Court addresses the Demurrer on its merits.

 

-----

 

Application – Defendants first present that the SAC is uncertain. That is, Defendants believe that the SAC reads as a stream of conscious letter, lacks coherence, does not state cognizable claims, and the causes of action does not specify which parties are plaintiffs or defendants to each.

 

Pursuant to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(f), “uncertainty” is a proper demurrer ground where the pleading is ambiguous and unintelligible.¿ Demurrers for uncertainty will only be sustained where the complaint is so vague that the defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., determine what issues must be admitted or denied or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.¿ (Khoury v. Maly’s Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

A special demurrer should not be sustained when the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.¿(Lord v. Garland (1946) 27 Cal.2d 840, 853.)¿Such a demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach the failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading, but is directed at the uncertainty existing in the allegations actually made. (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.) ¿A demurrer which attacks an entire pleading should be overruled if one of the counts therein is not vulnerable to the objection.¿ (Lord, supra, 27 Cal.2d at 850.) ¿The sole issue on a demurrer for ambiguity is whether “the allegations are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues that must be met, even if the allegations of the complaint may not be as clear and as detailed as might be desired.”¿(Merlino v. West Coast Macaroni Mfg. Co. (1949) 90 Cal. App. 2d 106, 108.) 

 

Here, the SAC is unclear as the allegations mix Frank’s own interests with that of others. (See SAC at 2:25-3:4 [discussing Downey], 5:5-6 [discussing Davis], 4:22-28 [discussing Langham], among others.) As such, the Complaint lacks coherence as the individual rights of each previous plaintiff are blended in with allegations pertaining to Frank. Plaintiff’s SAC also appears to contain a multitude of conclusory allegations as well. Conclusory allegations, without facts to support them, are ambiguous and uncertain. (Ankeny v. Lockheed Missiles & Space Co. (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 531.) Throughout the SAC, there are references criminal and civil law as well as federal, state, and city codes. The Complaint is unclear as to whether Cockrell has a private right of action to bring the alleged breaches of Cal. Pen. Code statutes (see SAC at Fourth Cause of Action.) Further, there appears to be references to undefined nouns. (See SAC [“management/owners” (4:18), “manager” (3:12), “Management” (4:21), “Defendant Heirs” (6:8), “owners” (6:22), among others].)

 

As pled, the Court agrees with Moving Defendants that the Complaint is so uncertain that Moving Defendants cannot reasonably respond. As pled, the Complaint would require Defendants to determine which claims are being asserted against it and which alleged actions not only apply to Defendants, but also comprise each claim.

 

Frank’s response does not address the deficiencies within the SAC. Rather, it contains reiterations of the allegations and Frank’s ability to prove them at trial.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.) 

 

This is Frank’s third pleading and there are no substantial changes from the FAC. (Compare FAC and SAC.) Accordingly, it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.

 

The Court does not allow leave to amend.
