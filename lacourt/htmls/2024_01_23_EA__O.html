DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 22PSCV00580    Hearing Date: January 23, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

Defendant BO SUN’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens is GRANTED, notably for the reason by way of striking the quiet title COA, none of the remaining COAs would affect title or right to possession of the Joint Venture Properties; attorney fees are not imposed on either party.

 

Background

 

This is a real property case involving properties located at 16485 Monte Cristo Drive, Hacienda Heights, CA 91745 (the “Monte Cristo Property”), 1621 Derringer Lane, Diamond Bar, CA (the “Derringer Lane Property”), and 14211 Skyline Drive, Hacienda Heights, CA (the “Skyline Property”). Plaintiff Wendy Lin (“Lin” or “Plaintiff”) alleges the following against Defendant Bo Sun and ALL PERSONS UNKNOWN, Claiming Any Legal or Equitable Right, Title, Estate, Lien, or Interest in the Property Described in this Complaint Adverse to Plaintiff’s title, or any Cloud upon Plaintiff’s Title Thereto: Plaintiff and Defendant, who were in a relationship and have two children, entered into a Joint Venture Agreement to invest in real estate. Despite the agreement that Plaintiff, in exchange for providing certain funds to purchase the Derringer and Skyline Properties), would be entitled to rights to title and equity, Defendant did not do so. As for the Monte Cristo Property, pursuant to a judgment in Case No. BC330493, Plaintiff transferred full title to Defendant, but Defendant has not reimbursed Plaintiff for mortgage, taxes, and other costs. 

 

On June 14, 2022, Plaintiff filed suit asserting the following COAs:

 

1.     Breach of Fiduciary

2.     Conversion

3.     Fraud

4.     Quiet Title

5.     Common County—Money Lent

 

On July 12, 2022, Plaintiff filed two Notice of Pendency of Action (Lis Pendens); one on the Monte Cristo property and the other on the Derringer Lane Property.

 

On August 24, 2022, Defendant filed a Demurrer, which on October 4, 2022 the court sustained with leave to amend.

 

On January 12, 2023 Defendant filed its demurrer to the FAC, which on 2/28/23 the court sustained with leave to amend as to the fraud and quiet title causes of action, and overruled as to the others.

 

On March 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC).

 

On June 14, 2023, the parties filed a stipulation for leave for Plaintiff to file a proposed third amended complaint (TAC). That same day, Plaintiff filed a TAC, alleging the following COAs:


1.     Breach of Fiduciary Duty

2.     Quiet Title

3.     Fraud and Concealment [the third COA is categorized into two counts].

 4.     Declaratory Relief

5.     Injunctive Relief

6.     Unjust Enrichment/Constructive Trust

 

On August 3, 2023, Defendant filed a demurrer and motion to strike (MTS) to the TAC, which the court (on 9/20/23) sustained in its entirety without leave to amend, meaning that the 2nd COA (for Quiet Title) and 3rd COA (for fraud) for were stricken from the pleading. According to the minute order, Defendant was given until October 20, 2023 to file a responsive pleading.

 

On October 11, 2023, Defendant filed a motion to expunge lis pendens.

 

On October 24, 2023, Defendant filed his answer along with a cross-complaint against Plaintiff Wendy[1] for:

 

1.     Declaratory relief

2.     Quiet title and

3.     Partition

On December 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed her opposition to the motion to expunge lis pendens.

The petition argues that the trial court erred in sustaining the demurrer without leave to amend as to the fraud and quiet title COAs.

 

Legal Standard

 

A¿lis¿pendens¿is a¿recorded instrument¿(“Notice of Pending Action”), recorded in the office of the county recorder where land is located, that gives¿constructive notice¿of a pending lawsuit affecting title to described real property.  (Gale v. Superior Court¿(2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1395.)  

 

“A party to an action who asserts a¿real property claim¿may record a notice of pendency of action in which that real property claim is alleged.”  (CCP § 405.20.)  Anyone having an interest in the property affected by a lis pendens, whether or not a party to the pending lawsuit, may move to expunge the lis pendens any time after it is recorded.  (CCP § 405.30.)  A lis pendens may be expunged on any of the following grounds:

 

(1) defects in the statutory service and filing requirements;[2]

(2) the complaint does not contain a real property claim;[3]

(3) the claimant cannot prove the probable validity of the real property claim by a preponderance of the evidence; or

(4) “adequate relief” can be “secured to the claimant by the giving of an undertaking.”  (CCP §§ 405.23, 405.31, 405.32, 405.33; McKnight v. Sup.Ct. (Faber)¿(1985) 170 Cal.App.3d 291, 303 [recognizing defective servicing and filing requirements as grounds for expungement].) 

 

“‘Probable validity,’ with respect to a real property claim, means that it is more likely than not that the claimant will obtain a judgment against the defendant on the claim.”  (CCP § 405.32.) The motion may be made even while an appeal is pending. (Peery v. Superior Court (1981) 29 Cal.3d 837, 842.)  

 

Discussion

 

The issue before the court is whether Plaintiff’s remaining COAs for declaratory relief, injunctive relief, and unjust enrichment/constructive trust—COAs that unlike a quiet title COA do not patently affect real property—are sufficient to maintain a lis pendens? For reasons to be discussed below, as the remaining COAs are predicated upon the quiet title COA which was stricken, the motion is granted.

 

A real property claim is defined as the “cause or causes of action in a pleading which would, if meritorious, affect (a) title to, or the right to possession of, specific real property....If the pleading filed by the claimant does not properly plead a real property claim, the lis pendens must be expunged upon motion under CCP 405.31.” (Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642, emphasis and underline added.) Quiet title actions, by definition (CCP 760.020), are a type of COA that affect to or right to possession of specific real property.

 

The court in its 9/20/23 ruling struck the quiet title COA because Plaintiff failed to state sufficient facts that she had rights to possession of the properties or any title to the properties; by way, only Defendant was deemed to hold title to the Joint Venture Properties. This ruling—that there is no viable quiet title COA—inherently rendered the other COAs moot because they are predicated upon a purported dispute in title, but one that the court found does not exist.

 

By way of illustration, turning to the TAC, the 4th COA for declaratory relief seeks a judicial declaration concerning the Plaintiff’s equity interest in the Derringer Lane Property (P75); The 5th COA for injunctive relief seeks to enjoin Defendant from making any further claims including, but not limited to, continuing to assert superior rights to possession or initiating any sale of Joint Venture Property (p. 11); The 6th COA for constructive trust seeks imposition of a constructive trust in favor of Plaintiff against Defendant’s property interest in the Joint Venture Properties. But by way of the court’s ruling on the demurrer, no dispute exists as to who holds title to the properties. Thus, absent an “actual controversy” as to title affecting real property, the other COAs are moot. (Reply p. 2, citing to RGC Gaslamp, LLC v. Ehmcke Sheet Metal Co. (2020) 56 Cal.App5th 413, 433 [quiet title COA moot because there was no longer an ‘active controversy’ for declaratory relief; and injunctive relief was unnecessary].)[4]

 

To the extent that Plaintiff predominantly relies upon Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642 and Shoker v. Superior Court of Alameda County (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 271, the cases are not instructive.[5] Neither involved cases wherein a court already ruled on a motion that disposed of a cause of action nor did either involve a quiet title COA.

 

In short, even though other COAs remain, those COAs would at most merely allow for a money judgment.[6]

 

Attorney Fees

 

A prevailing party on a motion to expunge a lis pendens is entitled to recover “reasonable attorney’s fees and costs of making or opposing the motion unless the court finds that the other party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of attorney's fees and costs unjust.” (CCP § 405.38.) For purposes of attorneys’ fees and costs, a court’s ruling on a motion made pursuant to CCP Sections 405.31 and 405.32 necessarily decides who is the “prevailing party” since such motions place in issue whether the lis pendens was proper. The award of attorney’s fees is only against the losing party, not the losing party’s counsel. (Doyle v. Sup.Ct. (Jacinth Develop., Inc.) (1991) 226 Cal.App.3d 1355, 1359.) 

 

Here, the court declines to impose monetary sanctions because of insufficient meet and confer efforts and a determination that Plaintiff acted with substantial justification.

 

First, there is an inconsistent explanation of meet and confer efforts. In its motion, Defendant states that “Plaintiff never responded to Defendant’s meet and confer efforts.” (Motion p. 4:6-7.) However, Defense Counsel’s then proceeds to state in his declaration that Plaintiff responded to the 9/29/23 email on 10/6/23 by “planning on fil[ing] another lawsuit.” (Motion p. 7.)[7] To the contrary, Plaintiff’s counsel provides a 10/11 email response regarding the lis pendens motion; an email that is largely identical to the instant opposition.

 

Second, Plaintiff’s opposition was detailed and thorough. Thus, even if unmeritorious, Plaintiff attempted to craft a legal argument to support her position.

 

Therefore, as the parties did not engage in adequate meet and confer efforts and as Plaintiff made a good faith attempt in opposition, the court DENIES attorney fees under the statutory exceptions.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the motion is GRANTED but attorney fees are denied (as to both parties).

Tentative Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background

 

This case arises from a breach of a lease agreement.

 

On August 31, 2023, Plaintiff Best Western Summit, Inc. filed suit against Defendants CPI Security Corp. and Joseph Carmona.[1]

 

On November 1, 2023, default was entered against both Defendants.

 

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant application. 

Legal Standard

California Code of Civil Procedure section 585 (“CCP”) permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served. A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1800.)

 

 

Discussion

 

At the outset, it is unclear as to the judgment sought as Plaintiff has filed two proposed orders (not on JUD-100 forms). (One proposed order should be filed.) On both proposed orders, Plaintiff seeks to recover from each Defendant the principal amount of $46,645.00, plus interest in the sum of $4,413.24, court costs of $1,492.63, and attorney fees of $9,595.50, for a total of $62,146.37.[2]

 

1.     Individual Defendant’s Liability

 

First, it is unclear as to the individual Defendant’s liability. Neither the complaint, summary of the case, nor lease provide that Defendant Joseph Carmona is the guarantor of the lease. The only guarantor referenced appears to be the landlord, not the tenant. (See Lease, Ex. A-6.) Therefore, to the extent that Plaintiff seek to hold the individual defendant liable in his capacity as an officer of the corporation, it has failed to plead alter ego. (See Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538-599 [background and framework for alter ego/piercing the corporate veil].)

 

2.     Attorney Fees

 

Second, though Plaintiff does not provide the provision in the lease which allows for recovery of attorney fees, a review of the lease provides that provision 40 allows for attorney fees. However, the prevailing party is entitled to recovery of “reasonable” attorney fees. Absent an explanation of attorney fees (i.e., Lodestar factors), the court finds attorney fees of $9,595.50 unreasonable for a simple breach of contract case.

 

3.     Interest

 

Third, Plaintiff has not provided a computation of interest.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing (four defects), the default judgment application is denied without prejudice.
