Case Number: 23AVCV00370    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a wrongful death action. Plaintiffs Susan Ekdahl, individually, and as Administrator of the Estate of Edward A. Ekdahl (“Susan”[1]); and Edward A. Ekdahl, by and through his Successor in Interest, Susan Ekdahl (“Decedent” and collectively “Plaintiffs”), allege that on (1) January 02, 2022, Decedent was transported to Defendant  Lancaster Hospital Corporation dba Palmdale Regional Medical Center, erroneously sued as Palmdale Regional Medical Center (“PRMC”)’s Emergency Department by ambulance for chest main and shortness of breath; (2) Decedent was admitted shortly after midnight on January 03, 2022, with Defendant Sonia P. Sethi, M.D. (“Dr. Sethi”) as his attending physician; (3) while in the patient room Decedent was connected to an intravenous line, heated high-flow oxygen, and some vital sign monitoring cables; (4) at around 6:00 pm on January 03, 2022, as a result of failure to provide medial and non-medical services, including supervision, Decedent was able to remove various lines and cables from his body and proceeded to the bathroom unattended without supervision; (5) there was no alerting device in place or protocol to alert Dr. Sethi and attending medical staff in real-time that a patient was exiting the bed alone without supervision; (6) Decedent collapsed and went into cardiac arrest while in the bathroom; (7) when medical staff discovered Decedent, they were unable to retrieve him because the bathroom door was locked; (8) Decedent was unresponsive when medical staff reached him; (9) Decedent was transferred to PRMC’s Intensive Care Unit (“ICU”); and (10) Decedent never came back to consciousness and passed away on January 13, 2022, as a result of Defendants’ acts and omissions.

 

On April 05, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint alleging three causes of action against PRMC.

 

On June 14, 2023, PRMC filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike.

 

On July 21, 2023, before the Demurrer with Motion to Strike was heard, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). The FAC added Dr. Sethi as a defendant and alleged three causes of action for: (1) Wrongful Death against all defendants; (2) Survivor Cause of Action – Medical Negligence against all defendants; and (3) Survivor Cause of Action – Negligence: Premises Liability against PRMC.

 

On August 23, 2023, PRMC filed this Demurrer, subsequently overruled.

 

On October 19, 2023, Plaintiffs filed two Amendments to the Complaint changing the fictious name of Doe 1 to Envision Healthcare Corporation and Doe 2 to Envision Physician Services, LLC.

 

On December 04, 2023, dismissed Envision Healthcare Corporation and Envision Physician Services, LLC.

 

On February 06, 2024, Dr. Sethi’s Ex Parte Application to Continue Trial was granted.

 

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint.

 

On March 19, 2024, PRMC filed its Opposition.

 

On March 25, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Reply.

 

On April 02, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the matter. The Court took the matter under submission before issuing a continuance as it was presented that (1) Plaintiffs seek to add an additional cause of action due to discovery responses; (2) a meet and confer attempt to address the proposed pleadings may alleviate the need for the motion; and (3) the motion and ruling should address the amended proposed pleading. The Court provided the following briefing schedule:

 

Supplemental Opening Papers: April 15, 2024

Supplemental Opposing Papers: April 24, 2024

Supplemental Reply Papers: April 30, 2024

 

(04/05/2024 Minute Order.)

 

On April 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Supplemental Opening Papers.

 

On April 24, 2024, PRMC filed its Supplemental Opposing Papers.

 

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Supplemental Reply Papers.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Leave to Amend – “A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. A party may amend the pleading after the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike, upon stipulation by the parties. The time for responding to an amended pleading shall be computed from the date of service of the amended pleading.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 472(a).)¿ 

 

Trial courts may, in furtherance of justice and on any proper terms, allow a party to amend any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §473(a)(1); Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Association (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) Trial courts may also, in their discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §473(a); Branick, supra, 39 Cal.4th at 242.) Leave to amend is generally liberally granted, provided there is no statute of limitations concern. (Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 411.) Additionally, “judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.) Trial courts may deny the plaintiff’s leave to amend if there is prejudice to the opposing party, such as delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation. (Branic, supra, at 242.)¿ 

 

Under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324, a motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a).) A separate supporting declaration specifying (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier must accompany the motion. (Id., Rule 3.1324(b).)¿

Discussion

 

Application – The Court provides a summary of the previous documents and hearings.

In the original moving papers, Plaintiffs sought leave to amend to add three causes of action to the pleadings: (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (2) Fraudulent Concealment; and (3) Misrepresentation, as well as to expand the allegations in its operative First Amended Complaint (“Complaint”) based on information recently obtained in discovery in this action. These changes include a request for punitive damages in the proposed Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). Plaintiffs presented that the changes stem from responses received during discovery and the motion was brought in good faith. PRMC argued that (1) as to the proposed Breach of Fiduciary Care claim, it is written as on behalf of the Estate of Decedent and alleges only damages to Susan; (2) as to the proposed claim for Fraudulent Concealment, the proposed claim fails to allege that PRMC failed to disclose any of the allegations to Decedent, that PRMC intended to defraud Decedent, and that Decedent would not acted as he did if he had known of the above listed alleged concealed or suppressed facts and that proposed pleading once again mentions Susan though it is only on behalf of Decedent’s Estate; and (3) as to the claim for Misrepresentation, the proposed SAC does not allege specific facts (i.e., ow, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered) and that the cause of action is alleged by Susan and does not apply to “Plaintiffs” in the plural. PRMC further argued that none of the claims can be rectified by amendment and Plaintiffs failed to state facts sufficient to constitute any of the proposed additional causes of action. Plaintiffs argued that PRMC provided no valid arguments as the arguments are more suited for a demurrer than leave to amend the complaint. The Court provided the following analysis:

 

As to PRMC’s first argument, the Court notes that Susan is in the action individually, as the Administrator of Decedent’s Estate, and as Decedent’s Successor in Interest. It is unclear, based on the substance of the pleading, whether the claim is meant to be on behalf of Susan. The Court notes that this is not the first time this has occurred as the FAC alleges a survivor action written as brought by Susan alone but alleging a survivor cause of action. (See FAC at First Cause of Action Header and ¶ 30; see also Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.30.) “The law respects form less than substance.” (See Cal. Civ. Code § 3528.) Thus, it appears in either case that the claim of Fraudulent Concealment may be rectified. The Court inquires.

 

As to PRMC’s second argument, “the elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are:(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3)the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.) The proposed SAC lists various facts that were allegedly not disclosed. The proposed facts do switch between the plural use of “Plaintiffs,” actions directed at Susan, as well as actions directed at Decedent. It is unclear as to which Plaintiff this proposed claim applies to. The damages alleged are as to Susan only. (See Proposed SAC ¶ 116.) However, the claim is written as brought by Decedent’s Estate. (See Proposed SAC Fifth Cause of Action Header.) As the claim is unclear, it appears that this, too, may be rectified by amendment.

 

Finally, as to PRMC’s third argument, there are two forms of misrepresentation. Plaintiff’s proposed sac alleges intentional misrepresentation. (See Proposed SAC ¶¶ 122-24.) “The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974; see also Service by Medallion, Inc. v. Clorox Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1807, 1816.) Plaintiff has alleged that PRMC intentionally failed to disclose: (1) Decedent was able to detach the wires on his body for a self-search of the closest bathroom, (2) PRMC staff only first discovered Decedent was alone in the PRMC emergency department bathroom upon hearing an individual breathing laboriously behind the bathroom door but unable to get out, and (3) PRMC’s emergency room staff did not have ready access to the key to the PRMC bathroom door in the emergency department to unlock it, nor the fact that the key was nowhere to be found by the PRMC staff in the emergency department. (Proposed SAC ¶¶ 122-24.) However, it also appears that the gravamen of the claim is that PRMC informed Susan that Decedent was supervised at every minute and that the alleged failures indicate otherwise. Though not written as clearly as possible, the claim for misrepresentation alleges that (1) PRMC falsely represented to Susan that Decedent was being supervised, (2) the allegations in paragraphs 122 to 124 in the proposed SAC show knowledge of the falsity, and (3) intent may exist as “fraudulent intent is an issue for the trier of fact to decide” (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1061; see also Proposed SAC ¶ 128); and (4) damages in the form of Susan’s continued extreme state of unrest, frustration, uncertainty, and emotional distress. Reliance is unclear as paragraph 130, which alleges the reliance, jumps from Decedent’s death to autopsy. As with the other claims this, too, may be rectified by amendment.

 

As the claims may be rectified, there is a potential basis for punitive damages as the punitive damages are derivative of the proposed causes of action.

 

It is the Court’s intent to grant this motion upon the filing of a new proposed SAC that address the current deficiencies in the attached proposed SAC.

 

(04/02/2024 Tentative.)

 

Plaintiffs presented that the Court’s tentative was correct and that Plaintiffs intended to file a new proposed SAC that included a new cause of action that was neither in the filed proposed SAC nor discussed in the moving papers while led to the Court’s continuation of the matter and a supplemental briefing schedule. (See 04/02/2024 Minute Order and 04/05/2024 Minute Order.)

 

Despite the representation by Plaintiffs’ counsel, Kat Kazemi for Elizabeth Yang (“Kazemi”) at the hearing, Plaintiffs now state they are “somewhat in the dark as to what exactly they are to be ‘Supplemental Briefing’ insofar as they already urged to this Court that its April 1, 2024 Tentative was on point. . .” (Supplemental Moving Papers 3:8-10.) The Court made clear that there were issues within the proposed SAC in its tentative, provided ante. Thus, the Ruling on the Submitted Matter made clear that Plaintiffs’ new proposed SAC should address the deficiencies and it is patent that a new proposed SAC would need corresponding moving papers that address the statutory requirements surrounding the new proposed cause of action. (See legal standard, ante.) In light of the context of the supplemental briefing schedule, including Kazemi’s own representation that the Court’s tentative was correct and a new cause of action was needed, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ representations that they do not know what briefing is required availing.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the proposed SAC rectifies any challenge points taken by PRMC, that the proposed SAC contains six new causes of action, that the proposed SAC is deemed filed and served as of the date of “this ruling,” and that the proposed SAC follows Radar Co. v. Stone (1986) 178 Cal. App. 3d 10, 29.   

 

The Court notes that Kazemi informed it that one new cause of action would be added to the proposed SAC, instead there are now four more proposed causes of action: (1) Negligent Hiring, Training, Supervision, and/or Retention; (2) Constructive Fraud; (3) Intentional Misrepresentation; and (4) Negligent Misrepresentation. That is, there are three completely new causes of action and one delineation of what was previously titled “Misrepresentation” in the original Motion for Leave to Amend. This is drastically different from what Kazemi presented to the Court on April 02, 2024. Whether this misrepresentation was inattentional or not, the Court reminds Kazemi that she owes a duty of candor to the tribunal. (See The State Bar of California Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 3.3 [Candor Toward the Tribunal].)

 

PRMC states that was surprised to find the changes in Plaintiffs’ new proposed SAC. PRMC argues that all fraud-based causes of actions are actually premised on medical or professional malpractice as they are based upon either an alleged (1) failure to report Decedent’s death to the coroner pursuant to Health & Safety Code § 102850, (2) failure of PRMC to report Decedent’s death as an “adverse event” pursuant to Health & Safety Code § 1279.1, and/or (3) failure to provide adequate telemetry supervision and monitoring to Decedent while he was a patient at PRMC. PRMC presents that such actions fall within the Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (“MICRA”), and do not allow for a third-party cause of action. PRMC contends that the fraud-based causes of actions are prejudicial and untimely. PRMC further argues that all fraud-based causes of action are brought by Susan in her individual capacity and, therefore, standing is an issue. As Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages are derived from the proposed fraud-based causes of action, they must fail.

 

Plaintiffs argue that PRMC’s Supplemental Opposition “misses the park” as Plaintiffs have addressed the deficiencies in the previously proposed SAC. Plaintiffs further present that any prejudicing in timing is due to PRMC’s request for supplemental briefing and that Plaintiff’s SAC should be appropriately deemed filed and served as of the date of this ruling.

 

The Court directs the parties to the legal standard surrounding motions for leave to amend, outlined ante. For convenience, the Court reiterates: “Under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324, a motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a).) A separate supporting declaration specifying (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier must accompany the motion. (Id., Rule 3.1324(b).)” (See Legal Standard, ante.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ moving papers include the proposed SAC, which is serially numbered. Exhibit B to Kazemi’s declaration for corresponding Supplemental Opening Papers includes a red-lined version of the proposed SAC which satisfies Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324’s requirement of showing which items are deleted and/or added by page, paragraph, and line number. Paragraph five of Kazemi’s declaration specifies the effect of the amendment. Kazemi’s declaration discusses (1) that the facts giving rise to the amendments were discovered on or about December 11, 2023 when PRMC produced discovery which explains why the request for amendment was not made earlier must accompany the motion; and (2) that the amendment is necessitated because it seeks in good faith to add/amend causes of action that are consistent with the facts pleaded, but that were not originally pleaded because they only recently came to light.

 

The arguments in PRMC’s Supplemental Opposing Papers are more suited to a demurrer rather than the current motion.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Motion for Leave to Amend.
