Case Number: 22PSCV01097    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: H

Zhang v. Freevolt USA, Inc., Case No. 22PSCV01097

ORDER ON DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION

Plaintiff Weidong Zang’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background

Plaintiff Weidong Zang (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

          On February 19, 2020, Plaintiff and Defendant Freevolt USA, Inc. (“Defendant”) entered into a contract wherein Defendant agreed to install a 25.5 KW solar photovoltaic system (85 solar panels system) at Plaintiff’s property located at 14736 Valley Blvd., La Puente, CA 91746 (“Project”) in exchange for payment. Defendant guaranteed to complete the Project within 3 months, or would compensate Plaintiff's electricity bills for any delays up to $2,000 per month. Plaintiff followed Defendant’s directions and completed a loan application for $100,000 to cover the expenses of the Project (“Loan”), and paid Defendant $2,000 as a deposit. The loan was later approved, and Defendant received $90,000 from proceeds of the loan (the remaining $10,000 of the loan proceeds was on hold for the reason of incompletion of the Project). Plaintiff made payments for the Loan from April of 2020-present. Defendant never started the Project after entering into the contract.

On September 26, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting a breach of contract cause of action against Defendant and Does 1-25.

On January 30, 2023, Defendant’s default was entered.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s application for default judgment.

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted:

          1.       Paragraph 2(a) of Judicial Council form CIV-100 Request for Court Judgment lists the “Demand of complaint” as $157,346.75, which coincides with the amounts identified in the prayer of Plaintiff’s complaint, minus the $500.00 court costs identified in Paragraph 12(vii) therein (court costs are separately identified in Paragraph 2(d) of CIV-100). Paragraph 12(vi) of the prayer in Plaintiff’s complaint seeks $1,000.00 in legal fees “to demand the completion of the Project (Attorney letters).” Plaintiff has failed to identify any entitlement to attorney’s fees.

          2.       The Purchase and Installation Agreement attached as Exhibit 1 to Plaintiff’s declaration filed November 22, 2024 does not appear to be the same Purchase and Installation Agreement attached as Exhibit 1 to Plaintiff’s declaration filed July 11, 2024. Exhibit A, Attachment 1 to Plaintiff’s complaint appears to contain two versions of the Purchase and Installation Agreement, one with a handwritten notation on the right-hand side reading, “ELN $100,000.00 + check $15,400.00” and the other appearing to read “ELN 12 years.” Plaintiff is directed to explain why these agreements are different.

          3.       The monetary amounts identified in Paragraphs 9-11 of Plaintiff’s Declaration (i.e., $35,970.37, plus $2,000.00, plus $84,263.75, plus $24,112.63) total $146,346.75, not $157,346.75 as requested. Plaintiff is requested to reconcile this discrepancy.

          4.       Plaintiff incorrectly lists the “6/16/2020” billing statement as $1,193.43. It is actually $1,139.43.

          5.       Plaintiff fails to attach any documentary evidence in support of his statement in Paragraph 3 of his declaration that “Defendant received $90,000 of the loan proceeds to complete the Project.”

The Court instructs Plaintiff to comply with California Rules of Court rule 3.1800 in any future default prove up application. Any subsequent default prove-up application must be full and complete and not refer back to any previously submitted documents.

Case Number: 24PSCV00758    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: H

Castro v. Pomona Valley Mining Co., Case No. 24PSCV00758

ORDER ON PAGA SETTLEMENT

Plaintiff Alma Castro’s, individually and on behalf of all others similarly situated, Motion to Approve PAGA Settlement is GRANTED.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Alma Castro, individually and on behalf of all persons similarly situated (“Plaintiff” or “Castro”), alleges that Pomona Valley Mining Co. (“Defendant”) denied its non-exempt employees minimum and overtime pay and meal and rest periods and committed other Labor Code wage and hour violations. 

On March 12, 2024, Castro filed a Representative Action Complaint, asserting a cause of action against Defendant and Does 1-50 for civil penalties pursuant to Labor Code § 2699, et seq. for violations of Labor Code §§ 201, 202, 203, 204 et seq., 210, 218, 221, 351, 510, 512, 558, 1194, 1197, 1197.1, 1198, 2800, 2802 and the California Code of Regulations, Title 8, Section 11040, Subdivision 5(A)-(B). 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion to approve the Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”) settlement agreement.


II.      Legal Standard

Labor Code § 2699, subdivision (l)(2) states: “The superior court shall review and approve any settlement of any civil action filed pursuant to this part [Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004 (“PAGA”)].  The proposed settlement shall be submitted to the [Labor and Workforce Development Agency (“LWDA”)] at the same time that it is submitted to the court.”[FN1] 

Any settlement of any civil action filed under PAGA must be “fair and adequate in view of the purposes and policies of the statute.”  (O’Connor v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2016) 201 F.Supp.3d 1110, 1135.)


Seventy-five percent of all PAGA penalties must be paid to the LWDA. (Lab. Code § 2699(i).) Further, reasonable attorney’s fees and costs are awarded to a prevailing employee. (Lab. Code § 2699(g)(1).)

 

III.     Discussion

Plaintiff requests entry of an order granting of the parties’ “Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA) Settlement Agreement and Release” (the “Agreement”). 

 

Terms of the Settlement Agreement

The essential terms of the Agreement[FN2] are as follows:

Definition of Aggrieved Employees: “Aggrieved Employee(s)” or “PAGA Group Members” means “all persons within the State of California who are or were employed by [Defendant] as hourly paid non-exempt employees during any portion of the PAGA Period” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.4).

Definition of PAGA Period: “PAGA Period” means “the period from January 2, 2023 to September 6, 2024.” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.22; Barvie Decl., ¶ 18.)

Definition of PAGA Pay Period: “PAGA Pay Periods” means “pay period during which an Aggrieved Employee worked for [Defendant] for at least one day during the PAGA Period.” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.23.)

Definition of Gross Settlement Amount (“GSA”): The GSA is a non-reversionary $185,000.00 (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.12 and Section 3, ¶ 3.1; Barvie Decl., ¶ 5), which includes the following[FN3]:

          Individual PAGA Payments: The “Individual PAGA Payment” is the “Aggrieved Employee’s pro-rata share of 25% of the PAGA Penalties calculated according to the number of Pay Periods the Aggrieved Employee worked during the PAGA Period” and then multiplying the result by each Aggrieved Employee’s PAGA Pay Periods. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.13 and Section 3, ¶ 3.3.2; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 18 and 20.)

Aggrieved Employees assume full responsibility and liability for any taxes owed on their Individual PAGA Payment. (Agreement, Section 3, ¶ 3.3.2.1.) Administrator (defined below) will prepare and issue IRS 1099 Forms. (Id.) Defendant estimates there are approximately 111 Aggrieved Employees who worked approximately 4,034 PAGA Pay Periods[FN4] within the PAGA Period. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.1; Barvie Decl., ¶ 21.)[FN5] Twenty-five percent of PAGA Penalties will be approximately $21,500.00. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 17.) 

          LWDA PAGA Payment: Seventy-five percent of the PAGA Penalties paid will be distributed to the LDWA. (Agreement Section 1, ¶ 1.15). This will be approximately $64,500.00. (Agreement, Section 3, ¶ 3.3.1; Barvie Decl., ¶ 16.)

          Plaintiff’s Counsel Fees and Litigation Expenses Payments: Attorney’s fees to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel are not to exceed 40% of the GSA (i.e., $74,000.00). (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.19 and Section 3, ¶ 3.2.2.1; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 16 and 25.) Costs are not to exceed $15,000.00.[FN6] (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.20 and Section 3.2.2.2; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 16 and 25.) 

          Plaintiff Enhancement Payment: Plaintiff will receive a service payment of $5,000.00. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.28; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 16 and 25.) Administrator (defined below) will pay this amount using IRS Form 1099 and Plaintiff assumes responsibility for employee taxes owed on this payment. (Agreement, Section 3, ¶ 3.2.1.) 

          Administration Expenses Payment: Settlement administration fees are not to exceed $5,000.00. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.3 and Section 3, ¶ 3.2.3; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 16 and 25.)

Definition of Net Settlement Amount (“NSA”): The NSA is the GSA, less the Plaintiff’s Counsel Fees and Litigation Expenses Payments, Plaintiff Enhancement Payment, and the Administration Expenses Payment. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.16.) The remainder is to be paid to the LWDA and PAGA Group Members. (Id.)

Claims Process: There is no claims process.

Settlement Administrator: Apex Class Actions (“Apex”) is the third-party settlement administrator. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.2.) 

Within 15 business days after the Court issues its order approving the settlement, Defendant will deliver the Aggrieved Employee Data (i.e., “Aggrieved Employee identifying information in [Defendant’s] possession including the Aggrieved Employee’s name, last-known mailing address, social security number and number of PAGA Pay Periods within the PAGA Period”) (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.5 and Section 4, ¶ 4.2; Barvie Decl., ¶ 24) to Administrator in the form of a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.5.) Defendant will fully fund the GSA by transmitting funds to Administrator not later than 20 business days after the Effective Date (i.e., “the date the Court has (1) approved the settlement terms and (2) issues a Judgment in the Action”). (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.11 and Section 4, ¶ 4.3; Barvie Decl., ¶ 24.) 

Within 14 business days after Defendant funds the GSA, Administrator will mail checks for all Individual PAGA Payments, the LWDA PAGA Payment, the Administration Expenses Payment, Plaintiff Enhancement Payment, and the Plaintiff’s Counsel Fees and Litigation Expenses Payments. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.4; Barvie Decl., ¶ 24)). The checks will be sent via First Class U.S. Mail, postage prepaid. (Id.) 

Before mailing any checks, Administrator will update the recipients’ mailing addresses using the National Change of Address Database. (Id.) Within 7 business days of receiving a returned check, Administrator must re-mail checks to the USPS forwarding address provided or to an address ascertained through the Aggrieved Employee Address Search (i.e., made by “using all reasonably available sources, methods and means including, but not limited to, the National Change of Address database, skip traces and direct contact by . . .Administrator with Aggrieved Employees”). (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.6 and Section 7, ¶ 7.3.) Administrator need not take further steps to deliver checks to Aggrieved Employees whose re-mailed checks are returned as undelivered. (Agreement, Section 7, ¶ 7.3.)

Uncashed Funds: The face of each check shall prominently state the date (not less than 180 days after the date of mailing) when the check will be voided. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.4; Barvie Decl., ¶ 24.) Administrator will promptly send a replacement check to any Aggrieved Employee whose original check was lost or misplaced, requested by the Aggrieved Employee prior to the void date. (Agreement, Section 7, ¶ 7.3.) Administrator will cancel all checks not cashed by the void date. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.4.) Any uncashed check canceled after the void date will be transmitted by Administrator to the California Controller’s Unclaimed Property Fund in the name of the Aggrieved Employee. (Agreement, Section 7, ¶ 7.3; Barvie Decl., ¶ 24.).

Notice to LWDA: Concurrently with this motion, Castro provided a copy of the Agreement to the LWDA. (Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 5 and 25, Exh. 3.) Castro will also submit to the LAWDA a copy of the Court’s judgment and order approving the Agreement within 10 days after entry of judgment or order. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 25.)

Release: “PAGA Claims” means “all claims asserted in the action, all claims asserted in the PAGA Notice sent by or on behalf of Plaintiff to the LWDA, all claims that could have been asserted in the Action (whether under federal law or state law) or any other claims under PAGA, based on the facts alleged, including but not limited to any and all claims for PAGA penalties based on the Labor Code sections asserted therein, including, PAGA Penalties for violations under the California Labor Code §§ 201, 202, 203, 204 et seq., 210, 218, 221, 226 et seq., 226.3, 226.7, 227.3, 246 et seq., 350, 351, 353, 510, 512, 558, 1194, 1197, 1197.1, 1198, 2699, 2699.3 et seq., 2800, 2802, the applicable IWC Wage Orders, and California Code of Regulations, Title 8, Section 11040, Subdivision 5 (A)-(B).” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.24; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 22-23.) 

“Released Parties” means Defendant “its past and present officers, directors, shareholders, owners, current and former employees, agents, representatives, members, attorneys, insurers, re-insurers, assigns, affiliates, principals, heirs, representatives and their respective successors and predecessors in interest, management companies, parent companies and any and all other entities with whom they have been, are now, or may hereafter be affiliated and/or deemed joint employers or jointly liable with [Defendant].” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.29.)  

Fairness and Adequacy of Settlement

“Despite the fact that a representative action under PAGA is not a class action and is instead a type of qui tam action, a standard requiring the trial court to determine independently whether a PAGA settlement is fair and reasonable is appropriate.” (Moniz v. Adecco USA, Inc. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 56, 76 [internal quotations and citations omitted].) “[A] trial court should evaluate a PAGA settlement to determine whether it is fair, reasonable, and adequate in view of PAGA’s purposes to remediate present labor law violations, deter future ones, and to maximize enforcement of state labor laws. (Id. at 77.)

Arm’s Length Bargaining Process 

On September 6, 2024, the parties participated in a full-day mediation conducted by with Tagore Subramaniam (“Subramaniam”), Esq., “an experienced attorney and mediator in wage and hour representative actions.” (Barvie Decl., ¶ 13.) At the conclusion of the mediation, the parties reached an agreement for settlement in principle based on Subramaniam’s proposal, which led to the signing of the Agreement. (Id., ¶ 14.) The settlement was negotiated after extensive arm’s length negotiations, with Subramaniam’s assistance. (Id.). 

          Sufficiency of Investigation and Discovery 

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff submitted a notice and draft complaint to the LWDA. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 10.) On March 12, 2024, after the statutory notice period expired, Castro filed the operative complaint. (Id., ¶¶ 11-12.) 

Prior to the September 6, 2024 mediation, the parties engaged in significant efforts to obtain evidence regarding the claims asserted, including all of Defendant’s relevant wage and hour policies, the exchange of detailed statistical data regarding assignments performed by Plaintiff and the Aggrieved Employees, the production of hundreds of pages of documents, including a large sample of payroll records and time records, and detailed briefing and analysis in connection with the mediation. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 13.) Plaintiff was able to perform damages calculations and assess her claims relevant to Defendant’s defenses based on this information. (Id., ¶ 13.)

          Experience of Counsel 

Barvie has provided the Court with satisfactory information relative to her firm’s, as well as her personal, experience in class action and representative action (PAGA) litigation. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. 6.)

          Strengths and Weaknesses of Plaintiff’s Case 

Barvie has provided the Court with a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of Plaintiff’s action on pages 8-10 of her corrected declaration. With the assistance of an expert, Castro calculated that the maximum amount of statutory PAGA civil penalties was potentially $1,526,700 based on 4,034 pay periods applicable to the Aggrieved Employees during the PAGA Period. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 27.) The valuation of $1,526,700.00 is the civil penalty amount without “stacking,” which is where more than one civil penalty is imposed in a pay period for the same conduct. (Id., ¶ 27, fn. 10.) Castro is not aware of any PAGA award which permitted stacking. (Id.)

The GSA is 12.12% of Defendant’s maximum liability exposure. (Id., ¶ 29) The settlement amount of PAGA claims here constitutes approximately $45.86 per pay period. (Id., ¶ 26.) 

Defendant maintains that it did not violate the Labor Code and that the Aggrieved Employees were properly compensated. (Id., ¶¶ 30 and 31.) Defendant also asserted additional defenses as to the amount of the penalties. (Id.) Defendant could also argue that no penalties prior to the PAGA notification should be awarded because the violations were not intentional. (Id.) 

The Court finds that the settlement is “fair, reasonable, and adequate in view of PAGA’s purposes,” based on the foregoing.

Scope of the Release

“PAGA Released Claims” means “all claims asserted in the ACTION, all claims asserted in the PAGA NOTICE sent by or on behalf of PLAINTIFF to the LWDA, all claims that could have been asserted in the ACTION (whether under federal law or state law) or any other claims under PAGA, based on the facts alleged, including but not limited to any and all claims for PAGA penalties based on the Labor Code sections asserted therein, including, PAGA PENALTIES for violations under the California Labor Code §§ 201, 202, 203, 204 et seq., 210, 218, 221, 226 et seq., 226.3, 226.7, 227.3, 246 et seq., 350, 351, 353, 510, 512, 558, 1194, 1197, 1197.1, 1198, 2699, 2699.3 et seq., 2800, 2802, the applicable IWC Wage Orders, and California Code of Regulations, Title 8, Section 11040, Subdivision 5 (A)-(B).” (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.24.) 

“Because an aggrieved employee’s action under the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004 functions as a substitute for an action brought by the government itself, a judgment in that action binds all those, including nonparty aggrieved employees, who would be bound by a judgment in an action brought by the government. The act authorizes a representative action only for the purpose of seeking statutory penalties for Labor Code violations . . .” (Arias v. Superior Court (2009) 46 Cal.4th 969, 986.) 

Castro has additionally agreed to a general release of all claims arising out of her employment and a waiver of rights under Civil Code section 1542. (Agreement, Section 5, ¶ 5.1.1 and 5.1.1.1 and Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 48.) 

The Court finds that the release is fair, reasonable, and adequate.

Notice 

“PAGA has no notice requirements for unnamed aggrieved employees, nor may such employees opt out of a PAGA action.” (Baumann v. Chase Inv. Services Corp. (9th Cir. 2014) 747 F.3d 1117, 1122.)

Again, within 15 business days after the Court issues its Order approving the settlement, Defendant will provide Apex with Aggrieved Employee Data in the form of a Microsoft Excel spreadsheet, which will include each Aggrieved Employee’s name, last-known mailing address, social security number and number of PAGA Pay Periods within the PAGA Period. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.2 and Section 1, ¶ 1.5.) 

Within 14 days after Defendant funds the GSA (i.e., due no later than 20 business days after the Effective Date), Apex will mail checks for all Individual PAGA Payments. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶¶ 4.3 and 4.4.) Before mailing any checks, Apex will update the recipients’ mailing addresses using the National Change of Address Database. (Agreement, Section 4, ¶ 4.4.) Within 7 business days of receiving a returned check, Apex must re-mail checks to the USPS forwarding address provided or to an address ascertained through the Aggrieved Employee Address Search, which involves the use of all reasonably available sources, methods and means including, but not limited to, the National Change of Address database, skip traces and direct contact by Apex with Aggrieved Employees. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.6 and Section 7, ¶7.3.) 

The Court finds that the foregoing procedure is appropriate.

Attorney Fees and Costs 

The Agreement provides that attorney’s fees to be paid to Castro’s counsel are not to exceed 40% (i.e., $74,000.00) of the GSA. (Agreement, Section 3, ¶ 3.2.2 and Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 25 and 38). Costs have been incurred in the amount of $11,737.70. (Barvie Decl., ¶ 38, fn. 15.) 

The Court finds the requested attorney’s fees to be reasonable pursuant to the percentage method from a “common fund.” (Lafitte v. Robert Half Intern. Inc. (2016) 1 Cal.5th 480, 486.) 

Castro’s has provided the Court with a Lodestar calculation of $48,178.00, with a multiplier of 1.5 with additional work to be performed (Barvie Decl., Decl., ¶¶ 6 and 41, Exh. 4). The Court further finds the requested costs to be reasonable and adequately supported by Barvie’s declaration. (Id., ¶¶ 7, 44 and 45, Exh. 5.)

Enhancement Award 

Castro will receive $5,000.00 as an enhancement payment. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.28 and Section 3, 3.2.1; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 48.) The Court finds this award to be reasonable and adequately supported by Barvie’s declaration.

Administration Costs 

Settlement administration costs will not exceed $5,000.00. (Agreement, Section 1, ¶ 1.3 and Section 3, ¶ 3.2.3; Barvie Decl., ¶¶ 16 and 25). The Court finds these costs to be reasonable. 

Conclusion 

The motion is granted.

Case Number: KC068890    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: H

Yee v. Hon, Case No. KC068890

TENTATIVE RULING ON DISTRIBUTION OF PROPERTY PROCEEDS

 I.       Background

This is a partition case that has been pending for several years involving the property located at 1446 Cabinda Drive in Hacienda Heights (the “subject property”). In August 2020, following trial, Judge Gloria White-Brown determined that Plaintiff Herman Yee (“Plaintiff” or “Yee”) was entitled to partition of the subject property and directed Plaintiff’s counsel to prepare and submit an Interlocutory Order wherein Plaintiff has a 50% interest in the subject property and Defendants Ann Hon (“Hon”) and Charles Lai (“Lai”) (collectively “Defendants”) shared a 50% interest. The court further ordered partition of the subject property and directed the property to be listed for sale on or before November 2, 2020.

In the Interlocutory Judgment, Judge White-Brown ordered that “[a]fter the sales, the referee shall report back to the Court, at which time this Court shall take up the issue of determining apportionment, off-sets, credits, costs and issue a final judgment in this case.” 

On December 1, 2020, Judge White-Brown appointed Matthew L. Taylor (“Taylor”) as Referee and instructed him to list the subject property for sale as soon as possible. The sale was delayed due to, among other things, prolonged litigation regarding liens on the subject property. 

On July 18, 2024, a “Stipulation and Order Confirming Sale of Real Property by Partition Referee” was entered. 

The case was transferred to this Court on August 5, 2024. On September 20, 2024, the Court held a Status Conference Re: Sale of Property and set a briefing schedule for the parties to address the appropriate distribution of the sale proceeds. At that time, the Court set a further Status Conference Re: Sale of Subject Property for December 20, 2024. 

On November 26, 2024, Taylor filed a “Notice of Completion of Sale of Real Property,” confirming that the sale had been completed.

Merits

On September 10, 2020, an Interlocutory Judgment was entered, which ordered, adjudged and decreed, in relevant part, that Plaintiff held an undivided 50% ownership interest in the subject property, that Defendants each held an undivided 25% ownership interest in the subject property, that Defendants were tenants in common, that there were no other owners of the subject property, and that the subject property would be partitioned by sale. The July 18, 2024 Order further provided, in relevant part, as follows: 

“4. For the Proposed Sale, the following provisions apply: . . .

Yee-FTB DOT - $ 2,089.26 (plus interest and penalties demanded by the Franchise Tax Board) . . .”

Escrow on the subject property apparently closed on November 26, 2024. (Defendants’ Dec. 6, 2024 Opp., 9:22).

“The court may, in all cases, order allowance, contribution, or other compensatory adjustment among the parties according to the principles of equity.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.140.) “Every partition action includes a final accounting according to the principles of equity for both charges and credits upon each cotenant’s interest. Credits include expenditures in excess of the cotenant’s fractional share for necessary repairs, improvements that enhance the value of the property, taxes, payments of principal and interest on mortgages, and other liens, insurance for the common benefit, and protection and preservation of title.” (Wallace v. Daley (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 1028, 1035-1036.) 

Defendants take the position that “[s]ince at least 2007, [they] have spent over $500,000[FN3] on the maintenance of the [subject] property (Exhibit 5) and they have paid in excess of $460,000 in attorney’s fees and costs.” (AOB, 2:23-26.) 

Defendants further assert that Plaintiff is not entitled to reimbursement or an offset in the form of rent for the subject property: “[I]n the absence of third party tenants, an ouster, or an agreement, a CoTIP [i.e., “cotenant in possession”] is not entitled to rent or other reimbursement for a CoTIP’s exclusive use and possession, because each cotenant is entitled to occupy all of the common property.” (In re Fazzio (1995) 180 B.R. 263, 269, citing Pico v. Columbet (1859) 12 Cal. 414, 420). Indeed, Judge White-Brown specifically remarked, “[t]he Court does not find . . . that defendants wrongfully denied Herman Yee the benefit of living at the [subject] property since 2007. The court finds that Yee left [the subject property] on his own volition and has not attempted to return.” (11/8/24 Soriano Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A, 10:23-25.) 

Defendants propose that, out of the $845,273.00 in funds on deposit (See Supp. Soriano Decl. filed Dec. 6, 2024, ¶ 3, Exh. A), they be credited with $535,344.20, that Plaintiff’s interest be debited $164,119.90 for liens identified in the July 18, 2024 Order, that estimated fees of $50,000.00 for the partition referee be split between Plaintiff and Defendants, and that the estimated $95,808.90 net proceeds be split in half between Plaintiff and Defendants for payment of attorney’s fees. 

The Court’s issue with Defendants’ position regarding reimbursement for expenditures made for the subject property, however, is that it is not supported by any admissible evidence. None of Defendants’ exhibits attached to the AOB have been authenticated. The identity of the individual(s) who prepared Exhibit 5 is unclear. Further, Exhibit 5 appears to be an itemization of purported expenses paid by Defendants on the subject property, unsupported by any actual mortgage or bank statements, tax records, receipts, or other documentation. The Court queries why such documentation was not furnished; Defendants have had ample time to procure supporting documentation, as this case has been pending since 2016. It is also unclear to the Court why Defendants believe they would be entitled to reimbursement for expenses on the subject property such as water, gas, electric, and internet. The Court disregards Exhibit 5. 

Plaintiff asserts that he is entitled to offset Defendants’ request for reimbursement of expenditures made for the subject property by the reasonable value of Defendants’ use. (Hunter v. Schultz (1966) 240 Cal.App.2d 24, 31.) The Court need not entertain any such offset request because Defendants’ request for reimbursement is declined as stated above.

Plaintiff has provided Defendants and the Court with a proposed distribution attached as Exhibit F to Soriano’s Supplemental Declaration filed December 6, 2024. The proposed distribution reflects total proceeds of $1,050,583.84, minus $41,190.94 in costs shared between the parties, resulting in a $504,696.45 subtotal of proceeds to Plaintiff and $504,696.45 subtotal of proceeds to Defendants. The proposed distribution further identifies debits of $164,119.90 to Plaintiff, resulting in a calculation of $340,576.55 for Plaintiff’s proceeds less debits. The proposed distribution identifies proposed debits of $340,969.23 to Defendants (including estimated partition referee fees of $30,000.00 and payment of Plaintiff’s attorney’s fees in the amount of $310,969.23), resulting in a calculation of $340,576.55 for Plaintiff’s proceeds less debits. 

The Court is inclined, at this juncture, to adopt Plaintiff’s proposed distribution, up until his proposed debit of $340,969.23 to Defendants. The Court agrees that referee fees should be split equally between Plaintiff and Defendants but declines to speculate at this juncture the amount of fees Mr. Taylor will be requesting, absent the benefit of a noticed motion. The Court also declines to rule on the issue of attorney’s fees absent a noticed motion. The Court is inclined to order that the monies remaining, up until Plaintiff’s proposed debit of $340,969.23 to Defendants, be held on deposit in an interest-bearing account for the benefit of the parties (as set forth in Paragraphs 7 and 9 of the July 18, 2024 Order) for a maximum period of 60 days, until the respective receiver’s fees and attorney’s fees motions are determined. 

The Court will hear further from the parties at the time of the hearing.
