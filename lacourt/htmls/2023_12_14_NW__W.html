DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20VECV00119    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: W

HSIU-FANG LAI, et al. v. PARADISE LODGE WINNETKA, INC.

 

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Mulan Kuo’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES AND PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION (SET ONE) TO PLAINTIFF AND CROSS-DEFENDANT HSIU-FANG LAI

 

Date of Hearing:        December 14, 2023              Trial Date:       March 11, 2024

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        20VECV00119

 

Moving Party:            Defendant/Cross-Complainant Mulan Kuo

Responding Party:     Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Hsiu-Fang Lai

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs allege they entered into a joint venture whereby Plaintiff Hsiu-Fang Lai, Defendant Pi Lien Fong, and Defendants Martin C. Kuo and Mulan Kuo each contributed $200,000 in exchange for a one-third ownership interest in the Motel. Plaintiffs further allege the Defendants managed the operations of the Motel to the exclusion of the Plaintiffs and as part of their misappropriation of the revenues generated by the business, the Defendants formed a number of business entities, including defendant entities Paradise Lodge Winnetka, Inc., 20128 Roscoe Boulevard, LLC, and Paradise Lodge General Partnership. Plaintiffs initiated this lawsuit on January 28, 2020.

 

On February 24, 2020, Plaintiffs Hsui-Fang Lai and Hsin-Hsiao (Scott) Wang filed a First Amended Complaint against Paradise Lodge Winnetka, Inc., 20128 Roscoe Boulevard, LLC, Paradise Lodge General Partnership, Pi Lien Fong, Ming-Hua Fong, Allison Baskett, and Mu-Lan Kuo Martin Kuo asserting causes of action for (1) Partition of Real Property; (2) Accounting; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (4) Breach of Partnership Agreement; (5) Declaratory Relief; and (6) Unjust Enrichment.

 

On April 20, 2020, default was entered against Defendants 20128 Roscoe Boulevard, LLC and Paradise Lodge Winnetka, Inc. On April 22, 2020 and April 23, 2020, default was entered against Defendants Paradise Lodge General Partnership and Pi Lien Fong respectively. The default was set aside on January 27, 2021.

 

On May 13, 2020, Defendants Paradise Lodge Winnetka, Inc.; 20128 Roscoe Boulevard, LLC; Paradise Lodge General Partnership; Pi Lien Fong; Minghua Fong; Ming-Hui Fong; Allison Baskett; Mu-Lan Kuo; and Martin Kuo filed an answer to the FAC.

 

Mu-Lan Kuo and Martin Kuo filed a cross-complaint against Hsiu-Fang Lai, Hsin-Hsiao (Scott) Wang, Ming-Hui Fong, Minghua Fong, Allison Baskett, and Paradise Lodge General Partnership for partition of real property, accounting, and declaratory relief on July 25, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Mulan Kuo’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Production of Documents to Requests for Production (Set One) to Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Hsiu-Fang Lai is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Mulan Kuo move the court for an order compelling Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Hsiu-Fang Lai to provide further responses and documents responsive to Request Nos. 8 and 10 of the Requests for Production of Documents (Set One) propounded by Cross-Complainant.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (CCP § 2031.310(h).)

 

The requests at issues are as follows: Request No. 8: All DOCUMENTS, including but not limited to account statements that EVIDENCE payments made on any credit card in YOUR name for the last ten (10) years. Request No. 10: All DOCUMENTS, including but not limited to account statements that EVIDENCE the deposit of cash at any time in the last ten (10) years into any bank account titled in YOUR name.  

 

Mulan Kuo argues discovery in this action has raised an issue as to whether the Partnership made unequal cash distributions to the partners with Lai claiming to have received distributions in an amount of at least $600,000 more than the distributions Mulan received during the same time period. As a result, Request Nos. 8 (all documents evidencing Lai’s payment of any credit card balance in the last 10 years) and 10 (all document evidencing cash deposits by Lai in any account in the last 10 years) are relevant.

 

In opposition, Hsiu-Fang Lai argues she has already complied with the two requests for production at issue and therefore, the motion is moot. Mulan Kuo contends, however, the further responses are still deficient. The court agrees. As for RPD No. 8, the request is not limited to credit card statements or cash deposits. If this is all that is in Hsiu-Fang Lai’s possession, control, or custody, she must state so. (CCP §2031.230.) In regards to RPD No. 10, the court agrees that if Hsiu-Fang Lai cannot obtain documents or does not have knowledge of documents, the response must comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230.

 

Accordingly, Defendant and Cross-Complainant Mulan Kuo’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Production of Documents to Requests for Production (Set One) to Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Hsiu-Fang Lai is GRANTED.

 

           


Case Number: 21STCV15600    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: W

MICHAEL COROMINAS, et al. v. WEST HILLS HOSPITAL AND MEDICAL CENTER, et al.

 

motion FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT  

 

Date of Hearing:        December 14, 2023              Trial Date:       November 25, 2024

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        21STCV15600

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Tarzana Medical Center LLC dba Providence

Cedars-Sinai Tarzana Medical Center

Responding Party:     Non-Opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 26, 2023, Plaintiffs Michael Corominas and Lisa Corominas filed a complaint against Defendants West Hills Hospital and Medical Center, Martin M. Mortazavi, M.D., Jessica Thomas, M.D., Paul Diehl, M.D., Dean Wilson, Jr., P.A., Specialty Imagining Center, Tejpal Singh, M.D., Providence Cedars-Sina Tarzana Medical Center, Chris Y. Wang, M.D., Jamie Soo Moi Eng, M.D., Exer Urgent Care, Michael H. Lee, M.D., and California Institute of Neuroscience for medical negligence and loss of consortium. Plaintiffs allege that further testing and workup should have been performed during the ER visit including testing for signs and symptoms and were negligent in failing to do so.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Tarzana Medical Center LLC dba Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzana Medical Center’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Tarzana Medical Center LLC dba Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzana Medical Center (“PCSTMC”) moves this court for an Order granting summary judgment in Defendant PCSTMC’s favor against Plaintiffs Michael Corominas and Lisa Corominas, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §437c. Defendant makes the motion on the grounds that Plaintiff’s Complaint has no merit and fails to present any triable issue of material fact as defendant PCSTMC met the standard of care. The Motion is further made on the grounds that there is no vicarious liability because no agency or employment relationship existed between PCSTMC and the named individual defendant physicians, specifically Chris Wang, M.D. and Jamie Eng, M.D., who were assigned to treat decedent at PCSTMC.

 

The elements of medical malpractice are:¿“(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”¿¿(Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 696, 701-02 (citations omitted).)¿ “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”¿¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Defendant presents the expert declaration of Carol Ingalls, BSN, RN, a Registered Nurse licensed in the State of California. Nurse Ingalls received her Diploma in Nursing from the Los Angeles County USC Medical Center School of Nursing in 1985, an Associate of Arts Degree from East Los Angeles College in 2007, and a Bachelor of Science Degree in Nursing from Western Governors University in 2011. (Ingalls Decl. ¶2.) Nurse Ingalls has numerous current certifications including Certified Emergency Nurse (CEN) and Certified Critical Care Nurse (CCRN), and memberships in the Emergency Nurses Association and Association of Critical Care Nurses. (Ingalls Decl. ¶2.) Nurse Ingalls testifies based on her education, training, knowledge, experience, expertise, and review of the materials, it is Nurse Ingalls’ opinion that the care and treatment rendered to Plaintiff by PCSTMC, its nurses and non-physician staff, complied with the nursing standard of care in the community. Specifically, the nursing staff appropriately monitored him, performed timely assessments including neurological status checks, obtained and monitored plaintiff’s vital signs, accurately recorded his complaints, addressed complaints of pain with interventions, documented changes in his status, and appropriately documented his course and treatment. (Ingalls Decl. ¶9.) It is the physicians’ decision to admit, perform additional workup and/or discharge the patient and the nursing staff implemented Dr. Wang’s orders for interventions including medications in a timely manner. (Ingalls Decl. ¶10.) Plaintiff was also timely triaged. (Ingalls Decl. ¶11.) Nurse Ingalls further attests it is not within the nursing standard of care to identify and diagnose cauda equina and there is no evidence that any other assessments or interventions were required of the nurses to meet the standard of care. (Ingalls Decl. ¶13.) Furthermore, the plaintiff was offered an MRI while in the Emergency Department but declined. (Ingalls Decl. ¶13.) Accordingly, it is Nurse Ingalls expert opinion did not commit any negligent act and/or omissions nor did they contribute to plaintiff’s alleged damages. 

 

Defendant PCSTMC has met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury.  As Defendant PCSTMC is entitled to judgment on the first cause of action for negligence, Defendant PCSTMC is also entitled to judgment on the second cause of action for loss of consortium. (See Vanhooser v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 921, 927.) Defendant PCSTMC has also sufficiently demonstrated Plaintiffs cannot establish vicarious liability. (UMF No. 17.)

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff instead filed a notice of non-opposition. Therefore, Defendant PCSTMC is entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendant PCSTMC’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 22VECV02618    Hearing Date: December 14, 2023    Dept: W

JANE DOE 1, et al. vs LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER REGARDING IN-PERSON DEPOSITION OF PLAINTIFFS

 

Date of Hearing:        December 14, 2023                          Trial Date:       None set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02618

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, And Jane Doe 4

Responding Party:     Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 30, 2022, Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, And Jane Doe 4 filed a lawsuit against Defendants Los Angeles Unified School District for negligence. Plaintiffs were minors and students at elementary schools in LAUSD where their perpetrator was employed. Plaintiffs allege the perpetrator groomed and sexually abused Plaintiffs and LAUSD breached their duty by failing to protect Plaintiffs from sexual abuse.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Motion for Protective Order Regarding In-Person Deposition of Plaintiffs is GRANTED.

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

discusion

 

I.                    PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER REGARDING IN-PERSON DEPOSITION OF PLAINTIFFS

 

Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, And Jane Doe 4 moves this court for a Protective Order quashing the deposition notices issued to Plaintiffs in this action by Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District and limiting the location of Plaintiff depositions in this matter.

 

A court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.”  (CCP §2030.090(b).)  The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks.  (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  A motion for a protective order “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (CCP §2030.090.)  “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (CCP §2016.040.)   

 

Plaintiffs voluntarily submit to a deposition. However, Plaintiffs object to the in-person depositions of Plaintiffs and insistence on being in-person at the office of defense counsel on the grounds an in-person deposition would subject Plaintiffs to unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, humiliation, and oppression that would have a direct impact on Plaintiffs’ mental health. As a result, Plaintiffs seek this protective order to participate in the deposition remotely.

 

In opposition, LAUSD contends pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.250, the party giving notice has a right to set the location of the deposition provided that it be within 75 miles of the deponent’s residence or within the county where the action is pending and within 150 miles of the deponent’s residence. Here, the depositions were noticed to take place at the offices of counsel for the District, which meets the distance requirements of the Code.  Moreover, remote depositions can be unpredictable and subject to technological failure.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have made the required showing why a protective order allowing remote depositions of Jane Doe Plaintiffs is warranted. Plaintiffs’ treating therapist, Alejandrina Burrell, LMFT, testifies the emotional damage that Mr. Tenas’ abuse caused Plaintiffs can be easily triggered by being in the presence of persons who have real or even perceived authority over them and/or when they are in a physical environment that conveys a real or perceived sense of authority and/or formality. (Carrillo Decl., Exh. I, ¶7.) As a result, an in person deposition of Plaintiffs presents a real risk further triggering this type of emotional response and damage. Carrillo Decl., Exh. I, ¶8.) LAUSD has not sufficiently shown a zoom deposition will hinder LAUSD’s counsel to gauge Plaintiffs’ claimed distress. Unlike Willoughby v. Superior Court (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 890, 891, Plaintiffs are not asking to exclude opposing parties from attending the deposition. Instead, Plaintiffs are asking to do the depositions remotely. Any one from LAUSD can attend the deposition remotely.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Protective Order Regarding In-Person Deposition of Plaintiffs is GRANTED.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $3,210

 

Plaintiffs Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, and Jane Doe 4 moves for an order compelling Defendant LAUSD to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set 1, Nos. 1, 2, 4, 6-51, 53, 54, 55, 56, 58, 59-99. Plaintiffs contend LAUSD’s response to Plaintiff’s Request for Production is improper, deficient, and not code compliant.

 

Code of Civil Proedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).)  

 

Plaintiffs argue Request for Production of Documents, Set One seeks Tenas’s employee file, evaluations, complaint history, disciplinary record, any communications regarding Tenas, performance reviews, class rosters, and I-Star incident reports prepared by LAUSD employees. LAUSD, however, objected to every single request and produced deficient documents. Plaintiffs contend LAUSD failed to produce any documents or responses to Plaintiffs’ requests, produced redacted documents, and did not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230 for documents it could not locate. Specifically, LAUSD withheld Tenas’s personnel files, site files, disciplinary records, evaluations, reviews, complaint records, LAUSD investigations, SSIT, I-STAR reports, LAUSD administrative rules, class rosters, and board policies.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ discovery requests are substantially over broad, and her motion fails to set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand. For example, with exception to iSTAR report No. 12320 related to Plaintiff Jane Doe 4, no iSTAR included in the Privilege Log deals with, or relates to, a single Plaintiff in the case at bar. Moreover, all of the documents at issue—all of which are outlined in the privilege log—are post-incident. None elucidate what the District knew about the alleged perpetrator before the claims made by Plaintiffs in the instant lawsuit. Defendant also objects to the discovery on the basis of attorney-client privilege and third-party privacy interests.

 

The court finds the discovery reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. First, the court finds the discovery requests are not overbroad based on Plaintiffs’ representation Plaintiffs agreed to limit the scope of their requests to include board policies and administrative rules regarding employee interactions with students, including employee-to-student sexual misconduct prevention, and related discipline policy for the 2000-2020 school years. As noted by Plaintiffs, Tenas was employed by Defendant LAUSD from 2000-2017, during which he was assigned to work at various LAUSD school sites.

 

Moreover, under Lopez v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.Appl4th 566, post-incident and pre-incident conduct may be discoverable. As for LAUSD’s attorney-client privilege claim of the iStar and SSIT documents, “[t]he landmark case of D.I. Chadbourne, Inc. v. Superior Court (1964) 60 Cal.2d 723 … details 11 basic principles to be applied in determining whether the attorney-client privilege exists in a corporate setting.  Where, as here, a corporate employer requires that its employees make a report, the privilege of that report is determined by the employer's purpose in requiring the report. When the corporate employer has more than one purpose in requiring the report, the dominant purpose will control.” (Scripps Health v. Superior Court (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 529, 533.) Unlike D.I. Chadbourne, Inc. v. Superior Ct. (1964) 60 Cal.2d 723, Plaintiffs have shown the dominant purpose of the reports are not for confidential transmittal to its attorney. D.I. Chadbourne noted “[w]hen the corporate employer has more than one purpose in directing such an employee to make such report or statement, the dominant purpose will control, unless the secondary use is such that confidentiality has been waived.” (D. I. Chadbourne, Inc. v. Superior Court of City and County of San Francisco (1964) 60 Cal.2d 723, 737.) As for LAUSD’s official information privilege claim, Defendant LAUSD has not identified any state or federal statute that prohibits the disclosure of the SSIT or I-STAR or demonstrated that disclosure is against the public interest. (See Evid. Code §1040(b).)

 

Lastly, as noted by Plaintiffs, the right to privacy is not absolute. In Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, the California Supreme Court recently criticized a long list of California decisions that required too stringent a test for discovery that invaded a party’s privacy, particularly when one party is asserting the privacy interests of a third party. Here, Plaintiff is seeking relevant records related to LAUSD's investigation of the abuses perpetrated by Defendant Rene Tenas. As noted by Plaintiffs, Plaintiffs are not seeking documents that would invade the privacy rights of third-party minors, mandated reporters or other witnesses and a Protective Order will rectify any privacy concerns.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request Defendant LAUSD and its counsel to pay monetary sanctions for $3,210.00 for the reasonable costs and attorney’s fees incurred in bringing the present motion.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(h).)   

 

There being substantial justification, with merit to both sides, the court denies the requests for sanctions.
