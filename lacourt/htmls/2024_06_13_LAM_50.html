                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

                        Defendants.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF SRI RAMANATHAN’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

 

Background

On August 17, 2022, Plaintiff Sri Ramanathan (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants G2 Secure Staff LLC and Saeed Bholat (jointly, “Defendants”). On February 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging eight causes of action.

Defendants now move to strike the FAC pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436. Plaintiff opposes. 

Discussion

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Defendants’ counsel’s declaration filed in support of the motion to strike indicates, inter alia, that “[b]etween February 23, 2024, and March 5, 2024, the parties attempted to meet and confer regarding Plaintiff’s filing of the First Amended Complaint. Unfortunately, the Parties were unable to reach an agreement and Defendants were forced to proceed with the filing of this motion.” (Woods Decl., ¶ 9.)

The Court notes that Defendants’ counsel’s declaration does not demonstrate that the parties met and conferred in person, by telephone, or by video conference, or that Defendants’ counsel attempted to do so. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), “[b]efore filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike. If an amended pleading is filed, the responding party shall meet and confer again with the party who filed the amended pleading before filing a motion to strike the amended pleading.” (Emphasis added.) Such meeting and conferring must be done in good faith with an effort to try to resolve the issues subject to the motion to strike.

In light of the foregoing, the hearing on Defendants’ motion to strike is continued to _______________, 2024 at 2 p.m. in Dept. 50. 

Defendants are¿ordered to meet¿and confer¿with Plaintiff within 10 days of the date of this order.¿If the parties are unable to resolve the pleading issues¿or if the parties are otherwise unable to meet and confer in good faith, Defendants are to¿thereafter¿file and serve¿a declaration setting forth the efforts to meet and confer in compliance Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a)(3) within 15 days of this order.¿

Defendants are ordered to give notice of this order. 

 

DATED:  June 13, 2024                                 ________________________________

Background

Plaintiffs Tea Hermankova (“Hermankova”) and Tomas Hermanek, a Minor, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Tea Hermankova (jointly, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action on April 27, 2023 against Defendants Runway Sub Reit, LLC (“Runway”) and Bozzuto Management Company (jointly, “Defendants”). The Complaint alleged causes of action for (1) breach of lease, (2) breach of implied covenant of habitability, (3) trespass, (4) negligence, (5) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment, (6) maintenance of nuisance, (7) constructive eviction,

(8) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (9) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

Runway demurred to the Complaint and moved to strike portions of the Complaint. On December 13, 2023, the Court issued a minute order in this action providing, inter alia, that “Runway’s demurrer to the first, sixth, and eighth causes of action is sustained, with leave to amend. Runway’s demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained, without leave to amend. Runway’s demurrer to the second, third, fifth, and seventh causes of action is overruled.” The December 13, 2023 minute order further provides that “Runway’s motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part, as set forth above. Plaintiffs are ordered to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days, the Court orders Runway to file and serve its answer to the Complaint within 30 days of the date of this order.”

On January 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) breach of lease, (2) breach of implied covenant of habitability, (3) trespass, (4) negligence, (5) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment, (6) maintenance of nuisance, (7) constructive eviction, and (8) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Runway now moves for an order striking portions of the FAC. Plaintiffs oppose.

Discussion

A.    Legal Standard

A court may strike any “¿irrelevant, false, or improper matter¿inserted in any pleading¿” or any part of a pleading “¿not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿” (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 436¿¿.) “¿The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437¿.)¿¿ 

B.    Allegations of the FAC  

In the FAC, Plaintiffs allege that since on or about May 16, 2022, Hermankova leased premises from Defendants, located at 12760 W. Millennium Dr., Unit A408, Playa Vista, California (the “Premises” and/or “Unit A408”). (FAC, ¶ 11.) Plaintiffs allege that “[d]ue to the unlawful and outrageous conduct as hereinafter set forth in detail, Plaintiff Hermankova and her minor son, Plaintiff Tomas Hermansek, could not reside at the Premises, and instead moved into 12760 W. Millennium Dr., Unit A424, Playa Vista, California, (‘Unit A424’), pursuant to an agreement entered into with the lessee of Unit A424, whereby Plaintiff Hermankova paid monthly rent to the lessee of Unit A424.” (FAC, ¶ 11.)

Plaintiffs allege that “[f]rom the very beginning of Plaintiffs’ occupancy of Unit A408, pursuant to the Rental Agreement, Plaintiffs experienced intolerable noise at night emanating from nine old and unmaintained air conditioning units located directly above Plaintiffs’ unit on the roof, as well as the refrigerator in Plaintiffs’ unit.” (FAC, ¶ 14.) Hermankova made several complaints to Runway management regarding these conditions. (FAC, ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs allege that “[o]n or about August 3, 2022, Hermankova was confronted by Runway’s head manager of maintenance, ‘Dan,’…on the roof over Hermankova’s unit, who physically pushed her and according to Runway’s own written admission, Hermankova ‘almost fell over one of the pipes on the roof.’” (FAC, ¶ 15.)

Plaintiffs allege that “[i]mmediately thereafter, instead of rectifying the situation, Runway served Hermankova with a Three-Day Notice,” and that “[t]he August 5, 2022 Three-Day Notice was not properly served and was never followed up upon.” (FAC, ¶ 16.) Plaintiffs further allege that “[a]fter Plaintiffs’ complaints continued to remain unaddressed, and even hostilely rebuffed, Hermankova entered into an arrangement with the lessee of Unit A424, Ivan Philip, to pay him rent to stay in his unit while he was away in Europe, so that Hermankova could recuperate in peace after her major cervical spine and neck surgery scheduled for January 17, 2023. Pursuant to her agreement with Ivan Philip, Hermankova and her minor 14-year-old son, Tomas, began occupying Unit A424 on or about December 14, 2022.” (FAC, ¶ 18.)

Plaintiffs allege that “[o]n January 28, 2023, Runway and Bozzuto, by and through their General Manager, Damanpak called the police, and informed the police that Runway intended to change the door lock of Unit A424. After meeting with the police at Unit A424, and Plaintiff Hermankova providing appropriate paperwork concerning her arrangement with Mr. Philip and their payment of rent to Mr. Philip, the police advised Runway that changing the locks would be illegal and that the police did not recommend doing so. The police then left.” (FAC, ¶ 19.)

Plaintiffs allege that “on February 2, 2023, Runway, at Damanpak’s direction, conducted an illegal lockout of Plaintiff Hermankova and her minor son, Plaintiff Tomas from Unit A424, of which Hermankova and her minor son, were in possession. Runway employees changed the lock on the door of Unit A424 while Hermankova was at a medical facility receiving post-operative tests and treatment, and Tomas, her then 14-year-old son, was walking their dog. Hermankova’s minor son returned to the unit with his dog and discovered that Plaintiffs had been locked out of Unit A424.” (FAC, ¶ 20.) “After waiting outside Unit A424 for over an hour, three male Runway employees approached Tomas, one armed with a handgun. Two of the individuals entered the unit, and told the armed individual not to let Hermankova’s son in. They left the unit with unknown possessions in their hands. Tomas then called the Los Angeles Police Department.” (FAC, ¶ 20.) “Police officers of the LAPD arrived at the scene, and advised Runway management that their lockout was illegal and directed them to return the keys for Unit A424 to Hermankova.” (FAC, ¶ 21.)

Plaintiffs further allege that “[o]n two more occasions, February 6 and on February 7, 2023, Runway personnel unlocked the door to Unit A424 and again attempted to illegally enter.” (FAC, ¶ 22.) Plaintiffs allege that “[i]n addition to the August 5, 2022 Three Day Notice, Runway served two subsequent Three Day Notices, one on January 25, 2023 and another on January 31, 2023.” (FAC, ¶ 23.) Plaintiffs allege that “Runway management, including Damanpak and Runway’s Leasing Manager, Keri Moody…forbade Hermankova or her minor son from entering, or even calling the leasing office. Moreover, Runway denied access by Hermankova to her account through the Runway portal. In fact, Runway’s management continued a pattern of terrorizing Hermankova, apparently with the intent to attempt to oust Plaintiff Hermankova and her minor son from the Premises by creating an environment so hostile that it amounted to a constructive eviction.” (FAC, ¶ 24.)

 

C.    Alleged Allegations Pertaining to “Overruled Causes of Action, Causes Not In Issue, and Introductory Allegations.”

As set forth above, on December 13, 2023, the Court issued a minute order pertaining to Runway’s demurrer to the Complaint and motion to strike portions of the Complaint. The Court’s December 13, 2023 minute order provides, inter alia, that “Runway’s demurrer to the first, sixth, and eighth causes of action is sustained, with leave to amend. Runway’s demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained, without leave to amend. Runway’s demurrer to the second, third, fifth, and seventh causes of action is overruled. Runway’s motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part, as set forth above.”  

Runway asserts that here, “without leave to amend, Plaintiff amended the Second Cause of Action for Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability, the Third Cause of Action for Trespass, the Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Use and Enjoyment, and the Seventh Cause of Action for Constructive Eviction. The new allegations are set forth in Paragraphs 58, 59, 67, 68, 82, 83, 99, 100, 101, 102, and 103 and should be stricken.” (Mot. at p. 12:18-22.)

 Runway also asserts that “without leave to amend, new introductory allegations were added. Those new allegations appear in Paragraphs 14, 25, 26, 27, 28, 29, and 30 of the FAC.” (Mot. at p. 12:23-24.) In addition, Runway asserts that “new allegations were added in the Fourth Cause of Action for Negligence, which was not the subject of the Demurrer. The new allegations appearing in the Fourth Cause of Action are Paragraphs 70, 71, 73, and 75.” (Mot. at p. 13:1-3.) Runway asserts that the foregoing allegations should be stricken.

Runway cites to Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[f]ollowing an order sustaining a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings with leave to amend, the plaintiff may amend his or her complaint only as authorized by the court’s order. The plaintiff may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so, unless the new cause of action is within the scope of the order granting leave to amend.” (Internal citation omitted.)

In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that the “amendments were clearly within the scope of the Court’s Order.” (Opp’n at p. 4:6-7.) As set forth above, the Court’s December 13, 2023 minute order in this matter provides, inter alia, that “Runway’s motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part, as set forth above.” (December 13, 2023 Minute Order at p. 14.) As noted in the December 13, 2023 minute order, Runway moved to strike Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages. The December 13, 2023 minute order provides, inter alia, that “Plaintiffs seek punitive damages in connection with the second cause of action for breach of implied covenant of habitability (Compl., ¶ 51), the third cause of action for trespass (Compl., ¶ 58), the fifth cause of action for breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment (Compl., ¶ 69), the sixth cause of action for nuisance (Compl., ¶ 77), the seventh cause of action for constructive eviction (Compl., ¶ 83), and the eighth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress (Compl., ¶ 90).” (December 13, 2023 Minute Order at p. 13.) The minute order further provides as follows:

 

“Runway asserts that here, ‘[a]t no point in the Complaint do Plaintiffs allege how or when Runway’s unnamed managing agent came to know of the alleged wrongdoings of which Plaintiffs complains.’ (Mot. at p. 24:23-24.) Plaintiffs do not respond to this point or address Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b) in their opposition. In addition, as noted by Runway in the reply, Plaintiffs’ Complaint also does not identify any officer or director of Runway ‘who engaged in the wrongful conduct or who had ‘advance knowledge’ and/or authorized or ratified the alleged wrongdoing.’ (Reply at p. 6:26-7:1.) Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Runway’s motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations (other than those pertaining to the sixth and eighth causes of action), with leave to amend.” (December 13, 2023 Minute Order at p. 14, emphasis added.)[1]

The Court finds that the allegations of paragraphs 14, 26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 58, 59, 67, 68, 82, 83, 101, 102, and 103 of the FAC are within the scope of the Court’s December 13, 2023 minute order granting Plaintiffs leave to amend their punitive damages allegations. Thus, the Court denies Runway’s motion to strike these allegations. 

Paragraphs 99 and 100 of the FAC are contained in the seventh cause of action for constructive eviction and allege, inter alia, that  “[a]s a further proximate result of Defendants’ conduct and Plaintiffs’ mental anguish, pain and physical injury, Plaintiffs were required to, and did, incur medical and related expenses, all to their further damage, in the sum according to proof at trial…As a result of these acts of Defendants, Plaintiffs were required to, and did, obtain necessary medical care and attention and will continue to require these services for an indefinite period…” (FAC, ¶¶ 99-100.) In addition, Paragraph 25 of the FAC was amended to add the sentence, “[b]y reason of Defendants’ unlawful and wrongful eviction of Plaintiffs, and as a proximate result of being ejected from the Premises in the manner herein described, Plaintiffs suffered great mental distress and became nervous and ill, all to their damage.” (FAC, ¶ 25.)

In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that “Plaintiffs’ addition of emotional distress damages allegations to their existing causes of action (including negligence), i.e., additions to paragraphs 25, 75, 99 and 100, in response to the Court’s prior order sustaining Defendants’ previous demurrer as to the cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, is entirely proper,” and that “Plaintiffs are entitled to prove their damages for emotional distress resulting from Defendants’ constructive eviction and negligent maintenance of premises.” (Opp’n at p. 5:18-25.) But as Plaintiffs acknowledge, the Court sustained Runway’s demurrer to original Complaint’s ninth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, without leave to amend. (December 13, 2023 Minute Order at pp. 11, 14.) As discussed, “[f]ollowing an order sustaining a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings with leave to amend, the plaintiff may amend his or her complaint only as authorized by the court’s order.” (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB, supra, 185 Cal.App.4th at p. 1023.) Thus, the Court finds that it is appropriate to strike paragraphs 99 and 100 of the FAC as well as the relevant portion of paragraph 25 of the FAC.

Lastly, paragraphs 70, 71, 73, and 75 of the FAC contain additional allegations. These paragraphs are contained within the FAC’s fourth cause of action for negligence. Runway did not demur to the cause of action for negligence in the original Complaint, and the Court did not grant Plaintiffs leave to amend the negligence cause of action. In the opposition, Plaintiffs do not appear to provide any argument as to how the new allegations in the FAC’s negligence cause of action are purportedly within the scope of the Court’s December 13, 2023 minute order granting leave to amend.

Runway moves to strike the last sentence of paragraph 25 of the FAC, and the first sentence of paragraph 70 of the FAC. Runway also moves to strike the entirety of paragraphs 71, 73, 75, 99, and 100 of the FAC. In light of the foregoing, the Court grants Runway’s motion to strike these allegations.

D.    Punitive Damages

Runway also moves to strike Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages. Plaintiffs seek punitive damages in connection with the second cause of action for breach of implied covenant of habitability (FAC, ¶¶ 57-58), the third cause of action for trespass (FAC, ¶¶ 66-67), the fifth cause of action for breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment (FAC, ¶¶ 81-82), the sixth cause of action for nuisance (FAC, ¶¶ 91-92), the seventh cause of action for constructive eviction (FAC, ¶¶ 101-102), and the eighth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress (FAC, ¶ 111-112).

In the motion, Runway asserts that “Plaintiffs have failed to plead facts that any ‘managing agent’ knew any employee was unfit or authorized/ratified any act.” (Mot. at p. 19:1-3.)[2] However, Plaintiffs now allege, inter alia, that “Damanpak is the General manager of both Runway Bozzuto, and Moody is the Leasing Manager for Runway. In addition to the acts and conduct set forth in paragraph hereinabove committed by ‘Dan,’ the Head Manager of Maintenance for Defendants in assaulting Plaintiff Hermankova, and the acts and conduct of Runway employees as set forth in paragraphs and hereinabove in threatening to, and in fact, locking Plaintiffs out and physically threatening minor Plaintiff Tomas, Plaintiffs are informed and believe, and thereon allege that all conduct complained of was authorized, ratified, directed, undertaken or carried out by Damanpak, Moody and Dan, and/or Does 1-10, whose identities have not yet been ascertained, but when they are through discovery, Plaintiffs will seek leave to amend accordingly.” (FAC, ¶ 26.)

Runway cites to CRST, Inc. v. Superior Court (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1255, 1273, where the Court of Appeal found that “[a]s triable issues exist regarding Davis’s preaccident knowledge of Contreras’s driving, we examine whether she was a managing agent. Generally, principal liability for punitive damages [does] not depend on employees’ managerial level, but on the extent to which they exercise substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy. Thus, to establish that an individual is a managing agent, a plaintiff seeking punitive damages must show that the employee exercised substantial discretionary authority over significant aspects of a corporation’s business. In this context, corporate policy refers to formal policies that affect a substantial portion of the company and that are the type likely to come to the attention of corporate leadership…The key inquiry thus concerns the employee’s authority to change or establish corporate policy.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.)

Runway asserts that here, “the FAC is void of any facts that an alleged leasing agent, general manager, or manager of maintenance at the apartment complex, had ‘the authority to change or establish corporate policy’ for Runway.” (Mot. at p. 20:1-3.) In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that this argument is factual. Indeed, CRST, relied upon by Runway, concerned the trial court’s denial of summary adjudication as to a request for punitive damages. (Id. at p. 1258.) The Court of Appeal in CRST noted that “[b]ecause there is no dispute that CRST is a ‘corporate employer,’ the remaining issue is whether ‘the advance knowledge’ and ‘conscious disregard’ was by a ‘managing agent of the corporation.’ (§ 3294, subd. (b).) Our focus is on Davis and Stanek, the two employees identified as potential ‘managing agents’ in the TAC.” (Id. at p. 1272.) The Court of Appeal found that “[n]othing before us suggests that Stanek had the requisite ‘advance knowledge’ regarding Contreras's lack of fitness,” and that “[t]he record discloses no evidence that Davis had the requisite authority.” (Id. at pp. 1273-1274.) With respect to Runway’s motion to strike here, the Court is not determining whether there is evidence demonstrating that “Damanpak” or “Moody,” are the “Managing Agents of Defendants Runway and Bozzuto.” (See FAC, ¶ 29.)

Runway also argues that “Plaintiffs have failed to allege specific facts which satisfy the heightened pleading burden in connection with a request for punitive damages.” (Mot. at p. 13:7-9.) Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a), “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”

As used in Civil Code section 3294, “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).)   “‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)  

            Runway argues that “[t]he FAC, on its face, establishes the action is grounded upon Defendant’s alleged failure to repair a noisy refrigerator in Unit A408 and refrigerator condensors units on the roof. Further, Plaintiffs complain that after they vacated Unit A408 and entered into an ‘arrangement’ with the tenant of Unit A424 to sublet his Unit, notwithstanding the fact subleasing is prohibited in the apartment building, Defendant attempted to evict them from Unit A424…These facts do not show malice nor do they meet the heightened burden, set forth above, for an award of punitive damages.” (Mot. at pp. 17:22-18:2, emphasis omitted.)

            But as noted by Plaintiffs, the FAC alleges that “[o]n January 28, 2023, Runway and Bozzuto, by and through their General Manager, Damanpak called the police, and informed the police that Runway intended to change the door lock of Unit A424. After meeting with the police at Unit A424, and Plaintiff Hermankova providing appropriate paperwork concerning her arrangement with Mr. Philip and their payment of rent to Mr. Philip, the police advised Runway that changing the locks would be illegal and that the police did not recommend doing so. The police then left…Nevertheless…despite the police officers’ admonition, and in direct contravention of California law, on February 2, 2023, Runway, at Damanpak’s direction, conducted an illegal lockout of Plaintiff Hermankova and her minor son, Plaintiff Tomas from Unit A424, of which Hermankova and her minor son, were in possession…After waiting outside Unit A424 for over an hour, three male Runway employees approached Tomas, one armed with a handgun. Two of the individuals entered the unit, and told the armed individual not to let Hermankova’s son in.” (FAC, ¶¶ 19-20.) The Court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged “malice” for purposes of Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages.

            Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Runway’s motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Runway’s motion as to paragraphs 71, 73, 75, 99, and 100 of the FAC, the first sentence of paragraph 70 of the FAC, and the last sentence of paragraph 25 of the FAC, without leave to amend.

Runway is ordered to give notice of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTION FOR AN ORDER THAT THE TRUTH OF MATTERS SPECIFIED IN PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ADMISSIONS BE DEEMED ADMITTED AS AGAINST DEFENDANT BYOUNG LEE

 

Background

Plaintiff National Commercial Recovery, Inc. dba Blair Smith and Associates (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on July 31, 2023 against a number of defendants, including Byoung Lee aka Byoung D. Lee aka Byoung Don Lee (“Lee”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) common counts – open book account, (2) common counts – goods sold and delivered,

(3) account stated, and (4) money due on dishonored checks.

Plaintiff now moves for an order that the truth of the matters specified in Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One, be deemed admitted against Lee. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Lee. The motion is unopposed.

Discussion

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (a), “[i]f a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply: (a) The party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010).” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (b), “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c), “[t]he court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”

In his declaration in support of the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel states that on December 6, 2023, Plaintiff served Lee with “Plaintiff’s Request for Admission of Facts for Defendant Byoung Don Lee; Set One,” (herein, the “RFAs”). (Besnyl Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) Plaintiff’s counsel states in his declaration that “[o]n January 11, 2024, after the responses became overdue, I sent a demand letter to defendant BYOUNG LEE stating that a motion to deem the requests admitted would be brought by plaintiff unless the responses were received by the following Thursday, January 18, 2024.” (Besnyl Decl., ¶ 3 Ex. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[t]o date, I have not received any response to my letter or any responses to plaintiff’s Request For Admissions.” (Besnyl Decl., ¶ 4.)

On May 28, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held in this matter. The Court’s May 28, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he matter is called for hearing. There are no appearances by or for the defendant(s) this date. The plaintiff has fulfilled the Informal Discovery Conference Requirement re: The Request for Admissions.”

Lee does not oppose the instant motion and thus does not provide any evidence that Lee served a response to the subject RFAs. The Court finds that the evidence demonstrates that Lee did not timely serve a response to the RFAs. Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming the matters in Plaintiff’s RFAs to Lee admitted.

Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions in connection with the instant motion. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c), “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Emphasis added.)

In his supporting declaration, Plaintiff’s counsel states that “I will have spent approximately 2.0 hours in preparing and appearing for this motion. My hourly rate is billed at $275.00 per hour, resulting in a total fees of $550.00. Plaintiff has also incurred $60.00 in filing costs in bringing this Motion; thus the total attorney’s fees and costs in bringing this Motion will be $610.00.” (Besnyl Decl., ¶ 5.) The Court finds that $610.00 in monetary sanctions is reasonable and grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in this amount.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is granted. The Court orders that the matters in Plaintiff’s Request for Admission of Facts for Defendant Byoung Don Lee; Set One are deemed admitted.

The Court further orders Lee to pay $610.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days of notice of this Order.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this Order.
