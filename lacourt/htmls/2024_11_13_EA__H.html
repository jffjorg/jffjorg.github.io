DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV00785    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: H

Plaintiff Continental Casualty Company’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice. 


Background 

Plaintiff Continental Casualty Company (“Plaintiff”) alleges that while Defendant William Kellenberg (“Kellenberg”) was a guest at Springhill Suites by Marriot in Arcadia, California, Kellenberg caused a fire when he smoked in bed and left a candle unattended, resulting in extensive damage to the hotel, which was insured by Plaintiff.

On March 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting a single cause of action of general negligence against Kellenberg and Does 1-10.

On May 9, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Kellenberg.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment.


Discussion

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted:

1.       On May 30, 2024, Judge Peter A. Hernandez denied Plaintiff’s prior application for default judgment without prejudice; in doing so, the court noted, inter alia, that “Plaintiff has not provided any incident and/or police reports and/or photographic evidence regarding the incident, nor has it provided the court with any documentary evidence reflecting how damages were calculated.” 

The unsigned “Incident Investigation Form” and photographs attached as part of Exhibit C to the Declaration of Ryan Dillon appear to lack authentication and do not mention William Kellenberg.

2.       The Declaration of Alison K. Schlick (“Schlick”) regarding calculation of interest refers to a “Complaint filed date” of December 22, 2020. (See Schlick Decl., ¶ 3). Plaintiff’s complaint, however, was filed on March 16, 2023. Plaintiff is directed to explain this discrepancy and the basis for the December 22, 2020 start date. Additionally, Schlick has made references to a “loan principal” and “loan principal balance,” which appear to be typographical errors. (Id.)

3.       The Court is in receipt of a proposed “Order to Accept Copies in Lieu of Originals.” However, the Court does not appear to be in possession of the underlying request corresponding to this proposed order.

Accordingly, the Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice and Plaintiff is directed to submit a revised default judgment packet that addresses the aforementioned issues. 

Case Number: 23PSCV01616    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: H

Chinn, et al. v. Qi, Case No. 23PSCV01616

ORDER ON MOTION TO VACATE ARBITRATION ORDER AND LIFT STAY

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Yong Qi’s Motion to Vacate Order Compelling Arbitration and to Lift Stay is DENIED for lack of jurisdiction.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiffs Martin Chinn and Doreen K. Chiu (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows: 

On or about October 8, 2022, Plaintiffs, as sellers, entered into a written purchase agreement with Yong Qi (“Qi”), as buyer, for the sale of real property known as 3438 Fionna Place, West Covina, CA 91792. Qi subsequently sought to renegotiate the purchase price, which Plaintiffs rejected. Following this rejection, Qi refused to deposit monies into escrow and move forward with the sale. 

On May 30, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Qi and Does 1-10 for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) common count, and (4) intentional interference with contractual relations. 

On August 14, 2023, Qi filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Miranda Chen (“Chen”), APEC Enterprises, Inc. (“APEC”), and Roes 1-10 for (1) negligence and (2) equitable indemnity in proportion to fault. 

On January 24, 2024, Chen and APEC filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Qi and Does 1-10 for (1) equitable indemnity, (2) comparative fault, (3) apportionment and contribution, and (4) declaratory relief. 

On March 4, 2024, the Court granted Qi’s motion to compel arbitration; at that time, the case was ordered stayed, pending completion of the arbitration. 

A Status Conference Re: Arbitration is set for December 11, 2024. Pending before the Court is Qi’s motion to vacate the Court’s order compelling arbitration and to lift the stay. For the reasons set forth below, the motion is denied.

II.      Discussion

On December 12, 2023, Qi filed a “Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Court Action,” wherein he moved the Court for orders compelling arbitration of all claims and causes of action between Plaintiffs and himself and staying all further judicial proceedings in this action pending completion of arbitration. (Michel Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) On March 4, 2024, Judge Peter Hernandez granted that motion and ordered the case stayed, pending completion of the arbitration.

Qi now moves the Court for an order vacating the March 4, 2024 order compelling Plaintiffs to arbitrate the claims and causes of action raised in Plaintiffs’ complaint against Qi and to lift the stay in this case; alternatively, Qi moves the Court for an order compelling arbitration of all claims and causes of action between Qi, Chen, and APEC and staying this case pending completion of such arbitration.

The Court denies the motion for lack of jurisdiction. Once a motion to compel arbitration is granted and the case is stayed, “the action at law sits in the twilight zone of abatement with the trial court retaining merely vestigial jurisdiction over matters submitted to arbitration.” (Titan/Value Equities Group, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 482, 487.) During the stay, “under its ‘vestigial’ jurisdiction, a court may appoint arbitrators if the method selected by the parties fails (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.6); grant a provisional remedy ‘but only upon the ground that the award to which an applicant may be entitled may be rendered ineffectual without provisional relief’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.8, subd. (b)); and confirm, correct, or vacate the arbitration award (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285).” (Id.) However, “[a]bsent an agreement to withdraw the controversy from arbitration . . . no other judicial act is authorized.” (Id. [emphasis added].)

Here, the case has been stayed pending the completion of arbitration and there is no agreement to withdraw the controversy from arbitration. Accordingly, Qi’s motion is denied for lack of jurisdiction.

I.        Background

Plaintiff Dezarray Sandoval (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) regarding a 2023 Honda Civic, VIN No. 2HGFE2F50PH549716 (the “subject vehicle”), that the subject vehicle suffers from various defects, and that the subject vehicle has not been repaired after a reasonable number of attempts.

 

On April 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting two causes of action under the Song-Beverly Act against Defendant and Does 1-10.

 

Pending before the Court are five discovery motions, where Plaintiff seeks (1) to have certain matters be deemed admitted, (2) to compel responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production, and (3) to compel the deposition attendance of a person most qualified and custodian of records.  

 

 

II.      Legal Standards

Responses to requests for admission, interrogatories, and requests for production of documents are due 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.250, subd. (a), 2030.260, subd. (a), 2031.260, subd. (a).) If timely responses are not served, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses or deeming matters admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.280, subd. (b), 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).)

As to requests for admissions, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction . . . on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated [a] motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

As to interrogatories and requests for production of documents, “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories [or a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).) 

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230). 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, . . . a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection. . ., fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) 

“A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. . ., or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contracted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b).) 

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction. . ., unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request must be accompanied by a separate statement providing all information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses at issue. (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a),(c).) 


III.     Discussion

A.       Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order that the truth of the matters specified in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set No. One, served on Defendant on June 7, 2024, be deemed admitted and conclusively established. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and its counsel in the amount of $1,122.50. 

Plaintiff’s counsel Thach Tran (“Tran”) represents as follows:

On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with the subject discovery. (Tran Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.)[FN] Responses were initially due on or before July 9, 2024. (Id., ¶ 3). On July 29, 2024, Defendant’s counsel reached out for an extension and to revive their deadline. (Id., ¶ 4). On August 8, 2024, counsel spoke telephonically, during which time Plaintiff’s counsel advised that Defendant’s objections were waived and requested that responses be provided without objections. (Id.) On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email regarding the status of the responses; no response thereto was received. (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. B.)

Defendant contends that the motion is moot because it has now provided Plaintiff with Code-compliant, verified responses to the subject discovery. (Hom Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 2.) However, Defendant’s responses include objections, which have been waived since the responses were untimely. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) Defendant appears to contend that it should be relieved from waiver of its objections on the basis that the attorney assigned to handle discovery on this case, Vanessa Dao (“Dao”), had an undiagnosed medical condition that affected her ability to work.

First, it is unclear to the Court how blame for the failure to provide timely responses may be shifted to Dao here. Dao attests that “around the beginning of 2024, [she] began suffering from a previously undiagnosed medical condition,” that “[d]ue to [her] condition, [she]. . . mistakenly thought that discovery responses were served timely to Plaintiff’s counsel,” that “[o]n or about May 30, 2024, [she] had a medical emergency that rendered [her] unconscious” that she “subsequently resigned [her] position at Clark Hill early June of 2024,” and that “[t]he failure to serve the discovery responses timely is the result of [her] mistake.” (Dao Decl., ¶¶ 3-6.) The problem with Dao’s declaration is that the subject discovery was served on June 7, 2024, roughly around the time of Dao’s resignation from the firm in “early June of 2024.” (Id., ¶ 5.) Responses to the discovery were not due until July 2024, well after Dao had resigned.

Second, Defendant’s request for relief from any waiver of objections is inappropriate in an opposition; instead, it must be filed separately as a noticed motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) No such motion has been filed.

With that said, Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c) provides that “[t]he court shall make this order [i.e., ‘that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted’], unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Emphasis added).

“Subdivision (c) of section 2033.280 requires the court to evaluate whether the proposed response to the requests for admission substantially complies with section 2033.220. This suggests that the court evaluate qualitatively the proposed response to RFAs in toto to determine whether it substantially complies with the code. It does not permit the court to segregate each individual RFA response for the purpose of finding that portions of the document are code-compliant (and will therefore be accepted), while concluding that other portions are noncompliant (and will thus be rejected). Furthermore. . . there is an effective statutory vehicle by which a propounding party may seek a court order compelling a responding party to cure individual RFA responses deemed not to be in compliance with section 2033.220--namely, a motion to compel further responses under section 2033.290. . .” (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 779-780 [quotation marks, italics and citation omitted].)

The Court has reviewed Defendant’s responses to the subject discovery and determines that they are substantially compliant in toto. Accordingly, the instant motion is denied as moot, except as to the issue of sanctions.
