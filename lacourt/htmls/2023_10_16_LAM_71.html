Department 71: Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff  via the Department's email: SMCdept71@lacourt.org before the set hearing time.  See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).   All parties are otherwise encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect for all matters.

Case Number: 22STCV10865    Hearing Date: October 16, 2023    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MERARY GOMEZ CARBAJAL, 

 

         vs.

 

PREMIER AUTOMOTIVE OF PLACENTIA, LLC, et al.

 Case No.:  22STCV10865

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 16, 2023

 

Plaintiff Merary Gomez Carbajal’s motion for reconsideration is continued to Tuesday, December 12, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.

 

The Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration for Tuesday, December 12, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.  Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 28, 2023, regarding Yeh v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (Cal. Ct. App., Sept. 6, 2023, No. A166537) 2023 WL 5741703; Kielar v. Superior Court of Placer County (Cal. Ct. App., Aug. 16, 2023, No. C096773) 2023 WL 5270559; Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, review filed (Aug. 1, 2023); and Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, review granted (July 19, 2023).

 

Plaintiff Merary Gomez Carbajal (“Gomez Carbajal”) (“Plaintiff”) moves for this Court to reconsider its January 11, 2023, order (“1/11/23 Ruling”) granting Defendant FCA US, LLC’s (“FCA”) (“Defendant”) motion to compel arbitration.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.) 

 

Procedural Background

          On March 30, 2022, Plaintiff filed her operative complaint against FCA, Premier Automotive of Placentia, LLC (“Premier Automotive”), and Premier Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram of Buena Park (“Premier Buena Park”) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, Civil Code §§1790 et seq. (“Song-Beverly”) and one in common law tort: (1) violation of Song-Beverly- breach of express warranty [against FCA]; (2) violation of Song-Beverly- breach of implied warranty [against FCA]; and (3) negligent repair [against Premier Buena Park] arising from Plaintiff’s September 15, 2020 entry into a warranty contract with FCA regarding a 2020 Jeep Cherokee [“Subject Vehicle”].

          On January 11, 2023, this Court granted FCA’s motion to compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.  (1/11/23 Ruling.)

          Plaintiff filed the instant motion on June 22, 2023.  FCA and Premier Automotive filed their opposition on October 4, 2023.  Plaintiff filed her reply on October 9, 2023. 

 

Motion for Reconsideration

C.C.P. §1008(c) provides, as follows: “If a court at any time determines that there has been a change of law that warrants it to reconsider a prior order it entered, it may do so on its own motion and enter a different order.”  (C.C.P. §1008(c).)

Defendants’ objection to Plaintiff’s motion as untimely because it was not filed within 10 days of notice of entry of the 1/11/23 Ruling is unavailing.  In Malek v. Blue Cross of California (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 44, the Court of Appeal determined that a change in the law required the trial court to reconsider its prior ruling compelling arbitration.  (Id. at pgs. 59-60.)  The Malek Court stated, “the progress of the arbitration is not material when considering a change in the law affecting whether the arbitral forum was a correct one.”  (Id. at pg. 60.) 

“If a court believes one of its prior interim orders was erroneous, it should be able to correct that error no matter how it came to acquire that belief. For example, nothing would prevent the losing party from asking the court at a status conference to reconsider a ruling.  (See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial, supra, § 9:327.8, p. 9(1)–107.)  But a party may not file a written motion to reconsider that has procedural significance if it does not satisfy the requirements of section 437c, subdivision (f)(2), or 1008. The court need not rule on any suggestion that it should reconsider a previous ruling and, without more, another party would not be expected to respond to such a suggestion.”  (Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1108, as modified (June 10, 2005).) 

          Plaintiff requests this Court reconsider its 1/11/23 Ruling in light of the publication of Yeh v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (Cal. Ct. App., Sept. 6, 2023, No. A166537) 2023 WL 5741703; Kielar v. Superior Court of Placer County (Cal. Ct. App., Aug. 16, 2023, No. C096773) 2023 WL 5270559; Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, review filed (Aug. 1, 2023); and Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, review granted (July 19, 2023)

Here, the Court has been put on notice of new case law that disagrees with Felisilda, the case on which this Court relied to grant the 1/11/23 Ruling.  Accordingly, the Court will sua sponte reconsider its Order.

Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 28, 2023, regarding the new case law that disagrees with Felisilda: Yeh, Kielar, Montemayor, and Ford Motor Warranty Cases.

Accordingly, the Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration for Tuesday, December 12, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is continued to Tuesday, December 12, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.

The Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration for Tuesday, December 12, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.  Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 28, 2023.

Defendants Pinnacle Estate Properties, Inc.’s, Jeremy Rodriguez’s, and Sylvia Ena’s motion to compel the deposition of Cross-Complainant Jose Solano is granted but limited to three hours.  Jose Solano is ordered to appear for depositions and produce documents within 10 days of this ruling.

 

Defendants Pinnacle Estate Properties, Inc.’s, Jeremy Rodriguez’s, and Sylvia Ena’s request for sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,390.00.

 

          Defendants Pinnacle Estate Properties, Inc.’s (“Pinnacle”), Jeremy Rodriguez (“Rodriguez”), and Sylvia Ena (“Ena”) (collectively, “Defendants”) move to compel Cross-Complainant Jose Solano[1] (“Solano”) (“Cross-Complainant”) to appear for deposition.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2034.210 et seq., 2025.450, 2025.610.)  Defendants request sanctions against Solano and/or his counsel of record in the amount of $1,580.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)

 

          Meet and Confer

A motion to compel a deposition must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (C.C.P. §§2016.040, 2025.480(b).)

Defendants’ counsel declares that Cross-Complainant’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter on or about September 5, 2023, arguing that a second deposition for Solano is not allowed under the rules and proposed a limited scope of two hours for a second deposition.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶9, Exh. C.)   Defendants’ counsel declares she responded to the meet and confer letter by email on or about September 6, 2023, arguing the discovery cut-off had not passed and that Defendants did not have the benefit of knowing what Cross-Complainant’s allegations would be prior to its filing.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶10, Exh. D.). Defendants’ counsel’s declaration does not indicate the parties met and conferred by telephone.  Defendants’ counsel’s declaration does not substantially comply with the requirements of C.C.P. §2016.040.  Regardless, the Court in its discretion will consider Defendants’ motion.

 

Background

Defendants served a Notice of Deposition of Solano on August 24, 2023, for the date of September 14, 2023.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶8, Exh. B.)  On September 8, 2023, Solano served an untimely objection to Defendants’ notice of deposition.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶11, Exh. E.)  On September 14, 2023, Solano did not appear for his deposition.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶12, Exh. F.)  

Defendants filed the instant motion on September 18, 2023.  Solano filed his opposition on October 2, 2023.  Plaintiffs Teodulo Castellanos (“Teodulo”) and Maria Castellanos (“Maria”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed their opposition on October 2, 2023.  Defendants filed the replies to Solano and Plaintiffs on October 9, 2023.

 

          Discussion

C.C.P. §2025.450 states, in pertinent part, as follows:

(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, . . .  described in the deposition notice.

 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents . . . described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

(C.C.P. §2025.450(a).)

In the context of a motion to compel, the information or testimony should be compelled if the moving party simply shows “good cause” for the request.  (C.C.P. §2025.450(b)(1).)  Similarly, the Court can order the second deposition of a natural person by a party that was served with the first deposition notice based on a showing of “good cause.”  (C.C.P. §§2025.610(a), (b); Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 254.)  “Good cause” is liberally construed, and discovery is justified where specific facts show that the evidence is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial.  (Associated Brewers District Co., Inc. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 587.)  A party is not subject to repeated depositions on the same factual issue, but there is no absolute limitation that a party may only take a single deposition.  (Carlson v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 431, 439 n.5.)

          A party who seeks to resist or limit a self-executing form of discovery has the burden of producing facts that establish, to the Court’s satisfaction, good cause for denying or limiting the discovery sought.  (Southern California Edison Co. v. Superior Court (1972) 7 Cal.3d 832, 843.)

          Defendants’ motion to compel the deposition of Solano is granted.  Solano filed his Cross-Complaint about a month after his first deposition was taken, and it alleges new causes of action that necessitate further discovery by Defendants.   (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶¶ 4-6.)  However, in light of Solano’s prior deposition on November 10, 2022, lasting almost three hours, the second deposition at issue in this motion is limited to three hours.  (C.C.P. §2025.290(a).)

The Court grants Defendants’ motion pursuant to C.C.P. §2025.450 and orders Solano to appear for deposition within 10 days of this ruling.

 

Sanctions

C.C.P. §2023.030(a) provides that a court “may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”

Defendants’ counsel declares her hourly fees are $190 per hour, and she spent 5 hours preparing the instant motion and corresponding with counsel for Solano regarding his deposition.  (Decl. of Hawatmeh ¶14.)  However, reasonable attorneys’ fees cannot be compensated for time incurred meeting and conferring.  Defendants’ counsel also requests three additional hours to draft a reply brief and appear in court.  Defendants’ counsel also requests $60 for the costs to file the instant motion.

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff sanctions in the amount of $1,390.00, as calculated below:

(7 hours x $190/hour) + $60 filing fee = $1,390.00

 

Conclusion

Defendants’ motion to compel Solano to appear for deposition is granted and limited to three hours.  Solano is to appear for deposition with 10 days of this ruling.

Defendants’ request for sanctions is granted in the reduced amount of $1,390.00.

Moving Party is to give notice of this ruling.
