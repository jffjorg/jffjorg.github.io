Case Number: 23STLC01303    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                          October 1, 2024

Case Name:                             Maxwell + Bessie, Inc. v. Michael Onagbesan, et al.

Case No.:                                23STLC01303

Motions and Hearing:           Motion for Evidence Sanctions for Failure to Comply with Court Discovery Orders Issued on April 18, 2024;

 

Order to Show Cause Re: Why Defendant Should not be Sanctioned up to $1,400 for failing to obey the Court’s previous orders, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §2023.010, Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030(a);

 

Conference Re: Status of the Meet and Confer Order and Status on Discovery.   

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Maxwell + Bessie, Inc. 

Responding Party:                   Unopposed

Notice:                                     OK

 

 

Tenttive Ruling:                      Defendant has failed to comply with the Court Order of August 13, 2024, directing Defendant to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s Counsel regarding any outstanding discovery.  The Court sanctions Defendant for failure to comply with the Court’s previous order on August 18, 2024, to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $850 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030(a) and §2023.010.

 

The Motion for Evidence Sanctions is CONTINUED TO OCTOBER 22, 2024, at 10:00 a.m. in Department 25 of the Spring Street Courthouse.  This is the final continuance.

 

Parties are ordered to submit a declaration Re: Status on Outstanding Responses to Special Interrogatories and Request for Production of Documents by no later than five (5) court days before the hearing. 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 23, 2023, Plaintiff Maxwell + Bessie, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Michael Onagbesan (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of written agreement, (2) breach of oral agreement, (3) breach of oral agreement, and (4) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. The complaint arises from the alleged breach of an agreement to provide media advertising and marketing.  

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of written agreement, (2) breach of oral agreement, (3) breach of oral agreement, and (4) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

On August 3, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant’s responses to set one of Plaintiff’s request for production of documents. Also, on March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant’s responses to set one of Plaintiff’s special interrogatories.

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Sanctions

A.                Legal Standard

“A trial court may impose sanctions, including terminating sanctions, for a party’s misuse of the discovery process, which includes disobedience of a court order.” Sole Energy Co. v. Hodges (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 199, 207. A misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).) A misuse of the discovery process also includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).) A court may impose issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, or monetary sanctions against a party engaging in misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030.) An evidence sanction involves “an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (c).)

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992. “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” Ibid. “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” Ibid. Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” Ibid. “Although not expressly required by statute, courts have noted that, absent unusual circumstances, nonmonetary sanctions are warranted only if a party willfully fails to comply with a court order.” Aghaian v. Minassian (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 603, 619-20. The imposition of evidentiary sanctions does not require a finding of willfulness. Id. at p. 620.   

 

B.        Analysis

In support of the Motion, Plaintiff’s counsel, James Lee Reynolds, Esq. (“Reynolds”), provides a declaration. Mr. Reynolds declares the following: on April 18, 2024, the Court heard and granted Plaintiff’s motions to compel Defendant to provide initial responses to set one of Plaintiff’s special interrogatories and request for production. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. 1.) On April 18, 2024, Plaintiff served on Defendant and filed a Notice of Ruling as to such orders. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. 2.) With service via regular US mail on April 18, 2024, Defendant’s verified code-compliant responses and production had to be served by May 13, 2024. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 3.) With service via regular mail by Defendant of his initial responses and production, and allocating five business days, excluding weekends and holidays for delivery, Plaintiff should have received Defendant’s initial responses and production no later than May 20, 2024. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

On or about April 22, 2024, Plaintiff received from Defendant a non-descriptive pile of documents with bate-stamped numbers 1 through 145. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. 3.) On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff had not received from Defendant the Court-ordered verified code-compliant initial responses and production. (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 6; Ex. 5.) The pile of non-descriptive legal documents does not contain a document that complies with CCP § 2031.280(a).) (Reynolds Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

In his supplemental declaration, Mr. Reynolds indicates that on July 19, 2024, he advised Defendant of the Court-ordered meet and confer. (Reynolds Supp. Decl., ¶ 3.) After Defendant’s non-responsiveness to the initial meet and confer attempts and initial refusal to provide availability to meet and confer, the parties met and conferred on July 29, 2024. Accordingly, Plaintiff is withdrawing its request for exclusion of this information.” (Reynolds Supp. Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

According to the supplemental brief, Plaintiff is only seeking to prevent Defendant from introducing or attempting to introduce any matter or information responsive to Plaintiff’s special interrogatories. (Supp. Brief at pp. 2-3.) Plaintiff specifically indicates that it “withdraws its challenge to Defendant’s use in whole or in part of the unidentified documents, which he previously filed with the Court, and served on Plaintiff; provide[d], however, at [t]rial Defendant can properly lay a foundation for admissibility.” (Supp. Brief at p. 2:13-15.)

 

Thus, based on set one of Plaintiff’s special interrogatories, Plaintiff seeks an order prohibiting Defendant from attempting to place into evidence the following:

 

1.         All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action (Breach of Written Agreement);

2.         All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action (Breach of Oral Agreement);

3.         All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s Third Cause of Action (Breach of Oral Agreement); and

4.         All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action (Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith); and

5.         Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) documents required to be identified and disclosed in response to set one of Plaintiff’s special interrogatories.

 

Defendant has not opposed the Motion.

 

When determining whether evidentiary sanctions are appropriate, merely failing to obey a Court order is sufficient and a showing of willful disobedience is not required.  (Aghaian v. Minassian, 64 Cal.App.5th 603, 618-620.)  The Court has discretion to determine whether sanctions are appropriate.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.210 through 2030.310 set forth the requirements for providing Code-compliant responses to interrogatories.  Similarly, §§ 2031.210 through 2031.310 set forth the requirements for providing Code-compliant responses to a request for production. Plaintiff argues that the Defendant has disobeyed the Court’s Order compelling compliance with Plaintiff’s discovery requests because he has served “a non-descriptive pile of documents” and, as a result, seeks evidentiary sanctions regarding eleven matters that prohibit Defendant from presenting any evidence regarding non-liability or relating to Plaintiff’s claims.

 

In Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co., the Court reaffirmed the trial court’s broad discretion when imposing discovery sanctions.  (Rutledge, 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1193.)  The Court explained that this discretion must be exercised for the purpose of compelling disclosure of discoverable information not solely to punish the offending party.  (Ibid.)  Moreover, “the sanction chosen should not provide a windfall to the other party, by putting the prevailing party in a better position than if he or she had obtained the discovery sought and it had been favorable.  [Citations.]”  (Ibid.)  In Rutledge, the Court found that the trial court’s refusal to order evidentiary sanctions, despite seven prior orders to comply with discovery, was not an abuse of discretion because if sanctions had been granted, they “would provide a windfall to appellants relieving them of their burden of proving their theory of liability.”  (Ibid. at 1194.)

BACKGROUND 

 

On May 28, 2024, Petitioner Edward C. Yoon (“Petitioner”) filed the instant petition to confirm the arbitration award issued in FINRA Case No. 23-02061, Edward C. Yoon v. Mehdi Famili and Diana Offen.  The award recommends expunging two customer complaints made by Diana Offen (“Offen”) and Mehdi Famili (“Famili”), Occurrence Numbers 1766573 and 2191250 (both related to the same investments purchased by Offen), and 2241177 (related to an investment purchased by Famili), from Petitioner’s record on the Central Registration Depository (“CRD”).  The recommendation is based on the understanding that Petitioner obtains confirmation of the award before the CRD will execute the expungement directive. The petition is unopposed.

 

ANALYSIS 

 

I.          Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award

 

“Any party to an arbitration award in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct, or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1285.) “Regardless of the particular relief granted, any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.” (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.) “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: it may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.” (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.) “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.” (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.) “Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.” (EHM Productions, supra, at p. 1063-64.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4 states, “[a] petition under this chapter shall: (a) Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement. (b) Set forth the names of the arbitrators. (c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.”

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 states,“[t]he neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1283.6.) Additionally, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by filing and serving a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served on the petitioner. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.)

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4 states, in relevant part:

a.       A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.

b.      If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision.

1.      Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.

2.      Service outside this State shall be made by mailing the copy of the petition and notice of hearing and other papers by registered or certified mail. Personal service is the equivalent of such service by mail. Proof of service by mail shall be made by affidavit showing such mailing together with the return receipt of the United States Post Office bearing the signature of the person on whom service was made….

II.        Discussion  

Here, Petitioner has satisfied the procedural requirements to confirm the arbitration award issued in FINRA Case No. 23-02061, Edward C. Yoon v. Mehdi Famili and Diana Offen.

Petitioner provides Famili and Offen’s waivers and submission agreements wherein each agreed to waive service, notice, and participation in the FIRNA arbitration as well as this petition to confirm arbitration award. (Lowery Decl., ¶ 6; Exs. A, B.) Petitioner sets forth the names of the arbitrators: Robert Albini, Mary O’Neal, and Daniel Yasharel (collectively, the “Arbitrators”). (Lowery Decl., ¶ 8.) Petitioner provides a copy of the arbitration award issued by the Arbitrators on February 20, 2024 and a copy of the FINRA Award Service Letter sent on the same day. (Lowery Decl., ¶ 9; Exs. C, D.) The award states, “[a]fter considering the pleadings, the testimony and evidence presented at the expungement hearing, and any post-hearing submissions, the Panel [of Arbitrators] has decided in full and final resolution of the issues submitted for the determination as follows:

…The Panel awards the expungement of all references to Occurrence Numbers 1766573, 2191250, and 2241177 from registration records maintained by the CRD for claimant Edward C. Yoon (CRD Number 2408137) with the understanding that pursuant to Rule 2080, Claimant Edward C. Yoon must obtain confirmation from a court of competent jurisdiction before the CRD will execute the expungement directive.”

(Lowery Decl., ¶¶ 9-10, Ex. C.)

Further, the petition is timely because Petitioner filed the petition on May 28, 2024, which is more than 10 days, but less than four years, after the award was served on February 20, 2024. Lastly, FIRNA waived the requirement that it is named as a party to this petition, and, as noted above, both Famili and Offen waived service, notice and participation for this petition. (Lowery Decl., ¶¶ 6, 12; Exs. A, B, E.)

No oppositions has been filed.

III.       Conclusion 

 

Accordingly, the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award filed by Edward C. Yoon on May 28, 2024 is GRANTED.
