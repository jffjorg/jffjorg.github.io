DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV03857    Hearing Date: May 7, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian

Department 27

Tentative Ruling

 

Hearing Date:           5/7/2024 at 1:30 p.m.

Case No./Name.:      20STCV03857 KAREN WEBB vs CITY OF PASADENA

Motion Name:           MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Moving Party:           Defendant City of Pasadena

Responding Party:    Plaintiff

Notice:                     Sufficient

 

Ruling:                     MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED.

 

Background

 

On January 30, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present dangerous condition of public property case against the City of Pasadena. Plaintiff alleges that while walking along S. Raymond Avenue in Pasadena, she encountered a sidewalk uplift that caused her to trip. As she stumbled forward, Plaintiff stepped into the adjacent planter and tripped again due to the height differential between the planter and the sidewalk, resulting in her falling and sustaining injuries. Defendant now moves the Court for summary judgment, asserting that the sidewalk uplift that caused the initial trip is trivial. Additionally, the height differential of the planter that caused her to trip a second time adheres to approved design standards, thereby granting design immunity. Furthermore, Defendant has inspected the location previously and found no defects; thus, Plaintiff cannot prove that the city had notice of the uplifts.

 

Evidentiary Ruling

 

Defendant’s Objections No. 9 and 10 are sustained on the grounds of lack of foundation and speculation. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that was material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite its allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve its dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

A “defendant moving for summary judgment must show the plaintiff’s causes of action have¿no merit.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849, emphasis added.) In other words, summary judgment is not to be granted simply because a case is “weak” or because a “weak” showing was made in opposition. (Hagen v. Hickenbottom¿(1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 168, 187-188, superseded by statute on another point as recognized in¿Rice v. Clark¿(2002) 28 Cal.4th 89, 96;¿Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc.¿(2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 589, 722. [“to avoid summary judgment a showing need not be strong; it need only be sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact.”].)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).

 

Government Code Section 835

            Government Code section 835 sets out the exclusive conditions under which a public entity is liable for injuries caused by a dangerous condition of public property. (Metcalf v. County of San Joaquin (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1121, 1129, citing Brown v. Poway Unified Sch. Dist. (1993) 4 Cal.4th 820, 829; see also Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112.) Per Government Code section 835, “a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, and that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred.

 

Plaintiff also has to establish the element of notice where the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition . . . a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.” (Gov. Code, § 835.)

 

A “dangerous condition” is a condition of public property that “create[s] a substantial (as distinguished from a minor, trivial or insignificant) risk of injury when such property or adjacent property is used with due care in a manner in which it was reasonably foreseeable that it would be used.” (Gov. Code, § 830.2.)

 

The government entity has actual notice of a dangerous condition “if it had actual knowledge of the existence of the condition and knew or should have known of its dangerous character.” (Govt. Code § 835.2(a).)

 

The entity has constructive notice “if the plaintiff establishes that the condition had existed for such a period of time and was of such an obvious nature that the public entity, in the exercise of due care, should have discovered the condition and its dangerous character.” (Govt. Code § 835.2(b).) Regarding due care, “admissible evidence includes but is not limited to: (1) Whether the existence of the condition and its dangerous character would have been discovered by an inspection system that was reasonably adequate (considering the practicability and cost of inspection weighed against the likelihood and magnitude of the potential danger to which failure to inspect would give rise) to inform the public entity whether the property was safe for the use or uses for which the public entity used or intended others to use the public property and for uses that the public entity actually knew others were making of the public property or adjacent property. (2) Whether the public entity maintained and operated such an inspection system with due care and did not discover the condition.” (Id.)

 

Analysis

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot fulfill the notice requirement because when it conducted a citywide survey, including the location in question, it did not identify any dangerous conditions. In response, Plaintiff points to its expert declaration, arguing that Defendant observed the displacement during the survey but failed to report it.

 

1.  Defendant’s Initial Burden

 

In meeting Defendant's initial burden, Defendant introduces a declaration from Lydia Diaz, a Customer Service Specialist with the Public Works Department. She states that the City maintains a centralized service center called Citizen Service Center (CSC), which assists the public in submitting service requests and reporting injuries and incidents, including uneven sidewalks. (Diaz Decl., ¶¶ 1-2.) Ms. Diaz conducted a search for requests and incidents relating to 700 S. Raymond Avenue, the location where Plaintiff alleges she tripped and fell, and found no prior CSC requests. (Diaz Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.) Additionally, Ms. Diaz searched for any prior trips, injuries, or incidents at 700 S. Raymond Avenue, other than Plaintiff’s incident, and found no reported incidents prior to Plaintiff’s incident on July 27, 2019, up to the present date. (Diaz Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.)

 

Furthermore, Defendant introduces the deposition of Brent Mau, PMQ for the City of Pasadena, who testified that from 2014 to 2015, the City undertook a citywide sidewalk survey, which covered the location of Plaintiff’s fall. The purpose of the survey was to identify damaged sidewalk locations. (Deposition of Brent Mau, 31:9-25; 33:2-20 (Exhibit H to Retchless Declaration).) The location where Plaintiff allegedly fell, 700 S. Raymond Avenue, was not marked as damaged in the 2014/2015 citywide sidewalk survey. (Deposition of Brent Mau, 31:9-25; 33:2-20 (Exhibit H to Retchless Declaration).) Defendant has met its initial burden of demonstrating that the city did not receive notice of the alleged dangerous conditions, including both the uplifts in the planter box and the sidewalk uplift.

 

2.  Plaintiff’s Burden to Raise Triable Issue of Fact

 

In raising a triable issue of fact, Plaintiff argues that when Defendant conducted a citywide survey in 2014/2015, the city should have observed the displacement that was over ¾ inches but failed to report it. (Declaration of Mark Burns ¶ 15.) The sole piece of evidence provided to support Plaintiff’s expert’s claim is a Google Map image of the location at issue from 2016. (Burns Decl. ¶16 and Exhibit 13). Plaintiff alleges the photo shows an uplift/dangerous condition at the location in question in 2016. (Exhibit 13.) The Court examined the photo and found the image quality to be poor, noting that it is a zoomed-in image from Google Maps. The photo, presumably taken from the Google Maps car, is captured from a distance and does not provide a close-up shot of the displacement. It lacks any tape measure or object next to the displacement for scale. A significant portion of the alleged displacement is obscured by a red van, and the most crucial part of the alleged displacement appears even more blurred in comparison to the rest of the photograph. From the photo, it is impossible to discern whether an uplift exists, let alone if the size of the displacement exceeds 1 inch, a threshold where courts have typically found displacements to be non-trivial.

Plaintiff’s expert asserts that because the tree in the 2016 photo is similar in size to the tree in the 2014 and 2015 Google Maps photos, the size of the uplift when Defendant conducted the survey in 2014 and 2015 must have been the same as in 2016, thereby giving the city actual notice of the uplift. However, the Google images are not properly authenticated and are therefore inadmissible. In any event, Plaintiff’s expert testimony is based on a series of conjectures and speculations. First, the 2016 photo does not show an uplift exceeding 1 inch or an uplift at all. Second, the claim that the size of the uplift in 2016 is the same as in 2014 is speculation based solely on the size of the tree from Google Map Images. When an expert bases a conclusion on assumptions not supported by the record, matters not reasonably relied upon, or factors which are speculative, remote or conjectural, then the conclusion has no evidentiary value for purposes of a summary judgment motion or opposition. (Lockheed Litigation Cases (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 558, 563.) This is certainly the case here.

The 2016 image is said to show displacement of the planter box where Plaintiff fell. Plaintiff alleges she tripped on the sidewalk displacement and then stumbled forward approximately 8-11 feet before tripping and falling over another displacement at the other end of the planter box. However, that location where Plaintiff tripped and fell is obscured by a RV and is not visible from the photograph provided. Further, Plaintiff’s expert testimony is limited only to Defendant's actual notice of the sidewalk displacement, not the planter box displacement.

Based on the evidence provided by Plaintiff, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact that Defendant had actual notice of the displacements at issue.

As for constructive notice, Plaintiff must demonstrate the long-continued existence of the dangerous condition. Plaintiff cites to the same expert witness testimony and Google Map images (Burns Decl. and Exhibit 13), claiming they show evidence of uplift since 2015. However, as previously stated, the Google Map Images lack foundation.  Further, the Google Map image does not show an uplift exceeding 1 inch or an uplift at all; the area of the planter box where Plaintiff allegedly fell is obscured by a truck.

For both constructive and actual notice, Plaintiff needs to raise a triable issue of fact that the displacements had been in existence long before the incident or in 2014/2015 when the survey was conducted. Plaintiff attempted to do so with the 2016 image and the expert witness testimony based on that image; however, for reasons noted above, Plaintiff’s efforts are insufficient.

Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact as to the element of notice. Thus, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Hearing Date:           5/7/2024 at 1:30 p.m.

Case No./Name:       22STCV17013 CARLOS QUEZADA vs JAMIE LOPEZ, et al.

Motion:                    Demurrer

Moving Party:           Defendant Zeno King Jr.

Responding Party:    Plaintiff

Notice:                      Sufficient

 

Ruling:                    DEMURRER IS OVERRULED

 

 

Background

 

On May 24, 2022, Plaintiff Carlos Quezada filed the present case regarding an automobile accident that occurred on June 25, 2021. Case 22STCV18295 is a related case arising out of the same accident. Plaintiff Quezada is a defendant in the related case.

On October 28, 2022, a Doe amendment naming Zeno King, Jr. as a defendant was filed in the related case. On June 12, 2023, Zeno King, Jr. was served with the Doe Amendment in the related case. On November 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Doe amendment naming Zeno King, Jr. as a defendant in the present case. Defendant Zeno King, Jr. now demurs to Plaintiff’s complaint on the basis that he was brought into this case on November 3, 2023, two years after the statutory deadline.

Legal Standard

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Defendant asserts the Doe Amendment is barred by the applicable two-year statute of limitations. However, given that the complaint was filed within the statute of limitations and named Doe defendants, the issue here is not whether the action against Defendant is barred by the statute of limitations. (See McOwen v. Grossman (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 937, 943.) Rather, the issue is whether Plaintiff has met the requirements for amending the complaint to name Defendant as a responsible party. (See id.)  

 

CCP section 474 “allows a plaintiff in good faith to delay suing particular persons as named defendants until he has knowledge of sufficient facts to cause a reasonable person to believe liability is probable.” (McOwen, supra, 153 Cal.App.4th at 943.) Whether the plaintiff had knowledge of sufficient facts at the time of filing is generally a question of fact. (See id. at 945.)  

 

“Section 474 provides a method for adding defendants after the statute of limitations has expired, but this procedure is available only when the plaintiff is actually ignorant of the facts establishing a cause of action against the party to be substituted for a Doe defendant.” (McClatchy v. Coblentz, Patch, Duffy & Bass, LLP (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 371-72.) “‘Ignorance of the facts giving rise to a cause of action is the ignorance required by section 474, and the pivotal question is, ‘did plaintiff know facts?’ not ‘did plaintiff know or believe that [he] had a cause of action based on those facts?’” (Id. at 372 (quoting General Motors Corp. v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 580, 594).) “‘Although it is true that a plaintiff’s ignorance of the defendant’s name must be genuine (in good faith) and not feigned [citations] and that a plaintiff need not be aware of each and every detail concerning a person’s involvement before the plaintiff loses his ignorance [citations], it is equally true that the plaintiff does not relinquish [his] rights under section 474 simply because [he] has a suspicion of wrongdoing arising from one or more facts [he] does know.’” (Id. (quoting General Motors, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at 594-95).) “The fact that the plaintiff had the means to obtain knowledge is irrelevant.” (General Motors, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at 594.) “In short, section 474 does not impose upon the plaintiff a duty to go in search of facts she does not actually have at the time she files her original pleading.” (Id. at 596.) “A plaintiff may use section 474 whenever he has no actual knowledge of the defendant; constructive or legal knowledge will not deprive the plaintiff of the remedy.” (Sobeck & Associates, Inc. v. B&R Investments No. 24 (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 861, 867; see also Balon v. Drost (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 483, 488 (quoting Sobeck & Associates, Inc., supra, 215 Cal.App.3d at 867).)

 

Analysis

King, Jr. contends that the Doe amendment naming him as a defendant in a related case on October 28, 2022, along with the subsequent service of this amendment on June 12, 2023, demonstrates Plaintiff’s prior knowledge of King Jr.'s identity and involvement in the incident. Consequently, King Jr. contends Plaintiff’s filing of King Jr.’s Doe amendment in the present case on November 2, 2023, is not made in good faith.

In the opposition, Plaintiff argues that he only learned of King Jr.'s involvement on October 27, 2023, when he received discovery responses from King Jr., including a police report that identified Zeno King Jr. as an involved party. On November 2, 2023, shortly after receiving the police report, Plaintiff filed a Doe Amendment naming Zeno King Jr. as a Defendant in this action.

Defendant’s evidence, including the Complaint, Doe amendment and service of the Doe amendment in the related case (Exhibit A, Exhibit E and Exhibit F) are devoid of factual details concerning Defendant Zeno King Jr.'s involvement in the accident. Thus, without providing any additional evidence, Defendant failed to demonstrate that Plaintiff had knowledge of sufficient facts that would lead a reasonable person to believe King Jr.’s liability is probable, as required by McOwen v. Grossman (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 937, 943.

Furthermore, as to Defendant’s allegation of prejudice, the type of prejudice the court should be concerned with when amending the complaint is something beyond simply having to cope with a potentially successful new legal theory of recovery that has been revealed during discovery. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486.) As noted above, Plaintiff amended the complaint soon after acquiring the police report showing Defendant’s involvement in the incident and was not tardy in seeking amendment.

 

Thus, Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

Case No./Name:       22STCV25326 HOUSSEM ZIDI vs SANFORD SIGAL et al.

Ruling:                    MOTION TO STRIKE IS GRANTED

 

Background

 

On August 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a premises liability case against Defendants Sigal Family Trust, Allison Binder, and Diamante Electric & Construction, alleging that Defendants were performing construction at the front of the property which was left open with large trenches without any protective coverings. Plaintiff fell into the construction area while delivering food to the Defendants. On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the second amended complaint seeking punitive damages. Defendants Sigal Family Trust and Allison Binder now move the court to strike the allegations relating to punitive damages in the SAC, specifically the following allegations:

 

1. Page 3, Line 16-17: “willfully, maliciously, or with conscious disregard”

2. Page 3, Lines 18-20: “In a premises liability case like this one, the standard for punitive damages is ‘with conscious disregard of the safety of others,’ which is a subcategory of malice.”

Legal Standard

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civil Code section 3294 (c)(1).) Under the statute, malice does not require actual intent to harm. Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.) ‘Despicable’ is a powerful term that refers to circumstances that are ‘base,’ ‘vile,’ or ‘contemptible.’ (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)

A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿(Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)

Analysis

In this premises liability/negligence case, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant failed to ensure the safety of the construction area in front of their property, resulting in Plaintiff sustaining injuries while delivering food to Defendants. As for punitive damages, the complaint primarily consists of legal conclusions rather than the specific facts required to substantiate claims for punitive damages.

While portions of the complaint do allege facts with specificity, they do not rise to the level of willful and despicable conduct necessary for punitive damages. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants warned their family members of the danger but failed to warn Plaintiff. Defendant Binder instructed Plaintiff to deliver food to the front door via a walkway where open and hazardous trenches were located. It is further alleged that Defendant Binder directed Plaintiff to approach closer to the house to deliver the food, specifically to the front door area where the dangerous trench was located. This required Plaintiff to navigate over and between the open trenches to deliver the food. Defendant Binder directed Plaintiff to walk into an area without warning of the dangerous open trench containing  lightbulbs and equipment inside, whereas she had instructed friends and family members to avoid that area because it was so dangerous. (SAC at pg. 5)

In Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89, the court differentiates between risks that are foreseeable and those that are necessarily probable, holding that the risk must be necessarily probable for the court to find the conduct or the lack thereof to be willful. In this case, the allegations need to establish that the risk was not merely foreseeable but necessarily probable to support claims for punitive damages. Based on specific facts alleged, the risk associated with an open trench is certainly foreseeable, but it is not necessarily probable that an open trench with lightbulb and equipment inside located along the property’s entrance would pose a risk of harm.

Moreover, the facts alleged simply do not rise to the level of “despicable conduct.”  The Court can fairly presume that Plaintiff had no meritorious argument to the contrary given Plaintiff’s failure to oppose the Motion. 

Thus, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED. Leave to amend is denied because this is Plaintiff’s second amended complaint. Based on the allegations as stated in the SAC, it does not appear that Plaintiff, with reasonable probability, can allege facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. However, if during the discovery process, Plaintiff acquires sufficient information to substantiate a claim for punitive damages, the Court will consider Plaintiff’s motion to amend the complaint.

Responding Party:    Unopposed

Ruling:                    MOTION FOR LEAVE TO TAKE DEPOSITION OF PRISONER SYMONE GALLEGOS IS GRANTED

Background

 

On April 13, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the current case against Defendants Jiffy Lube International, Inc. and Alamitos Enterprises, LLC alleging that Symone Gallegos, an employee of Defendant XXX, was drinking and driving within the scope of her employment when she crashed into the plaintiffs, resulting in the death of one passenger. Defendants now moves the court for leave to depose Symone Gallegos to prepare their defense that she was not acting within the scope of her employment at the time of the incident. No opposition to this motion has been filed.

 

Legal Standard and Analysis

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1995, “[i]f the witness be a prisoner, confined in a jail within this state, an order for his examination in the jail upon deposition, or for his temporary removal and production before a court or officer may be made as follows:

 

1.  By the court itself in which the action or special proceeding is pending, unless it be a small claims court.

2.  By a justice of the Supreme Court, or a judge of the superior court of the county where the action or proceeding is pending, if pending before a small claims court, or before a judge or other person out of court.” (CCP § 1995.)

 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1996 provides that a court may order the deposition of a prisoner upon a motion supported by an ‘affidavit showing ... the testimony expected from the witness, and its materiality.’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1996.)” (Cadiz Land Co., Inc. v. Rail Cycle, L.P. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 74, 121.)

 

Defendants have filed an affidavit indicating their intention to elicit testimony regarding Prisoner Symone Gallegos' alcohol consumption and whether she was acting within the scope of employment at the time of the accident to prepare their defense. This information is material and Defendants have met all statutory requirements to compel a deposition. Consequently, the present motion is GRANTED and Symone Gallegos is ordered to sit for Defendants’ deposition within 30 days of today.

HON. LEE S. ARIAN

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Hearing Date:           5/7/2024 at 1:30 p.m.

Case No./Name:       23STCV10816 RAMIRO GUTIERREZ AYALA vs JUN LIANG

Motion:                    MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Moving Party:           Plaintiff

Responding Party:    Defendant Jun Liang

Notice:                      Sufficient

 

Ruling:                    MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

Background

On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present auto accident case. On October 10, 2023, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Defendant Jun Liang. Defendant served verified responses on December 7, 2023. The parties have met and conferred and attended an IDC but were unsuccessful in resolving the discovery dispute. Consequently, on January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, specifically Nos. 7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 13.1, 13.2, 15.1, 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, 16.5, 16.6, 16.7, 16.9, and 16.10.

Preliminary Issue

This motion was timely filed within 45 days of Defendant’s discovery responses. Plaintiff has met his meet and confer requirement and the parties have attended an IDC prior to this hearing. On April 19, 2024, Defendant served further responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 13.1, 13.2, 15.1, 16.2, 16.3, 16.4, 16.5, 16.6, and 16.7. Currently, only Form Interrogatories Nos. 7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 16.9, and 16.10 remain at issue.

Form Interrogatories Nos. 7.1, 7.2 And 7.3

Form Interrogatory 7.1 states “Do you attribute any loss of or damage to a vehicle or other property to the INCIDENT? If so, for each item of property…”

Form Interrogatory 7.2 states “Has a written estimate or evaluation been made for any item of property referred to in your answer to the preceding interrogatory? If so If so, for each estimate or evaluation state…”

Form Interrogatory 7.3 “states Has any item of property referred to in your answer to interrogatory 7.1 been repaired? If so, for each item state…”

Defendant responded "NO" to Interrogatory No. 7.1, indicating that he does not attribute any loss of or damage to a vehicle or other property to the incident. Defendant answered "not applicable" to Interrogatories Nos. 7.2 and 7.3, which request information relating to estimates and repairs for the damage claimed in No. 7.1. Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses by introducing purportedly contradictory evidence suggesting that Defendant did attribute damage to his vehicle to the incident.

Form Interrogatory 7.1 requires a yes or no response. Defendant answered “No” and this response is responsive, complete, and non-evasive. If Plaintiff believes that Defendant’s deposition contradicts this response, Plaintiff can use it to impeach Defendant or as grounds for sanctions. However, as stated in Holguin v. Superior Court (Hoage) (1972) 22 CA3d 812, 820, "[A] court [cannot] force a litigant to admit any particular fact if he is willing to risk a perjury prosecution or financial sanctions by denying them." Thus, Defendant’s response to No. 7.1 is code complaint and the Court has no grounds to compel defendant to answer “Yes” when they have provided verified responses stating “No”.

Interrogatories Nos. 7.2 and 7.3 only need to be answered if Defendant had answered "Yes" to 7.1. Since Defendant answered "No" to 7.1, his response that Nos. 7.2 and 7.3 are not applicable is appropriate.

Form Interrogatories Nos.16.9 and 16.10

Form Interrogatory No. 16.9 states “Do YOU OR ANYONE ACTING ON YOUR BEHALF have any DOCUMENT (for example, insurance bureau index reports) concerning claims for personal injuries made before or after the INCIDENT by a plaintiff in this case? If so, for each plaintiff state….”

Form Interrogatory No. 16.10 states “Do YOU OR ANYONE ACTING ON YOUR BEHALF have any DOCUMENT concerning the past or present physical, mental, or emotional condition of any plaintiff in this case from a HEALTH CARE PROVIDER not previously identified (except for expert witnesses covered by Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.210-2034.310)? If so, for each plaintiff state…”

To both Form Interrogatories Nos. 16.9 and 16.10, which elicit a yes or no response, Defendant answered "No." These responses are therefore code compliant. Again, if Plaintiff suspects that Defendant’s responses are untruthful, they can be used to impeach Defendant. As established in Holguin v. Superior Court (Hoage) (1972) 22 CA3d 812, 820, "[A] court [cannot] force a litigant to admit any particular fact if he is willing to risk a perjury prosecution or financial sanctions by denying them." Thus, there is no basis for compelling further responses to these interrogatories.

Conclusion

Plaintiff's motion to compel further responses is denied. Since the motion to compel further is denied, sanctions are likewise denied, as discovery responses allegedly contradicted by Defendant's testimony are not proper grounds for a motion to compel further.
