DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV11519    Hearing Date: May 30, 2024    Dept: 73

5/30/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

WHITFIELD v. MUSIC ROYALTY CONSULTING, INC. (22STCV11519)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Ayo Omotosho (Johnson Omotosho, LLP)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Kevin J Cole (KJC Law Group, APC)

 

Defendant’s motion for TERMINATING OR EVIDENTIARY SANCTIONS AGAINST PLAINTIFF (filed on 03/29/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s Motion for Terminating or Evidentiary Sanctions against Plaintiff is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Roland Whitfield brings this contractual fraud action against Defendants Music Royalty Consulting, Inc., Parviz Omidvar, Oliver Omidvar, and O’Neal Omidvar. Plaintiff’s first amended complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) anticipatory breach of contact; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) fraud in the inducement; (4) specific performance; and (5) declaratory judgment.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following: Defendant Music Royalty Consulting, Inc. (“MRCI”) is 100% owned by Defendant Parviz Omidvar. (FAC, ¶ 3.) Parviz’s two adult sons, Defendants Oliver Omidvar and O’Neal Omidvar, were agents or employees of MRCI. (Ibid.) As heir to the late Billie Rae Calvin (“Songwriter”), Plaintiff had rights to Songwriter’s works and was entitled to the same royalty payments Songwriter received. (Id., ¶¶ 6, 9.) On July 8, 2011, Plaintiff executed an Irrevocable Royalty Purchase Agreement (“Royalty Agreement”) with MRCI, where MRCI paid Plaintiff $250,000.00 for the rights to Songwriter’s works. (Id., ¶ 10.) The Royalty Agreement provided Plaintiff with the option of repurchasing the rights to Songwriter’s works for $1.00, effective 15 years from the date of the Agreement. (Id., ¶ 11.) However, MRCI added a term to the Agreement which purportedly gave MRCI the right to purchase Plaintiff’s buy-back option at any time for $50,000. (Id., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff intended to execute the $1.00 buy-back option within the period specified by the contract. (Id., ¶ 24.) But, on August 17, 2021, MRCI sent Plaintiff a letter and $50,000 check attempting to exercise their right to purchase Plaintiff’s option—and thus the rights to Songwriter’s works in perpetuity. (Id., ¶ 16.)

 

On March 29, 2024, Defendant Music Royalty Consulting, Inc. filed this Motion for Terminating or Evidentiary Sanctions against Plaintiff, due to:

·       The now-uncontroverted record evidence, following Plaintiff’s March 20, 2024 deposition, that Plaintiff has been on notice of these (alleged) claims since at least the end of 2011 (far outside the statute of limitations), and even consulted with an attorney.

o   Plaintiff’s testimony at his deposition—that he discovered Parviz Omidvar (Defendant’s founder) allegedly stole from him in the “fall of 2011,” at which point Plaintiff hired an attorney (in 2011)—entirely confirms that Plaintiff has been on notice of these (alleged) claims since at least the end of 2011 (far outside the statute of limitations).

·       Plaintiff’s own testimonial admission that he lacks the capacity to pursue these claims

o   About thirty minutes into his deposition, Plaintiff refused to answer a question about why Plaintiff is not presently employed, justifying his refusal by declaring, “I’m not all there, you dope . . . I was born defected, weirdo.” (Ex. A at 21:3–12.)

·       Plaintiff’s refusal to give further deposition testimony, with trial-related and dispositive motions deadlines fast approaching

o   Plaintiff abruptly ended his deposition after only thirty minutes

o   When Defendant’s counsel asked how soon Plaintiff could sit for another deposition—even offering to rearrange his schedule over the next few days (including over the weekend) to accommodate Plaintiff—Plaintiff’s counsel suggested that Plaintiff might never be available

·       Thus, Defendant requests an order dismissing the case with prejudice, or alternatively, Plaintiff is precluded from testifying at trial or otherwise introducing any evidence in his case in chief.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

The Court may issue a terminating sanction against a party who engages in the misuse of the discovery process by one of the following orders: “(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process; (2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; (4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (d).) 

 

Misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery; making an evasive discovery response, and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(d), (f), (g).) The trial court may terminate a party’s action as a sanction for discovery abuse “after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.” (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)¿ Terminating sanctions should be used sparingly and the trial court should attempt less severe alternatives unless the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective to induce the offending party’s compliance. (R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal. App. 4th 486, 496 (finding terminating sanctions proper where plaintiffs violated two discovery orders).) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Defendant moves for terminating or evidentiary sanctions against Plaintiff and seeks an order dismissing the case with prejudice, or alternatively, Plaintiff is precluded from testifying at trial or otherwise introducing any evidence in his case in chief.

 

Defendant asserts that terminating or evidentiary sanctions are warranted following Plaintiff’s March 20, 2024 deposition due to: (1) Plaintiff’s admission at his deposition that he has been on notice of these claims since at least the end of 2011 (which is outside the statute of limitations), and even consulted with an attorney, (2) Plaintiff’s testimonial admission that he lacks the capacity to pursue these claims, (3) Plaintiff having abruptly ended his deposition after only thirty minutes, and (4) Plaintiff’s refusal to give further deposition testimony, with trial-related and dispositive motions deadlines fast approaching.

 

In balancing Plaintiff’s conduct against the harm to Defendant, the Court finds that imposition of terminating sanctions is not appropriate at this time.

 

Defendant has not presented evidence that it attempted to depose Plaintiff again after the March 20, 2024 deposition. There is also no evidence that Defendant filed a motion to compel further deposition testimony, that Defendant requested an informal discovery conference, or that Defendant met and conferred prior to filing this motion. Thus, there is no indication at this time that Plaintiff repeatedly failed to respond to Defendant’s discovery requests such that he has misused the discovery process. Defendant cites to Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1618 in support of its contention that courts may impose terminating sanctions as a first penalty, without a prior court order, where lesser sanctions would fail to cure the prejudice. However in Collisson, the plaintiff first made “many attempts, both formal and informal” to receive discovery responses from defendant before it filed for sanctions. (Ibid.) On the other hand here, Defendant has not made further attempts to depose Plaintiff before filing this motion. As the Court stated in Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279, “A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” Thus, on this basis, the Court does not grant Defendant’s request for terminating or evidentiary sanctions.

 

The Court also notes that it does not find Defendant’s arguments regarding Plaintiff’s admissions during his deposition as to when he was on notice of the alleged claims and his comment “I’m not all there, you dope . . . I was born defected, weirdo” as warranting terminating or evidentiary sanctions. (Def’s Ex. A at 21:3–12.) The Court may issue terminating or evidentiary sanctions against a party who misuses the discovery process, which includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery; making an evasive discovery response, and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(d), (f), (g).) As discussed above, the record does not indicate at this time that there has been a pattern of Plaintiff repeatedly failing to respond to Defendant’s discovery requests such that he has misused the discovery process.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the Motion. 

 

 II. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s Motion for Terminating or Evidentiary Sanctions against Plaintiff is DENIED.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

Motions for summary adjudication are procedurally identical to motions for summary judgment. (Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 859.) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code of Civ. Proc., §¿437c,¿subd. (f)(1).)¿

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

a.     Summary Adjudication as to Issue No. 1: The District Is Not Liable For Negligence of Its Employees Where Harm to Plaintiff Was Not Foreseeable Based on Any Actual or Constructive Knowledge Of His Conduct or Propensity for Sexual Assault of Students

 

“Whether a defendant owes a duty of care to a plaintiff is primarily a question of law to be decided on a case-by-case basis.” (Bartell v. Palos Verdes Peninsula Sch. Dist. (1978) 83 Cal.App.3d 492, 498, citing Weirum v. RKO General, Inc. (1975) 15 Cal.3d 40, 46.) “As a general principle, a ‘defendant owes a duty of care to all persons who are foreseeably endangered by his conduct, with respect to all risks which make the conduct unreasonably dangerous.’” (Bartell, supra, 83 Cal.App.3d at p. 498, citing Tarasoff v. Regents of University of California (1976) 17 Cal.3d 425, 434-435.) “However, absent some special relationship, a defendant falls under no duty to control the conduct of another, and incurs no liability to those endangered by such conduct.” (Bartell, supra, 83 Cal.App.3d at p. 498, citing DeSuza v. Andersack (1976) 63 Cal.App.3d 694, 702-703.)

 

“While school districts and their employees have never been considered insurers of the physical safety of students, California law has long imposed on school authorities a duty to ‘supervise at all times the conduct of the children on the school grounds and to enforce those rules and regulations necessary to their protection. [Citations.]’ [Citations.] The standard of care imposed upon school personnel in carrying out this duty to supervise is identical to that required in the performance of their other duties. This uniform standard to which they are held is that degree of care ‘which a person of ordinary prudence, charged with [comparable] duties, would exercise under the same circumstances.’ [Citations.] Either a total lack of supervision [citation] or ineffective supervision [citation] may constitute a lack of ordinary care on the part of those responsible for student supervision. Under section 815.2, subdivision (a) of the Government Code, a school district is vicariously liable for injuries proximately caused by such negligence.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart, 53 Cal.4th 861, 869; Hoff v. Vacaville Unified School Dist. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 925, 932–933; Hoyem v. Manhattan Beach City Sch. Dist. (1978) 22 Cal.3d 508, 513.)¿

 

“In addition, a school district and its employees have a special relationship with the district’s pupils, a relationship arising from the mandatory character of school attendance and the comprehensive control over students exercised by school personnel, “analogous in many ways to the relationship between parents and their children.” (Hoff v. Vacaville Unified School Dist., supra, 19 Cal.4th at p. 935; see M.W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 508, 517; Leger v. Stockton Unified School Dist., supra, 202 Cal.App.3d at pp. 1458–1459.) Because of this special relationship, imposing obligations beyond what each person generally owes others under Civil Code section 1714, the duty of care owed by school personnel includes the duty to use reasonable measures to protect students from foreseeable injury at the hands of third parties acting negligently or intentionally. [Footnote omitted.]” (C.A. v. William S. Hart, 53 Cal.4th 861, 870.) 

 

As an initial matter, LAUSD does not appear to dispute that it owed Plaintiff a duty of care. Instead, LAUSD argues it is not liable for negligence of its employees where harm to Plaintiff was not foreseeable based on any actual or constructive knowledge of Giswein’s conduct or propensity for sexual assault of students.

 

                                                    i.     Actual Knowledge

 

First, LAUSD argues Plaintiff alleges in conclusory terms that the school had actual knowledge that Giswein posed a risk of sexual assault to Plaintiff and other students, but Plaintiff lacks any evidence to support those allegations.

 

However, as the court in Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 131 (“Lawndale”) established, actual knowledge is not required. In Lawndale, the defendant school district asserted that it did not have a duty to protect the plaintiff from sexual abuse unless it knew that the perpetrator/sexual abuser/employee had previously engaged in sexual misconduct with minors or had a propensity to do so. Yet, the Lawndale court held that “consistent with California negligence law. . . school administrators have a duty to protect students from sexual abuse by school employees, even if the school does not have actual knowledge of a particular employee’s history of committing, or propensity to commit, such abuse.” (Lawndale, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at 119.)

 

Therefore, the Court does not find LAUSD’s argument persuasive. Even if LAUSD did not have actual knowledge of Giswein’s conduct or propensity for sexual assault of students, LAUSD can still be held liable.

 

                                                   ii.     Constructive Knowledge/Foreseeability

 

Next, LAUSD argues that it cannot be liable without having had some reason to know or suspect wrongful conduct.

 

“Once a court determines a defendant owes a duty to a plaintiff, “the remaining liability questions—breach as well as factual and legal causation—are usually questions for the jury.” (Lawndale, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at 126-127.) Nevertheless, “‘[e]ven if an organization has a special relationship with the tortfeasor or plaintiff, “[t]he court may depart from the general rule of duty ... if other policy considerations clearly require an exception. (Ibid.)

 

“In¿Brown¿the Supreme Court established a two-step inquiry to determine whether a defendant has a legal duty to take action to protect a plaintiff from injuries caused by a third party: ‘First, the court must determine whether there exists a special relationship between the parties or some other set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty to protect. Second, if so, the court must consult the factors described in [Rowland v. Christian¿(1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, (Rowland)] to determine whether relevant policy considerations counsel limiting that duty.’ ”  (Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles¿(2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 657, 670 [quoting Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p.209.) 

 

The¿Rowland¿factors are “ ‘the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered, the moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct, the policy of preventing future harm, the extent of the burden to the defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for¿breach, and the availability, cost, and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.’ ” (Regents, supra, 4 Cal.5th at p. 628, 230 Cal.Rptr.3d 415, 413 P.3d 656, quoting¿Rowland, supra, 69 Cal.2d at p. 113, 70; see¿Brown,¿supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 217.) “The¿Rowland¿factors fall into two categories. The first group involves foreseeability and the related concepts of certainty and the connection between plaintiff and defendant. The second embraces the public policy concerns¿of moral blame, preventing future harm, burden, and insurance availability.” (Regents,¿at p. 629; see¿Brown v. USA Taekwondo,¿supra, 40 Cal.App.5th at p. 1096.) “ ‘The most important factor to consider in determining whether to create an exception to the general duty to exercise ordinary care ... is whether the injury in question was¿foreseeable.’ ” (Regents, at p. 629, quoting¿Kesner v. Superior Court¿(2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1145, (Kesner).) 

 

(Roman, supra, 70 Cal.App.5th at pp.673–674.) 

 

Here, LAUSD does not address each of the Rowland factors, but appears to argue in its motion that Plaintiff’s injury was not foreseeable. LAUSD argues Plaintiff cannot provide admissible evidence of any facts or behavior by Giswein or any other District employees, that would have signaled to LAUSD or its employees to take any particular precautions to address sexual assaults by Giswein or any other employee in this period of time.

 

However, regarding foreseeability, the Lawndale court stated the issue “is not whether it is foreseeable a particular adult will sexually abuse a student if left alone with the student…  the issue is whether it is reasonably foreseeable that organizations or entities that provide services primarily or exclusively for children have employees who may sexually abuse a child if the organization fails to take reasonable measures to prevent the abuse. In any event, to the extent John R. suggests sexual abuse of students by school employees is not reasonably foreseeable, it is inconsistent with the Supreme Court's more recent holding in [C.A.] that school personnel owe students a duty to take reasonable measures to protect them from foreseeable injury, including “injuries to a student resulting from a teacher's sexual assault.” [Citation.] .” (Lawndale, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at 132.) The Lawndale court further stated “sexual abuse by members “of an organization that provide[s] activities exclusively for children —like an elementary school district—is reasonably foreseeable, even where the organization had no knowledge that [the employee] had previously sexually or physically abused anyone or had a propensity to do so.” (Ibid.) Thus, based on the court’s reasoning in Lawndale, the Court finds it is similarly reasonably foreseeable that if LAUSD failed to take reasonable measures to prevent childhood sexual abuse of its minor students enrolled at its school, one of its employees may sexually abuse a minor student.

 

LAUSD has not carried its initial burden. Accordingly, the Court DENIES summary adjudication as to issue no. 1.

 

b.     Summary Adjudication as to Issue No. 2: Plaintiff Cannot Establish the Elements of the First and Third Causes of Action Because He Cannot Provide Evidence Any District Employee Actually Was Negligent With Respect to Hiring, Training or Supervision of Giswein

 

LAUSD argues Plaintiff has absolutely no evidence of any negligence in hiring, training, or supervising Giswein at El Sereno by any District supervisory or administrative employee.

 

The law is well established that “a public school district may be vicariously liable under [Government Code] section 815.2 for the negligence of administrators or supervisors in hiring, supervising and retaining a school employee who sexually harasses and abuses a student.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union School Dist. (“C.A. v. William S. Hart”) (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 879.) 

 

LAUSD asserts Plaintiff cannot establish that any LAUSD employee was negligent in hiring, training, or supervising Giswein. The Court notes that LAUSD does not present any evidence in support of its argument. For example, LAUSD does not provide any evidence of LAUSD’s due diligence prior to hiring Giswein, and that its due diligence failed to uncover any concerns about Giswein. LAUSD also asserts that many of the incidents of sexual assault Plaintiff describes, occurring in Giswein’s apartment or while Plaintiff was travelling with Giswein, did not occur on school premises and could not have been known to or observed by any school employee. However, LAUSD seems to ignore the undisputed material facts that Rudy Jimenez, a teacher at Defendant’s El Sereno Junior High School, knew about Giswein’s frequent off-campus trips with Plaintiff, that Jimenez attended some of these off-campus trips, and that Plaintiff’s pre-lunch classroom teacher would receive the hall passes and approve Plaintiff to go to Giswein’s office. (UMF 5, 12, 13, 25.)

 

Further, courts have held that whether a defendant breached its duty of care, and whether the breach caused or contributed to the plaintiff’s injuries are fact specific issues, appropriate for the trier of fact. (Hoyem, supra, 22 Cal.3d 508, 513.) The Supreme Court has explained: “the issues of whether or not the school acted negligently in failing to provide adequate supervision,” and “if so, whether the student’s injuries were proximately caused by such negligence, [fall] within the province of the jury.” (Ibid.) “The question of actual causation [that is, whether or not more diligent supervision would have prevented the accident] is essentially a factual determination for the jury.” (Id. at p. 520.) Here, LAUSD has not shown that the issue of whether or not the school acted negligently in failing to provide adequate supervision should be adjudicated at this stage.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds LAUSD has not carried its initial burden. The burden does not shift to Plaintiff. Thus, the Court DENIES summary adjudication as to issue no. 2.

 

Accordingly, LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s first and third causes of action are DENIED.

 

D.     Second and Fourth Causes of Action: Failure to Report Suspected Child Abuse and Negligence

 

LAUSD also moves for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second and fourth causes of action.

 

However, Plaintiff filed a request for dismissal as to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action against all Defendants on or about August 14, 2023. Thus, the Court finds LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to the Second Cause of Action is moot.

 

In addition, Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for negligence is not directed or alleged against Defendant LAUSD. Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is solely alleged against unnamed Defendant Does 41 through 50, only. As such, the Court also finds LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to the Fourth Cause of Action is moot.

 

LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second and fourth causes of action are MOOT.

 

 II. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s first and third causes of action are DENIED.

 

Defendant LAUSD’s motion for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second and fourth causes of action are MOOT.

JANET ANGLE v. SDLA COURIER SERVICE, INC. (23STCV05341) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Samvel Setyan (Setyan Law, APC) 

Counsel for Defendant:  None

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 4/15/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 10, 2023, Plaintiff Janet Angle filed this employment action against Defendant SDLA Courier Service, Inc. alleging the following causes of action:

1.     Discrimination in violation of Government Code Section 12940 et seq.

2.     Retaliation in violation of Government Code Section 12940 et seq.

3.     Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment and Retaliation in violation of Government Code Section 12940(k)

4.     Retaliation in violation of Government Code Section 12945.2

5.     Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodations in violation of Government Code Section 12940 et seq.

6.     Failure to Engage in Good Faith Interactive Process violation of Government Code Section 12940 et seq.

7.     Declaratory Judgment

8.     Wrongful Termination in violation of Public Policy

9.     Failure to Permit Inspection of Personnel and Payroll Records (Labor Code Section 1198.5)

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about October 1, 2021, Plaintiff was hired by Defendant to work as a delivery driver. (Compl., ¶ 16.) On April 25, 2022, Plaintiff’s vehicle was rear-ended while she was making deliveries. (Compl., ¶ 17.) As a result of the accident, Plaintiff injured her back. (Compl., ¶ 17.) Plaintiff notified her employers and was placed on medical leave till approximately the first week of July 2022. (Compl., ¶ 18.) Plaintiff alleges Defendant refused to accommodate Plaintiff’s restrictions and Plaintiff was not provided any assistance with lifting packages. (Compl., ¶ 18.) Defendant terminated Plaintiff on July 29, 2022. (Compl., ¶ 19.) Plaintiff seeks general and special damages; specifically loss of earnings and emotional distress, punitive damages, attorneys’ fees, and costs for these claims.

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default judgment.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.     Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.     Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                      Yes 

2.     Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                    Yes 

3.     Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                     Yes

JC Form CIV-100 must be used to request court judgment. On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff submitted her JC Form CIV-100. However, Plaintiff failed to fill out item number two of the form identifying her damages and the total amount that Plaintiff is seeking. Plaintiff also failed to fill out item number seven. Thus, Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment form is incomplete.

 

On November 29, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel, Samvel Setyan (“Setyan”), filed a declaration in support of Plaintiff’s request for default judgment. Setyan states he spent 8.5 hours on the case and his hourly rate is $550. (Setyan Decl., ¶¶ 6-8.) Thus, multiplying Setyan’s hourly fee and hours he worked on the case, Plaintiff is seeking $4,675.00 in total for attorney’s fees. (Setyan Decl., ¶ 8.) He states Plaintiff is also seeking $641.95 for filing and service costs. (Setyan Decl., ¶ 9.)

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Statement of Damages. Plaintiff states she is seeking judgment “in an amount according to proof but estimated to be no less than $96,760.00, in a money judgment representing compensatory damages including lost wages, earnings, commissions, retirement benefits, and other employee benefits, and all other sums of money, together with interest on these amounts; for other special damages; and for general damages for mental pain and anguish and emotional distress and loss of earning capacity. Plaintiff will also seek attorneys’ fees and costs estimated in the amount of $5,331.00.” (Amended Statement of Damages, p. 1.) Plaintiff further states she “reserves the right to seek $1,000,000 in punitive damages when Plaintiff seeks a judgment in the suit.” (Amended Statement of Damages, p. 1.) The Amended Statement of Damages does not indicate how Plaintiff calculated $96,760.00 in total amount of damages.

 

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Declaration in which she states she is seeking the total amount of $96,760.00 for her emotional distress damages and loss of earnings. Plaintiff requests emotional distress damages in the amount of $75,000. Plaintiff states as a result of Defendant’s conduct, she has suffered depression; inability to sleep; inability to concentrate; and her termination took a toll on her family and her relationship with her children. (Angle Decl., ¶¶ 9-12.) Plaintiff also seeks $21,760.00 in loss of earnings, calculated based on her weekly pay of $680 for the 32 weeks she was unemployed following her termination. (Angle Decl., ¶ 16.)

 

Plaintiff’s Amended Statement of Damages states she “reserves the right to seek” punitive damages, but her request for $1,000,000 in punitive damages contains no evidentiary support at all. An award of punitive damages, even on default, must be supported by proof of the Defendant’s financial condition. (See Soto v. BorgWarner Morse TEC Inc. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 165, 195 (“[e]vidence of a defendant's financial condition is a legal precondition to the award of punitive damages”). Ordinarily, financial condition is determined by comparing assets with liabilities, or income with expenses. (Id. at 194.) Plaintiff has supplied no such information.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED for failure to complete CIV-100 and failure to show entitlement to punitive damages.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.
