ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On August 4[1], 2023, arbitrator Shawn K. Aiken from the American Arbitration Association, issued an Award of Arbitrator in favor of Petitioner Forward Financing LLC (“Petitioner”) and against Respondents JAF Investments, Inc. dba JAF Investments and John Fowler (“Respondents”) in the sum of $22,757.41.  (Pet. ¶¶ 6-8, pp. 20-22.)

 

On April 22, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Petition”) as to Respondents.

 

On August 26, 2024, the Court noted that Respondents were not properly served with the Notice of Hearing and continued the hearing on the Petition.

 

On September 5, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of serving the Notice of Hearing on Respondents.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Once arbitration is concluded, “any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.).  Any of the parties may file a petition with the court, which must then “confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (Code of Civil Proc. §§ 1285, 1286; EHM Productions, Inc. v. ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On May 23, 2024, Petitioner Harout Keuroghlian; Serdar Dagkesen; and Johnhart Corp. (collectively Petitioners) filed a petition to vacate the arbitration award obtained by Patricia Petralia; Jeb Stuart Adams; and XQB Management, Inc (Respondents). Petitioners move to vacate the award on the grounds that the award was obtained through corruption, fraud or unfair means, the arbitrator was corrupt, the misconduct of the arbitrator substantially prejudiced petitioners’ rights, the arbitrator failed to hear evidence useful to settle the dispute, and the arbitrator failed to timely disclose grounds for disqualification.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

The court’s authority when a contractual arbitration has occurred is extremely limited. “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.” (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.) The merits of the controversy are generally not reviewable by the court when a petition to confirm or vacate is presented. (Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 10-11 .) The court will neither review the sufficiency of the evidence to support an award nor the validity of the reasoning. (Morris v. Zuckerman (1968) 69 Cal.2d 686, 691.)  The trial court’s review is restricted “to whether the award should be vacated under the grounds listed in section 1286.2.” (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc.¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1064.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

A.    Service of Petition and Notice of Hearing

 

“A petition to vacate an award or to correct an award shall be served and filed not later than 100

days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.” (Code Civ. Proc., §1288.)

 

Petitioners filed the petition on May 23, 2024. The attached arbitration award is dated April 9, 2024. Thus, the petition was timely filed. Petitioners do not provide the court with proof of service of the petition upon Respondents. However, Respondents do not object on the grounds of insufficient or untimely service. Accordingly, the court hears the merits of the argument.

 

B.     The Award was Obtained by Corruption, Fraud, or Unfair Mean; the Arbitrator was Corrupt; The Arbitrator’s Misconduct Substantially Prejudiced Petitioner’s Rights.

 

Petitioners argue the Arbitrator was corrupt and/or biased for three reasons: (1) he refused to refer to Respondents by their legal name due to “difficulty in pronunciation” and called them the Hart Team, (2) during closing arguments, a member of Respondents’ team went off screen for three minutes contrary to the procedures for the hearing and (3) at the conclusion of the hearing, the Arbitrator asked whether Petitioners (Respondents during the hearing) felt the matter was handled fairly and Petitioners reluctantly agreed.

 

“[A]n award may be vacated if ‘the record reveals facts which might create an impression of possible bias in the eyes of the hypothetical, reasonable person.’ ” (Roitz v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 716, 723.) “When reviewing a charge of bias, the litigants’ necessarily partisan views should not provide the applicable frame of reference.” (Id. at p. 724.) “Potential bias and prejudice must clearly be established [citation] and statutes authorizing disqualification of a judge on grounds of bias must be applied with restraint.” (Ibid.) “Neither strained relations between a judge and an attorney for a party nor expressions of opinion uttered by a judge, in what he conceived to be a discharge of his official duties, are evidence of bias or prejudice.” (Ibid. [internal quotations, brackets, and ellipses omitted].)

 

Petitioners’ argument regarding names appears to be an implicit argument that the Arbitrator lacked impartiality. However, the statement does not identify any traditional indication of bias. (See Cobler v. Stanley, Barber, Southard, Brown & Associates (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 518, 527 [“A person appointed as neutral arbitrator shall disclose to the AAA any circumstances likely to affect impartiality, including any bias or any financial or personal interest in the result of the arbitration or any past or present relationship with the parties or their counsel.”].) With respect to the names, though it may demonstrate the Arbitrator was somewhat intractable on referring to the parties by their names, Petitioners do not provide evidence linking this conduct to fraud or corruption. Without more, it appears purely speculative that the Arbitrator’s decision to call Petitioners the “Hart Team” demonstrates corruption, fraud, or undue influence.

 

Regarding the offscreen conduct, Petitioners point to the procedural requirements that “All parties are expected to appear on camera” (Ex. A. at p. 1.) Petitioners then contend the entire arbitration award should be vacated because during closing arguments, Respondent Jeb Adams “left the room and d[id] not return until more than three minutes later. . .” and he placed something in his pocket. Petitioners offer no evidence that something untoward happened during those three minutes. Petitioners also refer to the rules of confidentiality, but there is no evidence that Adams somehow revealed confidential information during his three-minute break. It seems equally, if not more likely, that nothing improper happened and Adams simply stepped out of the room for a brief personal reason. Nor have Petitioners identified any reason why the arbitration hearing was compromised due to the three-minute break. While it appears Adams leaving the room would violate the procedural requirements, and the arbitrator did not take action, Petitioners must demonstrate prejudice due to the conduct. This evidence is lacking.

 

Finally, regarding Petitioners’ acquiescence to the process of the hearing, there is nothing demonstrating the Arbitrator engaged in any misconduct. Petitioners have identified that with some reluctance they agreed the arbitration was sufficient. Nothing about that suggests the arbitrator engaged in corruption, undue influence, or fraud.

 

Accordingly, the award will not be vacated on any of these grounds.

 

C.     The Arbitrator Exceeded their Authority.

 

Next, Petitioners argue that the Arbitrator exceeded his authority by improperly ruling on three different objections to evidence.

 

“[T]he refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy” is grounds to vacate an arbitration award. (Code Civ. Proc., §1286.2, subd. (a)(5).) “The provision was designed as a safety valve in private arbitration that permits a court to intercede when an arbitrator has prevented a party from fairly presenting its case. [Citation.] It comes into play, for example, when an arbitrator, without justification, permits only one side to present evidence on a disputed material issue. [Citation.] The Arbitration Act codifies the fundamental principle that arbitration should give both parties an opportunity to be heard.” (Bacall v. Shumway (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 950, 963. [internal quotations and brackets omitted]) “Where, as here, a party complains of excluded material evidence, the reviewing court should generally focus first on prejudice, not materiality. To find substantial prejudice the court must accept, for purposes of analysis, the arbitrator’s legal theory and conclude that the arbitrator might well have made a different award had the evidence been allowed.” (Hall v. Superior Ct. (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 427, 439.)

 

Here, the three objections at issue are with the Arbitrator’s alleged misdescription of the rules of evidence in the proceeding, overruling a hearsay objection, and a dispute regarding the scope of questioning and whether the questioning was permissible. In each of these examples, Petitioners hinge their argument on a “disappointment with an arbitration decision [which] is not sufficient to prove substantial prejudice.” (Royal All. Assocs., Inc. v. Liebhaber (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1109.) These are not the types of evidentiary controversies which give rise to vacating an arbitration, such as preventing one side from wholly presenting its case or presenting evidence on a disputed issue. Instead, these appear to be disputes on specific rulings, none of which were dispositive. Petitioners have not demonstrated that the Arbitrator denied them the opportunity to present material evidence in a manner which would have changed the Arbitrator’s ruling.

 

The award will not be vacated on any of these grounds.

 

D.    The Arbitrator Failed to Postpone the Hearing or Hear Evidence Useful to Settle the Dispute.

 

Petitioners next argue the Arbitrator refused to permit impeaching testimony of Jeb Adams, narrowed the scope of Petitioners’ questioning in a manner Petitioners’ counsel disagreed with, and refused to permit certain evidence because the arbitrator contended the evidence had already been presented to the panel.

 

Similar to the prior discussion, in each of these cases, Petitioners are disputing individual evidentiary rulings. However, Petitioners have not demonstrated either that the Arbitrator denied Petitioners the opportunity to present sufficient evidence that would have changed the Arbitrator’s ruling or suffered any specific prejudice because of these rulings.

 

The award will not be vacated on any of these grounds.

 

E.     The Arbitrator Failed to Disclose Within the Time for Disclosure a Ground for Disqualification Which the Arbitrator was Then Aware.

 

Finally, Petitioners argue the arbitrator failed to timely disclose a current case he had with counsel Jonathan Malek and he should have been disqualified.

 

The court “shall vacate the award” if the arbitrator making the award “failed to disclose within the time required for disclosure a ground for disqualification for which the arbitrator was then aware”. (Code Civ. Proc., §1286.2, subd. (a)(6).) “A party who does not move to disqualify an arbitrator prior to the commencement of arbitration may nevertheless seek vacation of an arbitration award on the ground that the arbitrator “failed to disclose within the time required for disclosure a ground for disqualification of which the arbitrator was then aware.” (International Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees, etc. v. Laughon (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1380, 1386 (International Alliance).) This supports that a trial court is required to vacate an arbitration award once it is shown that the arbitrator “failed to make the required disclosure in a timely fashion.” (Ibid.)

 

However, since International Alliance was decided, other courts have expanded upon it to note that “[w]hile an arbitrator has a duty to disclose all of the details required to be disclosed pursuant to section 1281.9 and the Ethics Standards, a party aware that a disclosure is incomplete or otherwise fails to meet the statutory disclosure requirements cannot passively reserve the issue for consideration after the arbitration has concluded. Instead, the party must disqualify the arbitrator on that basis before the arbitration begins.” (United Health Centers of San Joaquin Valley, Inc. v. Superior Ct. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 63, 85 (United Health).)

 

Here, the Arbitrator filled out a disclosure form. (Ex. D.) The form requires the arbitrator to answer the following question:

 

Are you serving, or within the preceding five (5) years, have you served as a neutral arbitrator in another arbitration involving a party to the current arbitration, a lawyer for the party or other lawyer currently practicing law with an attorney for a party.

 

Petitioners and Respondents agree that the Arbitrator answered in the affirmative on the form but failed to initially disclose an ongoing matter with counsel Jonathan Malek. Instead, the arbitrator disclosed the relationship just prior to commencement of the arbitration proceeding. At that time, the Arbitrator disclosed the issue, stated he had made an error in filing out the disclosure form and then asked Petitioners if they would like him to recuse himself. They declined. Under the more recent United Health case, Petitioners’ failure to object or request disqualification at the time the Arbitrator made the disclosure renders Section 1286.2 inapplicable.

 

The award will not be vacated on any of these grounds.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

The Petitioners’ Petition to Vacate the Arbitration Award is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

On March 29, 2024, plaintiff Ian Wallach (plaintiff) filed suit against defendants Andrew John Marton and Child & Marton, LLP (collectively defendants) for a single claim of breach of contract. Plaintiff included no allegations regarding the contract at issue. Now plaintiff requests leave to file an amended complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473 and 576. Defendants filed a notice of non-opposition to the motion.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

The Court may, in the furtherance of justice, and upon any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 473, 576.)  There is “a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296.) Thus, the court generally grants requests for leave to amend unless the party seeking to amend has been dilatory in bringing the proposed amendment before the Court, and the delay in seeking leave to amend will cause prejudice to the opposing party. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490; Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565.)  The court may deny a motion for leave to amend where there is “inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party.” (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487 (Magpali) [permitting denial where plaintiff sought to add a new claim on the eve of trial which would subject defendant to prejudice in trial preparation].)

 

III.              Discussion

Plaintiff requests leave to amend to bolster their breach of contract claim. Specifically, the “privilege concerns regarding attaching the contracts at issue no longer exist” and, thus, plaintiff requests leave to include the agreement in the proposed amended complaint. (Mtn., at p. 2:09-11.) Plaintiff argues Defendants will suffer no prejudice as the amendment serves simply to clarify Plaintiff’s allegations against Defendants. Defendants filed a notice of non-opposition and requested ten (10) days to serve their response to the amended complaint. The court agrees that Plaintiff has demonstrated grounds for amendment and that there is no prejudice to Defendant in permitting it.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

The Motion for Leave to File an Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff Ian Wallach is GRANTED.
