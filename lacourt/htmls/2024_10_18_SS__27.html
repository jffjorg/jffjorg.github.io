Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV25473 ESTATE OF ROCHELLE TAYLOR vs LISA BEAUTY SALON & SPA

Moving Party: Defendant LISA BEAUTY SALON & SPA

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED

 

Background

On August 19, 2024, Defendant’s counsel attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding Plaintiff’s deposition and provided several proposed dates. After receiving no response and on August 22, 2024, Defendant served a notice of deposition for September 16, 2024. Plaintiff did not file an objection. On September 11, 2024, Defendant’s counsel requested confirmation of the deposition date and was informed by Plaintiff’s counsel that an email would be sent to Plaintiff to confirm by September 13, 2024. Despite follow-up attempts by Defendant’s counsel on both September 13 and September 16, no responses were received. Plaintiff did not appear for the deposition. Thereafter, Defendant’s sought new dates for Plaintiff’s deposition but received no replies. Defendant now moves the Court to compel Plaintiff’s deposition.

Legal Standard 

¿¿¿¿ 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450¿provides in pertinent part the following:¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040¿or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

Discussion

All the requirements for the present motion to compel are met. On August 22, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a notice of deposition set for September 16, 2024. Plaintiff did not file an objection and failed to appear at the scheduled deposition. Defendant attempted to obtain alternative deposition dates afterward but received no responses.

Plaintiff filed an objection, stating that Plaintiff's counsel has been making active efforts to contact Plaintiff but encountered difficulties. This lack of communication, however, should not be construed as an unwillingness to participate in the legal process. Plaintiff argues that the motion to compel should be reconsidered in light of these circumstances.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450, the requirements for the Court to compel a deposition are clear, and Defendant has met all of them. Plaintiff’s opposition does not dispute the timeline set forth by Defendant or claim that Defendant failed to meet statutory requirements. Therefore, Plaintiff does not oppose the motion on its merits.  Accordingly, the Court grants the motion. Plaintiff Sherikia Bagley is ordered to appear for her deposition and produce all required documents without objections within 20 days of today.

 

Furthermore, sanctions are granted in part to compensate Defendant for having to file this motion to obtain Plaintiff’s deposition. Plaintiff’s counsel informed Defendant's counsel that a response regarding Plaintiff’s appearance would be provided by September 13, 2024, but no response was given. Plaintiff’s counsel eventually responded after the deposition date had passed, but by then Defendant had already incurred court reporter fees. The Court finds both Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel responsible for the fees incurred by Defendant.

 

Defendant requests $1,490 in monetary sanctions. Despite Plaintiff’s objection, the Court finds this amount reasonable, as it covers the expenses incurred by Defendant in producing the certificate of non-compliance and filing the present motion. The Court, therefore, issues sanctions in the amount of $1,400 against Plaintiff and her counsel, jointly and severally, payable to Defendant within 20 days of today.

Case Number: 22STCV31068    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO STRIKE SUPPLEMENTAL EXPERT DESIGNATION

Hearing Date: 10/18/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV31068 MISTY STEVENSON vs VICTOR S KAO, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: GRANTED 

 

Background

·        On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant City with her expert designations.

·        On the same day, Defendant City served Plaintiff with its expert designations, identifying Jeffrey Korcheck, M.D., as an orthopedic expert.

·        On August 19, 2024, Defendant City served Plaintiff with a supplemental designation of expert witnesses, designating a second orthopedic surgeon, Stewart Shanfield, M.D., to testify on the same subject matter as its previously designated expert.

·        Plaintiff now moves the Court to strike Defendant's supplemental expert designation.

Legal Standard 

“Any party may demand a mutual and simultaneous exchange by all parties of a list containing the name and address of any natural person, including one who is a party, whose oral or deposition testimony in the form of an expert opinion any party expects to offer in evidence at the trial.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.210, subd. (b).)¿ The exchange of expert witness information shall include either of the following: (1) a list setting forth the name and address of a person whose expert opinion that party expects to offer in evidence at the trial, or (2) a statement that the party does not presently intend to offer the testimony of an expert witness.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.260, subd. (b).)¿ On objection of any party who has made a complete and timely compliance with Section 2034.260, the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness that is offered by any party who has unreasonably failed to do any of the following: (1) list that witness as an expert under Section 2034.260; (2) submit an expert witness declaration; (3) produce reports and writings of expert witnesses under Section 2034.270; or make that expert available for a deposition.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.300.) 

CCP § 2034.230 states “(b) The demand shall specify the date for the exchange of lists of expert trial witnesses, expert witness declarations, and any demanded production of writings. The specified date of exchange shall be 50 days before the initial trial date, or 20 days after service of the demand, whichever is closer to the trial date, unless the court, on motion and a showing of good cause, orders an earlier or later date of exchange.” 

Discussion

The central issue in this motion is whether the designation of Dr. Shanfield constitutes a permissible supplemental designation under CCP § 2034.280. Defendant argues that it has the right to supplement here because it is adding an expert to address a subject matter on which the opposing party plans to offer expert testimony.

Defendant concedes that under certain circumstances, the use of supplemental designations is impermissible, such as: (1) adding an expert on subjects already designated by the City in its original exchange; (2) adding experts on new subjects not raised by an opposing party's designation; or (3) substituting for an already-designated expert. [CCP § 2034.280(a); see Basham v. Babcock (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1717, 1723, 52 Cal.Rptr.2d 456, 460—party dissatisfied with a designated expert's deposition testimony could not call a supplementally named expert to testify on the same subjects at trial.] However, Defendant argues that this situation does not fall under those categories, as Dr. Shanfield, a foot and ankle specialist, is designated to address a subject raised by Plaintiff through her designation of Dr. Ouzounian, also a foot and ankle specialist. Defendant cites Du-All Safety, LLC v. Superior Ct., 34 Cal.App.5th 485, 498–99, for the position that a party has the right to supplement its expert witness exchange by adding experts to cover subjects on which the other party indicates it plans to offer expert testimony, and on which the opposing party had not previously retained an expert to testify.

This argument, however, is not supported by the evidence. First, Defendant attached no exhibits or declarations, such as one from Dr. Shanfield, attesting to his specialty as a foot and ankle specialist. Second, defense counsel’s declarations regarding both the initial expert disclosure of Dr. Korchek and the supplemental disclosure of Dr.Shanfield show that they are both orthopedic surgeons, and the area of testimony is exactly the same. (Ex. G and Ex. I to Motion.)

In the initial designation, defense counsel declares that “Dr. Korchek will testify as to his examination of plaintiff and his review of records related to plaintiff’s claims, as well as the deposition testimonies offered in this case and related documents and films, diagnostics, and x-rays/MRIs/CT scans pertinent to this action. His testimony will include, but is not limited to his opinions regarding the nature and extent of plaintiff’s alleged injuries and claims, and his findings, diagnosis, and prognosis. It is expected that Dr. Korchek will testify plaintiff’s past, present, and future medical condition and care, and the reasonableness of the care of plaintiff, plaintiff’s treatment and diagnoses, causation, alleged damages, and any rebuttal testimony. Dr. Korchek may also testify with regard to opinions expressed by the other parties’ experts whose testimony falls within the doctor’s area of expertise. Such testimony regarding other experts’ opinions may be provided at his deposition if said deposition occurs after that of the other parties’ experts.”

In the supplemental declaration, defense counsel declares that “Dr. Shanfield will testify as to his review of records related to plaintiff’s claims, as well as the deposition testimonies offered in this case and related documents and films, diagnostics, and x-rays/MRIs/CT scans pertinent to this action. His testimony will include, but is not limited to his opinions regarding the nature and extent of plaintiff’s alleged injuries and claims, and his findings, diagnosis, and prognosis. It is expected that Dr. Shanfield will testify plaintiff’s past, present, and future medical condition and care, and the reasonableness of the care of plaintiff, plaintiff’s treatment and diagnoses, causation, alleged damages, and any rebuttal testimony. Dr. Shanfield may also testify with regard to opinions expressed by the other parties’ experts whose testimony falls within the doctor’s area of expertise. Such testimony regarding other experts’ opinions may be provided at his deposition if said deposition occurs after that of the other parties’ experts.”

Aside from the name, the subject matter and area of expertise in which both experts will testify is exactly the same. There is no reference to ankles or feet in Dr. Shanfield’s designated testimony subject matter.

Finally, Dr. Shanfield’s CV does not show any specialty in ankles or feet, as alleged by Defendant. Based on the evidence submitted, the Court finds that the supplemental designation attempts to add an expert on subjects already designated by the City in its original exchange, which is an impermissible use of supplemental designations, as acknowledged by Defendant. Thus, Plaintiff’s motion to strike is granted.

Case Number: 23STCV05037    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 10/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV05037 EMILY WILSON vs JOSEPH ANTHONY CORRADO, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED

 

Background

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff served a notice for Defendant Corrado’s deposition, initially set for May 2, 2024. On April 15, 2024, counsel for Defendant Lyft, not Defendant Corrado, objected to the notice, claiming it was unilaterally scheduled. Plaintiff’s counsel met and conferred with counsel for both Defendants, and all parties agreed to reschedule Defendant Corrado’s deposition for May 22, 2024. On May 16, 2024, Defendant Corrado’s counsel requested a two-week postponement due to unexpected leave.  The parties agreed to continue the deposition to June 12, 2024. On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff sent an amended notice of Deposition. Defendants did not file an objection. On June 11, 2024, Defendant responded to Plaintiff’s meet and confer email, confirming that the deposition would proceed as scheduled the following day. On June 12, 2024, less than one-hour before the deposition, Defendant Corrado cancelled the deposition. From June to September 2024, the parties met and conferred to re-schedule Corrado’s deposition and discuss whether Defendant would stipulate to liability, which could eliminate the need for the deposition. Defendant refused to stipulate and failed to provide any alternative dates. Plaintiff now moves to compel Defendant Corrado’s deposition. Defendant did not file an opposition.

Legal Standard 

¿¿¿¿ 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450¿provides in pertinent part the following:¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040¿or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

Discussion

All the requirements for the present motion to compel are met. On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with an amended deposition notice for a deposition on June 12, 2024. Defendant did not file an objection and failed to appear at the scheduled deposition, despite the fact that it was confirmed a day prior, on June 11, 2024. Defendant also did not file an opposition. The motion is therefore granted.

Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $2,335. The Court finds that Defendant Corrado did not act with substantial justification, and sanctions are appropriate under the circumstances. Although the deposition was confirmed, it had been continued multiple times to accommodate Defendant Corrado. When Defendant failed to appear for the June 12, 2024, deposition, no further dates were provided thereafter. However, the Court reduces the requested sanctions to $1,500 given the simplicity of the issues and the lack of an opposition.

Defendant’s counsel indicated difficulties in controlling his client, stating that Defendant Corrado refused to participate in the litigation and was unwilling to appear for deposition. (Exhibit 15.) Under such circumstances, counsel should have moved to withdraw.  Counsel did not do so and thereby effectively condoned the conduct of his client.  Accordingly, the Court orders sanctions in the amount of $1,500 against Defendant Corrado and his counsel payable to Plaintiff within 20 days.
