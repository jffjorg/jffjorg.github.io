DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV01873    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01873

MOTION

Motion for a New Trial

MOVING PARTY

Defendant The Warehouse Restaurant

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Samantha Dewey-Gartner

 

MOTION

 

            This case arises from an injury Plaintiff Samantha Dewey-Gartner (“Plaintiff”) sustained when someone stepped on her foot while she was dining at the restaurant of Defendant The Warehouse Restaurant (“Defendant”).  Following a bench trial, the Court entered judgment on June 12, 2024 in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant. 

 

            Defendant moves for a new trial.  Plaintiff opposes the motion and Defendant replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS – NEW TRIAL

 

“As the motion for a new trial finds both its source and its limitations in the statutes . . . , the procedural steps prescribed by law are mandatory and must be strictly followed.”  (Mercer v. Perez (1968) 68 Cal.2d 104, 118.)   Code of Civil Procedure section 657 provides:

 

The verdict may be vacated and any other decision may be modified or vacated, in whole or in part, and a new or further trial granted on all or part of the issues, on the application of the party aggrieved, for any of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of such party:

 

1.     Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial.

 

2.     Misconduct of the jury; and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by a resort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors

 

3.     Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against.

 

4.     Newly discovered evidence, material for the party making the application, which he could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the trial.

 

5.     Excessive or inadequate damages.

 

6.     Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law.

 

7.     Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 658 provides: “When the application is made for a cause mentioned in the first, second, third and fourth subdivisions of Section 657, it must be made upon affidavits; otherwise it must be made on the minutes of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 658.)  As for the timing of the motion,

 

(a) The party intending to move for a new trial shall file with the clerk and serve upon each adverse party a notice of his or her intention to move for a new trial, designating the grounds upon which the motion will be made and whether the same will be made upon affidavits or the minutes of the court, or both, either:

 

(1) After the decision is rendered and before the entry of judgment.

 

(2) Within 15 days of the date of mailing notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court pursuant to Section 664.5, or service upon him or her by any party of written notice of entry of judgment, or within 180 days after the entry of judgment, whichever is earliest; provided, that upon the filing of the first notice of intention to move for a new trial by a party, each other party shall have 15 days after the service of that notice upon him or her to file and serve a notice of intention to move for a new trial.

 

(b) That notice of intention to move for a new trial shall be deemed to be a motion for a new trial on all the grounds stated in the notice. The times specified in paragraphs (1) and (2) of subdivision (a) shall not be extended by order or stipulation or by those provisions of Section 1013 that extend the time for exercising a right or doing an act where service is by mail.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 659, subd (a).) 

 

Within 10 days of filing the notice, the moving party shall serve upon all other parties and file any brief and accompanying documents, including affidavits in support of the motion. The other parties shall have 10 days after that service within which to serve upon the moving party and file any opposing briefs and accompanying documents, including counter-affidavits. The moving party shall have five days after that service to file any reply brief and accompanying documents. These deadlines may, for good cause shown by affidavit or by written stipulation of the parties, be extended by any judge for an additional period not to exceed 10 days.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 659a.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.    Procedural Issues

 

Following a bench trial, judgment was entered on June 12, 2024.  The Clerk of the Court mailed the Notice of Entry of Judgment on June 13, 2024.  Defendant filed a timely Notice of Intent to File Motion for New Trial on June 20, 2024.

 

On June 28, 2024, Defendant timely filed its memorandum of points and authorities in support of the motion.  Then, Plaintiff timely filed an opposition brief on the same day. 

 

Thereafter, Defendant filed a reply brief on July 29.  But under Section 659a, Defendant’s reply brief was due on July 3. 

 

Further, Defendant failed to establish good cause, by affidavit or written stipulation of the parties, to extend the reply brief deadline.  In any event, even if Defendant established good cause for an extension, the deadline would have been extended to July 13, not July 29, per Section 659a. 

 

Therefore, pursuant to Sections 659 and 659a, the Court does not have the authority to consider the untimely reply brief, and does not do so.

 

B.    Insufficiency of Evidence

 

Defendant contends there is insufficient evidence to support the Judgment pursuant Section 657, subdivision (6) as follows:

 

·       Issue 1:   The Court did not address in its Statement of Decision the effect that Plaintiff and her companion Earick's alcohol consumption had on their cognitive and physical abilities concerning perception, short term memory loss, ability to concentrate, thinking abilities, cognitive processing speed. They reported that a customer caused the injury in the bathroom, then Plaintiff made a report that it was an unidentified female "employee" and she wanted free drinks. Therefore, there was insufficient evidence under Section 657(6) to support the verdict, and a new trial could be granted.

 

·       Issue 2:  Plaintiff never specifically identified the employee that stepped on her foot, which she could have easily done at the time of the incident. All employees have name tags while working. It is still a complete mystery who caused her injury. Therefore, there was insufficient evidence under Section 657(6) to support the liability of defendant and the verdict, and a new trial should be granted.

 

Foremost, as the Court indicated in the Statement of Decision:   “A statement of decision need not address all the legal and factual issues raised by the parties. Instead, it need do no more than state the grounds upon which the judgment rests, without necessarily specifying the particular evidence considered by the trial court in reaching its decision.  A trial court rendering a statement of decision under section 632 is required to state only ultimate rather than evidentiary facts because findings of ultimate facts necessarily include findings on all intermediate evidentiary facts necessary to sustain them.  In other words, a trial court rendering a statement of decision is required only to set out ultimate findings rather than evidentiary ones.”  (Muzquiz v. City of Emeryville (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1106, 1124–1125 [cleaned up] (hereafter Muzquiz).) 

 

In Peak-Las Positas Partners v. Bollag, the defendant argued “that the statement of decision does not discuss all the evidentiary and legal issues raised in his written objections.”  But the Court of Appeal did not find the defendant’s contention to be convincing as “a trial court is required only to state ultimate facts and the legal grounds upon which the judgment rests.”  (Peak-Las Positas Partners v. Bollag (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 101, 112, emphasis added (hereafter Bollag).) [1]

 

Here, Defendant fails to proffer any argument or supporting authority warranting a departure from the edicts pronounced by Bollag and Muzquiz regarding the adequacy of Statements of Decision, including the subject Statement. 

 

In short, the Court has set forth the ultimate facts upon which the Judgment rests.  The Court is not required to answer any of the issues or the sub-issues as posed by Defendant.[2]

 

C.    Error In Law

 

Defendant contends the Court committed errors in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application under Section 657, subdivision (7) as follows:

 

·       The trial Court striking the testimony of OROZCO constituted was an omission of a material fact as a matter of law, and allowed the Court to accept a misrepresentation of material evidence in the case in support a non-proven inference of a fact not in evidence under Code of Civil Procedure, Section 657(7). Error in law, occurring at the trial, and excepted to by the party making the application in Statement of Decision.

 

            The Court finds Defendant’s contention as stated above to be nonsensical, and at best, fallacious. 

 

            First, Defendant does not identify the specific law or question of law upon which this Court made an error.  (See Defendant’s Memorandum of Points & Authorities, pp.16-20.)  Second, the Court did not “strike” any testimony of Paola Orozco, and Defendant fails to cite to the record, or the Statement of Decision, where the Court did so. 

 

 

 

D.    Excessive or Inadequate Damages

 

Defendant argues that the Court’s award of damages in the form of “lost earnings” is unsupported by the evidence.  (See Defendant’s Memorandum of Points & Authorities, pp.20-22.)  Again, the Court finds Defendant’s contention to be nonsensical, and at best, specious. 

 

First, the Court did not specifically award “lost earnings” to Plaintiff.  Second, the Court awarded Economic Damages to Plaintiff in the amount of $8,810, which encompasses Plaintiff’s  health care expenses and loss of earnings.  Third, the Court does not find any basis to view with distrust Plaintiff’s evidence in support of her claim for damages, and Defendant has not convinced the Court that it should do so, especially when Defendant did not advance any evidence calling into doubt the veracity of Plaintiff’s evidence regarding the harm caused by Defendant’s breach of the duty of care.   

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court denies Defendant’s motion for a new trial.  The Clerk of the Court shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling.
