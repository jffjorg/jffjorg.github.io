DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 20STCV13703    Hearing Date: October 28, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

DANNY BAKEWELL, SR.,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KEVIN COHEE; ONEUNITED BANK, and DOES 1-20, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          20STCV13703

 Hearing Date:   October 28, 2024

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Amend Judgment

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr. (Bakewell) sued Defendants Kevin Cohee (Cohee), OneUnited Bank (OneUnited) and Does 1 through 20 pursuant to an April 15, 2020, First Amended Complaint alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud and Deceit; (3) Declaratory Relief; (4) Unfair Competition; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (6) Fraudulent Sale of Securities.

The claims arose from allegations that in January 2000, Bakewell agreed to purchase preferred stock from OneUnited in the amount of $250,000.00. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell alleges that Cohee, the CEO and Chairman of OneUnited, orally represented and personally guaranteed that Bakewell could redeem the preferred stock shares at any time upon request and receive $250,000.00 plus any accumulated dividends. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell states that he never received the preferred stock certificates. (FAC ¶ 7.) When Bakewell tried to redeem his stock in 2019, Defendants denied the request and contended that Bakewell had no right to redeem the preferred shares. (FAC ¶ 9.) Bakewell subsequently filed this lawsuit.

B. Relevant Procedural History

This action was tried in July 2024. Only the causes of action for breach of contract, fraud (with jury instructions as to false promise and intentional misrepresentation at trial), and breach of fiduciary duty went to trial by jury.

On July 19, 2024, the jury rendered a verdict in favor of Bakewell on all causes of action.

On August 8, 2024, the Court signed and entered judgment and on August 13, 2024, the Clerk sent notice of entry of judgment to the parties.

C. Motion Before the Court

On October 8, 2024, Bakewell filed a motion to amend judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 187 and Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subdivisions (a) and (d). The Court specially set it and ordered a briefing schedule, providing Defendants with statutory time to oppose the motion.

On October 18, 2024, the Defendants opposed the motion.

On October 23, 2024, Bakewell filed his reply.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 187 provides that: “When jurisdiction is, by the Constitution or this Code, or by any other statute, conferred on a Court or judicial officer, all the means necessary to carry it into effect are also given; and in the exercise of this jurisdiction, if the course of proceeding be not specifically pointed out by this Code or the statute, any suitable process or mode of proceeding may be adopted which may appear most conformable to the spirit of this Code.”

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1) provides, in relevant part: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) provides that: “The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.”

B. Analysis

First, Bakewell argues that the Court has the authority to amend the judgment to hold Cohee jointly and severally liable for Bakewell’s damages award pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 187 (Code. Civ. Proc., § 187.) Bakewell argues that, in holding OneUnited liable, the jury implicitly found that Cohee, acting within the scope of his employment, caused the complained of harm to Bakewell. (Mot. p. 4:1-5.) Bakewell argues that the judgment should be amended “to maintain consistency with the jury’s findings and the legal doctrine of respondeat superior.” (Mot. p. 4:5-6.)

In support of his assertion that the Court has the authority to do so, Bakewell cites two cases that suggest Code of Civil Procedure section 187 authorizes courts to amend judgments to add an alter ego of an original judgment debtor. (see Highland Springs Conference & Training Genter v. City of Banning (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 267; see also, Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Weinberg (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 1.) As noted by Defendants, these cases cited are distinguishable from the circumstances here because Bakewell does not allege that Cohee is the alter ego of OneUnited.

It is true, as Bakewell argues on reply, that the language of section 187 is not exclusive to alter ego cases, and indeed, the statute has been invoked to allow the amendment of judgments in cases involving successor corporations. (Reno-Tahoe Specialty, Inc. v. Mungchi, Inc. (C.D. Cal. 2020) 468 F.Supp.3d 1236.)

However, amending the judgment to add Cohee as jointly and severally liable for the damages is not akin to adding an alter ego or successor of an already liable defendant. The Court agrees with Defendants that there is no basis to amend the judgment in this way. (Mac v. Minassian (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 510, 520-521.) To the extent Plaintiff believed that the verdict form should have made clear that a finding of liability for one defendant would have necessitated liability for the other, it was necessary to make that argument – which could then have been opposed by Defendant and resolved by the Court --  before the verdict and jury instructions were submitted to the jury.

Thus, the Court does not have the authority to amend the judgment as requested under Code of Civil Procedure section 187.

Next, Bakewell argues that the Court should amend the judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473. Bakewell asserts that the $956,058.00 should be modified from a “non-economic damages” award to an “economic damages” award. (Mot. pp. 4-5.) Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subdivision (d) grant Courts authority to correct mistakes. (Code. Civ. Proc., § 473 subd.)

Here, there is an indication that the judge made a clerical error in signing a Judgment characterizing the damages as it did. The verdict form signed by the Jury Foreperson indicated at Question No. 6 that it was awarding $956,058.00 against “OneUnited Bank [in] Economic Damages.” (Exhibit A to the Declaration of Rickey Ivie in support of Reply Brief.) However, the Judgment provided to the Court, which the Court signed, indicated at Question 6 that the damages were awarded against OneUnited Bank for “Non-Economic” damages. (Exhibit B to the Declaration of Rickey Ivie in support of Reply Brief.)

Accordingly, this does appear to be a proper use of the Court’s authority under section 473 subdivision (d) to correct a clerical mistake.

Thus, the Court does not have the authority to amend the judgment as requested under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.

Accordingly, the motion to amend judgment is GRANTED to correct the clerical order that the $956,058.00 in damages against OneUnited Bank are for Economic Damages, and the motion is otherwise DENIED.   

 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell Sr.’s Motion to Amend Judgment is GRANTED to correct the clerical order that the $956,058.00 in damages against OneUnited Bank are for Economic Damages, and the motion is otherwise DENIED. Plaintiff is ordered to lodge an Amended Judgment within three business days.

DANNY BAKEWELL, SR.,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KEVIN COHEE; ONEUNITED BANK, and DOES 1-20, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          20STCV13703

 Hearing Date:   October 15, 2024

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant OneUnited Bank’s Motion to Tax Costs

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr. (Bakewell) sued Defendants Kevin Cohee (Cohee), OneUnited Bank (OneUnited) and Does 1 through 20 pursuant to an April 15, 2020, First Amended Complaint alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud and Deceit; (3) Declaratory Relief; (4) Unfair Competition; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (6) Fraudulent Sale of Securities.

The claims arose from allegations that in January 2000, Bakewell agreed to purchase preferred stock from OneUnited in the amount of $250,000.00. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell alleges that Cohee, the CEO and Chairman of OneUnited, orally represented and personally guaranteed that Bakewell could redeem the preferred stock shares at any time upon request and receive $250,000.00 plus any accumulated dividends. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell states that he never received the preferred stock certificates. (FAC ¶ 7.) When Bakewell tried to redeem his stock in 2019, Defendants denied the request and contended that Bakewell had no right to redeem the preferred shares. (FAC ¶ 9.) Bakewell subsequently filed this lawsuit.

B. Relevant Procedural History

This action was tried in July 2024. Only the causes of action for breach of contract, fraud (with jury instructions as to false promise and intentional misrepresentation at trial), and breach of fiduciary duty went to trial by jury.

On July 19, 2024, the jury rendered a verdict in favor of Bakewell on all causes of action.

On August 8, 2024, the Court signed and entered judgment and on August 13, 2024, the Clerk noticed entry of judgment on the parties.

C. Motion Before the Court

On August 22, 2024, Bakewell filed and served an Amended Memorandum of Costs seeking $90,505.05 in costs.

On September 10, 2024, Defendant OneUnited Bank filed the instant motion to tax costs.

On October 2, 2024, Bakewell opposed the motion.

On October 8, 2024, OneUnited replied.

The motion was continued to be heard along with Bakewell’s motion to tax Defendants’ costs.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

¿Allowable costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount. An item not specifically allowable under Section 1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount). If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774 (Ladas).)

Moreover, a verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services therein listed were necessarily incurred. (Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Serv., Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) A party seeking to tax costs must provide evidence to rebut this prima facie showing. (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1266 (Jones), superseded by statute on other grounds in Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (c)(1) [whether costs permissible from filing of complaint or from date of 998 offer].) Mere statements unsupported by facts are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing that costs were necessarily incurred. (Jones, supra, at p. 1266.)¿On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Ibid.) 

Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion. (Ladas, supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.) Because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute, a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized. (Ibid.) Discretion is abused only when, in its exercise, the court “exceeds the bounds of reason, all of the circumstances being considered.” (Lincoln, supra, 39 Cal.App.4th at p. 105.)¿ 

B. Analysis

OneUnited moves to tax certain costs items on the grounds that Bakewell seeks inflated deposition costs, and service of process and court reporter fees, and seeks unauthorized parking charges and expert witness fees.

1.     Item No. 4, Deposition Costs

OneUnited challenges $31,804.83 of deposition costs as inflated. OneUnited argues that the $5,241.62 for a one-day deposition for Cohee, $5,896.10 for a one-day Zoom deposition for Cooper, and $3,390.10 for a partial day of deposition for Maxine Waters are significantly higher than normal. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 4:12-13.)  One United also argues that the $4,240.86 sought in non-appearance fees were not necessarily incurred because Bakewell was given ample notice ahead of time that the deponents were not appearing for the depositions. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 4:18-21; Fox Decl. ¶ 6.) 

In opposition, Bakewell asserts that deposition costs are authorized under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 subdivisions (a)(3)(A) and (a)(3)(C). (Opp. p. 6:12-15.) However, he does not provide an explanation of the costs incurred, copies of the invoices, or any other documentation.

Here, OneUnited has failed to meet its burden to challenge Bakewell’s deposition costs for Cohee, Cooper, and Waters’s depositions because the depositions were necessarily incurred in order to conduct discovery. Simply speculating that the costs are too high is insufficient. However, OneUnited has met its burden to show that the non-appearance fees were unnecessarily incurred because Bakewell had prior notice that the deponents would not appear, giving him sufficient time to cancel the court reporting services and avoid the fees.

The Court thus GRANTS OneUnited’s motion as to taxing “deposition costs” in part, in the amount of $4,240.86.

2.     Item No. 5, Service of Process

OneUnited challenges service of process costs of $2,886.95 as excessive because Bakewell lists six undated attempts of service on both Sidney Williams and Maxine Waters that vary widely in amount. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 5:6-14.) 

In opposition, Bakewell asserts that he is entitled to the costs because several attempts were made to serve the relevant parties. (Opp. p. 7:1-3.)

Here, Civil Code Section 1033.5 subdivision (a)(4) clearly allows for the recovery of costs for service of process. In the absence of contrary legal authority, OneUnited has failed to meet its burden to challenge the costs.

The Court thus DENIES OneUnited’s motion as to taxing “service of process” costs. 

3.     Item No. 6, Attachment Expenses

Next, OneUnited challenges $208 in parking costs because parking is not a proper attachment expense. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 5:16-18.)  Bakewell argues in opposition that various travel costs are allowable costs when reasonably necessary to attend a deposition. (Opp., p. 6.)

Here, parking is not an “attachment expense” within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 subdivision (a)(5). Nor has Bakewell provided any specificity as to when and where these parking costs were incurred, making it impossible to confirm that they would qualify as travel expenses to attend a deposition.

The Court thus GRANTS OneUnited’s motion as to taxing “attachment expenses” in the amount of $208.00. 

4.     Item No. 8, Witness Fees

OneUnited challenges $41,800.60 in expert witness fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 998. It argues that “[a]n unapportioned settlement offer to several defendants jointly is not sufficient to trigger section 998 penalties.” (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 6:13-14.)  OneUnited relies on Taing v. Johnson Scaffolding Co., (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 579, 586 to support this assertion. In Taing, the Court of Appeals held that awarding expert witness fees was improper where plaintiff served a joint 998 on three defendants because it was not “sufficiently specific to permit that individual defendant to determine the exact amount plaintiff is seeking from him or her.” (Ibid.) Bakewell served OneUnited and Kevin Cohee a single 998 offer which stated that he was willing to dismiss the action if the Defendants pay $500,000 and agree to have judgment entered against them. (Mot. to Tax Costs, pp. 6-7.) The offer only had one signature line for Defendants counsel to accept on behalf of both Defendants. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 7.) OneUnited also argues that the expert fees are excessive. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 7.)

In opposition, Bakewell argues that the 998 offer is not invalid because it was jointly made. (Opp. p. 4.) He specifies that a jointly made offer does not preclude a determination that a plaintiff received a more favorable judgment. (Opp. pp. 7-8) However, he does not make any counterargument to OneUnited’s contention that a 998 offer made to several defendants jointly is not sufficient to trigger the award of expert witness fees. The cases Bakewell cites in opposition do not address this specific question and do not support a different outcome.

The Court thus GRANTS OneUnited’s motion as to taxing “witness fees” in part, in the amount of $41,760.00.  The Court notes that $40.60 were for ordinary, not expert, witness fees. (See Amended Memorandum of Costs, Worksheet, 8(a).)

5.     Item No. 11, Court Reporter Fees

Lastly, OneUnited challenges $11,352.96 in court reporter fees as excessive because the parties agreed to equally split the cost of the court reporter, but OneUnited’s half was only $8,706.57. (Mot. to Tax Costs, p. 7:17-19.)

In opposition, Bakewell argues that recovery for court reporter costs is authorized under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 subdivision (a)(11). (Opp. p. 6:12-15.) While he states that he is “prepared to provide copies of the invoices” he neither submit the invoices nor addresses the discrepancy.

Here, OneUnited has properly challenged the reasonableness of the court reporter costs. The burden thus shifts to Bakewell to show that the cost was reasonably incurred. Bakewell offers no explanation for the additional $2,646.39 in his ‘half’ of the costs.

The Court thus GRANTS OneUnited motion as to taxing “court reporter fees” in part, in the amount of $2,646.39.

 

Based on the foregoing, OneUnited’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $48,855.25.

 

III. Conclusion

Defendant OneUnited Bank’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED, in part.

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell Sr.’s Amended Memorandum of Costs is TAXED in the amount of $48,855.25.

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs [RES ID # 3510]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell, Sr. (Bakewell) sued Defendants Kevin Cohee (Cohee), OneUnited Bank (OneUnited) and Does 1 through 20 pursuant to an April 15, 2020, First Amended Complaint alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud and Deceit; (3) Declaratory Relief; (4) Unfair Competition; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (6) Fraudulent Sale of Securities.

The claims arose from allegations that in January 2000, Bakewell agreed to purchase preferred stock from OneUnited in the amount of $250,000.00. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell alleges that Cohee, the CEO and Chairman of OneUnited, orally represented and personally guaranteed that Bakewell could redeem the preferred stock shares at any time upon request and receive $250,000.00 plus any accumulated dividends. (FAC ¶ 6.) Bakewell states that he never received the preferred stock certificates. (FAC ¶ 7.) When Bakewell tried to redeem his stock in 2019, Defendants denied the request and contended that Bakewell had no right to redeem the preferred shares. (FAC ¶ 9.) Bakewell subsequently filed this lawsuit.

B. Relevant Procedural History

This action was tried in July 2024. Only the causes of action for breach of contract, fraud (with jury instructions as to false promise and intentional misrepresentation at trial), and breach of fiduciary duty went to trial by jury.

On July 19, 2024, the jury rendered a verdict in favor of Bakewell on all causes of action.

On August 8, 2024, the Court signed and entered judgment and on August 13, 2024, the Clerk noticed entry of judgment on the parties.

C. Motion Before the Court

On August 22, 2024, Defendants OneUnited Bank and Kevin Cohee filed and served a Memorandum of Costs seeking $34,179.20 in costs.

On September 9, 2024, Bakewell filed the instant motion to strike and/or tax costs.

On October 15, 2024, the Defendants opposed the motion.

On October 21, 2024, Bakewell replied.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

¿ In general, the “prevailing party” is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs for suit in any action or proceeding. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b); Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 606; Scott Co. Of Calif. v. Blount, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1103, 1108.) Assuming the “prevailing party” requirements are met, the trial court has no discretion to order each party to bear his or her own costs of suit. (Michell v. Olick (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1194, 1198; Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 129.) The term “prevailing party” for costs purposes is defined by statute to include: ¿ 

(1) The party with a net monetary recovery; ¿ 

(2) A defendant who is dismissed from the action; ¿ 

(3) A defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant recovers anything; and ¿ 

(4) A defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4).) 

If the party does not fall within one of these four express categories, the court may exercise its discretion to award or deny costs. (See Lincoln v. Schurgin (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 100, 105.)  

If any party recovers other than monetary relief and in situations other than as specified, the “prevailing party” shall be as determined by the court, and under those circumstances, the court, in its discretion, may allow costs or not and, if allowed, may apportion costs between the parties on the same or adverse sides pursuant to rules adopted under Section 1034. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4).) For example, even if a plaintiff maintains a net monetary recovery, in determining the prevailing party in the litigation, the trial court should also consider, if applicable, the defendant’s success on its declaratory relief claims and exercise its discretion to allow costs or not and, if allowed, to apportion them as appropriate. (Wolf v. Walt Disney Pictures & Television (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1107, 1142; see, e.g., Lincoln v. Schurgin, supra, 39 Cal.App.4th at pp. 104-105 [when plaintiff wins net monetary recovery but defendant prevails in its cross-action for declaratory relief, case presents circumstance not otherwise specified; in that case, determination of prevailing party is matter within court’s discretion].) 

Allowable costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount. An item not specifically allowable under Section 1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount). If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774 (Ladas).)

Moreover, a verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services therein listed were necessarily incurred. (Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Serv., Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) A party seeking to tax costs must provide evidence to rebut this prima facie showing. (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1266 (Jones), superseded by statute on other grounds in Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (c)(1) [whether costs permissible from filing of complaint or from date of 998 offer].) Mere statements unsupported by facts are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing that costs were necessarily incurred. (Jones, supra, at p. 1266.)¿On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Ibid.) 

Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion. (Ladas, supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.) Because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute, a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized. (Ibid.) Discretion is abused only when, in its exercise, the court “exceeds the bounds of reason, all of the circumstances being considered.” (Lincoln, supra, 39 Cal.App.4th at p. 105.)¿ 

B. Analysis

Bakewell moves to strike Defendants’ entire memorandum of costs on the grounds that Defendant Cohee is not a prevailing party, or in the alternative, tax certain costs items on the grounds that Defendant Cohee is not a prevailing party and the memorandum of costs is deficient because it does not attach any documentation supporting the costs.  

1.     Prevailing Party

Bakewell argues that the Defendants are not entitled to recover their costs because Defendant Cohee is not a “prevailing party.” Bakewell argues that the August 8, 2024 Judgment neither finds in favor of nor awards any amount to Cohee. (Mot. p. 8.) In opposition, the Defendants assert that Cohee is a prevailing party because Code of Civil Procedure defines a “prevailing party” to include “a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032 subd. (a)(4).) The Defendants rely on the Supreme Court’s decision in Goodman v. Lozano for their assertion that where a plaintiff obtains no monetary recovery from a defendant, that defendant is a prevailing party. (Goodman v. Lozano (2010) 47 Cal.4th 1327 at 1333.) Bakewell argues in reply that the Defendants misapply Goodman, and that in this case the jury awarded damages based on “the implicit finding that [Cohee] was acting in the course and scope of his employment with [OneUnited] when the wrongful acts were committed which is the predicate for the award against [OneUnited].” (Reply. p. 3:6-8.)

The Supreme Court’s decision in Goodman is not instructive in this case. In Goodman, the Court only discussed when a party can invoke the “net monetary recovery” prong of Code of Civil Procedure section 1032 subdivision (a)(4). The Supreme Court did not reach the “defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant” prong. Thus, the Court is bound by the plain language of the statute, which clearly defines a defendant against whom the plaintiff obtains no relief as a prevailing party. (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1032 subd. (a)(4).) Bakewell does not cite any legal authority that interprets that prong of the section differently. Further, Bakewell’s assertion that the jury implicitly found against Cohee because the damage award was necessarily predicated on an understanding that Cohee, within the scope of his employment, committed wrongful acts, does not change the outcome that Bakewell obtained relief solely from OneUnited, and not from Cohee.

Thus, Cohee is a prevailing party under the language of Code of Civil Procedure section 1032 subdivision (a)(4) and is entitled to recover costs.

2.     Costs

Bakewell argues that each cost the Defendants seek should be stricken on the grounds that the Defendants do not provide any documentation for the cost, thus rendering it impossible to determine whether the cost was necessarily or reasonably incurred. (Mot. pp. 12-17.) In reply, the Defendants argue that such documentation is not required and that the memorandum is both itemized and clear. (Opp. pp. 4-5.) Plaintiff cites no specific requirement that the invoices or receipts be attached. (Cf. Bach v. County of Butte (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 294, 307-308.) The burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that the costs were not necessarily or reasonably incurred. (see Ladas, supra at 773-774; see also Jones, supra at 1266.) The burden only shifts if items are “properly objected to,” at which time they are put at issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Jones, supra, at 1266.)

Bakewell argues that the costs sought are unreasonable by citing again to Defendants’ failure to attach any receipts or invoices to their memorandum. (Mot. pp. 11-12.) The only specific items that Bakewell challenges as unreasonable are the parking and hotel expenses that cost for $3,272.81, photocopies of exhibits for $1,593.41, electronic filing at $891.85 and service of process at $2003.15 “without a single receipt or invoice.” (Ibid.) However, there are detailed breakdowns of these expenses in the Attachments to the memorandum, and the Court does not see prima facie any evidence of their being unreasonable.

Accordingly, Bakewell has not met his burden to show that the costs sought are improper.

However, upon review of the Memorandum, it is clear that it includes total costs for the defense of the entire case. No effort is made to distinguish between Cohee’s costs and OneUnited Bank’s costs. Considering that only one of the two Defendants is a prevailing party, it would be unjust to grant Defendants Cohee and OneUnited Bank their full costs. Thus, the motion to tax costs in the amount of $34,179.20 is reduced by half.

Based on the foregoing, Bakewell’s motion to tax costs is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $17,089.60.

C. Request for Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 permits a trial court to “order a party, a party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5 subd. (a).)  Actions or tactics include, but are not limited to, filing or opposing motions, complaints, answers, or other responsive pleadings.  (Code Civ. Proc. §¿128.5 subd. (b)(1).)  “‘Frivolous’ means totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 128.5 subd. (b)(2).)  Bad faith is determined using a subjective standard.  (In re Marriage of Sahafzadeh-Taeb & Taeb (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 139, 134-35.) 

Here, the Defendants memorandum of costs was not deficient, and the request for costs is not frivolous as Cohee fits into one of the clear statutory definitions of a prevailing party in Code of Civil Procedure section 1032 subdivision (a)(4).

Thus, Bakewell’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff Danny Bakewell Sr.’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED, in part.

Defendants Kevin Cohee and OneUnited Bank’s Memorandum of Costs is TAXED in the amount of $17,089.60.

 Trial Date:        None Set

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Special Motion to Strike Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 [RES ID # 1768]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Brett Stettner (Brett), as Executor for the Estate of Suzi G. Stettner, pro se, sues Defendants Wesley Mitchell and Anna Snihs (erroneously sued as Anna Snih) (collectively Defendants) pursuant to a July 30, 2024 First Amended Complaint (FAC) alleging causes of action for: (1) Misuse or Misappropriation of Funds; (2) Selective Enforcement – Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Unjust Enrichment; (4) Harassment; and (5) Wrongful Death.

To avoid confusion, the Court refers to parties by their first names. No disrespect is intended.

The claims arise from allegations that Suzi Stettner (Suzi) and the Defendants are residents and partial owners of a six-unit condominium property located at 1014 Hilldale Avenue in West Hollywood, Los Angeles, California. Brett asserts that the Defendants carried out extensive repairs to their unit, amounting to $180,000.00, which the HOA divided among the six units, assessing $31,000.00 per member. Brett alleges that HOA President John Vargas and Defendant Wesley Mitchell, the HOA Treasurer, threatened members with foreclosure if they did not pay the fees. Brett asserts that these threats led Tiffany Stettner, another HOA member and Suzi’s daughter, to take her own life. Brett further asserts that Tiffany’s death caused Suzi’s rapid health decline, ultimately leading to her death a few months later.  

B. Motion Before the Court

On September 9, 2024, Defendants filed the instant special motion to strike (Anti-SLAPP) all causes of action in the FAC.

Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.  

On October 16, 2024, the Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Receipt of Plaintiff’s Opposition.

Defendant’s Anti-SLAPP motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

Anti-SLAPP analysis under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 proceeds in two steps. In the first step, the defendant or moving party must make “a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one ‘arising from’ protected activity.” (Barry v. State Bar of California (2017) 2 Cal.5th 318, 321, quotations omitted (Barry).) In this context, the term “protected activity” refers to speech or petitioning activities. (Barry, 2 Cal.5th at p. 321.) A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim; otherwise stated, “the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action [must] itself [be] … an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1063, quotations omitted (Park).) “[T]he focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’” (Ibid.) In teasing out whether protected conduct exists, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability. (Ibid.) 

If the court finds the defendant or moving party succeeds at the first step, then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to “demonstrate[] a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Ibid. [quotations omitted].) At the second step, courts “evaluate the defendants’ evidence only to determine if it defeats that submitted by the plaintiff as a matter of law.’ [Citation.] ‘[I]n order to establish the requisite probability of prevailing [citation], the plaintiff need only have “‘stated and substantiated a legally sufficient claim.’” [Citation.] “Put another way, the plaintiff ‘must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.’”‘ [Citation.] … That burden [is] not particularly high.” (Area 51 Productions, Inc. v. City of Alameda (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 581, 602 (Area 51 Productions, Inc.).) “Claims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 94 (Navallier).) If the cause of action satisfies both prongs of the anti-SLAPP statute, then it is subject to being struck. (Barry, supra, 2 Cal.5th at p. 321.) 

As stated by our State’s highest Court: “This is a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure at an early stage of the litigation.’” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 192 (Varian).) 

B. Analysis

1. First-Prong Analysis 

a. Relevant Law 

A defendant meets his burden of showing that a plaintiff’s claim arises from that defendant’s exercise of free speech or petition rights by making a prima facie showing that the act or conduct underlying the plaintiff’s claims falls within one of the four categories found in Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e). (Navellier, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 88.) “Courts should analyze each claim for relief — each act or set of acts supplying a basis for relief, of which there may be several in a single pleaded cause of action — to determine whether the acts are protected [the first prong of the analysis] and, if so, whether the claim they give rise to has the requisite degree of merit to survive the motion [the second prong of the analysis].” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010.) “[W]hen the allegations referring to an arguably protected activity are only incidental to a cause of action based essentially on non-protective activity, collateral allusions to protected activity should not subject the cause of action to the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Martinez v. Metabolife Internat., Inc. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 188 (Martinez).) 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e) states: 

As used in this section, “act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes:  

(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,  

(2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,  

(3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or  

(4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.  

(Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e) (italics added.) 

At the first step of the anti-SLAPP analysis, the moving defendant bears the burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity, and the claims for relief supported by them. When relief is sought based on allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the unprotected activity is disregarded for the first prong of the anti-SLAPP discussion. If the court determines that relief is sought based on allegations arising from activity protected by the statute, the second step is reached. (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 396 (Baral).) 

b. Discussion 

The Defendants allege that all the causes of action in the TAC arise from protected activity because the claims center on the HOA’s decision to levy a special assessment on the HOA members for fees associated with exterior repairs of one unit. The Defendants rely on Country Side Villas Homeowners Assn. v. Ivie (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1110 for their assertion that this constitutes an issue of public-interest within the meaning of the Anti-SLAPP statute. In Country Side Villas, the Court found that a defendant homeowner’s complaints about an HOA’s new policy that the HOA, as opposed to individual homeowners, was responsible for maintaining balconies and siding on individual units was a matter of public interest because the homeowners’ complaints affected all members of the HOA. (Country Side Villas, supra, at 1118.) Notably, in Country Side Villas, the defendant homeowner circulated a petition amongst homeowners objecting to the new maintenance requirements, she requested financial documents from the HOA which the HOA’s counsel refused to provide unless she signed a confidentiality agreement, the HOA’s counsel threatened to sue the homeowner if she continued to request the documents, and the homeowner and HOA participated in alternative dispute resolution. (Country Side Villas, supra, at 1113.) Subsequently, the HOA sued the homeowner and other residents seeking, among other things, declaratory relief as to interpretation of the contested maintenance requirements. (Ibid.)

There, the Court found that Plaintiff HOA’s action arose from the Defendant homeowner’s exercise of her free speech and petition rights against the maintenance requirements, and that speech and petition concerned a matter of public interest. (Country Side Villas, supra, at 1118 [“Here. . . Ms. Ivie spoke out against the members of the homeowners’ association board and management, on matters that affected all members of the association”].) Here, the converse occurred; the Defendant is the HOA and plaintiff’s complaint about the fees is this lawsuit.

Similarly, in Ruiz v. Harbor View Community Association (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1456, 1467, the court held that there were two letters sent by the defendant that constituted the exercise of the constitutional right of free speech in connection with an issue of public interest. Again, this case supports the concept that an anti-SLAPP motion can arise in the context of homeowner associations, but the statutes still requires that defendant be sued for an act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e).)

Defendants contend that the complaint “centers on the Association’s decision to levy a special assessment on all homeowners for the fees associated with exterior repairs of one home within the Association.” (Mot. at p. 10.) However, Defendants cite no case for the proposition that a levy of a special assessment is an “act in furtherance of their free speech or petition rights.” Thus, while the HOA’s decision about assessing fees may be a matter of public interest, Defendants have not met the first element of ‘speech or petition’ and jump right to the assertion that, if the Defendants did exercise their free speech or petition rights, that speech would be a matter of public-interest. Thus, the Defendants have not met their burden to show that the complaint arises from protected activity.

2. Second-Prong Analysis 

Because the Defendants have not met the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis, the Court need not address whether Plaintiff would prevail on the merits.

However, the Court agrees that it is improper for Brett Stettner, in propria persona, to represent the Estate of Suzi G. Stettner in this action. (Mot., p. 6.) Outside the probate context, a personal representative, such as an executor, must have counsel to prosecute claims on behalf of the estate. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 6125; see also, Estate of Sanchez (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 331, 340.) 

III. Conclusion

Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 is DENIED.

The Court will set an Order to Show Cause why the claims of the Estate of Stettner should not be dismissed for failure to have legal representation, to be heard on December 16, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.
