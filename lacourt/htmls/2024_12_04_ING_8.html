I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On November 5, 2023, Plaintiff, DSG International, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, General Motors, LLC, and DOES 1 through 50. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2 (d); (2) Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3); (4) Breach of Express Warranty – Civil Code section 1791.2(a), 1794; and (5) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability – Civil Code section 1791.1, section 1794.

 

This action has since settled in favor of Plaintiff who obtained a $159,115.34 settlement including: (1) GM buying back the vehicle for the full amount provided by law ($70,048.55); (2) GM paying Plaintiff civil penalty damages ($56,951.45); and (3) GM having to pay Plaintiff prejudgment interest ($32,115.34).

 

Now, Plaintiff states the only issue left for this court to decide is whether the $53,989.50 in attorney’s fees are reasonable under the circumstances. Thus, Plaintiff has filed this Motion for Attorney’s Fees. Defendant GM has also filed a Motion to Tax Costs.

B.    Procedural

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion for Attorney’s Fees. On November 19, 2024, GM filed an opposition brief. On November 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.   On September 5, 2024, GM filed a Motion to Tax Costs. On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On November 26, 2024, GM filed a reply brief.

 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE:

 

            Along with Plaintiff’s moving papers, Plaintiff also requested this court take judicial notice of the following court orders of unpublished cases:

 

1.     An October 6, 2017 Fee Order in the matter of Fuller v. FCA US LLC (L.A. Super. Ct. Case No. BC556964), attached as Exhibit 3 to the Declaration of Joshua D. Fennell.

2.     An August 8, 2018 Fee Order in the matter of Caplan v. FCA US LLC (L.A. Super. Ct. Case No. BC580793), attached as Exhibit 4 to the Declaration of Joshua D. Fennell.

III. ANALYSIS¿

A.    Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees

 

Here, Plaintiff asks for attorney’s fees of $53,989.50 and submits the declaration of its counsel, Joshua D. Fennell (“Fennell Decl.”) in support of its motion. Per Fennell’s declaration, the firm took Plaintiff’s case on a contingency bases, and thus, the fees incurred in litigation were advanced by the firm and recovery of the fees incurred was contingent upon a successful resolution of the case, emphasizing that if Plaintiff’s case would have lost, the firm would not have recovered attorneys’ fees. (Fennell Decl., ¶ 213.) Exhibit 16 of Fennell’s declaration includes the schedule of fees on this case totaling in $48,989.50 in attorneys’ fees and costs. (Fennell Decl., ¶ 210, 212, Exhibit 16.) Fennell has also accounted for an anticipated additional fee of $5,000 for review of opposition, motion reply, and attendance at the hearing. (Fennell Decl., ¶ 211-212.)

 

                                   i.          Legal Standard

 

Under the Song-Beverly Act, Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d), “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” Since the California Supreme Court has held that the lodestar adjustment method is the prevailing rule for statutory attorney fee awards to be applied in the absence of clear legislative intent to the contrary (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1135–1136), the lodestar method of calculating fees is appropriate in motions for attorney’s fees under Civil Code section 1794. (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 818.)

 

“[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….” (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 140, 154.) In making a lodestar calculation of proper hourly rates to apply, “the court may rely on its own knowledge and familiarity with the legal market, as well as the experience, skill, and reputation of the attorney requesting fees (Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 1009), the difficulty or complexity of the litigation to which that skill was applied (Syers Properties [III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 700) . . . and affidavits from other attorneys regarding prevailing fees in the community and rate determinations in other cases. (Heritage, at p. 1009.)” (569 East County Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc. (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 426, 437.) The moving party plaintiff bears the burden of showing that the fees incurred were “allowable,” were “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation,” and were “reasonable in amount.” (Morris v. Hyundai Motor America (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 34; Levy v. Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 807, 816.)The trial court in Levy found that some submitted charges were duplicative, others were for unnecessary or unsuccessful motions, or for travel time. There was no abuse of discretion in the pruning of the requested hours for such items. (Id.)

 

“The verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.” (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal. App. 4th 359, 396.) If the motion is supported by evidence, the opposing party must respond with specific evidence showing that the fees are unreasonable. (Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys. v. California Ins. Guarantee Ass’n (2008) 163 Cal. App. 4th 550, 560–63.) The Court has discretion to reduce fees that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time. (Horsford, supra, at 395.) “[T]he contingent and deferred nature of the fee award in a civil rights or other case with statutory attorney fees requires that the fee be adjusted in some manner to reflect the fact that the fair market value of legal services provided on that basis is greater than the equivalent noncontingent hourly rate. (Ketchum v. Moses, supra, 24 Cal.4th at pp. 1132–1133.) “ ‘A lawyer who both bears the risk of not being paid and provides legal services is not receiving the fair market value of his work if he is paid only for the second of these functions. If he is paid no more, competent counsel will be reluctant to accept fee award cases.’ ” (Id. at p. 1133, quoting with approval from Leubsdorf, The Contingency Factor in Attorney Fee Awards (1981) 90 Yale L.J. 473, 480.) The contingency adjustment may be made at the lodestar phase of the court's calculation or by applying a multiplier to the noncontingency lodestar calculation (but not both). (Ketchum v. Moses, supra, 24 Cal.4th at pp. 1133–1134.)” (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 394-395.) Plaintiff was designated by the parties in the settlement as the prevailing party. Therefore, attorney fees may and will be awarded.

Although this is true at the trial court level, Plaintiff appears to be misrepresenting what the Court of Appeal actually decided, which was whether the trial court abused its discretion in awarding fees without considering the contingency fee agreement. (Id. at 1113.)

The Reynolds Court did not determine whether $500 was a reasonable hourly rate. Instead, the Court of Appeal acknowledged that “[t]he trial court acted well within its discretion in using ‘the prevailing market value in the community for similar legal services’ (see PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095), relying on its personal knowledge [based on the trial court’s over forty years of experience in the legal field in Mendocino and Sonoma Counties] and familiarity with the area legal services, as the “ ‘touchstone’ for determination” of the reasonable hourly rates.” (Reynolds, supra, 47 Cal.App.5th at 1114 citing Serrano v. Unruh  (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 643.) As the Attorneys for the Parties in this case may be aware, this Court has very extensive experience of over 33 years as a lawyer (with an additional nearly 10 years as a judge in Lemon Law litigation and is very aware from other fee motions in other cases what the prevailing hourly rates are for Plaintiff and defense firms in this area. Even in the absence of Mr. Fennell’s recitation of fee awards by other trial counsel in Los Angeles County and the published fee rates in numerous cases over the years, this Court has kept abreast of hourly rates not only in Song-Beverly litigation but also in other less and more complicated areas of practice. The Court finds that the reasonable hourly rates for Mr. Fennell is $400.

B.    Defendant GM’s Motion to Tax Costs

 

                                   i.          Legal Standard

The non-prevailing party may file a motion to strike or tax costs. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b).) Procedurally, “[a]ny notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum.” (Ibid.) “Unless the objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion to strike or tax costs must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(2).) “Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) 

 

            During the hearing, “the verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.) “This procedure provides an orderly and efficient way of placing disputed costs at issue on a line item basis.” (612 South LLC v. Laconic Ltd. Partnership (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1285.) 

 

            “Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 sets forth various categories of recoverable costs.  Some of the costs include filing, motion, and jury fees (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1)) and service of process by a public officer, registered process server, or other means (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(4)). Section 1033.5 also explains that if an item is not mentioned in this section, these items may be allowed or denied in the court’s discretion. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(4).) “Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary. Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.” (Foothill-De Anza Community College Dist. v. Emerich (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 11, 29-30.) 

 

                                  ii.          Discussion

GM asserts that Plaintiff’s Counsel’s firm has demanded $4,069.13 in costs for a Song-Beverly matter that did not go to trial or raise any complex or novel issues. Further, the total Counsel has proffered in its memorandum of costs includes $1,433.91 that GM argues is without merit. GM has requested an order from this Court taxing costs for several items it asserts are unreasonable, unnecessary, and for which Plaintiff is unentitled to under California law. With respect to the mentioned $1,433.91, GM states Plaintiff has failed to carry its burden in demonstrating the following expenses were actually or reasonably incurred:

·       $166.79 for “Jury fees” (See Memorandum of Costs, Worksheet, p. 1, line 2.) This case never went to trial and GM should therefore not be billed for jury fees. The Court should therefore strike these costs entirely.

o   DENIED. Despite this case not going to trial prior to settling, Plaintiff nonetheless had to pay a jury fee deposit in order to reserve Plaintiff’s right to a jury trial in this jurisdiction.

·       $12.34 in “Notice of Change of Address” costs. (Worksheet, p. 2, line 1e.) Such costs are considered business overhead costs and are in no way related to.

o   GRANTED. Irrespective of the California Rules of Court mandatory requirement, Plaintiff can cite to no portion of California Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 that would warrant this being an allowable cost.

·       $744.80 for the “Reply Courtesy Copy”, “MIL Courtesy Copies”, “Opposition Courtesy Copies”, “Courtesy Copies for Jury Instructions” and “Courtesy Copies for Trial Filings”(Worksheet, p. 4, Attachment 1g.) Such copies are by definition unnecessary. Because these copies were not required, GM should be forced to pay Counsel’s decision to incur the unnecessary expense.

o   GRANTED. Unless Plaintiff can show that GM specifically requested courtesy copies, the Court does not allow for Plaintiff to collect this cost.

·       $509.98 in service of process costs related to serving the dealership deposition and trial subpoenas. (Id.; Worksheet, p. 5, Attachment 5(d).) Again, these dealerships are non-parties with their own counsel and are not controlled by GM. Moreover, none of this work by Counsel advanced Plaintiffs’ case as the dealership employees were never deposed and the case settled before trial. As such, Counsel should not recover any of these costs.

o   DENIED. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(a)(4), costs related to service are allowable.

As such, the Court GRANTS GM’s Motion to Tax Cost in part and DENIES it in part, resulting in a reduction of $757.14, for a net of $3,212 in costs awarded.

A.    Legal Standard

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.” If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) ¿ 

"The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence." (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party's subjective intent to create burden or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to respond. (See West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, no such evidence is necessary where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)

B.    Discussion

 

                                   i.          Meet and Confer

 

On September 17, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel states he sent a meet and confer letter to Defense counsel. (Declaration of Andrew J. Dickson (“Dickson Decl.”), ¶ 8.) Dickson contends that on September 23, 2024, the parties entered into a stipulated protective order in this matter . (Dickson Decl., ¶ 9, Exhibit 7.) Dickson also states the parties continued to meet and confer about their discovery dispute since that time, but ultimately determined that some of the issues could not be resolved without recourse to this court. (Dickson Decl., ¶ 10, Exhibit 8.)

 

GM’s opposition briefs argue that Plaintiffs did not meet and confer in good faith as their communications did not provide substantive reasoning or analysis, declared Plaintiffs’ requests valid, and declared GM’s objections invalid, and demanded that GM produce all manner of documents that had nothing to do with this case. However, the meet and confer efforts appear to be in good faith. Defendant does not identify any specific issue with these efforts besides maybe the fact that Defense Counsel’s declaration stating that “instead of continuing in a good faith attempt to resolve this dispute, Plaintiffs filed the present Motion to Compel on October 1, 2024.” (Declaration of Natalie Keshishian (“Keshishian Decl.”), ¶ 8.) The court finds this stance to be curious, specifically because Defense Counsel admits to Plaintiffs’ Counsel’s September 27, 2024 communication requesting an additional two weeks to file this motion past the October 1, 2024 deadline but fails to state whether this request was granted or even acknowledged by Defense Counsel at the time. (Keshishian Decl., ¶ 7.) As such, Defense Counsel should not be surprised that Plaintiffs Counsel filed the motion on its deadline when there is no indication of a grant of the requested two-week extension.

 

The court finds that Plaintiffs’ meet and confer efforts were sufficient prior to filing this motion.

 

                                  ii.          Motion to Compel Further Requests for Production of Documents

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel GM’s Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, Plaintiff seeks further responses to RFP Nos. 25-28, 30, 31, and 36-45. The court rules on each as follows:

 

RFP No. 25: DISCUSS. Although the court agrees that the applicable warranty policy and procedure manual published by defendant and provided to its authorized repair facilities, within the State of California, for the year the lawsuit was filed would be relevant, this is not what is asked by RFP No. 25. As asked, the request seeks documents which evidence a GM program or system to identify vehicles (generally) that have been presented to its authorized repair facilities on more than one occasion. Is there any such system or program applicable to California vehicles?  Does GM have a program in its warranty reimbursement system to “flag” vehicles that have multiple repair visits for the same symptom or complaint?  A verified written response stating that there is or is not such a program or system would resolve the discovery dispute, but that is not what GM provided here.   

 

RFP Nos. 26-28: DISCUSS.  RFP 26 expands RFP 25’s request to include a system or program in place for GM to identify vehicles subject to a manufacturer’s affirmative obligation to buy back a vehicle.  The scope of the RFP seems overly broad, in that it is not limited to vehicles sold or leased in California, or limited in time to the period before this lawsuit was filed, but GM’s response does not indicate whether there is or is not any such system or program even with these limitations.  RFP 27 seeks documents indicating GM’s compliance with the Song-Beverly Act, but it does not narrow its scope to a written guideline to decision makers in California, or a policy statement that GM acknowledges or directs employees to affirmative offer a refund or replacement when certain parameters are met.  As drafted, the RFP seems overly broad but the general concept appears to warrant a verified response of ability or inability to comply with the production of some policy statement.     As for RFP 28, how can a party produce a document that evidences an unwritten policy?  Plaintiff should clarify this in oral argument. 

 

RFP Nos. 30-31: DISCUSS.  Although RFP 30 includes a time period of documents for which it seeks, the time period should be limited to the time before this lawsuit was filed.  RFP 30 appears to the Court to seek any guidelines, policy statement, or protocol for how in general GM evaluates a customer request for a buyback, i.e., steps GM is to take to evaluate and respond to a consumer’s telephonic or emailed, or written request for a Song-Beverly buy-back.  That seems to the Court to be a reasonable topic as to which GM either does or does not have a responsive document.   RFP 31 seem to seek a warranty policies and procedures manual, not as to any specific repair complaint but in general.  Such documents should be identified and produced in a further response. 

 

1.     RFP Nos. 36-37, 39, 41, 43, 45: DISCUSS.  RFP 36 seeks “investigation” documents as to a customer complaint of a temperature gauge fluctuation, i.e., internal, technical literature reflecting an evaluation of such a complaint.  Responsive documents might reasonably include a repair procedure, diagnostic trouble-shooting guide, ro similar documents as to the same make, model, and model year as Plaintiff’s vehicle.  A TSC or ISB on the subject if one exists should be identified and produced.  RFP 37 seeks document identifying other customers whose same make, model, and model year vehicle who made a complaint about temperature gauge fluctuations either by a warranty complaint, lawsuit, or buy-back request.  Other customers’ complaints similar to the alleged defects claimed by plaintiff, limited to vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make and model of the subject Model year 2018 Chevy Colorado vehicle are discoverable, such as from GM’s warranty reimbursement system.  The Court typically discusses with counsel the type of documents that may be required and the format for a production of documents such as in a sortable Excel spreadsheet that lists a compilation or summary of much more voluminous documents.  The spreadsheet typically would contain the repair date, the VIN last 6 of the other customers’ vehicle, the servicing dealer by name, location and/or dealer code, a description of the customer’s complaint as listed in the warranty claim submission (i.e., fluctuation of the temperature gauge), the determined cause if any of that complaint or whether no problem or cause was found, and the correction, repair, or replacement performed if any.

a.      The Court intends to narrow the scope of permissible discovery of other MY 2018 Chevy Colorado vehicles’ customer complaints as noted above.  Thus, the volume of customer repair files to be reviewed will be only the MY 2018 Chevy Colorado’s sold or leased in California, a much smaller and less burdensome amount than the national total of all MY 2018 Chevy Colorado vehicles distributed in the United States.  The Court will NOT order GM to identify the other customers by name, address, telephone number or any other PII.  Any such compilation will be subject to a Discovery Protective Order in this case, and the custodian or other person who generates the compilation may need to be identified if the parties cannot stipulate to the use and admissibility of the chart.

b.     The Court will not order production of ESI as to other California customers’ complaints of repair issues that occurred on a single occasion in the plaintiff’s repair history.  Merely because the Plaintiff experienced a repair issue does not make discovery of every other California customer’s records for that repair issue reasonable when considering the needs of the litigation, the importance of that issue to the issues disputed in this litigation, the amount in controversy, and the costs of providing the broader scope of discovery sought by Plaintiff. 

 

RFP Nos. 38, 40, 42, 44: DISCUSS. Although the court typically finds relevant, any internal analysis and/or investigation regarding the primary or other recurring alleged defects claimed by plaintiff in her/his vehicle, applicable to the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle. The requests are not limited as such. Instead, the request fails to limit request to the 2018 Chevy Colorado only.  If GM has a TSB or an ISB that mentions fluctuation of the temperature gauge applicable to a model year 2028 Colorado, such documents must be identified and produced.

 

                                iii.          Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One

 

SROG Nos. 12-14: GRANT. Even though Plaintiffs have not brought forth a class action lawsuit, SROG 12 simply seeks a number, i.e., the sale or lease figures for 2018 Chevy Colorado vehicles sold in California.  That is not a burdensome request.  As to whether GM has repurchased a California-sold 2018 Colorado because of a recurring complaint about fluctuation of a temperature gauge, the Court finds such an interrogatory to be reasonably calculated.  However, as phrased, SROGs13 and 14 are limited to the temperature gauge issue as the only basis for a buy-back, i.e., not a temperature gauge plus other repair complaint.   

 

SROG Nos. 15-28: DISCUSS in light of the Court’s rulings on SROGs 13-14. The Court will likely order the parties to meet and confer and return in 45 days for a contained hearing if the matters cannot be resolved informally.

III. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A protective order may issue under Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2017.020, 2025.420, 2030.090, 2031.060, and 2033.080. In each case, a protective order should be issued for good cause, such as to prevent unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, or undue burden and expense. Additionally, prior to making such a motion the moving party must attempt to informally resolve the issue and attach a declaration pursuant to CCP § 2016.040. 

 

A monetary sanction shall be imposed upon any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for protective order unless the court finds that the sanctioned party acted with substantial justification, or that the circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (h).) 

 

“ ‘[T]he issuance and formulation of protective orders are to a large extent discretionary”’ and a ruling on such motions will not be disturbed absent abuse of discretion. (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 316-17.)  

 

The burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for the order sought. (Id. at p. 318.)  

 

B.    Protective Order

 

At the previous October 28, 2024 hearing, this court continued this motion and ordered the parties to file a joint or separate statement detailing which documents categories at issue in this UD Action have not already had responsive documents produced in the Civil Action, and what RFPs remain at issue as to which of the 15 subject properties in this UD Action where none or insufficient documents have been produced already in the Civil Action. The parties have now filed a joint statement detailing the following:

 

Defendants maintain that the currently pending civil action concerns forty-three (43) properties, fifteen (15) of which are the same properties involved in this UD action. In that civil action, Defendants state they have produced 48,816 pages of documents, 493 of which consist of their prior production. Defendants also contend that the 48,816 pages of documents do not even take into account imbedded and downloadable files consisting of maintenance requests, text messages, and email communications, which account for approximately 2,420 pages. Defendants produce their post recent supplemental response as Exhibit D to the joint statement. Defendants argue that the Plaintiff in this UD action has served discovery, including RFPs of documents  against both Defendants, which consist of similar, and in many cases, identical requests for documents which have been produced in the civil action.

 

In Plaintiff’s portion of the joint statement, Plaintiff asserts that at the time Defendants filed this Motion for Protective Order, they had only produced approximately 500 pages of documents in the civil action, and represented to Judge Fujie in the civil action, that they had produced all responsive documents. However, about a month after maintaining they had produced all responsive documents, Defendants produced the approximately 50,000 pages of additional responsive documents, many of which were the same documents counsel for Defendants represented did not exist. Thus, Plaintiff questions Defense Counsel’s unsupported and unsworn claims by counsel concerning the purported completeness of Defendants’ document production.

 

Plaintiff concedes the October 2024 production appears to produce some responsive documents to most of the UD Plaintiff’s RFP. However, despite this, Plaintiff argues that there are substantial omissions, specifically relating to financial records. For example, Plaintiff points to the bank statements that have been produced in the civil action (METICULOUS-048508 through METICULOUS-048816) and contends that they are heavily redacted. Plaintiff asserts that because of said redaction, the Defendants have not fully complied with the landlord’s requests. Moreover, Plaintiff also states the redacted bank statements appear to reference transfers from other accounts held by Defendants (accounts for which statements have not been produced. Based on this, Plaintiff suggests there is substantial reason to believe Defendants have selectively withheld subsets of responsive documents.

 

Next, Plaintiff argues that it has run into issues related to the highly irregular form of Defendants production, which appears to impede the location and review of documents. For example, Plaintiff states that Defendants, rather than producing documents as they are kept in the ordinary course of business, has been inserting documents into and between documents that have already been Bates-Stamped, making them effectively hidden from view. Plaintiff also asserts that Defendants have been repeatedly re-producing the same documents, thereby forcing the propounding party to review every document each time to ascertain whether any documents have been added, altered, or removed. As such, Plaintiff is concerned that Defendants are selectively withholding damaging documents and seeking to avoid providing a verified statement of compliance.

 

Based on the above, Plaintiff argues that given Defendants’ repeated representations of “[f]ull compliance,” there are no possible grounds for any protective order, and Defendants motion should be denied as moot. Further, Plaintiff requests that Defendants should be ordered to serve code-compliant supplemental responses to the Request for Production of Documents no later than three (3) days from the date of the Court’s order on the motion. Plaintiff further concedes that any documents previously produced to undersigned counsel in either action do not need to be re-produced.

 

Here, the court requests oral argument as to why Defendants’ Counsel has been reluctant to provide a statement of compliance pursuant to the California Code of Civil Procedure. Moreover, the detailed review of the alleged responsive documents produced versus “Landlord’s response” provide that much of the production is not per se responsive to the specific request. Defendants are required to provide oral argument as to each of these issues. The court also requires oral argument on why the subsequent production of almost 50,000 pages of documents does not moot Defendants’ Motion for Protective Order. As production in the civil action appears to overturn more responsive documents, the court is reluctant to grant a protective order in this action.

 

C.    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories, Set One

 

In addition to the issues regarding the continued hearing on protective order above, Plaintiff has also filed a Motion to Compel Defendants’ Further Responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

                          i.          Legal Standard

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

"The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence." (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party's subjective intent to create burden or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to respond. (See West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, no such evidence is necessary where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)

                        ii.          Form Interrogatories – Unlawful Detainer

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks Defendants’ further responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 70.13, 71.3, and 72.3.

 

Form Interrogatory No. 70.13: GRANTED. Subsection (a) of this request asks for the name, address, telephone number, and birthdate of the human beings currently occupying the Premises, Meticulous identified the two named defendants in this action. However, the two Meticulous entities are limited liability companies and not capable of “occupying” a residential apartment. This response does not answer the posited question in subsection (a). The court overrules the objections as to overbreadth based on timing, as the request does not ask for such information for an unspecified amount of time. As framed, the interrogatory seeks current occupants. In fact, the only objection that may be relevant to this interrogatory is that of privacy of third parties, however, if this were the case, Defendants would be required to file a protective order outlining that said information was protected. However, because this case involves the potential and alleged illegal home-sharing business, information regarding tenants is presumptively relevant in proving or disproving whether Defendants were engaged in said business on Plaintiff’s property.

 

Form Interrogatory No. 71.3: AGRUE. The information sought in this interrogatory would aid in proving or disproving Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendants were engaged in the illegal home-sharing business. Again, the objections asserted are unmeritorious. For example, Defendants object on the grounds that the request is “overly broad, indefinite as to time and without reasonable limitation in scope,” despite the request clearly requesting information for the 12 months leading up to the notice to quit. Rent payments from Defendants would be particularly relevant in understanding the alleged “oral periodic lease agreement” Defendant claim to have had with the former owner in or about 2020.

 

            However, the court observes that Defendants produced rent payments made by Defendants to owner as well as payments received by Defendants. As such, the court seeks oral argument from Plaintiff on what it perceives to be missing from this production.

 

Form Interrogatory 72.3, subsection (a): GRANTED. Like with Form Interrogatory No. 70.13, this interrogatory requests the “name of the person who received” the Notice to Quit. Meticulous states that “occupants of the rental units and representatives of Meticulous Services, LLC received the notices” but does not identify those occupants and representatives. The court again does not find the majority of the objections to be meritorious and overrules them. For the same reasons as Form Interrogatory No. 70.13, the court GRANTS this request.

 

                      iii.          Special Interrogatories

 

As to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, set one, Plaintiff seeks an order from this court compelling further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 7 and 8. Plaintiff argues that further responses are required to be provided to both of these interrogatories on three grounds: (1) First, Plaintiff asserts the responses do not include verified statements attesting that the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230 have been met; (2) Second, Plaintiff contends the Defendants have not identified documents from which it can ascertain the requesting information, including but not limited to, the identity or identities of the actual or intended recipients of the payments – making the responses unclear and confusing. Like with the above issue regarding the protective order and RFPs, Plaintiff argues the documents Defendants identify do not contain the requested information; and (3) Lastly, Plaintiff states Defendant have not fully produced the underlying financial records from which its summary document was prepared – which would include the full, unredacted bank records, wire transfer receipts, and check receipts, among other things. Because this court is requiring Defense Counsel – in connection with the protective order and RFPs at issue – to present oral argument, the court will also get a clearer picture as to its ruling on this motion after said oral argument.

 

                       iv.          Sanctions

 

In connection with Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form and Special Interrogatories, Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions as imposed against Defendants in the amount of $3,240. This amount is based on Plaintiff’s Counsel, Daniel E. French’s declaration (“French Decl.”) which states his hourly rate is $540, and that he spent no less than 6 hours preparing the motion and supporting documents. The court will decide sanctions after hearing oral argument in connection with the issues identified by this court in its analysis above. However, the court does acknowledge that the claimed $540 hourly rate requires more foundation such as years of practice, expertise in this area of the law, etc.  Also, six (6) hours spent on a general discovery motion with only five interrogatories at issue appears to be excessive. As such, if the court grants sanctions, it will be in a lowered amount.
