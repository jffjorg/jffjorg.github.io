Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

L.O., et al. v. Manhattan Beach Unified School District, et al.

            20STCV02458

1.      Manhattan Beach Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication of Issues

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

 Manhattan Beach Unified School District’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication of Issues is denied.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff L.O., through his guardian ad litem Jules Laird, filed his Complaint on January 21, 2020. Plaintiff’s operative Third Amended Complaint was filed on January 18, 2022. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Minor Plaintiff, L.O., who has a disability, alleges that an instructional aide (“(Debra) Jane Doe”) employed at Jonas Salk Elementary School (“Salk”), a school within the Hawthorne Unified School District (“HUSD”), committed acts of physical assault upon Plaintiff on January 23, 2019. Plaintiff alleges that Manhattan Beach Unified School District (“MBUSD”) is his home district charged with providing him with a free and appropriate special needs education because his parents reside within the jurisdictional boundaries of MBUSD. Plaintiff alleges that pursuant to an agreement and master contracts among MBUSD, Salk, and HUSD, MBUSD enrolled L.O. to Salk. Individual Defendants Michael D. Matthews (“Matthews”), Megan Locklear (“Locklear”), and Michelle Sumner (“Sumner”) were former or current employees of MBUSD. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Negligence; 2. Negligent Hiring, Training, and Supervision; 3. IIED.

 

MBUSD’s demurrer to the third amended complaint was overruled, in part, and sustained, in part. The demurrer to the first cause of action was overruled and the demurrer to the second and third causes of action was sustained with leave to amend. However, Plaintiff did not amend the second and third causes of action.

 

Objections

 

Defendant’s objections 1 to 5 are sustained.

 

Motion for Summary Judgment and/or Adjudication


The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

 

“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; CCP § 437c(c).)

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment as to the Third Amended Complaint on the grounds that there is no triable issue of material fact, and that Defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Alternatively, Defendant moves for summary adjudication of each of the following purported issues: 1. “Plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence against MBUSD has no merit because Plaintiff cannot establish one or more elements of this cause of action.” 2. “Plaintiff’s cause of action against MBUSD has no merit because Plaintiff cannot establish that an act or omission of MBUSD caused his injuries.”  The Court notes that these are not really causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty that are proper “issues” for adjudication under CCP § 437c(f)(1), but instead simply grounds for adjudicating the sole cause of action for negligence remaining against Defendant. The first “issue” simply states that an unspecified element or elements of the cause of action cannot be established. The second “issue” narrows down that element and specifies the element as causation.

 

            First Cause of Action for Negligence

 

As to the first cause of action, the elements of a cause of action for negligence are duty, breach, causation, and damages. Johnson v. Prasad (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 74, 78. School districts and their employees have a duty to supervise their students, and either a lack of supervision or ineffective supervision can constitute a breach of the duty of care.  See, C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 869-70.

 

“The determination whether a duty exists is an issue of law.”  Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1369.  Defendant has not met its burden to show that no duty exists as a matter of law.  Thus, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to show the existence of a triable issue exists as to the issue of Defendant’s duty.  CCP § 437c(p)(2).

 

Gov. Code, § 895 states:

“As used in this chapter “agreement” means a joint powers agreement entered into pursuant to Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 6500) of Division 7 of Title 1 of the Government Code, an agreement to transfer the functions of a public entity or an employee thereof to another public entity pursuant to Part 2 (commencing with Section 51300) of Division 1 of Title 5 of the Government Code, and any other agreement under which a public entity undertakes to perform any function, service or act with or for any other public entity or employee thereof with its consent, whether such agreement is expressed by resolution, contract, ordinance or in any other manner provided by law; but “agreement” does not include an agreement between public entities which is designed to implement the disbursement or subvention of public funds from one of the public entities to the other, whether or not it provides standards or controls governing the expenditure of such funds.”

 

Gov. Code, § 895.2 states, in relevant part:

“Whenever any public entities enter into an agreement, they are jointly and severally liable upon any liability which is imposed by any law other than this chapter upon any one of the entities or upon any entity created by the agreement for injury caused by a negligent or wrongful act or omission occurring in the performance of such agreement.”

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that MBUSD and HUSD “entered into an agreement wherein HUSD, rather than MBUSD, performed the functions, services, and acts necessary to provide L.O. the special education and services specified by MBUSD in L.O.’s IEP.”  (TAC, ¶ 5.)  Thus, Plaintiff alleges that an agreement was made pursuant to Gov. Code § 895, and that pursuant to Gov. Code § 895.2, the public entities are jointly and severally liable upon any liability which is imposed upon any of the public entities based on the acts and omission of the employees of the public entities. (TAC, ¶ 26.)

 

In Ross v. Campbell Union School Dist. (1977) 70 Cal.App.3d 113, the County superintendents of schools entered into written agreements with other superintendents for the formation of a science and conservation education program. The Court held that Defendants were jointly liable when a student was injured because of the negligence of a supervising teacher in the program. Id. The facts are similar in the instant action wherein Plaintiff has alleged that MBUSD and HUSD participated in a program pursuant to a contractual agreement in which students who resided in the MBUSD boundaries were placed at HUSD schools. (TAC, ¶¶ 20-21.)  Defendant does not deny the existence of a contractual agreement, but instead simply argues that any liability and resulting damages pursuant to the agreement would be governed via indemnification or contribution rather than directly to Plaintiff.

 

As stated above, Defendant argues that there is no private right of action belonging to the Plaintiff but that the relevant statute only provides a basis for indemnification between the public entities. However, there is no authority that definitively establishes this argument. Defendant argues that the “liability imposed by any law other than this chapter” must be read to presume that this other law must form a basis for liability against MBUSD rather than HUSD. However, the statute states that this other law can be the basis for liability against any one of the entities. The TAC is replete with statutory bases for liability and the basis for duties to be imposed against HUSD. (TAC, ¶¶ 5, 29, 39.)

 

Defendant cites to Authority for Calif. Cities Excess Liab. v. Los Altos (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1207, 1212 for the proposition that no joint and several liability is authorized pursuant to Gov. Code § 895.2. In Authority, the Court of Appeal held that the city of Los Altos’ police chief's execution of a document with other cities’ police chiefs to form special law enforcement teams did not bind the city to pay a pro rata share of the other cities’ settlement with heirs of a police officer killed during a joint training session. The holding was specifically based on the finding that the relevant document was not, in fact, an agreement between the public entities governed by Gov Code § 895.2 because the city council never considered or approved it.

 

Authority is distinguishable. Authority’s holding was based on the lack of an agreement and not upon a finding that the entities may only pursue indemnification through Gov. Code § 895.2. The holding does not explicitly state that there is no private right of action for the tort plaintiff as Defendant attempts to argue.  Authority’s reference to lack of “joint enterprise” liability is limited to that context.  There was no “joint enterprise” liability because of the lack of agreement under Section 895.2.  Any attempt to conflate this to state a definitive statement regarding lack of joint and several liability is misleading.

 

Similarly, another case cited by Defendant, Gonzales v. State of California (1972) 29 Cal.App.3d 585, is distinguishable. In Gonzales, a bus driver employed by a local school district was not considered a state employee even though the district's authority to employ drivers was derived from the state’s duty to educate children. The Court disregarded the plaintiff’s attempt to argue that Section 895.2 provided a statutory basis for liability. First, the Court stated that no statutory basis for liability under Section 895.2 based on an agreement was set forth in the pleadings. Second, the Court found that, even if it had been pleaded, no specific agreement was in place, but instead merely an implied agreement which is not within the governance of Section 895.2. Thus, Gonzales is clearly distinguishable on the facts.

 

In addition, the Court refers the parties to Gov. Code § 895.4 as well as the law revision comments to this section. “As part of any agreement, the public entities may provide for contribution or indemnification by any or all of the public entities that are parties to the agreement upon any liability arising out of the performance of the agreement.” Gov. Code, § 895.4. “This section permits public entities that are parties to an agreement to allocate the ultimate financial responsibility among themselves in whatever manner seems most desirable to them. The section does not affect the right of the injured person to recover the full amount of his damages from any one of the public entities under Section 895.2.” Gov. Code, § 895.4 (Law Revision Comments)(emphasis added).  Thus, while Section 895.2 provides that the agreement may provide a ground for contribution or indemnification between the public entities, the law revision comments make clear that this section allows the injured party to recover from any of the public entities.

 

In sum, Defendant fails to establish that Gov. Code § 895.2 does not provide a private right of action in favor of Plaintiff, but, instead, is merely a basis for indemnification between the public entities. Thus, Defendant does not meet its burden to show that no duty of care exists on the part of Defendant in favor of Plaintiff.

 

As to issue 2, which again as noted above, is not really a proper issue, but simply grounds for summary judgment based on a failure to meet the element of causation, the Court notes that in the memorandum of points and authorities Defendant argues that no protective measures could have prevented Plaintiff’s harm. The Court notes, however, that none of the separate statement of facts directly address this argument. Facts 1 to 10 specifically address the duty argument ruled upon above.  Facts 11 and 12 generally claim “no negligence” without specifying the causation element.  Fact 12 is not supported with any supporting evidence and can be quickly disregarded by the Court.  In any event, as to the causation element, Plaintiff has set forth facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to Defendant’s Facts 11 and 12.  (Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Separate Statement of Facts 11 and 12 and Supporting Evidence.)  In addition, the Court notes that adjudication of issue 2, under the arguments made by Defendant, appears to also be premised upon the Court determining that no liability exists under the agreement between the parties.  As the Court has ruled that Defendant did not meet its burden of persuasion as to the element of duty, Plaintiff can still pursue a basis for liability based on joint and several liability between the two school districts. Defendant has not established that there is no triable issue of material fact that the employee or agent of the contracting school district, HUSD, did not cause or contribute to Plaintiff’s injuries.

 

Therefore, for the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment, and alternative, motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Norma J. Paez v. Ford Motor Company, et al.

23TRCV02076

1.      Ford Motor Company’s Motion for an Order to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings 


TENTATIVE RULING

 

Ford Motor Company’s Motion for an Order Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings is denied.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on April 7, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff alleges that her 2020 Ford Explorer suffers from engine defects, infotainment system defects, body defects, transmission defects, and other non-conformities. Plaintiff set forth causes of action under the Song-Beverly Act as well as causes of action for Fraudulent Inducement-Concealment and Negligent Repair. The Court notes that while both Ford Motor Company and Airport Marina Ford filed this motion, Airport Marina Ford was dismissed on March 25, 2024.  

 

            Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant and Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice are granted pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d).

 

            Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            “California law reflects a strong public policy in favor of arbitration as a relatively quick and inexpensive method for resolving disputes.  [Citation.]  To further that policy, [Code of Civil Procedure] section 1281.2 requires a trial court to enforce a written arbitration agreement unless one of three limited exceptions applies.  [Citation.]  Those statutory exceptions arise where (1) a party waives the right to arbitration; (2) grounds exist for revoking the arbitration agreement; and (3) pending litigation with a third party creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on common factual or legal issues.  (§ 1281.2, subds. (a)–(c).)”  Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 967.

 

            “The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.”  Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.

 

            “The party opposing arbitration has the burden of demonstrating that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.  Nonetheless, this policy does not override ordinary principles of contract interpretation.  [T]he contractual terms themselves must be carefully examined before the parties to the contract can be ordered to arbitration:  Although [t]he law favors contracts for arbitration of disputes between parties, there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate.”  Rice v. Downs (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 1213, 1223 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

            In Rowe v. Exline (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1286, the Court of Appeal found that “a nonsignatory sued as an agent of a signatory may enforce an arbitration agreement.”  Id. at 1286.  In addition, “a nonsignatory who is the agent of a signatory can even be compelled to arbitrate claims against his will.”  Id. at 1285, citing Harris v. Superior Court (1986) 188 Cal.App.3d 475, 477–78.  Further, “in many cases, nonparties to arbitration agreements are allowed to enforce those agreements where there is sufficient identity of parties.”  Valley Casework, Inc. v. Comfort Construction, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 1013, 1021.  This includes nonparties as agents of a party as well as “a third party beneficiary of an arbitration agreement.”  Ibid. 

 

            “Under the doctrine of equitable estoppel, [...] a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are intimately founded in and intertwined with the underlying contract obligations. By relying on contract terms in a claim against a nonsignatory defendant, even if not exclusively, a plaintiff may be equitably estopped from repudiating the arbitration clause contained in that agreement. Where the equitable estoppel doctrine applies, the nonsignatory has a right to enforce the arbitration agreement.” Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486, 495–96 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

            Defendant moves for an order compelling arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims and for an order for stay pending completion of arbitration.  The motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §1281 et seq. and the FAA, on the grounds Plaintiff is bound by a written agreement to arbitrate the subject matter of the Complaint.  Defendant argues that a valid arbitration agreement exists between the parties that requires arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims. 

 

            Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 states, in relevant part: “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists[. . .]” “Generally, an arbitration agreement must be memorialized in writing. A party's acceptance of an agreement to arbitrate may be express, as where a party signs the agreement. A signed agreement is not necessary, however, and a party's acceptance may be implied in fact or be effectuated by delegated consent. An arbitration clause within a contract may be binding on a party even if the party never actually read the clause.” Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236 (internal citations omitted).

 

            Defendant attached the Retail Installment Sales Contract which may indicate the existence of a sales agreement between Plaintiff and an entity named Airport Marina Ford.  (Declarations of Yoseph Rixit, Doug Goodrow, Ex. A.)  However, the Court notes that the buyer’s name has been redacted in the agreement.  It does not appear that Plaintiff is contesting whether the sales agreement is genuine.

 

            Moving Defendant has failed to establish that it is a party to the arbitration clause or that equitable estoppel principles allow it to enforce the arbitration provision.  As noted above, binding authorities exist to indicate that a non-signatory may enforce an arbitration provision.  However, no competent evidence was submitted by moving party to indicate that it is an agent of Airport Marina Ford, a third-party beneficiary of Airport Marina Ford, or that other circumstances exist, such as an identity of parties, to show that moving party can enforce the arbitration clause.  The only evidence that was attached to the declarations was the above-referenced sales agreement.

 

            Defendant attempts to rely on the equitable estoppel principle delineated in Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486.  However, here, Defendant has failed to provide any facts or evidence to show that the causes of action against it are intimately founded and intertwined with the underlying contract obligation.  Only conclusory arguments, set forth in the Memorandum of Points and Authorities, were provided, with no underlying evidence.  Further, in Felisilda it was actually the co-defendant dealership which moved to compel arbitration and also sought to include FCA in the arbitration within its own motion.  The Court notes that in the instant action Airport Marina Ford was a co-defendant and co-moving party in this action.  However, on March 25, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Airport Marina Ford.  “A plaintiff's voluntary dismissal of his action has the effect of an absolute withdrawal of his claim and leaves the defendant as though he had never been a party.” Cook v. Stewart McKee & Co. (1945) 68 Cal.App.2d 758, 761.  There are no facts or evidence to show that the moving party herein can enforce an arbitration clause which is in the name of a completely different entity.

 

            In addition, on April 4, 2023, in Ford Motor Warranty Cases (Cal. Ct. App., Apr. 4, 2023, No. B312261) 2023 WL 2768484, and on June 26, 2023, in Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (Cal. Ct. App., June 26, 2023, No. B320477) 2023 WL 4181909, the Court of Appeal declined to follow Felisilda, holding that equitable estoppel did not apply to the non-signatory manufacturer because the Plaintiffs’ claims against the manufacturer were not founded in or intertwined with the sales contract, and the manufacturer was not a third party beneficiary to the contract.  Even more recently another California Court of Appeals decision weighed in on Ford Motor and Montemayor. “Whether a manufacturers’ express or implied warranties that accompany a vehicle at the time of sale constitute obligations arising from the sale contract, permitting manufacturers to enforce an arbitration agreement in the contract pursuant to equitable estoppel is a question now pending before our Supreme Court. In the meantime, we agree with Montemayor and Ford Motor that they do not.” Kielar v. Superior Court (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, 621.

 

            The Court notes that the California Supreme Court has granted review in both Ford Motor Warranty Cases and Montemayor as well as Kielar. The instant Court cites these cases for its persuasive analysis only. Further, as noted above, even had the instant Court chosen to follow Felisilda, Defendant still did not meet its burden to show that the arbitration could be enforced as to it for the reasons noted above.

 

            As the moving party failed to meet its initial burden to show the existence of a valid arbitration clause between the parties, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to show that the arbitration clause should not be enforced.  Rice, supra, 247 Cal.App.4th at 1223.

 

            Therefore, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Action is denied.

 

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
