DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02343    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Defendant Philip Godlewski

Responding Party: Plaintiff Christine McCaslin

Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §425.16

The court considered the moving papers, opposition and reply.

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §425.16 is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Christine McCaslin is a hair stylist who owns a business in Los Angeles County known as Salon Touché. Plaintiff alleges Defendant Philip Godlewski posted her contact information and her business information to his over 300,000 Telegram social media followers and encouraged them to leave negative reviews about Salon Touché and harass Plaintiff personally. As a result of Defendant Godlewski’s Telegram posts, Plaintiff has received more than 1,000 negative reviews on Yelp! and Google. Plaintiff alleges Godlewski’s followers also

made negative posts on her Facebook and Instagram business accounts. Plaintiff alleges her business phone number has become essentially unusable due to the number of non-customers using the number to harass her instead of booking an appointment. Plaintiff has also personally received handwritten threats to her person at her place of business.

On July 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Godlewski alleging a single cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. On September 6, 2023, Godlewski filed the Special Motion to Strike (“anti-SLAPP motion”) currently before this Court. On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the anti-SLAPP motion. On April 16, 2024, Defendant filed a reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (CCP §425.16(b)(1).

“As used in this section, ‘act in furtherance of a person's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.” (CCP §425.16(e).)

“Litigation of an anti-SLAPP motion involves a two-step process. First, the moving defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged allegations or claims arise from protected activity in which the defendant has engaged. Second, for each claim that does arise from protected activity, the plaintiff must show the claim has “at least ‘minimal merit. If the plaintiff cannot make this showing, the court will strike the claim.” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1009 (where moving party seeks to strike an entire cause of action alleging multiple factual bases, court does not determine whether 1st prong is met based on

“gravamen” test but must determine whether each factual bases supplies the element of claim or merely provides context).) Bonni expressly rejected the “gravamen” test, which had been applied in the past. (Bonni, supra, 11 Cal.5th at 1009.)

On the first step, “courts are to consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability. The defendant's burden is to identify what acts each challenged claim rests on and to show how those acts are protected under a statutorily defined category of protected activity.” (Bonni, supra, 11 Cal.5th at 1009.)

Once defendant demonstrates that a cause of action arises from protected conduct, the burden shifts to plaintiff on the second step to demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited. (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 88-89.) “The second prong of the statute deals with whether the plaintiff has “demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim. Under section 425.16, subdivision (b)(2), the superior court, in making these determinations, considers ‘the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.’ For purposes of an anti-SLAPP motion, the court considers the pleadings and evidence submitted by both sides, but does not weigh credibility or compare the weight of the evidence. Rather, the court's responsibility is to accept as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff. A plaintiff need only establish that his or her claim has minimal merit to avoid being stricken as a SLAPP. With these descriptions in mind, we will not strike a cause of action under the anti-SLAPP statute unless it lacks even minimal merit.” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 261.)

The “probability of prevailing” is tested by the same standard governing a motion for summary judgment, nonsuit, or directed verdict. Thus, in opposing a SLAPP motion, it is plaintiff's burden to make a prima facie showing of facts that would support a judgment in plaintiff's favor.” (Taus v. Loftus (2007) 40 Cal.4th 683, 714 (a “summary-judgment-like procedure”).)

“Precisely because the statute (1) permits early intervention in lawsuits alleging unmeritorious causes of action that implicate free speech concerns, and (2) limits opportunity to conduct discovery, the plaintiff's burden of establishing a probability of prevailing is not high: We do not weigh credibility, nor do we evaluate the weight of the evidence. Instead, we accept

as true all evidence favorable to the plaintiff and assess the defendant's evidence only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff's submission as a matter of law. Only a cause of action that lacks ‘even minimal merit' constitutes SLAPP.” (Overstock.com, Inc. v. Gradient Analytics, Inc. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 688, 699.) “In order to establish a probability of prevailing on a cause of action in the context of an anti-SLAPP motion, a plaintiff must state and substantiate a legally sufficient claim.” (La Jolla Group II v. Bruce (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 461, 470.)

DISCUSSION

I. First Prong—Defendant Godlewski fails to establish that the complaint arises from protected conduct under CCP §425.16(e)(3)

A. Parties’ Positions

According to Godlewski, the complaint for IIED arises from protected conduct, because the conduct that supplies the “outrageous conduct” element of Plaintiff’s IIED claim are posts and statements he made on Telegram, a public forum and those statements were in connection with an issue of public interest.

Godlewski maintains his statements were in connection with an issue of public interest for several reasons. Godlewski argues he is an issue of public interest, because he has 300,000 Telegram followers, multiple “hate groups” that formed against him and tens of millions of views on his content in any given month. Godlewski contends McCaslin attacked him prior to his “doxxing” her by claiming to have insider knowledge that Godlewski was a pedophile, committed tax fraud and other wrongful acts.

Godlewski argues his statements were also in connection with an issue of public interest, because McCaslin’s statements attacked Godlewski and his followers. Finally, Godlewski argues there is a widespread interest in directing purchasing power to companies with shared values. Godlewski maintains his statements directed his followers to inform the public about what type of person McCaslin was and to discourage members of the public from patronizing her business.

In response, McCaslin argues her complaint arises from a private dispute between herself and Godlewski. McCaslin argues Godlewski 300,000 Telegram followers and his status as a well-known “Q-Anon” conspiracy theorist does not establish that everything he says or does is a matter of public interest. McCaslin argues Godlewski cannot establish that the statement or

conduct alleged against him could directly affect a large number of persons beyond the direct participants.

McCaslin argues the statements were also not in connection with the topic of boycotting and patronizing business based on shared values. McCaslin argues any connection between Godlewski’s doxxing and the general public interest in boycotting or patronizing businesses as a reflection of personal values is remote.

McCaslin argues the statements alleged against Godlewski are not tied to any issue of public interest. McCaslin maintains her action is based on a personal dispute between two individuals that is unrelated to any topic of public interest.

On reply, Godlewski restates his position that McCaslin’s claim arises from protected conduct under the anti-SLAPP statute. Godlewski argues (1) McCaslin placed herself in the public eye by harassing Godlewski and his followers on Telegram; (2) by creating a nuisance in Godlewski’s online community, McCaslin made her dispute with Godlewski a matter of public interest; and (3) Godlewski’s statements were intended to direct people away from a business that did not share common values.

B. Godlewski’s statements were not statements made in a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest

According to Plaintiff’s complaint, Godlewski is “a Q-Anon conspiracy leader and online personality.” (Complaint, ¶10.) Godlewski has been written up in the Scranton-Times Tribune and other publications such as Rolling Stone and Daily Beast have covered Godlewski’s “notorious behavior.” (Complaint, ¶10.) Scranton-Times Tribune published a profile about Godlewski’s past criminal charges involving a sexual relationship with a minor. (Id. at ¶10.) Godlewski also has 300,000 Telegram social media followers. (Id. at ¶11.)

Godlewski confirms these allegations in his declaration in support of the anti-SLAPP motion, including his community of 300,000 Telegram followers. Godlewski also testifies that his social media content garners tens of millions of views a month. (Godlewski Dec., ¶¶3-4.) Godlewski’s “online community” is a “place where people can connect, consider and debate ideas.” (Id. at ¶6.) Godlewski and his followers exchange messages amongst one another regarding topics of interest to their community. (Id. at ¶¶6-12.)

Godlewski also provides context for his statements “doxxing” McCaslin and encouraging his followers to post negative reviews and harass her. (Godlewski Dec., ¶¶8-12.) McCaslin

inserted herself into Godlewski’s “community” to post negative statements regarding Godlewski to his followers, stating that he was as pedophile and a registered sex offender and admonishing his followers for subscribing to “this monster.” (Godlewksi Dec., ¶11.)

In response to McCaslin’s posts, Godlewski (1) posted a picture of McCaslin’s salon business page; (2) posted an email McCaslin sent him privately with a picture of the salon’s information page on Google with the statement “I wouldn’t want to do business with this person. Would you?”; (3) posted a review of Touché salon based on the email exchange with McCaslin; and (4) posted a video jokingly stating that he would “own” the salon and turn it into a “taco stand.” (Godlewski, ¶18.) These are the precise statements that form the basis of Plaintiff’s complaint for IIED. (Complaint, ¶11.)

i. Godlewski’s statements were made in a public forum

“It is settled that Web sites accessible to the public ... are public forums for purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Wong v. Tai Jing (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1354, 1366.) “The California Supreme Court held that Web sites accessible to the public are ‘public forums’ for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute. Cases construing the term ‘public forum’ as used in section 425.16 have noted that the term is traditionally defined as a place that is open to the public where information is freely exchanged…Statements on [plaintiff’s] Web site are accessible to anyone who chooses to visit the site, and thus they hardly could be more public.” (Kronemyer v. Internet Movie Data Base, Inc. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 941, 950.)

Plaintiff concedes that Godlewski’s statements were made in a public forum. (Opposition, 11:24-25.) The “public forum” element of (e)(3) is therefore satisfied.

ii. Godlewski’s statements were not made in connection with an issue of public interest

In FilmOn.com Inc. v. Doubleverify Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 133, 139, the Supreme Court set forth a test to determine whether a statement was in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest under (e)(4). Because the “in connection with a public issue” language of (e)(4) is identical to that of (e)(3), FilmOn’s test to determine whether a statement was “in connection with a public issue” is applicable here. (Bernstein v. LeBouf (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 15, 23, fn 5

(“While FilmOn addressed the meaning of the phrase ‘in connection with’ as it is used in subdivision (e)(4) of section 425.16, we see no reason why the same analysis should not apply when determining whether a statement was made ‘in connection with’ a public issue or a matter of public interest for purposes of subdivision (e)(3) of section 425.16.”).)

“In articulating what constitutes a matter of public interest, courts look to certain specific considerations, such as whether the subject of the speech or activity was a person or entity in the public eye or could affect large numbers of people beyond the direct participants; and whether the activity occurred in the context of an ongoing controversy, dispute or discussion, or affect[ed] a community in a manner similar to that of a governmental entity.” (FilmOn.com Inc., supra, 7 Cal.5th at 145–146.)

In determining whether conduct was “in connection with a public issue,” “a court must consider the context as well the content of a statement in determining whether that statement furthers the exercise of constitutional speech rights in connection with a matter of public interest.” (Id. at 149.) Whether conduct is commercial or noncommercial is one aspect of context that the court must examine in applying the catchall provision, but it is not determinative. (Id. at 148.)

“[I]t is not enough that the statement refer to a subject of widespread public interest; the statement must in some manner itself contribute to the public debate.” (FilmOn.com Inc., supra, 7 Cal.5th at 150.) “We are not concerned with the social utility of the speech at issue, or the degree to which it propelled the conversation in any particular direction; rather, we examine whether a defendant—through public or private speech or conduct—participated in, or furthered, the discourse that makes an issue one of public interest.” (Id. at 151.) “But the inquiry of whether a statement contributes to the public debate is one a court can hardly undertake without incorporating considerations of context—including audience, speaker, and purpose.” (Id. at 151-152.)

Thus, under FilmOn, the court first looks to the “content of the speech” to determine what public issue or issue of public interest the speech in question implicates. (FilmOn, supra, 7 Cal.5th at 149.) The court then examines the context or “functional relationship [] between the speech and the public conversation about some matter of public interest,” focusing on whether the speech in some way contributed to or further the discourse that makes the issue one of public interest.” (Id. at ¶¶149-150.)

The “specific speech that is the subject of” the plaintiff's claims is “[c]ritical” to the public interest analysis. (Dual Diagnosis Treatment Center, Inc. v. Buschel (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 1098, 1104 (Buschel); accord Consumer Justice Center v. Trimedica International, Inc. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 595, 601; Commonwealth Energy Corp. v. Investor Data Exchange, Inc. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 26, 34.) Thus, at this first step, “the focus of our inquiry must be on ‘the specific nature of the speech,’ rather than on any ‘generalities that might be abstracted from it.’ ” (FilmOn, supra, 7 Cal.5th at 152.)

Later in Rand Resources, LLC v. City of Carson (2019) 6 Cal.5th 610, 621, the Supreme Court would identify the three categories of statements that qualify as “public interest”: (1) statements or conduct that concern a person or entity in the public eye; (2) statements or conduct that could directly affect a large number of persons beyond the direct participants; and (3) statements or conduct involving a topic of widespread interest. (Rand, supra, 6 Cal.5th at 631.)

Here, Godlewski’s speech was (1) posting of McCaslin’s business information on Google with the words “Ready…set” and a string of emojis—a smiley face with sunglasses, a finger pointing at the reader and a lit bomb;1 (2) posting the private email he received from McCaslin, the same Google site regarding her business and the statement, “So this is how the owner of the Salon speaks to people she doesn’t know. I wouldn’t want to do business with this person. Would you?”; and (3) a video stating that Godlewski would own Plaintiff’s salon and turn it into a taco stand. (Godlewski Dec., ¶¶13, 15 and 18.)

Godlewski asserts his statements relate to an issue of public interest based on the news articles reporting on his criminal record, as well as his 300,000 followers. However, although his statements were made in the context of an ongoing controversy or dispute over his criminal charges for sexual relations with a minor, none of Godlewski’s statements themselves were about himself or those charges. Godlewski’s statements all referred to McCaslin, her business, her statements to him and her moral character.

Merely disclosing his private dispute with McCaslin over his moral character, her moral character and her business to his 300,000 followers did not make McCaslin a person in the public eye either. A “private dispute does not become a matter of public interest simply because it was

widely communicated to the public.” (Bernstein, supra, 43 Cal.App.5th at 24.) “[A] matter of concern to the speaker and a relatively small, specific audience is not a matter of public interest,” and that “[a] person cannot turn otherwise private information into a matter of public interest simply by communicating it to a large number of people.” (Rand Resources, LLC v. City of Carson (2019) 6 Cal.5th 610, 621.) That is precisely what Godlewski attempted to do here.

The case of Woodhill Ventures, LLC v. Yang (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 624 is analogous to the facts presented here. In Woodhill, the plaintiff made multiple statements in public forums disparaging a birthday cake he ordered from defendant. The Court of Appeals found that no protected conduct was at issue, because (1) the statements complaining about the cake were not related to a topic of widespread interest and any attempt to link plaintiff’s complaints with the general topic of public concerns over the dangers of “candy confusion” was remote and attenuated and (2) the statements by the plaintiff did not concern any person or entity in the public eye, despite the fact that the plaintiff had a social media following of 1.5 million and because the defendant bakery had achieved national notoriety. (Woodhill, supra, 68 Cal.App.5th at 632-633.) The Court of Appeals explained that Yang’s celebrity status did not make everything he said of public interest, nor did widespread communication of his private dispute render his statements in connection with a matter of public interest. (Id. at 633.) The Court of Appeals also found the bakery to be a small business with two shops in Los Angeles and therefore not a business in the public eye. (Id.)

The reasoning of Woodhill applies with equal force here. Godlewski cites to Hall v. Time Warner, Inc. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1347 in support of his claim that his statements were in connection with an issue of public interest because of his widespread renown and status as a figure in the public eye. Godlewski has 300,000 followers, was written up in several publications as having been charged with having a sexual relationship with a minor and his content receives tens of millions of views a month. However, these facts do not make him a public figure of such renown and repute that every aspect of his life and what he says is an issue of public interest, like Marlon Brando in Hall.

Nor does the assertion that McCaslin created an online nuisance transform the dispute into one of public interest. The evidence as to the writings is fundamentally undisputed; therefore, the question of whether it concerned a “matter of public interest” is unaffected by who “started it.”

Finally, Godlewski’s statements about McClain, questioning her moral character and implicitly inviting his followers to harass her were not statements that could directly affect a large number of people beyond the direct participants. (Rand Resources, LLC, supra, 6 Cal.5th at 621.) If anything, the statements could only have directly affected McCaslin, as they implicitly called for an action against her business.

Godlewski therefore fails to satisfy the first prong of the anti-SLAPP statute. Godlewski fails to establish that Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress causes of action arises from protected conduct as defined under CCP §425.16(e)(3). Godlewski’s anti-SLAPP motion is therefore denied for failure to satisfy the first prong.

Having failed to establish the first prong of SLAPP, the Court need not reach the second prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis.

ORDER

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §425.16 is DENIED.

Case Number: 23TRCV02595    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Plaintiff Pamela Bazargan

Responding Party: Defendant Alpha Beta Company d/b/a Ralphs

The court considered the moving papers, opposition and reply.

RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff fell in the parking lot of Ralphs grocery store on May 31, 2023. On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint on August 9, 2023.

On October 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint against Ralph’s Grocery Company, Todd I Schiffman, as trustee of Todd I Schiffman Living Trust dated December 23, 1999 and Xavier Hendrix alleging (1) negligence; (2) negligent hiring; and (3) premises liability.

On December 7, 2023, Defendant Alpha Beta Company dba Ralphs filed an answer to the FAC. On March 12, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant Todd I Schiffman. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant Xavier Hendrix. The only remaining defendant is Alpha Beta Company dba Ralphs.

On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion for Trial Preference currently before the Court. On April 10, 2024, Defendant Ralphs filed an opposition. On April 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

According to CCP §36(a), “[a] party to a civil action who is over the age of 70 years may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes all of the following findings: (1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole. (2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation.” (CCP §36(a).)

An attorney declaration for trial preference under CCP §36(a) is sufficient and may be made on information and belief. See CCP §36.5. If a motion for trial preference is granted, the court shall set the trial date not more than 120 days from granting of the motion. (CCP §36(f).)

As the Court in Fox v. Supr. Ct. (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529 explained, subsections (a) and (d) are entirely distinct in their requirements for trial preference: “The standard under subdivision (a), unlike under subdivision (d), which is more specific and more rigorous, includes no requirement of a doctor's declaration. To the contrary, a motion under subdivision (a) may be supported by nothing more than an attorney's declaration based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.” (Fox, supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at 534.) Section 36(a) also does not require imminent death or incapacity for trial preference: “Section 36, subdivision (a), says nothing about death or incapacity. Whether there is ‘substantial medical doubt of survival ... beyond six months’ is, to be sure, a matter of specific concern under subdivision (d), but the relevant standard under subdivision (a) is more open-ended. The issue under subdivision (a) is not whether an elderly litigant might die before trial or become so disabled that she might as well be absent when trial is called. Provided there is evidence that the party involved is over 70, all subdivision (a) requires is a showing that that party's ‘health ... is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing [her] interest in the litigation.’” (Id.)

In Fox, plaintiff sufficiently demonstrated grounds for trial preference under CCP §36(a) based on the declarations of plaintiff and plaintiff’s counsel. “It is uncontroverted [plaintiff] suffers from stage IV lung cancer and severe coronary artery disease, among other ailments. She is undergoing chemotherapy treatments, but is in constant discomfort and has difficulty performing basic life functions. And critically, her mental state has deteriorated to a point where

she becomes confused and forgetful. All told, the evidence shows that while Ms. Fox is currently able to participate in a trial, she has good reason for concern that will not be the case for much longer as her health deteriorates.” (Fox, supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at 535.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff requests trial preference under CCP §36(a). Plaintiff is 74 years of age and is the sole plaintiff in this action. Plaintiff claims she has hypertension and is in constant discomfort due to a fall she experienced in June 2023. (Motion, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff maintains she underwent a procedure due to fractured femur and injury to her hip due to the fall. Taken together, plaintiff’s declaration suggests that there is a concern that her health may deteriorate in the future.

Plaintiff showing is insufficient to justify trial preference. Plaintiff’s medical records are from June 2023. The Court cannot determine from those records whether Plaintiff is on hypertension medication as it is not familiar with the medications listed. (Motion, Ex. 1). However, the records indicate blood pressure of 127/65 on that date. There is also no indication that the injuries from Plaintiff’s fall would require trial preference. Plaintiff’s controlled hypertension and discomfort due to a fall from nearly a year ago are insufficient to establish prejudice if trial preference is not granted. Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is denied.

ORDER

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is DENIED.

Case Number: 23TRCV03153    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Defendant Murchison & Cumming, LLP

Responding Party: N/A - Unopposed

DEMURRER

RULING

The Court considered the moving papers. Murchison & Cumming, LLP’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

Factual Background

On September 28, 2023, Ian Armstrong (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint in pro per against Murchison & Cumming, LLP (Defendant) for two causes of action: fraud and breach of contract. The Complaint stems from allegations that Defendant made misrepresentations in regard to an alleged contract that was formed between the two parties. The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Demurrer. The Demurrer is unopposed.

Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of

determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) Defendant provides the Declaration of Gina E. Och (Och Decl.) which states that the parties conferred telephonically on March 8, 2024, but were unable to reach an agreement. Nonetheless, the requirements of CCP § 430.41(a) are satisfied.

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard for Demurrer

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Analysis for Demurrer

Defendant demurs to the Complaint on two main grounds: (1) the Complaint fails to set forth sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action, and (2) the litigation privilege bars Plaintiff’s causes of action. The Court agrees and sustains the Demurrer without leave to amend.

(1) Sufficiency of the Complaint

A Complaint must plead the facts necessary to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source, and extent of the claims in a manner sufficient to enable preparation of a defense. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-550.) Here, the Complaint fails to do so, therefore, the Court will sustain Defendant’s Demurrer in its entirety.

a. Fraud

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false

representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

The Complaint fails to detail the exact misrepresentation made by Defendant. It is unclear from both the Complaint, and the exhibits attached, what statement was made by Defendant that was false and intended to induce reliance. Defendant is a law firm that was assisting its client, Integrated National Resources, Inc (INR). The communications Plaintiff attaches to the Complaint appear to be email communications regarding Defendant’s assistance in INR’s attempt to expand its business. (Complaint, Exh. FR-5.) The other communications appear to have come from INR themselves.

The email communications from Defendant regard (1) a Subscription, Stockholders, and Investors Rights Agreement sent on behalf of INR, and (2) an email from Ryan Flanagan from Murchison & Cumming, LLP informing Plaintiff of INR’s attempts to expand its business. Nowhere in the Complaint does Plaintiff point to representations within these communications that were false. Without a false representation, a fraud claim cannot stand. Therefore, the Demurrer is sustained as to the first cause of action.

b. Breach of Contract

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

Plaintiff must first establish the existence of a contract between the parties; however, no such attempt is made in the Complaint. It is clear that a contract between Plaintiff and INR is attempting to be made, however, no such contract exists between Plaintiff and Defendant. Therefore, the second cause of action must fall to Demurrer.

(2) Litigation Privilege

Defendant’s second argument is that the litigation privilege bars Plaintiff’s claims. The litigation privilege provides that a “publication or broadcast” made as part of a “judicial proceeding” is privileged. (Action Apartment Assn., Inc., v. City of Santa Monica (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1232, 1241.) The privilege is not limited to statements made during trial but may extend to steps taken prior to trial. (Id.) “The principal purpose of [the litigation privilege] is to afford litigants and witnesses the utmost freedom of access to the courts without fear of being harassed subsequently by derivative tort actions.” (Id.) The litigation privilege is an absolute privilege that

bars all tort causes of action except malicious prosecution. (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 771.)

Defendant argues that the communications between Plaintiff and Defendant on behalf of Defendant’s client INR, are covered by this privilege. The Court agrees. All the communications were made in service to Defendant’s client, INR. The litigation privilege was created this exact situation. Consequently, the litigation privilege bars Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant.

Legal Standard & Analysis for Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. The Complaint failed to allege any fraud on the part of Defendant and failed to allege a contract between the parties. Crucially, due to the voluminous emails attached to the complaint, it appears that there can be no claim of a contract between the parties. The fraud claim is based on the presence of a contract, as there is no contract, there is no possibility of successful amendment, therefore leave to amend is denied.

CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Murchison & Cumming, LLP’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV04104    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Defendant George Cashman

Responding Party: Plaintiff Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palos Verdes

MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS

RULING

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. George Cashman Jr.’s Motion to Quash the Service of the Summons and Complaint is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On December 11, 2023, the Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palos Verdes (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint alleging three causes of action against George Cashman Jr. (Cashman/Defendant), Cynthia C. Makin, and the GFC Administrative Trust. The Complaint stemmed from an alleged breach of the “Declaration No. 150 of Establishment of Basic Protective

Restrictions, Conditions, Covenants, Reservations, Liens, Charges and Certain Local Restrictions” (CCRs), when Plaintiff discovered that a fence extends beyond Plaintiff’s permitted easement in violation of the CCRs.

The motion before the Court is Cashman’s Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and Complaint (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion, and Cashman files a reply.

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

California Code of Civil Procedure Sections 415.10, 415.20, 415.30, and 415.50 describe four methods of service within California: (1) personal delivery of copy of summons and complaint to person to be served; (2) delivery of a copy of summons and complaint to someone else at defendant’s usual residence or place of business; (3) service by mail coupled with acknowledgment of receipt of summons; and (4) service by publication.

Complainants have the initial burden to evidence valid statutory service of a summons and complaint. (Dill v. Berquist Const. Co., Inc. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1439-40; Floveyor Internat. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 59 Cal. App. 4th 789, 794. Court findings as to conflicting proof regarding whether a summons and complaint were properly served are upheld if based upon substantial evidence. (Stafford v. Mach (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182.)

A trial court has discretion as to whether to accept a process server declaration, or contradictory evidence, in deciding whether service of a summons and complaint was validly accomplished. (Am. Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal. App. 4th 383, 390; Khourie, Crew & Jaeger v. Sabek, Inc. (1990) 220 Cal. App. 3d 1009, 1014) (“Sabek points out that it introduced evidence, in the form of declarations, that tend to contradict the declaration of Murray Woods…. The trial court, however, was entitled to believe Woods [the process server].”). “‘[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction.’” (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544. Accord Renoir v. Redstar Corp. (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 1145, 1152.) A filed proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper, if it complies with applicable statutory requirements. (Floveyor Internat. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 59 Cal. App. 4th 789, 795.)

Analysis

Cashman alleges in his moving papers that only one attempt at service has been made, when the process server – Gabriele Melendez (Melendez) – attempted to serve Cashman at his residence located at 2 Crest Road East Rolling Hills, California 90274 on January 9, 2024. (Declaration of George Cashman, hereinafter, Cashman Decl., Exh. 1.) In that one attempt, Cashman alleges that Melendez left the papers with a security guard, who does not live at the residence, and that therefore service was improper pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 415.20(b). Cashman is correct. Although stopping by his residence is a valid attempt, this alone is improper service.

However, Plaintiff alleges in their opposition papers that they have made prior attempts. On December 16, 2023, a separate process server – Scott Amundson (Amundson) – attempted to serve Cashman at a 28985 Palos Verdes Dr. E., Rancho Palos Verde, CA 90275, however, it appeared that Cashman did not reside there, and was therefore the incorrect address, which would not constitute a proper attempt. (Declaration of Scott Amundson, hereinafter Amundson Decl., ¶ 4-7.) Another attempt was made to serve Cashman in person on December 29, 2023 when Amundson traveled to Stanley Mosk Courthouse and attempted to serve Cashman while Cashman was appearing for a separate legal matter. (Id. ¶ 9.) Amundson states that he attempted to serve Cashman who “identified himself as George Cashman to me and immediately turned around and ran down the hallway.” Although service was not completed, this equates to a valid first attempt. This unsuccessful attempt, in combination with Melendez’s later unsuccessful attempt at Cashman’s residence, falls squarely within the definition of due diligence as defined by Espindola v. Nunez (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 1389, 1392 (Espindola), the case Cashman cites in arguing that the sole attempt by Melendez was insufficient.

Espindola held that “Ordinarily, ... two or three attempts at personal service at a proper place should fully satisfy the requirement of reasonable diligence and allow substituted service to be made.” Here, the Court finds two attempts of personal service at a proper place, the first at the Stanley Mosk Courthouse on December 29, 2023, and the second, at Cashman’s residence. Although Cashman’s opposition papers are silent as to the attempt at the Courthouse, the Court finds this a sufficient attempt. Melendez’s January 9, 2024 attempt at the Cashman’s residence was also proper. Accordingly, after these two attempts failed, Melendez then mailed the summons and complaint to Cashman’s residence, leaving Plaintiff in full compliance with Espindola, and Code Civ. Proc. §§ 415.10 & 415.20.

CONCLUSION

Therefore, the George Cashman Jr.’s Motion to Quash the Service of the Summons and Complaint is DENIED.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.
