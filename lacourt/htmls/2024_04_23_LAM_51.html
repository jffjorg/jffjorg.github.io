DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 23STCV20532     Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:    April 23, 2024                                    

 

CASE NAME:           Cubosfera, LLC v. Edward M. Buttwinick, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                23STCV20532

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT; MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Edward M. Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust a/r, dated June 11, 2019 and as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; Linda S. Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust a/r, dated June 11, 2019 and as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; Jill Buttwinick Zweier, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; and Karen Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Cubosfera, LLC

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.     Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and for uncertainty;

2.     Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and for uncertainty;

3.     An Order striking portions of the Complaint pertaining to punitive damages.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.     Demurrer to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend;

2.     Motion to Strike is DENIED as moot.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On August 28, 2023, Plaintiff Cubosfera, LLC (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendants Edward M. Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust a/r, dated June 11, 2019 and as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; Linda S. Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust a/r, dated June 11, 2019 and as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; Jill Buttwinick Zweier, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990; and Karen Buttwinick, as trustee of the Buttwinick Family Trust, dated May 4, 1990 (Defendants). The Complaint alleges seven causes of action for: (1) Declaratory Relief; (2) Breach of Contract; (3) Constructive Eviction; (4) Negligent Misrepresentation; (5) Intentional Misrepresentation; (6) Declaratory Relief; and (7) Nuisance.

 

According to the Complaint, Plaintiff leased property located at 13031 Montana Avenue (the Property) from Defendants. Plaintiff alleges Defendants have failed and refused to permit Plaintiff to proceed with a variance application and has refused to allow Plaintiff to operate its business at the Property or be present at the Property. Plaintiff alleges it was constructively evicted from the Property.

 

On January 8, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth and fifth causes of action with leave to amend.


On February 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On March 18, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike

 

On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions. 

 

On April 16, 2024, Defendants filed replies. 

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

Meet and Confer 

 

Prior to filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to satisfy their meet and confer obligations pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. (CCP) §430.41 and demonstrate that they so satisfied their meet and confer obligation by submitting a declaration pursuant to CCP §430.41(a)(2) & (3).¿The meet and confer requirement also applies to motions to strike. (CCP § 435.5.)

 

Here, Plaintiff object to the demurrer and motion to strike due to lack of meet and confer. (See Opposition to Demurrer, 5:7-6:28.) Still, failure to meet and confer is not a sufficient ground to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a)(4).)¿

 

Plaintiff further object on the ground that the demurrer was filed late. Defendants contend that Plaintiff has waived any objection based on timeliness by filing an opposition instead of a motion to strike the demurrer or moving for default. (Reply, 5:26-6:8.)

 

Demurrer¿ 

¿ 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿(Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. …. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿ 

¿ 

Motion to Strike¿ 

¿ 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id.¿§¿437.)¿“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)¿  

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Fourth & Fifth Causes of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation & Intentional Misrepresentation

 

Defendants contend that the FAC did not correct the defects that existed in the original pleading. The misrepresentation causes of action fail because Plaintiff cannot allege damages or justifiable reliance, because the mistake in the contract as to the name of the parties was a harmless scrivener’s error. Plaintiff argues that the identity of a party to a contract is a material representation, and that it sufficiently pled damages because it would not have incurred the rental costs and consultant fees but for Defendants’ misrepresentation of the identity of the true owners. Defendants Reply points out logical fallacies in Plaintiff’s arguments and maintains that Plaintiffs cannot allege with the requisite specificity the reliance and damages elements.

 

“The essential¿elements¿of a count for¿intentional¿misrepresentation¿are (1) a¿misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3)¿intent¿to induce reliance,¿(4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. [Citation.] (Chapman v. Skype, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-31.) “ ‘ “The elements of negligent misrepresentation are similar to intentional fraud except for the requirement of scienter; in a claim for negligent misrepresentation, the plaintiff need not allege that the defendant made an intentionally false statement, but simply one as to which he or she lacked any reasonable ground for believing the statement to be true.” [Citations.] [Citation.]’ ” (Bains v. Moores (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 445, 454.) “Causes of action for intentional and negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud and, therefore, each element must be pleaded with specificity. [Citations.]” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166, disapproved of on other grounds by Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905.)  

 

A cause of action for fraud must be alleged with specificity such that Plaintiff pleads facts that “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Similarly, “[t]he requirement of specificity in a fraud action against a corporation requires the plaintiff to allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Tarmann v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.) 

 

Upon reviewing the FAC, the court agrees that Plaintiff, once again, has not sufficiently alleged causes of action for negligent or intentional misrepresentation. In particular, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged facts indicating reliance and damages. The only alleged misrepresentation is that the 2019 Buttwinick Trust owned the Property at the time of the lease. However, the contract identified the landlord as the 1990 Buttwinick Trust. The 1990 Trust has as Trustees defendants Edward and Linda Buttwinick. The 2019 Trust includes their children, Jill and Karen Buttwinick as additional trustees. Plaintiff alleges that it was damaged because it would have never entered the Lease had it known the true identity of the landlord. Plaintiff claims that the landlord it believed it was renting from would be amenable to the use proposed use and that landlord would work collaboratively with Plaintiff as the landlord had with the School of the Arts. Plaintiff claims that the actual landlord was a different entity all together. But throughout the FAC and Opposition, Plaintiff glosses over who it thought the landlord was. Instead, Plaintiff uses vague phrases like “a landlord that was amenable.” (FAC ¶¶ 43, 76, 88, Opposition pp. 8, line 18 and 14, line 5). The FAC has no allegations as to how Edward and Linda Buttwinik as trustees of the 2019 trust are fundamentally different from Edward and Linda Buttwinik as trustees of the 1990 trust. The FAC does not contain factual allegation as to why Plaintiff would have been willing to work with Edward and Linda Buttwinick as trustees of one Buttwinick trust but not as trustees of another Buttwinick Family Trust. The Opposition does not cite to any portion of the FAC where Plaintiff states why it would not have entered the Lease if the landlord was identified as the 1990 Trust and not the 2019 Trust.

 

The FAC falls short of the pleading standard for causes of action based in fraud. Plaintiffs have also failed to explain how the alleged misrepresentation is material.

 

Accordingly, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action without leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike the following portions of the Complaint pertaining to punitive damages:

·       Paragraph 94, 18:25-19:3 – “Defendants’ conduct was intentional and malicious . . . Defendants thus acted with the intention to deceive and defraud Plaintiff and are guilty of fraud, oppression and malice. Plaintiff accordingly also seeks an award of exemplary and punitive damages against Defendants in an amount to be determined at trial.”

·       Prayer for Relief, Paragraph 5, lines 9-10 – “As to all applicable causes of action, for punitive and exemplary damages, in an amount according to proof.”

In light of the ruling on demurrer, the motion to strike is MOOT. 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

1.Demurrer to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend;

2.Motion to Strike is DENIED as moot.

Moving party is to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

CASE NAME:           S.T. v. Eric Weinberg, et al. 

 

CASE NO.:                23STCV21986

 

MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA AND/OR PROTECTIVE ORDER

 

MOVING PARTY:  Defendants Eric Weinberg and Guitar and Pen Productions, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff S.T.

 

REQUESTED RELIEF: Quash subpoenas for production of business records on healthcare providers or issue a protective order limiting the time and scope of the subpoenas

 

TENTATIVE RULING: Defendant Eric Weinberg and Guitar and Pen Productions, Inc.’s motion to quash subpoenas on Blue Tiger Recovery; Omar Minwalla, Ph.D at the Institute for Sexual Health; Marilyn Ruman, Ph.D.; and Sharon O'Hara, MFT is GRANTED.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On September 12, 2023, Plaintiff S.T. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Eric Weinberg (“Weinberg”), Guitar and Pen Productions, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”), MTV Networks (“MTV”), and Liquid Theory, Inc. The operative first amended complaint was filed on February 23, 2024, following a demurrer by MTV, and alleges eleven causes of action for: (1) Sexual Assault, (2) Sexual Battery, (3) Sexual Harassment, (4) Gender Violence, (5) False Imprisonment, (6) Assault, (7) Battery, (8) Intentional Inflection of Emotional Distress, (9) Negligence, (10) Respondeat Superior, and (11) Aiding and Abetting. 

 

According to the Complaint, Weinberg sexually assaulted and harassed Plaintiff while she was a working actress on MTV’s show Death Valley and after she left the show.

 

On October 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Related Case to thirteen pending cases pending in Dept. 62 of the Stanley Mosk Courthouse. 

 

On February 28, 2024, Defendants filed this motion to quash subpoenas and/or for protective order.

 

On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.

 

On April 11, 2024, Defendants replied.

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

C.C.P. §1987.1 provides, in part: “If a subpoena requires . . . the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, . . . the court, upon motion reasonably made by [a party], or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon such terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (C.C.P. §1987.1.)

 

ANALYSIS:

 

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff issued four deposition subpoenas for production of business records on four different health care providers: Blue Tiger Recovery; Omar Minwalla, Ph.D at the Institute for Sexual Health; Marilyn Ruman, Ph.D.; and Sharon O'Hara, MFT (the “Therapy Providers”). All four subpoenas seek the same categories of documents:

 

All treatment records (including but not limited to counseling notes, reports, session recordings, photographs, videos, diagnoses, treatment plans, prescriptions and assessments) for Eric Weinberg RELATED TO treatment for sexual dependency, sexual addiction, sexual recovery, sexual aggression, sexual harassment and/or sexual assault from 2000 to Present.

 

All treatment records for Eric Weinberg RELATED TO treatment for impulse control from 2000 to Present.

 

All treatment records for Eric Weinberg RELATED TO anger management from 2000 to Present.

 

(Motion, 3:13-4:3, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff requested records from 2000 to present, covering 10 years prior to her initial assault in 2011 to 4 years after her last interaction with Weinberg in 2020. (Opp’n, ¶ 19.)

 

1.     Good Cause

When seeking documents through discovery, a party must show “good cause” to compel production of such records. (Calcor Space Facility v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 219, 224.) “The matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Id. at 223.) “To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.” (Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

Plaintiff contends that good cause exists to compel production of Weinberg’s medical records because they are “relevant to Defendant’s affirmative defenses and understanding of consent as well as relevant to Plaintiff’s claims.” (Opp’n, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff does not explain anywhere how the requested documents could support Plaintiff’s claims. Plaintiff goes into slightly more details as to the documents’ relevance to Weinberg’s defenses. Plaintiff specifically identifies Weinberg’s defenses of consent, assumption of risk and comparative fault. (Opp’n, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff contends that Weinberg has put his sexual disfunction at issue by raising those defenses. However, Plaintiff’s argument lacks any reasoning and merely states, repeatedly and conclusorily, that Weinberg’s medical records are “highly relevant”. The Court cannot discern how those defenses put Weinberg’s medical records at issue.

 

Plaintiff has failed to show good cause to compel production of the requested documents. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion should be granted.

2.     Psychotherapist-Patient Privilege

Defendants contend that the psychotherapist-patient privilege applies to Plaintiff’s subpoena requests because all of the Therapy Providers hold themselves out as providers of psychotherapy by psychiatric professionals, licensed psychologists, or a licensed Marriage & Family Therapist. Consequently, all of the treatment records sought, if they exist, would be privileged under Evidence Code sections 990 et seq. and 1010 et seq. There is an exception to the psychotherapist-patient privilege when otherwise protected communications are directly relevant to the specific mental/emotional condition tendered by the patient. (Evidence Code § 1016.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant put his sexual disfunction at issue through his affirmative defenses. But as discussed above, Plaintiff failed to make any cognizable argument beyond conclusory statements that the requested documents are relevant. Plaintiff has not shown that Weinberg tendered his mental or emotional condition.

 

Accordingly, the psychotherapist-patient privilege applies to the records Plaintiff seeks to subpoena, and Defendants’ motion should be granted.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

Defendant Eric Weinberg and Guitar and Pen Productions, Inc.’s motion to quash medical record subpoenas on Blue Tiger Recovery; Omar Minwalla, Ph.D at the Institute for Sexual Health; Marilyn Ruman, Ph.D.; and Sharon O'Hara, MFT is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
