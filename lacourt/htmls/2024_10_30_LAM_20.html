DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation. 



           


Case Number: 23STCV17840    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: October 30, 2024

Case Name: Koblanski v. Ultralytics, Inc., et al.

Case No.: 23STCV17840

Matter: Motion for Protective Order

Moving Party: Defendants Ultralytics, Inc. and Glenn Jocher

Responding Party: Plaintiff John Koblanski

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Motion is denied.


Moving party to give notice.


On July 28, 2023, John Koblanski filed the operative Complaint against Defendants Ultralytics, Inc. and Glenn Jocher for (1) declaratory relief, (2) breach of fiduciary duty, and (3) failure to pay minimum wages under FLSA.  Plaintiff alleges that he and Jocher formed a company, Ultralytics, Inc.  Plaintiff claims there was an agreement in which he was supposed to have a 35% interest in Ultralytics, but Jocher now asserts Plaintiff is owed nothing despite the time and effort that Plaintiff invested in making Utralytics successful.  

On October 1, 2024, Defendants sought to dismiss this action for forum non conveniens. That request was denied.

Defendants now seek a stay of discovery pending a ruling on the above motion to dismiss.  It’s not apparent why the instant Motion has not been withdrawn given the denial of the motion to dismiss.

The Motion for Protective Order is denied as moot.

Moving parties to give notice.


Case Number: 24STCV01868    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: October 30, 2024

Case Name: Romo, et al. v. Vartanian, et al.

Case No.: 24STCV01868 

Matter: Demurrer

Moving Party: Defendants Sandra Vartanian, trustee of the Sandra Vartanian Revocable 

Trust dated 6/22/2022

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Leticia Romo, John Michael Sanchez, and Juan Jose Sanchez 

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Demurrer is overruled.


Moving parties to give notice.


On January 24, 2024, Plaintiffs Leticia Romo, John Michael Sanchez, and Juan Jose Sanchez filed the operative Complaint against Defendants Alexan Vartanian, Incan Project Development Group, Inc., and Does 1-20 for (1) negligence, (2) breach of warranty of habitability, (3) violation of LAMC § 45.33, and (4) violations of the UCL.

On May 22, 2024, Doe 1 was identified as Sandra Vartanian, trustee of the Sandra Vartanian Revocable Trust dated 6/22/2022 (“Sandra”). 

Sandra now demurs to the Complaint for uncertainty and failure to state sufficient facts.

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, and “treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  It is error “to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if the plaintiff shows there is a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment.”  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist.¿(1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Sandra primarily argues that her “only . . .  ownership/landlord connection with Plaintiffs . . . was between [July] 20, 2022 [ ] and June 16, 2023 . . . . As pointed out in the INTRODUCTION herein, however, Plaintiffs complaint allegations do not specify the dates the alleged landlord/owner wrongdoing occurred, NOR does it even mention specific acts or representations of Demurring Defendant SANDRA.”

This argument is rejected.  The Complaint plainly states that in 2022/2023—when Sandra was admittedly an owner—nothing was done to restore the utilities for Plaintiffs’ units.  Rather, Defendants fought each other for title to the property, which was the basis of 22STCV27277—a case that was heard in this department.  

The only other argument is that “the negligence claim is likely time barred under the applicable statute of limitations as the statute of limitations runs when the Plaintiff knew or should have known of the wrongful conduct. It was more than three years since the Property was constructed allegedly without permits, and therefore, this is barred under the applicable statute of limitations . . . .”  (emphasis added.)  The Complaint, however, addresses a number of different wrongs—for example, Sandra’s failure to restore utilities to the entire property.    

The Demurrer is overruled.  An answer is to be filed within 20 days. 

Moving parties to give notice.



Case Number: 24STCV04306    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date: October 30, 2024

Case Name: Saeta v. West Hills Hospital, et al.

Case No.: 24STCV04306  

Matter: Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

Moving Party: Defendant West Hills Hospital and Medical Center

Responding Party: Plaintiff Joshua Saeta

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Motion for Summary Adjudication is granted as to the claim for 

fraudulent concealment, but the Motion is otherwise denied.


Moving party to give notice.


On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff Joshua Saeta filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) (case no. 24STCV04306) against Defendants West Hills Hospital and Medical Center and HCA Healthcare, Inc. for (1) dependent adult neglect, (2) dependent adult neglect (enhanced remedies), (3) violation of the Health Care Decisions Law, (4) medical battery, (5) fraudulent concealment, and (6) negligence.  

Defendant West Hills Hospital and Medical Center now seek summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication of all causes of action.

The law of summary judgment provides courts “a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts employ a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue, in which case the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).)  To show a triable issue of material fact exists, the opposing party may not rely on the mere allegations or denials of the pleadings, but instead must set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  (Aguilar, at p. 849.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

Defendant first argues that the necessary custodial relationship does not exist under the dependent adult abuse act: “there is no evidence that plaintiff was ‘particularly vulnerable and reliant’ at the time he arrived at the hospital. (Winn, supra, 63 Cal.4th at 160.) Further, plaintiff presented at West Hills Hospital for medical care, not custodial care. (See UMFs 22, and 23.) He sought ‘competent professional medical attention,’ which could only be provided by a health care provider. (See Oroville, supra, 74 Cal.App.5th at 405.) An able-bodied person cannot diagnose and treat the symptoms plaintiff presented with. (Ibid) This means the type of relationship at issue here should not be covered by the Act.”

There must be a “robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder's basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  (Winn v. Pioneer Med. Grp., Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 158.)

  The Court believes there are triable issues on this point as Plaintiff’s health deteriorated quickly, he needed fluids intravenously, needed a catheter, needed assistance with breathing, was described as being in a “critical” state and in risk of multiorgan failure, his acuity was described as “very unstable”, and he needed considerable supervision.  

Defendant next argues that this is not a case in which there was a lack of medical care provided so as to constitute neglect—rather, this is a case about professional negligence, which is the subject of another suit.  Defendant argues that “Plaintiff received extensive professional services that included ED evaluation and care by Cynthia Pfeiffer, M.D. (UMFs 7-9) and Anh T. Nguyen, M.D. (UMF 11-12); a CT scan and interpretation of that CT by radiologist Bruce Shragg, M.D. (UMF 8); admission to West Hills under Dr. Nasim (UMF 12); laboratory studies (UMF 10); nursing care (UMFs 14, 15, 16, 19 and 20); response to and care in response to cardiopulmonary arrest (UMFs 20 and 21); and subsequent physician care on and after February 3,2017 through discharge (Exh. F-9 and F-10).”

For the purposes of the Welfare & Institutions Code, neglect refers to “the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.”  (Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 34.)  Defendants may only be held liable under Welfare & Institutions Code § 15657 where the misconduct alleged is reckless, oppressive, fraudulent or malicious in nature.  (Id. at p. 31.)  Recklessness may be inferred from the Defendant’s failure to follow a medical care plan and make basic observations about the patient.  (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 90 [“[W]e easily conclude that there is a triable issue as to whether [defendant’s] employees acted with recklessness, oppression or malice. A trier of fact could find that when a care facility's employees ignore a care plan and fail to check the skin condition of a resident with Huntington's Chorea, such conduct shows deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that she will suffer injury.”].)  

The following are a few examples of when courts have found liability: 

• A skilled nursing facility: (1) failed to provide an elderly man suffering from Parkinson's disease with sufficient food and water and necessary medication; (2) left him unattended and unassisted for long periods of time; (3) left him in his own excrement so that ulcers exposing muscle and bone became infected; and (4) misrepresented and failed to inform his children of his true condition. (Covenant Care, supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 778 [11 Cal.Rptr.3d 222, 86 P.3d 290].)

• An 88–year–old woman with a broken ankle “was frequently left lying in her own urine and feces for extended periods of time”; and she developed pressure ulcers on her ankles, feet and buttocks that exposed bone, “despite plaintiff's persistent complaints to nursing staff, administration, and finally, to a nursing home ombudsman.” (Delaney, supra, 20 Cal.4th at pp. 27, 41 [82 Cal.Rptr.2d 610, 971 P.2d 986].)

• A facility caring for a dependent adult with a known condition causing progressive dementia, requiring nutrition and hydration through a gastrostomy tube, and subjecting her to skin deterioration, ignored a medical care plan requiring the facility to check the dependent adult's skin on a daily basis and failed to notify a physician when pressure ulcers and other skin lesions developed. (Sababin, supra, 144 Cal.App.4th at pp. 83–87, 90 [50 Cal.Rptr.3d 266].)

(Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (1986) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 405-406.)

The Court believes there is, at the very least, a triable issue as to whether there was reckless understaffing.  The Opposition chronicles a wealth of evidence indicating that Defendant was dangerously understaffed, leading to Plaintiff suffering a severe and unwitnessed ischemic attack that left him in a vegetative state.  It’s not even clear how long Plaintiff was left on the verge of death by himself.  One RN testified: “I kept saying over and over something bad is going to happen. Someone is going to die or worse. And worse is right. This, right now, what happened to Josh. . . We were working with bare minimum. Bare minimum equipment, staff. We were all at risk. Every nurse, every staff member, every patient. Every day.”  Further, Defendant fails to carry its burden on the issue of whether a director, officer, or managing agent ratified the staffing conditions.  The moving papers inappropriately focus on the care Plaintiff did receive, which is not dispositive.     

Defendant next argues that the abuse claims are barred by the statute of limitations, but this argument is a mere continuation of Defendant’s unilateral proclamation that this a medical malpractice suit such that CCP § 340.5 applies.  This has already been rejected.

Defendant further argues that “Even if plaintiffs claims sounded in dependent adult neglect, the limitations period is not tolled by Code of Civil Procedure section 352 under these facts. Section 352 is intended to toll the statute of limitations where the plaintiff lacks the capacity to make decisions. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 352.) But here plaintiffs interests have been protected by the conservatorship, which filed the Original Case on plaintiffs behalf more than six years ago, within the limitations period. (Exh. A and C; UMFs 1 and 3.) The conservatorship has had six years to investigate and evaluate the care provided to plaintiff by West Hills and, consequently, to determine which legal claims to assert against which defendants. (Exh. C.) It was only when new counsel was substituted in that plaintiff opted to assert the new causes of action.”

As Plaintiff points out, however, the conservatorship does not preclude tolling as demonstrated by Tzolov v. International Jet Leasing (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 117.  Defendant’s attempt to distinguish this case by indicating that it relates to a guardian ad litem rings hollow.  Moreover, tolling would also apply to Plaintiff’s other claims.

Defendant also argues that “Plaintiffs claim for violation of the Health Care Decisions Law fails because there is no evidence that any employee of West Hills intentionally refused to comply with a health care instruction of plaintiff. Indeed, the undisputed evidence reflects that the team responding to the Code did not know at that time that he was listed as ‘No Code.’ (UMF 48-49.) There was therefore no intentional violation of plaintiffs health care instruction.”  Defendant argues the medical battery claim fails for the same reason.

The elements for medical battery under these circumstances are: “1. That [name of defendant] performed a medical procedure without [name of plaintiff]’s consent . . . ; 2. That [name of plaintiff] was harmed; and 3. That [name of defendant]’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing [name of plaintiff]’s harm.”  (CACI no. 530A.)

The Motion is denied as to these claims because there is evidence that Plaintiff orally requested a “no code” from a nurse; that nurse failed to obtain a written order according to policy; and that same nurse participated in the subsequent unconsented code blue.  Intent here is a matter best left for the jury.

The last cause of action at issue is concealment.  “The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact. [Citation.]”  (Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 606.)

The Court will grant the Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the claim for concealment because Plaintiff has failed to raise triable issues with respect to fraudulent intent. 

The final issue is punitive damages.  There are triable issues as to malice or oppression.  Indeed, “[m]alice does not require actual intent to harm. (Id. at p. 895, 157 Cal.Rptr. 693, 598 P.2d 854.) Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. (Id. at pp. 895–896, 157 Cal.Rptr. 693, 598 P.2d 854.) Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.”  (Angie M. v. Superior Ct. (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228.)

In sum, the Motion for Summary Adjudication is granted as to the fraud claim, but is otherwise denied.  The objections are overruled.

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Hearing Date: October 30, 2024

Case Name: Elat Properties, Inc. v. 801 South Grand Avenue (LA), LLC, et al.

Case No.: 24STCV05104

Matter: Demurrer; Motion to Strike

Moving Party: Defendants 801 SOUTH GRAND AVENUE (LA), INC., 801 SOUTH 

GRAND AVENUE (LA) HOLDINGS, LLC,  801 SOUTH GRAND AVENUE (LA) OWNER, LLC, CIM GROUP, L.P., JONATHAN TAO, and CIM SMA I INVESTMENTS, LLC

Responding Party: Plaintiff Elat Properties, Inc. 

Notice: OK

Ruling: The Demurrer is overruled.


The Motion to Strike is denied.


Moving parties to give notice.


On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff Elat Properties, Inc. filed the operative Complaint against Defendants 801 SOUTH GRAND AVENUE (LA), INC., 801 SOUTH GRAND AVENUE (LA) HOLDINGS, LLC,  801 SOUTH GRAND AVENUE (LA) OWNER, LLC, CIM GROUP, L.P., JONATHAN TAO, and CIM SMA I INVESTMENTS, LLC for (1) fraud and (2) negligent misrepresentation.  Plaintiff alleges that it purchased a building from Defendants on a short timeline so as not to pay the “mansion tax” and that during the transaction Plaintiff relied on a spreadsheet presented by Defendants that falsely stated that the obligation amount for future tenant improvements for two units was $0 when it was actually $250,000.


  1. Demurrer

All Defendants now demur to the Complaint for uncertainty and failure to state sufficient facts.

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, and “treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  It is error “to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if the plaintiff shows there is a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment.”  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist.¿(1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Defendants first argue that the claims are not specifically pleaded and that reliance has not been shown.  Defendants’ arguments merely raise factual issues.  The Complaint adequately alleges that Plaintiff relied on the statements in the spreadsheet because the purchase was on a short timeline and that Defendants were aware that some improvements could not be reasonably discovered.  It is further alleged that had Plaintiff known about the improvements, it would have negotiated the purchase price down as was done for a handful of other improvements it did discover.  Factual issues remain.

Defendants next argue that Plaintiff’s claims were waived via provision in the purchase agreement: “[Plaintiff] hereby expressly waives, relinquishes and releases any right or remedy available to it at law, in equity or under this Agreement to make a claim against [801 South Grand] for damages that [Plaintiff] incurs or may incur, or to rescind this Agreement and the transactions contemplated hereby, as the result of any of [801 South Grand]’s representations or warranties being untrue, inaccurate or incorrect when made if (1) [Plaintiff] had knowledge or is “deemed to have known” at the time of the Close of Escrow that such representation or warranty was untrue, inaccurate or incorrect when made and [Plaintiff] nevertheless consummates the purchase of the Property hereunder, or (2) [Plaintiff]’s damages as a result of such representations or warranties being so untrue, inaccurate or incorrect are, in the aggregate, less than the Threshold. [Plaintiff] shall be ‘deemed to have known’ at the time of the Close of Escrow that a representation or warranty was untrue, inaccurate or incorrect when made if any [801 South Grand], the PTR and the recorded documents identified therein, any estoppel certificates delivered to [Plaintiff] pursuant to Section 5.1, or any other information or materials obtained by [Plaintiff] prior to the Close of Escrow contains any information which contradicts such representation or warranty by [801 South Grand], or if [801 South Grand] shall notify [Plaintiff] in writing that such representation or warranty was untrue, inaccurate, or incorrect when made. [801 South Grand]’s representations and warranties contained in Section 8.1, as reaffirmed and restated by [801 South Grand] at Closing, shall survive the Close of Escrow for the Survival Period, unless [Plaintiff] asserts a breach of any such representation or warranty in a written notice delivered to [801 South Grand] prior to the expiration of the Survival Period, in which case such applicable representation or warranty shall survive until [Plaintiff]’s claims set forth in such written notice have been resolved.” 

Defendants argue that, given the above provision, the estoppel certificates provided to Plaintiff would preclude any liability.  Defendants argue that the Court should take judicial notice of such estoppel certificates.  The Court declines to do so as it is generally not appropriate to take judicial notice of a contract or its components unless there is no objection and the document is undisputed.  That is not the case here as Plaintiff has levied an objection.  Therefore, Defendants’ argument is premature.

The Demurrer is overruled.  An answer is to be filed within 20 days.


  1. Motion to Strike 

Defendants also seek to strike the Complaint’s references to punitive damages.

Motions to strike are used to challenge defects in the pleadings not subject to demurrer.  Any party may move to strike the whole or any part of a pleading within the time allotted to respond to the pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 435(b)(1).)  The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the Court is required to take judicial notice.  (Id. § 437(a).)  The Court may strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading, and strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Id. § 436.)  An “irrelevant” matter includes any “demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.”  (Id. § 431.10(b)(3), (c); see also Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1036-1042.)

Because the fraud claim has survived, the Motion to Strike is denied.
