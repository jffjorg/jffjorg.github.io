     CASE NO.: 23VECV05457

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT SALMA HALLOUMI’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT SALMA HALLOUMI’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSE TO FORM INTERROGATORIES

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT SALMA HALLOUMI’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSE TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT SALMA HALLOUMI’S MOTION TO HAVE THE TRUTH OF THE MATTERS SPECIFIED IN HER REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION BE DEEMED ADMITTED

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This personal injury case arises out of a pedestrian/motor vehicle accident that allegedly took place between Plaintiff Diane Williams (“Plaintiff”) and Defendants Salma Halloumi (“Salma”) and Mohamed Halloumi (collectively, “Defendants”) on April 6, 2023.

Salma now seeks to compel Plaintiff’s responses to her request for production of documents (“RFPs”), her form and special interrogatories (“FROGS” and “SROGS”, respectively), and also requests an order deeming the truth of the matters admitted in her requests for admissions (“RFAs”).

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On December 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging negligence.

On April 8, 2024, Defendants filed their Answer.

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff substituted her attorney to represent herself.

On July 18, 2024, Salma filed her Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to her RFPs (“Motion RFPs”).

On July 18, 2024, Salma filed her Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to her FROGS (“Motion FROGS”).

On July 18, 2024, Salma filed her Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to her SROGS (“Motion SROGS”).

On July 18, 2024, Salma filed her Motion to have the Truth of the Matters Specified in her RFAs Be Deemed Admitted (“Motion RFAs”).

On September 30, 2024, Salma filed her Notices of Plaintiff’s Non-Opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Requests for Production

 A party upon whom a demand for the production of documents has been made has 30 days after service of the demand to respond. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)[1] The propounding and responding parties may agree to extend the time for response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.270(a).) When a party fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a motion to compel response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b).) A responding party that does not provide responses waives the right to object to the demand, including objections based on privilege or work product. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).) 

B.             Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)    

C.             Requests for Admission

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared…” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).)

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)

I.        INTRODUCTION

This case is a negligence action by Plaintiff Craig Schumacher (“Plaintiff”) against Defendants Michael Anthony Manzanero (“Manzanero”); Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Uber”); Rasier, LLC. (“Rasier”); and Does 1-20 (collectively “Defendants”).

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the complaint, alleging: (1) Motor Vehicle Negligence against all Defendants (2) Negligence against all Defendants; (3) Gross Negligence/Reckless Misconduct-Violation of California Civil Code §1714 against Uber, Rasier, Manzanero and Does 1 through 10.

On September 11, 2024, Defendants Uber and Rasier (“Moving Defendants”) filed their Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for Gross Negligence/Reckless Misconduct-Violation of California Civil Code §1714.

On September 11, 2024, defendants Uber and Rasier filed their Motion to Strike punitive damages from Plaintiff’s complaint.

On September 12, 2024, Manzanero filed an answer to the complaint.

On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed their Opposition to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

On October 02, 2024, Uber and Rasier filed a Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

          A.       Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007 (citation omitted); see also Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.”  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App. 3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).  

          B.      Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (See Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (See id.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (See id.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.) 

To succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.) The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. (See Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.) 

IV.     MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

          Prior to filing the instant demurrer and motion to strike, Moving Defendants’ counsel initiated meet and confer efforts with Plaintiff’s counsel via a four page letter dated August 9, 2024. (See Declaration of Kym Kanenaka (“Kanenaka Decl.”), ¶ 4, Ex. B.) On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded via telephone call and refused to dismiss the third cause of action or strike the claims for punitive damages. (See id., ¶ 5.) The Court therefore finds the parties have fulfilled their meet and confer requirements.

V.       ANALYSIS 

Moving Defendants demurrer to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for gross negligence or reckless misconduct. Moving Defendants also move to strike punitive damages from Plaintiff’s complaint.

Ultimately, the Court will sustain the Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action with leave to amend. The motion to strike will be denied as moot.

A.             Summary of Allegations in the Complaint

Plaintiff alleges that on or about April 2, 2023, while operating his 2018 Mercedes Benz E-Class vehicle on Topanga Canyon Blvd. in Los Angeles, the Manzanero’s vehicle collided with Plaintiff’s vehicle, causing multiple bodily injuries and property damage. (Complaint ¶¶8-15).

Plaintiff alleges that at the time of the incident, Defendants were operating Manzanero’s vehicle as a commercial hire, transporting a passenger. Furthermore, Plaintiff alleges that Manzanero operated his vehicle “at an unsafe speed far in excess of posted speed limits…” and “acting in a knowingly unsafe, reckless and dangerous manner.” (Id. at ¶23).

In addition to seeking general and special damages, Plaintiff is also seeking punitive damages in connection to his third cause of action for gross negligence or reckless misconduct. (See id. at p.7).

B.             Plaintiff’s Third Cause of Action For Gross Negligence Is Improperly Pled.

Defendants Uber and Rasier contend the Court should sustain their demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for gross negligence or reckless misconduct because “there is no separate cause of action for gross negligence that is separate and apart from ordinary negligence ‘independent of statutory basis’.” (Demurrer, p. 4). Furthermore, they claim Plaintiff’s third cause of action is “a conclusory allegation” lacking factual support for the contention that Defendants acted with gross negligence. (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff first contends California law permits maintaining a separate cause of action for gross negligence, citing Weber v. Pinyan (1937) 9 Cal.2d 226, 231. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends it has adequately pleaded gross negligence.

Generally, in addition to alleging the traditional elements of negligence, gross negligence also requires plaintiffs to allege “‘an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct’” or “‘want of even scant care’.” (Chavez v. 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 632, 640; see also City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 747, 754.) As Moving Defendants explain in their Reply, California generally “does not recognize a distinct cause of action for ‘gross negligence’ independent of a statutory basis.” (Saenz v. Whitewater Voyages (1990) 226 Cal.App.3d 758, 766.)

Here, Plaintiff alleges Defendants – ostensibly via Manzanero – acted in a “knowingly unsafe, reckless and dangerous manner.” (Complaint, ¶ 23). However, Plaintiff failed to provide either a statutory basis or a factual basis upon which a fact finder could conclude Defendants’ conduct represented an extreme departure from what a reasonably careful person would do in the same situation.

As a result of these shortcomings, the Court SUSTAINS the Moving Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for gross negligence WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

C.             The Court Denies Moving Defendants’ Motion To Strike As Moot.

Having sustained Moving Defendants’ demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for gross negligence, their motion to strike is DENIED as moot because it focuses on Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages arising out of that very same cause of action.

VI.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court SUSTAINS the Court SUSTAINS the Moving Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for gross negligence WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

          Plaintiff has until November 1, 2024, to amend his Complaint.

          IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER GRANTING PETITIONER’S PETITION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

I.        BACKGROUND 

Petitioners Mher Gevorgyan and Nazeli Cherkezyan (collectively, “Petitioners”) allege they were passengers in a vehicle operated by Lyft, Inc. when they were hit by an uninsured motorist who then fled the scene. (See Petition to Compel Arbitration and for an Appointment of a Neutral Arbitrator (“Petition”), pg. 4.) At the time of the accident, Lyft, Inc. had an insurance policy providing uninsured or underinsured motorist coverage with Respondent Mobilitas Insurance Company (“Respondent”). (See id.) Petitioners claim they are covered under Respondent’s insurance policy and that their demands to Respondent to arbitrate their claims have gone unanswered. (See id.)

Petitioners now seek to compel Respondent arbitrate their uninsured motorist claim.   

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 26, 2024, Petitioners filed their Petition.

On September 3, 2024, Petitioners filed their Notice of Petition to Compel Arbitration.

Although apparently properly served on September 10, 2024, Respondent has not appeared in the case.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2[1] permits a party to file a petition to request that the Court order the parties to arbitrate a controversy.¿The Court may grant the motion if the Court determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless the petitioner has waived the right to arbitrate or there are other grounds for rescission. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2(a)-(b).)

Insurance Code § 11580.2 establishes certain rules governing uninsured and underinsured motor vehicle insurance policies. Insurance Code § 11580.2(f) requires that “the determination as to whether the insured shall be legally entitled to recover damages ... shall be made by agreement between the insured and the insurer or, in the event of disagreement, by arbitration.” The provisions of Insurance Code § 11580.2 are deemed to be a part of every uninsured motorist policy. (See Quintano v. Mercury Casualty Co. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1049, 1053; Harford Fire Insurance Co. v. Macri (1992) 4 Cal.4th 318, 324; Travelers Indemnity Co. v. Kowalski (1965) 233 Cal.App.2d 607, 609.)   

When a motion to compel arbitration is filed and accompanied by prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy, the court itself must determine whether the agreement exists and, if any defense to its enforcement is raised, whether it is enforceable.¿(See Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Sec. Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) The moving party bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition.¿(Id.)¿¿

There are two “common themes” in case law interpreting the scope of arbitration under Insurance Code § 11580.2(f): “(1) arbitration of issues other than liability and damages is appropriate if the parties have contractually agreed to arbitrate more than is required by section 11580.2, subdivision (f); and, (2) only issues of liability and damages may be decided in an arbitral forum if the parties did not agree to arbitrate more than is required by section 11580.2, subdivision (f).” (Bouton v. USAA Casualty Insurance Co. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 1190, 1197.)¿¿

“If the insured has or may have rights to benefits, other than nonoccupational disability benefits, under any workers’ compensation law, the arbitrator shall not proceed with the arbitration until the insured's physical condition is stationary and ratable. In those cases in which the insured claims a permanent disability, the claims shall, unless good cause be shown, be adjudicated by award or settled by compromise and release before the arbitration may proceed.” (Ins. Code § 11580.2(f).)¿

“Any demand or petition for arbitration shall contain a declaration, under penalty of perjury, stating whether (i) the insured has a workers' compensation claim; (ii) the claim has proceeded to findings and award or settlement on all issues reasonably contemplated to be determined in that claim; and (iii) if not, what reasons amounting to good cause are grounds for the arbitration to proceed immediately.” (Ins. Code § 11580.2(f).)¿

IV.          ANALYSIS

Petitioners argue they are covered under Respondent’s insurance policy with Lyft, Inc. and that the insurance policy contains an uninsured motorist arbitration provision. (See Petition, pg. 2, ¶¶ 3-4.) Petitioners’ counsel declares he sent a settlement demand letter to Respondent on May 13, 2024, and again on June 11, 2024, but that Respondent did not respond. (See Declaration of Mihran Ghazaryan filed August 26, 2024 (“Ghazaryan Decl.”), ¶ 4.) Petitioners’ counsel further declares he initiated the uninsured motorist coverage arbitration by serving the Respondent, via certified mail and via email, with a formal demand for arbitration. (See Ghazaryan Decl., ¶ 6.) Because Respondent has not responded to Petitioners’ letters or formal settlement demand, Petitioners request this Court compel Respondent to arbitrate Petitioners’ claims and appoint a neutral arbitrator. (See Petition, pp. 2-3.)

In support of their request, Petitioners have attached the insurance policy containing the arbitration provision and a copy of their demand letter. (See Petition, Exs. A, B.)

The Court finds Petitioners are entitled to an order compelling Respondent to arbitrate their claims and appointing a neutral arbitrator. As a preliminary matter, Petitioners are covered under the insurance policy. (See Ins. Code § 11580.2(b) (stating uninsured motorist coverage applies to “any other person while in or upon or entering into or alighting from an uninsured motor vehicle”).) The arbitration provision states “[i]f we and an ‘insured’ disagree whether the ‘insured’ is legally entitled to recover damages from the owner or driver of an ‘uninsured motor vehicle’ or do not agree as to the amount of damages that are recoverable by that ‘insured’, the disagreement will be settled by arbitration. Such arbitration may be initiated by a written demand for arbitration made by either party. The arbitration shall be conducted by a single neutral arbitrator. However, disputes concerning coverage under this endorsement may not be arbitrated. Each party will bear the expenses of the arbitrator equally.” (Petition, Ex. A (emphasis added).) “Insured” is in turn defined as “[a]nyone ‘occupying’ a covered ‘auto’ or a temporary substitute for a covered ‘auto’.” (Id.)

Because they are covered under the insurance policy, and because Petitioners have included the workers’ compensation declaration required by Ins. Code § 11580.2(f), Petitioners can petition this Court to enforce the arbitration agreement and compel Respondent arbitrate their claims. (See Petition, Ex. B.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Petitioners’ Petition to Compel Arbitration. The Court stays the matter pending arbitration completion. The Court will hold a Status Conference re: Arbitration on May 9, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. The parties are ordered to file a Joint Status Update on or before May 2, 2025.
