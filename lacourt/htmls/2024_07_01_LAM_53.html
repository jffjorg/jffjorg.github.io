Case Number: 22STCV12687    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Plaintiff,

 

 

vs.

 

 

nissan north america, inc. , et al.;

 

Defendants.

July 1, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Gustavo Rosas         

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants Nissan North America, Inc., and Wish Automotive, Inc., d/b/a Nissan of Van Nuys

Motion for Attorney’s Fees

The court considered the amended moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Gustavo Rosas (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding attorney’s fees and costs in his favor and against defendants Nissan North America, Inc. and Wish Automotive, Inc., d/b/a Nissan of Van Nuys (“Defendants”) in the total amount of $23,367.15, consisting of $22,388.50 in attorney’s fees and $978.65 in costs.

First, the court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees pursuant to the Song-Beverly Act.

Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d), provides:¿ “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.”¿¿ 

Plaintiff has presented evidence, and Defendants do not dispute, that (1) the parties settled this action for the amount of $36,000 on Mrach 23, 2024, and (2) Defendants’ offer (which Plaintiff accepted) “provided for Plaintiff’s ‘attorney’s fees and costs reasonably incurred by Plaintiff[,]’” by either stipulation or noticed motion presented to the court.  (Saeedian Decl., ¶¶ 20-21.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees and costs from Defendants pursuant to Civil Code section 1794.

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has established, as to the attorney’s fees incurred in connection with the commencement and prosecution of this action and the preparation of this fee motion, a lodestar amount of $20,697.50.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted); Reck v. FCA US LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 682, 691 [“To determine a reasonable attorney fee award, the trial court applies the lodestar method”].)  “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿¿ 

Plaintiff has submitted the declaration of attorney Michael Saeedian, in which Saeedian attests to the qualifications, skill, and experience of the attorneys that worked on this action.  (Saeedian Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4-5.)  The court finds that a reasonable hourly rate for attorney Michael Saeedian is $600.  The court finds that (1) the $525 hourly rate charged by Christopher Urner is reasonable in light of his experience and qualifications, and (2) the two hourly rates charged by Jorge Acosta—i.e., $250 for work performed as a law clerk and $350 for work performed as an attorney—are reasonable in light of his experience and qualifications.  (Saeedian Decl., ¶¶ 4-5, 23.) 

The court has reviewed (1) each work and time entry on the billing statement attached to Saeedian’s declaration, which establish that Plaintiff’s attorneys expended a total of 46.4 hours in prosecuting this action (consisting of 17.8 hours expended by Michael Saeedian, 8.5 hours expended by Christopher Urner, 14.8 hours expended by Jorge Acosta as a law clerk, and 5.3 hours expended by Acosta as an attorney), and (2) Defendants’ objections to the 28 entries set forth in their opposition papers.  (Saeedian Decl., Ex. A, Billing Statement; Saeedian Decl., ¶ 23; Opp., p. 6:1-28.)  Upon the court’s review of the billing statement and Defendants’ opposition papers, the court finds that all of the 46.4 hours were reasonably expended.  (Saeedian Decl., Ex. A.)   Moreover, while the court notes that Defendants take issue with the billing entries by Acosta on the ground that he was an “operations manager, not an assigned attorney on the matter[,]” the court disagrees that they are unreasonable, because Saeedian has explained in his declaration that, as operations manager, Acosta “work[s] on active cases in litigation” and “directly handles the litigation of lemon law cases for clients in [the] office as the primary assigned attorney.”  (Opp., p. 7:12-15; Saeedian Decl., ¶ 5.)

The court therefore finds that Plaintiff has shown that his attorneys reasonably expended a total of 46.4 hours in connection with the commencement and prosecution of this action at the hourly rates of $600 (Michael Saeedian), $525 (Christopher Under), $250 (Jorge Acosta, law clerk), and $350 (Jorge Acosta, attorney).  (Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).)

Third, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown that he is entitled to costs in the amount of $978.65, consisting of $526.68 in filing fees, $168.52 in jury fees, $100 in service of process fees, and $91.46 in electronic filing and/or service fees.  (MC-010, ¶¶ 1, 2, 5, 14, 16; Opp., p. 2:19-20 [“Defendants do not dispute the costs and expenses of $978.65 for purposes of this motion”]; Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1486 [“A ‘verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of the propriety’ of the items listed on it, and the burden is on the party challenging these costs to demonstrate that they were not reasonable or necessary”].)  

The court therefore finds that Plaintiff has shown that he is entitled to recover $20,697.50 in attorney’s fees and $978.65 in costs from Defendants.  (Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).)

ORDER

            The court grants in part plaintiff Gustavo Rosas’s motion for attorney’s fees as follows.

            The court orders that plaintiff Gustavo Rosas shall recover a total of $21,676.15 from defendants Nissan North America, Inc., and Wish Automotive, Inc., d/b/a Nissan of Van Nuys, consisting of $20,697.50 in attorney’s fees and $978.65 in costs, pursuant to Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d).

            The court orders plaintiff Gustavo Rosas to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTIES:              Plaintiffs Eduardo Luna and Sinai Mariel Valverde Dominguez    

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:     Defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc.     

Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs Eduardo Luna and Sinai Mariel Valverde Dominguez (“Plaintiffs”) move the court for an order granting them leave to file a First Amended Complaint (1) to clarify that Plaintiffs are alleging both individual and derivative claims against defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc. (“Defendants”), (2) to plead facts relating to the formation of the new homeowner’s association and to add the new association as a defendant, and (3) to plead additional facts regarding Defendants’ alleged misconduct.  (Kurtz Decl., ¶¶ 2-3, subd. (b).)

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect . . . .  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., §¿473, subd. (a)(1).) 

The court finds that (1) it is in furtherance of justice to allow Plaintiffs to amend their complaint to clarify that they are bringing derivative claims and to plead additional facts regarding the newly formed homeowner’s association and Defendants’ alleged wrongful acts or omissions, and (2) Defendants have not shown that (i) Plaintiffs’ approximate four-month delay was unwarranted and justifies denying their motion, or (ii) Defendants will be unduly prejudiced by the proposed amendment. 

First, the court finds that Plaintiffs have shown that (1) it is in furtherance of justice to allow them to amend their pleading in the manner requested, and (2) permitting amendment is supported by the liberal policy favoring leave to amend.  (Tung v. Chicago Title Company (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 734, 747 [“Statutes like section 473 are ‘construed liberally so that cases might be tried upon their merits in one trial where no prejudice to the opposing party . . . is demonstrated’”] [internal citation omitted].)

Second, the court finds that Defendants have not shown that Plaintiffs’ delay in filing this motion was unwarranted and therefore supports denial of this motion.

Plaintiffs’ counsel has stated, in his declaration, that (1) “[t]he major impetus for the amendment is the Court’s ruling on the disqualification motion, where it was pointed out that the operative complaint may be interpreted in a way that did not include derivative claims[,]” such that (2) the court’s ruling “gave rise to the need to amend the complaint.”  (Kurtz Decl., ¶ 4.)  As noted by Defendants, the court issued its ruling denying plaintiff Eduardo Luna’s motion for disqualification on October 31, 2023.  (Oct. 31, 2023 Order, p. 4:14.)  Plaintiffs filed the pending motion a little under four-and-a-half months later, on March 12, 2024.  The court finds that this approximate four-month delay does not constitute “unwarranted delay” that “justifies denial of leave to amend.”  (Miles v. City of Los Angeles (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 728, 739.)  

Third, the court finds that Defendants have not shown that they will be unduly prejudiced by the amendment.  Although Defendants assert that they will need the opportunity to challenge the amended pleading and potentially file appropriate dispositive motions, they may still do so before trial, which is set to begin on November 20, 2024.  Moreover, to the extent that Defendants are concerned that they will be unable to set a hearing on responsive pleadings directed to the First Amended Complaint before trial, Defendants may file an ex parte application or other appropriate motion requesting that the court advance any hearing that is scheduled after trial.

            Thus, the court grants Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to file first amended complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a); Tung, supra, 63 Cal.App.5th at p. 747 [liberal policy of granting leave to amend “applies to amendments at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial, absent prejudice to the adverse party”] [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiffs Eduardo Luna and Sinai Mariel Valverde Dominguez’s motion for leave to file first amended complaint.

            The court orders plaintiffs Eduardo Luna and Sinai Mariel Valverde Dominguez to file their First Amended Complaint, in the form attached to the declaration of Gary Kurtz as Exhibit 2, within 5 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders plaintiffs Eduardo Luna and Sinai Mariel Valverde Dominguez to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Royal Halo, LLC

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Ricardo Hernandez Bezares

(3)   Request for Court Judgment by Default

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with each motion to set aside default.  No reply papers to those motions were filed.

The court considered the default judgment papers filed on April 5, 2024.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Asiana Resources, Inc., d/b/a Asiana USA, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed its Complaint in this action on October 11, 2023, against defendants Royal Halo, LLC (“Royal”) and Ricardo Hernandez Bezares (“Bezares”) (collectively, “Defendants”).

The clerk entered (1) Royal’s default on December 13, 2023, and (2) Bezares’s default on December 18, 2023.

Now pending before the court are the following three matters: (1) defendant Royal’s motion to set aside the entry of its default, (2) defendants Bezares’s motion to set aside the entry of his default, and (3) Plaintiff’s request for court judgment by default against Defendants.[1]

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFENDANT ROYAL’S DEFAULT

Defendant Royal moves the court for an order setting aside its default, entered by the clerk on December 13, 2023.

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)  “[T]he moving party bears the burden of establishing a right to relief” under this statute.  (Austin v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 918, 928.)  

First, the court finds that Royal’s motion is timely because (1) its default was entered on December 13, 2023, and (2) it filed this motion on April 23, 2024, i.e., within six months after its default was entered.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928 [“This six-month time limitation is jurisdictional”].)  

Second, the court finds that Royal has not met its burden to show that its default was entered because of its “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928.)

Royal does not appear to dispute that it was personally served with the summons and complaint on October 16, 2023.  (Oct. 25, 2023 POS-010, ¶¶ 2-5; Bezares Decl., ¶ 2 [“Plaintiff asserts that it effected personal service by delivering the Summons and Complaint to Royal’s authorized agent for service of process on October 16, 2023”].)  However, defendant Bezares—the managing member of Royal—asserts, in his declaration, that he attempted to file Royal’s answer to the Complaint on December 6, 2023 (i.e., more than 30 days after the date of service of the summons).  (Bezares Decl., ¶¶ 1, 3.)  Royal’s answer was rejected by the clerk because the case name did not match the court’s records.  (Bezares Decl., ¶ 3; Bezares Decl., Ex. A [Notice of Rejection of answer submitted on December 6, 2023].)  Bezares was not aware that the answer had been rejected, and he discovered that fact when he attempted to participate in the February 8, 2024 case management conference.  (Bezares Decl., ¶ 5.)

While the court acknowledges that Royal has presented evidence establishing that it attempted to file an answer and did not discover its rejection until February 8, 2024, the court finds that Royal has not presented argument or evidence establishing that its default was taken as a result of this conduct.  Specifically, Royal did not address its failure to file an answer within 30 days of October 16, 2023 (i.e., the date that summons was served).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20, subd. (a)(3).)  By the time that Royal attempted to file its answer, the deadline to do so had passed.  (Ibid.)  Royal did not present evidence explaining (1) why it failed to timely file an answer and (2) why such failure constitutes excusable neglect, mistake, or inadvertence.  Thus, even if the court were to find that Royal’s conduct, in attempting to file an answer and discovering its rejection two months later, constituted excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, Royal has not shown that it was this conduct that caused its default to be entered, since Royal attempted to file its answer beyond the 30-day deadline.  (County of San Bernardino v. Mancini (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1102 [“Section 473(b) authorizes the trial court to vacate a dismissal, order, or judgment if it occurred because of the excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect of a party or its attorney”] [emphasis added].)  

The court also notes that, even if Royal had filed its answer on that date, Royal attempted to do so as a self-represented corporate entity, such that its answer would have been invalid.  (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1145 [“a corporation, unlike a natural person, cannot represent itself before courts of record in propria persona, nor can it represent itself through a corporate officer, director, or other employee who is not an attorney.  It must be represented by licensed counsel in proceedings before courts of record”].)

Thus, the court finds that Royal has not met its burden “of establishing a right to relief” and therefore denies Royal’s motion.  (Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928; Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFENDANT BEZARES’S DEFAULT

Defendant Bezares moves the court for an order setting aside his default, entered by the clerk on December 18, 2023, on the grounds that (1) service of the summons did not result in actual notice in time to defend the lawsuit, and (2) his lack of actual notice was not caused by avoidance or inexcusable neglect.

“When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action.  The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)  A motion made pursuant to this statute must be accompanied by (1) an affidavit showing that the lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, and (2) a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (b).)

First, the court finds that Bezares’s motion is timely because (1) his default was entered on December 18, 2023, and (2) he filed this motion on April 23, 2024, i.e., within two years after the entry of his default.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)

Second, the court finds that Bezares has not met his burden (1) to show that he did not have actual knowledge of this case in time to defend himself in this action, or (2) to submit “an affidavit showing under oath that [Bezares’s] lack of actual notice in time to the defend the action was not caused by his . . . avoidance of service or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subds. (a), (b).) 

Although Bezares submitted a declaration in support of this motion, his declaration does not state that he did not receive actual notice of this lawsuit in time to file an answer, nor does the declaration state the date on which Bezares learned that he was a defendant to this action.  For example, while Bezares states that he first learned of his default on January 10, 2024, Bezares did not state that he did not have actual knowledge of this action at this time, or that the lack of such notice was not taken as a result of his avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.  (Bezares Decl., ¶ 5.)  Further, while Bezares’s motion states that he first learned of this lawsuit on February 8, 2024 (which the court notes appears to be inconsistent with his representation that he learned of his default on January 10, 2024), Bezares did not state that fact in his declaration.  (Mot., pp. 4:12, 4:17-18, 7:22-23.)  Bezares also did not state that his lack of actual notice was not caused by his avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, as required.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (b).)

The court notes that Bezares has also argued that service was improper, such that the default is void for lack of jurisdiction.  (Mot., pp. 5:26-7:5.)  However, Bezares did not move for relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d), which provides for relief from void judgment or orders.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (d); Mot., pp. 5:1-4 [beginning argument under section 473.5], 7:7-10 [beginning argument under section 473, subdivision (b)].)

Third, the court finds that Bezares has not met his burden to show that his default was entered because of his “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”[2]  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928.)

It appears that Bezares contends that he is entitled to relief pursuant to section 473, subdivision (b) on the ground that he “was not aware that he was named as a defendant in his individual capacity until February 8, 2024.”  (Mot., p. 7:22-23.)  However, Bezares did not submit evidence in support of that assertion.  Moreover, while Bezares states that he “first learned of the default entered against” him on January 10, 2024, he did not state facts establishing when he discovered that he was a defendant to this action.  (Bezares Decl., ¶ 5 [emphasis added].)  Bezares also did not present argument establishing what act of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect caused his default to be entered.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Mancini (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1102.)  Bezares did not, for example, argue that his default was entered as a result of his surprise based on improper service.  Instead, Bezares has only generally argued that he did not discover that he had been sued in this action until February 8, 2024.

Thus, the court finds that Bezares has not met his burden “of establishing a right to relief” and therefore denies Bezares’s motion.  (Austin, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 928; Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR COURT JUDGMENT BY DEFAULT

Plaintiff requests that the court enter default judgment against Defendants in the total amount of $78,210.85, consisting of $73,832.88 in damages, $2,431.77 in interest, and $1,946.20 in costs.

First, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown that it is entitled to damages in the amount of $73,832.88.  (Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 880 [“plaintiffs in default cases must still prove their damages to obtain monetary recovery”]; Compl., ¶¶ 13, 15, 17, 20, 25, 27; Cohen Decl., ¶¶ 2-3, 5, 6, 7-8; Cohen Decl., Ex. A [Personal Guarantee of Payment executed by Bezares]; Cohen Decl., Ex. B [Invoices issued by Plaintiff to Royal in total amount of $73,832.88].)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that it may recover prejudgment interest because Plaintiff did not request interest in its Complaint and “the relief awarded in such a default judgment ‘cannot exceed’ the ‘type and amount of relief’ sought in the plaintiff’s operative pleadings.”  (Compl., p. 6, Prayer; Sass, supra, 32 Cal.App.5th at p. 1034; Code Civ. Proc., § 580, subd. (a).)

The court notes that Plaintiff appears to contend that it may recover interest notwithstanding its failure to pray for interest, citing North Oakland Medical Clinic v. Rogers (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 824.  (Urasi Decl., p. 3, n. 1.)  The court acknowledges that, there, the court stated that “[a] general prayer in the complaint is adequate to support an award of prejudgment interest.  ‘No specific request for interest need be included in the complaint; a prayer seeking “such other and further relief as may be proper” is sufficient for the court to invoke its power to award prejudgment interest.  [Citations.]’”  (North Oakland Medical Clinic, supra, 65 Cal.App.4th at p. 829.)  However, that case concerned the appeal from an order denying prejudgment interest on a damages award following a jury trial, and not the award of prejudgment interest on default.  (Id. at p. 826.)  Thus, this case appears to reflect the principle that prejudgment interest may be awarded after trial even if the complaint does not pray for interest.  (Newby v. Vroman (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 283, 286 [“It has long been settled that, in a contested action, prejudgment interest may be awarded even though the complaint contains no prayer for interest”].)  The court therefore finds that Plaintiff is not entitled to an award of prejudgment interest on default judgment.

Third, the court finds that the requested costs are allowable as costs.  (CIV-100, ¶ 7; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subds. (a)(1) [filing fees], (a)(4) [process server fees].)

Thus, the court grants Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Defendants in the modified amount of $75,779.08, consisting of $73,832.88 in damages and $1,946.20 in costs. 

ORDER

            The court denies defendant Royal Halo, LLC’s motion to set aside default.

            The court denies defendant Ricardo Hernandez Bezares’s motion to set aside default.

            The court grants in part plaintiff Asiana Resources, Inc., d/b/a Asiana USA, Inc.’s request for court judgment by default.

The court will enter default judgment in favor of plaintiff Asiana Resources, Inc., d/b/a Asiana USA, Inc. and against defendants Royal Halo, LLC and Ricardo Hernandez Bezares, jointly and severally, in the modified amount of $75,779.08.

            The court orders plaintiff Asiana Resources, Inc., d/b/a Asiana USA, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.
