DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV02552    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING ATTORNEY JASON A. FEAZELL’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a breach of contract action arising from a settlement agreement (“Agreement”) between Defendant Luanne Lihjy Chang (“Defendant” or “Chang”) and Jeanette Francis (“Francis”) for Chang to make compensatory payments to Francis over a thirteen (13) year period, and provided for Francis to assign the payments to her living trust without approval from Chang.

          Plaintiff Gloria Hopper (“Plaintiff”), as Trustee of the Francis Family Survivor’s Trust alleges on September 12, 2023, Francis assigned all of her interest/rights in the Agreement to the Francis Family Survivor’s Trust. (Compl., ¶ BC-6.) Francis also provided for Plaintiff to enforce the such rights under the Agreement. (Id.) Plaintiff further alleges Chang failed to make the payments due 9/1/2023 and 10/1/23. (Compl., ¶BC-2.) Plaintiff further alleges she emailed a Written Notice of Default and acceleration of payments to Chang’s attorney pursuant to the Agreement. (Id.) Plaintiff also alleges she mailed a copy of the Written Notice of Default and acceleration of payments to Chang and her attorney. (Id.) Plaintiff also alleges Chang was given the right to cure by 10/31/23 and failed to do so. (Id.) The remaining principal balance on the Agreement is $950,000.00. (Compl., ¶BC-4.)

 

          On February 7, 2024, Defendant Chang filed an Answer.

 

          On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One) and a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Admissions (Set One). The Court took these matters under submission.

 

On August 22, 2024, the Court granted both discovery motions and ordered sanctions against Defendant Chang’s former counsel Jason A. Feazell in the amount of $3,320.00 payable within 30 days.

 

          On September 3, 2024, Jason A. Feazell (“Feazell”) filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration of Court’s August 22, 2024. On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On September 25, 2024, Feazell filed a reply.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008, subdivision (a) provides that any party affected by an order of the Court “may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

 

As it relates to new or different facts, circumstances, or law under Code of Civil P § 1008(a), “the moving party must provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Shiffer v. CBS Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 246, 255.) Furthermore, “facts of which the party seeking reconsideration was aware at the time of the original ruling are not “new or different.” (In re Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1468.)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          Defendant Chang’s former counsel of record, Jason A. Feazell, moves for reconsideration of the Court’s August 22, 2024 order as to the granting of sanctions in the amount of $3,320.00. As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Defendant Chang was not served with notice of the present motion per the Proof of Service attached therein. Nevertheless, the Court will exercise its discretion and rule on the merits.

 

          Feazell contends he could not provide supplemental responses to the propounded discovery or file an opposition to the discovery motions on Defendant Chang’s behalf due to several matters that cannot be explained without divulging any privileged or confidential information. Feazell further contends it was these matters that led to him to substitute out of the case.

As to the substitution of attorney, Feazell argues the substitution forms were sent to Defendant Chang on June 24, 2024.  Feazell subsequently followed up with her regarding either discovery responses and/or the signed substitution. Feazell contends that after getting into contact with Defendant Chang, he re-sent the substitution forms on July 24, 2024. Feazell asserts that Defendant Chang electronically signed the substitution on July 31, 2024, which Feazell only found out about on August 6, 2024 when he returned from being out of the office. Finally, Feazell argues that he kept Plaintiff’s counsel apprised of the situation at all times.

          In opposition, Plaintiff argues the discovery motions were filed on June 17, 2024, however, the Substitution of Attorney signed on June 24, 2024 was not filed until August 7, 2024. As such, Plaintiff contends Feazell was fully aware the discovery motions were seeking sanctions against him and Defendant Chang but filed no opposition to the discovery motions. Plaintiff also contends the present motion fails because Feazell does not specify why any purported new or different facts, law or circumstances could not have been presented by him in opposition to the underlying discovery motions of which he was fully aware.

 

          The Court finds that Feazell fails to establish new or different facts, circumstances, or law to support reconsideration of the sanctions ordered in the August 22, 2024 order. First, Feazell was aware of the filing of the underlying discovery motions including the sanctions sought against him. Next, Feazell was aware of the fact that Defendant Chang had not signed the substitution forms on June 24, 2024 but on July 31, 2024. Despite Feazell’s awareness of these facts or circumstances, the present motion does not indicate why he failed to present these facts to the Court at an earlier time, such as at the August 22, 2024 hearing or even in opposition to the underlying discovery motions.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, The Court DENIES Attorney Jason A. Feazell’s Motion for Reconsideration of the Court’s August 22, 2024 Order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT VAHE KHACHATRYAN’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS-COMPLAINT

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a tort action arising from a Homeowner’s Association (“HOA”) dispute. Plaintiffs Maureen Gonzalez and Kathleen Lemke (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Central Park North Association (“CPN”), Bonnie Hartounian (“Hartounian”), George Gevorgyani (“Gevorgyani”), Bella Nazarian (“Nazarian”), and Vahe Khachatryan (“Khachatryan”) (collectively, “Defendants”) on March 28, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) fraud; (2) negligence; (3) breach of fiduciary Duty; (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (5) negligent infliction of emotional distress.  On December 7, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).

Plaintiffs allege that CPN manages their condominium complex located at 1422 Central Ave., Glendale 91202 (“Complex”).  Defendants Hartounian, Gevorgyani, and Nazarian (collectively, “CPN Defendants”) sit on CPN’s Board.  Plaintiffs own Unit 2 within the Complex (“Plaintiffs’ Unit”).  Defendant Khachatryan is Plaintiffs’ upstairs neighbor.  According to the SAC, the parties’ dispute began when Khachatryan hired an unlicensed contractor to perform construction work on his own condominium.  The contractor’s sub-par work caused damage to Plaintiffs’ Unit.  Plaintiffs allege that the CPN Defendants interfered with Plaintiffs’ efforts to repair their Unit, including by obstructing access to the HOA’s insurance funds.

          On June 20, 2023, CPN Defendants filed a Cross-Complaint against Defendant Khachatryan, alleging causes of action for (1) indemnity and (2) contribution.

          On January 3, 2024, CPN Defendants filed their Answer to SAC and on January 8, 2024, Defendant Khachatryan filed his Answer to the SAC.

          On August 19, 2024, Defendant Khachatryan filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint (“Motion”).  On August 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a response.  Defendants have not opposed the Motion, and no reply has been filed.  On September 11, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion and requested that Defendant Khachatryan file an amended Motion and the cross-complaint prior to the next scheduled hearing.  No additional papers have been filed.

          On August 20, 2024, the Court granted the CPN Defendants’ Application for Determination of Good Faith Settlement as to the settlement executed by Plaintiffs and Defendants CPN, Hartounian, Gevorgyani, and Nazarian.  Subsequently, on September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendants CPN, Hartounian, Gevorgyani, and Nazarian from the Complaint with prejudice.

          On August 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion to Quash Deposition Subpoenas for Production of Records, scheduled for hearing on October 3, 2024.

          On September 11, 2024, pursuant to the Stipulation of the parties, the Court continued the jury trial scheduled for September 30, 2024, to October 15, 2024.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

“A party against whom a cause of action has been asserted in a complaint or cross-complaint may file a cross-complaint setting forth either or both of the following:

(a) Any cause of action he has against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him…

(b) Any cause of action he has against a person alleged to be liable thereon, whether or not such person is already a party to the action, if the cause of action asserted in his cross-complaint (1) arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause brought against him or (2) asserts a claim, right, or interest in the property or controversy which is the subject of the cause brought against him.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10.)  After the trial date has been set, a party seeking to file a cross-complaint must obtain leave of court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50, subd. (b).)

A cross-complaint may be either compulsory or permissive.  Compulsory cross-complaints are those involving causes of action that arise out of the same transactions or occurrences as the causes of action which the plaintiff alleges in his or her complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c).)  To be considered compulsory, the causes of action must exist at the time an answer to the original cross-complaint is required.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.30. subd. (a).)  All other cross-complaints are permissive.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10.)  Failure to bring a compulsory cross-complaint results in waiver of the right to bring the causes of action in a separate action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.30.)

If the proposed cross-complaint is permissive, leave of court may be granted “in the interest of justice” at any time during the course of the action. (Code Civ. Proc. § 428.50, subd. (c).)  On the other hand, if the proposed cross-complaint is compulsory, then leave must be granted so long as the defendant is acting in good faith.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 426.50.)¿

“The legislative mandate is clear.  A policy of liberal construction of section 426.50 to avoid forfeiture of causes of action is imposed on the trial court.  A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result.”  (Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98-99.)  Bad faith involves conduct, not prompted by an honest mistake, oversight, or neglect, but by some sinister motive or conscious wrongdoing because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity.  (Id. at 99-100.)  Bad faith contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.  ¿(Ibid.)

III.     ANALYSIS

          A. Discussion

          Defendant Khachatryan moves for an order granting leave to file a cross-complaint against CPN Defendants on the grounds that he recently took the deposition of non-party James Harvey from Maurehan Plumbing, which has given rise to a theory of comparative fault.  (Gonter Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. B.)  Defendant Khachatryan contends that he did not have adequate information concerning the subject incident at the time he filed his Answer.  Defendant Khachatryan further contends that the cross-complaint will not cause any additional expense or burden on discovery as it only pertains to indemnification, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief.  He asserts that the relative facts involved are the same and he has acted in good faith by diligently conducting discovery, which has now resulted in him seeking leave to file the proposed cross-complaint.

         

Neither Plaintiffs nor CPN Defendants filed a formal opposition to the present motion.  However, Plaintiffs filed a response and request that the Cross-Complaint be severed to preserve the trial date preference.

          Prior to the September 11, 2024, hearing on this same motion, the Court posted an order tentatively granting Defendant Khachatryan’s motion for leave to amend.  However, at the hearing, Counsel for the CPN raised an issue that the Court had not considered.  Counsel argued that due to the Court’s prior approval of the good faith settlement application by the CPN Defendants (which Defendant Khachatryan had not opposed), Defendant Khachatryan was now foreclosed from proceeding with an indemnity cause of action against those parties.  The Court then realized that Defendant had failed to attach a proposed cross-complaint to his motion.  As such, the Court could not determine which parties Defendant Khachatryan sought to proceed against or what his proposed cause(s) of action were.  Therefore, the Court continued this hearing to today’s date and asked that Defendant Khachatryan submit a supplemental motion addressing these issues as well as provide a copy of the proposed cross-complaint.  Defendant Khachatryan has failed to do so.  It is impossible for the Court to determine whether Defendant Khachatryan’s proposed cross-complaint is legally permissible under Code of Civil Procedure Section 877.6.   Therefore, Court denies the Motion.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant Vahe Khachatryan’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint.

III.      ANALYSIS

(Ibid.)  On August 7, 2024,

at ¶ 4, Ex. A.)  Plaintiffs did not
provide authorization and object to production of their insurance records.  (Id. at ¶ 5.)  In their responses to Defendant Khachatryan’s

B.)  All other communications between

no additional information Defendant would need from Allstate.  (Ibid.)  Moreover, these insurance records are not
relevant to this case or likely to lead to discovery of admissible

secondary insurance to the HOA, Plaintiffs paid for repairs out-of-pocket, and presented
receipts of these costs to Defendant.  (Id.
at ¶ 8.)  Furthermore, counsel Gonzalez

Khachatryan has not opposed the Motion.

Insurance Code section 791.13 provides, in part, that an insurance institution
shall not disclose any personal or privileged information about an individual
collected or received in connection with an insurance transaction unless the

warrant or subpoena.  (Ins. Code., §
791.13, subd. (a), (h).)  Here, the
records are being sought through a subpoena and, thus, may be exempt from the
protections of section 791.13.

of Civil Procedure section 2017.010 permits a party to “obtain discovery
regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter .

calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  According to a leading treatise, “For
discovery purposes, information should be regarded as ‘relevant’ to the subject
matter if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing
for trial, or facilitating settlement.  [Gonzalez

party asserting the privilege only needs to present facts which ‘support a
prima facie claim of privilege.’”  (OXY
Resources California LLC v. Superior Court (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 874, 894.)  After the objecting party meets the burden to
show preliminary facts in support of the privilege, “the burden shifts to the

apply, an exception exists, or there has been an express or implied waiver.”  (Venture Law Group v. Superior Court

The attorney work product doctrine
is codified in section 2018.020 of the Code of Civil Procedure, which provides
in relevant part,

It is the policy of the state to do
both of the following:

(a) Preserve the rights of
attorneys to prepare cases for trial with that degree of privacy necessary to
encourage them to prepare their cases thoroughly and to investigate not only
the favorable but the unfavorable aspects of those cases.

(b) Prevent attorneys from taking
undue advantage of their adversary's industry and efforts.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.020.)

2018.030, subd. (a).)  “The work product

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANT'S DEMURRER

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This action arises out of Plaintiff Gulf Harbour Investments Corporation’s (Plaintiff) purchase of a loan and interests in property resulting in an inadvertent gap in the chain of assignment. On or about June 4, 2002, Tom Larsen (Defendant) was granted an interest in the real property located at 2180 Crawford Ave, Altadena, California, 91001. On or about October 18, 2006, Indymac Bank, F.S.B. executed a note granting a loan to Allison Murao and Defendant in connection with this property. Plaintiff subsequently purchased the loan and interests in the note and deed of trust. After commencing foreclosure proceedings on the property, Plaintiff noticed a gap in the chain of title due to a failure to record Plaintiff’s title. On April 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint with causes of action for an assignment of equitable lien and for declaratory relief to resolve issues in the chain of title.

          On June 18, 2024, Defendant brought the instant demurrer alleging that all causes of action fail to state a cause of action upon which relief can be granted and are uncertain. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc. 430.10 subd. (e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Aubry v. Tri-Defendants Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.)¿ 

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)¿ 

Further, “we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 958 [102 Cal.Rptr.3d 343].).) Because a demurrer tests only the legal sufficiency of the pleading, we accept as true even the most improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff's ability to prove its factual allegations. (Ibid.) ‘“Facts appearing in exhibits attached to the [] complaint also are accepted as true and are given precedence, to the extent they contradict the allegations.”’ (Paul v. Patton (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1091.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

“Even as against a special demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to ‘set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.’” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858). “[S]pecific dates and details . . . are properly addressed during discovery, not on demurrer.” (People v. Highland Fed. Sav. & Loan (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 1692, 1716).

 

III.     ANALYSIS

A.   Meet and Confer Requirement

            Before filing a demurrer, the moving party must meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41.) A demurring party may file a declaration “[t]hat the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3)(B).) “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

          The meet and confer requirement has been met. Defendant’s counsel declares he sent a letter to Plaintiff’s counsel on May 10, 2024. (Alkana Decl., ¶ 2.) He also called Plaintiff’s counsel on May 15, 2024. (Alkana Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant’s counsel received no response. (Alkana Decl., ¶ 2.)

B.    Demurrer

First Cause of Action

          Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and for uncertainty.

“The doctrine of equitable assignments is typically used to enforce an attempted assignment of rights that is technically defective or to create a right of subrogation.” (Recorded Picture Co. v. Nelson Entertainment, Inc. (1967) 53 Cal.App.4th 350, 368, citations omitted.) “[A]n ‘equitable assignment’ is still an ‘assignment.’ ‘To ‘assign’ ordinarily means to transfer title or ownership of property ..., but an assignment, to be effective, must include manifestation to another person by the owner of his intention to transfer the right, without further action, to such other person or to a third person.... It is the substance and not the form of a transaction which determines whether an assignment was intended.... If from the entire transaction and the conduct of the parties it clearly appears that the intent of the parties was to pass title to the [property], then an assignment will be held to have taken place.” (Ibid.)

The court finds that Plaintiff provides sufficient facts to state a cause of action. Here, Plaintiff states it is entitled to an equitable assignment from Indymac Bank. (Complaint ¶ 19.) Plaintiff states that it purchased the loan and interests in the note and deed of trust to 2180 Crawford Ave, Altadena, California, 91001. (Complaint ¶ 11.) Plaintiff attached Defendant’s recorded grant deed and Defendant’s loan documents with Indymac Bank. (Complaint, Exhs. A-B.) Plaintiff states that it holds the original note, is the current payee under the note, and is authorized to enforce the terms of the note and deed of trust. (Complaint ¶ 11.) Further, Plaintiff enforced the terms of its security interest by commencing foreclosure proceedings on the property upon Defendant’s default. (Complaint ¶ 13-14.) Plaintiff learned about now defunct Indymac Bank’s inadvertence, error, or unintentional omission in recording the assignment when Plaintiff obtained the title report and found the gap in the chain of title. (Complaint ¶ 17.) Although Plaintiff does not specify the date or details of purchase, the court only concerns itself with the legal sufficiency of the pleading in deciding a demurrer. (See Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 958.) Plaintiff therefore has pled sufficient facts to allege that Indymac Bank attempted to assign the rights to the property to Plaintiff but failed due to an issue in recording Plaintiff’s title.

Defendant argues that “[t]he Complaint fails to attach any of the underlying documents upon which this action is predicated that would demonstrate that Plaintiff is currently the lawful holder of the note and/or deed of trust. To allege an equitable assignment, Plaintiff must either attach a copy of the assignment to the Complaint or allege verbatim the terms of the assignment.”  However, Defendant offers no authority to support this argument. The Court notes that a complaint need not set forth a detailed recitation of the evidence underlying a Plaintiff’s allegations.  Nor does the law require that a Plaintiff attach all documents upon which a cause of action is based.  For example, in a breach of contract case, California law does not require that a copy of the contract be attached to the complaint or pled verbatim. (See Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Company (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 401-402.)  The logical extension of this rule is that Plaintiff in the present matter need not attach the documents Defendant demands, nor quote from them verbatim. The court overrules the demurrer as to this cause of action.

Further, the court does not find that Defendant has met the high standard to sustain a demurrer on uncertainty grounds. Plaintiff provides the address of the property. (Complaint ¶ 7.) Plaintiff states that it commenced foreclosure proceedings against Defendant on this property. (Complaint ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges ownership of property interests. (Complaint ¶ 11.) Further, Plaintiff identifies itself, its attorneys, and its authority to do business in the County of Los Angeles, California. (Complaint ¶ 1.) The complaint does not include all facts upon which this cause of action is based, but is not incomprehensible. (See A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695.) A demurrer that is missing dates and details is not uncertain because specific dates and details are property addressed during discovery. (See People v. Highland Fed. Sav. & Loan (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 1692, 1716). Therefore, the court does not find that this complaint fails based on uncertainty.

 

Second Cause of Action

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and for uncertainty.

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

Here, Plaintiff provides sufficient facts to support a cause of action for declaratory relief. Plaintiff seeks a judicial declaration that it holds a valid interest in the note and Deed of Trust which is the senior lien on the property and that it is entitled to an equitable assignment of the deed of trust. (Complaint ¶ 23.) Addressing the gap in the chain of title which is missing a record of Plaintiff’s title is a proper subject of declaratory relief. Additionally, whether Plaintiff holds a valid interest in the note and deed of trust based on an assignment from Indymac Bank poses an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to Plaintiff’s rights in the property. Accordingly, the court finds that Plaintiff presents sufficient facts to meet the two elements necessary for a cause of action for declaratory relief.

IV.     ORDER

          Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.

          Plaintiff is to give notice of this ruling.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO BIFURCATE

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This action was filed on April 14, 2016, and arises from a business dispute that occurred on April 16, 2014, resulting in a physical altercation and ultimately, the shooting of Plaintiffs Arutyun Fitilchyan (Fitilchan), Hrachya Gasparyan (Gasparyan) and Defendant Robert Torosian (Robert).

Plaintiffs Fitilchyan, Galina Azumanova, Gasparyan, and Marine Hovhannisyan filed a complaint against Defendants Akop Torosian aka Jack Torosian (Akop), and Robert. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) assault and battery, (2) conspiracy, (3) negligence, (4) loss of consortium, and (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

The complaint alleges that Fitilchyan was contacted by Akop to perform locksmith services for him. Fitilchyan alleges he was on his way to deliver brochures and schedule the service when he ran low on fuel. On telephoning Akop about his tardiness, Akop began to verbally abuse Fitilchyan, which caused Fitilchyan to decide he was no longer willing to provide the services. Fitilchyan alleges that after this he went to visit his friend Gasparyan at his body shop. Fitilchyan alleges that while the two were having coffee at the body shop, Akop showed up and proceeded to scream obscenities at him and the two of them got into a scuffle which body shop employees broke up. Fitilchyan alleges Akop left but then returned with his brother Robert, who charged Fitilchyan and struck him with his fist. He alleges that Akop then produced a gun and began shooting at them, in response to which Gasparyan retrieved a gun and returned fire.

On September 6, 2024, Akop filed this motion to bifurcate. On September 19, 2024, Robert filed a joinder. No opposition has been filed.

II. LEGAL STANDARD

Civil Code, Section 3295, subdivision (d) provides:  

 

“The court shall, on application of any defendant, preclude the admission of evidence of that defendant’s profits or financial condition until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finds that a defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud in accordance with Section 3294. Evidence of profit and financial condition shall be admissible only as to the defendant or defendants found to be liable to the plaintiff and to be guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud. Evidence of profit and financial condition shall be presented to the same trier of fact that found for the plaintiff and found one or more defendants guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud.”  

 

(Id.) 

 

Subdivision (a) of that same section provides: 

 

(a) The court may, for good cause, grant any defendant a protective order requiring the plaintiff to produce evidence of a prima facie case of liability for damages pursuant to Section 3294, prior to the introduction of evidence of: 

(1) The profits the defendant has gained by virtue of the wrongful course of conduct of the nature and type shown by the evidence. 

(2) The financial condition of the defendant. 

(Id.)  

          III.     DISCUSSION

Akop moves for bifurcation of trial in two phases: the first phase to determine liability, compensatory damages, and whether he was guilty of malice, and the second phase to determine the amount of punitive damages. Akop also requests that the Court issue a protective order prohibiting any counsel or witness from mentioning or disclosing his financial status until the second phase of trial.  

 

Akop states there is good cause for the Court to issue a protective order because the causes of action do not require evidence of his financial condition or his profits and the evidence may prejudice the jury. (See Las Palmas Associates v. Las Palmas Center Associates (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1220, 1241, stating there is a presumption that “evidence of a defendant's wealth can induce fact finders to abandon their objectivity and return a verdict based on passion and prejudice.”)  

 

Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition to indicate there is no good cause.  

 

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

          The motion to bifurcate is GRANTED as to both Defendants.

          Defendants are to give notice of this ruling.
