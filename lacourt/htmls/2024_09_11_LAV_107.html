DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV11867    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

SHERYL ROSS ET AL.

 

Plaintiffs,

 

      

   v.

VICTORIA GALLARDO,

Defendants.

Case Number             Department

        20STCV11867    107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

Motion to Reopen Discovery

 

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 25, 2020, plaintiffs Sheryl and Sarina Ross (plaintiffs) filed suit against defendant Victoria Gallardo (defendant). They alleged claims for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence arising out of a car collision between the parties on July 1, 2019. Trial was originally set for September 22, 2021. It was continued many times. Relevant to this motion, on October 12, 2023, trial was continued to June 4, 2024. Just under two weeks before the trial date, on May 24, 2024, Kingsley Forester and Oscar Lopez of Forrester Lopez APC substituted in as counsel for plaintiffs.

 

On June 4, 2024, defendant requested a trial continuance to September 11, 2024, which the court granted. Then on July 10, 2024, on the court’s own motion, it continued the trial to December 9, 2024.

 

Now plaintiffs move for an order reopening discovery and calculating all discovery and witness designation deadlines from the new trial date of December 9, 2024, or, alternatively, an order providing the limited reopening of discovery so plaintiffs may depose defendant’s designated experts.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The motion is GRANTED in part. The court grants a limited reopening of discovery. Discovery is opened for thirty days from the date of this hearing, for the sole purpose of permitting plaintiffs to depose defendant’s designated experts Dr. Jay Tsuruda and Dr. Ranon Udkoff.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Legal Standard

 

The court has the discretion to reopen discovery for good cause shown. The factors for the court to consider include (1) the necessity and reason for the discovery, (2) the diligence or lack of diligence of the party in seeking the discovery and the reason the discovery was not heard earlier, (3) the likelihood the discovery will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, and (4) the length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set and the date presently set for the trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §2024.050, subd. (b).) “[D]ecisions about whether to grant a continuance or extend discovery must be made in an atmosphere of substantial justice. When the two policies collide head-on, the strong public policy favoring disposition on the merits outweighs the competing policy favoring judicial efficiency.” (Hernandez v. Superior Ct. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1246, as modified (Feb. 24, 2004).)

 

Analysis

 

Here, plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause for a limited reopening of discovery.

 

Plaintiffs argue persuasively that deposing defendant’s experts is necessary because defendant has already deposed plaintiffs’ two non-retained medical experts leaving plaintiffs at a severe disadvantage at trial without deposition testimony of their own. They also argue with the depositions, defendant’s experts “will be able to testify without having the veracity of their opinions, qualifications, or potential biases ascertained before trial which will lead to extensive and confusing testimony presented to the jury.” (Mtn., at p 6:12-14.) On the other hand, by taking the expert depositions trial testimony will be significantly streamlined.

 

The court agrees. Plaintiffs will be greatly hindered in their suit if they are unable to depose defendant’s experts, as the expert opinions will be untested until trial. There is also a strong public policy embedded in many of the rules of procedure supporting dispositions on the merits; this motion is no different. (See Hernandez, supra, 115 Cal.App.4th at p. 1246.)

 

Regarding the delay, Plaintiffs’ counsel contends they are bringing the motion now because when they substituted into the case, there was less than two weeks until trial. There was no time to bring the motion. However, the trial has now been unexpectedly continued twice allowing counsel time to meet and confer with defendant about the issue and to make this motion. Plaintiffs attach emails dated July 11, 2024, the day after the court continued the trial to December supporting their efforts to meet and confer. The court finds that this is a sufficient explanation for the delay and demonstrates diligence by plaintiffs.

 

Defendant vigorously opposes the motion on the grounds that plaintiffs have not explained why they could not conduct the discovery earlier. It is clear why plaintiffs’ counsel could not take the depositions earlier; plaintiffs retained them on the eve of trial. With respect to their predecessor’s decisions, that question rests with prior counsel, not  present counsel. Defendant is aware of this; as defendant stated in their meet and confer email “File your Motion. We will oppose it. Discovery was closed while your predecessors had the case. Period.” (Lopez Decl., ¶11, Ex. A at p. 3.)

 

There is no reason to punish plaintiffs for a fortuitous change of circumstances which was outside of their control. Indeed, plaintiffs’ counsels were willing to take the case to trial having just been substituted in and without the deposition testimony of defendant’s experts. It was defendant who requested the first continuance. The depositions can also be conducted prior to trial which means judicial economy will not be hampered by granting the motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is GRANTED in part. The court grants a limited reopening of discovery. Discovery is opened for thirty days from the date of this hearing, for the sole purpose of permitting plaintiffs to depose defendant’s designated experts Dr. Jay Tsuruda and Dr. Ranon Udkoff.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

BACKGROUND

 

            On May 17, 2021, Maria Salgado and Alexis Banuelos (Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Southern California Edison Company, alleging a sole cause of action of wrongful death in negligence. This case stems from a motorcycle accident that took place on December 28, 2019, at the intersection of Florence Avenue and Crofton Avenue in the City of Bell California. (Complaint, ¶¶ 4-5.) (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

On June 22, 2023, Plaintiffs filed an opposition to the motion for summary judgment and requested that the Court continue the hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(h).

 

On June 29, 2023, Defendant filed a reply.

 

On July 6, 2023, the Court continued the hearing at Plaintiffs’ request.

 

            On August 28, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a supplemental opposition.

 

            To date, no supplemental reply has been filed.

 

The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (the Motion). The Court considers the moving papers, the supplemental opposing papers, and the original filed reply on June 29, 2023.

 

Tentative] Ruling

 

            Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            The Court GRANTS SCE’s request for judicial notice.

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice.

 

Legal Standard

 

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

            “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Statement of Facts

 

            SCE’s Statement of Facts

 

On December 28, 2019, Ismael Banuelos Quintero (Decedent) died after being ejected from his motorcycle and striking a “guy wire” located at 5150 East Florence Avenue, Bell, California. (UMF No. 1.) Plaintiffs allege that the guy wire was “dangerous and lacked a covering” that could have prevented the injuries suffered by Decedent. (UMF No. 2.) The guy wire was attached to joint utility pole number 4452100E, which contains both electrical and telecommunications equipment. (UMF Nos. 3-4.) Decedent only made contact with the guy wire and did not contact the pole or any other equipment attached to it. (UMF No. 5.)

 

SCE owns, maintains, and controls the electrical equipment on utility pole number 4452100E but does not own, maintain, or control the telecommunications equipment. (UMF Nos. 6-7.) The guy wire is attached to the telecommunications level of the utility pole. (UMF No. 8.) SCE did not own, maintain, or control the guy wire at any time; instead, a telecommunications company was responsible for its ownership, maintenance, and control. (UMF Nos. 9-10.) SCE also asserts that it does not install, maintain, or repair any facilities not owned by SCE on joint utility poles and did not have any involvement with the guy wire in question. (UMF Nos. 11-12.)

 

Plaintiffs assert a single cause of action against SCE for negligence – wrongful death. (Complaint ¶¶ 15-24.)

 

            Plaintiffs’ Statement of Facts

 

On or about December 28, 2019, Decedent was ejected from his motorcycle and struck a guy wire attached to a jointly owned utility pole, resulting in his death. The utility pole in question was jointly owned by members of the Joint Pole Association (JPA), including SCE and Crown Castle Fiber, LLC (CCF). The guy wire was attached to the utility pole by CCF to provide stabilizing support because telecommunications equipment was also attached to the pole by CCF. (Burgos Decl. ¶¶ 6-7, Exh’s. 2-3; Marshall Decl. ¶¶ 3-4; Khalid Decl. ¶¶ 1, 11.)

 

The Southern California Joint Pole Committee (SCJPC), which includes both SCE and CCF, governs the management of joint utility poles, as described on the SCJPC’s website. Once the guy wire was attached, it became an integral part of the joint pole configuration, stabilizing the pole by counteracting forces such as power lines, fiber optic equipment, and wind. (Burgos Decl. ¶¶ 6-7, Exh’s. 2-3; Khalid Decl. ¶¶ 1, 11.)

 

The guy wire benefited SCE by maintaining the stability of the pole to which SCE’s electrical facilities were attached. (Khalid Decl. ¶¶ 1, 11.) Although SCE claims it does not own, maintain, or control the telecommunications equipment or the guy wire, SCE, as a member of the JPA and SCJPC, shared a joint interest in the pole and benefited from the guy wire’s stabilizing function. (Burgos Decl. ¶¶ 6-7, Exh’s. 2-3; Marshall Decl. ¶¶ 3-4; Khalid Decl. ¶¶ 1, 11.)

 

Analysis

 

“The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort, such as negligence, and resulting death.” (Lopez v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 675, 685.) In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiffs must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

            Duty

SCE moves for summary judgment, arguing that it did not owe a legal duty of care to Plaintiffs because it did not own the guy wire alleged to have caused the death. SCE argues that it did not own, maintain, or control the guy wire alleged to have caused the death of Plaintiffs’ deceased. SCE contends that no duty of care exists because it did not have ownership, possession, or control over the guy wire in question.

SCE states a defendant cannot be liable for a defective or dangerous condition on property it does not own, possess, or control. (Gray v. America West Airlines, Inc. (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 76; Whitney’s at the Beach v. Hovis (1970) 3 Cal.App.3d 258.) In Gray, the court affirmed summary judgment for the defendant airline in a premises liability case because the airline did not own or control the luggage that caused the plaintiff’s injury. (Gray, supra, 209 Cal.App.3d at 79-85.) Similarly, in Whitney’s at the Beach, the court granted summary judgment for the defendant because no evidence was presented that contradicted the defendant’s denial of ownership, possession, or control of the skating rink where the injury occurred. (Whitney’s at the Beach, supra, 3 Cal.App.3d at 267, 269.) SCE argues that it is an electric utility, not involved in the telecommunications or cable TV business, and that a telecommunications company owned, maintained, and controlled the guy wire at all relevant times. SCE further asserts that it does not install, maintain, or repair any facilities not owned by it on joint utility poles, and it had no involvement with the guy wire in question. Therefore, under California law, SCE argues that it cannot be liable for injuries caused by property it does not own, maintain, or control.

 

            In their supplemental opposition, Plaintiffs argue that SCE owes a legal duty to them due to its joint ownership and control of the utility pole and the attached guy wire, contrary to SCE’s reliance on cases such as Gray and Whitney’s at the Beach. Unlike in Gray, where the airline did not control the common area where the injury occurred, in this case, Plaintiffs assert that SCE had control over the joint utility pole and the installation of the guy wire when the telecommunications equipment was attached. (Gray, supra, 209 Cal.App.3d 76.). Similarly, in Whitney’s at the Beach, the court found the defendant did not own or control the premises where the injury happened; however, here, Plaintiffs state that SCE is a joint owner of the utility pole and had control over its placement and the guy wire, which benefited its operations by stabilizing the pole. (Whitney’s at the Beach, supra, 3 Cal.App.3d 258.)

 

Plaintiffs assert that all entities, including public utilities, have a general duty to exercise reasonable care in managing their property. (Laabs v. Southern California Edison Co. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1271-1272; Bily v. Arthur Young & Co. (1992) 3 Cal.4th 370, 397.) As a member of the JPA, Plaintiffs assert that SCE holds a joint ownership interest in the utility pole and any appurtenances, including the guy wire. Plaintiffs argue that provisions in the JPA create joint ownership and shared responsibility for the utility poles and appurtenances. Section 2(b) of the JPA allows members like SCE to propose and approve work on jointly owned poles, indicating control over the installation of appurtenances such as the guy wire. Section 2(i) establishes that utility poles are jointly owned as tenants in common, making all members, including SCE, jointly liable for any tortious conduct related to the property. (Davert v. Larson (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 407, 411.) Section 3(A) and 3(b) confirm that all JPA members jointly own or occupy the poles and benefit from appurtenances like the guy wire, which stabilizes the pole and prevents potential hazards. Additionally, Section 6(b)(2) and Section 9 require members to maintain liability insurance and indemnify each other for claims arising from injuries or deaths due to negligence, indicating shared liability among all members.

 

Plaintiffs further contend that SCE, as a tenant in common with other JPA members, has equal rights of possession and ownership of the utility pole, which includes liability for any negligence connected to the pole or its appurtenances. (Johns v. Scobie (1939) 12 Cal.2d 618, 623.) The JPA presumes joint liability of all members for injuries to third parties and does not limit liability solely to the member who placed the equipment on the pole. While the JPA provides for negligent members to indemnify and hold harmless other members, this does not absolve SCE of liability to third parties. Plaintiffs cite Myers Building Industries, Ltd. v. Interface Technology, Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 949, 969 to argue that indemnification clauses generally apply to reimbursements between members and do not eliminate liability toward third parties. Therefore, Plaintiffs contend that SCE owes a duty of care to them, as outlined in the JPA, due to its joint ownership and the benefit derived from the guy wire, which was necessary to support the stability of the utility pole.

 

            In the reply filed on June 29, 2023, SCE argues that the cases cited by the Plaintiffs—Gerberich v. SCE (1935) 5 Cal.2d 46, Laabs v. SCE (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1260, Schauf v. SCE (1966) 243 Cal.App.2d 450, and Norton v. City of Pomona (1935) 5 Cal.2d 54—are irrelevant to this case because none of them involved a guy wire owned and controlled by a telecommunications company. Instead, all of the cited cases involved utility poles. In this case, Plaintiffs allege that the guy wire, not the utility pole, was the contributing factor to the accident, claiming it was “dangerous and lacked a covering which would have prevented injuries of the type suffered by decedent.” (UMF No. 2, citing Complaint.) The Decedent only made contact with the guy wire and did not make contact with the utility pole itself. (UMF No. 5.) SCE further contends that unlike the defendants in the cases cited by the Plaintiffs, it is not arguing that it has no duty because the guy wire is in a franchise position. Instead, SCE argues that it has no duty because it does not own or control the guy wire. (UMF Nos. 3-12.) SCE maintains that a defendant cannot be held liable for a defective or dangerous condition on property it does not own, possess, or control, relying on Gray and Whitney’s at the Beach.

 

The Court finds that there are no triable issues of fact indicating that SCE owed a duty of care to the Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs’ argument that the JPA creates a duty for SCE fails. Plaintiffs claim that SCE cannot avoid liability by stating that CCF was responsible for the installation, maintenance and control of the guy wire because SCE and CCF are both tenants in common to the JPA, and all members to the JPA are jointly liable. While the JPA governs the joint ownership and management of utility poles among its members, the Court previously determined that there was no duty owed in relation to CCF’s installation of the guy wire. Under Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 (Rowland), whether a duty is owed is determined by weighing seven factors: (1) the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff; (2) the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury; (3) the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered; (4) the moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct; (5) the policy of preventing future harm; (6) the extent of the burden to the defendant and the consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach; and (7) the availability, cost, and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.

 

The Court incorporates its previous duty analysis from the minute order regarding CCF’s motion for summary judgment, where it found that the Rowland factors weigh in favor of imposing no duty on CCF. Given this finding, without a duty, the cause of action against CCF failed. Accordingly, even if the JPA establishes that all members jointly own the utility poles and could potentially be subject to liability for the negligence of another member, Plaintiffs fail to establish that SCE owed a duty of care. This is because the Court has already found that CCF’s installation of the guy wire did not give rise to a duty. Therefore, without a foundational duty established in connection with the guy wire’s installation, there can be no derivative duty imposed on SCE under the terms of the JPA.

 

            Thus, there is no duty imposed on SCE.

 

            Causation

SCE argues that its actions were not a substantial factor in causing the injury claimed in the negligence suit. In negligence cases, to prove causation, the plaintiff must show that the defendant’s act or omission was a “substantial factor” in bringing about the injury. (Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 774; Merrill v. Navegar Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 465, 490).) A defendant’s conduct is not considered a substantial factor if the injury would have occurred regardless of that conduct. (Yanez v. Plummer (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 180, 187.) As noted by the California Supreme Court, a factor that plays only an “infinitesimal” or “theoretical” role in causing harm does not meet the substantial factor standard. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chem. Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 79.) While causation is typically a question for the jury, it can be decided as a matter of law on summary judgment if undisputed facts show that the defendant’s actions were not a substantial factor in causing harm, as the harm would have occurred regardless of those actions. (Lawrence v. La Jolla Beach & Tennis Club (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 11, 33.) A plaintiff cannot avoid summary judgment by making abstract allegations of negligence or relying on speculative expert testimony; they must demonstrate that the injury was actually caused by the defendant’s conduct. (Saelzler, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 774-776.) SCE asserts that no act or omission on its part was a substantial factor in causing the death of the Decedent. Plaintiffs allege that the guy wire was “dangerous and lacked a covering” that could have prevented the injuries suffered. (UMF No. 2.) However, SCE reiterates that it did not own, maintain, or control the guy wire in question, which was managed by a telecommunications company.

            In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that SCE was a substantial factor in causing the injuries that resulted in the death of Decedent. SCE contends that it did not perform any act that could be considered actual or legal causation of Decedent’s death and that its actions regarding the guy wire were so minimal that they cannot be considered a substantial factor. Plaintiffs point out that SCE’s argument relies on the claim that it did not install or maintain the guy wire; therefore, its actions could not have contributed to the accident. However, Plaintiff states that SCE determined the placement of the utility pole, which in turn determined the placement of the guy wire. Moreover, SCE ratified the installation of the guy wire, which was necessary to stabilize the utility pole and directly benefited SCE. Plaintiffs rely on Laabs v. Southern California Edison Co. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1268-1269, to argue that SCE had a duty of care, which is a legal determination based on factors such as the foreseeability of harm, the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury, and the policy of preventing future harm. In Laabs, the court found it foreseeable that a vehicle could lose control and strike a light pole placed 18 inches from the curb, creating a triable issue regarding the foreseeability of the event. Plaintiffs assert that the facts in this case are similar, as it is foreseeable that a motorcyclist could lose control for various reasons and collide with a guy wire placed near the roadway. Here, SCE placed the utility pole 18 inches from the roadway, which necessitated the guy wire’s placement at the same distance, acting as a dangerous obstacle when Decedent struck it at high speed. Plaintiffs argue that if the utility pole and guy wire had been positioned further from the street or in a safer location, the impact could have been less severe, potentially preventing Decedent’s death.

Furthermore, Plaintiffs contend that even if SCE installed the utility pole according to California Public Utility Commission guidelines, this does not exempt it from negligence. In Laabs, the court noted that compliance with local guidelines does not negate a utility company’s duty of care in determining the safest placement of utility poles. Plaintiffs maintain that, like in Laabs, SCE had input into the placement decision and could have chosen a safer location. Additionally, the Laabs court stated that the likelihood of serious injury or death increases when a vehicle collides with a fixed object near a roadway, and this principle also applies to the placement of the guy wire in this case. (Laabs, supra, 175 Cal.App.4th at 1277-1278.) Plaintiffs argue that all of the facts presented by SCE in its motion for summary judgment are disputed. They assert that as a joint owner of the utility pole, SCE owed a legal duty to maintain the pole and the guy wire in a way that accounted for foreseeable risks. Plaintiffs claim that SCE’s suggestion that it does not owe a duty is conjectural and unsupported by facts, while the JPA clearly establishes SCE as a joint owner of the utility pole, thereby making it jointly liable for any negligent actions of co-members of the JPA.

            In the reply filed on June 29, 2023, SCE argues that Plaintiffs have failed to establish legal causation in their negligence claim. SCE asserts that Plaintiffs have failed to provide any evidence of legal causation linking SCE to the alleged dangerous condition of the guy wire, despite the pole being jointly owned.

 

            Without a duty, the cause of action must fail. As SCE has successfully negated the elements of duty, the Court need not address causation.

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Accordingly, Defendant Southern California Edison Company’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            “The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object, by demurrer or answer as provided in Section 430.30, to the pleading on any one or more of the following grounds:... The pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (CCP §430.10(e).)

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) The court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.) A “demurrer based on an affirmative defense cannot properly be sustained where the action might be barred by the defense, but is not necessarily barred.” (CrossTalk Productions, Inc. v. Jacobson (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 631, 635.)

Application to Facts

            Issue and Claim Preclusion

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ FAC is barred by the doctrine of re judicata, or claim preclusion. Defendants argue Plaintiffs have litigated their right to title to the Property and the validity of the foreclosure sale in two prior actions, a state court lawsuit filed prior to the trustee’s sale in February 2016 (“State Lawsuit”) and a federal lawsuit after the trustee’s sale in February 2019 (“Federal Lawsuit”). Plaintiffs acknowledge the likelihood that Defendants would raise res judicata as a defense based on the Federal Lawsuit. (FAC, 2:26-3:1-5.)

“Claim and issue preclusion have different requirements and effects. Claim preclusion prevents relitigation of entire causes of action. Claim preclusion applies only when a second suit involves (1) the same cause of action (2) between the same parties or their privies (3) after a final judgment on the merits in the first suit. Issue preclusion, by contrast, prevents relitigation of previously decided issues, rather than causes of action as a whole. It applies only (1) after final adjudication (2) of an identical issue (3) actually litigated and necessarily decided in the first suit and (4) asserted against one who was a party in the first suit or one in privity with that party.” (Samara v. Matar (2018) 5 Cal.5th 322, 326.)

“The doctrine promotes judicial economy by preventing claim splitting. It requires all claims based on the same cause of action, which were or could have been raised, to be decided in a single suit.” (Kim v. Reins International California, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 73, 92–93.)

“Under what circumstances is a matter to be deemed decided by the prior judgment? Obviously, if it is actually raised by proper pleadings and treated as an issue in the cause, it is conclusively determined by the first judgment. But the rule goes further. If the matter was within the scope of the action, related to the subject-matter and relevant to the issues, so that it could have been raised, the judgment is conclusive on it despite the fact that it was not in fact expressly pleaded or otherwise urged. The reason for this is manifest. A party cannot by negligence or design withhold issues and litigate them in consecutive actions. Hence the rule is that the prior judgment is res judicata on matters which were raised or could have been raised, on matters litigated or litigable.” (Thibodeau v. Crum (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 749, 755 (homeowner’s lawsuit against driveway subcontractor was barred by doctrine of res judicata arising from prior arbitration between homeowner and general contractor; although issue of driveway cracks was not raised in prior arbitration, they could and should have been raised).)

“California employs the primary rights theory to determine if two successive proceedings involve the same cause of action. Under the primary rights theory, the invasion of one primary right gives rise to a single cause of action. A cause of action consists of 1) a primary right

possessed by the plaintiff, 2) a corresponding primary duty devolving upon the defendant, and 3) a derelict or wrong done by the defendant which consists in a breach of such primary right and duty. The existence of a cause of action is based upon the harm suffered, as opposed to the particular theory asserted by the litigant.” (Brinton v. Bankers Pension Services, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 550, 557.)

“The cause of action is based upon the harm suffered, as opposed to the particular theory asserted by the litigant. Even where there are multiple legal theories upon which recovery might be predicated, one injury gives rise to only one claim for relief. Hence a judgment for the defendant is a bar to a subsequent action by the plaintiff based on the same injury to the same right, even though he presents a different legal ground for relief. Thus, under the primary rights theory, the determinative factor is the harm suffered. When two actions involving the same parties seek compensation for the same harm, they generally involve the same primary right.” (Boeken v. Philip Morris USA, Inc. (2010) 48 Cal.4th 788, 798.)

Defendants fail to establish that all three lawsuits seek redress for the same harm. Defendants argue Plaintiffs seek redress for loss of the Property due to their purported loan default in all three lawsuits. However, Plaintiffs’ FAC alleges wrongdoing and damages flowing from the issuance of a 1098 Form in connection with the sale of the property by Bank of New York to third parties in July 2020, the licensing status of the entities involved in that sale and other aspects of the sale that do not pertain to Plaintiffs’ right to the Property. The focus of the alleged wrongdoing in this litigation are actions by NewRez, LLC in 2020, after the May 2016 trustee’s deed sale that was the subject to the State Lawsuit and Federal Lawsuit. The Court cannot find based on the face of the convoluted FAC that the same primary right is necessarily involved in all three litigations. In fact, it is difficult to say what precise harm Plaintiffs are seeking redress for in this action, because the FAC is so poorly drafted.

In addition, while Defendants discuss the “same cause of action” element, they do not brief at all whether the February 2016 and February 2019 lawsuits satisfy the “same parties” and “final judgment on the merits” elements of claim preclusion.

Based on the caption page of the State Lawsuit, none of the demurring Defendants were named therein. (RJN, Ex. E.) Based on the caption of the Federal Lawsuit, only demurring Defendant The Bank of New York Mellon fka The Bank of New York, as Trustee for the Certificateholders, CWALT, Inc. Alternative Loan Trust 2007-22 Mortgage Pass Through Certificates Series 2007- 22 (“Bank of New York”) was named as a party. Thus, of the demurring Defendants, only Bank of New York establishes the “same party” element of claim preclusion. Plaintiffs also raised the lack of identity of parties as grounds to reject the res judicata defense in the FAC. (FAC, 3:6-9.)

Defendants also submit evidence that the Federal Lawsuit was dismissed with prejudice. (RJN, Exs. H and I.) Defendants do not brief whether this qualifies as a final judgment on the merits. However, it is well-established that a dismissal with prejudice is considered a judgment on the merits preventing subsequent litigation between the parties on the dismissed claim. (Kim v. Reins International California, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 73, 91.)

Defendants’ Demurrer based on claim preclusion fails and demurrer on that ground is accordingly overruled.

            First Cause of Action—Unfair Business Practices Licensing

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for “unlicensed companies” is sustained for failure to state a cause of action. There is no recognized cause of action for “unlicensed companies,” nor is it clear from the substantive allegations what cognizable cause of action could be stated based on the facts alleged. Moreover, on demurrer, the burden is on Plaintiffs to establish that the defect is reasonably capable of cure with leave to amend. (Hendy v. Losse  (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.) On Opposition, the Court notes that Plaintiffs expound at length as to the customary business practices of real estate proceedings in California, including discussion the “Great Recession of 2008.” Thus, Plaintiffs’ primary argument is that Defendants did not adhere to common practices of real estate. Plaintiffs continue to conclude that Defendants’ improper business practices led to Plaintiffs being foreclosed on. Plaintiffs further assert that if there is no recognized cause of action in California for an unlicensed company, this “would be news to Defendants’ compliance officers, the California Secretary of State, California Franchise Tax board, and the NSML.” (Opp., pg. 8.) However, the Court finds once again that Plaintiffs do not establish a cause of action as to unfair business practices concerning licensure here. Plaintiffs do not establish how the foregoing commentary or discussion translates into an applicable cause of action or what the law or requirements are to establish such cause of action. Thus, Plaintiffs fail to meet their burden on Opposition.

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for “unlicensed companies” is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Second Cause of Action—Fraudulent Concealment

Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action for “Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform Act and IRS Violations” is sustained. According to Plaintiffs, Defendant NewRez, LLC d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing prepared and issued an IRS 1098 Form showing they were liable for $10,287.85 of the escrow money disbursements. Plaintiffs claim this is a violation of the Dodd- Frank Wall Street Reform Act, because under that Act, the settlement agent (escrow officer) on the Closing Disclosure is responsible for preparing, issuing and filing tax documents upon closing of a real estate transaction. (FAC, ¶105.)

Plaintiffs fail to state a cognizable claim. While Plaintiffs cite to the Dodd Frank Act, Plaintiffs fail to cite to a specific subsection of the act or any authority creating a private right of action for violation of the Act. On Opposition, Plaintiffs only address the Dodd-Frank Act in their “Additional Closing Arguments” section whereby Plaintiffs briefly reference it by stating “[t]he claims in violating escrow procedure and the preparing of the IRS tax documents of the Dodd-Frank Act are already covered by other claims within this document.” (Opp., pg. 21.) Thus, on Opposition, Plaintiffs also fail to cite to any legal authority establishing they have a private right to pursue a cause of action for violation of the Act.

Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action for Dodd-Frank Act and IRS violations is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Third Cause of Action—Fraud-Extrinsic

Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained. Plaintiffs fail to allege any of the elements of concealment or fraud. Plaintiffs allege that NewRez LLC d/b/a Shellpoint Mortgage committed “concealment of key events involving an ongoing case, thus giving rise to extrinsic fraud in influencing Judge Steven V. Wilson’s decision against the Plaintiffs. (CA, Civil Code §1710 and 3924(c)(3)(d).” (FAC, ¶113.)

Defendants argue the fraud claim is also barred by statute of limitations. Plaintiffs’ fraud claim fails to identify any specific act of fraud, and it is unclear if Plaintiffs are claiming fraud based on the 1098 Form issued on February 2, 2020. For this reason, the Court cannot find, based on the face of the complaint, that the fraud claim is necessarily barred by statute of limitations. Demurrer therefore cannot be sustained based on statute of limitations.

Plaintiffs’ allegations are conclusory and do not allege any fraud. On Opposition, Plaintiffs continue to provide conclusory arguments, concluding that the alleged fraud and deceit prevented the Honorable Steven V. Wilson from making a fair ruling in federal court. (Opp., pgs. 12-13.) Such argument is insufficient to demonstrate the specific acts required to establish a cause of action for fraud. Moreover, as a trial court, this Court does not have jurisdiction to review the merits of the aforementioned case, nor disturb its rulings.

Defendants’ Demurrer to the third cause of action for fraud is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

            Fourth Cause of Action—Dodd-Frank Act

Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action for “vacate of ruling and res judicata” is sustained. There is no cognizable cause of action for “vacate of ruling and res judicata.” Moreover, Plaintiffs are requesting that this Court vacate the dismissal with prejudice entered in 2:19-cv-00991-SVW-PJW and to “vacate” any “res judicata” effect that case may have. The Court does not have jurisdiction or authority to vacate the ruling of a federal district court, nor is there a cognizable cause of action stated by these allegations. On Opposition, Plaintiffs merely restate and reiterate the same language with respect to res judicata and prejudice. Such argument remains deficient for the foregoing reasons.

Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Fifth Cause of Action—Fraud-Conveyance (“Escrow Fraud”)

Defendants’ demurrer to the “escrow fraud” cause of action is sustained. There is no cognizable cause of action for “escrow fraud,” nor do Plaintiffs’ allege the elements of common law fraud. Plaintiffs do not allege a clear misrepresentation by Defendants upon which they detrimentally relied. On Opposition, Plaintiffs go as far as to state that “it is no longer a question that Plaintiffs have standing within the Prominent Escrow File No. . . .” (Opp., pg. 20.) Thus, instead of alleging any elements for a common law fraud cause of action, Plaintiffs speculate as to which of the Defendants executed which escrow documents and continue to reiterate that the answers are contained within the “Prominent Escrow File.” (Opp., pg. 21.) As such, Plaintiffs continue to fail to establish any cause of action for “escrow fraud” and fail to connect any applicable facts therein.

Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action for escrow fraud is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Sixth Cause of Action—Vacate Ruling (“Void Grant Deed”)

Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action for “void grant deed” is sustained. Plaintiffs seek to cancel the grant deed issued on July 23, 2020, conveying the Property from Bank of New York to third parties, Ronald A. Rosenblum and Jacqueline D. Rosenblum, Trustees of the Rosenblum Family Trust dated December 1, 2010.

Civil Code section 3412 states that a written instrument may be cancelled if “there is a reasonable apprehension that if left outstanding it may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable.” “To prevail on a claim to cancel an instrument, a plaintiff must prove (1) the instrument is void or voidable due to, for example, fraud; and (2) there is a reasonable apprehension of serious injury including pecuniary loss or the prejudicial alternation of one's position.” (Thompson v. Ioane (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1180, 1193-1194.)

Plaintiffs were not parties to the grant deed, nor do they allege any fraud that would entitle them to void the grant deed. (FAC, ¶¶122-130.) Plaintiffs do not directly address this cause of action in their Opposition. Instead, Plaintiffs continue to assert conclusory allegations such that the grant deed is already void and is, therefore, evidence of an improper sale of Plaintiffs’ property. Thus, Plaintiffs do not argue they have standing with respect to this cause of action. Moreover, Plaintiffs seem to be conflating the sixth and fourth causes of actions here.

Defendants’ Demurrer to the sixth cause of action for “void grant deed” is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

            Seventh Cause of Action—Business Practices/ Pooling and Service Agreements

Defendants’ Demurrer to the seventh cause of action for “business practices/pooling and service agreements” is sustained. There is no cognizable cause of action for “business practices/pooling and service agreements,” nor did Plaintiffs assert a comprehensible argument on Opposition. The only time this cause of action is referenced is as following:

“Nor does the Plaintiff wish to pursue its claims of Certificateholders ,CWALT Inc., Alternative Loan Trust, 2007-22 Mortgage Pass Through Certificates, Series 2007-22 in violating its pooling and servicing agreements.”

(Opp., pg. 22.)

 

On Reply, Defendants seem to think Plaintiffs are electing to dismiss their seventh cause of action. To the Court, Plaintiffs intentions are entirely unclear. However, it is clear that Plaintiffs have not alleged a sufficient cause of action here.

Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action for “business practices/pooling and service agreements” is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

           

CONCLUSION

            The Demurrer to the FAC is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to each of the seven causes of action herein.

            Defendants to give notice.
