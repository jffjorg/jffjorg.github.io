The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR.

Case Number: 23STLC01716    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 26

X-Tream Cleaning Service Inc. v. Royal Personal Training et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:           

Plaintiff X-Tream Cleaning Service, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and Request for Sanctions, is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

On March 14, 2023, Plaintiff X-Tream Cleaning Service, Inc. filed the instant action for breach of contract against Defendant Royal Personal Training (“Defendant”). On February 21, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions. (Minute Order, 02/21/24.) Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Discovery Responses and Request for Sanctions, which came on for hearing on October 22, 2024. The Court noted a number of defects with the Motion to Compel, including improperly filing a single discovery motion to compel responses with respect to four separate sets of discovery and failing to attach copies of the discovery requests served on Defendant. (Minute Order, 10/22/24.)

Plaintiff filed a supplemental declaration on October 22, 2024 and additional notices of motion demonstrating that these defects have been corrected.

Discussion

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s initial responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290. (Notice of Motion, filed 10/29/24, p. 1:24-26.)

However, the form interrogatories propounded by Plaintiff are in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 94, subdivision (a), which prohibits interrogatories with subparts in the limited jurisdiction court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 94, subd. (a)(1).) The form interrogatories propounded on Defendant contain subparts. (Motion, Supp. Hurt Decl., Exh. A, Form Interrogatories Nos. 17.0 and 50.0.) The Court declines to compel responses to discovery propounded in violation of the Code of Civil Procedure.

Conclusion 

Plaintiff X-Tream Cleaning Service, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and Request for Sanctions, is DENIED.

Case Number: 23STLC08034    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 26

Cuadros et al., v. Tompkins et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiffs Rita Cuadros, Nancy Cuadros, and Veronica O’Hacer’s Motion to Reclassify is DENIED.

AN ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE REGARDING WHY THIS ACTION SHOULD NOT BE RECLASSIFIED TO THE UNLIMITED JURISDICTION COURT IS SET FOR FEBRUARY 5, 2025 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

ANALYSIS: 

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs Rita Cuadros, Nancy Cuadros, and Veronica O’Hacer (“Plaintiffs”) filed the instant action for breach of contract, declaratory relief, and injunctive relief against Defendants Martha Tonkins (erroneously sued as Martha Tompkins), Kimberly Stoll (erroneously sued as Defendant Kimberly Cuadros) (“Defendant Stoll”), Miguel Cuadros (“Defendant Cuadros”), SCI California Funeral Services, Inc. dba Oakdale Memorial Park (erroneously sued as Oakdale Cemetery) (“Defendant SCI”) and Dignity Memorial (“Defendant Dignity”). Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint adding causes of action for fraud and negligent infliction of emotional distress on April 25, 2024. Defendants Stoll, Cuadros, Tonkins and SCI filed responses to the action in June 2024.

Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion to Reclassify this Action on October 3, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

Discussion

The Motion is not accompanied by a proof of service showing service of the moving papers or of the notice of the motion and hearing date on Defendants. Failure to give notice of a motion is not only a violation of the statutory requirements but of due process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005; Jones v. Otero (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 757.) Accordingly, the Motion to Reclassify this Action is denied. 

However, in light of the declaratory and injunctive relief sought in the Complaint and First Amended Complaint with respect to the parties’ rights and the placement of the headstone ordered for Plaintiffs’ mother, the Court finds this action should be reclassified to an unlimited jurisdiction court. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 85, 86.) 

An order to show cause regarding why this action should not be reclassified to an unlimited jurisdiction court is set. Each Defendant may file and serve a response to the order to show cause, not to exceed five pages, at least ten (10) court days prior to the date of the order to show cause.  

Conclusion

Plaintiffs Rita Cuadros, Nancy Cuadros, and Veronica O’Hacer’s Motion to Reclassify is DENIED.

AN ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE REGARDING WHY THIS ACTION SHOULD NOT BE RECLASSIFIED TO THE UNLIMITED JURISDICTION COURT IS SET FOR FEBRUARY 5, 2025 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

Case Number: 24STCP02398    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 26

Fang v. Chen et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Petitioner Qiang Fang’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is DISMISSED.  

ANALYSIS:

On August 18, 2022, an Arbitration Award was issued in favor of Petitioner Qiang Fang (“Petitioner”) and against Respondent Thomas T. Chen (“Respondent”). Petitioner, in propria persona, filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (the “Petition”) on July 30, 2024. No response to the Petition has been filed to date.

Legal Standard

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.  The petition shall name as respondent all parties to the arbitration and may name as respondents any other persons bound by the arbitration award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) “If a petition or response under this chapter is duly served and filed, the court shall confirm the award as made, whether rendered in this state or another state, unless in accordance with this chapter it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award or dismisses the proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.)

A response to a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award that seeks to vacate or correct the award must be served and filed no later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the respondent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1288.2.) 

Discussion

Service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4 requires that the Petition and Notice of Hearing be served on Respondent “in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice” or “[i]f the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made . . . [s]ervice within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4, subds. (a), (b).)

No copy of the arbitration agreement itself has been filed so the Court finds service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing must conform to the requirements for service of a summons. However, no code-compliant proof of service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing has been filed with the Court. The proof of service by mail filed on August 13, 2024 only shows that the Petition and Notice of Hearing were served on Respondent by first class mail. (Proof of Service, filed 08/13/24, ¶6.) This does not conform to any manner of service permitted for service of a summons. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 415.10 – 415.95.) Therefore, the Court cannot find that the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 are satisfied.

Service of the Arbitration Award and Timing of Service of Petition (CCP §§ 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 requires that “[t]he neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1283.6.) This requirement may be satisfied by service by the arbitrator, or upon proper service of the Award with the Petition. (See Murry v. Civil Service Employees Ins. Co. (1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 796, 799-800.) As no copy of the arbitration agreement is attached to the Petition, Petitioner must show that the Award was served personally or by registered or certified mail. Service by certified mail is demonstrated by the copy of the arbitration award attached to the Petition. (Pet., Attachment 8(c)-2.) Therefore, the Court finds that the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 are met.

 

Also, a party seeking a court judgment confirming an arbitration award must file and serve the petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award is served. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.) As no code-compliant proof of service of the Petition has been filed, however, the Court cannot find that the Petition has been timely served as required by Code of Civil Procedure sections 1288 and 1288.4. 

Confirmation of the Arbitration Award

An arbitration award is not directly enforceable until it is confirmed by a court and judgment is entered.  (CCP § 1287.6; Jones v. Kvistad (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 836, 840.) The court must confirm the award as made, unless it corrects or vacates the award, or dismisses the proceeding.  (CCP § 1286; Valsan Partners Limited Partnership v. Calcor Space Facility, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 809, 818.) Code of Civil Procedure, section 1285.4 states a petition under this chapter shall:

 a) Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

b) Set forth the names of the arbitrators. 

c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.) The Petition complies with the above requirements. It sets forth the nature of the agreement to arbitrate and the name of the Arbitrator. (Pet., ¶¶4-6.) The Petition also attaches a copy of the Award, which awards Petitioner $7,122.91 against Respondent. (Pet., Attachment 8(c).)

Attorney’s Fees and Costs

To the extent Petitioner requests attorney’s fees and costs according to proof (Pet., Prayer, ¶¶10(e)-(f)), those expenses are permitted on a petition to confirm arbitration award, if granted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1293.2; MBNA America Bank, N.A. v. Gorman (2006) 147 Cal.App.4th.Supp. 1, 7.) However, as Petitioner is in pro per, attorney’s fees would be denied. (See Do v. Superior Court (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1218 [citing Argaman v. Ratan (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1173, 1180-1181].) 

Conclusion 

Based on Petitioner Qiang Fang’s failure to meet the statutory requirements for service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4, the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is DISMISSED.

Case Number: 24STLC04501    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 26

Gupta v. DiSalvo, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendants Lee Ann DiSalvo and Intuitive Healing Institute, LLC’s Motion for Sanctions is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $23,816.00.

ANALYSIS:

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiff Sushant Gupta (“Plaintiff”) filed the Complaint in this action against Defendants Lee Ann DiSalvo (“Defendant DiSalvo”), Intuitive Healing Institute, LLC (“Defendant IHI”), and Kristen Lahey (“Defendant Lahey”). On August 7, 2024, Defendant Lahey filed a Motion to Deem Plaintiff a Vexatious Litigant. Defendants DiSalvo and IHI filed their Motion to Deem Plaintiff a Vexatious Litigant on September 4, 2024. On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed the action without prejudice. Despite the dismissal, the Court retained jurisdiction to rule on the motion to deem Plaintiff a vexatious litigant, and granted the motion on October 14, 2024, after taking the matter under submission. (Minute Order, 10/14/24.)

On October 3, 2024, Defendants Intuitive Healing Institute and Lee Ann DiSalvo (“Moving Defendants”) filed the instant action Motion for Sanctions. No opposition has been filed to date.

Discussion

The Motion for Sanctions is brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, pursuant to which, the judge may order a party or counsel, or both, “to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).) “‘Actions or tactics’ include, but are not limited to, the making or opposing of motions or the filing and service of a complaint, cross-complaint, answer, or other responsive pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (b)(1).) “‘Frivolous’ means totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (b)(2).) The Court grants Moving Defendants’ request for judicial notice of cases previously filed by Plaintiff. (See Cal. Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

The statute also provides a party making or opposing a written motion or the filing and service of a complaint, cross-complaint, answer or other responsive pleading a 21-day safe harbor provision where the pleading or paper can be withdrawn or appropriately corrected. (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (f)(1)(B).) Furthermore, “[i]n determining what sanctions, if any, should be ordered, the court shall consider whether a party seeking sanctions has exercised due diligence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (f)(1).)

To a large part, the instant Motion is based on the filing of Plaintiff’s Complaint, to which Defendants responded by filing a motion to deem Plaintiff vexatious. (Motion, pp. 13:9-14:23.) That motion was filed on September 4, 2024 and decided on October 14, 2024. Before that motion could even be heard, or moving Defendants could file the instant Motion for Sanctions, Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed the action on October 1, 2024. The Court finds Moving Defendants acted with diligence in bringing this Motion for Sanctions promptly after filing the motion to deem Plaintiff vexatious. Furthermore, due to Plaintiff’s decision to voluntarily dismiss the action, the safe harbor provision is inapplicable.

The Motion argues that the filing of the Complaint was frivolous and Plaintiff’s conduct during the litigation was in bad faith. The Complaint alleged causes of action for (1) breach of contract; (2) fraud; (3) unjust enrichment; (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (5) wrongful conduct; and (6) malice. Moving Defendants are correct to point out the last two purported causes of action are not causes of action at all under California law. However, as for the remaining causes of action, the Motion does not adequately demonstrate the inadequacies of the Complaint. The Motion argues that the remaining causes of action fail to allege sufficient facts but provides no citation to any of the allegations in the Complaint. (Motion, p. 13:18-14:20.) The Motion also points to Plaintiff’s alleged contract damages of $175.00 as evidence of bad faith filing in the limited jurisdiction court. Although, this alone does not show bad faith, Moving Defendants also point out that Plaintiff’s motivation to file this action and conduct during the litigation was in bad faith.

Defense counsel provides a declaration stating that during many hours of conversation with Plaintiff, Plaintiff admitted that their modus operandi is to harass people through litigation to extort money, threatened to report defense counsel as a racist to the Court and the State Bar if the case was not dropped. (Motion, Brainard Decl., ¶2.) Defense counsel also declares that Plaintiff wasted many hours by using various tactics such as calling from various numbers at different times of day, texting, late night calls, cagey emails, and insisting on conferences that would go nowhere, while threatening defense counsel with being reported to the Bar for failing to cooperate. (Id. at ¶¶2, 5.) The Court finds this demonstrates the action was filed and litigated in bad faith and through frivolous conduct in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, which that warrants an award of sanctions based on attorney’s fees and costs incurred. 

Moving Defendants incurred $23,000.00 in fees based on 47 hours of time billed at $500.00. (Id. at ¶4-6 and Request for Judicial Notice.) 12 hours of this time was spent on Plaintiff’s bad faith and frivolous communication tactics; five hours was spent on the instant Motion, and 30 hours was spent on the motion to deem Plaintiff a vexatious litigant. (Ibid.) Moving Defendant also spent $816.00 on filing fees. (Id. at ¶7.)

Conclusion

Defendants Lee Ann DiSalvo and Intuitive Healing Institute, LLC’s Motion for Sanctions is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $23,816.00.

Case Number: 24STLC05881    Hearing Date: December 5, 2024    Dept: 26

Maradiaga v. Macias et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiffs Anselmo Lorenzo Maradiaga and Sandra Osorto’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

AN ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE REGARDING FAILURE TO ENTER DEFAULT AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT IS SET FOR MARCH 6, 2025 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.  

ANALYSIS:

On August 14, 2024, Plaintiffs Lorenzo Maradiaga and Sandra Osorto (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Ignacio Macias dba Imperial Motor Sports (Defendant Macias”), Bayside Credit (“Defendant Bayside”), and Hudson Insurance Company (“Defendant Hudson Insurance”). Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration on September 11, 2024.

On October 11, 2024, Defendant Hudson Insurance filed an answer to the Complaint and a cross-complaint against Plaintiff. Proof of service of the Summons and Complaint were filed with respect to all Defendants on October 14, 2024. To date, Defendants Macias and Bayside have not filed responses to the action.

Discussion

Plaintiff brings the instant Motion seeking to compel Defendants Marcias and Bayside to submit to arbitration based on a vehicle Retail Installment Sale Contract (the “RISC”), pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2, 1281.97, and 1281.99. (Notice of Motion, pp. 1:25-2:13.)

However, due to Defendants Macias and Bayside having been served with the Summons and Complaint and failing to file a responsive pleading, Plaintiff is now required to enter their default. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.110(g) [“If a responsive pleading is not served within the time limits specified in this rule and no extension of time has been granted, the plaintiff must file a request for entry of default within 10 days after the time for service has elapsed.”].) Plaintiff has not shown that the Court has jurisdiction to do anything other than enter default as to Defendants Macias and Bayside.

In fact, Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.97, on which Plaintiff relies, provides that if the drafting party of the arbitration agreement is in material breach by failing to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, the “consumer may do either of the following:

 (1) Withdraw the claim from arbitration and proceed in a court of appropriate jurisdiction.

(2) Compel arbitration in which the drafting party shall pay reasonable attorney's fees and costs related to the arbitration.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b) [emphasis added].) Here, Plaintiff has improperly sought to both proceed in court and compel arbitration, contrary to the express language of the statute. Plaintiff filed this action bringing five separate causes of action against Defendants, while also seeking to compel Defendants Macias and Bayside to arbitration. This is not permitted under the authority on which Plaintiff relies.

Conclusion

Plaintiffs Anselmo Lorenzo Maradiaga and Sandra Osorto’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

AN ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE REGARDING FAILURE TO ENTER DEFAULT AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT IS SET FOR MARCH 6, 2025 AT 9:30 AM IN DEPARTMENT 26 IN THE SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.
