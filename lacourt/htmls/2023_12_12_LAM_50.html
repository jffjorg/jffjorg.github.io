TENTATIVE RULING RE:

 

MOTION FOR AWARD OF MANDATORY ATTORNEY FEES AND COSTS

 

            Background

On October 21, 2021, Plaintiffs 800 E. Pico Blvd., LLC and Xiang Hao Cui (jointly, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Online Edugo, Inc. (“Edugo”), Andrew Kim (“Kim”) and Law Office of Andrew Kim, APC (the “Law Office”). The Complaint asserts one cause of action for wrongful use of civil proceedings.

On August 3, 2022, the Court issued an Order granting Edugo’s special motion to strike Plaintiffs’ Complaint. In addition, on April 28, 2023, the Court issued an Order granting Kim and the Law Office’s special motion to strike Plaintiffs’ Complaint.

Edugo and the Law Office (jointly, the “Moving Parties”) now move for an order compelling Plaintiffs to pay the Moving Parties attorney fees “in connection with the successful Special Motions to Strike, and to prepare this Motion, for all the mandatory fees and costs.” (Notice of Motion at p. 2:3-5.)

 

Discussion

The anti-SLAPP statute provides that “a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover that defendant’s attorney’s fees and costs.” (Code Civ. Proc.,     § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).) An award of fees may also include “the fees incurred in enforcing the right to mandatory fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1141.)

            As an initial matter, Plaintiffs assert in the opposition that the instant motion is untimely. Plaintiffs cite to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1702, which provides, inter alia, that “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute, this rule applies in civil cases to claims for statutory attorney’s fees and claims for attorney’s fees provided for in a contract. Subdivisions (b) and (c) apply when the court determines entitlement to the fees, the amount of the fees, or both, whether the court makes that determination because the statute or contract refers to ‘reasonable’ fees, because it requires a determination of the prevailing party, or for other reasons.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702, subd. (a).) Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1702, subdivision (b), “[a] notice of motion to claim attorney’s fees for services up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court--including attorney’s fees on an appeal before the rendition of judgment in the trial court--must be served and filed within the time for filing a notice of appeal under rules 8.104 and 8.108 in an unlimited civil case…”

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 8.104, subdivision (a), “[u]nless a statute or rules 8.108, 8.702, or 8.712 provides otherwise, a notice of appeal must be filed on or before the earliest of: (1) (A) 60 days after the superior court clerk serves on the party filing the notice of appeal a document entitled ‘Notice of Entry’ of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, showing the date either was served; (B) 60 days after the party filing the notice of appeal serves or is served by a party with a document entitled ‘Notice of Entry’ of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, accompanied by proof of service; or (C) 180 days after entry of judgment.” California Rules of Court, rule 8.104, subdivision (e) provides that “[a]s used in (a) and (d), ‘judgment’ includes an appealable order if the appeal is from an appealable order.” Plaintiffs note that “[a]n order granting or denying a special motion to strike shall be appealable under Section 904.1.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (i).)  

            Plaintiffs assert that accordingly, the “deadline to appeal an order granting a special motion to strike is 60 days after service by the clerk of a filed-endorsed copy of the order granting the anti-SLAPP motion, or 60 days after notice of entry of that order is served by a party, whichever is earlier.” (Opp’n at p. 2:10-13.)

             Plaintiffs note that here, on April 28, 2023, the Court issued an Order granting Kim and the Law Office’s special motion to strike. The Court’s April 28, 2023 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Special Motion to Strike under CCP Section 425.16 (Anti-SLAPP motion) filed by Andrew Kim, Law Office of Andrew Kim, APC on 11/28/2022 is Granted.”

On April 28, 2023, a Certificate of Mailing was filed which provides, inter alia, that “I, the below-named Executive Officer/Clerk of the above-entitled court, do hereby certify that I am not a party to the cause herein, and that on this date I served the Minute Order (Hearing on Special Motion to Strike under CCP Section 425.16 ...) of 04/28/2023 upon each party or counsel named below by placing the document for collection and mailing so as to cause it to be deposited in the United States mail at the courthouse in Los Angeles, California, one copy of the original filed/entered herein in a separate sealed envelope to each address as shown below with the postage thereon fully prepaid, in accordance with standard court practices.” This certificate of mailing filed on April 28, 2023 is dated May 1, 2023. Sixty days after May 1, 2023 is June 30, 2023. As noted by Plaintiffs, the instant motion was filed on September 28, 2023.

            The Court notes that the Moving Parties did not file any reply in support of the motion, and thus do not dispute that the motion is untimely.

            The Court also notes that to the extent the instant motion concerns Edugo’s motion to strike, the Court issued an Order on August 3, 2022 granting Edugo’s special motion to strike Plaintiffs’ Complaint. The docket for this action does not appear to show the filing of a certificate of mailing of the August 3, 2022 Order. In any event, 180 days after August 3, 2022 is January 30, 2023. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.104, subd. (a)(1)(C).) The instant motion was filed on September 28, 2023.

            In light of the foregoing, the Court denies the instant motion as untimely.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Moving Parties’ motion for attorney’s fees is denied.

Plaintiffs are ordered to provide notice of this Order.

Case Number: 22STCV09612    Hearing Date: December 12, 2023    Dept: 50

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

GARY HUERTA,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

MICHAEL SIMMS, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

22STCV09612

Hearing Date:

December 12, 2023

Hearing Time:    10:00 a.m.

 

TENTATIVE RULING RE: 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SET VALUATION DATE FOR DECEMBER 31, 2022

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

           

 

Background

On March 18, 2022, Plaintiff Gary Huerta (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Michael Simms (“Simms”), Carmen Santillan (“Santillan”), Plant Ranch, Inc. (“Plant Ranch”), and Cena Kitchen, LLC (“Cena Kitchen”).  

Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on August 29, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) constructive fraud, (2) accounting, (3) conversion, (4) breach of the operating agreement, (5) breach of fiduciary duty, (6) declaratory relief, and (7) dissolution.

On August 24, 2023, Plant Ranch and Cena Kitchen filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) breach of contract (operating agreement), (3) breach of contract (bylaws), and (4) declaratory relief.

Plaintiff now moves “for an order setting the current valuation date as to the buyout of Plaintiff in his interest in [Cena Kitchen] for December 31, 2022.” Simms and Santillan (jointly referred to as the “Defendants” herein) oppose. 

Discussion

Defendants previously moved “for an order that: (1) stays this dissolution action in its entirety, including all discovery and Plaintiff’s pending motion for dissolution; (2) appoints appraisers in accordance with the terms of the Operating Agreement for purposes of ascertaining the fair market value of Cena Kitchen; (3) orders a buyout of Plaintiff’s membership interest in Cena Kitchen in accordance with the terms of the Cena Kitchen Operating Agreement; and (4) sets a bond at $5,000 pursuant to ¿Corporations Code section 17707.03(c)(2)¿.” (See November 14, 2022 Order at p. 2:1-7.) Plaintiff opposed.

The Court’s November 14, 2022 Order provides, inter alia, as follows:

 

“Defendants’ motion is granted in part. Discovery and further proceedings are stayed as to Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for dissolution. For purposes of the appraisal of the fair market value of the membership interests owned by the moving parties, the Court orders that Plaintiff shall select one appraiser, Defendant shall select one appraiser, and those two appraisers shall jointly select a third appraiser. In addition, Defendants shall post a bond in the amount of $31,500, on or before 11/23/22. The Court sets a status conference on [1/26/22] [sic], at 10:00 a.m. in Dept. 50 as to the status of the appraisals. The parties are ordered to file a joint report regarding the status of the appraisals five court days prior to the status conference, with a courtesy copy delivered directly to Department 50.¿Regarding the issue of whether Plaintiff is entitled to only 50% of the ‘Buy-Out Purchase Price’ per Section 8.05(e) of the Operating Agreement and payment in accordance with the ‘Buy-Out Terms’ in Section 1.01 of the Operating Agreement, the Court finds that this issue must be tried.” (November 14, 2022 Order at pp. 9:21-10:7.)[1]

In the instant motion, Plaintiff asserts that “[h]ere, the trial court on November 14, 2022 issued an order directing the parties to each select an appraiser and that each party’s respective appraiser shall jointly select a third appraiser; Defendants are to post a bond in the amount of $31,500.00 on or before November 23, 2022; a status conference is set for January 26, 2022; the parties are to file a joint report regarding the status of the appraisal, 5 days before the status conference; and that the issue of any discount on Plaintiffs ownership interest must be tried…However, the Court did not state what date is the date of valuation for appraisal.” (Mot. at pp. 5:23-6:2.)

But as Plaintiff acknowledges, Corporations Code section 17707.03, subdivision (c)(5) provides that “[f]or the purposes of this section, the valuation date shall be the date upon which the action for judicial dissolution was commenced. However, the court may, upon the hearing of a motion by any party, and for good cause shown, designate some other date as the valuation date.” (Emphasis added.)

In the instant motion, Plaintiff asserts that “there is good cause to set the date of valuation for December 31, 2022.” (Mot. at p. 6:8-9.) Plaintiff asserts that “the impacts of COVID, the nature of the business of Cena Kitchen and accounting processes associated therewith necessitate an end of year valuation as that would be a more accurate and fair valuation. As such, the valuation date should be set for December 31, 2022.” (Mot. at p. 6:23-25.) The Court notes that the instant action was filed on March 18, 2022, but the original Complaint did not contain any cause of action for dissolution. Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) filed on May 9, 2022 contains a cause of action for judicial dissolution of Plant Ranch and Centa Kitchen. (See FAC, Ninth Cause of Action.)

In his declaration in support of the instant motion, Plaintiff states that he is a 1/3 owner and member of Cena Kitchen. (Huerta Decl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff asserts that “[t]he valuation date for Cena Kitchen should be December 31, 2022, instead of a date which falls within the early part of the year for 2022. Cena Kitchen’s business revolves around the selling of its foods at vegan food festivals, large outdoor concert events like Coachella, and more. Those events are less frequent in the first quarter. Events in the second and third quarters of the year draw as many as 100,000 attendees per day for up to three days at a time, who pay in cash and via credit card for the vegan Mexican food sold by Cena Kitchen.” (Huerta Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff states that “during the second and third quarters of the year, Cena Kitchen typically participates in as many as four to five different events/locations per weekend in addition to regular weekly operations where it delivers food or offers food for pickup.” (Ibid.) In addition, Plaintiff states that “a comparison of Cena Kitchen’s Gross Sales for the year of 2022 shows that Cena Kitchen earns and reports substantially more income from May through December.” (Mot. at p. 7:10-11; citing Huerta Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. F.)

Plaintiff also asserts that “Cena Kitchen’s fiscal calendar is the calendar year. The actual profits and expenses of the company are not clear until the end of the year when Defendants receive year-end statements from the various vendors they use…and are forced to prepare all the necessary documents to provide to the account for the preparing and filing of taxes. For this reason, as well, a valuation date at the end of the year and not the beginning will provide a more accurate and fair valuation of Cena Kitchen.” (Huerta Decl., ¶ 8.)

In addition, Plaintiff states that “[f]rom March 2020 - June 2021, local and federal and mandates restricted public gatherings” and that “[i]n July of 2021, Cena resumed sales of food at outdoor events, concerts and catering…” (Huerta Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff asserts that “[b]y Q2 and Q4 of 2022, Cena once again began to grow business with great success, picking up where it had left off in March of 2020. The Covid period was an anomaly in the financial operations of Cena.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff asserts that “an early 2022 valuation is not an accurate reflection of the value of the business given drop in business suffered by Cena Kitchen arising from the COVID pandemic.” (Mot. at p. 8:20-22.)

As discussed above, Plaintiff’s FAC filed on May 9, 2022 contains a cause of action for judicial dissolution of Plant Ranch and Centa Kitchen. In the opposition, Defendants assert that “Plaintiff’s Motion is fatally flawed because he has not stated any good cause to alter the May 9, 2022 valuation date.” (Opp’n at p. 1:17-18.)

First, Defendants assert that “Plaintiff does not support his argument with any legal authority justifying a deviation from the statutory default date.” (Opp’n at p. 4:3-4.) But as discussed, Corporations Code section 17707.03, subdivision (c)(5) provides that “[f]or the purposes of this section, the valuation date shall be the date upon which the action for judicial dissolution was commenced. However, the court may, upon the hearing of a motion by any party, and for good cause shown, designate some other date as the valuation date.”

Defendants also assert that “Plaintiff’s ‘seasonality’ argument, i.e., that the valuation date must be deferred to account for the purported year-end influx of cash, ignores the standard practice utilized in business valuations of this sort.” (Opp’n at p. 5:1-4.) Defendants submit the Declaration of Edward Fixen, a Certified Business Appraiser who has been designated as one of the appraisers in this matter tasked with valuing Plaintiff’s membership interest in Cena Kitchen. (Fixen Decl., ¶ 1.) Mr. Fixen states that “I understand that Plaintiff is requesting to move the valuation date from May 9, 2022 to December 31, 2022 because he claims that Cena Kitchen typically makes more money in the latter half of the year. However, in my professional opinion, this ‘seasonality’ argument ignores the standard practice utilized in business valuations of this sort.” (Fixen Decl., ¶ 2.) Mr. Fixen states that “[a]ppraisers typically do (and should) utilize a ‘trailing’ 12-month assessment of the business whereby the appraiser analyzes the 1-year period prior to the assigned valuation date. This is the method I will be utilizing in assessing the value of Plaintiff’s membership interest in Cena Kitchen.” (Fixen Decl., ¶ 3.) Mr. Fixen states that “[s]uch an assessment is intended to capture any seasonality that could otherwise be overlooked by analysis of a partial year and would capture Plaintiff’s seasonality argument, such that if it is true that Cena Kitchen historically has turned a larger profit in the latter portion of the year, then a trailing assessment (back to May 2021 in this instance) necessarily would capture relevant data and satisfy Plaintiff’s purported concern.” (Fixen Decl., ¶ 4.)

Plaintiff counters that “Defendants’…assertion that a ‘trailing 12- month assessment’ may be used will not accurately reflect the income of Cena Kitchen (in light of the manner in which it reports income and the delays from COVID-19) and thus will not accurately reflect the value of Plaintiff’s ownership interest.” (Reply at p. 5:6-9.) Indeed, Defendants do not appear to address Plaintiff’s assertion that “an early 2022 valuation is not an accurate reflection of the value of the business given [sic] drop in business suffered by Cena Kitchen arising from the COVID pandemic.” (Mot. at 8:20-22.) Plaintiff asserts that “[t]he Covid period was an anomaly in the financial operations of Cena. Defendants are seeking to set the appraisal date within the time when Covid had the biggest negative impact on business profitability and valuation...” (Huerta Decl., ¶ 7.) This does not appear to be disputed by Defendants.

In addition, as set forth above, Mr. Fixen states that “[a]ppraisers typically do (and should) utilize a ‘trailing’ 12-month assessment of the business whereby the appraiser analyzes the 1-year period prior to the assigned valuation date.” (Fixen Decl., ¶ 3) Defendants do not appear to present any argument as to why such “trailing” 12-month assessment could not be conducted in connection with an assigned valuation date of December 31, 2022.

In addition, although Defendants argue that “Plaintiff’s argument boils down to the speculative assertion that Cena Kitchen may have done better in the latter half of 2022,” (Opp’n at p. 5:13-14), Plaintiff’s declaration attaches, inter alia, a “copy of a comparison of Cena Kitchen LLC’s monthly gross sales” for 2022. (Huerta Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. F.) This does not appear to be addressed by Defendants.

Based on a consideration of the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause for the Court to designate December 31, 2022 as the valuation date. (Corp. Code, § 17707.03, subd. (c)(5).)

Conclusion

In light of the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for an order designating December 31, 2022 as the valuation date is granted.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this Order.
