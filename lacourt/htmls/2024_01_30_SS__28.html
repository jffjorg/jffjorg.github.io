DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.

                                       Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.  This does not excuse a moving party's need to do one of the following: appear; submit; or take a matter off calendar by canceling the motion in the case reservation system before issuance of the tentative ruling if the matter moving party does not intend to proceed.    
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 20STCV31315    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On August 18, 2020, Plaintiff Rosa Munoz-Ledo Valdez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“LACMTA”) and Does 1-50 for negligence. 

On November 20, 2020, LACMTA filed an answer. 

On December 21, 2020, Plaintiff amended the complaint to include Defendant Lonnie Gonzalez (“Gonzalez”) as Doe 1.  

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel, Angella S. Farokhzad, Esq., filed a motion to be relieved as counsel to be heard on January 30, 2024. 

Trial is currently scheduled for July 9, 2024. 

COUNSEL’S REQUEST 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Angella S. Farokhzad, Esq., requests to be relieved as counsel. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, provides: 

“(a) Notice 

“A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051). 

“(b) Memorandum 

“Notwithstanding any other rule of court, no memorandum is required to be filed or served with a motion to be relieved as counsel. 

“(c) Declaration 

“The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1). 

“(d) Service 

“The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service, electronic service, or mail. 

“(1)  If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either: 

“(A)   The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or 

“(B)   The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. 

“(2)  If the notice is served on the client by electronic service under Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6 and rule 2.251, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating that the electronic service address is the client's current electronic service address. 

“As used in this rule, ‘current’ means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned or no electronic delivery failure message was received is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011(b) applies. 

“(e) Order 

“The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.” 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.) 

DISCUSSION 

Counsel has submitted completed MC-051, MC-052 and MC-053 forms. Counsel has provided a declaration stating that there has been a complete breakdown of the attorney-client relationship. Counsel stated that she served Plaintiff by mail at her last known address with return receipt requested. 

However, counsel has not provided a proof of service for any of the submitted documents.  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, requires that counsel serve the motion on all parties who have appeared in the case, including counsel’s client. 

The Court denies the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES without prejudice the motion of Plaintiff’s counsel to be relieved as counsel. 

Counsel is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Counsel is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 21STCV13224    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposing and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On April 7, 2021, Plaintiff Yolanda Aguilar Murillo (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Califa Investment LLC (“Califa”) and Does 1-50 for general negligence and premises liability. 

On October 4, 2022, Califa filed a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants Moore Family Trust, f/k/a Moore & Ellestad GST, LLC (“Trust”) and Roes 1-20 for total indemnity, declaratory relief – implied partial indemnity, and express indemnity.  On January 6, 2023, Trust filed an answer. 

On June 7, 2023, the Court struck the express indemnity claim against Trust from Califa’s cross-complaint based on a stipulation by Califa and Trust. 

On October 24, 2023, Califa filed a motion for protective order to be heard on January 30, 2024. On January 12, 2024, Trust filed an opposition. On January 23, 2024, Califa filed a reply. 

On November 1, 2023, Plaintiff amended the complaint to include Defendant Trust as Doe 1.  On December 4, 2023, Trust filed an answer and a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants Califa and Moes 1-20 for implied equitable indemnity, comparative contribution, and declaratory relief. 

Trial is currently scheduled for August 21, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Califa requests that the Court grant a protective order to prevent Trust from taking the deposition of non-party Alon Varsha. 

Trust requests that the Court deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

          Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.030 provides:  

“(a) The court shall restrict the frequency or extent of use of a discovery method provided in Section 2019.010 if it determines either of the following: 

“(1) The discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive. 

“(2) The selected method of discovery is unduly burdensome or expensive, taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation. 

“(b) The court may make these determinations pursuant to a motion for a protective order by a party or other affected person. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.030.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420 provides in part: 

“(a) Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(b) The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: 

“(1) That the deposition not be taken at all. 

* * *

 (5) That the deposition be taken only on certain specified terms and conditions.

  (6) That the deponent's testimony be taken by written, instead of oral, examination.

* * *
 (10)  That the scope of the examination be limited to certain matters. . . ." 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subds. (a), (b).) 

DISCUSSION 

A.   The complaint 

The complaint alleges the following: 

Califa owned, possessed, maintained, and/or controlled the real property located at 15137 Califa Street, Van Nuys, California 91411 (“premises”). 

On April 9, 2019, Plaintiff was severely injured when she was struck by a gate on the premises.  Defendants negligently, recklessly, and carelessly maintained and operated the gate on the premises in a dangerous condition, causing Plaintiff’s injuries. Defendants knew or, through the exercise of reasonable care or diligence, should and would have known that the gate was in a dangerous condition before they allowed the Plaintiff to enter the premises. As a direct and proximate result of Defendants’ conduct, Plaintiff has suffered physical injuries, mental anguish, loss of consortium, pain and suffering. 

B.   Califa’s motion for protective order 

          On August 25, 2023, Trust served a notice of taking the deposition of Alon Varsha (“Varsha”), Califa’s owner and top executive.  Califa asks for a protective order to prevent the deposition, arguing Trust previously deposed Califa’s person most knowledgeable, Emma Gamez (“Gamez”), the property manager at the premises where Plaintiff alleges she was injured.  Gamez testified that neither she nor Varsha had first-hand knowledge of the incident.  Califa argues that (1) Varsha has no knowledge or information that Gamez did not provide and (2) Trust can obtain Varsha’s indirect knowledge about the incident using written discovery requests.  As a result, Califa argues, a deposition of Varsha would be unduly burdensome and the burden would outweigh any benefits of taking the deposition. 

          Califa relies on Liberty Mutual Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282 (Liberty Mutual), where the Court of Appeal “conclude[d] it amounts to an abuse of discretion to withhold a protective order when a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president, or corporate officer at the apex of the corporate hierarchy, absent a reasonable indication of the officer's personal knowledge of the case and absent exhaustion of less intrusive discovery methods.” (Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at p. 1287.)  The appellate court held: 

“[W]hen a plaintiff seeks to depose a corporate president or other official at the highest level of corporate management, and that official moves for a protective order to prohibit the deposition, the trial court should first determine whether the plaintiff has shown good cause that the official has unique or superior personal knowledge of discoverable information. If not, as will presumably often be the case in the instance of a large national or international corporation, the trial court should issue the protective order and first require the plaintiff to obtain the necessary discovery through less intrusive methods. These would include interrogatories directed to the high-level official to explore the state of his or her knowledge or involvement in plaintiff's case; the deposition of lower level employees with appropriate knowledge and involvement in the subject matter of the litigation; and the organizational deposition of the corporation itself, which will require the corporation to produce for deposition the most qualified officer or employee to testify on its behalf as to the specified matters to be raised at the deposition.  [Citation.] Should these avenues be exhausted, and the plaintiff make a colorable showing of good cause that the high-level official possesses necessary information to the case, the trial court may then lift the protective order and allow the deposition to proceed.”  (Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at p. 1287.) 

C.   Trust’s opposition  

 Trust asserts that Gamez testified at her deposition that (1) Varsha paid to install the gate over twenty years ago, prior to Gamez’s affiliation with Califa, and (2) Gamez’s knowledge of the gate’s installation is limited to what Varsha told her. Therefore, Trust argues, it is entitled to take Varsha’s remote deposition about the installation of the gate.  In addition, Trust contends that it cannot propound written discovery to Varsha because he is not a party to the case. 

D.   Califa’s reply 

Califa argues that Trust has not shown Varsha has any information about installation of the gate that Gamez did not testify about.  Califa also disputes Trust’s interpretation of Gamez’s deposition testimony and argues that it served verified answers to Trust’s written discovery about the gate’s purchase and installation. 

E.   Analysis 

The “apex” doctrine is designed for “large national or international corporation[s] . . . .”  (Liberty Mutual, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at p. 1287.)  Gamez testified that Califa consists of two people, Varsha and Gamez.  Even assuming the apex doctrine applies here, it does not bar Trust from deposing Varsha about the purchase and installation of the gate subject to certain terms and conditions. 

At her October 31, 2023 deposition, Gamez testified: The gate had already been installed when she began working as Califa’s property manager in 2008.  (Page 19.)  To the best of Gamez’s knowledge, Califa’s owner and the owner of the adjacent building hired the company that installed the gate.  (Page 20.)  Gamez had spoken to Varsha when she was started working for Califa about the installation of the gate, who paid for the installation, or if Varsha knew when the gate was installed.  (Pages 20-21.)  The gate was installed over 20 years ago.  (Page 21.)  As far as Gamez knows, Varsha and the neighbor paid for the purchase and installation of the gate.  (Pages 21, 24.)  Gamez did not know who the neighbor was.  (Page 21.)  Gamez believes that Varsha told her that he and the neighbor split the cost of installing the gate 50/50 but Gamez can’t remember the exact percentage.  (Page 24.)  Varsha told Gamez the gate was installed as a safety precaution because people were accessing the parking lot without authorization.  (Page 22.)   Gamez does not know who purchased the lock put on the gate.  (Page 22.) 

Gamez’s testimony shows that Varsha is likely to have information about the purchase and installation of the gate that Gamez does not have.  In addition, Trust has conducted discovery through less intrusive methods: written discovery to Califa and the deposition of Gamez.  

“The only methods by which someone who is not a party to the action can be compelled to provide testimony or produce other evidence are:

·       an oral deposition under CCP §2020.310 (the most common method [citation];

·       an oral deposition with production of ‘business records’ under CCP §2020.510 [citation];

·       a written deposition under CCP §2028.010 [citation]; or

·       an ‘business records’ subpoena under CCP §2020.410 [citation].”  

(L. Edmon & C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2023) ¶ 8:464, p. 8E-16, citing Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.010, subd. (a).) 

          The Court grants Califa's request for a protective order and rules that Trust may take Varsha's deposition subject to certain terms and conditions.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §  2025.420, subd. (b)(5).)  Varsha may choose between an oral deposition under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.310 and a written deposition under Code of Civil Procedure section 2028.010.  In either format, the deposition will be limited to Varsha’s knowledge of the purchase and installation of the gate at the premises.  If Varsha chooses an oral deposition, the deposition will be conducted remotely unless Varsha chooses to appear in person.  

          The Court denies Califa’s request to appoint a discovery referee. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Califa Investment, LLC’s motion for a protective order and rules that Cross-Defendant Moore Family Trust, f/k/a Moore & Ellestad GST, LLC may take Alon Varsha's deposition subject to certain terms and conditions. 

The Court orders that non-party Alon Varsha may choose whether his deposition by Cross-Defendant Moore Family Trust, f/k/a Moore & Ellestad GST, LLC will be an oral deposition under Code of Civil Procedure section 2020.310 or a written deposition under Code of Civil Procedure section 2028.010. 

In either format, the deposition will be limited to Alon Varsha’s knowledge of the purchase and installation of the gate at 15137 Califa Street, Van Nuys, California 91411.

If Alon Varsha chooses an oral deposition, the deposition will be conducted remotely unless Alon Varsha chooses to appear in person.  

The Court denies Defendant Califa Investment, LLC’s request to appoint a discovery referee.

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.   Summary judgment and summary adjudication      

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (f), provides in part: 

“(1) A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty. 

“(2) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. . . .” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f).) 

“‘[F]rom commencement to conclusion, the party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.’”  (LAOSD Asbestos Cases (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 949, 945, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (Aguilar).) “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850.)  

          “Supporting and opposing affidavits or declarations . . . shall set forth admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (d).) “Matters which would be excluded under the rules of evidence if proffered by a witness in a trial as hearsay, conclusions or impermissible opinions, must be disregarded in supporting affidavits.”  (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639.) 

          A party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must support the motion with “a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts which the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Parkview Villas Assn. v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. (2006) 133 Cal.App.4th 1197, 1209 (Parkview Villas), quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1) [motion for summary judgment]; see Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(2) [motion for summary adjudication “shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment”].) The party opposing the motion must file with the opposition papers “a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating whether the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts that the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(3).)  If either party fails to comply with the applicable separate statement requirement, that failure may in the court’s discretion constitute a sufficient ground to decide the motion adversely to the offending party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (b)(1), (3).)   

          In ruling on the motion, the court must consider all of the evidence and all of the inferences reasonably drawn from it and must view such evidence and inferences in the light most favorable to the opposing party.  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 843.) 

B.   Negligence and premises liability 

“The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.) 

DISCUSSION 

A.   The complaint 

The complaint alleges the following: 

Defendants negligently, carelessly, and recklessly and with disregard for the rights of the Plaintiff, managed, maintained, supervised, entrusted, repaired, authorized, approved, leased, loaned, bailed, controlled, and operated a facility located at 26101 Magic Mountain Parkway, Valencia, CA 91355 (“the premises”). 

On August 19, 2019, Defendants failed to maintain, design, inspect, control, supervise, attend to, and care for the premises.  Defendants breached their duty of care to Plaintiff when they failed to properly maintain, manage, and control the premises, failing to alert or warn Plaintiff of an inherently unsafe condition. As a proximate result of Defendants’ negligence, Plaintiff sustained severe injuries. 

B.   Undisputed facts 

On August 19, 2019, Plaintiff visited Defendant’s water park Six Flags Hurricane Harbor with her husband and children.  Plaintiff had not previously ridden a water ride at an amusement park. 

Plaintiff got in line for the Black Snake Summit water slide.  A sign posted at the entrance to the ride stated: 

“ATTENTION ALL GUESTS READ IMPORTANT SAFETY INFORMATION AND FOLLOW INSTRUCTION OF WATER SAFETY STAFF: RIDER DESCRIPTION: HIGH THRILL BLACK SNAKE SUMMIT

Do not ride if you have any of the following conditions: Neck, Back or Bone Ailments

For your safety, you should be in good health to ride. . . . Only you know your physical conditions and limitations. If you suspect your health could be at risk for any reason, or you could aggravate a pre-existing condition of any kind, DO NOT RIDE!

Rider Responsibility There are inherent risks in the participation in or on any amusement ride or attraction. Patrons have a duty to exercise good judgment and act in a responsible manner while participating on the ride or attraction and to obey all oral and/or written warnings. Patrons also have a duty to properly use all ride or attraction safety equipment provided.” 

At the top of the slide’s tower, additional signage was posted that stated: 

“Do not ride if you have a significant physical ailment or condition.”

“Do not ride if you have neck or back problems”

“Have high blood pressure, heart trouble, nervous disorder, are pregnant or under the influence of drugs or alcohol.” 

A sign was also posted at the entrance of the ride instructing patrons: “Exit slide run-out quickly. Failure to follow all rules and instructions can result in serious injury to yourself or others.” 

Plaintiff saw the sign posted at the top of the slide. 

Before she got onto the ride, she understood that once she got to the top, she would be riding on one of the slides to get all the way to the bottom ground level again.  (Exh. A p. 131.) 

When Plaintiff got to the top, two park employees were standing next to the slides.  (Exh. A p. 134.)  One of the employees told Plaintiff to get on the ride.  (Exh. A pp. 136-137.)  Plaintiff got on the ride and went down the tube.  (Exh. A p. 137; Exh. B p. 152.)  Her body was moving on the water and she did not stop while she was inside the enclosed flume.  (Exh. B p. 141.) 

Plaintiff testified at her deposition: “I did whatever [the park employees] told me anyways. And I slid down the tube. And I believe I was in the middle of the way when all of a sudden out of the blue something severely hit me. I don't know what it was. But it severely hit me in my head, my shoulder, and my neck.  [¶]  It was so horrible, so unexpected, it was so terrifying that when I ultimately ended up at the end of the tube, at the bottom of the tube, I don't remember exactly what had happened or what happened next. I was semi unconscious when I got to the bottom of the tube.”  (Exh. B p. 152.)  Something hit Plaintiff while she was inside the black enclosed tube, where it was dark and Plaintiff could  not see anything.  (Exh. B pp. 152-153.)  “And later I realized that it was the gentleman who had followed me and the worker had mistakenly got that gentleman on the ride right after me.”  (Exh. B p. 153.)  After Plaintiff was hit, she landed in the water.  (Exh. B p. 153.) 

Despite Plaintiff’s deposition testimony, the evidence shows that if Plaintiff was bumped, the incident occurred after she was already stopped in the run-out area.  (Defendant’s additional undisputed material fact 25.) 

C.   Legal issues 

1.    Statute of limitations 

Defendant’s fourth affirmative defense, titled Statute of Limitations, alleges that “each cause of action thereof, is barred the [sic] California Code of Civil Procedure.” 

Plaintiff asks the Court to grant summary adjudication of Defendant’s statute of limitations affirmative defense, arguing Plaintiff filed her complaint within the two-year limitations period provided by Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1.  The incident in which Plaintiff allegedly was injured took place on August 19, 2019.  Plaintiff filed the complaint on May 26, 2021.  Plaintiff has carried her initial summary adjudication burden on the limitations issue. 

In response, Defendant notes that Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant in the Santa Clara County Superior Court on January 25, 2021.  Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot prove timely filing of the Los Angeles County case unless she shows the Santa Clara County case was dismissed before May 26, 2021.  Defendant provides no legal authority to support this argument. 

Defendant has not raised a triable issue of fact regarding the statute of limitations.  The Court grants summary adjudication of Defendant’s statute of limitations affirmative defense. 

2.    Assumption of risk 

Defendant’s second affirmative defense, titled Assumption of Risk, alleges that “Plaintiffs injuries, if any, were caused and contributed to by Plaintiff’s own assumption of risk.” 

Relying on Sharufa v. Festival Fun Parks, LLC (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 493 (Sharufa), Plaintiff asks the Court to grant summary adjudication of Defendant’s assumption of the risk affirmative defense.  In Sharufa, the plaintiff was injured on a waterslide at Raging Waters, a theme park operated by the defendant.  The defendant argued that riding a waterslide carries with it certain inherent risks that the plaintiff assumed by engaging in the activity.  (Sharufa, supra, 49 Cal.App.5th at p. 498.)  The Court of Appeal rejected the defendant’s argument that the primary assumption of the risk doctrine barred the plaintiff’s claims.  (Id. at p. 500.)  Instead, the appellate court concluded the waterslide operator was a common carrier because riders do not control their own movements as they are transported to the pool below and the route is predetermined.  (Id. at pp. 499-500.)  As a common carrier, the defendant owed the plaintiff a heightened standard of care.  (Id. at p. 500.)  Based on Sharufa, Plaintiff argues, Defendant cannot establish the affirmative defense of primary assumption of the risk. 

Plaintiff also argues that Defendant cannot establish an affirmative defense based on secondary assumption of the risk because “[s]secondary assumption of risk is essentially a form of comparative negligence under which a defendant owes a duty of care to the plaintiff, but the plaintiff bears some fault for voluntarily encountering a known risk.”  (Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29, 53; see Gregory v. Cott (2014) 59 Cal.4th 996, 1001 [“Secondary assumption of risk applies when the defendant does owe a duty, but the plaintiff has knowingly encountered a risk of injury caused by the defendant's breach. Liability in such cases is adjudicated under the rules of comparative negligence”].) 

Plaintiff has carried her initial summary adjudication burden of showing that Defendant cannot prevail on a primary assumption of risk affirmative defense.  However, for the reasons discussed below, Plaintiff has not carried her initial summary adjudication burden on the secondary assumption of risk affirmative defense, which is merged into the doctrine of comparative fault.  Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiff's motion for summary adjudication of Defendant's assumption of risk affirmative defense. 

3.    Comparative negligence 

Defendant’s first affirmative defense, titled Comparative Negligence, alleges that “Plaintiff's injuries, if any, were caused and contributed to by Plaintiff s own negligence.” 

Plaintiff asserts that Defendant cannot prove that Plaintiff breached any duty or that any negligence by Plaintiff caused her injuries.  Plaintiff relies on her deposition testimony that while she was on the water slide, she was hit by the gentleman who followed her down the slide.  In addition, Plaintiff has presented evidence that the accident occurred when one of Defendant’s employees violated Defendant’s policies by pressing a button that sent another rider down the slide before Plaintiff had exited the ride.  (See Exh. E pp. 115-118; Exh. I at MM0012-0014.)  Based on this evidence, Plaintiff has carried its initial summary adjudication burden on the comparative negligence affirmative defense. 

Defendant argues that “Plaintiff entered the slide with full knowledge that she had pre-existing orthopedic problems, despite the signage. She failed to timely exit the slide and was bumped by the other rider.”  (Opposition p. 14.)  According to Defendant, it has produced “admissible evidence that (1) Plaintiff voluntarily engaged in the activity; and (2) Plaintiff's decision to encounter the risk, without following instructional signage, should be characterized as unreasonable.” 

Defendant argues it has raised a triable issue concerning whether Plaintiff was negligent because she rode the water slide despite pre-existing orthopedic problems.  More specifically, Defendant contends that “there is a triable issue of fact as to why Plaintiff did not exit the run-out promptly.”  (Opposition p. 18.)  However, Defendant has presented no evidence showing that any pre-existing orthopedic problems caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s delay in exiting the run-out area.  A fact-finder would have to speculate that the asserted pre-existing orthopedic problems caused Plaintiff’s delay in exiting. 

Nonetheless, Defendant has presented evidence that, for whatever reason, Plaintiff delayed in exiting the run-out area despite the signage instructing patrons to exit quickly to avoid injury.  (Ounjian Dec. exh. I, p. MM0012 [“The lady came out of the right slide. I saw that she was still in slide and pushed button and walked down and told her to get out of the slide. She took a long time. The next rider came down and hit her”]; see exh. Q, pp. 25-36.) The undisputed facts show that the incident occurred while Plaintiff was in the run-out area.  (Defendant’s additional undisputed material fact 25.)  This evidence raises a triable issue concerning comparative negligence and secondary assumption of risk.  Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiff's motion for summary adjudication of Defendant's comparative negligence affirmative defense. 

4.    Fault of others and Proposition 51 

Defendant’s fifth affirmative defense, titled Fault of Others, alleges that “other persons or entities, whether or not parties, each, were negligent and such negligence was a proximate cause of Plaintiff's injuries, if any, and should any judgment be awarded to Plaintiff, it must be apportioned among all such negligent persons or entities and offset against any judgment against Defendant.” 

Defendant’s seventh affirmative defense, titled Proposition 51 - Joint and Several Liability, alleges that under Civil Code section 1431.2, each Defendant's liability for non-economic losses will be several only and not joint, and each answering Defendant shall only be liable for the amount of non-economic damages in the proportionate amount of liability assessed to such Defendant. 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant may not assert these affirmative defenses to reduce any judgment (or judgment for non-economic damages) entered against it by the comparative fault of Defendant’s employee who allegedly was responsible for sending another patron down the water slide before Plaintiff exited it because Defendant is vicariously liable for its employee’s negligence.  (Defendant denies it is making this argument.) 

In addition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant may not assert these affirmative defenses to reduce any judgment (or judgment for non-economic damages) by the comparative fault of the patron who allegedly struck Plaintiff in the run-out area because Defendant cannot show that the patron breached any duty or that any breach caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries.  Plaintiff appears to reason that Defendant cannot present such evidence because Defendant was responsible for the accident. 

Plaintiff has failed to carry her initial summary adjudication burden of showing that Defendant cannot present evidence that other persons or entities were negligent and their negligence caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries.  The Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication of the fault of others and Proposition 51 affirmative defenses. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS IN PART AND DENIES IN PART Plaintiff Sedigheh Hajizadeh’s motion for summary adjudication as follows. 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Sedigheh Hajizadeh’s motion for summary adjudication of Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC’s statute of limitations affirmative defense. 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff Sedigheh Hajizadeh’s motion for summary adjudication of Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC’s assumption of risk affirmative defense to the extent the affirmative defense involves the doctrine of secondary assumption of risk (which is merged into Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC’s comparative negligence affirmative defense).

Case Number: 21STCV27987    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On July 29, 2021, Plaintiff Cynthia Hopes (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Costco Wholesale Corporation (“Costco”), Sergio (Last Name Unknown), and Does 1-50 for negligence. 

On October 21, 2021, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Costco, Sergio (Last Name Unknown), and Does 1-50 for negligence and premises liability. On October 26, 2021, the Court struck the first amended complaint, overruled Costco’s demurrer, and ordered Costco to answer. 

On November 24, 2021, Costco filed an answer. 

On May 24, 2023, the Court granted Costco’s motion to continue the trial and continued the trial from July 27, 2023 to February 27, 2024, with all discovery and related dates set to trail the new trial date. 

On December 21, 2023, the Court granted in part Costco’s ex parte application to continue the trial and related dates.  The Court continued the trial from February 27, 2024 to July 23, 2024, but denied without prejudice Costco’s request to continue or reopen discovery deadlines to correspond with the new trial date. 

On January 3, 2024, Costco filed a motion to move the discovery cutoff and trial-related dates to go with the current trial date and reopen discovery, to be heard on January 30, 2024.  On January 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On January 23, 2024, Costco filed a reply. 

Trial is currently set for July 23, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Costco requests that the Court continue discovery and all related dates to go with the current trial date and reopen discovery. 

Plaintiff requests that the Court deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020 provides: 

“(a) Except as otherwise provided in this chapter, any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action. 

“(b) Except as provided in Section 2024.050, a continuance or postponement of the trial date does not operate to reopen discovery proceedings.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020.) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 provides: 

“(a) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(b) In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following: 

“(1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery. 

“(2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier. 

“(3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party. 

“(4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action. 

“(c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to extend or to reopen discovery, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050.) 

DISCUSSION 

          Costco asks the Court to re-open discovery because (1) Plaintiff had back surgery in September 2023, (2) between September 15, 2023 and November 2, 2023 Plaintiff’s claimed medical specials increased from approximately $27,000 to over $950,000, and (3) in December 2023, Plaintiff disclosed a new claim of incontinence that she attributes to the incident at Costco. 

          Plaintiff argues that Costco has not acted diligently in conducting discovery and Plaintiff has already disclosed the information Costco is requesting.  According to Plaintiff, Costco “has known about all of Plaintiff’s injuries from the subject incident and prior injuries since December 24, 2021, when Plaintiff served its initial set of discovery responses.”  (Opposition p. 3.)  Plaintiff asserts that she disclosed a recommendation for back surgery at her February 2, 2023 deposition and disclosed all of her previous injuries, including back pain, at a May 4, 2023 defense medical examination.  Plaintiff does not, however, challenge Costco’s contention that Plaintiff’s claimed medical specials increased from approximately $27,000 to over $950,000 between September 15, 2023 and November 2, 2023. 

The Court has considered the factors listed in Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050 and grants the motion to reopen discovery. 

Discovery is reopened.  Discovery and related deadlines will be based on the July 23, 2024 trial date.            

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Costco Wholesale Corporation’s motion to reopen discovery.  Discovery is reopened.  Discovery and related deadlines will be based on the July 23, 2024 trial date.
