DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00427    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: H

Liu v. Sunnyland International Group, et al., Case No. 21PSCV00427 

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

Defendant Sunnyland International Group’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

I.        Background

In this quiet title action, Plaintiff Yongyi Liu (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows: 

Plaintiff is the equitable owner and is in possession and control of the real property located at 636 East Boxwood Lane, Azusa, California, 91702 (the “subject property”). On or about August 18, 2005, Plaintiff and Defendant Feng Li (“Li”) had a child, Meixi Li. At the time Meixi Li was born and to date, Li was and has been married to another woman. On July 27, 2012, Li purchased and gifted the subject property to Plaintiff since she was raising Meixi Li by herself. Prior to and upon closing of the escrow of the subject property, Li told Plaintiff that he had purchased the subject property for her and was giving it to her. Li told Plaintiff he would transfer title to the subject property at a future time.

In or about early August 2012, shortly before escrow closed on the subject property, Li asked Plaintiff to come to the United States with him so that he could transfer title to the subject property from him to her. Plaintiff came with her mom, her other daughter Helin Xu (“Xu”), and Meixi Li and took possession of the subject property; however, due to an unexpected event, Li had to return to China before the transfer paperwork was completed. 

In or about December 2013, Li came to the United States for a short trip; Plaintiff was then in China. Li promised to transfer title to the subject property to Plaintiff when they were both in the United States. In or about April 2014, Li was incarcerated in China on a 13-year sentence. Li remains incarcerated. On March 27, 2021, while still incarcerated, Li purportedly signed a grant deed granting the subject property to Sunnyland International Group (“Sunnyland”). On or about April 14, 2021, Li allegedly sent a Notice to Quit to Plaintiff’s address in China, claiming therein that the subject property was owned jointly by Li and his wife, Jing Li, and that they had sold the subject property to a third party, Sunnyland. On April 16, 2021, Xi Lin (“Lin”) went to Plaintiff’s house and told Xu that Lin was the President of Sunnyland and demanded possession of the subject property. Lin represented to Xu that Li had transferred title of the subject property to Sunnyland to settle a loan he owed to the company and placed a Notice to Quit on the front door of the subject property. On April 26, 2021, Xu served a 30-day Notice to Terminate Tenancy at Will on Plaintiff. 

On May 21, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Sunnyland, Lin, Li, Jing Li, and Does 1-50 for (1) quiet title, (2) cancellation of instrument, and (3) declaratory relief. 

On August 19, 2021, the Court related and consolidated this case with Case No. 21WCUD00362 and designated this case as the lead case. On September 30, 2021, Sunnyland filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Plaintiff, Li, and Meixi Li for (1) declaratory relief, (2) quiet title, (3) equitable lien, and (4) unjust enrichment. 

On April 11, 2024, an “Order Entering Default Against Defendants Feng Li and Jing Li” was entered.

The final status conference is set for November 4, 2024 and a bench trial is set for November 8, 2024. Pending before the Court is Sunnyland’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is denied.

 

II.      Legal Standard

“A party may move for summary judgment. . . if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).) 

“The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)

“A defendant or cross-defendant has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action. . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action. Once the defendant. . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

III.     Discussion 

A.       Request for Judicial Notice and Objections 

The Court first addresses Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice and Sunnyland’s objections to the Declaration of Yongyi Liu.

Plaintiff asks the Court to take judicial notice of the following documents: (1) Feng Li’s criminal trial judgment dated September 9, 2016, (2) The People’s Intermediate Court of Benxi City of Liaoning Province’s Administrative Ruling dated April 27, 2021, and (3) the grant deed from Feng Li to Sunnyland International Group dated April 14, 2021. A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court denies the request as to the first two items but grants the request as to the third item (the grant deed). 

Turning to Sunnyland’s objections, the Court overrules the objections. Sunnyland principally objects to the Declaration of Yongyi Liu on hearsay grounds. However, “[t]here is a well established exception or departure from the hearsay rule applying to cases in which the very fact in controversy is whether certain things were said or done and not as to whether these things were true or false, and in these cases the words or acts are admissible not as hearsay, but as original evidence.” (Am-Cal Inv. Co. v. Sharlyn Ests., Inc. (1967) 255 Cal. App. 2d 526, 528 [“In these situations, the words themselves, written or oral, are ‘operative facts,’ and an issue in the case is whether they were uttered or written.” (Internal citations and quotation marks omitted)].) Here, because Plaintiff recounts the statements by Li to demonstrate the terms of their oral contract, Li’s statements are not excluded by the hearsay rules. 

The Court similarly overrules Sunnyland’s objection that Plaintiff lacks personal knowledge of Li’s arrest and incarceration. The Court need not address Sunnyland’s objections to Plaintiff’s request in light of the Court’s ruling declining to take judicial notice of Li’s criminal trial judgment and the foreign court ruling.

B.       The Alternative Request for Summary Adjudication

At the outset, the Court notes that, while the instant motion is styled as a “Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication,” Sunnyland has failed to identify any issues for summary adjudication in its notice of motion, as required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1350(b) (i.e., “[i]f made in the alternative, a motion for summary adjudication may make reference to and depend on the same evidence submitted in support of the summary judgment motion. If summary adjudication is sought, whether separately or as an alternative to the motion for summary judgment, the specific cause of action, affirmative defense, claims for damages, or issues of duty must be stated specifically in the notice of motion and be repeated, verbatim, in the separate statement of undisputed material facts” [Emphasis added]). The Court thus construes the instant motion as a motion for summary judgment only.

C.       The Motion for Summary Judgment 

Sunnyland contends that Plaintiff cannot establish the elements of each of her causes of action and there is no dispute as to the material facts.

Generally, a holder of equitable title cannot maintain an action for quiet title against the legal owner. (Stafford v. Ballinger (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 289, 294-295.) However, Sunnyland acknowledges an exception to this general rule that entitles an equitable title holder to challenge a legal title holder if the title was obtained by fraud. (Liberty National Enterprises, L.P. V. Chicago Title Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 62, 81 [“[W]hen legal title has been acquired through fraud[,] . . . available remedies include quieting title in the defrauded equitable title holder’s name and making the legal title holder the constructive trustee of the property for the benefit of the defrauded equitable titleholder.” (Internal quotation marks and citations omitted)].)

Here, Plaintiff alleges that the grant deed Sunnlyand relies on was procured by fraud, as Li (the alleged grantor) was imprisoned in China on the alleged date the transfer occurred, March 27, 2021. Sunnyland takes the position that this fact does not matter because Plaintiff admitted that Li sent a Notice to Quit to Plaintiff informing Plaintiff that the subject property was sold to a third party. The Court is unpersuaded. As explained below, Sunnyland has failed to carry its burden of demonstrating that no triable issues of material fact exist. Sunnyland’s motion is denied.
    

          (1)      Quiet Title 

An action for quiet title seeks “to establish title against adverse claims to real or personal property or any interest therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 760.020, subd. (a).) Plaintiff must plead (1) “[a] description of the property that is the subject of the action,” specifically the location of tangible personal property and the legal description and street address or common designation of real property, (2) “[t]he title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title,” (3) “[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought,” (4) “[t]he date as of which the determination is sought,” and (5) “[a] prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.) 

At a minimum, the Court determines that there are triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff is an equitable titleholder and whether Li’s purported transfer of the property to Sunnyland was fraudulent. According to Plaintiff’s declaration, she has paid for all expenses, maintenance, and taxes on the subject property since the subject property was purchased in 2012. (Liu Decl., ¶ 8.) While generally a holder of equitable title like Plaintiff cannot maintain an action for quiet title against the legal owner, Plaintiff alleges that Sunnyland’s grant deed was fraudulently obtained because Li was imprisoned in China on the date the alleged transfer took place. (See Liu Decl., ¶9.) Moreover, the grant deed is notarized by a Notary Public from Texas, alleging Li appeared before her in the county of Dallas. (Appendix of Evidence ISO Sunnyland’s Motion for Summary Judgment, Exh. 4.) While Sunnyland contends this is irrelevant because of a subsequent Notice to Quit purportedly sent to Plaintiff by Li, Sunnyland fails to carry its burden on summary judgment as it provides no explanation for how Li, who is allegedly in prison in a foreign country, could have appeared before a notary in Texas. Outside of the grant deed, Sunnyland provides no other evidence to rebut Plaintiff’s allegation of fraud. Accordingly, the Court determines that there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether the deed transfer was fraudulent. 

Sunnyland additionally contends the first cause of action should fail because Plaintiff failed to name all parties that hold an adverse claim to Plaintiff’s per Code of Civil Procedure section 762.010. Sunnyland argues that Miao Li, Li’s daughter from another relationship, has an interest in the title because Sunnyland transferred the title to her on April 12, 2021, 16 days after Sunnyland purportedly acquired the subject property from Li. However, this ignores the fact that Sunnyland was only able to transfer the property to Miao Li based on the initial purported deed transfer from Li, which Plaintiff alleges was fraudulent due to Li’s incarceration in China. Plaintiff’s complaint was filed only a month later. Although Plaintiff failed to name Miao Li in the lawsuit, Plaintiff alleges that Miao Li only has this interest because of the alleged fraud. The Court agrees. 

None of the cases cited by Sunnyland compels a different result. G.R. Holcomb Estate Co. v. Burke (1935) 4 Cal.2d 289 makes no mention of fraud at all, and Stafford v. Ballinger (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 289, 294-295 dismisses the alleged fraudulent acts as irrelevant because they were alleged to have occurred 17 years before the case being filed such that any cause of action would be barred by the statute of limitations. Here, of course, Plaintiff filed the lawsuit within a month of the alleged fraudulent transfer. Lewis v. Superior Court (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1850, 1858-60 is similarly inapposite, both because the case did not involve an actual title claim (as the lis pendens only served as a means of collecting monetary damages) and because the Lewises were good faith purchasers who lacked actual and constructive notice of the fraud, as the lis pendens had not been indexed until after the Lewises acquired title. In this case, title is clearly in dispute. Moreover, Sunnyland was not far removed from the purported fraud; it was a party to the transfer of property from a man who was allegedly incarcerated in China. While Sunnyland will have the opportunity to prove at trial that the sale was legitimate, the notarized grant deed showing that Li personally appeared before a notary in Texas, despite being allegedly incarcerated in China, provides a triable issue of fact as to whether the sale was fraudulent.

(2)           Cancellation of Instrument and Declaratory Relief 

To prevail on claim to cancel an instrument, plaintiff must prove: (1) the instrument is void or voidable due to, for example, fraud, and (2) there is a reasonable apprehension of serious injury including pecuniary loss or prejudicial alteration of one’s position. (U.S. Bank National Assn. v. Naifeh (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 767, 778; see also Cal. Civ. Code § 3412.) 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

Both the second and third causes of action largely flow from the first cause of action. As to the cancellation of instrument cause of action, because a finding of fraud can make an instrument voidable, Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of material fact. Additionally, Plaintiff has demonstrated apprehension of serious injury as she could lose her home without judicial intervention. As to the cause of action for declaratory relief, the subject property and the instant quiet title action are proper subjects for declaratory relief as there is a clear controversy involving each of the party’s rights.

IV.     Conclusion

Because Sunnyland fails to carry its burden of demonstrating no triable issues of material fact, its Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. In advance of the final status conference on November 4, 2024, the parties are directed to review the Department H Case Management Standing Order and serve and file trial documents in accordance with that order.

Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club v. Leal, Case No. 23PSCV01168

ORDER ON DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION

Plaintiff Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.


Background

Plaintiff Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club (“Plaintiff”) seeks subrogation against Defendant Santos Leal (“Defendant”) for damages sustained by its insured, James Albert Chinchilla, in an April 9, 2021 motor vehicle collision.

On April 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting a subrogation cause of action against Defendant and Does 1-10.

On June 27, 2024, Defendant’s default was entered.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment.

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted:

1.       Plaintiff has not provided the Court with a summary of the case, as per California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(1). 

2.       Tara Murray (“Murray”) represents that the total amount paid to Plaintiff’s insured was $43,776.14 (Murray Decl., ¶ 3), which is comprised of $17,565.14 for collision damages ($27,940.14 minus the salvage recovery of $10,375.00), plus $25,000.00 for bodily injuries sustained by the insured, plus $211.00 in towing and storage expenses and plus $1,000.00 for the collision deductible assigned by the insured to Plaintiff. Murray is directed to explain where the $1,000.00 and $17,565.14 amounts are located on Exhibit B to her declaration or to otherwise explain how these amounts are shown on Exhibit B.
 

3.       The medical bills attached as Exhibit G to Murray’s declaration total $6,270.00, yet $25,000.00 was apparently paid to the insured for medical expenses. Murray is directed to explain this discrepancy to the Court.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s application is denied without prejudice, and Plaintiff is directed to submit a revised default judgment packet addressing the aforementioned issues.

Case Number: 23PSCV02347    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: H

Herrera, et al. v. Reliant Tank Division LLC, et al., Case No. 23PSCV02347

ORDER ON MOTION FOR PREFERENTIAL TRIAL SETTING

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preferential Trial Setting Pursuant to C.C.P. § 36(d) is GRANTED.

 

Background 


In this wrongful death action, Plaintiffs Roselia Herrera and Jose Mendoza, individually and as successors in interest to Juan Carlos Mendoza, deceased (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege that their son died after his vehicle collided with a tractor trailer parked on the shoulder of the I-210 westbound freeway.

On December 4, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against Reliant Tank Division LLC, Carl Kenyon Parker, and Does 1-100 for (1) negligence (wrongful death) and (2) negligence (survivor’s action).

 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiffs’ motion for preferential trial setting. 

Legal Standard

“In its discretion, the court may also grant a motion for preference that is accompanied by clear and convincing medical documentation that concludes that one of the parties suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of survival of that party beyond six months, and that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (d).). 

Subdivision (c)(1) requires that all essential parties be served with process or have appeared in the action. 

If a motion for preference is granted, the Court must set a trial date not more than 120 days from the date that the motion is granted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (f).)

Discussion

Plaintiffs move the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure § 36, subdivision (d), for an order granting trial preference in this action.

Plaintiff Herrera represents that she suffered a heart attack in 2008; that she has since had “persistent cardiac issues requiring frequent, lengthy hospitalizations;” that she has been in and out of the hospital at UCLA Medical Center (“UCLA”) for cardiac-related issues approximately 11 times since 2021; that her doctors have told her that she has end-stage heart failure and that her condition is continuing to get worse; that her UCLA health care providers have repeatedly told her that hospice care is a good option for her but that she has instead chosen to receive a medication at home through a portable pump in order to be close to her family; that she was being considered by UCLA for an open-heart transplant before decedent passed away but has since been told she no longer qualifies because she does not have sufficient caregiver support; and that she was intubated during her most recent hospitalization at UCLA, from July 3-22, 2024. (Herrera Decl., ¶¶ 3-6.)

Herrera’s counsel, Farnaz Salessi, has provided the Court with copies of Herrera’s medical records, which he personally downloaded directly from Herrera’s myUCLAhealth online medical record portal using login information provided to him by Herrera’s daughter, Joelyn Mendoza. (Salessi Decl., ¶¶ 2 and 3, Exhs. 1-13.) These records are comprised of various discharge summaries reflecting hospitalizations on November 19, 2021-January 12, 2022, March 8, 2022-March 13, 2022, May 21, 2022-June 19, 2022, July 5, 2022-July 13, 2022, September 17, 2022-September 27, 2022, October 30, 2022-November 14, 2022, March 18, 2023-March 24, 2023, September 4, 2023-September 25, 2023, November 23, 2023-December 9, 2023, April 17, 2024-April 29, 2024 and July 3, 2024-July 22, 2024, as well as a January 10, 2024 progress note and a July 2, 2024 admission note. (Id., ¶ 3, Exhs. 1-13.) Herrera was advised on December 8, 2023 that “her treatment options are limited and her overall condition continues to worsen despite aggressive medical treatment” and that hospice care was “a reasonable option given her overall poor prognosis.” (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. 9.) In a January 10, 2024 clinical note, Herrera’s physician noted having conveyed to Herrera that “she has end stage heart failure” at “a high risk of frequent hospitalizations,” and “is not a surgical transplant/VAD candidate.” (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. 12.)

During Herrera’s most recent hospitalization, doctors noted that she had “a high probability of sudden, clinically significant deterioration, which requires the highest level of physician preparedness to intervene urgently.” (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. 13.) The motion was filed August 6, 2024. Defendants filed their answers on July 10, 2024. (Id., ¶ 4.)

The Court determines that Plaintiffs have provided clear and convincing medical documentation reflecting that Herrera suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of her survival beyond six months. Defendants do not dispute as much.

The Court further determines that the interests of justice will be served by granting preference. Herrera is decedent’s mother (Herrera Decl., ¶ 2) and thus has a significant interest in presenting her claims and testimony before a jury and in any settlement or judgment obtained in this case. While Defendants contend that they will not have enough time to prepare a defense if this motion is granted, they acknowledge that they have served, and received responses to, written discovery (Hatch Decl., ¶¶ 7 and 8), and are in possession of the decedent’s medical records following the accident (See Opp., 2:16-17). Defendants also advise that Parker’s deposition is set for October 4, 2024. (Hatch Decl., ¶ 9.) The Court does not believe Plaintiffs have unnecessarily delayed in filing the instant motion, as Herrera’s condition has declined more significantly in recent months.

Accordingly, the Court grants trial preference. The Trial and Final Status Conference dates will be set at the time of the hearing. 

Case Number: 24PSCV00273    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: H

Hamilton v. Factory Five AT, Inc., et al., Case No. 24PSCV00273

ORDER ON PRO HAC VICE APPLICATION

Defendants Factory Five AT, Inc., Factory Five Holdings Inc., Factory Five Licensing, Inc., Factory Five Racing, Inc., and Factory Five XT, Inc.’s Application for an Order Admitting Randall Weeks to Appear as Counsel Pro Hac Vice is GRANTED, effective upon the filing of proof of payment of the remaining $450.00 owed to the State Bar. 

Background 

Plaintiffs, Keegan Hamilton, Dylan Hamilton and Olivia Hamilton, minors by and through their guardian ad litem, Marni Olsen (“Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

On April 5, 2021, Plaintiffs’ father Jesse Phelan Hamilton (“Decedent”) was riding as a passenger in a 2012 Factory Five Racing GTM Supercar (“subject vehicle”) owned and driven by Robert Adomeit (“Adomeit”) and sold by Factory Five AT, Inc., Factory Five Holdings Inc., Factory Five Licensing, Inc., Factory Five Racing, Inc. and Factory Five XT, Inc. (collectively, “Factory Five Defendants”) when the subject vehicle collided with a sports utility vehicle. Decedent sustained traumatic injuries in the collision and died later that evening. 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Factory Five Defendants, Adomeit, and Does 1-20 for:

1.       Negligence;

2.       Failure to Warn; and

3.       Strict Products Liability. 

A Case Management Conference is set for October 7, 2024.
 

Legal Standard 

“A person who is not a licensee of the State Bar of California but who is an attorney in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States, and who has been retained to appear in a particular cause pending in a court of this state, may in the discretion of such court be permitted upon written application to appear as counsel pro hac vice, provided that an active licensee of the State Bar of California is associated as attorney of record. No person is eligible to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule if the person is:

(1) A resident of the State of California;

(2) Regularly employed in the State of California; or

(3) Regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(a).)

“A person desiring to appear as counsel pro hac vice in a superior court must file with the court a verified application together with proof of service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1013a of a copy of the application and of the notice of hearing of the application on all parties who have appeared in the cause and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office. The notice of hearing must be given at the time prescribed in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 unless the court has prescribed a shorter period.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(c)(1).) 

“The application must state:

(1) The applicant’s residence and office address;

(2) The courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission;

(3) That the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts;

(4) That the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court;

(5) The title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and

(6) The name, address, and telephone number of the active licensee of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(d).) 

“The State Bar of California may set an appropriate fee to be paid by counsel pro hac vice.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(e)[FN1].)

Discussion

Factory Five Defendants move, pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40, for an order allowing the pro hac vice admission of Randall Weeks.

The application complies with the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 9.40, except that counsel has only paid the State Bar a $50.00 fee. (Tabak Decl., ¶ 5.) Accordingly, the Court grants the application, effective upon the filing of proof of payment of the remaining $450.00 owed to the State Bar.


[FN1] The fee is currently $500.00. (See https://www.calbar.ca.gov/Portals.0/documents/rules/Rules_Appendix_ASched-Chgs-Deadlines.pdf)

Case Number: 24PSCV01698    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: H

Flores v. Gomez, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01698

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC’s Demurrer to Complaint is OVERRULED.


Background 

Plaintiff Eliazar Flores (“Plaintiff”) alleges he sustained injuries and damages in a November 15, 2022 motor vehicle collision. 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting motor vehicle and negligence causes of action against Luis Alberto Gomez, Red 1 Express, Inc., Juan Requena Montero (“Montero”), Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC (“Crossroads”), and Does 1-25. 

A Case Management Conference is set for October 7, 2024. Pending before the Court is Crossroads’ demurrer to the complaint.

Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

Discussion

Crossroads demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to Plaintiff’s complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

At the outset, the Court denies Crossroads’ Request for Judicial Notice as to Exhibit 1 (i.e., bill of sale), Exhibit 2 (i.e., lien release), and Exhibit 3 (i.e., “Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC Master Vehicle Lease Agreement”). “The burden is on the party seeking judicial notice to provide sufficient information to allow the court to take judicial notice.” (Ross v. Creel Printing & Publishing Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 736, 744.)

Turning to the merits, Plaintiff alleges in the complaint that at the time of the alleged accident, “Defendant Montero was under the course and scope of employment of Defendant Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC” (Complaint, ¶ GN-1) and that Crossroads “owned and entrusted the motor vehicle operated by Montero” (Id., ¶ MV-2). While Crossroads asserts that it “was never Moreno’s employer” (Demurer, 2:17-18) and that it only leased Montero’s truck and was no longer the lienholder on Montero’s truck on the date of the alleged accident (Id., 2:15-17), these arguments are extraneous to the face of the complaint and are improper for resolution on demurrer.

The demurrer is overruled.
