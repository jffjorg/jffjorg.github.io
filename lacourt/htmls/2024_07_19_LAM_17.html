DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV34846    Hearing Date: July 19, 2024    Dept: 17

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA 

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES 

 

DEPARTMENT 17 

 

JENNIFER A. AYLWARD, an individual, 

 

                        Plaintiff, 

   

         vs. 

 

BRINKER INTERNATIONAL, INC., a Delaware corporation, et al., 

 

                        Defendants. 

 Case No.:   22STCV34846 

 

  

 

 Hearing Date: July 19, 2024

 

 

 

 

Plaintiff’s motion to quash the Annie Ortega Subpoena is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff’s motion to quash the California Pizza Kitchen Subpoena is DENIED.

 

Each of Plaintiff’s following motions to compel is GRANTED:

 

(1) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production (Set No. 2);

 

(2) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set No. 3);

 

(3) Motion to Compel Rosa Vasquez To Produce Documents;

 

(4)  Motion to Compel Courtney Peck To Produce Documents;

 

(5) Motion to Compel Frank Meija To Produce Documents; and

 

(6) Motion to Compel Defendant To Produce A PMQ Witness For Deposition

 

On November 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed suit against Brinker International, Inc., Brinker International Payroll Co. LP, Brinker Restaurant Corporation, and Paul Aoun, alleging:

 

1.    Disability Discrimination in Violation of FEHA; 

2.    Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy; 

3.    Failure to Accommodate Disability in Violation of Government Code § 12940(m); 

4.    Failure to Engage in Interactive Process, California Government Code section 12940(N); 

5.    Retaliation Under FEHA; 

6.    Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment; 

7.    Discriminatory Termination/Interference in Violation of California Family Rights Act, California Government Code § 12945.2; 

8.    Retaliation for Exercising the Right to CFRA Leave Under Government Code § 12945.2; 

9.    Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy Based Upon CFRA 

10.                       Retaliation for Exercising the Right to CFRA Leave Under Government Code § 12945.2; 

11.                       Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 232.5; 

12.                       Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5; 

13.                       Harassment Based on Disability in Violation of FEHA; 

14.                       Declaratory Relief; 

15.                       Injunctive Relief; 

16.                       Failure to Allow Inspection of Personnel File; and 

17.                       Unfair Business Practices. 

 

On 6/27/2024, Plaintiff moved to quash Defendant’s Notice of Deposition and/or Subpoena for Annie Ortega.

 

On 6/27/2024, Plaintiff also moved to quash Defendant’s Subpoena to California Pizza Kitchen.

 

For ease, the Court has consolidated its analysis into a single ruling.

 

Discussion

 

I.                   Motion to Quash (Annie Ortega)

 

            Plaintiff seeks to quash the deposition of Annie Ortega, arguing it is defective because: (1) the Notice of Deposition fails to provide the requisite ten-day notice period); (2) it is unreasonable to issue and serve a Notice of Deposition and a subpoena based on only a few days’ notice, especially given that (a) Brinker has known for almost two months that Plaintiff intended to obtain testimony from the deponent, (b) the deponent is a current or former employee of Brinker, and (c) Brinker has had the deponent’s contact information; and (3) the deponent is a third party and must be served with a deposition subpoena. A copy of the subpoena must have been attached to the Notice of Deposition. (CCP § 2025.240(c).)

 

            In opposition, Defendants argue that “[w]hile Defendants acknowledge the notice was served with less than 10 days’ notice, Defendants did so because (a) the witness was available on this date, (b) the Court recently instructed the parties to work together in good faith on completing depositions even if the dates fell after the discovery cut off; and (c) the 10th day fell on the same date as this Court’s hearing on Defendants’ motions for summary judgment. Despite Plaintiff’s position on witness Ortega’s deposition, Defendants have been working with Plaintiff in good faith to finalize depositions including the deposition of witness Sara Phinney noticed by Plaintiff and scheduled to proceed after the discovery cut-off.” (Opp., 2: 11-18.)

 

            The Court has instructed the parties to cooperate and work in good faith to complete outstanding discovery, including depositions.

 

Ms. Ortega is an important witness as she submitted a declaration in support of Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendants’ motion for summary judgment, and her deposition is necessary due to Plaintiff’s reliance on her statements in support of Plaintiff’s claims.

 

On the evening of 6/21/2024, Defendants learned that Plaintiff had obtained a written declaration from Ms. Ortega when Plaintiff served her opposition papers to Defendants’ motion for summary judgment. (Quiles Nevarez Dec. at ¶8.) On 6/24/2024 (the next business day), Defendants noticed Ms. Ortega’s deposition for 7/3/2024. (Quiles Nevarez Dec. at ¶9.)

 

As such, Defendants’ conduct is not the result of delay or gamesmanship. Moreover, just as Plaintiff continues to seek discovery past the technical cut-off date, Defendants are entitled to seek relevant discovery.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to quash is denied. The deposition of witness Ortega to proceed within 10 days of entry of the Court’s ruling.

 

II.               Motion to Quash (California Pizza Kitchen)

 

Plaintiff seeks to quash the subpoena served on California Pizza Kitchen (CPK) for documents relating to Aylward.

 

The Subpoena seeks Plaintiff’s personnel file from CPK, the job that Plaintiff held, and continues to hold, since her termination by Defendants. More specifically, the Subpoena seeks

 

All employment records pertaining to PLAINTIFF, including, but not limited to, (a) Personnel records, compensation, and pay records; (b) Job descriptions related to PLAINTIFF’s employment; (c) All performance reviews, disciplinary notices, and counseling memos related to PLAINTIFF’s job performance; (d) Employment agreements, or offer letters; (e) Separation letters, or resignation notices.

 

Plaintiff contends that the subpoena is overbroad because it requests all employment records pertaining to Plaintiff, and because it contains specific categories that request documents that are protected by Aylward’s constitutional right to privacy, such as “performance reviews, disciplinary notices, and counseling memos related to [Aylward’s] job performance” (Subpoena ¶ 2(c)), as well as a catchall request for all “personnel records.” (Subpoena ¶ 2(a).)

 

Plaintiff requests that the Subpoena be quashed or modified to only those categories of documents to which Plaintiff does not object. Plaintiff does not object to the following specific categories: documents relating to Aylward’s efforts to obtain employment, her compensation records, the job descriptions relating to her job, employment agreements or offer letters, and any resignation or separation letters.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that the scope of the subpoena is not overbroad, and that the scope of documents are relevant to showing whether or not Plaintiff has a history of ongoing performance problems with meeting or exceeding restaurant sales, profit, and other KPI goals. (Nevarez Decl. ¶ 3). Additionally, Plaintiff’s subsequent employment records will confirm whether she has suffered the alleged loss of earnings damages she now claims and how she explained her separation from employment with Defendants and whether her explanation is consistent with the allegations in this lawsuit. (Nevarez Decl. ¶ 4)

 

After review, the Court agrees with Defendants that the documents sought are relevant, and the request is appropriately tailored. Plaintiff’s privacy interests in her own employment records are diminished because Plaintiff placed her employment history at issue by filing this litigation. Specifically, Plaintiff claims that she was not terminated for cause (poor performance) but rather because of her alleged disability. As such, the relevance of her performance records outweighs the intrusion into Plaintiff’s privacy. (See e.g. Board of Trustees v. Superior Court (1981) 119 Cal.App.3d 516, 526-27.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to quash the CPK Subpoena is denied.

            No such notice was filed. Rather, the only filing was a 7/17/2024 reply from Plaintiff indicating that all motions remained on calendar, and indicating that Defendants had, on July 8, 2024, asked Plaintiff to stipulate that their opposition could be filed on July 16, 2024. As noted by Plaintiff, this offer made little sense as it would leave only two days before the hearing, and virtually no time for Plaintiff to respond to Defendant’s opposition (nor would it provide this Court any reasonable opportunity to review the moving papers).

 

Defendants did not respond, and did not thereafter file an opposition to any of these motions.

 

As such, in addition to failing to oppose any of these motions, the evidence also indicates that they made no effort to engage in meet and confer to resolve any or all of these motions. Plaintiff’s reply sets forth multiple emails sent to Defendants counsel to address the relevant issues here, and those emails were ignored.  As such sanctions are warranted.
