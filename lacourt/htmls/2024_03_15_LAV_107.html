DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00082    Hearing Date: March 15, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Melania Almonte, et al.,

     

         Plaintiffs,

 

   v.

 

April Moore, as trustee of the Panorama City Separate Property Trust, et al.,

          Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV00082            107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING:

Motion to Compel

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

Granted as to appearance of Plaintiffs at depositions (but not as to documents of which production is sought). Granted as to sanctions against Plaintiffs and Plaintiff’s counsel in the requested amount of $575.00.

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Melania Almonte (“Almonte”) and Gloria Gonzales Almonte (“Gonzales Almonte” and, together with Almonte, “Plaintiffs”) have lived at an apartment since 2002 that is owned or managed by defendants April Moore, as trustee of the Panorama City Separate Property Trust, (“Moore”) and Gregory Mizen, as trustee of the Panorama City Separate Property Trust, (“Mizen” and, together with Moore, “Defendants” and Defendants, together with Plaintiffs, “Parties”). (Complaint, p. 2.) Plaintiffs have incurred injuries “as a result of the uninhabitable conditions existing at the building.” (Ibid.)

 

On January 19, 2022, Plaintiffs initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of the warranty of habitability, (2) negligent maintenance of premises, (3) nuisance, (4) breach of quiet enjoyment, and (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On January 29 or 31, 2024, Defendants served notices of depositions on Plaintiffs seeking to take both Plaintiffs’ depositions on February 16, 2024. (Motion p. 3, Exh. A; Opposition p. 4.) On February 16, 2024, neither Plaintiff appeared for the deposition and certificates of non-appearance were taken. (Id. at p. 3.) Because trial is set for April 4, 2024, the discovery cut-off date was March 5, 2024. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2024.020, subd. (a) [“[A]ny party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day… before the date initially set for the trial of the action.”].)

 

On February 21, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to compel discovery (the “Motion”). On March 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) to the Motion, and on March 8, 2024, Defendants replied to the Opposition.

 

II. PARTIES’ POSITIONS

 

  1. The Motion

 

Defendants contend that they are entitled to a court order compelling Plaintiffs’ attendance at a deposition. (Motion pp. 3–4.) They argue that Plaintiffs’ counsel “alleged he had another trial mere days before the duly noticed depositions” and “has categorically refused to provide dates of his clients’ deposition availability despite the looming trial date.” (Id. at p. 4, emphasis original.) Defendants further argue that sanctions are therefore warranted in the amount of their attorney’s fees and costs associated with bringing the Motion (specifically, $575.00). (Ibid.)

 

  1. The Opposition

 

Plaintiffs contend that the Motion is jurisdictionally defective because Defendants “failed to file the required separate statement setting forth the reason why depositions should be compelled since the request included a document production.” (Opposition p. 4.) Plaintiffs argue that their counsel informed Defendants’ counsel that they would be unable to attend the deposition on the set date because Plaintiffs’ counsel was engaged by a trial in another case. (Id. at pp. 4–5.) “At all times, Plaintiff’s counsel made every attempt to work with the Defendant’s attorney to avoid motion practice.” (Id. at p. 5.) “Plaintiff’s counsel made [two] attempts to meet and confer with the defendant’s counsel regarding the deposition and even agreed to produce the Plaintiffs for shorter notice.” (Ibid.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendants do not include a meet and confer statement with the Motion and “Defendant[s] did not advance any justifiable basis for proceeding with the deposition or for filing this motion even after [being told that Plaintiffs’ counsel was busy in trial].” (Id. at p. 6.) “Defendants do not set forth good cause for the production of any documents.” (Ibid.) “The motion was served by e-mail and/or fax on 12/21/17 [sic], providing only 13 court days’ notice.” (Id. at p. 8.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendants did not act with “civility and professionalism” in bringing the Motion. (Id. at p. 10.) Because the Motion was “entirely unjustified,” Plaintiffs seek sanctions against Defendants in the amount of their attorney’s fees and costs associated with opposing the Motion (specifically, $6,924.25). (Id. at pp. 9–10.)

 

  1. The Reply

 

Defendants begin by stating that “[d]espite repeated efforts by Defense counsel to obtain deposition dates of both plaintiffs, and even after having filed this instant motion, Plaintiffs’ counsel has still not provided dates of his clients’ availability.” (Reply p. 2, emphasis original.) Then, after calling the Opposition “excessively long,” Defendants allege that “Plaintiffs’ counsel spent more time drafting an Opposition than simply meeting and conferring with Defense counsel to provide dates.” (Ibid.) Defendants explain that no separate statement is needed in this case and argue that “Plaintiffs’ counsel failed timely object to the deposition notices.” (Ibid.) Defendants contend that their counsel made reasonable efforts to meet and confer. (Id. at p. 4.) “Defense counsel merely desires to take the deposition of both plaintiffs prior to trial. Defendants are so entitled and merely seek dates that plaintiffs are available for deposition.” (Ibid.)

 

III. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Separate Statement

 

Rule 3.1345, subdivision (a) of the California Rules of Court states, “Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request must be accompanied by a separate statement. The motions that require a separate statement include a motion… [t]o compel answers at a deposition.” Rule 3.1345, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court states, “A separate statement is not required under the following circumstances: (1) When no response has been provided to the request for discovery.”

 

The Motion does not seek to compel answers at a deposition, but rather seeks to compel Plaintiffs’ appearances at the deposition. Plaintiffs’ non-appearances at the scheduled depositions makes this a case where no response has been provided to the request for discovery. Thus, no separate statement was required to be filed with the Motion.

 

  1. Meet and Confer Efforts

 

No “meet and confer” is required to compel initial deposition attendance, but there must be “a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).) "Implicit in the requirement that counsel contact the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance is a requirement that counsel listen to the reasons offered and make a good faith attempt to resolve the issue," including by rescheduling.  (Leko v. Cornerstone Bldg. Inspection Serv. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1109, 1124; see also L.A.S.C.L.R. 3.26 (“[G]uidelines adopted by the Los Angeles County Bar Association are adopted as civility in litigation recommendations.”).)

 

Defendants attach to the Motion a declaration by their counsel that states he “diligently attempted to obtain the dates to take plaintiff’s deposition before and after the deposition dates, but plaintiffs’ counsel simply did not provide them.” (Khurin Decl. ¶ 6.) Defendants’ counsel attaches copies of the correspondences between Parties’ counsel as exhibits to his declaration, though we note that none of the attached correspondences take place after the deposition dates. (Khurin Decl. Exh. C.) Nonetheless, on the strength of the Khurin Declaration, we are satisfied that this requirement has been met in good faith.

 

  1. Filing Fees

 

Defendants filed a single motion for what should have been two separate motions. Combining discovery motions allows the moving party to avoid paying the requisite filing fees. “But while it is mandatory for the court clerks to demand and receive statutorily required filing fees, it is not… a jurisdictional defect if the precise fee is not collected.” (Duran v. St. Luke’s Hospital (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 457, 460.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants are ordered to pay an additional filing fee.  

 

IV. ANALYSIS

 

  1. Judicial Notice

 

“Judicial¿notice¿is the recognition and acceptance by the court, for use by the trier of fact or by the court, of the existence of a matter of law or fact that is relevant to an issue in the action without requiring formal proof of the matter.” (Unruh-Haxton v. Regents of University of California¿(2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 343, 364.) “Judicial¿notice¿may not be taken of any matter unless authorized or required by law.” (Cal. Evid. C. §¿450.) Matters subject to¿judicial¿notice¿are listed in Cal. Evid. Code §§¿451–452. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp.¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113.) Judicial notice may be taken of the “[r]ecords of… any court of this state.” (Cal. Evid. Code § 452, subd. (d).) Further, judicial notice shall be taken of any matter in Cal. Evid. Code § 452 requested by a party if the adverse party is given sufficient notice and the court is furnished with sufficient information to enable it to take judicial notice of the matter. (Cal. Evid. Code § 453.)

 

Plaintiffs request in the Opposition that this court take judicial notice of another lawsuit “and its docket,” apparently as proof that Plaintiffs’ counsel was engaged in a jury trial in the week before the scheduled depositions. (Opposition p. 2.) In this instance, first, we note the questionable relevance of the other lawsuit and its docket, and second, we question whether, given that judicial notice was requested with a single sentence in an opposition and that a copy thereof was not provided with the Opposition, whether the adverse party has been given sufficient notice of this request.

 

Accordingly, we decline to take judicial notice of the other lawsuit or its docket.

 

  1. Depositions

 

A motion to compel deposition attendance and testimony or document production may be brought when a party fails to appear for a deposition following service of a deposition notice without having served a valid objection under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.410. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.410, subd. (a).) Any such motion that seeks document production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production,” and if production of electronically stored information is sought, the burden falls on the party opposing the motion to show that “the information is from a source that is not reasonably accessible because of the undue burden or expense.” (Id. at § 2025.410, subd. (b)–(c).)

 

Defendants properly bring the Motion under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.410, subd. (a) because the Motion follows Plaintiffs’ failure to appear for their depositions, which followed service of the deposition notices without either deponent having served any valid objections under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.410. Both deposition notices seek document production along with oral deposition testimony, but Defendants do not include any specific facts or argument showing good cause to justify such production. We observe that Defendants do not argue for production of the requested documents in the Motion or the Reply but emphasize that they merely “desire[] to take the deposition of both plaintiffs prior to trial.” (Reply p. 4.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request to compel the appearances of Plaintiffs at depositions as sought in the Motion is GRANTED but Plaintiffs need not produce any of the requested documents at their depositions.

 

\\

  1. Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory in favor of the moving party and against the deponent, or the party affiliated with the deponent or both, whose failure to appear at the deposition necessitated the motion to compel the deposition “unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [The court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.].) “If the party seeking a monetary sanction meets its burden of proof, the burden shifts to the opposing party attempting to avoid a monetary sanction to show that it acted with ‘substantial justification.’” (Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1436.) “‘[S]ubstantial justification’ as used in the above statutes means a justification that is ‘well-grounded in both law and fact.’” (Diepenbrock v. Brown (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 743, 747.) Behavior during discovery that is intended “for purposes of delay or harassment, or [is] otherwise unreasonable,” for example, would point against a finding of substantial justification. (Union Mut. Life Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1978) 80 Cal.App.3d 1, 15.) “As to the amount of sanctions, Cal. C. Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing ‘the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result’ of a party's misuse of the discovery process. (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.)

 

Here, since Defendants prevailed on the Motion and sanctions are mandatory, the burden shifts to the Plaintiffs to show they acted with substantial justification in failing to produce the deponents for their noticed depositions. Plaintiffs argue that they failed to respond to the deposition notices more than four days before they were set because their counsel was in jury trial, which “fact was made known to the Defendant’s counsel on 2/12/2024 and thereafter 2/14/2024, that Plaintiff’s counsel was still in trial and that the depositions previously set would not move forward.” (Opposition p. 2.) We remind Plaintiffs’ counsel of his obligation to litigate each of his cases with equal diligence and note that being busy is not an adequate excuse to avoid discovery sanctions. (See Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 269.) Parties exchanged communications about the depositions’ scheduling prior to the noticed dates and Plaintiffs had ample opportunity to reschedule or postpone the depositions but failed to do so. Therefore, we find that Defendants did not act with substantial justification in failing to produce the deponents for their noticed depositions.

 

Defendants seek $575.00, which they allege “represents the reasonable cost incurred by Defendants in bringing this motion.” (Motion p. 5.) Defendants fail to attach a declaration attesting to those costs. However, we note that the Motion is signed by Defendants’ counsel, Mr. Arthur Khurin, and that by signing the Motion, Defendants’ counsel thereby attested to its contents. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 128.7, subd. (b) [“By presenting to the court, whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating, a pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met… (3) The allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support.”].) We find the requested amount of sanctions, $575.00, to be a reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred in the filing of the Motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request for sanctions against Plaintiffs is GRANTED in the amount of $575.00

COURT’S RULING RE:

Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

According to the Complaint, on November 29, 2021, plaintiff Ira Mandel (“Plaintiff”) purchased a certain used vehicle (the “Vehicle”) from defendant Haute, LLC d/b/a Vegas Auto Gallery Lotus Cars Las Vegas (“Defendant”). (Complaint p. 2; Motion p. 3.) Plaintiff asserts that he later learned that the Vehicle’s odometer had been “rolled back” so that it showed significantly lower mileage than it actually possessed. (Id. at p. 3.) According to the Plaintiff, Plaintiff notified Defendant of the odometer “roll back” and demanded that Defendant rescind the purchase agreement, but Defendant refused. (Ibid.)

 

On November 28, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) intentional misrepresentation, (2) negligent misrepresentation, (3) violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq, and (4) violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act.

 

On February 15, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to quash service of summons (the “Motion”). On February 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Motion (the “Opposition”), and on March 7, 2024, Defendant replied to the Opposition.

 

II.              PARTIES’ ARGUMENTS

 

A.    The Motion

 

Defendant moves to quash the service of summons on the grounds that this court lacks personal jurisdiction over him.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff “must prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, the factual basis justifying the exercise of the jurisdiction.” (Motion p. 5.) Defendant contends that as a resident of Nevada who does business in Nevada, this court does not have general personal jurisdiction over him. (Id. at p. 6.) Defendant further contends that this court additionally lacks specific personal jurisdiction over him because he has not availed himself of any benefits of the forum, it offends traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice, and it would be a hardship for Defendant to have to travel to California multiple times for this lawsuit. (Id. at pp. 6–7.)

 

B.     The Opposition

 

Plaintiff rebuts that Defendant is subject to specific personal jurisdiction of this court because he purposefully and voluntarily directed his activities toward the forum. (Opposition p. 3.) Plaintiff observes that “purposeful availment” exists even as to acts done outside the state so long as “the act is done with the intention of causing effect in California or with a reasonable foreseeability that it would cause an effect in California.” (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Plaintiff argues that fraudulently tampering with an odometer was directed at California because it violated a specific state regulation. (Id. at p. 5.) Plaintiff further argues that Defendant availed himself to the benefits of the forum when he “engage[d] in affirmative conduct to deliver its product into the forum state, and d[id] so voluntarily and for its own financial benefit.” (Ibid.) Finally, Plaintiff argues that the sale was only completed when the Vehicle was delivered to him in California at Defendant’s expense, and that the sale therefore took place in California. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff contends that his claims are “eminently related to Defendant’s contacts with California” because “shipping a car with a rolled back odometer into the state could result in unsuspecting buyers purchasing vehicles at inflated prices based on false mileage readings,” the car may not meet California’s stringent emissions standards, the fraud could affect California’s “tax revenue that would have been generated from vehicles accurately reflecting their mileage,” and committing fraud, as a criminal offense, could burden California’s judicial system. (Id. at pp. 6–7.)

 

Plaintiff additionally argues that traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice are not offended when considering the relative financial abilities of the parties, who are an individual plaintiff and a corporate defendant, all material witnesses are in California, the relevant acts or incidents all took place in California, and Defendant “fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its assertion that the exercise of jurisdiction in California would not comport with fair play and substantial justice” in the Motion. (Id. at pp. 7–8.)

 

C.    The Reply

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff has not met his initial burden of showing that this court has personal jurisdiction over him because Plaintiff, as evidence, only attaches a declaration of Plaintiff. (Reply p. 2.) “Here, the defendant’s relationship to a state is primarily ‘legalistic.’” (Id. at p. 3.) Defendant argues that the law Plaintiff cited (a portion of the Song-Beverly Act) for his proposition that the sale was completed in California is inapplicable to this case and, in fact, the sale was completed in Nevada. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) “Further, plaintiff’s claim in his declaration about an agreement on shipment is barred by the integration clause of the sales agreement” and “confirms defendant’s position that the vehicle purchase agreement did not require for defendant to deliver the vehicle to defendant at a destination.” (Id. at p. 5.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff submits no evidence that Defendant “rolled back” the Vehicle’s odometer or knew that the Vehicle “was to be strictly used in California.” (Id. at p. 6.) Defendant repeats his arguments from the Motion, then concludes, “because of defendant’s lack of contacts with California as a whole, it could not reasonably be expected to be subject to California’s jurisdiction.” (Id. at p. 10.)

 

III.            ANALYSIS

 

Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 418.10, “[a] defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion…to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)

 

When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) The plaintiff must establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence. (Aquila, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568.) “When a nonresident defendant challenges personal jurisdiction the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that all necessary jurisdictional criteria are met. This burden must be met by competent evidence in affidavits and authenticated documentary evidence. An unverified complaint may not be considered as an affidavit supplying necessary facts.” (Jewish Defense Organization, Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1054–55, internal citations omitted.)

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure Section 410.10, California courts may exercise jurisdiction over nonresidents on any basis consistent with the Constitution of California or of the United States. “The exercise of jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant comports with these Constitutions if the defendant has such minimum contacts with the state that the assertion of jurisdiction does not violate traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” (Pavlovich v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 268, internal quotation marks omitted.) “If the nonresident defendant does not have substantial and systematic contacts in the forum sufficient to establish general jurisdiction, he or she still may be subject to the specific jurisdiction of the forum, if the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits, and the controversy is related to or arises out of a defendant's contacts with the forum.” (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446, internal citations and quotation marks omitted.) “When determining whether specific jurisdiction exists, courts consider the relationship among the defendant, the forum, and the litigation. A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: (1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant's contacts with the forum; and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.” (Pavlovich v. Superior Court, supra, at 269, internal citations and quotation marks omitted.) “The purposeful availment inquiry focuses on the defendant's intentionality.” (Ibid.) “[M]erely asserting that a defendant knew or should have known that his intentional acts would cause harm in the forum state is not enough to establish jurisdiction under the effects test.” (Id. at 270–71.)

 

As the court in Moncrief v. Clark, 238 Cal. App. 4th 1000, 1008, explained: “If a defendant has purposely availed himself of the benefits of the forum state, and the controversy is related to the defendant's contacts with the forum, the defendant will be subject to specific jurisdiction if it would be fair. (See, e.g., Snowney, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 1070.) ‘In making this determination, the ‘court “must consider the burden on the defendant, the interests of the forum State, and the plaintiff's interest in obtaining relief. It must also weigh in its determination ‘the interstate judicial system's interest in obtaining the most efficient resolution of controversies; and the shared interest of the several States in furthering fundamental substantive social policies.”’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.) However, where a defendant who “‘purposefully has directed [his or her] activities at forum residents seeks to defeat jurisdiction, [he or she] must present a compelling case that the presence of some other considerations would render jurisdiction unreasonable.’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.)

 

 “Vehicle Code section 5901, subdivision (d) provides a ‘sale’ is deemed completed when the purchaser of a vehicle has paid the purchase price or signed a purchase contract or security agreement and taken physical possession or delivery of the vehicle.” (Durbin v. Fletcher (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 334, 343.) “It has long been established [in California] that a transfer of the property interest in a motor vehicle is effective as between the immediate parties even though they have not complied with the registration statute.” (Brasher’s Cascade Auto Auction v. Valley Auto Sales & Leasing (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1063. In Davis v. Newmar Corp. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 275, 278 (hereafter, Davis), a vehicle sale that was negotiated by the consumer while in California but which provided in the sale agreement for possession of the vehicle to be transferred out of state was found to have taken place outside of California because title to the vehicle passed upon delivery. By contrast, in Gusse v. Damon Corp. (2007) 470 F.Supp.2d 1110, 1112–14 (hereafter, Gusse), the agreement for the sale of a vehicle was negotiated and signed in California and did not require the seller to make delivery outside the state. Still in California, parties signed a number of forms, including a DMV form that provided the sale, transfer, and delivery of the vehicle had taken place on that day, and the buyer was given keys to the vehicle and allowed to load his belongings into it. Subsequently, still in California, the buyer signed a DMV form that indicated that the sale had taken place out of state and a California Board of Equalization form indicating the same. Buyer then directed the seller to deliver the vehicle to him out of state. In this case, the court found that the sale had taken place in California. The Gusse court found that “the contract is one which required or authorized [the seller] to transport the [vehicle], but did not require delivery at the destination. Accordingly, title passed to [the buyer] at the time and place of shipment.”

 

Plaintiffs dispute whether the sale of the Vehicle from Defendant to Plaintiff was completed in Nevada or California. Defendant contends that this case is most akin to Gusse because the sale agreement in this case does not require delivery to be made to California. However, a number of facts distinguish Gusse from the instant case: namely, in this case, parties did not sign any forms agreeing that the sale was completed in the origin state and Plaintiff was not given control of the Vehicle (via possession of keys, storage of belongings therein, and power to direct third-party contractor where to deliver the Vehicle). Rather, in Gusse, the buyer unilaterally attempted to class the vehicle sale as occurring out of state afterwards despite the apparent intent of the seller that the sale take place on the day the agreement was signed in California. The case in Davis, on the other hand, is much more similar to the instant case. Davis mentions the matter of delivery being a term of the sale agreement but does not base its holding on this fact. Rather, Davis declares its holding to be based on the fact that title passed in the state in which delivery occurred, citing California State Electronics Assn. v. Zeos Internat. Ltd. (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1276–77, which stands for the same proposition. Thus, this court finds that Defendant did not surrender possession of the Vehicle until it was delivered to Plaintiff in California, and thus, that the sale was completed in California.

 

By finding that Defendant remained in possession of the Vehicle when he directed it to be sent into the state and delivered to Plaintiff in California, we may further find that Defendant thereby purposefully availed himself of the benefits of doing business in California with California residents and that the controversy at issue arises out of Defendant’s contacts with the state since the sale at issue took place in California and the controversy here arises out of that sale.

 

Finally, we are persuaded by Plaintiff’s arguments that it does not offend traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice to hear this case in California. Rather, it is in the interest of economy to have this case be heard in California given that most if not all material witnesses are located here, and so the net burden of travel is reduced.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion to Quash.
