[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR SUBSTITUTION 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Jesse Lauricella (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Firefly Fabrications, Justin Campbell, and Does 1 through 20 for allegations of a failure to timely make repairs and upgrades to a boat owned by Plaintiff pursuant to a contractual agreement. 

Plaintiff’s counsel has now filed this motion seeking Matthew Thompson (“Thompson”) to be substituted as Plaintiff’s personal representative because of Plaintiff’s passing. 

The motion was initially heard on July 9, 2024. The Court noted there was no declaration by Thompson to satisfy the requirements of Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32. Plaintiff was ordered to file a supplemental declaration curing the noted defect no later than eight court days prior to the continued September 9, 2024 hearing. 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff failed to abide by the Court’s July 9, 2024 Order. Instead of filing the supplemental declaration at least eight court days prior to the hearing, Plaintiff filed the supplemental declaration the day of the hearing. The motion was continued to December 27, 2024. 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

The person who seeks to commence or continue a pending action as the decedent’s successor-in-interest shall execute and file an affidavit or declaration stating: (1) the decedent’s name, (2) the date and place of decedent’s death, (3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate,” (4) a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor-in-interest, if the decedent’s estate was administered, (5) either the affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest or the affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest, with facts in support thereof, (6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding,” and (7) the statements are true, under penalty of perjury.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32(a)(1)-(7).) Additionally, a certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32(c).) 

The Court has reviewed the supplemental declaration filed on September 9, 2024. Thompson’s executed declaration satisfies the requirements of CCP section 377.32. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the motion is to substitute Plaintiff is GRANTED. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Kevin Voong (“Plaintiff”) initiated this legal malpractice action against defendants Dawn M. Coulson, Esq., EPPS & Coulson, LLP, and Does 1 through 10 arising from defendants’ representation of Plaintiff for collection of a judgment. The complaint sets forth two causes of action for legal malpractice/professional negligence and breach of fiduciary duty. 

On July 30, 2024, defendants Dawn M. Coulson, Esq. and EPPS & Coulson, LLP (collectively “Defendants”) filed a motion to compel Plaintiff to respond to special interrogatories, set one, and a request for monetary sanctions. 

On October 22, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to continue trial in order for their motion for summary judgment (“MSJ”) be heard at least 30 days prior to trial. Trial is currently set for January 21, 2025. 

The motions were initially set to be heard on December 2, 2024 and December 3, 2024. Due to the unavailability of the Court, the motions were continued to December 27, 2024. (Min. Order, Nov. 21, 2024.) 

As of December 13, 2024, no opposition to either of the two motions have been filed. 

 

II. MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL 

  1. Legal Standard 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits. (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).) The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance. (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).) The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial.¿(See generally, CRC Rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).) 

Additional factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application. (CRC Rule 3.1332(c), (d).) 

  1. Discussion 

Here, there is good cause to continue the trial date. A MSJ shall be heard no later than 30 days before the trial date, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise. (CCP §437c(a)(3).) Defendants timely filed their MSJ on October 4, 2024, but it is set to be heard on January 8, 2025, which is less than 30 days prior to the current trial date. The trial court cannot refuse to hear a summary judgment motion filed within the time limits of CCP § 437c. (Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 918. 919.) Further, Defendants filed this motion to continue trial on October 22, 2024, which is three months before the current trial date as opposed to waiting until the eve of trial. There are no alternative means identified to address these issues, and there is otherwise no prejudice shown to any party if trial is continued, especially as no parties oppose the motion. 

While the Court will briefly continue the trial date to accommodate the MSJ, the Court denies Defendants’ additional request to continue all discovery related dates. The stated purpose of Defendants’ motion is so that its MSJ may be heard at least 30 days prior to trial pursuant to CCP §437c(a)(3). Defendants have not articulated any specific and necessary discovery yet uncompleted to justify the continuance of all discovery-related dates. Discovery and motion cut-off dates shall remain based on the January 21, 2025 trial date pursuant to the Court’s prior order. (Min. Order, June 3, 2024.) 

  1. Conclusion & Order 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion to continue trial is GRANTED.¿ 

The January 21, 2025 trial date is continued to March 10, 2025 at 9:30 a.m. at 8:30 a.m. in Department 78 of the Stanley Mosk Courthouse.¿The January 8, 2025 Final Status Conference is continued to  February 25, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. in Department 78 of the Stanley Mosk Courthouse. All discovery and motion cutoff dates shall remain based on the January 21, 2025 trial date. 

 

III. MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL DISCOVERY 

  1. Legal Standard 

For a motion to compel initial discovery responses, all a propounding party must show is that it properly served its discovery requests, that the time to respond has expired, and that the party to whom the requests were directed failed to provide a timely response. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 906.) Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may compel responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including those based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).) 

  1. Discussion 

Defendants propounded special interrogatories, set one, on Plaintiff on March 18, 2024. (Schneider Decl. ¶ 4; Exh. A.) Responses were due on or before April 19, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 5.) Defendants granted an extension to Plaintiff to respond to all discovery by May 15, 2024. (Ibid.) Plaintiff served responses to the other written discovery, but did not serve any responses to the special interrogatories, set one. (Ibid.) To date, Plaintiff has not served responses to the special interrogatories, set one. (Id. ¶ 7.) Defendants therefore seek an order compelling Plaintiff to respond, without objections, to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions. 

Plaintiff did not file any opposition to the motion demonstrating that discovery responses have been served. A failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion. (CRC, Rule 8.54(c).) Therefore, the unopposed motion -is granted. 

Defendants seek $1,235.00 in monetary sanctions for the motion. Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response, unless a court makes certain findings.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).) Plaintiff did not file any opposition. However, sanctions may be awarded, even though no opposition was filed, pursuant to CRC, Rule 3.1348(a).  

A court has discretion to award sanctions that are “suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks” but they should not be punitive in nature or levied for the purposes of punishing an offending party. (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.)¿This is a simple and unopposed motion. The Court awards Defendants one hour to draft the motion and one hour to appear at the hearing at the requested and reasonable rate of $235.00 per hour, for a total of $470.00 as attorney fees. Further, Defendants are awarded the motion filing fee of $60.00 as costs. 

  1. Conclusion & Order 

Defendants’ unopposed motion is therefore GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses to Defendants’ special interrogatories, set one, without objections, within fifteen (15) days. (CCP § 2030.290(a),(b).) 

Sanctions are imposed against Plaintiff and/or his counsel of record, jointly and severally. Plaintiff and/or his counsel are ordered to pay sanctions to Defendants, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $530.00, within thirty (30) days.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES  

 

I. BACKGROUND 

On January 10, 2023, plaintiffs Isaiah Lomeli Barrios and Brenda Alvarez (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed this action under the Song-Beverly Warranty act against defendant Nissan North America, Inc. (“Defendant”). 

On April 16, 2024, counsel advised the Court that the case had been settled. A notice of settlement was ordered to be filed by April 30, 2024. (Min. Order, April 16, 2024.) 

On August 23, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel stated settlement was not complete and that a Motion for Attorney Fees would be filed. The Court ordered counsel to file the motion by or before August 30, 2024. (Min. Order, Aug. 23, 2024.) 

On September 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Motion for Attorney Fees. 

On December 13, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. 

On December 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

Attorneys’ fees are allowed as costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc, § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10)(B).) In a lemon law action, costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees, may be recovered by a prevailing buyer under the Song-Beverly Act. (See Civ. Code, § 1794(d).) 

Section 1794 provides: “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794.) 

Thus, the statute includes a “reasonable attorney’s fees” standard.  The attorney bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(c)(5).) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.) “Testimony of an attorney as to the number of hours worked on a particular case is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.” (Ibid.) 

A plaintiff’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred. (See Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.” (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488, quoting Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys., Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Ass’n (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.) The Court has discretion to reduce fees that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time. (Horsford v. Bd. of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal. App. 4th 359, 395.) 

In determining a reasonable attorney fee, the trial court begins with the lodestar, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 36.)  The lodestar may then be adjusted based on factors specific to the case in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided. (Ibid.) These facts include (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. (Ibid.) 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

It is undisputed that Plaintiffs are the prevailing party in this action, and are therefore entitled to attorney fees, costs, and expenses pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code 1794(d). Defendant’s first contention is that the motion should be denied because Plaintiffs failed to file the motion by August 30, 2024, pursuant to the Court’s August 23, 2024 Order. In reply, Plaintiffs argue the late filing was based on a mistake since the parties stipulated in their joint status report that the fee motion would be filed by early September 2024, and that Defendant has not demonstrated that any late filing caused surprise or undue prejudice.  

Here, the joint Status Report filed on August 20, 2024 reported that Plaintiffs would file the fee motion by early September 2024. Thereafter, the Hon. Michelle Kim ordered the parties to file the motion by August 30, 2024. Plaintiffs failed to abide by the Court’s later order, which takes precedence over the representations in the Status Report. Nonetheless, there is no basis to deny the motion in its entirety based on the late filing, as Defendant was able to fully oppose the motion, and there is no other apparent prejudice caused by Plaintiffs’ delayed filing. In the interests of justice, the Court will consider the motion on the merits notwithstanding Plaintiffs’ failure to follow the Court’s order. 

Plaintiffs move for an award of attorney fees and costs in the amount of $48,294.83, consisting of (1) $42,900.00 in attorney fees, (2) $1,104.83 in costs, and (3) a 1.1 multiplier enhancement on in the amount of $4,290.00. 

A. Reasonableness of Fees 

i. Reasonable Hourly Rate 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) 

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees for 2 different attorneys: (1) Isaac Kohen (“Kohen”) at $525.00/hour and (2) Tamara R. Imber (“Imber”) at $325.00/hour. Kohen attests that since he was admitted to practice in 2012, he has focused his practice primarily on CLRA and lemon law litigation, and has litigated approximately six hundred lemon law and CLRA cases for over a decade. (Kohen Decl. ¶¶ 16-17.) Kohen attests that Imber was admitted to practice law in 2021 and works as an associate at Law Offices of Isaac Kohen. (Kohen Decl. ¶ 19.) In opposition, Defendant argues that the hourly rate sought by Plaintiffs’ counsel is excessive, unsupported, and unreasonable. Defendants contend Kohen’s rate should be reduced to $400.00 per hour and Imbar’s to $265.00 per hour. In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Kohen’s rate is supported by his thirteen years of practice and multiple prior awards of his hourly rate of at least $500 and $525 for lemon law cases. 

Based on the Court’s review of the rates and Defendant’s lack of specificity as to why Kohen’s rate should be reduced to $400.00, the Court finds that Plaintiffs’ requested hourly rate for Kohen to be reasonable for attorneys with the experience stated in the his declaration. However, the Court finds Kohen’s attestation of Imber’s rate and experience to be lacking. Kohen merely provides that Imbar was admitted into practice three years ago, does not state when she began practicing lemon law, and provides in a conclusory manner that she had been approved in another department at an hourly rate of $285.00 (and attaches an exhibit that appears to support that she was approved in another department at an hourly rate of $295.00). Additionally, Plaintiffs’ reply does not provide any additional information, nor does it address Defendant’s contention challenging Imbar’s rate. Nevertheless, Defendant is also unspecific as to why Imber’s rate should be reduced to $265.00, or how Defendant arrived at that rate. 

As such, the Court finds Imbar’s $325.00 per hour rate to be less well-supported and reduces the rate to $285.00 per hour, the amount to which was attested and at least partially supported by the evidence. 

ii. Reasonable Hours Incurred 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) A reduced award might be fully justified by a general observation that an attorney overlitigated a case or submitted a padded bill or that the opposing party has stated valid objections. (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 101.) 

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees for 65 hours spent by Kohen and 27 hours spent by Imbar litigating this matter for a total of 92 hours. (Kohen Decl. Ex. 1.) A plaintiff’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred. (See Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.)  

Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ counsel block billed, and that the Court should reduce Plaintiffs’ block billing hours by half for a reduction of 15.9 hours. The Court has reviewed the specific entries provided in Exhibit A to Doudar’s Declaration. The Court finds that most of the entries are sufficiently delineated, such that the tasks do not make it difficult to determine the reasonableness of the bill, especially as the vast majority billed for a small fraction of an hour.  The Court also finds no reason to reduce billing for “administrative” tasks when it appears that they were necessary to the litigation, and the amount of time billed appears reasonable as it ranges mostly around 0.1 hours to 0.5 hours at the highest. Further, Defendant has not met its burden that the time spent to review documents, conduct research, and draft motions was excessive based on the argument that initial discovery and fee motions are templates that Plaintiffs’ counsel uses in every case. Even if Plaintiffs’ counsel made use of templates from prior cases to assist in drafting discovery and motions, the Court does not find that this justifies cutting the amount billed to half, especially when the particular entries challenged by Defendant do not appear unreasonable on its face.  

However, the Court will reduce the amount of anticipated time to review and draft a reply to the motion, especially as the amount of estimated time for these entries provide that the time “will vary depending upon the complexity of the opposition brief” or notes that the estimated time is contingent on whether it is “necessary.” Plaintiffs’ reply brief does not address how much actual time was spent in reviewing the opposition and drafting the reply, nor do Plaintiffs provide whether a memorandum of costs was ever drafted or necessary. Further, the estimated time for “Misc. tasks to complete case, draft RFD and close file” is speculative. The Court reduces the hours sought for those “estimated” times from 6 hours to 3 hours total, which represents 2 hours to review the opposition and reply collectively, and 1 hour to attend the hearing on the fee motion at Kohen’s billing rate of $525.00 per hour. 

Thus, of the $42,900.00 in attorney fees requested by Law Offices of Isaac Kohen, the Court reduces the amount to $40,245.00 (comprised of Kohen’s reduced 62 hours billed at a $525/hour rate & Imbar’s 27 hours billed at the reduced $285/hour rate).  

C. Multiplier 

While the lodestar reflects the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community, it may be adjusted based on various factors, including “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill displayed in presenting them; (2) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys; (3) the contingent nature of the fee award” and (4) the success achieved. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.) 

Nonetheless, the court must not consider extraordinary skill and the other Serrano factors to the extent these are already included with the lodestar. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal. 4th 1122, 1138-1139.) “[A] trial court should award a multiplier for exceptional representation only when the quality of representation far exceeds the quality of representation that would have been provided by an attorney of comparable skill and experience billing at the hourly rate used in the lodestar calculation. Otherwise, the fee award will result in unfair double counting and be unreasonable.” (Id. at 1139.) 

Plaintiffs request a lodestar multiplier enhancement of 1.1. The Court finds that a lodestar multiplier is not warranted here. Nothing before the Court indicates that the case presented novel issues or that the quality of representation far exceeded the quality of representation that would have been provided by attorneys of comparable skill and experience billing at the same rates. The¿contingent risks, skill, and difficulty¿Plaintiffs’ attorneys¿assert are absorbed by their hourly rates.¿(See¿Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California. Inc.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 822.) 

Accordingly, the Court declines to award a lodestar multiplier. 

D. Costs 

Allowable costs “shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) Any items not specifically mentioned by statute “may be allowed or denied in the court's discretion.” (Id., subd. (c)(4).) The Beverly-Song Act allows a successful plaintiff to recover both “costs” and “expenses.” (See Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).) 

'“If the items appear to be proper charges the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the defendant [citations], and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the [objecting party].' [Citation.]” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.)¿ 

Plaintiffs request a total of $1,104.83 in costs. The cost items appear to be proper and Defendant did not challenge any costs. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ request for $1,104.83 in costs is granted. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion for attorney fees is GRANTED IN PART. The Court awards the Law Offices of Isaac $40,245.00 in attorney fees $1,104.83 in costs.

I. BACKGROUND

On October 28, 2024, defendants Elevance Health, Inc. (“Elevance Health”), Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company, Blue Cross of California, The Elevance Health Companies, Inc., The Elevance Health Companies of California, Inc., The Anthem Companies, Inc., and Anthem Insurance Companies, Inc.   1. Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections

IV. LEGAL STANDARD 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)¿The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties.¿(Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) This is usually done by presenting a copy of the signed, written agreement to the court. “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330.) The moving party must also establish the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy.¿(Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) The filing of a lawsuit against the moving party for a controversy clearly within the scope of the arbitration agreement affirmatively establishes the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy.¿ (Hyundai Amco America, Inc. v. S3H, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 572, 577.) 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 requires a trial court to grant a petition to compel arbitration ‘if the court determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.’”¿(Avery v. Integrated Healthcare Holdings, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 50, 59 (quoting CCP § 1281.2).) Accordingly, “when presented with a petition to compel arbitration, the court’s first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ A petition to compel arbitration is in essence a suit in equity to compel specific performance of a contract.¿ (Id. at 71.)¿ As with any other specific performance claim, “a party seeking to enforce an arbitration agreement must show the agreement’s terms are sufficiently definite to enable the court to know what it is to enforce.”¿ (Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)¿ “Only the valid and binding agreement of the parties, including all material terms well-defined and clearly expressed, may be ordered specifically performed.”¿(Ibid.)¿ An arbitration agreement “must be so interpreted as to give effect to the mutual intention of the parties as it existed at the time of contracting, so far as the same is ascertainable and lawful.”¿(Civ. Code, § 1636.) The language of the contract governs its interpretation if it is clear and explicit.¿ (Civ. Code, § 1368.) If uncertainty exists, “the language of a contract should be interpreted most strongly against the party who caused the uncertainty to exist.”¿(Civ. Code, § 1654.) 

The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.¿(Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.) It would then be plaintiff’s burden, in opposing the motion, to prove by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to her opposition. (Ibid.) “In these summary proceedings the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.¿(Ibid.) 

 

V. DISCUSSION 

Defendants argue Elevance Health trains its employees annually on ethics and compliance, and that its human resources policies, including the arbitration policy, are available through its company-wide intranet. Defendants contend Plaintiff regularly completed his annual compliance refresher trainings acknowledging his ability to access and abide by all HR policies on the intranet. Defendants provide a copy of the scripts and certifications of the online annual compliance refresher trainings for the years 2017 to 2021. (Brewer Decl. ¶ 11; Exhs. 3 to 7.) 

The training script does not explicitly mention or provide a copy of any arbitration policy. Rather, Defendants point to the admonition at the end of the training program to support their position that Plaintiff agreed to binding arbitration because the arbitration policy was on Elevance Health’s site. (Brewer Decl. Exh. 6.) That admonition states, “[n]ow you will electronically sign the [year] Ethics and Compliance Certifications. When you do this, you certify that you have received training on the Code of Conduct and that you acknowledge the other statements in the certification” and the Code of Conduct Statement, which provides, “I received the Company’s Ethics and Compliance training and have access to a copy of our Code of Conduct. Compliance with Company policies is a condition of employment and the Company may take corrective action, including termination, for violations of: . . . Company policies and procedures . . . I have access to Human Resources policies via the Company intranet site and understand I am responsible for reading and abiding by the policies and procedures listed in them and as amended from time to time.” Defendants provide a screenshot of the interface in effect from at least July 2018 through the date Plaintiff worked at the company prior to leave of absence on February 16, 2022, where the arbitration policy was under the “My Job” category of Elevance Health’s human resources policies in the Pulse interface. (Drew Decl. ¶ 6; Exh. 1.) 

The arbitration policy provides, in relevant part, the following: 

“By entering into the employment relationship, the company and its associates subject to the policy consent to the resolution by binding arbitration of all claims arising out of or related to the termination of that relationship (and for associates commencing employment in 2001 or later, all legal disputes). This includes claims the company may have against the associate and claims the associate may have against any of the following: (1) the company, (2) its officers, directors, associates or agents, (3) the company’s parent, subsidiary and affiliates and agents, and/or (4) all successors and assigns of any of them. By agreeing to be bound by this policy, the company and its associates waive the right to a jury trial. 

… 

Except as provided in this policy, the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) shall govern the interpretation and enforcement of, and all proceedings under this policy. To the extent that the FAA is inapplicable, or is held not to require arbitration of a particular claim, state law governing agreements to arbitrate shall apply.” 

 (Drew Decl. ¶ 2; Exh. 2) 

Defendants argue that had Plaintiff sought a copy of any of the arbitration policy, the Plaintiff’s supervisor would have directed him to its location on the intranet or provided him with physical copies. In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants lost twice on the same exact grounds in the California Court of Appeal for the Second Appellate District in cases Antoniono v. Elevance Health, Inc., et al. (B327595), 2024 WL 4553074 (October 23, 2024) and Gonzalez v. Anthem, Inc., et al. (B323876), 2024 WL 805140 (February 27, 2024).  

Both Antoniono and Gonzalez are listed as unpublished and noncitable. Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 8.1115, an unpublished opinion of a California Court of Appeal or superior court appellate division must not be cited or relied on by a court or a party in any other action except when the opinion is relevant under the doctrines of law of the case, res judicata, or collateral estoppel. Plaintiff argues Defendants are collaterally estopped pursuant to Antoniono and Gonzalez; the Court reviews the issue independently and need not delve into the persuasive value of arguments and documents filed in Antoniono and Gonzalez. 

The Court’s first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute. (Avery, 218 Cal. App. 4th at 59.) "Although the FAA preempts any state law that stands as an obstacle to its objective of enforcing arbitration agreements according to their terms...we apply general California contract law to determine whether the parties formed a valid agreement to arbitrate their dispute. [Citations]." (Id. at 60.) “Contract law also requires the parties agree to the same thing in the same sense.” (Ibid.) Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff never directly signed an arbitration agreement. Rather, Defendants argue Plaintiff must still arbitrate his claims because he signed certifications agreeing that he had “access to Human Resources policies” and that he is “responsible for reading and abiding by the policies and procedures listed in them and as amended from time to time.” (Brewer Decl. Exh. 6.) Thus, Defendants seek to enforce an implied-in-fact contract, wherein Elevance Health’s policies required employees to arbitrate any claims against it as a condition of their employment. 

"California law permits employers to implement policies that may become unilateral implied-in-fact contracts when employees accept them by continuing their employment." (Asmus v. Pac. Bell (2000) 23 Cal. 4th 1, 11.) However, “[c]ase law clearly requires an employee receive notice of a condition the employer places on the employee's employment before the employee can impliedly accept that condition by beginning or continuing to work for the employer.” (Avery, supra, 218 Cal. App. 4th at 159 citing Schachter v. Citigroup, Inc. (2009) 47 Cal. 4th 610, 620.) Defendants contend that the arbitration policy was incorporated by reference in the annual compliance trainings. “Incorporation by reference requires that (1) the reference to another document was clear and unequivocal; (2) the reference was called to the attention of the other party, who consented to that term; and (3) the terms of the incorporated documents were known or easily available to the contracting parties.” (Kleveland v. Chicago Title Ins. Co. (2006) 141 Cal. App. 4th 761, 765.) Here, Defendants have provided a copy of a training course, but nowhere is an arbitration provision expressly referenced, much less clearly or unequivocally, such that it can be deemed to have been incorporated by reference. (See generally Kleveland v. Chicago Title Ins. Co. (2006)141 Cal. App. 4th 761 [insurer could not enforce arbitration provision in policy because preliminary title report did not incorporate that policy and therefore parties did not agree to its arbitration provision; party must establish precise arbitration provision the parties incorporated into their agreement.] Further, Plaintiff declares that the “training was online and consisted of videos and power point slides that addressed subjects like regulatory compliance” and that he does “not recall the training ever mentioning or referring to arbitration.” (Plaintiff Decl. ¶ 3.) 

Defendants have not demonstrated that Plaintiff ever received notice that an arbitration policy existed at the time Plaintiff received and signed certifications for his annual compliance refresher trainings. As such, Defendants have not met their burden that there exists a valid enforceable arbitration agreement, whether it be express or implied-in-fact. Because Defendants have not met their burden, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to establish any defense of unconscionability. 

 

VI. CONCLUSION 

Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and to stay further proceedings is DENIED.

TENTATIVE 

 

A. The Complaint 

 

Plaintiff Creative Circle, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this breach of contract action against defendants Quantasy & Associates LLC, Quantasy + Associates, Quantasy, LLC, and Does 1 to 20. The complaint alleges that on May 8, 2018, Plaintiff entered into a Temporary Staffing and Direct Hire Services Agreement with defendant Quantasy LLC, aka Quantasy. (Compl. ¶ 10; Exh. A.) Pursuant to the terms of the Agreement, Plaintiff provided contract employees with Quantasy during the period of March 3, 2023 through October 16, 2023 and billed Quantasy for the time the contract employees worked at the hourly rate agreed upon. (Ibid.) On or about July 18, 2022, Plaintiff entered into a Temporary Staffing and Direct Hire Services Agreement with defendant Quantasy & Associates LLC. (Compl. ¶ 11; Exh. B.)  

 

Pursuant to the terms of the Agreement, Plaintiff contracted employees to Quantasy & Associates LLC during the period of October 16, 2023 through March 4, 2024 and billed Quantasy & Associates LLC for the time that the contract employees worked at the hourly rate agreed upon. (Ibid.) Defendants failed to pay the invoices sent from March 3, 2023 through March 4, 2024 in the amount of $117,851.00. (Compl. ¶¶ 12-15.) The complaint sets forth four causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) account stated, (3) book account, and (4) quantum meruit. 

 

 

B. Default Judgment 

 

Plaintiff has dismissed all unnamed Doe defendants. Plaintiff now seeks court judgment against Quantasy & Associates LLC, Quantasy + Associates, and Quantasy, LLC in the principal amount of $117,851.00, in addition to $553.80 for costs. 

 

In support thereof, Plaintiff provides the declaration of its custodian of records, Christopher Coates (“Coates”). Coates declares that a true and correct copy of the correct balance due and unpaid to Plaintiff is set forth in Exhibit “C”. However, the Court notes that Exhibit “C” provides that the total amount due is $116,581.00, not $117,851.00. The Court is unclear as to where Plaintiff included an additional $1,270.00 in the request based on the evidence submitted. 

 

Therefore, the principal amount of $116,581.00 shall be granted pursuant to the evidence proffered. Plaintiff’s costs in the amount of $553.80 for clerk’s filing fees and process server’s fees are allowable pursuant to CCP §1033.5(a) and therefore granted. 

 

C. Conclusion 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment submitted on August 30, 2024 is GRANTED against Quantasy & Associates LLC, Quantasy + Associates, and Quantasy, LLC, jointly and severally, in the principal sum of $116,581.00, and $553.80 in costs.
