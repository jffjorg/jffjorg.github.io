DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call.

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

Norlund Separate Property Trust dated December 11, 1997”; RICHARD L. NORLUND and SHARON K. BACON-NORLUND, as Trustees of the “Norlund Family Trust dated May 13, 2019” and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

NANCY HYMAS, as Trustee for Trust A FBO NANCY HYMAS and Trust B FBO NANCY HYMAS,

                        Nominal Defendants.

 Case No.:          20STCV27665

 Hearing Date:   10/3/23

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiffs Sandra Norlund, an individual, and Sandra Norlund, Trustee of the Norlund Family Trust Dated September 15, 2000’s Motion for Summary Adjudication.

 

 

Background

Pleadings Framing Motion

Plaintiffs Sandra and Sandra [Trustee] (collectively, Plaintiffs, or Sandra) sue the Norlund Defendants and Nominal Defendants Nancy Hymas pursuant to a July 29, 2020 First Amended Complaint that alleged claims of (1) Judicial Foreclosure, (2) Fraud, and (3) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage.

By way of background, the Fraud and Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage claims were stricken by Department 40 on April 13, 2021, based on a representation by Plaintiffs that only the Judicial Foreclosure cause of action remained.

As explained by the court of appeal, the sole remaining claim in this complaint, the first cause of action, arises from allegations that:

“… Richard [Norlund] defaulted on [a] promissory note [between the parties] [(hereafter, Note)] by failing and refusing ‘to pay [Sandra] the agreed upon $7,668.22 per month required by’ the [N]ote. Sandra also alleged Richard breached the settlement agreement [that gave rise to the Note (hereafter, Settlement Agreement)] because, after [Sandra] approved the proposed form of the deed of trust for Richard to record with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office, Richard modified the terms of the deed of trust so that it did not refer to the right promissory note. Specifically, Sandra claimed that Richard changed the date of the promissory note appearing on the deed of trust, which meant the date of the promissory note on the deed of trust did not match the date of her … [N]ote, and that [Richard] recorded the altered deed of trust without her approval. Sandra further alleged Richard, by refusing ‘to provide the valid Deed of Trust,’ breached his agreement to provide Sandra with ‘appropriate documentation to allow her to obtain a Lender[’s] Policy of Title Insurance.’ Sandra alleged Sharon ‘conspired’ with Richard to alter the deed of trust.”

Motion for Summary Adjudication

Legal Standard

“A court may grant a motion for summary judgment ‘“only when ‘all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.’”’” (Fajardo v. Dailey (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 221, 225; see Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c); Regents of University of California v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 607, 618.) A party may also seek summary adjudication of select causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty, which may be made by a standalone motion or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and proceeds in all procedural respects like a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (f)(1)-(2), (t); see Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854-1855, questioned by dictum in Bagley v. TRW, Inc. (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1092, 1094, fn. 2 [finding that summary adjudication may be granted as to separate factual grounds supporting a claim stated as a single count because the separate grounds state a separate cause of action].) “… ‘[A] summary adjudication motion is subject to the same rules and procedures as a summary judgment motion.’ [Citation.]” (Kerkeles v. City of San Jose (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1008.)

“A defendant moving for summary judgment has the initial burden of presenting evidence that a cause of action lacks merit because the plaintiff cannot establish an element of the cause of action or there is a complete defense.” (Sabetian v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 1054, 1068; see Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) “‘Only after the defendant carries that initial burden does the burden shift to the plaintiff “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action …..”’” (Fajardo, at p. 226; see Luebke v. Automobile Club of Southern California (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 694, 703.) The burden on summary judgment or adjudication “is more properly one of persuasion rather than proof, since he must persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find, and not to prove any such fact to the satisfaction of the court itself as though it were sitting as the trier of fact.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850, fn. 11.)

“[I]n ruling on motions for summary judgment courts are to ‘“liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”’ [Citations].” (Cheal v. El Camino Hospital (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 736, 760.)

Order Granting Summary Adjudication of Issue Nos. 1-5: DENIED, Without Prejudice.

As an initial matter, the Court questions whether the motion seeks relief relating to issues that go beyond the scope of the pleadings in the FAC—see e.g., Issue No. 1.

Moving to the merits, Plaintiffs’ motion presents five “issues” for summary adjudication. (See Mot., Second Amended Notice.)

The Norlund Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication should be denied because it does not specify any issues of duty or cause of action sought to be adjudicated. (Opp’n, p. 8.)

After review, the Court agrees and therefore DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion for summary adjudication because no proper issues are before the Court upon which summary adjudication may be granted.

Summary adjudication may properly be granted as to questions of whether a defendant does or does not owe a duty to a plaintiff, whether certain damages exist, and whether there is merit to an affirmative defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1); see Linden Partners v. Wilshire Linden Asscs. (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 508, 518-519 (Linden) [“it may fairly be concluded from settled authority and upon a reasonable interpretation of legislative intent that if, under the facts and circumstances of a given case, a court finds it appropriate to determine the existence or non-existence of a duty in the nature of a contractual obligation, it may properly do so by a ruling on that issue presented by a motion for summary adjudication”].) Here, however, Issue Nos. 1-5 do not involve the determination as to whether a duty does or does not exist, whether certain damages do or do not exist, or whether an affirmative defense has merit. (See Mot., Second Amended Notice.) Instead, Plaintiffs seek summary adjudication of “issues” that involve a determination of whether liability exists in specific and limited circumstances. (See Mot., pp. 13-25.)

Summary adjudication may also be properly granted as to causes of action in a pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) Here, however, Issue Nos. 1-5 request that this Court make determinations as to certain “issues,” not that the Court summarily adjudicate any specific cause of action, of which only one remains: Judicial Foreclosure, which the Court reads as breach of contract. (Cf. below discussion re: Issue No. 2.)

Issue Nos. 1, 3, and 4 involve issues of whether contractual duties in the Note and the Settlement Agreement were breached by the Norlund Defendants through various defaults on contractual obligations, which is improper matter for summary adjudication. Issue No. 2 seeks summary adjudication that “this Court should find that there is an ‘Equitable Mortgage’ and order that a Judicial Foreclosure take place forthwith because RICHARD NORLUND is in default on the Promissory Note and the agreed upon relief Foreclosure if he defaulted”].) See Mot., p. 15 However, Plaintiffs’ motion is framed as seeking disposition of an issue, not summary adjudication of a cause of action, or even summary adjudication of damages. (See Mot., pp. 14-15; see also Mot., Second Amended Notice.) Plaintiff’s point and authorities support this conclusion insofar as the points and authorities do not address the elements of the first cause of action but instead seek adjudication on five discrete issues involving liability. (See Mot., Second Amended Notice; Mot., pp. 13-25.) The Court also notes that Plaintiffs’ motion does not seek summary adjudication of affirmative defenses raised by the Norlund Defendants in their Answer to the FAC. This is despite Plaintiffs’ motion arguing that they “can also establish that Defendants have no viable Affirmative Defenses to excuse these breaches.” (Mot., p. 13; see also Mot., p. 26 [Without making a showing as to each affirmative defense, arguing that “[t]here is no meritorious Affirmative Defense[] to” the cited defaults or other wrongful conduct].) These deficiencies merit a denial.

Issue No. 5 involves a question of whether constructive fraud took place (see Mot., Second Amended Notice; Mot., pp. 19-25), which is a question different than whether summary adjudication is proper as to an operative cause of action (here, only the breach of contract/judicial foreclosure claim remains), an affirmative defense, the existence of a duty, or damages. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

Plaintiffs and Nancy Hymas cite no authority permitting summary adjudication of elements of causes of action—contrasted with summary adjudication of the entire cause of action or to a duty pleaded in a cause of action. (See Mot. & Joinder.) At most, the reply argues that “[a] duty to comply with the terms of a Contract and the breach thereof are clearly issues of duty.” (Reply, ¶ 18.) The Court disagrees with this statement as framed because while the existence of a duty is a matter proper for summary adjudication (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1); Linden, supra, 62 Cal.App.4th pp. 518-519, 522), whether a breach of a duty took place lies outside the scope of the summary adjudication statutory scheme (see Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1)).

Summary adjudication as to Issue Nos. 1 to 5 is therefore improper and must be denied, without prejudice. Issues relating to procedure have already led to one reversal in this action. The Court must ensure that proper relief is requested in summary judgment/adjudication motions and that other applicable procedures are followed. 

Conclusion

Plaintiffs Sandra Norlund, an individual, and Sandra Norlund, Trustee of the Norlund Family Trust Dated September 15, 2000’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED, Without Prejudice.
