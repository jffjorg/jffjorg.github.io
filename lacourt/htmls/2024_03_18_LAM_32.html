DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV05697    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: 32

 

CECILE LOPEZ MACABAGDAL,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

NORMAN ROY LEVINE, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  20STCV05697

  Hearing Date:  March 18, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion to establish admissions

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On February 13, 2020, Plaintiff Cecile Lopez Macabagdal initiated this action against Defendants Norman Roy Levine and Hancock Plaza Homeowners Association, Inc. arising from alleged habitability issues. Defendant Levine was allegedly Plaintiff’s landlord. However, Levine died in January 2021. On July 13, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint adding Herschel Manalo, the trustee of Levine’s estate, as a Defendant. Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint on April 24, 2023.

            On February 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to deem matters admitted as against Defendant Manalo. Defendant filed his opposition on March 5, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

Responses to requests for admission are due thirty days after service of the requests. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).) If a party fails to timely respond to RFAs, the propounding party may move to deem the matters admitted. (Id., § 2033.280(b).) The motion must be granted unless the responding party serves substantially compliant responses before the hearing on the motion. (Id., subd. (c).) Monetary sanctions are mandatory regardless of whether the matters are deemed admitted and regardless of substantial justification. (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

I. The Admissions

Plaintiff served the RFAs on November 14, 2023. (Burton Decl. ¶ 1.) Defendant requested an extension to January 9, 2024, and Plaintiff granted it. (Id., ¶ 2, Ex. 2.) Defendant did not serve responses by this date, and on February 9, 2024, Plaintiff requested verified responses without objection by February 12, 2024. (Id., ¶ 3.) Defense counsel replied that responses would be served by “tomorrow.” (Id., Ex. 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel claims that at the time this motion was filed on February 14, 2024, Defendant had not served any responses to the RFAs. (Id., ¶ 4.)  

            In opposition, defense counsel avers that he emailed Defendant’s RFA responses on February 14, 2024 shortly before Plaintiff filed the motion. (Uss Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. 3.) Defense counsel spoke with Plaintiff’s counsel telephonically to inquire about the filing of the motion after responses had already been received. (Id., ¶ 12.) Plaintiff’s counsel stated that their firm’s practice is to proceed with filing a motion once it has been prepared. (Ibid.) On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel served a meet and confer letter requesting “further” responses to certain RFAs. (Id., Ex. 8.) This confirms that Defendant served responses at least prior to the hearing on the motion. The responses are substantially compliant with Section 2033.220. (See Uss Decl., Ex. 4.) Therefore, the requests are not deemed admitted. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)    

II. Sanctions

            Ordinarily, sanctions are mandatory against the party “whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) However, Defendant’s failure to timely respond would not have “necessitated” the motion if Defendant had already responded prior to the motion being filed. Therefore, sanctions are not warranted.  

III. Relief from Waiver of Objections

            The failure to timely respond to RFAs results in waiver of objections. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) However, the Court may relieve a party from this wavier if (1) the party serves substantially compliant responses, and (2) the failure to timely respond was caused by mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Ibid.)     

            As discussed above, the responses are substantially compliant. Defendant’s failure to timely respond was also caused by excusable neglect because defense counsel was out of office due to a medical emergency and believed that another attorney from the firm would handle Defendant’s responses to discovery. (Uss Decl. ¶¶ 19-21.)   

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion to establish admissions is DENIED. Sanctions are denied. Objections are not waived.  

 

 


Case Number: 22STCV34203    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: 32

 

JANE DOE,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

CASA DE LA FAMILIA, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV34203

  Hearing Date:  March 18, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses (CRS# 0522, 5298, 3150, 3553)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 24, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Doe filed this action against Defendants Casa de la Familia (CDLF), Nogales Psychological Counseling, Inc. (Nogales), and Oscar Aquino (Aquino). The complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9; (2) sexual battery in violation of Civil Code section 1708.5; and (3) negligence. Nogales has been dismissed from the lawsuit.

            According to the complaint, Aquino was employed by CDLF as a marriage and family therapist from April 2019 to May 2020. (Compl. ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that on October 5, 2019, she attended a therapy appointment with Aquino wherein Aquino sexually assaulted her. (Id., ¶¶ 16-17.)

            On February 14 and 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant four motions to compel further responses from Defendant CDLF as to interrogatories, requests for production, and requests for admission. Defendant filed its oppositions on March 5, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).)

MEET AND CONFER

A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Bulger Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Special Interrogatories

            The SROGs seek information related to other complaints against Aquino, complaints about inappropriate therapist conduct at CDLF, and the contact information of the complainants. Defendant objected that the requests are irrelevant and invade privacy.

            a. Relevance

            “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) “‘Relevant’ evidence is evidence ‘having any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.’” (D.Z. v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 210, 229.)

            In this action, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant CDLF knew of Aquino’s propensity for sexual misconduct. Information on other complaints, including the contact information of the complainants, is directly relevant to establishing a pattern of misconduct and Defendant’s knowledge thereof. However, the relevance of this information extends only to events up to the date of the subject incident, October 5, 2019. Complaints after October 5, 2019 are not relevant.

b. Privacy

            “The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.” (Williams v. Sup. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) If the court finds a privacy interest, the court must balance the privacy concerns against the need for the information. (Id. at p. 552.) Discovery of private information is governed by the more stringent standard of direct relevance in order to prevent a fishing expedition of “tangentially pertinent sensitive information.” (Boler v. Sup. Ct. (1987) 201 Cal.App.3d 467, 472.) “The burden is on the party seeking the constitutionally protected information to establish direct relevance.” (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1017.)

            “The disclosure of the names and addresses of potential witnesses is a routine and essential part of pretrial discovery.” (Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1249-50.) Protected medical information consists of “a patient’s medical history, mental health application information, reproductive or sexual health application information, mental or physical condition, or treatment.” (Civ. Code, § 56.05(j).) It does not include mere contact information. Additionally, even medical information is subject to disclosure if compelled by a court order. (Id., § 56.10(b)(1).) Therefore, the protection is not absolute. Defendant cites no authority for its proposition that contact information falls under the “inalienable right of privacy.”

            Defendant has not established a reasonable expectation of privacy in mere contact information, especially for patients who had already filed complaints. (See Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 371 [“Revealing names, addresses and contact information on persons who have already complained about their Pioneer DVD players would not be particularly sensitive or intrusive”].) Even if there were a reasonable expectation of privacy, the information sought is directly relevant because Plaintiff is entitled to establish that Defendant was aware of Aquino’s propensity for sexual misconduct. Plaintiff’s need for the information outweighs the privacy concerns. Furthermore, a protective order will sufficiently protect any privacy interests, such that the intrusion is not serious. (See Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 38.)

            In sum, further responses are required for the SROGs.  

II. Form Interrogatories

An answer to an interrogatory must be as complete and straightforward as possible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220(a).) Form Interrogatories are drafted and approved by the Judicial Council and seek basic information fundamental to routine discovery. (See Puerto, supra, 158 Cal.App.4th at p. 1250.) A party “is entitled to demand answers to its interrogatories, as a matter of right . . . [and] the burden of justifying any objection and failure to respond remains at all times with the party resisting an interrogatory.” (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 541.) 

For FROG Nos. 3.7 and 12.1, Defendant’s only contention is that it has provided or will provide supplemental responses. Defendant does not otherwise contend that its initial responses were proper. This constitutes a concession that the original responses were defective as set forth in Plaintiff’s separate statement. Therefore, the motion is granted as to FROG Nos. 3.7 and 12.1.

Defendant substantively opposes FROG Nos. 14.1 and 14.2. FROG No. 14.1 asks whether Defendant contends that any person involved in the incident violated any law which was a proximate cause of the incident. If so, the FROGs ask for the contact information of each such person and the law that was violated. FROG No. 14.2 asks whether any person was cited or charged with a legal violation as a result of the incident. If so, the FROG asks for each person’s contact information, the law violated, whether the person entered a plea, contact information for the court or administrative agency, the names of the parties, and the case number.

In response to FROG Nos. 14.1 and 14.2, Defendant objected and then identified the investigation conducted by the Department of Consumer Affairs (DCA). This is nonresponsive. The FROGs clearly ask a series of discrete questions. Defendant never stated whether any person violated a statute or ordinance in connection with the incident, nor did Defendant identify the person(s) or provide the corresponding information required by the subparts.

Defendant argues that it attached the DCA investigation and is not obligated to go through each page to list out the specifics for Plaintiff’s benefit. This argument ignores the real issue, which is Defendant’s failure to answer each interrogatory completely and straightforwardly. Again, the FROGs ask a series of discrete questions, and Defendant answered none of them. Defendant cannot simply reference a document unless “the answer to an interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of or from the documents of the party to whom the interrogatory is directed, and if the burden or expense of preparing or making it would be substantially the same for the party propounding the interrogatory as for the responding party.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230.) Even then, Defendant’s response must provide “sufficient detail to permit the propounding party to locate and to identify, as readily as the responding party can, the documents from which the answer may be ascertained.” (Ibid.) Defendant merely cited the entire DCA investigation without providing any information on where to find the answers to the specific questions posed by the FROGs.

In sum, Defendant must fully and clearly respond to each subpart of the subject FROGs. To the extent Defendant does not possess responsive information, it must provide a code-compliant response to that effect. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220(c).)  

 

 

 

III. Requests for Production

            The information sought in the RFPs parallels the information sought in the SROGs discussed above. Defendant asserts the same relevance and privacy arguments. For the same reasons, Defendant must provide further responses to the RFPs. 

IV. Requests for Admission

            Like the discovery discussed above, the RFAs seek information pertaining to complaints against Aquino and Defendant’s potential knowledge of Aquino’s pattern of misconduct. Defendant primarily objected to the RFAs as being vague and ambiguous. In particular, Defendant argues that it could not respond to RFAs alleging that other patients had complained about Aquino asking “inappropriate questions” or exhibiting “inappropriate behavior.”

            Defendant’s contention is without merit. It is not credible that Defendant cannot ascertain what constitutes “inappropriate” conduct, considering the context of the lawsuit. Plaintiff alleges that Aquino asked her invasive questions about her sex life before inappropriately touching her. Defendant is readily capable of determining whether others have made complaints of similar or related conduct. Where “the nature of the information sought is apparent, the proper solution is to provide an appropriate response,” even if “the question is somewhat ambiguous.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783.)

            As discussed above, the relevance of this information is limited to events occurring on or before October 5, 2019.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses are GRANTED in part as set forth above. Defendant CDLF shall provide further responses to the subject discovery within 15 days of this order. Production shall be subject to a protective order. Sanctions are denied as the parties acted with substantial justification.

             


Case Number: 23STCV25128    Hearing Date: March 18, 2024    Dept: 32

 

HAKOB JOHNNY NAZARETYAN, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

DSD MANAGEMENT,  

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV25128

  Hearing Date:  March 18, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiffs’ motion for trial preference

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff Hakob Johnny Nazaretyan, individually and as guardian ad litem for minor Ariana Nazaretyan; and Sati Gevorgyan, individually, filed this action against Defendant DSD Management, LLC. The complaint asserts causes of action for (1) breach of the implied covenant of habitability, (2) negligence, and (3) nuisance.

            On February 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for trial preference on the grounds that Ariana Nazaretyan is a minor. Defendant filed its opposition on March 5, 2024. Plaintiffs filed their reply on March 11, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

“A civil action to recover damages for wrongful death or personal injury shall be entitled to preference upon the motion of any party to the action who is under 14 years of age unless the court finds that the party does not have a substantial interest in the case as a whole.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36(b).) The statute makes preference mandatory as long as the conditions are met, regardless of prejudice. (See Swaithes v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1082, 1085 [“The trial court has no power to balance the differing interests of opposing litigants in applying the provision”].)

DISCUSSION

There is no dispute that Ariana Nazaretyan is a minor under the age of 14. Defendants instead argue that the minor plaintiff does not have a substantial interest in the case as a whole. The statute does not define “substantial interest,” nor is there caselaw imposing a definition. 

            Plaintiffs argue that the minor has a substantial interest in the case as a whole because she was exposed to the same uninhabitable conditions as the adult plaintiffs, suffered numerous injuries as a result, and is suing for substantial damages. Defendant argues that the minor does not have a substantial interest for the following reasons: Plaintiffs have no actual evidence of the minor’s injuries beyond a single diagnosis of ringworm with an unidentified cause; the minor is only 2.5 years old and will not be testifying, and therefore has no interest in expediting the case; the minor did not suffer lost income and did not pay for the medical expenses; the adult plaintiffs are the ones bringing the suit and will be the ones producing the evidence; and any damages awarded to the minor will be deposited in a blocked account.

            The Court agrees with Defendant. Here, the interest of the minor plaintiff is tangential compared to the adult plaintiffs, who are the primary litigants. The numerical proportion of minors to adults is not dispositive. The evidence will primarily concern and come from the adult plaintiffs. The adults would have suffered the majority of damages because they would have been the ones who paid the rent and medical expenses. Therefore, the Court finds that the minors do not have a substantial interest in the case as a whole.   

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiffs’ motion for trial preference is DENIED.
