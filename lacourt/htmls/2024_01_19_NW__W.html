Case Number: 20VECV00815    Hearing Date: January 19, 2024    Dept: W

CR RODAS, INC. v. 14222 VICTORY, LLC, et al.

 

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

Date of Hearing:        January 19, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        20VECV00815

 

Moving Party:            Brenda E. Vargas, Esq., Counsel for Plaintiff

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff alleges Defendants entered into a written agreement with Plaintiff and that pursuant to that agreement, Defendants became indebted to Plaintiff for materials and labor it delivered.

 

On February 25, 2022, Plaintiff CR Rodas, Inc. filed a second amended complaint against Defendants Varuzh Vic Avedisian, 14222 Victory, LLC, Holtman Construction Inc., and Angel Martinez asserting causes of action for breach of contract, common counts, and quantum meruit.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: GRANT

 

Counsel contends their office has lost all communication with Plaintiff and/or the owner of the company. As a result, Counsel cannot effectively perform their duties to represent the legal interests of Plaintiff in the continuation of this case. The Client has not returned any of Counsel’s written requests for meetings, documents, etc. Counsel has made several attempts to meet and discuss with the client only to have the Client cancel and/or not show up.

 

Counsel has complied with the requirements of CRC Rule 3.1362 by filing forms MC-051 and MC-052 and lodging with the court a copy of the proposed order on form MC-053. Counsel confirmed client’s last known mailing address by telephone within the last 30 days.  

 

The MC-053 form is blank. It should be resubmitted with the client’s information. 

 

Trial is set for June 24, 2024. Counsel is to warn corporate Plaintiff that they can only appear through counsel because it is a corporation, and corporations cannot proceed in pro per.

 


Case Number: 22VECV01364    Hearing Date: January 19, 2024    Dept: W

JANET YONJUNG JHA v. SMAN TONYALA, et al.

 

MOTIONS BY PLAINTIFF JANET YONJUNG JHA FOR ORDER COMPELLING RESPONSES OF DEFENDANTS SMAN TONYALA AND BUAKAI TONYALA TO PLAINTIFF'S FIRST SET OF REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND FIRST SET OF FORM INTERROGATORIES and Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala’s Motions to Set Aside Order Deeming Requests for Admissions, Set One, Admitted

 

Date of Hearing:        January 19, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01364

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 19, 2022, Plaintiff Janet Yonjung Jha filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala asserting causes of action for specific performance and breach of written agreement. Plaintiff alleges on August 30, 2022, Plaintiff, as buyer, and Defendants, as sellers, entered into a California Residential Purchase Agreement and Joint Escrow Instructions (the "Agreement"), pursuant to which Plaintiff agreed to purchase and Defendants agreed to sell for a total purchase price of $900,000.00 certain residential real property. However, on September 6, 2022, Plaintiff alleges Defendants, through their broker and agent, expressly repudiated the Agreement by unequivocally notifying Plaintiff that Defendants would refuse to perform their obligations under the Agreement and to sell the Subject Property to Plaintiff.

 

Defendants filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Federico Cabral, Mission Real Estate, Inc. and Gerardo “Jerry” Ascencio on November 28, 2022, asserting causes of action for indemnification, apportionment of fault, declaratory relief, intentional infliction of emotional distress, fraud in the inducement and misrepresentation, conspiracy, elder abuse, breach of contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and breach of fiduciary duty. Defendants filed a Second Amended cross-complaint on November 15, 2023 to allege 1. fraudulent inducement 2. financial elder abuse 3. breach of fiduciary duty 4. constructive fraud5. declaratory relief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala’s Motions to Set Aside Order Deeming Requests for Admissions, Set One, Admitted are GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff Janet Yonjung Jha’s Motions for Order Compelling Responses of Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala to Plaintiff's First Set of Requests for Production of Documents and Form Interrogatories are MOOT.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                    DEFENDANTS SMAN TONYALA AND BUAKAI TONYALA’S MOTIONS TO SET ASIDE ORDER DEEMING REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS, SET ONE, ADMITTED

 

Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala move the court for an order setting aside the Court’s September 11, 2023 order deeming admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One (RFAs I), propounded by Plaintiff Janet Yonjung Ja on Defendants.

 

The court may permit withdrawal or amendment of an admission only if it determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (CCP § 2033.300(b).) “The court may impose conditions on the granting of the motion that are just, including, but not limited to, the following: (1) An order that the party who obtained the admission be permitted to pursue additional discovery related to the matter involved in the withdrawn or amended admission. (2) An order that the costs of any additional discovery be borne in whole or in part by the party withdrawing or amending the admission.” (CCP § 2033.300(c)(1)-(2).) 

 

“[A]ny doubts in ruling on a motion to withdraw or amend an admission must be resolved in favor of the moving party.” (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1408 (the record did not “clearly establish” the mistake in admitting the matter was inexcusable or show the withdrawal of the admission would prejudice the other party).)  Such requirement comports with the policy in favor of trial being held on the merits. (Id.) “The court's discretion to deny a motion under [CCP § 2033.300] is limited to circumstances where it is clear that the mistake, inadvertence, or neglect was inexcusable, or where it is clear that the withdrawal or amendment would substantially prejudice the party who obtained the admission in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (Id. at 1420-21; see also Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973 (By permitting relief, . . . we eliminate such undeserved windfalls [caused by a party’s failure to timely respond to RFAs] and the resulting subversion of the policy favoring the resolution of lawsuits on the merits.)

 

Defendants contend they are hard of hearing, speak poor English, and understand it even more poorly and require translation. However, none of the document served upon the pro per Tonyalas in this proceeding have been translated to Thai for them. It was not until their daughter realized how prejudiced they were and found counsel to get involved in the case that they understood their obligations with respect to discovery responses.  As such, Defendants’ failure understand the legal documents served upon them and take action in response was mistake, inadvertence and excusable neglect for unsophisticated foreign 80+ year old retirees in poor health.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the Tonyalas have failed to present any basis for granting their motion. Plaintiff contends the Tonyalas clearly understood their obligation to respond to the RFAs as their daughter, Ms. Rowe, requested additional time to respond to the RFAs. Moreover, the motion clearly spelled out the consequences of failing to provide responses to the RFAs. Yet no opposition was filed to the motion and neither the Tonyalas nor Ms. Rowe appeared at the hearing on the motion. Plaintiff also contends the fact that the Tonyalas were in pro per until recently is no excuse. (See Stover v. Bruntz (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 19, 31.) Plaintiff states if the court decides to grant the motion, the granting of the motion should be conditioned on the Tonyalas' paying Jha $22,305.00.

 

The court finds excusable neglect. In Stover, the pro per plaintiff argued the court “should have set the deemed admissions aside because as a pro per litigant she was unfamiliar with the discovery statutes.” (Stover, supra, 12 Cal.App.5th at p. 31.) The Court of Appeal rejected this argument and refused to afford pro per litigants “greater consideration than other litigants and attorneys.” (Id.) The Court of Appeal found the plaintiff “was not entitled to disregard the rules for timely responding to discovery and she was not immune from the consequences of a failure to do so.” (Id.) Stover is distinguishable, however, as here defendants do not merely state that they were self-represented as an explanation of their mistake and neglect; they also present evidence of their advanced age, ill- health and lack of even basic understanding of English.  The court has had the opportunity observe them in court and finds their claims about their language abilities and advanced age credible; neither has appeared to understand the proceedings even when assisted by their daughter and a Thai interpreter.  Accordingly, the court finds that they have established mistake or excusable neglect. 

 

As for Plaintiff’s request for fees/costs, Plaintiff requests: $1,777.50 for preparing the RFAs, $1,442.50 as awarded in the motion to compel, $6,320.00 for preparing an MSJ based on Defendants’ admissions, $2,765.00 for preparing the instant opposition, and $10,000 to take depositions of the Tonyalas on facts previously established by the deemed admitted RFAs. Section 2033.300(c)(2) provides: “The court may impose conditions on the granting of the motion that are just, including, but not limited to, the following: (2) An order that the costs of any additional discovery be borne in whole or in part by the party withdrawing or amending the admission.”  (C.C.P. Section 2033.010 See also Rhule v. WaveFront Technology, Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1223.)   The court grants the request in the reduced amount of $8,000, which constitutes reasonable attorney fees for preparing the motion for summary judgment based on the RFAs and for preparing the instant opposition.  The court does not find any further sanction justified or reasonable. Defendants remain separately responsible for the sanctions previously ordered in the amount of $1442.50.

 

Accordingly, Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala’s Motions to Set Aside Order Deeming Requests for Admissions, Set One, Admitted are GRANTED.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF JANET YONJUNG JHA’S MOTIONS FOR ORDER COMPELLING RESPONSES OF DEFENDANTS SMAN TONYALA AND BUAKAI TONYALA TO PLAINTIFF'S FIRST SET OF REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND FORM INTERROGATORIES; REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff Janet Yonjung Jha’s motions for Order Compelling Responses of Defendants Sman Tonyala and Buakai Tonyala to Plaintiff's First Set of Requests for Production of Documents and Form Interrogatories are MOOT. On December 18, 2023, both Defendants’ responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, as well as production of D1-172, were served. (Kim Decl. ¶3, Exhs. 1-5.)

 

Plaintiff still request monetary sanctions against the Tonyalas in the amount of $1,305.00. The court grants Plaintiff’s request.


Case Number: 23VECV01899    Hearing Date: January 19, 2024    Dept: W

Frank Harris v. Jason Desrouleaux

 

demurrer

 

Date of Hearing:        January 19, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01899

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Jason Desrouleaux  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Frank Harris

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 1, 2023, Plaintiff Frank Harris filed a complaint against Defendant Jason Desrouleaux p/k/a Jason Derulo (“Derulo”) for breach of contract, promissory estoppel, quantum meruit, accounting, and declaratory relief. Plaintiff was Defendant’s professional manager for recording and entertainment for over 20 years. Plaintiff alleges pursuant to the parties’ 2014 Oral Agreement, Derulo is obligated to pay Harris commissions on all engagements, agreements, and contracts substantially negotiated or entered into while Harris was acting as Derulo’s personal manager. However, despite sending Plaintiff Commission Statements reflecting the  amounts owed to Harris, Derulo has failed and/or refused to pay the commissions owed to Harris as set forth in the NuKing and JDI Commission Statements. Plaintiff further alleges in addition to the amounts set forth in the NuKing and JDI Commission Statements, Derulo owes Harris his 15 percent commission on the revenue that Derulo has received, directly or indirectly through his companies.

 

Request for entry of default was entered August 14, 2023. Plaintiff and Defendant entered a stipulation to set aside the default and on October 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint.

 

[Tentative] Ruling:

 

Defendant Jason Desrouleaux’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

disucssion

 

Defendant Jason Desrouleaux, professionally known as Jason Derulo, demurs to the First Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff Frank Harris on the grounds each cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendant.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds Plaintiff fails to allege a sufficiently certain and definite contract, the alleged 2014 Oral Agreement is an invalid oral modification of the 2009 Management Agreement, and the alleged 2014 Oral Agreement is barred by the statute of frauds.

 

The elements for breach of contract cause of action are: (1) existence of contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach (or anticipatory breach); and (4) resulting damage.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) “A written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) “In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (Id.) 

 

Defendant first argues a contract must be so sufficiently definite “for the court to ascertain the parties’ obligations and to determine whether those obligations have been performed or breached.” (Bustamante v. Intuit, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal. App. 4th 199, 209.) In the 2009 Management Agreement, Derulo agreed to pay Harris 20% of his “Gross Earnings” during a five-year term. After the expiration of the five-year term, their relationship would automatically terminate, and Derulo agreed to pay Harris tiered residual commissions that decreased over time – 15% for the first year, 10% for the second, third, and fourth year, and 5% for the fifth year and every year following. Defendant contends as a result, Plaintiff’s claims that the 2014 Oral Agreement adopts “the same terms and conditions” as the written 2009 Management Agreement is inconsistent with his claims that he continued to serve as Derulo’s manager and received a 20% commission on all Derulo’s gross earnings for eight and a half years under the 2014 Oral Agreement.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff Harris argues the complaint specifically identifies the relevant terms of the parties’ 2014 Oral Agreement and the commission amount due to be paid under that agreement and Defendant’s hyper technical reading of the complaint is incorrect. Plaintiff contends the complaint specifically alleges the 2014 Oral Agreement remained in effect until September 24, 2022 and until that date, Harris was entitled to—and in fact, received—20 percent commissions, and after that date, Harris was entitled to reduced commission rates.

 

The court finds the complaint sufficiently alleges definite terms of a contract for the purposes of a demurrer. Paragraph 15 of the complaint alleges in January 2014, Defendant and Plaintiff orally agreed that Plaintiff would continue to act as Defendant’s personal manager, performing the same services then being performed, on the same terms and conditions then existing under the 2009 Management Agreement, including Derulo’s obligation to pay Harris a commission of 20 percent of any and all Gross Earnings received by Defendant during the parties’ professional. (FAC ¶15.) The complaint goes on to say the agreement called for defendant to continue paying Plaintiff, after termination of the parties’ professional relationship, 15 percent of Derulo’s Gross Earnings derived from engagements, contracts and agreements entered into or negotiated while the parties’ professional relationship remained in effect. (FAC ¶15.) These allegations sufficiently show the basic aspects of the contract.  

 

Next, Defendant argues regardless of the above inconsistencies in Harris’ allegations, the purported 2014 Oral Agreement constitutes an invalid oral amendment to the terms of the written 2009 Management Agreement, which is expressly prohibited therein. Defendant contends where a written agreement “expressly prohibits modification other than by a signed writing, however, a contract claim based on subsequent oral modifications is not cognizable” and “must be dismissed.” (Min Prods. PTE. Ltd v. Fireforge, Inc. (2015) WL 13907702 at *10.) The 2009 Management Agreement was for a set term of five years and expressly stated that “no modification, amendment, waiver, termination or discharge of any provision hereof shall be binding upon the parties unless confirmed by a written instrument executed by the parties.” (FAC Ex. 1 ¶ 13(a).)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Derulo mischaracterizes the allegations of the complaint. Plaintiff contends the complaint alleges that “as the term 2009 Management Agreement expired,” the parties orally entered into a new agreement “under which Harris would continue to act as Derulo’s personal manager on the same terms and conditions existing under the 2009 Management Agreement.” Moreover, even if the complaint did allege an oral modification of the written 2009 Management Agreement, Plaintiff contends the parties may modify a written contract by executed oral agreement, waiver, or estoppel even if the written contract expressly precludes oral modification. (See LGCY Power, LLC v. Superior Court (2022) 75 Cal. App. 5th 844, 866-868; Biren v. Equality Emergency Medical Group, Inc. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 125, 141; Conely v. Matthes (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1453, 1466.)

 

The court finds as alleged, the 2014 Oral Agreement is not a modification of the 2009 Management Agreement. “Whether a writing has been modified by an executed oral agreement is a question of fact.” (Grove v. Grove Valve & Regulator Co. (1970) 4 Cal.App.3d 299, 313.) The FAC alleges the 2009 Management Agreement expired and in January 2014, the parties orally agreed Plaintiff could continue to represent Defendant and receive 20% commission until the relationship is terminated and thereafter, Plaintiff would receive 15% commission.

 

Lastly, Defendant argues the Statute of Frauds applies to the alleged 2014 Oral Agreement. According to Harris, the 2014 Oral Agreement adopted and incorporated “the same terms and conditions” as the written 2009 Management Agreement, which included a fixed five-year term followed by tiered residual commissions for at least another five years (and possibly in perpetuity). As noted above, the court finds the complaint does not allege a fixed five-year term for the 2014 Oral Agreement. Moreover, as alleged, the 2014 Oral Agreement may be terminable at will. (See White Lighting Co. v. Wolfson (1968) 68 Cal.2d 336, 344.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract is OVERRULED.

 

Promissory Estoppel

 

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for promissory estoppel on the grounds Harris has failed to allege any detrimental reliance calling for the application of the promissory estoppel doctrine.

 

“The elements of promissory estoppel are (1) a promise, (2) the promisor should reasonably expect the promise to induce action or forbearance on the part of the promisee or a third person, (3) the promise induces action or forbearance by the promisee or a third person (which we refer to as detrimental reliance), and (4) injustice can be avoided only by enforcement of the promise.”  (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 803, reh’g denied (Apr. 11, 2013), review denied (July 10, 2013).) “The party claiming estoppel must specifically plead all facts relied on to establish its elements.”  (Smith v. City and County of San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 48.)  A conclusory allegation of detrimental reliance is insufficient.  (See id.)             “If the promisee's performance was requested at the time the promisor made his promise and that performance was bargained for, the doctrine is inapplicable.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irr. Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 249.)

 

Defendant contends Harris merely asserts that he served as Derulo’s manager based upon Derulo’s promise to pay commissions. Thus, the detrimental reliance upon which Harris bases his claim for promissory estoppel is nothing more than the existence of the purported 2014 Oral Agreement.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues his claim for promissory estoppel is sufficient because Harris pleads that he relied on Derulo’s promise to pay for his management services to Harris’ detriment and that he was injured as a result. The complaint alleges “Harris reasonably relied on [Derulo’s] promise [to pay commissions] to his injury and detriment, in part, because based upon Derulo’s promise, Harris diverted his professional efforts and time that would have been spent working for other clients and potential clients to Derulo.” (FAC ¶ 51.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff’s allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. In Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 779–780, the Court of Appeal found the plaintiff sufficiently alleged a claim for promissory estoppel when the defendants promised to pay plaintiffs an amount sufficient to retire upon the sale of the company if they remained employed, the promise was sufficiently clear and definite and plaintiffs relied on this promise to their detriment by remaining employed there and forgoing other employment and residential opportunities. As noted above, Plaintiff has alleged a clear and definite promise to continue to pay Plaintiff 20% commission until the parties terminated their professional relationship and Plaintiff relied on that promise to his detriment by diverting his professional efforts and time that would have been spent on other clients.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Quantum Meruit

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for quantum meruit on the grounds Harris’ quantum meruit claim does not turn on whether Derulo was unjustly enriched for having received a benefit from Harris.

 

To recover in quantum meruit, a party need not prove the existence of a contract [citations], but it must show the circumstances were such that ‘“the services were rendered under some understanding or expectation of both parties that compensation therefor was to be made.”’ (Huskinson & Brown v. Wolf (2004) 32 Cal.4th 453, 458.) ‘“The measure of recovery in quantum meruit is the reasonable value of the services rendered, provided they were of direct benefit to the defendant.”’ [Citation.] ‘[A] plaintiff must establish both that he or she was acting pursuant to either an express or implied request for such services from the defendant and that the services rendered were intended to and did benefit the defendant.’” (Advanced Choices, Inc. v. State Dept. of Health Services (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 1661, 1673; see Chodos v. Borman (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 76, 96–97.)  

 

Defendant argues the crux of Plaintiff’s claim is that he is entitled to commissions on Derulo’s gross earnings from any engagement that arose during his time as manager regardless of whether Harris was involved at all. As a result, because Harris claims entitlement for services he did not render and for benefits he did not confer upon Derulo, his quantum meruit claim fails.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the complaint alleges that Derulo received a direct benefit from Harris’ efforts. (FAC, ¶ 56.) The reasonable value of Harris’ services is a quintessential factual issue. (See, e.g., Children’s Hosp. Cent. California v. Blue Cross of California (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1275 (reasonable value set by market rates, expert testimony, or the parties’ own contract).) Since Harris pled that his services created significant value for Derulo, but Harris has not been appropriately compensated, the cause of action must stand.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a claim for quantum meruit. “ ‘”The measure of recovery in quantum meruit is the reasonable value of the services rendered provided they were of direct benefit to the defendant.”[Citations.]’ (Ibid.) In other words, quantum meruit is equitable payment for services already rendered.” (E.J. Franks Construction, Inc. v. Sahota (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1123, 1128.) The complaint alleges specific performances Harris provided to defendant, including meeting with various publishing companies regarding a potential book deal for Derulo, advising Derulo and representing his interests in negotiations with Adobe Software, which resulted in a brand deal for Derulo, and so on. (FAC ¶56.) While a portion of the complaint may rely on engagements that arose during Plaintiff’s time as manager regardless of whether he was involved at all, “[a] demurrer is not the appropriate vehicle to challenge a portion of a cause of action demanding an improper remedy.” (Caliber Bodyworks, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 365, 384-85.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Accounting

 

Defendant demurs to the fourth cause of action for accounting on the grounds the accounting claim depends entirely on Plaintiff having stated viable claims for breach of contract, promissory estoppel, and quantum meruit.

 

As the court has overruled the demurrer to the breach of contract, promissory estoppel, and quantum meruit causes of action, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for accounting is OVERRULED.

 

Declaratory Relief

 

Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action for declaratory relief on the grounds Harris’ claim for declaratory relief is duplicative of his claim for breach of contract.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1060 provides that “Any person interested . . . under a contract, . . . may, in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties, bring an original action or cross-complaint in the superior court for a declaration of his or her rights and duties . . . including a determination of any question of construction or validity arising under the instrument or contract.” (Emphasis added.)

 

Defendant argues a declaratory relief action will not lie to determine issues raised in other causes of action before the court as “the object of the [declaratory relief] statute is to afford a new form of relief where needed and not to furnish a litigant with a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues.” (California Ins. Guar, Ass ‘n v. Sup. Ct. (Jakes) (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 1617, 1623-1624.) As such, Harris’ claim for declaratory relief is duplicative of his claim for breach of contract in that it is merely another attempt to validate the unwritten terms of his alleged oral agreement.

 

The court disagrees. Demurrer is appropriate when “an alleged cause of action adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery, containing only allegations of other causes of action.” (Cisco Systems, Inc. v. STMicroelectronics, Inc. (2014) 77 F.Supp.3d 887, 896; see also Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501 [sustaining, without leave to amend, demurrer to a cause of action entirely duplicative of preceding causes of action].) The breach of contract cause of action focuses on the $1 million in commissions and the declaratory relief cause of action focuses on future revenue that Derulo will receive from engagements, agreements, and contracts entered into while Harris was acting as Derulo’s manager.   

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.
