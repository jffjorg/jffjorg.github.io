DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV15480    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Enforce Court’s January 21, 2024 Order

Moving Party: Defendant AEG Management LACC
Responding Party: Christina Gutierrez
Tentative Ruling: Granted

STATEMENT OF CASE

This is a premises liability case in which Plaintiff Christina Gutierrez (“Plaintiff”) sues Defendant City of Los Angeles and Does 1 to 50, including Defendant AEG Management LACC, LLC (“Defendant” or “AEG”) for premises liability and personal injury. Plaintiff filed the Complaint on April 22, 2022, alleging negligence, willful failure to warn, and dangerous condition of public property against Defendant. Plaintiff prayed for relief for wage loss, general damages, hospital and medical expenses, and loss of earning capacity. 

On January 31, 2024, the Court ordered Plaintiff to provide code-compliant responses within 20 days of its Order and provided that “interrogatory responses are to be served without objections and without the option to produce writing under CCP 2030.290(a).” Furthermore, the Court instructed Plaintiff to substantially answer all sub-sections. 

On March 7, 2024, Defendant AEG filed the instant motion, requesting the Court enforce the January 31, 2024, Order, instructing Plaintiff to serve compliant responses to Interrogatories, Set One, because Plaintiff had still yet to do so. Furthermore, Defendant requested terminating and monetary sanctions. 

On April 5, 2024, Defendant AEG filed a Notice of Non-Receipt of Plaintiff’s Opposition.

On April 17, 2024, the Court, on its own motion, continued the Hearing on the instant Motion from April 17, 2024 to June 6, 2024. In addition, the Court ordered Supplemental briefing to be filed and served ten calendar days prior to the instant hearing, along with the Response to be filed six days prior, and the Reply three days prior. 

On May 24, 2024, Defendant filed its Supplemental Briefing, along with the Declaration of Defendant’s counsel of record, Virginia E. Lopez and a Separate Statement. Defendant requested the Court grant either evidentiary sanctions or in the alternative, monetary sanctions. 

On May 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Response, entitled “Plaintiff’s Supplemental Brief in Support of Opposition to Defendant AEG’s Motion to Enforce.”

On June 3, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply, along with another declaration by Counsel Lopez. 

Defendant moved the Court for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff, in the form of striking Plaintiff's Complaint and dismissing her case in its entirety. Defendant's motion was made on the grounds Plaintiff has disobeyed a valid Court Order. Thus, Defendant moved the Court to strike Plaintiff's Complaint in its entirety pursuant to CCP §§128(a)(4), 2023.010(g), 2023.030(d)(3), and 2023.030(d)(1). Defendant asserted this was the third motion to enforce a valid Court Order against Plaintiff; a significant factor, which Defendant asked the Court consider in deciding whether to impose terminating sanctions against Plaintiff. Plaintiff had a history of disobeying the Court's orders and has demonstrated a disinterest in obeying this Court's orders, prosecuting her case, and/or submitting to the discovery process. Lastly, Defendant requested the Court award monetary sanctions against Plaintiff, and her attorney of record, in an amount sufficient to punish Plaintiff, or in the amount of at least $722.50 for attorney time and costs incurred in bringing this motion. 

In Defendant’s Supplemental Brief, Defendant AEG requests an Order in the form of evidentiary sanctions to preclude Plaintiff' from introducing any evidence that she will need or has been advised of the need for future treatment, including surgery to her neck and back. The request for evidentiary sanctions is related, connected, and arises from the fact that Plaintiff continues to disobey the Court's Order by failing to substantially answer Form Interrogatory 6.7 which concerns future medical treatment. Additionally, Plaintiff fails to answer all subparts. Furthermore, AEG”s Separate Statement provides the language of the interrogatory at issue, the response, a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further response (in this case AEG seeks an order for evidentiary sanctions), and a summary of other relevant documents upon which AEG may rely. CRC 3.1345(a)-(d). As such and in addition to Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions, Defendant now requests evidentiary sanctions against Plaintiff to preclude Plaintiff from introducing, referring to, or mentioning any evidence at trial that she will need and has been advised she will need future treatment, including surgery, to her neck and back. 

Plaintiff argues that on March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed code compliant verified Responses to all outstanding discovery on March 22, 2024, and that despite Defendant now being in receipt of such requested discovery responses, Defendant declined to take the Hearing for the instant Motion off calendar. Counsel further argues that Plaintiff’s responses were untimely because at the relevant time, Plaintiff was not communicating with her Counsel due to her persistent medical complications, rendering her physically unable to assist in finalizing her discovery responses. Additionally, Plaintiff was physically hospitalized for over a week during this period 
of incapacitation. Thus, Plaintiff was unavailable for a justifiable reason and should therefore not be punished. Moreover, Plaintiff’s Counsel was attempting to resolve the matter, knowing that the lack of communication with the client was not usual. 

Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s request to exclude evidence of future medical treatment regarding Plaintiff’s neck and back treatment seems overboard, abusive, punitive, and unjust because nowhere in the January 31, 2024, Order does the Court discuss interrogatory 6.7 in any manner. Similarly, Defendant did not bring up any concerns regarding 6.7 being evasive or incomplete. Lastly, Defendant has not been prejudiced because Plaintiff has previously responded to two independent sets of discovery requests propounded by the other two co- defendants in this litigation, all represented by the same defense counsel. Thus, Defendant is propounding excessive discovery and utilizing its position of representing three separate Defendants to engage in duplicative and abusive discovery. 

Plaintiff's arguments are inapposite. The January 31, 2024, Order (which is the subject of the instant motion) expressly states, “Plaintiff must serve code compliant responses within 20 days . . . and must substantially answer all subsections.” Thus, the purpose of the Court continuing the instant motion hearing was for the purpose of determining sanctions. The issue is whether monetary sanctions or evidentiary exclusion sanctions should be ordered. The fact that AEG's counsel also represents the other defendants in this case does not excuse Plaintiff from answering discovery propounded by AEG in this case. Plaintiff decided after almost three years to sue AEG as a new defendant. Thus, AEG is entitled and has a right to complete its own discovery. Second, the argument that AEG "already has the responses" is disingenuous because when represented by its prior counsel, Los Angeles City received the same evasive nonresponsive responses to its form interrogatories. 

The fact Plaintiff refuses to pay monetary sanctions on at least two prior occasions reinforces the argument that the only sanctions the Court should order are evidentiary sanctions. Fourth, although Plaintiff states she summarized the future medical care with the information her medical providers noted in their medical records, this is precisely what the January 31, 2024, Court order said not to do: the Court ordered Plaintiff to serve interrogatory responses “without the option to produce writings under CCP 2030.290(a).” Moreover, it said Plaintiff must substantially answer all sub-sections. Finally, Opposing Counsel’s argument that he was unable to communicate with Plaintiff due to her medical issues is made without providing any competent evidence legitimizing Plaintiff’s medical status and Plaintiff has repeatedly made this argument on previous occasions related to discovery. 

APPLICABLE LAW

CCP section 2023.030 provides that, "[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method..., the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose... [monetary, evidence, and terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process...." CCP section 2023.010 provides that "[m]issues of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following:... (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.... (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery...." 

"The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse 'after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.'" (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390 (quoting Lang v. Hachman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246).) "Generally, '[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.'" (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 (citation omitted).) 

"Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders." (Id. (citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1244- 1246); see, e.g., Collisson X Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 (terminating sanctions imposed after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery); Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal App 3d 481, 491 (disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCucchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4) (terminating sanctions imposed against plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes).) 

“The court may impose an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Section 2023.030 subd. (c).) The court may also impose an issue sanction ordering that the designated facts shall be established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process." (Code of Civil Procedure Section 2023.030 subd. (b).) 

DISCUSSION

On January 31, 2024, this Court, after having heard argument of counsel, made the following ruling: 
Defendant AEG Management LACC, LLC's Motion to Compel Responses from Plaintiff Christina Gutierrez to Interrogatories, Sets One; and Request for Sanctions filed by AEG Management LACC, LLC on 10/25/23 is Granted. Plaintiff is ordered to provide code- compliant responses within 20 days of this order. Interrogatory responses are to be served without objections and without the option to produce writings under CCP 2030.290(a) and must substantially answer all sub-sections. 
Defendant AEG Management LACC, LLC's Motion to Compel Responses from Plaintiff Christina Gutierrez to Request for Production of Documents, Set One; and Request for Sanctions filed by AEG Management LACC, LLC on 10/25/23 is Denied as Moot. 
Defendant's request for sanctions is Denied. 
Defendant is ordered to give notice.
Defendant argues that although, Plaintiff served verifications to her further amended responses on March 29, 2024, Plaintiff’s responses to AEG's Form Interrogatory No. 6.7 failed to substantially answer the 6.7 sub-sections; were incomplete, and were still evasive. 

Form Interrogatory No. 6.7, approved by the Judicial Council, asks for relevant information: 
“Has Any HEALTH CARE PROVIDER advised that you may require future or additional treatment for any injuries that you attributed to the INCIDENT? If so, for each injury state: 
(a) the name and ADDRESS of each HEALTH CARE PROVIDER; (b) the complaints for which the treatment was advised;
(c) and the nature, duration, and estimated cost of the treatment.” 
(See Separate Statement filed concurrently hereto; Lopez Dec 3) 

Plaintiff responded: 

RESPONSE TO FORM INTERROGATORY NO. 6.7:

Yes, including, re-surgery of her left knee, total knee replacement for her right knee, and surgeries to her neck and back. Discovery and investigation are ongoing. Responding party reserves the right to amend and/or supplement this response.
(See Separate Statement filed concurrently hereto; Lopez Dec para 19, 20). 

Defendant argues that an evidence preclusion sanction simply prohibits Plaintiff from producing at trial the evidence that she repeatedly refused to produce in discovery. (Def. Supp. Brief, pg. 8.)  "[T]he discovery process is designed to make a trial less of a game of blindman's bluff and more of a fair contest with the basic issues and facts disclosed to the fullest practicable extent." (Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 377, 389 (disapproved on other grounds by Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204).) “Absent extenuating circumstances... the appropriate sanction when a party repeatedly and willfully fails to provide certain evidence to the opposing party as required by the discovery rules is preclusion of that evidence from trial—even if such a sanction proves determinative in terminating plaintiff's case.” (Id. at 390.) 

Thus, Defendant contends there is a reasonable nexus between the evidentiary sanction, i.e. to exclude evidence of any future treatment to the neck and back, and the interrogatory question Plaintiff refuses to answer as ordered by the Court. (Def. Supp. Brief, pg. 8.) Moreover, it has been evident throughout this litigation’s history that monetary sanctions directed to Plaintiff do not have the desired effect in making Plaintiff comply with discovery requests. (Lopez Dec, para 35) Defendants have obtained at least two separate prior monetary sanctions orders against Plaintiff which Plaintiff has not paid despite being ordered to do so. (Def. Supp. Brief pg. 9.)

Although the Court acknowledges Plaintiff’s medical status, the Court agrees that Plaintiff’s Counsel has not provided documentation legitimizing Plaintiff’s inability to communicate during the relevant time. Although Plaintiff claims Defendant is propounding excessive discovery, Plaintiff has not provided evidence of this and even if Plaintiff had provided evidence of discovery responses in other cases, the Court finds only this case before it. As Plaintiff is the party bringing suit, Plaintiff finds herself in Court by her own intents and purposes. Thus, Plaintiff is subject to the rules of procedure and must fairly engage in the discovery process if she wishes to avail herself to the process and administration of justice.

The Court was clear in setting forth in the January 31, 2024 Order that Plaintiff was to provide Interrogatory responses without objections and without the option to produce writings under CCP 2030.290(a). The Order was also clear in providing that Plaintiff must substantially answer all sub-sections. As Plaintiff failed to timely abide by the Court’s orders, the Court finds evidentiary sanctions appropriate in this circumstance.

Given Plaintiff did not comply with the January 31, 2024 Court Order, it is now appropriate to preclude Plaintiff from introducing, referring to, or mentioning any evidence at trial that she will either: (a) need; or (b) has been advised she will need future treatment, including surgery, to her neck and back. This preclusion shall serve to exclude any treatment obtained or incurred as of the date of the Order for the instant motion. 

In light of awarding evidentiary sanctions, the Court will forego awarding monetary sanctions at this time and hereby orders and enforces the January 31, 2024 Order, as well as all other Court Orders, past and future.

CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Enforce the January 31, 2024, Court order and hereby awards the foregoing evidentiary sanctions.

BACKGROUND
On March 2, 2022, Plaintiff Gideon Victor (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Etan Boritzar (“Defendant”) alleging a cause of action for Negligence. The Complaint arises from an automobile accident which occurred on March 4, 2020. (Complaint, ¶ 9.) 

On August 16, 2023, a Final Status Conference was scheduled and there was no proof of service filed showing service on Defendant. (08/16/23 Minute Order.) The Court placed the Final Status Conference off calendar and set an OSC re: Failure to File Proof of Service for October 27, 2023. (08/16/23 Minute Order.) 

On October 27, 2023, the OSC re: Failure to File Proof of Service was not held as Plaintiff requested additional time to serve Defendant. (10/27/23 Minute Order.) The Court continued the OSC re: Failure to File Proof of Service to March 4, 2024. (10/27/23 Minute Order.) The Court advised counsel that it “intend[ed] to exercise its discretion to dismiss this case, at the continued OSC date for Plaintiffs failure to file a Proof of Service within two years per CC P583.410 and 583.420(a)(1), as ordered by this Court.” (10/27/23 Minute Order.) 

On March 4, 2024, the Court continued the OSC to May 1, 2024 as Plaintiff requested additional time to file proof of service. (03/04/24 Minute Order.) 

On April 29, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Lynne M. Hobbs to the Honorable Ian C. Fusselman sitting in Department T at Alhambra Courthouse effective May 13, 2024.   

On May 1, 2024, no appearances for Plaintiff were made at the OSC re: Dismissal for Failure Serve/Prosecute and the Court ordered the Complaint dismissed without prejudice. (05/01/24 Minute Order.) The Court noted that proof of service had not been filed. (05/01/24 Minute Order.) 

On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Dismissal. The Court notes that the motion sets forth Department 30 as the department in which the motion will be heard, even though this action was reassigned to this department prior to the filing of the motion. Moreover, no proof of service was filed with the motion. Although Defendant has not yet been served with the Complaint and was not served with the instant motion, the Court does not find that the aforementioned procedural irregularities prevent the Court from ruling on the motion or that ruling on the motion will prejudice Defendant. 
 
DISCUSSION

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding was taken.” (Ibid.) “It is well settled that appellate courts have always been and are favorably disposed toward such action upon the part of the trial courts as will permit, rather than prevent, the adjudication of legal controversies upon their merits.” (Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 525.)  

Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) also contains a mandatory relief provision. (SJP Limited Partnership v. City of Los Angeles (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 511, 516.)  “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment; or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Ibid.) “If the prerequisites of the mandatory relief provision of section 473, subdivision (b) exist, the trial court does not have discretion to refuse relief.” (Ibid.) 

“Whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, the court shall direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties, in addition to whatever additional fees are authorized by subdivision (c), of Code Civ. Proc. § 473.” (J.A.T. Entertainment, Inc. v. Reed (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 1485, 1491.) Whenever the court grants relief from a dismissal based on any grounds in Code Civ. Proc. § 473, the Court may: (1) impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party; (2) direct that an offending party pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund; or (3) grant other relief as is appropriate.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(1)(A)-(C).)    

Analysis 

A. Appropriateness of Setting Aside the Dismissal 
Counsel for Plaintiff, D. Hess Panah, Esq. (“Panah”), declares that the OSC in this matter escaped entry on his firm’s calendar because his firm hired an appearance attorney due to being double booked in departments on March 4, 2024. (Panah Decl., p. 6:10-11.) Counsel declares that the new OSC date for the matter was not calendared and therefore his office missed the hearing on May 1, 2024. (Panah Decl., p. 6:12-13.) Counsel declares that this was an excusable error and there was no undue prejudice caused to either party. (Panah Decl., p. 6:15-16.) 

The Court finds that counsel has shown that the dismissal of this action was due to counsel’s mistake and inadvertence in failing to properly calendar this matter. The Court finds that the motion is also timely. Plaintiff has shown grounds for relief under both the mandatory and discretionary prongs of CCP § 473(b). The Court will not require Plaintiff to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees or penalties under CCP § 473(c). 

CONCLUSION
  
Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is GRANTED. Plaintiff to give notice.
