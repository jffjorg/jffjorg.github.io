I.            INTRODUCTION

On September 30, 2019, plaintiff Jose R. Rocaleon (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Harley-Davidson Motor Company Group, LLC (“Harley-Davidson”) (erroneously sued as Harley-Davidson Motor Company, Inc.), TMCHB, Inc. dba Huntington Beach Harley-Davidson (“TMCHB”) (erroneously sued as The Motorcycle Company, LLC), and Precision Performance, LLC dba TNT Cycle Works.

On October 11, 2023, Harley-Davidson and TMCHB (collectively, “Defendants”) filed this motion to seal evidence submitted in support of their motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication. Defendants also filed a redacted version of their motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication.

On January 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a brief opposing this motion to seal.

On January 11, 2024, Defendants filed a reply brief in support of their motion to seal.

On January 19, 2024, the Court continued this hearing to February 9, 2024, so that Defendants could lodge an additional copy of the proposed materials to be sealed and file supplemental evidence. Defendants lodged and filed the Declaration of Leah Orloff and a supplemental brief on February 2, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

Pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 2.550 the court may seal a record “only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)

A “reasoned decision about sealing or unsealing records cannot be made without . . . (1) identifying the specific information claimed to be entitled to such treatment; (2) identifying the nature of the harm threatened by disclosure; and (3) identifying and accounting for countervailing considerations. The burden of presenting information sufficient to accomplish the first two steps is logically placed on the party seeking the sealing of the documents, who is presumptively in the best position to know what disclosures will harm and how.” (H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 894.)

III.        DISCUSSION

Defendants state that “[c]ourts should find business information confidential and entitled to protection from public view where the party asserting confidentiality has taken measures to guard the information’s secrecy and where the material contains information valuable to the business or its competitors.” (Motion, p. 4.)

Defendants filed a supplemental declaration from Leah Orloff which attached five exhibits (“Supplemental Orloff Declaration” or “Supp. Orloff Decl.”). In the Supplemental Orloff Declaration, Leah Orloff, a Principle Engineer in Product Integrity at the Harley-Davidson Product Development Center, describes multiple documents which she states contain proprietary instruction, including: a pre-delivery inspection checklist for the motorcycle at issue in this action (2018 FXLR) regarding the preparation of motorcycles for delivery (Ex. AA), engineering drawings and seat renderings for the 2018 FXLR (Ex. BB-CC), and a durability test report for the 2018 FXLR (Ex. EE). Orloff states that these documents are highly sensitive and would place Harley-Davidson at a competitive disadvantage if they were available in the public record. (Supp. Orloff Decl., ¶ 4.) Orloff also states that certain information regarding Harley-Davidson’s manufacturing process, design, and warranty search refer specifically to the sequential steps that are taken to develop the 2018 FXLR and bring the motorcycle to market. (Supp. Orloff Decl., ¶ 5.) Orloff states that the information and evidence is maintained with the strictest confidence within the Harley-Davidson organization and by its manufacturers and suppliers, and that access to this confidential information is limited to prevent it from entering the public domain. (Supp. Orloff Decl., ¶ 8.)

The Court must identify the specific information to be sealed in order to grant Defendants’ motion to seal. (H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 894 [reasoned decision about sealing or unsealing records cannot be made without identifying the specific information claimed to be entitled to such treatment].) The notice of Defendants’ motion to seal requested an order sealing unspecified “portions of” their summary judgment motion’s memorandum of points and authorities and separate statement of undisputed facts as well as the entire declaration of Leah Orloff in Support of their summary judgment (“Orloff MSJ Declaration”) and the attached exhibits. That is why the Court previously ordered Defendants to file an amended notice identifying each proposed item to be sealed. The Court will not scour through more than a hundred pages in search of a redaction, then cross-reference another hundred pages to determine whether the proposed redaction is appropriate, especially when the entire motion is submitted as one exhibit without physical tabs or electronic bookmarks distinguishing the memorandum of points and authorities, declarations and their exhibits, and the separate statement.  

But even if an amended notice was filed, there are several substantive issues with the motion to seal. First, the Supplemental Orloff Declaration is too conclusory to justify a sealing order. Orloff provides no facts regarding the steps purportedly taken to keep this information confidential or that access to this information is actually limited, such as using password-protected databases or requiring employees to nondisclosure agreements.

Second, the Court is unable to conclude that the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored. For example, Defendants request “portions of” the Orloff MSJ Declaration to be sealed, but there are entire pages of the declaration which are redacted. Similarly, Exhibit AA is 28 pages long and is completely redacted. The Court and Plaintiff cannot avoid referring to entire Exhibit AA or most of Orloff’s declaration when discussing Defendants’ summary judgment motion. (CRC 2.551(c).) Defendants must make some effort to identify the specific information which it truly finds confidential. For example, whether the motorcycle seat is composed of one or several pieces of foam is not “confidential and proprietary information”, whereas the composition of that foam or the purpose of the seat’s design might qualify. The Court will not permit large swathes of this dispositive motion to be filed under seal based solely on the agreement or stipulation of the parties. (CRC 2.551(a).)

IV.         CONCLUSION

In light of the foregoing, the hearing is continued once more to _________________. Defendants are to file an amended notice of motion identifying each particular item within their motion for summary judgment and supporting documents which they propose to be sealed. The notice of motion shall serve as an index for the Court to review the proposed items to be sealed. Each item shall be described by document, page, and line (or exhibit and line and Bates-number, if applicable). For example, if there are specific lines in the separate statement of undisputed facts that are to be sealed, Defendants shall state: Separate Statement, page number at line numbers. A more substantive declaration from an individual with personal knowledge must also be submitted to address each item specifically and explain why sealing of that particular item is necessary. A proposed order incorporating this index shall also be filed with the Court together with a version of that proposed order in Word format.

At its next hearing the Court will address whether the proposed items identified in this amended notice, and only such items, should be sealed. If Defendants – in the course of their preparation of this index – determine upon reflection that any part of their motion need not or cannot be sealed, Defendants shall omit any description of that part from the amended notice.

If an amended notice of motion is not filed least five days before the date of the hearing, the motion will be denied without prejudice and all lodged copies will be returned to Defendants. (CRC 2.551(b)(6).)

          On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff Lyco 1, LLC filed this motion for an order to Truong Ngoc (“Ngoc”) to show cause why Ngoc should not be held in contempt for willfully disobeying a deposition subpoena. The deposition subpoena was issued to Ngoc on August 30, 2023, and personally served on her on September 7, 2023. The deposition was scheduled for September 28, 2023. It is undisputed that Ngoc did not appear for her deposition.

On February 1, 2024, Ngoc filed a “response” to this motion, in which her attorney, Dan Hogue, states that Ngoc’s failure to attend the deposition was due to his understanding that the deposition would not be proceeding.

Mr. Hogue explains that on September 13, 2023, he conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel, Shun C. Chen, in preparation of a joint status conference statement. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 7.) Mr. Hogue states that he had informed Plaintiff’s counsel of a preexisting mediation commitment on September 28, 2023, and that he would be unavailable for Ngoc’s deposition. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 7.) Following this, the parties did not discuss whether the deposition would proceed. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 8.)

On November 21, 2023, Ngoc filed a cross-complaint.

On December 20, 2023, Mr. Chen sent an e-mail to Mr. Hogue requesting to schedule the depositions of Ngoc and defendant/cross-complainant Michael Cheng for January 5 and January 8, 2024. (Response, Hogue Decl., Ex. D.) However, Mr. Hogue soon experienced various medical issues. Following the week of Christmas, Mr. Hogue began experiencing flu-/cold-like symptoms. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 11.) Mr. Hogue consulted with his primary doctor because Mr. Hogue had a history of pneumonia and on January 3, 2024, Mr. Hogue advised Mr. Chen of his condition and inability to accommodate the depositions on January 5 and January 8, 2024. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 14.) Mr. Chen acknowledged Mr. Hogue’s unavailability and wished him a swift recovery. (Response, Hogue Decl., Ex. E.) On January 5, 2024, Mr. Hogue was informed by his primary doctor that his symptoms were consistent with those for respiratory syncytial virus (“RSV”) and instructed to limit his activities. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 15.)

Upon Mr. Hogue’s return to work, Mr. Hogue learned that Michael Cheng had also been experiencing severe health concerns including hospitalization for and recovery from abdominal surgery, with a second surgery scheduled on February 2, 2024, for pancreatic cancer. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 16.)

Mr. Hogue also states that Ngoc is an 82-year-old woman in poor health who has been advised by her cardiologist to avoid unnecessary stress. (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 17.) Mr. Hogue requests that any scheduled deposition “show appropriate concern for her age and medical condition, such as limited the number of hours per day she will be required to testify.” (Response, Hogue Decl., ¶ 17.)

On February 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief objecting to the untimeliness of Ngoc’s “response” and requesting that the Court “order Ngoc to appear for a deposition on a date certain.” (Reply, pp. 2-3.) Plaintiff states that reasonable accommodations will be provided. (Reply, p. 3.)

Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2020.220, subd. (c).) A deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena may be punished for contempt without the necessity of a prior order of the court directing compliance by the witness. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2020.240.)

          The Court exercises its discretion to consider Ngoc’s untimely response and, as an initial matter, questions the necessity of Plaintiff’s motion. The parties apparently discussed whether Mr. Hogue’s was available on September 28, 2023, concluded that he was not, and there is no indication of Plaintiff’s intent to proceed with the deposition after this discussion. Furthermore, in early January, the parties were already making efforts to schedule Ngoc and Michael Cheng’s depositions.

Therefore, instead of issuing an OSC re: contempt, the Court orders Ngoc to comply with the deposition subpoena and appear for her deposition within the next 30 days. The Court expects the parties to meet and confer regarding a future deposition date for Ngoc and any reasonable accommodations that may be required. If the parties fail to agree on those accommodations, Ngoc may file a motion for a protective order.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On August 2, 2023, plaintiffs Faheem Asmar Ali (“Ali”) and Gryphon Security Service, LLC (“Gryphon Security”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendant Tesla, Inc. dba Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Tesla”). Plaintiffs assert causes of action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act and allege that Tesla has failed to comply with its warranty and repair obligations in connection with Plaintiffs’ leased 2022 Tesla Model 3 (the “Subject Vehicle”).

On September 5, 2023, Defendant filed this motion for an order compelling Plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims.

On January 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition to Defendant’s motion.

On February 2, 2024, Defendant filed its reply brief.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

When seeking to compel arbitration, the initial burden lies with the moving party to demonstrate the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of evidence. (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841-42; Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-65.) It is sufficient for the moving party to produce a copy of the arbitration agreement or set forth the agreement’s provisions. (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of evidence any defense to enforcement of the contract or the arbitration clause. (Ruiz, supra, 232 Cal.App.4th at p. 842; Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at p. 165.) The trial court then weighs all the evidence submitted and uses its discretion to make a final determination. (Id.)

III.        REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Tesla requests judicial notice of Plaintiff’s Complaint.  The Court grants the request pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d).

IV.         DISCUSSION

In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the Court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541; Victoria v. Superior Court (1985) 40 Cal. 3d 734, 835.) “[A]rbitration is a matter of contract and a party cannot be required to submit to arbitration any dispute which he has not agreed so to submit.” (AT&T Technologies v. Communications Workers (1986) 475 U.S. 643, 648 (citations and internal quotations omitted); see also Sparks v. Vista Del Mar Child & Family Services (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 1511, 1518 [“Because arbitration is a contractual matter, a party that has not agreed to arbitrate a controversy cannot be compelled to do so”].)

Tesla has met its burden to show that an arbitration agreement exists. The lease agreement between Plaintiff and Tesla (“Lease”) requires arbitration of “any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you [Plaintiff] and Tesla”. (Kim Decl., Ex. 1.) The arbitration will be “administered by the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules” and Tesla will pay all AAA fees. (Id.) Ali’s electronic signature is on the Lease as an authorized signatory of Gryphon Security. (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs do not dispute that the arbitration agreement in the Lease is signed or authentic, but solely argue that the agreement is unenforceable because it is procedurally and substantively unconscionable. (Opp., pp. 2-8.) Unconscionability generally includes the absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms that unreasonably favor the other party. (Carboni v. Arrospide (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 76, 82-83.) Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element.” (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486.)  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both procedural and substantive unconscionability is shown. (Stirlen v. Supercuts, Inc. (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1533.) Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is “oppression” arising from an inequality of bargaining power or “surprise” arising from buried terms in a complex printed form. (Id. at 1280.)  Substantive unconscionability addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms. (Id.)

Plaintiff has the burden of proving both procedural and substantive unconscionability. (Crippen v. Central Valley RV Outlet. Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1165). Both, however, need not be present to the same degree. A sliding scale is applied so that the “more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Morris v. Redwood Empire Bancorp (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1317.)

Plaintiffs argue that the Lease is an adhesive contract because it was offered on a “take it or leave it” basis without any meaningful opportunity to negotiate with Tesla regarding any of the terms. But even if it were offered on a take it or leave it basis, the contract is not adhesive because it includes an opt-out provision which allows the Plaintiffs to opt out of the arbitration agreement within 30 days. (Kim Decl., Ex. 1.) Plaintiffs also argue that the arbitration agreement is unconscionable because the Consumer Arbitration Rules were not attached to the agreement. But Tesla informs the consumer of two ways to obtain information about the Rules. The arbitration agreement states: “To learn more about the Rules and how to begin an arbitration, you may call any AAA office or go to www.adr.org.” (Kim Decl., Ex. 1.) This instruction, when coupled with opt-out provision, reduces any procedural unconscionability caused by the “adhesive” nature of the contract to a minimal amount.

Next, Plaintiffs argue that the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable. First, Plaintiffs claim the arbitration agreement allows the party electing arbitration to choose the arbitration organization and rules. (Opp., p. 6.) However, this is not the text of the agreement, which provides that the arbitration will be administered by AAA and under AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules. Courts have found AAA and its rules to be neutral and fair. (Lagatree v. Luce, Forward, Hamilton & Scripps (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1126-1127.) Second, Plaintiffs claim that the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable because the consumer is responsible or arbitration costs above $5,000. (Opp., p. 7.)  But the agreement provides that Tesla will “pay all AAA fees for any arbitration, which will be held in the city or county of your [Plaintiff’s] residence.” (Ex. 1 to Kim Decl.) Therefore, Plaintiffs fail to show how the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable.

 

V.           CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Tesla’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. The action is stayed pending the completion of arbitration. The Court sets a status conference for August 8, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. in Department 3 of the Alhambra Courthouse. A joint statement is to be submitted to the Court no later than five days before the hearing.
