DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV25399    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 10-7-24 (a/f 10-15-24 a/f 3-11-25)

Case: 20STCV38935 c/w 21STCV25399

Trial Date: Not Set

 

DISQUALIFY COUNSEL

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiffs, 828 Media Capital, et al.

RESPONDING PARTY:  Defendants, SSS Film Capital, LLC, et al.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Disqualify Counsel

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

20STCV38935

On February 11, 2020, Plaintiffs 828 Media Capital, LLC and Todd Lunbohm and Defendants David Brown, Clear Distribution, LLC, Clear Entertainment Inc., and Fallout, LLC, entered into “various agreements” for the financing and production of a “film” entitled “Fallout.” The terms required a “commitment amount” of $1,500,000 from 828 Media, with a promised return of $1,800,000 at or prior to the maturity date established under the terms. The parties also executed a separate “side letter,” whereby the 828 Media and Clear Distribution, LLC agreed to an equal distribution of “any and all sales” following distribution. “throughout the world.”

 

Following investment of $285,000, the project shut down due to Covid restrictions. New terms were orally agreed upon whereby 828 Media would only invest $365,000. Plaintiff denies ever executing any written amended contract.

 

Meanwhile, production “unilaterally” continued without the knowledge of 828 Media and its principal, plaintiff Lundbohm, until the fourth day of principal photography. Plaintiff was subsequently presented a follow-up offer to return the $285,000 loan proceeds, due to new investors joining the project. Plaintiffs maintains the resumption of production without notice, along with other actions by Defendants, violated the terms of the contract.

 

On October 9, 2020, Plaintiffs filed a complaint for Breach of Contract, Breach of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, Breach of Fiduciary Duty, Aiding and Abetting, and Promissory Fraud. A 170.6 challenge to the assigned officer led to a transfer of the action to Department 68.

 

On January 11, 2021, the case was ordered to arbitration. On May 30, 2023, the court lifted the stay on the matter.

 

On April 3, 2024, defaults were entered as to Clear Distribution, LLC, David Brown, and Fallout, LLC.

 

21STCV25399

In the subject action, Plaintiffs 828 Media Capital, LLC, 828 Productions, LLC, and Todd Lunbohm allege said failure of the other referenced financing agreement with Fallout, LLC, et al. was the result of new financing obtained from Defendants SSS Film Capital LLC. In exchange, SSS Film Capital LLC was purportedly provided a “first position producer credit” in direct conflict with the 828 Plaintiffs’ agreement, as well as sales commission agreement. Plaintiffs also allege a similar commission agreement with Defendant SSS Entertainment LLC. Both commission agreements purportedly violate the 50% commission agreement with the 828 plaintiffs.

 

On July 9, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint for Intentional Interference with Contract, and Declaratory Relief. On October 8, 2021, Defendants filed a notice of removal to federal court. On November 6, 2023, the United States District Court, Central District, filed a notice of remand.

 

On January 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint for Intentional Interference with Contract; Fraudulent Conveyance; False UCC-Filings (Cal. Comm. Code § 9625); Conversion;  Breach of Contract; Breach of Fiduciary Duty; Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty;  Fraud; Declaratory Relief;  Judicial Foreclosure on Collateral; and, Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage. Plaintiffs added new defendants Shaun Sanghani, Fallout LLC; Clear Entertainment, Inc.; Clear Horizon Entertainment, LLC; Clear Distribution LLC; David Raymond Brown F/K/A David Brown F/K/A David Brown Levy; and, Warner Bros. Pictures.

 

On February 14, 2024, the court deemed the cases related.

 

On February 28, 2024, the court entered the stipulation for leave to file a second amended complaint. On March 13, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their second amended complaint: 1. Intentional Interference with Contract 2. Fraudulent Conveyance 3. False UCC-Filings (Cal. Comm. Code § 9625) 4. Aiding and Abetting False UCCFilings (Cal. Comm. Code § 9625) 5. Conversion 6. Breach of Contract 7. Breach of Fiduciary Duty 8. Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty 9. Fraud 10. Declaratory Relief 11. Judicial Foreclosure on Collateral 12. Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage. Plaintiffs added in Warner Bros. Pictures (WB) on grounds that the SSS defendants contracted with WB for “worldwide distribution” of the film for $5,000,000, thereby violating Plaintiffs’ contract with the Fallout defendants. Plaintiffs depends on a conspiracy allegation among the parties.

 

On May 30, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Warner Brothers Pictures from the first cause of action for Intentional Interference with Contract without prejudice.

 

RULING: Denied

Request and Supplemental Notice for Judicial Notice: Granted in Part/Denied in Part

The court takes judicial notice of the filed pleadings for the existence of the documents, but not for the truth of the matter asserted in any item. The court will not take judicial notice of any proposed pleadings. The court also takes judicial notice of its prior orders.

 

Defendants SSS Film Capital LLC, SSS Entertainment LLC and Shaun Sanghani, move to disqualify counsel for Plaintiffs 828 Media Capital, LLC, 828 Productions, LLC, and Todd Lunbohm. Defendants bring the motion on grounds that the previously obtained emails constitutes confidential information thereby materially prejudicing Defendants. Plaintiffs in opposition contends the instant motion constitutes the second effort at disqualification; the first motion was rejected by the arbitrator. Plaintiffs maintain extreme prejudice in terms of the volume of work required for new counsel and the incurrence of over $500,000 in attorney fees already billed on the subject motion. Plaintiffs deny the emails constitute a basis of the operative first amended complaint based on the ruling of the District Court in granting leave to amend. Plaintiffs conclude with the delays in bringing the motion constituted improper dilatory conduct. Defendants in reply contend the opposition fails to refute core basis of the motion—lack of notification, continued use of the privileged information, and refusal to return the items. Defendants minimize any claims of prejudice caused from disqualification, and deny any sought after tactical advantage in bringing the motion. Prejudice weighs heavier towards defendants than Plaintiffs. Defendants deny any estoppel since the case is not at issue.

 

“Motions to disqualify counsel present competing policy considerations. On the one hand, a court must not hesitate to disqualify an attorney when it is satisfactorily established that he or she wrongfully acquired an unfair advantage that undermines the integrity of the judicial process and will have a continuing effect on the proceedings before the court. On the other hand, it must be kept in mind that disqualification usually imposes a substantial hardship on the disqualified attorney's innocent client, who must bear the monetary and other costs of finding a replacement…” (Gregori v. Bank of America (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 291, 300; see Oaks Management Corporation v. Superior Court (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 453, 467.)

 

“‘[D]isqualification is proper as a prophylactic measure to prevent future prejudice to the opposing party from information the attorney should not have possessed’; an affirmative showing of existing injury from the misuse of privileged information is not required. (Citation.) A trial court, however, may not order disqualification ‘“simply to punish a dereliction that will likely have no substantial continuing effect on future judicial proceedings.”’” (McDermott Will & Emery LLP v. Superior Court (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 1083, 1120.)

 

In the August 22, 2024, minute order, the court specifically found the emails constituted privileged information, and no waiver of any privilege occurred as to the other jointly participating persons and entities, including moving defendants, upon the dissemination of the emails by a single cooperating party. The issue of intentional versus inadvertent disclosure was not addressed by the parties, and the court declined to sua sponte address the issue purposes of ruling on the privilege determination itself. The subject motion however requires consideration of the notification requirements from Plaintiffs’ counsel upon receipt of the information.

 

Defendants correctly cite to the standard regarding inadvertently disclosed confidential information, and receiving attorney obligations to both inform counsel and refrain from using said information. “[T]he obligation of an attorney receiving privileged documents due to the inadvertence of another is as follows: When a lawyer who receives materials that obviously appear to be subject to an attorney-client privilege or otherwise clearly appear to be confidential and privileged and where it is reasonably apparent that the materials were provided or made available through inadvertence, the lawyer receiving such materials should refrain from examining the materials any more than is essential to ascertain if the materials are privileged, and shall immediately notify the sender that he or she possesses material that appears to be privileged. The parties may then proceed to resolve the situation by agreement or may resort to the court for guidance with the benefit of protective orders and other judicial intervention as may be justified. We do, however, hold that whenever a lawyer ascertains that he or she may have privileged attorney-client material that was inadvertently provided by another, that lawyer must notify the party entitled to the privilege of that fact.” (State Compensation Ins. Fund v. WPS, Inc., supra, 70 Cal.App.4th at pp. 656–657.)

 

The court cannot factually determine whether the forwarding of the emails constituted an inadvertent act or not within the presented confines of the motion. Plaintiff represents Brown intentionally forwarded the materials and agreed to the recording of the conversation, which supports a finding of intentional conduct. A review of the applicable law presents no particularly significant distinction between inadvertent versus intentional disclosure in terms of both the required notification and lack of use to the material disadvantage of the aggrieved party(ies). (See Clark v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 37, 48, 54; Rico v. Mitsubishi Motors Corp. (2007) 42 Cal.4th 807, 818-819.) Thus, no matter the means of receipt, the court finds Plaintiffs’ counsel was still obliged to notify Defendants of the content given Brown was in no way entitled to waive the privileges of the joint parties.

 

Defendants extensively note the incorporation of the provided content into the operative complaint before any determination of privilege, which Plaintiff disputes as to scope. The ruling of the District Court while purportedly downplaying the reliance in no way mitigates the impacts from possession of the knowledge in later proceedings. Dismissal of the impacts by Plaintiffs based on a conditional sentence in a ruling on a motion for leave to amend disingenuously downplays the scope of the impact and considerations for relief. The reliance on the disclosed information in and of itself, regardless of any after the fact determination of privilege as to the non-consenting parties in no way relieved Plaintiff of any consequences from utilizing the information before engaging in proper review and notification protocols. While the court finds the information privileged, the existence of the information and improper response to receipt of the information in no way presumes automatic disqualification. (McDermott Will & Emery LLP v. Superior Court, supra, 10 Cal.App.5th at p. 1121 [The trial court ... could not base disqualification solely on ... violation of ... State Fund duties, but also must determine whether there was a likelihood the violation would impact the outcome of the Action[] and the public's trust in both the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar].)

 

Disqualification depends on a finding of the misuse of improperly obtained information and prevention of the misuse of said information. (McDermott Will & Emery LLP v. Superior Court, supra, 10 Cal.App.5th at p. 1120.) The information was not acquired through any wrongful means, and in fact voluntarily disclosed by a joint party. While Plaintiffs’ counsel facilitated the recording of the conversations disclosing the strategies, Plaintiffs can in no way “unlearn” the furnished information. Knowledge of the purported agreement(s) and planned strategy, while extremely useful to Plaintiffs was also not something readily if ever discoverable, and therefore becoming part of the court record. The court therefore finds a significant material advantage to Plaintiffs. To downplay the blatant use of the information obtusely disregards a core link to the case. [Declarations of Crystal Jonelis, Shaun Sanghani.] (Clark v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th at p. 55.) The sharing of information by Brown was in no way required, and only benefits Plaintiffs. (See Roush v. Seagate Technology, LLC (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 210, 225-226.)

 

Nevertheless, Plaintiffs’ now familiarity with the information in no way prevents Plaintiffs from passing along the subject information to new counsel should the court disqualify current counsel. While the information never originated from Plaintiffs, the court finds the public policy concerns remain the same. (In re Complex Asbestos Litigation (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 572, 592; see Neal v. Health Net, Inc. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 831, 841; Bell v. 20th Century Ins. Co. (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 194, 198.) While Plaintiffs’ counsel clearly violated the notification standards, the public policy factors require consideration of paramount concern for judicial integrity and impacts.

 

The court, in its discretion must consider the balance between the “genuine likelihood” of impacts to the outcome of the proceeding balanced against the “‘paramount concern... to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar.’” (Clark v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 37, 55 accord (People ex rel. Dept. of Corporations v. SpeeDee Oil Change Systems, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1145.) “[T]o allow continued representation of a client after counsel has been provided with, and then used, improperly obtained confidential information would undermine the public's trust in the fair administration of justice and the integrity of the bar.” (Militello v. VFARM 1509 (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 602, 622.)

 

Plaintiff raises a valid counter in that motions for disqualification should not be sanctioned as means of obtaining potential strategic gains in litigation. Replacement costs constitute a significant factor. (Gregori v. Bank of America (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 291, 300-301.) Plaintiff also challenges the timing of the motion as a form of seeking to gain a tactical advantage. (Liberty National Enterprises, L.P. v. Chicago Title Ins. Co. (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 839, 847.)

 

The motion comes well after arbitration, where the information was disclosed. The arbitration began in 2022, and the arbitrator in fact considered a prior motion for disqualification presented by Brown. [Declaration of Vikram Arimtraj, Ex. 1.] Defendants in reply however deny any participation in said arbitration in the related cases of Fallout LLC v. 828 Media Capital LLC (20STCV38935). Defendants also deny any association with 828 Medical Capital LLC, et al v. Brown, et al (22STCV36609) or 828 Media Capital LLC v. Brown, et al. (22STCV27225).

 

Following arbitration in the related case, the subject case motion for leave to amend came before district court on October 31, 2022, where the court granted leave to amend. The action was pending in October 2021. [Supplemental Declaration of Crystal Jonelis.]

 

The disclosed information was considered by the court, but the privilege determination motion was deferred until the return to the superior court. Following the return to the superior court, Defendants filed special motions to strike, demurrers, and the privilege determination motion. The subject motion to disqualify was only filed after the privilege determination hearing and order.

 

In ruling on the determination of the privileged materials itself and considering the subject motion to disqualify, the court finds no specific requirement of the preceding determination of privilege before consideration for disqualification. The issue of possession of privileged material can readily be addressed in a motion to disqualify.

 

While it’s not clear if Defendants were aware of the privileged material prior to the October 31, 2022, hearing for leave to amend, the court again finds no dispute as to the direct notification no later than this date. The extensive progression of items leading up to the subject motion demonstrates a course of conduct apparently somewhat accepting of the acquired, privileged information before this latest decry of outrage.

 

Given the prophylactic standard for the motion, Defendants show no earlier prioritized efforts to disqualify counsel before the case continued for nearly two more years following the district court hearing. While Defendants deny any tactical advantage intent, the delays in bringing the instant motion somewhat undermine a finding of support for a party seeking true cautionary relief, as opposed to some form of retribution. (McDermott Will & Emery LLP v. Superior Court, supra, 10 Cal.App.5th at p. 1120.)

 

The court also finds no basis to discount the representation of counsel in the resources dedicated to the subject case albeit even if in conjunction with the other identified actions. Disqualification in the subject action would most certainly complicate representation in coordinating with the related and unrelated cases (even assuming the other parties also didn’t seek disqualification as well).

 

In balancing the “paramount” public policy concern of judicial integrity, relative to the other public policy goal of providing prophylactic relief and avoidance of retroactive imposition of punishment for strategic gain, the court finds the circumstances undercut the basis for requested disqualification. (Clark v. Superior Court, supra, 196 Cal.App.4th at 55.) The formation of the possibility of an unfair proceeding effectively appeared during the time before the federal court, thereby invoking any requirement to seek disqualification at that time, or at certainly earlier than before filing the other number of motions before this court. The circumstances therefore demonstrate an (implied) acceptance of the known information, and undercuts findings of impropriety or lack of integrity with the judicial process. Later declared unfair advantage must be tempered by conduct. The hardship imposed on Plaintiff after almost two years of litigation weighs significantly in the determination in the consideration of the court. The court finds the prejudice to Plaintiffs outweighs any perceived improprieties reflecting a lack of judicial integrity in the adjudication of the subject action, and offers insufficient prophylactic relief given the procession of the case, even if not at issue yet.

 

The motion is therefore DENIED.

 

The order denying the motion is directly appealable. (Apple Computer, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1263-1264.) The court can stay the action for a limited period of time, if Defendants wish to appeal. “A petition for extraordinary relief on the merits accompanied by a request for an immediate stay is preferable, because generally extraordinary writs are determined more speedily than appeals. The specter of disqualification of counsel should not be allowed to hover over the proceedings for an extended period of time for an appeal.” (Reed v. Superior Court (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 448, 455.) Defendants may request a stay for the purpose of the filing of a writ.

RULING: Granted.

Defendant, Marvin Engineering Co., Inc. moves for pro hac vice admission of attorney Andrew Kolozsvary.

 

Pro hac vice admission in California is governed by California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40.  To be admitted pro hac vice, one must be “a member in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States.”  (California Rules of Court, rule 9.40(a).)  However, in no case shall an attorney appear pro hac vice if the attorney is a resident of California, regularly employed in California, or “regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.”

 

An attorney seeking pro hac vice admission must file a verified application in both court and the State Bar of California establishing:

 

(1) [t]he applicant's residence and office address; (2)[t]he courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission; (3)[t]hat the applicant is a member in good standing in those courts; (4)[t]hat the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5)[t]he title of court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and (6)[t]he name, address, and telephone number of the active member of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.

 

(California Rules of Court, rule 9.40(d).)

 

The application complies with California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40. Andrew Kolozsvary has not sought admission within the last two years, and practices within the State of Michigan. The application includes proof of payment to the California State Bar on a August 26, 2024. [Declaration of Jeffrey Huron.]

 

The application for pro hac vice admission is granted.

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Bewind Company Limited alleges a $1,067,854.66 balance due against predecessor  Mobileessentials LLC, and seeks to collect on said balance against Defendant Solaray, LLC, following Defendant’s acquisition of Mobileessentials LLC with all attendant liabilities.

 

On March 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint for (1) Breach Of Contract (2) Common Counts (3) Open Book Account (4) Account Stated (5) Unjust Enrichment. Defendant answered on April 19, 2023.

 

RULING: Denied.

Defendant, Solaray, LLC moves to compel further responses to Request for Production to of Documents (set one), numbers 1-36. Defendant summarizes the purported responses in the separate statement, but the actual omnibus declaration incorporating multiple sets of discovery items across separately filed motions lacks any actual copy of any responses. Notwithstanding, Defendant challenges the objections. Plaintiff in opposition contends Defendant failed meet and confer prior to filing the motion, Plaintiff served full verified responses to numbers 1-5 and 26-33 with 473 pages of documents, and the motion should otherwise be denied and sanctions imposed in favor of Plaintiff. The court electronic filing system shows no reply on file at the time of the tentative ruling publication cutoff.

 

"Whether a particular response does resolve satisfactorily the issues raised by a motion is a matter best determined by the trial court in the exercise of its discretion, based on the circumstances of the case. In many cases involving untimely responses, the propounding party will take the motion off calendar or narrow its scope to the issue of sanctions. If the propounding party proceeds with the motion, however, the trial court has the discretion to rule on the motion. The trial court might compel responses without objection if it finds no legally valid responses  have been provided to one or more interrogatories; it might deny the motion to compel responses as essentially unnecessary, in whole or in part, and just impose sanctions; it might treat the motion as one under section 2030.300 and either determine that further answers are required, or order the propounding party to “meet and confer” (§ 2030.300, subd. (b)) and file a separate statement (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1020(a)(2), (c)); or it might take the motion off calendar, thereby requiring the propounding party to file a motion under section 2030.300.”

 

(Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 408–409.)

 

The court declines to consider the propriety of the responses to numbers 1-5 and 26-33. [Declaration of William Niu, Ex. D.] If Defendant elects to challenge the electronic signature verification, the court will set a separate hearing, but otherwise accepts the verification for purposes of the subject motion.

 

On numbers 6-25, the court finds no evidence of any meet and confer effort prior to filing the subject motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2).)

 

The motion is therefore denied as moot on numbers and 1-5 and 26-33, and denied on the validity of the objections as to numbers 6-25, due to the lack of any meet and confer effort.

 

The court declines to impose sanctions in favor of Plaintiff given the supplemental responses were still only served after the filing of the motion. Plaintiff therefore conceded some validity to the motion and should not recover sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

QUASH

 

MOVING PARTY: Specially Appearing Defendant, Ramiro Perez

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Carmen Solis

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Quash Service of the Summons

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On April 3, 2024, Plaintiff Carmen Solis filed a complaint against Zacatecas YNJ, LLC and Ramiro Perez for 1. Negligence; 2. Premises Liability 3. Negligent Hiring, Supervision, And Retention. 4. Intentional Infliction Of Emotional Distress; 5. Assault And Battery.

 

RULING: Denied.

Specially Appearing Defendant Ramiro Perez moves to quash service of the summons and complaint on grounds of invalid service. Defendant contends service was improperly attempted on a person present on the premises, and said person was not an agent or authorized person to receive service. Plaintiff in opposition represents personal completion of service on August 18, 2024. The court electronic filing system shows no reply on file at the time of the tentative ruling publication cutoff.

 

A plaintiff has the initial burden to establish valid statutory service of a summons and complaint. (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413; Dill v. Berquist Const. Co., Inc. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1439-40; Floveyor Internat. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 794.) “The return of a process server registered [under] Division 8 of the Business and Professions code upon process or notice establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return.” (Evid. Code, § 647.)

 

The instant motion to quash was filed on May 31, 2024. Perez contends the motion was improperly served on Soledad Rivera, a bartender employed on the premises, but in no way an authorized agent for service. No proof of service was on file prior to the filing of the motion.

 

The proof of service representing the August 18, 12024, service was filed on September 16, 2024. The proof of service is executed by a licensed process server. Plaintiff apparently completed new service following the filing of the motion. The court finds no dispute as to the proof of service executed after the instant motion to quash. The motion is DENIED.

 

Perez to file a responsive document within 10 days of the order.

 

Perez to give notice.
