Hearing Date:             March 15, 2024

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Gwendy Ketting

Responding Party:       Defendant Edward Castro dba New Starboard Attitude

MOTION FOR TRIAL SETTING

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

The MOTION FOR TRIAL SETTING is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

On 4/6/2023, plaintiff Gwendy Ketting (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Mike Shepard, Edward Castro dba New Starboard Attitude, and Does 1 to 100, asserting two causes of action for (1) general negligence and (2) premises liability.

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff tripped and fell due to a dangerous condition at Starboard Attitude, a bar in Redondo Beach, California. The alleged dangerous condition was a multiple-inch difference in height between the walking path leading to the entry of the bar and the bottom of the door allowing patrons entry to the bar. The defendants allegedly negligently owned, maintained, managed, and operated the bar, failed to correct the dangerous condition and failed to warn invitees of the dangerous condition.

 

On 8/14/2023, defendant Edward Castro dba New Starboard Attitude (“Defendant”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Roes 1 to 100, asserting causes of action for (1) indemnity, (2) apportionment of fault, and (3) declaratory relief.

 

On 12/27/2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for trial preference. 

 

On 2/15/2024, Plaintiff dismissed without prejudice defendant Mike Shepard.

 

On 3/5/2024, Defendant filed his opposition to the motion for trial preference.

 

On 3/11/2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to the opposition.

 

4/14/2026 is the date set for trial.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

 “A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings: ¶ (1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole. ¶ (2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).)

 

However, the Court “[i]n its discretion, … may also grant a motion for preference that is accompanied by clear and convincing medical documentation that concludes that one of the parties suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of survival of that party beyond six months, and that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (d).)

 

Once the trial court grants a motion for preference, the trial must be set within 120 days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (f).)

 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

           

No requests for judicial notice were filed.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

           

No separate document of evidentiary objections was filed. However, in his opposition, Defendant raises hearsay objections to the declaration and medical reports submitted in support of the moving papers.

 

DISCUSSION

 

As an initial matter, “[u]nless the court otherwise orders: ¶ (1) A party may file and serve a motion for preference supported by a declaration of the moving party that all essential parties have been served with process or have appeared.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (c).)

 

Here, the only remaining named parties are Plaintiff and Defendant.

 

Plaintiff has submitted proofs of service showing she served Defendant with the moving papers.

 

Plaintiff moves for trial preference under the discretionary provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 36 (“Section 36”), arguing that (1) she suffers from an illness or condition that raises substantial medical doubt of her survival beyond six months and (1) the interests of justice will be served by granting preference in this matter.

           

A “heightened clear and convincing proof standard is required for motions seeking discretionary grants of preference under subdivision (d), but not for motions seeking mandatory preference under subdivision (a) [of Section 36].” (Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534.)

 

“‘“Clear and convincing” evidence requires a finding of high probability [, or] evidence ... ‘“‘so clear as to leave no substantial doubt’; ‘sufficiently strong to command the unhesitating assent of every reasonable mind.’”’ [Citation.]” (Katie V. v. Superior Court (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 586, 594.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel attests to the following facts in support of the motion. Plaintiff has cancer and other serious conditions that raise substantial medical doubt about her survival beyond six months. (Motion, declaration of Mary L. Caruso (“Caruso Decl.”), ¶ 5; Exhibit A – a copy of Plaintiff’s relevant medical records.) On November 16, 2023, Plaintiff’s brother contacted counsel’s office to inform them that Plaintiff had been placed on life support and a breathing machine. (Caruso Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel’s office was also told that the Plaintiff had a dangerously low platelet count, was receiving blood transfusions, and her family feared she would not survive beyond six months. (Caruso Decl., ¶ 5.) Based on that medical diagnosis and prognosis, a trial setting is necessary to prevent prejudicing the Plaintiff’s interests since she may pass away or become too ill to participate in this lawsuit. (Caruso Decl., ¶¶ 6, 7.) 

 

In opposition, Defendant argues, among other things, that the motion does not satisfy the clear and convincing standard for the following reasons. First, it is based on hearsay information since an unnamed person in Plaintiff’s counsel’s office was allegedly contacted by an unnamed brother of Plaintiff who related information from an unknown source regarding Plaintiff’s medical condition. (Opposition, p. 3:5-17.) Second, the copies of medical records Plaintiff’s counsel has attached to her declaration do not show “any form of staging of the cancer [or] any prognosis,” any “indication she is receiving palliative care or in hospice,” and any “ongoing treatment and follow-up visit schedule[ed].” (Opposition, pp. 3:26-4:3.) Finally, Plaintiff has not submitted any document showing her alleged recent hospitalization wherein she was placed on life support and breathing machine. (Opposition, p. 4:7-10.) For those reasons, Defendant asks the Court to deny the motion.

 

In reply, Plaintiff’s counsel highlights several conditions in Plaintiff’s medical records to highlight Plaintiff’s health condition. Below are some of the highlighted facts.

 

·         Exhibit A, p. 5: 11/11/2023 – ED to Hosp-Admission

 

Gwendy Miranda Kelling is a 62 y.o. female who presents to the ED for evaluation of vomiting and shortness of breath. Patient presents with vomiting and shortness of breath. Patient has known history of cirrhosis and GI cancer.

 

·         Exhibit A, p. 248: 11/11/2023 – ED to Hosp-Admission:

 

Dr. Esmail clarified and expanded on Harold’s [i.e., Plaintiff’s brother’s] understanding. The doctor’s candid update included informing him that his sister’s (pts) recent gall bladder scan was negative for disease, but that her liver cancer was not operable. The doctor informed Harold that his sister was critically ill as indicated by her need for artificial life support that included mechanical ventilation, blood pressure medicines, and IV antibiotics for an infection of (as yet) undetermined origin that had caused her internal organs to go into shock. Additionally, she let Harold know that the neuro-intensivist had been consulted due to signs of seizure activity.

 

Harold was given the opportunity to ask questions, all of which were answered. He talked about his own awareness in recent changes he had observed in his sister due to disease progression, and lamented that he had hoped that she would be able to recover sufficiently to go home to spend ‘one last Christmas’ with her family. He expressed concern that she might be suffering and that the interventions currently occurring in the ICU may serve merely to prolong her life without benefit of returning her to a meaningful quality of life. The PC team offered empathy and validated his feelings.

 

Next, the team provided education on code status and suggested that it would be appropriate for the family to consider placing a DNR boundary on the patient’s care at this time.”

 

            As stated above, clear and convincing evidence requires a finding so clear as to leave no substantial doubt.

 

Here, as dire as Plaintiff’s condition appears to be paper, Plaintiff has not submitted a declaration from her doctors or other expert with requisite medical background, indicating substantial medical doubt of her survival beyond six months. Indeed, some of the medical records counsel has submitted have the notation “11/11/2023.” To the extent that notation represented November 11, 2023, then it has been at least five (5) months since that date and Plaintiff is still alive and the motion is silent as to her current condition.

 

Therefore, the Court denies the motion since Plaintiff has failed to meet her burden of showing she is entitled to a discretionary grant of preference under Section 36(b).

 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Documents (Set Three); and (2) Request for Production of Documents (Set Four) (collectively, the “RFPs”). Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b) provides that “[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”

Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a misuse of the discovery process includes making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery. A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct under Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a). Case Number: 23TRCV00285    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: SWP

CASE NO: 23TRCV00285 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE: PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO STRIKE COSTS, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, TAX COSTS Dept. P 8:30 a.m. June 26, 2024

Having considered plaintiffs’ motion, defendants’ opposition, and the associated declarations and exhibits thereto, the court rules as follows: the motion to strike costs or, alternatively, tax costs is DENIED.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On February 1, 2023, Plaintiffs Theodore Halkias and Jill Halkias (collectively, the “Plaintiffs”) filed an Unlawful Detainer Complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants Rory Kuhn, Je’Nai Kuhn (collectively, the “Defendants”), and Does 1 to 10.

On February 27, 2023, Defendants filed an Answer to the Complaint.

On June 8, 2023, after hearing oral argument and taking the matter under submission, the Honorable Deirdre Hill sitting in Department M of this Court granted Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings. (06/08/23 Minute Order.)

On October 5, 2023, the Court entered judgment in favor of Defendants and against Plaintiffs. Defendants were deemed the prevailing parties and were awarded possession of the property. (10/05/23 Judgment.)

On October 16, 2023, Defendants filed a Memorandum of Costs (Summary) in which Defendants sought $948.58 in costs.

On October 16, 2023, Defendants filed a Motion for Attorneys’ Fees, which Plaintiffs opposed. Defendants sought attorneys’ fees in the sum of $5,000.00 pursuant to the clause in the rental lease which indicated that the prevailing party shall be entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs not to exceed $5,000.00. (Motion for Attorneys’ Fees at p. 1:21-28.)

On January 12, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Douglas W. Stern sitting in Department B at Torrance Courthouse effective January 16, 2024.

On January 22, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court granted Defendants’ Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and awarded Defendants attorneys’ fees in the amount of $4,051.42. (01/22/24 Minute Order.)

On January 24, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable David K. Reinert sitting in Department P at Torrance Courthouse effective January 24, 2024.

On February 27, 2024, the Court entered an order indicating that, pursuant to the Motion for Attorneys’ Fees, “the Court concluded that the maximum award for attorneys fees and costs to the prevailing party for the matter at present would not exceed $5,000.00 collectively. Given Defendants’ memorandum of costs totaling $948.58, the Court grants Defendants motion for attorneys fees and awards $4,051.42 in attorney fees. Defendants Rory Kuhn and Je’Nai Kuhn are awarded $4,051.42 in attorneys’ fees, payable by Plaintiffs to Defense Counsel ‘Casey M. Law, PC’ and mailed or delivered to 1250 W. Glenoaks Blvd STEE [sic] #419, Glendale, CA 91201.” (02/27/24 Order at p. 1:22-28.)

On April 23, 2024, Defendants filed an Abstract of Judgment, which identified Defendants as judgment creditors and Plaintiffs as judgment debtors.

Also, on April 23, 2024, Defendants filed a Notice of Association of Counsel, which indicated that their counsel associated in Kevin Hermansen/Law Offices of Kevin P. Hermansen, P.C. as counsel for Defendants.

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an Application and Order for Appearance and Examination for Enforcement of Judgment as to Plaintiff Theodore Halkias.

On May 9, 2024, Defendants filed and served a Memorandum of Costs After Judgment, Acknowledgment of Credit, and Declaration of Accrued Interest (the “Costs Memorandum”). Defendants indicate that post-judgment costs total $6,275.71, of which $5,850.00 is for attorneys’ fees. (Costs Memorandum at ¶ 1(a)(7).)

On May 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed and served the instant Motion to Strike Costs, or in the alternative, Tax Costs (the “Motion”). Plaintiffs seek to strike or, alternatively, tax the Costs Memorandum. Plaintiffs take issue with Defendants’ attempted recovery of attorneys’ fees in the Costs Memorandum.

On May 30, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition to the Motion.

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff’s filed a reply.

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs initiated this unlawful detainer action based on a three-day notice to pay rent or quit for a property located at 1605 Magnolia Avenue, Manhattan Beach, CA 90266 (the “Property”). Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to pay rent for the Property pursuant to a written agreement between the parties.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

“The judgment creditor is entitled to the reasonable and necessary costs of enforcing a judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.040.) “Attorney’s fees incurred in enforcing a judgment are not included in costs collectible under this title unless otherwise provided by law.” (Ibid.) “Attorney’s fees incurred in enforcing a judgment are included as costs . . . if the underlying judgment includes an award of attorney’s fees to the judgment creditor pursuant to subparagraph (A) of paragraph (10) of Section 1033.5.” (Ibid.) Attorney’s fees are allowable as costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)

A “judgment creditor may claim costs as authorized by Section 685.040 by noticed motion” and such motion must be made before the judgment is fully satisfied “but not later than two years after the costs have been incurred.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080, subd. (a).) “The notice of motion shall describe the costs claimed, shall state their amount, and shall be supported by an affidavit of a person who has knowledge of the facts stating that to the person’s best knowledge and belief the costs are correct, are reasonable and necessary, and have not been satisfied.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080, subd. (b).) “The notice of motion shall be served on the judgment debtor. Service shall be made personally or by mail.” (Ibid.)

A judgment creditor may also claim attorneys’ fees through a memorandum of costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.070, subd. (a)(6).) “Before the judgment is fully satisfied but not later than two years after the costs have been incurred, the judgment creditor claiming costs under this section shall file a memorandum of costs with the court clerk and serve a copy on the judgment debtor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.070, subd. (b).)

“The judgment creditor may seek to recover attorney’s fees incurred in enforcing a judgment by filing either a memorandum of costs under section 685.070, subdivision (b), or a noticed motion under section 685.080, subdivision (a).” (David S. Karton, A Law Corp. v. Dougherty (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 133, 145.)

“[T]he items on a verified cost bill are prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred.” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum.” (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1267.) “Only if the costs have been put at issue must supporting documentation be submitted.” (Ibid.) “If the items appearing on a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to

tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Plaintiffs argue that the Costs Memorandum seeks attorneys’ fees even though the Court’s February 27, 2024 order states that the maximum amount of attorneys’ fees and costs would not exceed $5,000.00. Plaintiffs contend that Defendants are attempting to double dip in recovering their attorneys’ fees. Plaintiffs further argue that a motion for attorneys’ fees is required and the costs of enforcing the judgment were not reasonable or necessary.

Responding Party’s Opposition

On opposition, Defendants assert that the post-judgment attorneys’ fees are not limited by the underlying contract’s cap on fees. Defendants contend that post-judgment attorneys’ fees may be claimed by a cost memorandum after judgment and a noticed motion is not required. Lastly, Defendants argue that the time spent collecting the judgment was reasonable.

Moving Party’s Reply

Plaintiff restates its arguments from the motion.

Merits of the Motion

In support of the Motion, Plaintiffs’ counsel, Jason M. Stone (“Stone”), provides a declaration stating the following: on February 27, 2024, the Court filed and signed an order concluding the maximum award for attorneys’ fees and costs to the prevailing party would not exceed $5,000.00. (Stone Decl., ¶ 3.) On or about April 23, 2024, Defendants served Plaintiffs with a Notice of Association of Counsel. (Stone Decl., ¶ 4.) On or about May 9, 2024, Defendants filed the Costs Memorandum. (Stone Decl., ¶ 5.) As of May 9, 2024, Defendants did not contact Plaintiffs or Plaintiffs’ counsel for payment of the order and Plaintiffs never received a signed copy of the order or a notice of entry of judgment from Defendants. (Stone Decl., ¶ 6.) On May 10, 2024, Plaintiffs mailed overnight a $5,000.00 check to Defendants’ counsel to fully satisfy the order. (Stone Decl., ¶ 7.)

In opposition to the Motion, Defendants’ counsel, Casey Movsessian (“Movsessian”) provides a declaration. Counsel declares that on February 1, 2024, he e-mailed Plaintiffs’ counsel a notice of the Court’s ruling on the motion for attorneys’ fees, a copy of the proposed order, and a request that his client pay the fees. (Movsessian Decl., ¶ 4; Exh. A.) Mr. Movsessian declares that at no time did Mr. Stone ever respond to his e-mail, call him, or otherwise send payment for the fee award. (Movsessian Decl., ¶ 4) Counsel waited until April 23, 2024 before retaining the Law Office of Kevin P. Hermansen, P.C. to collect the fee award. (Movsessian Decl., ¶ 5.)

Kevin Hermansen (“Hermansen”) provides a declaration in opposition to the Motion, which sets forth his efforts collecting on the judgment in this action. (Hermansen Decl., ¶ 2-3.) It was not until after Mr. Hermansen filed the Costs Memorandum did he receive a check for the original

$5,000.00 fee award in the mail, which he returned to Mr. Stone with an explanation as to why it was being returned. (Hermansen Decl., ¶ 4; Exh. B.)

Plaintiff argues that, because the underlying lease agreement caps attorneys’ fees at $5,000.00, Defendants cannot obtain post-judgment attorneys’ fees. “[U]nder the merger doctrine the specific limitations of the contract . . . no longer apply once the judgment is entered, and instead, the operative question in considering whether fees are available as enforcement costs under section 685.040 is simply whether the judgment included attorneys’ fees awarded pursuant to contract because the ‘judgment extinguishes all further contractual rights, including the contractual attorney fees clause.’” (Nash v. Aprea (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 21, 32.) Here, neither the judgment nor the Court’s February 27, 2024 order set forth a limitation on Defendants’ ability to obtain post-judgment attorneys’ fees. Therefore, the contractual cap of $5,000 is inapplicable to attorneys’ fees expended in collecting a judgment.

The Court also rejects Plaintiffs’ argument Defendants must file a motion for attorneys’ fees to recover attorneys’ fees incurred to enforce the judgment. Pursuant to David S. Karton, A Law Corp. v. Dougherty, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at p. 145, Defendants may recover post-judgment attorneys’ fees through a memorandum of costs.

Lastly, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not met their burden in showing that the costs incurred in enforcing the judgment were unreasonable or unnecessary. The declaration of Plaintiffs’ counsel in support of the motion does not state that the costs claimed were unreasonable or unnecessary, and Defendant’s counsel supported the costs being sought.

Accordingly, the Motion is therefore DENIED.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Motion to Strike Costs, or in the alternative, Tax Costs is DENIED.

DHA OPPORTUNITY 1 LP, etc., Plaintiff, vs. STEVEN A. TIMPER, etc., et al., Defendants.

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT FOR DAMAGES

 Dept. P 8:30 a.m. June 26, 2024

 Having considered plaintiff’s motion and the associated exhibits, the court rules as follows: the motion for leave to file a first amended complaint is GRANTED.

 I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On February 5, 2024, Plaintiff DHA Opportunity 1 LP (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Steven A. Timper, Hilda Timper, Fidelity National Title Company, Infinity Escrow Company, Home Team Realty, Eric Mori Schaub, and Does 1 through 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Fraud and Deceit; (2) Negligence; (3) Negligent Misrepresentation; and (4) Breach of Written Title Insurance Policy.

On February 29, 2024, pursuant to a Request for Dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Defendant Fidelity National Title Company was dismissed from this action without prejudice.

On March 13, 2024, Defendants Steven A. Timper and Hilma Timper filed a joint Answer to the Complaint, as well as a Cross-Complaint for Declaratory Relief against Cross-Defendant Richard Ovalle.

On May 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant unopposed Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint for Damages (the “Motion”). Plaintiff seeks an order granting leave for Plaintiff to file a First Amended Complaint.

As of June 21, 2024, the Motion is unopposed. Any opposition to the Motion was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (CCP § 1005(b).)

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiff alleges wrongful actions concerning the purchase of real property. (Complaint, ¶¶ 31-32.) Plaintiff alleges that due to Defendants’ fraud and deceit, it was induced to pay more than the value of the property and was also induced to incur expenses in past and current unlawful detainer lawsuits. (Complaint, ¶ 32.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

The trial court has discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding.” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945, citation omitted.) “An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion.” (Ibid., citation omitted.)

Under Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, a separate declaration must accompany a motion for leave to amend and must specify: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) A party seeking leave to amend must attach a copy of the proposed pleading to the motion for leave to amend. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1).) A motion for leave to amend must also state what allegations are proposed to be added or deleted to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number the additional or deleted allegations or located. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(2)-(3).)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Plaintiff contends that a court shall liberally grant leave to amend or supplement a complaint so that the action may be tried fully on its merits in a single action.

Responding Party’s Opposition

No opposition filed as of June 21, 2024.

Moving Party’s Reply

No reply filed as of June 21, 2024.

Merits of the Motion

A. Procedural Defects in the Motion

Plaintiff argues that the proposed First Amended Complaint deletes certain parties no longer intended to be defendants in this action, substitutes a correct entity for an individual, deletes a claim on a title insurance policy or against a title insurer, but otherwise is based on the same facts as the Complaint. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 9:9-13.)

The Court finds that the Motion is procedurally sufficient and the declaration of H.G. Long (“Long”) in support of the Motion is compliant with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324. The declaration of counsel states: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier, as required by Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

The Motion is GRANTED.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint is GRANTED

DONALD PAYNE, Plaintiff, vs. REGINA BEACH, et al., Defendants.  CASE NO: 24TRCV00644
 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE: DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT
Dept. P
8:30 a.m.
 June 26, 2024

Having considered defendant’s demurrer, the court rules as follows: the demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff Donald Payne (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint for Negligence (the “Complaint”) against Defendants Regina Beach (“Beach”), EAN Holdings, LLC (“EAN”), and Does 1 through 10.

On May 13, 2024, Defendant Beach filed an Answer and an Amended Answer to the Complaint.

On May 14, 2024, Defendant EAN filed and served the instant unopposed demurrer to the Complaint.

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Complaint; however, the Amended Complaint was rejected by the Court on the grounds that a court order was required in order to file the Amended Complaint. (See 05/30/24 Notice of Rejection—Pleadings.)

The demurrer is unopposed. Any opposition to the demurrer was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (CCP § 1005(b).)

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiff alleges that on January 24, 2023, he suffered severe physical and psychological injuries due to an automobile collision caused by Defendant Beach. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) The collision occurred on a public roadway in Los Angeles County. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Beach drove her vehicle in a negligent, careless, reckless, and wanton manner which caused her vehicle to strike Plaintiff at a high speed. (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Defendant EAN contends that the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action as it is nothing more than conclusory allegations. Additionally, Defendant EAN argues that, under federal law, a rental car company and its affiliates cannot be held vicariously liable for the negligent acts of its customers.

Responding Party’s Opposition

No opposition filed as of June 21, 2024.

Moving Party’s Reply

No reply filed as of June 21, 2024.

Merits of the Demurrer

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has not been met. Defendant EAN’s counsel, Adam I. Miller (“Miller”), attests to attempting to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel by sending a meet and confer letter. (Miller Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. D.) Plaintiff’s counsel has not responded to the meet and confer correspondence and has not dismissed Defendant EAN. (Miller Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.)

Defendant did not meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by videoconference regarding the issues raised in the demurrer. As such, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has not been satisfied. The Court, however, will still consider the merits of the demurrer. The Court reminds the parties of the need to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure.

B. Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant EAN’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)

C. Negligence

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) A complaint must contain “[a] statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a)(1).)

The Court finds that the Complaint does not state a sufficient cause of action for negligence. (Complaint, ¶¶ 5-12.) Plaintiff does not allege the elements of duty, breach, or proximate cause as to Defendant EAN. The Complaint is merely a recitation of conclusions of fact. The Court does not accept conclusions of fact or law on demurrer pursuant to Simonyan, supra, 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895. The Complaint is simply conclusory. There are no specific facts alleged as to Defendant EAN.

The demurrer of Defendant EAN to the Complaint is SUSTAINED.

Due to the lack of sufficient factual allegations in the Complaint, the Court need not address the argument of Defendant EAN that it is not liable under federal law for the actions of the renters of its vehicles. However, the court also takes judicial notice that EAN is a rental car company and is not liable for the negligence of those who rent its vehicles. This provides the second basis for which to sustain the demurrer.

Moreover, given that the demurrer is unopposed, Plaintiff has conceded to the arguments raised therein as “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

D. Leave to Amend

The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) Plaintiff has not filed an opposition and has failed to show how the pleading can be successfully amended. Therefore, the court will not allow leave to amend.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer of Defendant EAN to the Complaint without leave to amend.
