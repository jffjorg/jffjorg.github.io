DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV04006    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: W

ACEVEDO, et al. v. JOE POTCHANARD CHAUVAPUN, M.D., et al.

 

DEFENDANTS' JOE POTCHANARD CHAUVAPUN, M.D., URGENTPOINT, INC. AND URGENTPOINT HEALTH, P.C. MOTION TO COMPEL KARINA CARVAJAL-FRAKT, AMFT TO COMPLY WITH SUBPOENA FOR BUSINESS RECORDS PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 1987.1

 

Date of Hearing:        January 30, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22STCV04006

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Joe Potchanard Chauvapun, M.D., Urgentpoint, Inc., and Urgentpoint Health, P.C.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Alejandra Acevedo

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (DeFalco Decl. ¶8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff alleges she and the decedent, John Francis McLaughlin, spoke about the decedent’s desire to rid himself of his dependency on alcohol and decided for the decedent to enter a 24-hour custodial care facility where he would undergo detoxification in a safe environment. Decedent went to West Health, Inc. for care. Upon being admitted, Defendants conducted a “telehealth” assessment of decedent. Plaintiff alleges, however, Defendants failed to perform an adequate assessment and the decedent passed away the next morning.

 

On February 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for: (1) Professional Negligence; (2) Abuse/Neglect of Dependent Adult; (3) Concealment/Intentional Misrepresentation; (4) Negligence Per Se; and (5) Wrongful Death.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants' Joe Potchanard Chauvapun, M.D., Urgentpoint, Inc. and Urgentpoint Health, P.C. Motion to Compel Karina Carvajal-Frakt, AMFT to Comply with Subpoena for Business Records Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Defendants Joe Potchanard Chauvapun, M.D., Urgentpoint, Inc., and Urgentpoint Health, P.C. moves this court for an order compelling the custodian of records for Karina Carvajal-Frakt, AMFT at Bay Area Mental Health to comply with the Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records. Defendants make the motion on the basis he documents sought are discoverable because (1) they are relevant to the subject matter of this litigation; (2) they are admissible or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence; and (3) they are vital to Defendants development of defenses to Plaintiff Alejandra Acevedo’s claims.

 

The service of a deposition notice, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.240, is effective to require any party deponent to attend, testify, and produce materials for inspection at a deposition. (CCP §2025.280(a).) To require the attendance and testimony of a non-party deponent, as well as his or her production of any document or tangible thing for inspection and copying, the party seeking discovery must serve on that deponent a deposition subpoena, pursuant CCP § 2020.010, et seq. (CCP §§ 2020.010(b), 2025.280(b); See also Sears, Roebuck & Co. v. National Union Fire Insurance Company of Pittsburgh (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1350 [discovery from nonparties is governed by CCP §§ 2020.010, et seq., and is primarily carried out by way of subpoena].)

 

If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production. (CCP § 2025.480(a).) If the court determines that the answer or production sought is subject to discovery, it shall order that the answer be given or the production be made on the resumption of the deposition. (CCP § 2025.480(i).)

 

Defendant argues Mrs. Acevedo has put her mental health records at issue in this litigation and has represented she will testify that she sought therapy as a result of her husband's death. As a result, Defendant is to the records to determine veracity of Plaintiff's claim and whether the therapy sought was related to her husband's death or for some other reason.

 

Plaintiff opposes the subpoena on the grounds it is overbroad. Plaintiff does not deny that Defendants are entitled to obtain the therapy records that are directly relevant to her treatment involving the grief of losing her husband. Plaintiff contends she simply requests that the scope of the subpoena be limited to the therapy received relating such treatment, and to ensure that additional, irrelevant and privileged information is not inadvertently disclosed. As a result, Plaintiff requests the court limit its scope to records that are directly relevant the treatment she received related to the grief of losing her husband. Additionally, Plaintiff requests the court order that she be allowed to first review the records and redact irrelevant information while keeping a privilege log.

 

Both parties cite In re Lifschutz (1970) 2 Cal.3d 415. Defendant argues the California Supreme Court held that "the compelled disclosure of relevant information obtained in a confidential communication does not violate any constitutional privacy rights of the psychotherapist." (In re Lifschutz (1970) 2 Cal.3d 415, 423.) As such, because Mrs. Acevedo has put her mental health records at issue in this litigation, then those records are discoverable as Plaintiff has tendered them in this litigation.

 

Plaintiff claims the California Supreme Court emphatically rejected the notion that the patient-litigant exception to the psychotherapist-patient privilege in Evidence Code section 1016 required a plaintiff to waive her right to privacy as to her entire medical history. (In re Lifschutz, supra, 2 Cal.3d at p. 435.) Specifically, the court in Lifschutz stated “communications which are not directly relevant to those specific conditions do not fall within the terms of section 1016's exception and therefore remain privileged.” (Id.) Accordingly, Plaintiff contends therapy provided relating to Plaintiff’s relationships with her co-workers, family members and friends have no connection to the therapy provided for Plaintiff’s grief and are thus protected from disclosure. Plaintiff further contends the records relating to the status and event of the current lawsuit are also unrelated to the therapy Plaintiff has received for the grief relating to her husband’s death.  Plaintiff also cites Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1379, for the proposition that she is entitled to withhold all discussions with her grief therapist about any subject other than her husband’s death. 

 

The court agrees that the records here should be produced. The court has now read all of the records as part of an in camera review it has conducted.  The court has determined that all of the records relate to treatment plaintiff received after her husband’s death, as part of counseling relating to her grief.  In the records, the plaintiff talks freely about her feelings about her husband’s death, how it has affected her in many aspects of her life and how she is dealing with it.  Her discussion of her feelings about the litigation is relevant as an alternate stressor and discoverable.  Her few discussions about other matters, e.g., work, friends, moving to a new apartment and her relationship with her mother, are all discoverable and do not unreasonably intrude on the privacy rights of third parties.   Because plaintiff discussed all of these issues with the therapist she sought out for grief therapy, these other matters are relevant to the efficacy of that treatment, whether plaintiff had other stressors in her life during the grieving period, the weight plaintiff placed on the loss of her husband as compared to other stressors in her life, and whether plaintiff’s emotional state has improved.  

 

Accordingly, Defendants' Joe Potchanard Chauvapun, M.D., Urgentpoint, Inc. and Urgentpoint Health, P.C. Motion to Compel Karina Carvajal-Frakt, AMFT to Comply with Subpoena for Business Records Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 is GRANTED.  The records are to be produced in unredacted format.

 


Case Number: 22VECV01754    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: W

TAYLOR COMMERCIAL PROPERTIES, INC., V. SHARI’S MANAGEMENT CORPORATION, ET AL.

 

MOTION TO VACATE DEFAULT JUDGMENT AGAINST DEFENDANTS; MOTION TO QUASH THE WRITS OF EXECUTION ENTERED ON MARCH 15, 2023 IN SACRAMENTO COUNTY AND JULY 27, 2023 IN LOS ANGELES COUNTY, AND VACATE ANY RELATED EXECUTION LEVY

 

Date of Hearing:        January 30, 2024                               Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01754

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Shari’s Management Corporation and Coco’s Restaurants, LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Taylor Commercial Properties, Inc.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Taylor Commercial Properties, Inc. alleges on December 6, 2010, Plaintiff and Defendant CoCo’s Restaurants, Inc., entered into a written lease to certain commercial property. On March 28, 2021, Defendant Shari’s Management Corporation entered into an agreement with Plaintiff amending the lease and confirming that Shari’s would pay certain revised based rent and deferred amounts owed to Plaintiff beginning July 1, 2021. Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached the lease agreement by failing to pay the full amount of monthly rent and charges as well as deferred rent and charges due under the lease. Plaintiff served Defendants with a Five Day Notice. After Defendants failed to cure the default, Plaintiff filed a complaint for unlawful detainer on July 25, 2022. Defendants surrendered possession of the Property on August 30, 2022. Defendants have still not paid the rent and charges due under the lease.

 

Request for entry of default was entered December 2, 2022.  The court entered default judgment against Defendants on February 24, 2023.

 

TENTATIVE RULLING

 

Defendants’ Motion to Vacate Default Judgment Against Defendants is DENIED.

 

Defendants’ Motion to Quash the Writs of Execution Entered on March 15, 2023 in Sacramento County and July 27, 2023 in Los Angeles County, and Vacate any Related Execution Levy is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    MOTION TO VACATE DEFAULT JUDGMENT AGAINST DEFENDANTS

 

Defendants Shari’s Management Corporation and Coco’s Restaurants, LLC move this court to vacate the default judgment granted against Defendants on February 24, 2023 based on mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.   

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473(b), an application for relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the moving party’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (CCP § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (CCP § 473(b).)¿ 

¿ 

“It is the policy of the law to favor, wherever possible, a hearing on the merits, and appellate courts are much more disposed to affirm an order where the result is to compel a trial upon the merits than they are when the judgment by default is allowed to stand and it appears that a substantial defense could be made. Stated another way, the policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.” (Weitz v. Yankosky (1966) 63 Cal.2d 849, 854–855.)¿

 

Defendants move to set aside the default and default judgment entered against them on the grounds inadvertence and excusable neglect can clearly be found due to James S. Bell, PC (Defendants’ prior counsel) not being properly notified of the instant action. Defendants further contend Defendants’ Manager of Legal Service, Tina Sohne, was unavailable due to a medical procedure from November to December 22 and Defendants inadvertently failed to request that a further answer be filed beyond the answer they had already served in the underlying unlawful detainer action (LASC No. 22VECV01044).

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion on several grounds. Plaintiff first opposes the motion on the grounds that if the motion seeks to set aside the default, it is time barred. Plaintiff contends the relief sought here is discretionary and the application for discretionary relief under Code of Civil Procedure Section 473(b) must be made "within a reasonable time" and "in no case exceeding six months" after the entry of the default. In the instant matter, the court entered the Defendants' default on December 2, 2022 yet Defendants waited nearly nine months (until August 22, 2022) to file the instant motion.

 

The court finds that the relief sought is mandatory, not discretionary. Because the relief sought is mandatory, the wording of the statute makes clear that the six-month time period runs from entry of the default judgment, not the original entry of default. (Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 297.) Here, Defendants submit the declaration of their former counsel that the failure to timely file an answer was one of mistake or inadvertence as they were unaware an answer was due. (Nash Decl. ¶6.) The court will discuss further below, however, why the attorney declaration is insufficient to show mandatory relief is required.

 

Plaintiff argues Defendants do not dispute that they were properly served with the Summons and Complaint. However, Defendants’ claim Mr. Nash did not receive the Summons and Complaint fails because he is not their attorney in this action and nothing in his declaration is relevant. Further, the declaration of Tina Sohne does not excuse Defendants' belated Motion. Plaintiff contends there is no explanation of why Ms. Sohne's absence resulted in Defendants' failure to respond to the complaint. Additionally, the declaration does not explain the eight months that lapsed between Ms. Sohne's return from medical leave in December 2022 and Defendants' Motion filed in August 2023.

 

Although not expressly stated, it appears Defendants are arguing that Mr. Nash should have been served the Summons and Complaint in this action. However, all Defendants have shown is that Mr. Nash was retained to represent Defendants in a separate matter. Mr. Nash never stated that he was authorized to accept service in this case or would have done so. (See CRC Rule, 1.21(a).) As for Ms. Sohne’s declaration, generally, excusable neglect and a basis for relief has been found where an attorney relies on an employee or calendaring system to calendar deadlines, and the employee or system fails. (Renteria v. Juvenile Justice, Dept. of Corrections & Rehabilitation (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 903, 911.) However, it is not clear by Ms. Sohne’s declaration that current counsel relied on her mistake or inadvertence.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Vacate Default Judgment Against Defendants is DENIED.

 

II.                  MOTION TO QUASH THE WRITS OF EXECUTION ENTERED ON MARCH 15, 2023 IN SACRAMENTO COUNTY AND JULY 27, 2023 IN LOS ANGELES COUNTY, AND VACATE ANY RELATED EXECUTION LEVY

 

Defendants Shari’s Management Corporation and Coco’s Restaurants, LLC move this Court to recall and quash the writs of execution entered on March 15, 2023 in Sacramento County and July 27, 2023 in Los Angeles County, and vacate any execution levy related to the aforementioned writs of execution based on their motion to vacate the default judgment entered against them.   Because the court denies the request to vacate the judgment, the motion to quash is denied. 

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV03504    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: W

MICHAEL CASTILLO v. ASTGHIK HAMBARDZUMYAN, et al.

 

Motion TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF plaintiff’s COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 30, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        23VECV03504

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Astghik Hambardzumyan and Arthur Hambardzumyan

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a dog bite that occurred on February 8, 2023. Plaintiff Michael Castillo alleges while working, Plaintiff encountered Defendants’ dog who was present without a leash. Plaintiff alleges the dog had escaped from the premises because of the Defendants’ negligence. As a result, of Defendants’ dog attacking Plaintiff, Plaintiff alleges he suffered injuries and bites to his left arm. On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants for negligence.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Astghik Hambardzumyan and Arthur Hambardzumyan’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Astghik Hambardzumyan and Arthur Hambardzumyan move this court for an order striking Plaintiff’s allegations of punitive damages on the ground the allegations are irrelevant, improper and not drawn in conformity with California law.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.)

 

To sufficiently plead a claim for punitive damages pursuant to Civil Code §3294, a plaintiff must satisfy circumstances of “malice, oppression, or fraud,” supported by facts alleged with sufficient particularity. (G.D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29.) These allegations are held to a heightened pleading standard: a plaintiff may not state a mere conclusion of law to support a cause of action. (Perkins v. Sup. Ct. (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6.) More importantly, the plaintiff may not simply "plead . . . a claim for damages in the language authorizing such damages." (Id.) While some conclusory statements may be permitted, they must make sense in the context of the Complaint taken as a whole. (Id.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff has prayed for punitive damages but has failed to plead specific facts that support such allegations. Specifically, Plaintiff makes conclusory allegations by simply stating Defendants’ conduct was willful and malicious by knowingly permitting the dog on the premises despite its dangerous and aggressive tendencies.

 

The court agrees the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. Punitive damages are “typically awarded for intentional torts” while “cases involving unintentional torts are far fewer.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188, 1212.) Code of Civil Procedure section 3294(c)(1) defines “malice” as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” While the complaint alleges Defendants were negligent, or even grossly negligent, in letting the dog escape without a leash, the complaint lacks specific facts which establish that Defendants’ conduct was so despicable that it warrants the imposition of punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff has not opposed the motion. The motion is therefore GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Plaintiff’s allegations regarding punitive damages. 


Case Number: 23VECV03895    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: W

JAMES GRIFFITHS v. VOLKSWAGEN GROUP OF AMERICA, INC.

 

DEFENDANT’s demurrer with motion to strike

 

Date of Hearing:        January 30, 2024                               Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03895

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff James Griffiths

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Kumar Decl. ¶¶5-6.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On September 6, 2023, Plaintiff James Griffiths filed a complaint against Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc., asserting causes of action for 1. Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and 2. Magnusson-Moss Warranty Act. Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff leased a new 2021 Audi e-tron and express warranties accompanied the lease of the vehicle. Plaintiff alleges the vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to, a malfunctioning door lock system.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Defendant’s Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of Status Certificates issued by California’s Department of Consumer Affairs to Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. certifying Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.’s compliance and ability to operate a state-certified arbitration program in California (RJN, Exh. 1). The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for Violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act on the grounds that the cause of action fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute causes of action, as well as California Code of Civil Procedure section 431.10(f) on the grounds that the cause of action is fatally uncertain against Defendant.

 

“Magnuson-Moss¿governs warranties for consumer products distributed in interstate commerce. It requires disclosures in connection with written warranties, regulates the substantive content of warranties, and establishes a federal cause of action for breach of a written or an implied warranty (15 U.S.C. § 2310(d)), among other provisions.¿Magnuson-Moss¿does not substitute federal law for state law of consumer product warranties, but instead supplements state law.”¿(Orichian¿v. BMW of N. Am., LLC¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1322, 1330.) “Magnuson–Moss¿‘calls for the application of state written and implied warranty law, not the creation of additional federal law,’¿except in specific instances in which it expressly prescribes a regulating rule.” (Daugherty v. Am. Honda Motor Co.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 833.)¿ Accordingly, the Daugherty court held that the “failure to state a warranty claim under state law necessarily constitute[s] a failure to state a claim under¿Magnuson-Moss.” (Id.) 

 

Defendant argues the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for violation of the Magnuson-Moss Act because Plaintiff failed to allege exhaustion of the Act’s informal dispute resolution requirement. Defendant contends VWGoA has made available a valid informal dispute resolution process through the Better Business Bureau Auto Line, which is recognized as an appropriate form of informal dispute resolution under the [Magnuson-Moss] Act’s requirements.  (Zingerman, D.D.S., P.C. v. Nissan North America, Inc. (N.D. Ill., Apr. 20, 2015, No. 14 C 7835) 2015 WL 1840952, at *3; RJN, Exh. 1.) Because Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege that he utilized the BBB Auto Line to attempt to resolve their claim before filing this lawsuit, Plaintiff has not stated facts sufficient to assert a cause of action under the Magnuson-Moss Act.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues VWGoA has not shown that its written disclosures relating to the “informal dispute resolution process” comply with the MMWA and other federal disclosure rules intended to protect consumers and as a result, VWGoA has not, and cannot, show that Plaintiff was required to participate in VWGoA’s alternate dispute resolution (“ADR”) program before filing suit. (See Carrillo v. BMW of North America, LLC (C.D. CA 2021) 543 F.Supp.3d 856, 861, holding the plaintiff was not “bound by BMW's informal dispute resolution procedure because the information about BBB Auto Line is not contained on the page on which the warranty text begins, and therefore is not “on the face of the warranty.” 16 C.F.R. § 703.1(h)(2).”) Moreover, Plaintiff reached out directly to VW prior to retaining counsel and initiating the instant litigation. However, Defendant wrongfully rejected Plaintiff's prelitigation repurchase request, and offers no evidence that it directed Plaintiff to its ADR program at that time when Plaintiff had no alternative recourse for redress.

 

Here, the court finds that Plaintiff has failed to plead facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act. The complaint is currently silent as to any facts confirming Plaintiff either adhered to informal dispute resolution prior to filing the complaint (which Plaintiff does not seem to contend) or facts excusing Plaintiff from such a requirement. Plaintiff must plead facts demonstrating that they either attempted to informally resolve the subject dispute prior to filing the complaint, that Plaintiff was not required to pursue informal dispute resolution due to Defendants’ failure to comply with the Federal Trade Commission rules (see see Carrillo v. BMW of North America, LLC (C.D. CA 2021) 543 F.Supp.3d 856), and/or doing so would have been futile. (See Carrillo v. BMW of North America, LLC (C.D. CA 2021) 543 F.Supp.3d 856) In re Toyota Motor Corp. Unintended Acceleration Marketing, Sales Practices, and Products Liability Litigation (C.D. Cal. 2010) 754 F.Supp.2d 1145, 1189.) The court notes, however, whether or not Plaintiff was, indeed, required to adhere to informal dispute resolution under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act prior to filing the complaint is an evidentiary determination which may not be determined on Demurrer.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905 [holding a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters].)   

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. moves to strike Plaintiff’s second cause of action under Mag-Moss fails as a matter of law. Plaintiff has not, and cannot allege, that he exhausted the informal dispute remedies available to her as required under 15 U.S.C. section 2310(a)(3) before bringing suit.

 

The motion to strike is DENIED AS MOOT. The court has sustained the demurrer with leave to amend.
