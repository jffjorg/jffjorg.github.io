DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01159    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: W

Faustino Pacheco Morales v. RAVIT ohana and Sharon Ohana

 

Motion TO INTERVENE

 

Date of Hearing:   November 14, 2024           Trial Date:       None set.

Department:         W                                       Case No.:         22VECV01159

 

Moving Party:             Prospective Intervenor Brenda E. Vargas

Responding Parties:    Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales (Plaintiff) providing contracting services to Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana (Defendants), totaling $74,860.00. According to the first amended complaint, Defendants have failed to pay Plaintiff for his services.

 

On August 13, 2022, Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales filed a complaint against Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana. On April 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative first amended complaint (FAC) against Defendants, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Common Counts, and (3) Quantum Meruit.

 

On November 9, 2023, this Court entered a default judgment against Defendants in the amount of $99,380.40. On March 12, 2024, this Court set aside and vacated the judgment entered against Defendants.

 

On November 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Acknowledgment of Assignment, wherein he transferred and assigned all title rights and interest in the judgment to his attorney Brenda E. Vargas (Ms. Vargas).

 

On March 25, 2024, Defendants filed both an answer to the FAC and a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff for Disgorgement.

 

On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Substitution of Attorney to substitute Ms. Vargas for himself. 

 

On August 28, 2024, Ms. Vargas filed the instant motion to intervene.

 

On October 30, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Prospective Intervenor Brenda E. Vargas’s Motion to Intervene is DENIED.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (d) provides the following: 

 

(1) The court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if either of the following conditions is satisfied:  

 

(A) A provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene. 

 

(B) The person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties. 

 

(2) The court may, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 387(d).)  

 

Whether the petitioner has an interest in the matter in litigation is a question of fact that must be determined by the court before leave to file is granted. (Muller v. Robinson (1959) 174 Cal.App.2d 511, 515; In re Yokohama Special Bank (1948) 86 Cal.App.2d 545.) The burden rests on the one seeking to intervene to show that this is a proper case for intervention. (Id. at p. 515.) 

 

With respect to permissive intervention, “[w]hen the proper procedures are followed, the trial court has the discretion to permit a nonparty to intervene in litigation pending between others, provided that (1) the nonparty has a direct and immediate interest in the action; (2) the intervention will not enlarge the issues in the litigation; and (3) the reasons for intervention outweigh any opposition by the parties presently in the action.”  (Noya v. A.W. Coulter Trucking (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 838, 842 (quoting Truck Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 342, 346).)  

 

“A trial court has broad discretion in determining whether to permit intervention. [Citations.]”
(City of Malibu v. California Coastal Com. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 897, 902.)

Ms. Vargas moves to intervene on the grounds that she has an interest in the litigation, as Plaintiff assigned his right to collect on a judgment or settlement to Ms. Vargas, which includes assigning Plaintiff’s interest in collecting on his alleged open invoices with Defendants. The assignment states that Ms. Vargas is to retain 80% of the assigned claim, with Plaintiff retain 20%. (Vargas Decl., Exhibit E.)

 

In this matter, on March 3, 2023, the State Bar of California filed disciplinary charges against Ms. Vargas. (Judicial Notice, Exhibits 1-2.) On May 6, 2024, the Supreme Court of California issued its final discipline, stating that Ms. Vargas was prohibited from practicing law for the first 18 months of her three-year probationary period starting June 5, 2024. (Id., Exhibit 3.) As of the date of this order, Ms. Vargas is prohibited from practicing law in California. 

 

Business & Professions Code, Section 6130 provides that:

 

            No person, who has been an attorney, shall while a judgment of disbarment or

suspension is in force appear on his own behalf as plaintiff in the prosecution of any action where the subject of the action has been assigned to him subsequent to the entry of the judgment of disbarment or suspension and solely for purpose of collection.

 

Here, Ms. Vargas has engaged in actions that fit the scenario delineated in Business & Professions Code, Section 6130. Ms. Vargas is seeking to intervene in the current action to collect on the interest transferred to her by Plaintiff. The assignment explicitly states that it is made for the purpose of pursuing Plaintiff’s collection claim. Additionally, Ms. Vargas’ motion states that Ms. Vargas seeks to “to stand in the ‘shoes’ of the Plaintiff to collect” on the disputed invoices. (Motion, page 5.) Lastly, the assignment was executed after the State Bar initiated its disciplinary proceedings against Ms. Vargas. These facts are sufficient for the Court to conclude that the assignment was made “solely for the purpose of collection,” in violation of Business & Professions Code, Section 6130. (See Wilde v. Superior Ct. of San Diego Cnty. (1942) 53 Cal.App.2d 168, 188 [“However, it might not be out of place to say that we have no sympathy with a disbarred attorney who attempts to evade the consequences of his disbarment by purchasing claims after he is disbarred and prosecuting them in court in his own name and propria persona.”].)

 

However, disregarding the application of Business & Professions Code, Section 6130, Ms. Vargas does not meet the standard for either intervention as a matter of right or permissive intervention. Ms. Vargas is attempting to intervene in the current matter to collect on the invoices that Defendants have allegedly not paid, given that Plaintiff assigned part of his interest in his claim to Ms. Vargas. (Vargas Decl., Exhibits D, E.) However, Plaintiff still retains a 20% interest in the financial outcome of the case, thus showing that Plaintiff can adequately represent Ms. Vargas’ interest because both have the similar interest of collecting on the alleged unpaid invoices. This is further supported by Ms. Vargas’ prospective complaint, which is based on the same facts and same causes of action as Plaintiff’s complaint. (Vargas, Decl.) Thus, Ms. Vargas does not have a right to intervene, and the Court, in its discretion declines to permit Ms. Vargas to intervene.

ANALYSIS

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.¿(Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley¿v.¿Oakshade¿Town Center¿(2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)¿ Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c);¿Villa v.¿McFarren¿(1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party¿moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element.¿(Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿(Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party's evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak.¿(See¿Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475;¿Salesguevara¿v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384, 387.)

¿ 

Once the¿moving¿party has met the burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party¿to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material facts exists as to a cause of action or a defense thereto.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(2).)

When¿a¿party¿cannot¿establish an essential element or defense, a court must grant a motion for summary adjudication.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (o)(1)–(2).) 

 

“If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be present, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (h).)

 

First Cause of Action – Manufacturing Defect

 

Moving Defendants contend this claim fails because Plaintiff fails to provide evidence that the Subject Vehicle contained a manufacturing defect at the time it left Moving Defendants’ possession or that the alleged defect caused Plaintiff’s injury. Plaintiff contends he shows evidence both that a manufacturing defect existed and that such defect caused his injury.

“A product has a manufacturing defect if it differs from the manufacturer's intended result or from other ostensibly identical units of the same product line.” (Barker v. Lull Engineering Co. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 413, 429.) The product must have contained the manufacturing defect when it left defendant's possession. (Doupnik v. General Motors Corp. (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 849, 859.) A plaintiff must plead and prove that defendant “produced, manufactured, sold, or was in some way responsible for the product.” (Garcia v. Joseph Vince Co. (1978) 84 CA3d 868, 874.) However, all participants in the chain of distribution of a defective product are strictly liable. (Peterson v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1185, 1188.) To show the manufacturing defect is the legal cause of injury, a plaintiff must show that the product’s defect was a substantial factor in causing the injury. (Soule v. General Motors Corp. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, 572.)

i.                    Whether the Product Contained a Manufacturing Defect When it Left Defendants’ Possession

Here, Moving Defendants meet their initial burden. First, the declaration of Christopher Previtire, the service director for Terry York, establishes that according to business records maintained by Terry York, the Subject Vehicle was originally distributed by Jaguar Land Rover Automotive, PLC on February 6, 2018, the Subject Vehicle was returned to Terry York after its initial lease, and that on April 18, 2021, Terry York found no defects or leaks upon inspection of the Subject Vehicle. (UMF 15–16; Previtire Decl. ¶¶ 4–8.) Moving Defendants state that on July 5, 2021, about two months after Plaintiff’s mother purchased the Subject Vehicle, Plaintiff’s mother presented the Subject Vehicle to Terry York for service complaining of a coolant leak. (Previtire Decl. ¶ 10; Moving Defendants’ Compendium of Exhibits (DCE), Exh. J.) Terry York verified a leak from the front coolant manifold pipe, removed and replaced the coolant manifold pipe and verified the repair with a successful coolant pressure test. (Previtire Decl. ¶ 10; DCE, Exh. J.) Further, the repair order submitted by Moving Defendants for a subsequent service of the Subject Vehicle on February 22, 2022 identified no leaks of any kind. (Previtire Decl. ¶ 11; DCE, Exh. L.) Accordingly, Moving Defendants contend Plaintiff has failed to show the Subject Vehicle contained any defect when it left Terry York’s possession or that any such defect caused Plaintiff’s injury. (UMF 3; DCE, Exh. R.) Moving Defendants have, thus, successfully negated the elements that a manufacturing defect existed at the time the Subject Vehicle left Defendants’ possession and that any such defect caused the engine fire that injured Plaintiff. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a triable issue of material fact.

 

To meet his burden, Plaintiff presents the declaration of Robert Little, former general sales manager and vehicle auditor for Jaguar Land Rover dealerships, who opines that the pre-owned inspection checklist provided by Moving Defendants for the Subject Vehicle indicates a defect in the Subject Vehicle because (1) a check box that states “Approval for Certification” remained unchecked and (2) the checklist indicates Terry York completed a recall repair on the Subject Vehicle. (Little Decl. ¶ 6; DCE, Exh. H.) In his declaration, Mr. Little also notes that the March 30, 2021 repair order lacks any mention of a cooling system pressure test performed on the Subject Vehicle during the certification process, which would be warranted, based on Mr. Little’s experience as a vehicle auditor, for a vehicle with a known prior cooling system defect such as the Subject Vehicle. (Little Decl. ¶ 9; DCE, Exh. G.) Further, Plaintiff submits evidence of the Subject Vehicle experiencing coolant leaks three times within a two-year period, including once during the original lease of the Subject Vehicle and twice during Plaintiff’s possession, further showing the existence of a defect. (Little Decl. ¶¶ 5, 7; Webb Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s attorney in his declaration states that this coolant system defect is a widespread issue with the 2018 Range Rover class of vehicles, the same model as the Subject Vehicle, and provides evidence that this is the subject of a separate class action suit filed in September 2024 against JLRNA. (Lingenfelter Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 3.) Moreover, Plaintiff submits an expert investigation report conducted by Sage Forensic Investigations, which found upon post-fire inspection of the Subject Vehicle that a leak or spray from the coolant lines, the same defect Plaintiff alleges, caused the engine fire that injured Plaintiff. (DCE, Exh. R.) As such, the evidence presented by Plaintiff is sufficient to show triable issues as to both whether the Subject Vehicle contained a manufacturing defect at the time the Subject Vehicle left Moving Defendants’ possession and whether this defect caused Plaintiff’s injury.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES summary judgment, or summary adjudication, as to the First Cause of Action.

 

Second Cause of Action – Design Defect

 

Moving Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to show that the Subject Vehicle’s design was a substantial factor in causing harm to Plaintiff. Plaintiff argues he has shown evidence that the Subject Vehicle’s defective coolant hoses were a substantial factor in causing the engine fire and explosion that injured him.

 

A manufacturer, distributor, or retailer is liable in tort if a defect in the manufacture or design of its product causes injury while the product is being used in a reasonably foreseeable way. (Cronin v. J.B.E. Olson Corp. (1972) 8 Cal.3d 121, 126-130.)

 

In California, courts apply two different tests to determine whether a product contains a design defect, the “Consumer Expectation Test” and the “Risk-Benefit Test,” but “[t]he crucial question in each individual case is whether the circumstances of the product’s failure permit an inference that the product’s design performed below the legitimate, commonly accepted minimum safety assumptions of its ordinary consumers.” (Soule v. General Motors Co. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, 568.) The Consumer Expectations Test is reserved for cases where the everyday experience of the product’s users allows for a conclusion as to whether the product’s design violated minimum safety assumptions. (Soule, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 567.) This test should not be used when the alleged design defect involves technical or mechanical details of a product or complex circumstances of an accident. (Ibid.; see Sparks v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 461, 473–474 [applying the consumer expectations test in a case involving the safety of asbestos-laden dust created by normal installation and removal processes].) The Risk-Benefit Test is used when an ordinary consumer would not know what to expect concerning design safety. (Soule, supra, at p. 562). Under the Risk-Benefit Test, a product may be found defective in design if a plaintiff proves the product’s design proximately caused his injury and the defendant fails to prove that “the benefits of the challenged design outweigh the risk of danger inherent in such design.” (Id. at p. 566, citing Barker v. Lull Engineering Co. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 413, 435.)

 

To show causation, a plaintiff must establish “a substantial probability that the design defect, and not something else, caused the plaintiff's injury.” (Stephen v. Ford Motor Co. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1373.) If the plaintiff proves the required elements, then the burden shifts to defendants to prove that the benefits of the Subject Vehicle’s design outweigh the risks of the design. (Soule, supra, 8 Cal.4th at pp. 572–573.) Several relevant factors may be considered by the jury in this secondary analysis, including but not limited to “[t]he likelihood that this harm would occur,” “[t]he feasibility of an alternative safer design at the time of manufacture,” [t]he cost of an alternative design,” and “[t]he disadvantages of an alternative design.” (Ibid.)

 

i.                    Proximate Cause

 

The Court notes that given the technical and mechanical nature of the Subject Vehicle’s coolant and engine components at issue, the Risk-Benefit Test applies to the instant case. (Soule, supra, at p. 562.) To support their contention that Plaintiff has failed to show proximate cause, Moving Defendants submit that Plaintiff’s investigative report identifies no design defect in the Subject Vehicle. (UMF 9, 10, 11.) Further, Moving Defendants state Plaintiff’s claims that other vehicles in the Subject Vehicle’s product line may have a propensity to catch fire are unsupported and insufficient to show that the Subject Vehicle’s design proximately caused the engine fire in this instance. (UMF 9, 10, 11.) Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s claim of a recall on the Subject Vehicle is false, and that Plaintiff provides no evidence of such a recall. (UMF 9.) Lastly, Moving Defendants state that even assuming Plaintiff has met the required elements of a design defect claim, Plaintiff has not alleged any safer alternative design, weighing against a finding of design defect. (UMF 12; Cronin Decl. ¶ 4; Exh. A.) Moving Defendants have met their initial burden to show no triable issue of fact as to whether the alleged design defect was a substantial factor in causing harm to Plaintiff. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact.

 

Plaintiff has met his burden to show a triable issue of material fact as to whether a design defect proximately caused Plaintiff’s injury. First, Plaintiff points to Moving Defendants’ March 30, 2021 repair order, which notes a recall of unspecified origin was completed on the Subject Vehicle, showing the existence of both a defect and a recall. (UMF 9, 10, 11; DCE, Exh. G.) Second, Plaintiff submits expert evidence in the form of an investigation report that, based upon a post-fire inspection of the Subject Vehicle, identified the cause of the engine fire as “a leak/spray from the coolant lines for the supercharger aftercooler at the right side of the engine compartment.” (DCE, Exh. R.) Third, Plaintiff submits evidence of the Subject Vehicle’s Vehicle History Report, which reflects three instances of coolant leaks within a two-year period, including once during the original lease of the Subject Vehicle and twice during Plaintiff’s possession. (Little Decl. ¶ 7; Webb Decl. ¶ 4.) Such evidence of a recall, persistent coolant leaks, and a coolant leak causing the engine fire that caused Plaintiff’s injury creates a triable issue of material fact as to both whether the Subject Vehicle’s design performed below the commonly accepted safety assumptions of ordinary users and whether the defect caused the engine fire that injured Plaintiff. (Soule, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 568.) Therefore, Plaintiff has shown the existence of triable issues of material fact as to the existence of a design defect and whether such a defect was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s injury.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES summary judgment, or summary adjudication, as to the Second Cause of Action.

 

Third Cause of Action – Negligence

 

Moving Defendants contend this claim fails because Plaintiff fails to show that Defendants breached the standard of care in inspecting or repairing the Subject Vehicle or that any alleged breach was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm. Plaintiff argues he has shown evidence of negligent inspection and repair to the Subject Vehicle that caused his injury.

 

To state a claim for negligence, a plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) A tort is a legal cause of injury only when it is a substantial factor in producing the injury. (Mitchell v. Gonzales (1991) 54 Cal.3d 1041, 1048-1054.) “Negligence law in a failure-to-warn case requires a plaintiff to prove that a manufacturer or distributor did not warn of a particular risk for reasons which fell below the acceptable standard of care, i.e., what a reasonably prudent manufacturer would have known and warned about.” (Anderson v. Owens-Corning Fiberglas Corp. 53 Cal.3d 987, 1002.)

 

i.                    Breach

 

In support of their contention that Plaintiff has shown no evidence of breach, Moving Defendants submit that upon return of the Subject Vehicle from the prior lessee, Defendants conducted an inspection of the Subject Vehicle using the Land Rover Select Certified Pre-Owned Inspection Checklist, which “memorializes all checks conducted, all vehicle features present, and any parts or subsystems in need of replacement or repair,” noted no defects in the Subject Vehicle’s engine cooling system, and certified the Subject Vehicle for resale. (UMF 14, 15, 16; Previtire Decl. ¶¶ 7–8; DCE, Exh. H.) Moving Defendants state that on July 5, 2021, about two months after Plaintiff’s mother purchased the Subject Vehicle, Plaintiff’s mother presented the Subject Vehicle to Terry York for service complaining of a coolant leak, and Terry York verified a leak from the front coolant manifold pipe, removed and replaced the coolant manifold pipe and verified the repair with a successful coolant pressure test. (Previtire Decl. ¶ 10; DCE, Exh. J.) Further, Moving Defendants submit that Plaintiff’s post-fire vehicle investigation report states nothing about Moving Defendants as to any breach of the standard of care and, thus, provides no basis that any such alleged breach proximately caused the fire in the Subject Vehicle. (UMF 3; DCE Exh. R.) This evidence is sufficient to meet Moving Defendants’ initial burden to show no breach of the standard of care owed to Plaintiff or that the breach proximately caused Plaintiff harm. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact.

 

Plaintiff has met his burden that a triable issue of material fact exists. First, despite Moving Defendants’ contention that its pre-certification inspection found no problem with the Subject Vehicle’s coolant system, Plaintiff shows contradicting evidence indicating a prior radiator coolant leak in October 2020, which Moving Defendants did not disclose to Plaintiff prior to sale, and no mention of checking the coolant hoses or conducting a cooling system pressure test during the pre-certification inspection. (Webb Decl. ¶ 9, Exh. 1; DCE, Exh. G.) Second, Plaintiff provides evidence that Plaintiff’s mother complained to Terry York two months after Plaintiff took possession of the Subject Vehicle of coolant leak similar to that reported in October 2020. (Little Decl. ¶ 7; Webb Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s complaint of a coolant leak so soon after the prior lessee’s complaint suggests Moving Defendants failed to exercise reasonable care in repairing the Subject Vehicle before resale. Third, Plaintiff provides an investigative report that, upon inspection of the Subject Vehicle, identified the cause of the engine fire as “a leak/spray from the coolant lines for the supercharger aftercooler at the right side of the engine compartment,” implicating the very components subject to Terry York’s repairs as the cause of Plaintiff’s injury. (DCE, Exh. R.) The evidence submitted by Plaintiff, thus, shows triable issues of material fact as to whether Moving Defendants breached their duty of care in repairing and reselling the Subject Vehicle and whether such breach was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s injury.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES summary judgment, or summary adjudication, as to the Third Cause of Action.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendants Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC and Terry York Motor Cars, LTD’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. Defendants Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC and Terry York Motor Cars, LTD’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

tavita v. Millbrae care center, llc

 

Motion to Transfer venue and request for sanctions

 

Date of Hearing:          November 14, 2024                Trial Date:       N/A     

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV02842

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Millbrae Care Center, LLC

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Richard Tavita

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action for dependent adult abuse by a long-term healthcare facility. On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff Richard Tavita (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Millbrae Care Center (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 200. The Complaint alleges Plaintiff suffered from various pressure injuries, sepsis, and other conditions under Defendant’s care, where Defendant failed to prevent and treat such conditions. The Complaint alleges Defendant was understaffed and failed to adequately train its staff, resulting in Plaintiff’s neglect. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Dependent Adult Abuse (Pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code § 15600, et. seq.), 2) Negligence, and 3) Violation of Residents’ Rights (Pursuant to Health and Safety Code §1430(b)).

 

On September 5, 2024, Defendant filed this Motion to Transfer Venue.

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed the opposition.

 

On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed the reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Court DENIES the Motion to Transfer Venue and Request for Sanctions.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 395, subdivision (a) states that in personal injury actions, venue is proper where the defendant resides or where the injury occurred, subject to the Court’s discretion to transfer. 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 397, the discretionary venue statute, provides that “the court may, on motion, change the place of trial . . . (a) [w]hen the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court” and “(c) [w]hen the convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice would the promoted by the change.” Subdivision (c) refers only to the convenience of non-party witnesses, and not the convenience of party witnesses and party-affiliated witnesses. (Rios v. Lacey Trucking Co. (1954) 123 Cal.App.2d 865, 868-869.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Motion to Transfer

 

            Defendant argues the case should be transferred to San Mateo County in the interests of justice and for the convenience of non-party witnesses. Defendant argues Millbrae Care Center is located in San Mateo County and its staff members and leadership live in San Mateo County or nearby counties. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, 4.) Millbrae Care Center does not provide care to its patients in Los Angeles County , and no staff members live in Los Angeles County. (Ibid.) Defendant argues that the specified employees have treated Plaintiff and will testify to the issues in this case.

 

            Plaintiff argues that the venue is proper under Code of Civil Procedure section 395, subdivision (a). Plaintiff argues Defendant’s “residence” is its principal place of business located at 13345 Ventura Boulevard Los Angeles, California 91423, within Los Angeles County. Further, Plaintiff argues Defendant failed to provide the required factual showing for motions under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c).

 

            A more extensive factual showing is required for motions for venue transfer based on the “convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 397, subd. (c).) Declarations must contain admissible evidence, not hearsay and conclusions. (Lieppman v. Lieber (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 914, 919; Tutor-Saliba-Perini Joint Venture v. Sup.Ct. (San Diego Unified Port Dist.) (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 736, 744.) Declarations in support must contain the following: 1) the names of each witness expected to testify for both parties, 2) the substance of their expected testimony, 3) whether the witness has been deposed or has given a statement regarding the facts of the case, 4) the reasons why it would be “inconvenient” for the witnesses to appear locally, 5) the reasons why the “ends of justice” would be promoted by transfer to a different county. (Juneau v. Juneau (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 14, 16.)

 

            The Court finds venue is proper as filed because Defendant’s principal place of business is in Los Angeles County. (Peck Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court further finds that Defendant has failed to make the required factual showing for motions to transfer venue under on Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c).

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff that Defendant’s declaration of Abby Ma, Administrator of Defendant, fails to meet the requirements set forth by Juneau v. Juneau (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 14, 16. The declaration identifies certain employees, but fails to state the substance of their expected testimony, whether they have been deposed or have given a statement, the reasons why it would be inconvenient to testify in Los Angeles County, and why “ends of justice” would be promoted by transfer to a different county. (See ibid.) Neither does the declaration of Defendant’s counsel, Richard M. Acosta meet these requirements. Even if it did, declarations from a party’s counsel regarding the convenience of the witnesses are usually hearsay or conclusions. (Rycz v. Sup.Ct. (McGarry) (2022) 81 CA5th 824, 846, fn. 22.)

 

            Further, for the purposes of determining whether to grant a motion for change of venue under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c), a court need not give consideration as to the convenience of witnesses who are employees of the litigants, unless the employee is called to testify by the opposing party. (See

Stute v. Burinda (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d Supp. 11, 17; J.C. Millett Co. v. Latchford-Marble Glass Co. (1959) 167 Cal.App.2d 218, 227.) Here, in support of its motion, Defendant speaks to the convenience of its own employees. There is no indication Plaintiff seeks to call these employees to testify. Thus, Defendant has failed to make the required factual showing for its Motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c).

           

Sanctions

 

            Defendant requests for attorney’s fees and costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 396b, subdivision (b). The Court finds that it is unresolved whether sanctions are recoverable under section 396b, subdivision (b). (See Lieppman, supra, 180 Cal. App. 3d at p. 919.) Either way, the Court finds sanctions are unwarranted where Defendant was unsuccessful in its Motion to Transfer. The Court thus denies Defendant’s request for attorney’s fees and costs.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Court DENIES the Motion to Transfer Venue and Request for Sanctions.

 tavita v. Millbrae care center, llc

 

Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay

 

Date of Hearing:          November 14, 2024                Trial Date:       N/A     

Department:               W                                             Case
No.:         24VECV02842

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Millbrae Care Center, LLC

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Richard Tavita

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action for dependent adult
abuse by a long-term nursing facility. On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff Richard
Tavita (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Millbrae Care Center
(“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 200. The Complaint alleges Plaintiff suffered from
various pressure injuries, sepsis, and other conditions under Defendant’s care,
where Defendant failed to prevent and treat such conditions. The Complaint
alleges Defendant was understaffed and failed to adequately train its staff,
resulting in Plaintiff’s neglect. The Complaint alleges the following causes of
action: 1) Dependent Adult Abuse (Pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code §
15600, et. seq.), 2) Negligence, and 3) Violation of Residents’ Rights
(Pursuant to Health and Safety Code §1430(b)).

 

On September 9, 2024, Defendant filed
this Motion to Compel Arbitration pursuant to arbitration clauses (the “Arbitration
Clauses”) Plaintiff purportedly signed with his admission agreement (the
“Admission Agreement”) on March 23, 2023. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2-4, Exhs. A-C.)

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed
the opposition.

 

On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed
the reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The
Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay is DENIED.

 

LEGAL
STANDARD

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a
dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to
arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or
controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (CCP § 1281.2; see also Omar v.
Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party
petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence
of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the
burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to
its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62
Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

“If a court of competent jurisdiction .
. . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an
action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which
such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such
action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had
in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the
court specifies.” (CCP § 1281.4.)¿¿

 

ANALYSIS

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California
law, Arbitration Clauses are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on
such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v.
Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The
party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written
arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In
ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine
whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general
principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this
determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220
Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide
evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally
sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee
v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218, 105
Cal.Rptr.2d 597 (Condee¿); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A
petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil
Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required
allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that
provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy
must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated
by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden
shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any
challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those
challenges. [Citation]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13
Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.)

 

i.                   
Plaintiff’s Capacity

 

Plaintiff argues that he
lacked capacity to enter into the Arbitration Clauses when he signed them,
rending them invalid. Defendant argues that Plaintiff has provided no evidence
supporting his incapacity.

 

The Court agrees with
Defendant that Plaintiff has failed to prove he lacked contractual capacity at
the time he signed the Arbitration Clauses. As support, Plaintiff merely
attached the Complaint. Such allegations are not evidence. The Arbitration
Clauses thus may not be invalidated based on lack of capacity. Nevertheless,
the Court finds the DPR Agreement and Arbitration are invalid on other grounds.

 

ii.                  
Mutual Assent

 

Defendant attaches the Admission Agreement and
separate Arbitration Clauses and asserts Plaintiff signed all documents on
March 23, 2023. (Ma Decl., ¶¶ 2-4, Exhs.
A-C.) Plaintiff argues Defendant did not sign the Dispute Resolution Program
Agreement or the incorporated Arbitration Clauses, and thus, there was no
mutual assent, rendering them invalid.

 

The Court finds that while Defendant has presented a
copy of the Arbitration Clauses, the parties did not mutually assent to
arbitration. The Court agrees with Plaintiff that Defendant’s failure to sign
the Dispute Resolution Program Agreement or the Arbitration Clauses indicates a
lack of mutual assent.

 

The Court notes that, following the Admissions
Agreement, there is a Dispute Resolution Program (DPR) Agreement that
incorporates the two Arbitration Clauses. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A (DPR Agreement is page 73 to 86 of the Motion).) The two Arbitration
Clauses are for 1) arbitrations relating to medical malpractice and 2) for
claims other than medical malpractice. The Arbitration Clauses are incorporated
in the DPR Agreement and also attached as Exhibits B and C to the Motion. (Ma
Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exhs. B, C.)

 

Where an arbitration
agreement is separate from a larger agreement and there is a designated place
for both parties to sign, separate assent as to the arbitration agreement is
required. (See Romo v. Y-3 Holdings, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1153,
1160 (finding that “the [arbitration agreement within the employment handbook].
. .contemplated that the arbitration of disputes provision would be effective
only if both [parties] assented to that [particular] provision. Since the
[parties] did not assent to this [particular] provision the parties did not
agree to binding arbitration”).)

 

Here, the DPR Agreement and
Arbitration Clauses are separate from the Admissions Agreement and require both
parties to sign. Article 1 of the DPR Agreement clearly states: “By entering
into this DRP Agreement and one or both of the attached arbitration clauses,
both parties give up their constitutional right to have any such dispute
decided in a court of law before a jury, and instead accept the use of
mediation or ultimately arbitration.” (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 73.) Both
Defendant and Plaintiff failed to sign in certain locations where both
parties were required to sign. Firstly, Defendant and Plaintiff failed to sign the DPR Agreement. The signature
page of the Dispute Resolution Program Agreement (page 11 of the Agreement),
which requires the signature of the resident, the signatures of two facility
witnesses, and the signature of the facility administrator, is completely blank.
(Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 83.) Plaintiff signed the two Arbitration Clauses,
although they are not dated. (Ma Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exhs. B, C.) Defendant signed
neither of the Arbitration Clauses. (Ibid.) Defendant has failed to
offer an explanation regarding its missing signatures or address arguments of
mutual at all. Because the parties failed to sign in multiple places, the Court
finds a lack of mutual assent. The DPR Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are
thus invalid.

 

Although the inquiry would
generally end when the arbitration agreement is found invalid, the Court will
continue to evaluate the parties’ arguments regarding enforceability.

 

Enforceability

 

            The parties
dispute whether the Arbitration Clauses are enforceable under Title 42 Code of Federal
Regulations section 483.7, subdivision (n) (now subdivision (m)) and Health
& Safety Code section 1599.81, which both govern arbitration agreements by
long-term nursing facilities. The Court will address each in turn.

 

i.                   
Title 42 Code of Federal Regulations section 483.70,
subdivision (m)

 

Title 42 Code of Federal Regulations section 483.70 governs
requirements for institutions to participate as nursing facilities in the
Medicare and Medicaid programs. (42 CFR § 483.1, subd. (b).) Subdivision (m)
sets forth requirements for arbitration clauses for such facilities, including,
but not limited to the following: the arbitration cannot be condition of
admission, the arbitration agreement must be explained to resident in manner he
or she understands, the resident acknowledges he or she understands the
agreement, and the arbitration agreement must for a neutral arbitrator, selection
of venue agreed upon by both parties, and must be the resident a right to
rescind within 30 days of signing. (42 CFR § 483.70, subd. (m).)

 

Plaintiff argues that the declaration of Abby Ma in
support of the Motion fails to state that Defendant complied with these
requirements as to Plaintiff specifically, not just as to all patients. Defendant
argues the DPR Agreement and the Arbitration Clauses comply with these
requirements.

 

The Court finds that the requirements of C.F.R.
section 483.1, subdivision (m) are likely met. The DPR Agreement states in
multiple places that signing the agreement is not a precondition for admission
to Defendant’s facility. (Ma Decl., ¶
2, Exh. A, p. 73-74.) Further, the DPR Agreement states it may be revoked
within 30 days of signing and provides for a neutral arbitrator and mutual
venue selection. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 73-78.) Defendant has also
provided evidence that during the admissions process, Defendant allows their residents the opportunity to review the agreements
prior to signing them, explains the documents during the process, and answers
questions. (Ma Decl., ¶ 5) Residents are also advised that the Arbitration
Clauses are voluntary and not a precondition to admission. (Ibid.) The
Court thus finds that the DPR Agreement and Arbitration Clauses do not run
afoul of C.F.R. section 483.1, subdivision (m) as to render them unenforceable.

 

ii.                  
Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, subdivision
(d)

 

Plaintiff argues its Health & Safety Code section
1430, subdivision (b) cause of action may not be compelled to arbitration
pursuant to Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, subdivision (d). Defendant
argues that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) applies to the DPR Agreement and
Arbitration Clauses, and the FAA preempts Health & Safety Code sections
1430 and 1599.81 to the extent they limit arbitration.

 

Health & Safety Code section 1430, subdivision (b)(C)
states the following: “An agreement by a resident or patient of a skilled
nursing facility or intermediate care facility to waive that resident’s or
patient’s rights to sue pursuant to this subdivision is void as
contrary to public policy.” Health & Safety Code section 1599.81,
subdivision (d) states the following: “In the event the contract contains an
arbitration clause, the contract attachment pertaining to arbitration shall
contain notice that under Section 1430, the patient may not waive his or
her ability to sue for violation of the Patient's Bill of Rights.”

 

The Court finds that DPR Agreement explicitly states
the FAA applies. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh.
A, p. 80.) The Court further finds that Health & Safety Code sections 1430
and 1599.81 are preempted by the FAA to the extent they prevent arbitration.
(See Valley View Health Care, Inc. v. Chapman (E.D. Cal. 2014) 992
F.Supp.2d 1016, 1040–1041 (“[Section 1430, subdivision (b)(C)] effectively
“prohibits outright arbitration” of Patient Bill of Rights claims and as a
“conflicting rule is displaced by the FAA.”[Citations.] [Section 1430,
subdivision (b)(C)] violates and is preempted by the FAA as an obstacle to
accomplishment and execution of FAA purposes. . .Section[] 1599.81(d) fall[s] as
contrary to the FAA to the extent that [it] require[s] notice of the bar on
arbitration of Patient's Bill of Rights claims.”).) Thus, while the DPR
Agreement and the Arbitration Clause on medical malpractice claims contain a
section 1599.81 notice, the Court finds Plaintiff’s section 1430 cause of
action arbitrable due to FAA preemption. The Court finds Plaintiff’s argument
insufficient to render the DPR Agreement and Arbitration Clauses unenforceable.

 

            The Court does
not address other issues of enforceability because no other issues were raised
by the parties. The Court finds that, based on the parties’ arguments, the DPR
Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are not unenforceable on Plaintiff’s asserted
grounds. Yet the DPR Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are still invalid due to
a lack of mutual assent—there is no valid agreement to enforce. The Court thus
denies the Motion to Compel.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Motion to Compel and Request to Stay is DENIED.

Sarkisian, et al. v. leisure healthcare, llc dba leisure glen post acute care center, et al.

 

Motion to compel arbitration and to stay proceedings

 

Date of Hearing:          November 14, 2024                            Trial Date:       None set.

Responding Parties:    Plaintiffs Lida Sarkisian (individually and as successor in interest to Susan Khodagulyan), Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from the admittance of Susan Khodagulyan (Decedent) to Defendant Leisure Healthcare LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center (Leisure Center).

 

Decedent was admitted to Leisure Center on November 19, 2022 for “full-time ‘24/7’ residence and safety protection.” (Compl., ¶ 10.) According to the complaint, while Leisure Center was aware that Decedent had a “fall risk,” a few days after being admitted to Leisure Center, Decedent suffered a fall. (Id. ¶¶ 9, 14.) By May 12, 2023, Decedent suffered “three or more falls.” (Id. ¶ 15.) On July 3, 2023, Decedent suffered another fall which resulted in a head injury. (Id. ¶ 16.) On August 27, 2023, Decedent suffered another fall, which resulted in a head injury and brain bleed, after which Decedent’s family terminated her residence at Leisure Center. (Id. ¶¶ 17-19.)

 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiffs Lida Sarkisian (individually and as successor in interest to Susan Khodagulyan), Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian filed a complaint against Leisure Healthcare LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center and B.V. General Inc. dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center. Plaintiff Lida Sarkisian (Lida) as successor in interest to Decedent alleged causes of action for (1) Survivor Action – Elder Abuse and (2) Survivor Action – General Negligence. Plaintiffs Lida Sarkisian individually, Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian (together, Plaintiffs) alleged a cause of action for (3) Wrongful Death – Negligence.

 

On August 16, 2024, Leisure Center filed the instant motion to compel arbitration and to stay the current proceedings.

 

On October 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition.

 

On October 14, 2024, Leisure Center filed a reply.

On October 21, 2024, this Court requested further briefing.

 

On November 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their supplemental brief.

 

On November 5, 2024, Leisure Center filed its supplemental brief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.      Leisure Center’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED only as to Lida’s wrongful death claim. Leisure Center’s Motion is DENIED as to the rest of the claims alleged in the complaint, including the motion to stay.    

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, a court may order arbitration of a controversy if it finds that the parties have agreed to arbitrate that dispute. Because the obligation to arbitrate arises from contract, the court may compel arbitration only if the dispute in question is one in which the parties have agreed to arbitrate. (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 352.) Since arbitration is a favored method of dispute resolution, arbitration agreements should be liberally interpreted, and arbitration should be ordered unless the agreement clearly does not apply to the dispute in question. (Id. at p. 353; Segal v. Silberstein (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 627, 633.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Leisure Center is seeking to compel arbitration on the grounds that an Admission Agreement and an Arbitration Agreement was executed by Lida in her individual capacity and in her representative capacity for Decedent, which would thus force Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian’s claim into arbitration. (Reyes Decl., Exhibits A, B, C.)

 

1.      Existence of a Valid Arbitration Agreement

 

“Arbitration is a matter of contract.” (Metalclad Corp. v. Ventana Env't Organizational P'ship (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1705, 1711.) “General principles of contract law determine whether the parties have entered a binding agreement to arbitrate.” (Craig v. Brown & Root, Inc., 84 Cal.App.4th 416, 420 (2000); Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc., 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72 (1997).) “In determining whether an arbitration agreement exists, we apply the same rules of contract formation as for any other contract.” (Weeks v. Interactive Life Forms, LLC (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1084.)

 

Under the California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. Section 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

Once petitioners allege that an arbitration agreement exists, the burden shifts to respondents to prove the falsity of the purported agreement, and no evidence or authentication is required to find the arbitration agreement exists. (See Condee v. Longwood Mgt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219.) However, if the existence of the agreement is challenged, "petitioner bears the burden of proving [the arbitration agreement's] existence by a preponderance of the evidence." (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413. See also Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1058-1060.) 

 

“With respect to the moving party's burden to provide evidence of the existence of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court.” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160). “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1330.) “Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges.’ (Baker, supra, 13 Cal.App.5th at p. 1160.)

 

Here, in support of whether a valid arbitration exists, Leisure Center provided the text of the Arbitration Agreement that was signed by Lida. (Reyes Decl., ¶ 2; Exhibit A.).

 

Article 2 of the Arbitration Agreement provides:

 

It is further understood that any dispute between Resident and B.V. General, Inc. dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center; Leisure Healthcare LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center, its owners, operators, officers, directors, administrators, staff, employees, agents, . . . [etc.], including any action for injury or death arising from negligence, intentional tort and/or statutory causes of action (including all California Welfare and Institutions Code sections and Health and Safety Code section 1430), will be determined by submission to binding arbitration and not by lawsuit or resort to court process except as California law provides for judicial review of arbitration proceedings.

 

Further, Article 4 of the Arbitration Agreement provides:

 

This Agreement is binding on all parties. including the Resident's representatives, executors, family members, and heirs who bring any claims individually or in a representative capacity. The Resident's representatives, agents, executors, family members, successors in interest and heirs who execute this Agreement below on the signature line are doing so not only in their representative capacity for the Resident, but also in their individual capacity and thus agree that any claims brought individually by any such representatives. agents. executors. family members, representatives, successors in interest and heirs are subject to binding arbitration.

 

However, Plaintiffs contest that a valid arbitration agreement exists. While Plaintiffs do not dispute that Lida signed the Arbitration Agreement, Plaintiffs contend that (1) Lida did not have the authority to act as an agent and sign the Arbitration Agreement on behalf of Decedent, and (2) The Arbitration Agreement does not bind Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian as non-signatories to the Agreement, and therefore no valid arbitration agreement exists.

 

a.      Lida Was Not the Agent of Decedent

 

Leisure Center contends that a valid arbitration agreement exists because Lida signed the Arbitration Agreement in her individual capacity and on behalf of Decedent. Leisure Center argues that because Linda also signed the Declaration of Healthcare Agent form under penalty of perjury, which states that the signatory (Lida) is acting on behalf of the patient (Decedent), the Arbitration Agreement should be enforced. (Reyes Decl., Exhibit C.) Specifically, the Declaration of Healthcare Agent form provides:

 

On or about November 19, 2022, the Resident requested that I make healthcare decisions on his/her behalf. I have been signing documents on behalf of the Resident and making healthcare decisions on behalf of the Resident at the Resident’s request since that time. I do this because the Resident request/gave consent for me to do so on his/her behalf. I now voluntarily signed the Admission Agreement and the Arbitration Agreement for this skilled nursing facility on behalf of the Resident as the Resident appointed healthcare agent. By signing my name below, I attest under the penalty of perjury that the above is true and accurate, and that I am authorized by the Resident’s consent/direction to sign the Admission and Arbitration Agreement on the Resident’s behalf.

(Ibid.)

 

“[A] person who is authorized to act as the patient's agent can bind the patient to an arbitration agreement.” (Kinder v. Capistrano Beach Care Ctr., LLC (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 804, 812.) However, “[A]n agency[, whether actual or ostensible,] cannot be created by the conduct of the agent alone; rather, conduct by the principal is essential to create the agency.” (Flores v. Evergreen at San Diego, LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 581.) “’The principal must in some manner indicate that the agent is to act for him, and the agent must act or agree to act on his behalf and subject to his control.” [Citations.] Thus, the ‘formation of an agency relationship is a bilateral matter. Words or conduct by both principal and agent are necessary to create the relationship . . . .’” (Flores, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 588.)

 

“The party seeking to compel arbitration does not meet its burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement when it does not present any evidence that the purported principal's conduct caused the agent or the third party to believe that the agent had the authority to bind the principal.” (Rogers v. Roseville SH, LLC (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 1065, 1075.)

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs that Leisure Center has not met its burden in establishing whether a valid arbitration agreement exists because it has not met its burden in establishing that an agency relationship existed between Decedent and Lida.

 

In Kinder, a patient was admitted to the defendant nursing facility, when a dispute between the patient and the nursing facility arose. (Kinder, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 809.) There, the nursing facility petitioned the court to compel arbitration, claiming the patient was bound to the arbitration agreement that his adult children purportedly executed on his behalf. (Ibid.) The Kinder court declined to compel arbitration because the nursing facility “presented no evidence that [the patient] did anything to lead [his adult children] to believe they had actual authority [or ostensible authority] to enter in arbitration agreements on her behalf.” (Id. at p. 813.)

 

The court in Flores came to a similar conclusion. In Flores, the court declined to compel arbitration when the patient’s husband signed an admission agreement, which included an arbitration agreement, on behalf of the patient. (Flores, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at pp. 585-86.) The Flores court concluded that even though the nursing facility presented evidence that the patient’s husband signed the admission documents on the patient’s behalf, which demonstrated evidence regarding the husband’s conduct, the nursing facility failed to offer evidence regarding the patient’s conduct. (Id. at p. 588.) Because the nursing facility failed to meet its burden in showing an actual or ostensible agency relationship between the patient and her husband, the Court, as in Kinder, denied the nursing facility’s petition to compel arbitration. (Id. at p. 594.)

 

As in both Kinder and Flores, Leisure Center has not provided the Court with any evidence reflecting affirmative conduct on behalf of Decedent to create an agency relationship between herself and Lida. The evidence is “completely silent regarding [Decedent’s and Lida’s] dealings prior to her admission and/or their interactions at the time of her admission.” (Flores, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 588.) Because “agency cannot be created by the conduct of the agent alone,” Leisure Center’s contention that Lida unilaterally signing Declaration of Healthcare Agent form indicates that an agency relationship was created between her and Decedent is not sufficient. (Id. at pp. 587–88 [“The mere fact [Lida] signed the admission documents, including the arbitration agreements, is insufficient.”].)

 

Additionally, Leisure Center’s argument that Lida signed Declaration of Healthcare Agent form under penalty of perjury does not alter the analysis. Lida’s action in signing the Declaration of Healthcare Agent form is not a representation that the Decedent affirmatively conferred agency status upon Lida, which is required to create an agency relationship. The evidence is devoid of any such representation. (Hearden v. Windsor Redding Care Ctr., LLC (2024) 103 Cal. App. 5th 1010, 1020-21 [“Defendants point to the declarations the family signatories submitted in opposition to the motion to compel arbitration, but those declarations do not aver that decedents expressly authorized the family signatories to sign the arbitration agreements or that any conduct by decedents caused the facility to reasonably believe decedents consented to having the family signatories bind decedents to arbitration.”].)

 

Further, Leisure Center in its motion states that Lida was the Decedent’s “self-appointed agent,” further displaying a lack of evidence that Decedent consented to an agency relationship between herself and Lida. (Motion, page 6.)  Leisure Center has not presented any evidence that Lida had actual or ostensible authority to sign the Arbitration Agreement on behalf of Decedent, beyond Lida’s own representations that she was Decedent’s agent reflected in the Declaration of Healthcare Agent form.

 

Thus, the Court finds that Leisure Center has not met its burden in establishing that an agency relationship existed between Lida and Decedent. (Flores, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 589 [“It was [Leisure Center’s] burden to show the validity of the arbitration agreement based on [Decedent’s] express or implied consent to have [Lida] act as her agent.”].) Because Leisure Center has not sufficiently shown that an agency relationship existed between Lida and Decedent, it has not met its burden in demonstrating that a valid arbitration agreement exists. (See also Lopez v. Bartlett Care Center, LLC (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 311, 313; Pagarian v. Libby Care Center Inc. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 298, 301-303; Rogers v. Roseville SH, LLC (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 1065, 1074-75.)

 

2.      The Arbitration Agreement Does Not Extend to Non-Signatories

 

“Although, as noted, California has a strong public policy in favor of arbitration, there is no preference for the arbitral forum when the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.” (Garrison v. Superior Ct. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 253, 263–64.) “There are three exceptions to the rule: (1) an agent can bind a principal, (2) spouses can bind each other, and (3) a parent can bind a minor child.” (Monschke v. Timber Ridge Assisted Living, LLC (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 583, 587.)

 

In the absence of evidence that Lida had actual or ostensible authority to bind Decedent to the Arbitration Agreement, the arbitration does not bind Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian as Decedent’s “family members” or “heirs.” (Reyes Decl., Exhibit B.) “The individual claims of [Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian] are also not subject to the arbitration agreement because . . . there was no evidence that [Lida] had the authority to sign the arbitration agreement on behalf of [Andranik Sarkissian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian].” (Rogers, supra, 75 Cal.App.5th at p. 1077.)  

 

Leisure Center’s reliance on Ruiz v. Podolsky (2010) 50 Cal.4th 838 is misplaced. Leisure Center argues that under Ruiz, Wrongful Death claimants who do not sign an arbitration agreement should nonetheless be “bootstrapped” into arbitration when the arbitration agreement involves a healthcare provider. (Motion, page 8.) The Court in Ruiz held that Code of Civil Procedure “[S]ection 1295, construed in light of its purpose, is designed to permit patients who sign arbitration agreements to bind their heirs in wrongful death actions.” (Ruiz, supra, 50 Cal.4th at p. 849.) However, the Ruiz Court dealt with a situation wherein the patient himself signed the arbitration agreement that validly bound the patient’s heirs to arbitrate their wrongful death claims. (Id. at 851 [“Although a wrongful death claim is an independent action, wrongful death plaintiffs may be bound by agreements entered into by decedent that limit the scope of the wrongful death action (emphasis added).”].) Here, Decedent did not sign the arbitration agreement, and there is no evidence that Decedent otherwise intended to bind her heirs to arbitration. Thus, Ruiz is inapplicable.

 

Because Plaintiffs Andrank Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian and Henrick Sarkisian are not parties to the arbitration agreement and there is no evidence that Lida had the authority to execute the agreement on their behalf, their wrongful death claim is not compelled to arbitration.

 

3.      Lida’s Wrongful Death Claim is Compelled to Arbitration

 

Here, Lida, in her individual capacity, also pursues a wrongful death claim against Leisure Center. The Arbitration Agreement states that it is “binding on all parties. including the Resident's representatives, executors, family members, and heirs who bring any claims individually or in a representative capacity” [emphasis added], and that the Resident’s “family members . . . execute this Agreement below on the signature line are doing so not only in their representative capacity for the Resident, but also in their individual capacity.” (Reyes Decl., Exhibit A.) There is no dispute that Lida signed the Arbitration Agreement and her wrongful death claim would be within the purview of the Arbitration Agreement.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure, “Section 1281.2, subdivision (c) gives a trial court discretion to deny a motion to compel arbitration as to all or part of an arbitrable controversy when ‘[a] party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.’”(Hearden, supra, 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 1017.)

 

“The court's discretion under section 1281.2, subdivision (c) does not come into play until it is ascertained that the subdivision applies, which requires the threshold determination of whether there are nonarbitrable claims against at least one of the parties to the litigation (e.g., a nonsignatory).” (Avila v. S. California Specialty Care, Inc. (2018)  20 Cal.App.5th 835, 840.)

 

Here, Leisure Center argues that Lida’s wrongful death claim would be subject to arbitration because under section 1281.2, subdivision (c) would not apply, given that the section states “[t]his subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2(c).) Section 1295 “contemplates arbitration agreements to resolve disputes concerning ‘professional negligence’ . . . .” (Ruiz, supra, 50 Cal.4th at p. 844.)

 

Leisure Center again points to Ruiz, which concluded that “all wrongful death claimants are bound by arbitration agreements entered into pursuant to section 1295, at least when, as here, the language of the agreement manifests an intent to bind these claimants.”(Ruiz, supra, 50 Cal.4th at p. 841.) Firstly, as stated above, Ruiz is inapplicable in the present instance because Lida, rather than Decedent herself, signed the Arbitration Agreement without any evidence demonstrating that she had the authority to do so.

 

Secondly, even if the holding of Ruiz were applicable, Lida’s wrongful death claim would still not be compelled to arbitration under Ruiz. Plaintiffs’ complaint does not allege professional negligence or medical malpractice, but rather elder abuse and general negligence. (Compl.) While the complaint “includes allegations that could be categorized as professional negligence as well as elder abuse” and there could be “some overlap between the two,” Plaintiffs’ claims do not fall within the purview of section 1295. (Avila, supra, 20 Cal.App.5th at p. 843.) “What matters is . . .  the basis of the claims as pleaded in the complaint. If the primary basis for the wrongful death claim sounds in professional negligence . . . , then section 1295 applies. If, as plaintiffs claim here, the primary basis is under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15600 et seq.), then section 1295 does not apply . . . .” (Id. at p. 842.) Thus, Plaintiffs’, including Lida’s, wrongful death claim is not included in section 1295, and therefore, section 1281.2 subdivision (c)’s exemption does not apply.

 

However, Leisure Center also contends that Lida’s wrongful death claim is subject to arbitration because the Arbitration Agreement that Lida signed states that California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1281.2, subdivision (c) “is excluded from this Agreement,” and specifically, that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) applies (Reyes Decl., Exhibit A.) Leisure Center specifically contends that because the FAA applies, section 1281.2, subdivision (c) should not apply. Leisure Center is correct.

 

In Volt, the Court held that because the FAA “contains no provision design to deal with the special practical problem that arise in multiparty contractual disputes” and “California has taken the lead in fashioning a legislative repones to this problem” in the form of section 1281.2, subdivision (c), the FAA did “not prevent application of Cal. Civ. Proc. Code Ann. § 1281.2(c) to stay arbitration where, as here, the parties have agreed to arbitrate in accordance with California law.” (Volt Info. Sciences v. Leland Stanford Jr. U. (1989) 489 U.S. 468, 479.) However, the Arbitration Agreement here explicitly states that it is to be governed by the FAA.

 

In Cronus, the Court similarly held that the “spirit” of the FAA is not frustrated by section 1281.2, subdivision (c). (Cronus Invs., Inc. v. Concierge Servs (2005) 35 Cal.4th 376, 391.)  However, the court concluded that “its opinion does not preclude parties to an arbitration agreement to expressly designate that any arbitration proceeding should move forward under the FAA's procedural provisions rather than under state procedural law.” (Cronus, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 394.) Here, the Arbitration Agreement expressly states that the FAA is to apply.


Because the Arbitration Agreement explicitly references the application of the FAA, the Court cannot look to section 1281.2, subdivision (c) to allow Lida to litigate her wrongful death claim. (Victrola 89, LLC v. Jaman Properties 8 LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 337, 342–43 [“The parties incorporated the procedural provisions of the FAA into the Agreement; thus the court could not look to Section 1281.2(c) to deny the [] motion.”].)

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing reasons, Leisure Center’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Proceedings is granted only as to Lida’s wrongful death claim. The rest of Leisure Center’s Motion is denied.
