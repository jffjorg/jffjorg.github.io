DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV08660    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing:          12/17/2024

Re:                   21STCV08660, Van Kirk, MTC Plaintiff’s Depo.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 7, 2023, Defendant Secure Property Management Inc. (“Defendant”) initially noticed the deposition of Plaintiff Kahlee Van Kirk (“Plaintiff”), for December 1, 2023. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 3; Exh. A [notice].) After Plaintiff retained new counsel, the parties agreed to reschedule Plaintiff’s deposition to January 3, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 3; Exh. B [correspondence].) After Plaintiff indicated further unavailability, the deposition was rescheduled to April 5, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 4-6; Exh. C, D, and E [meet and confer correspondences].) Plaintiff failed to appear for their April 5, 2024, deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 6.) At this time, defense counsel initially learned that Plaintiff also suffered a back injury, had a lumbar MRI, and saw a pain management doctor. (Ibid.) Based on this new information, defense counsel served additional discovery on Plaintiff. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)

 

On September 12, 2024, Defendant served their new notice of taking Plaintiff’s deposition, scheduled for October 9, 2024. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 9; Exh. F [notice].) Plaintiff did not object. (Ibid. at ¶ 10.) However, Plaintiff failed to appear at the noticed October 9, 2024, deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 12; Exh. I [certificate of non-attendance].) Neither Plaintiff nor Plaintiff’s counsel provided a reason for their non-appearance. (Ibid. at ¶ 13.)

 

On October 23, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

 

CCP § 2025.450 provides in pertinent part the following:

 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director,

managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that

is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under

Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce

for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing

described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order

compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection             of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the          deposition notice.

 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

  

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

DISCUSSION

 

CCP § 2025.010 states that “[a]ny party may obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.” (CCP § 2025.010.) Here, Defendant properly served each notice of deposition on Plaintiff. (Ruiz Decl. ¶¶ 3 and 9; Exhs. A and F [notices].) Plaintiff did not object to these notices. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 10.) However, Plaintiff failed to appear at their noticed April 5, 2024, and October 9, 2024, depositions. (Ruiz Decl. ¶¶ 6 and 12; Exh. I [certificate of non-appearance].)

 

Therefore, pursuant to CCP §§ 2025.010 and 2025.450, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. Plaintiff is ordered to appear for oral deposition within 20 days of this hearing.

 

Sanctions

“[i]f a motion under [CCP § 2025.450(a)] is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (CCP § 2025.450(g).)

Here, Plaintiff failed to appear for their properly noticed depositions without substantial justification. Therefore, sanctions are mandatory pursuant to CCP § 2025.450(g).

Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $1,215.00, representing 4.5 hours of attorney work at an hourly rate of $170.00, a non-appearance fee of $220.00, and a $60.00 filing fee. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 14; Exh. J [non-appearance invoice].) The Court awards sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, payable within 20 days of this Order in the reduced amount of $1,045.00 as no opposition was filed.

CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to appear for oral deposition within 20 days of this hearing. Additionally, Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, are ordered to pay $1,045.00 in sanctions to defense counsel within 20 days of this Order.

I.       BACKGROUND

On May 6, 2022, Plaintiff Federal Express Corporation (“FedEx”) filed a complaint against Defendant Alice Hsueh (“Hsueh”) and Does 1 through 40 alleging two causes of action: (1) premises liability; and (2) general negligence. FedEx alleges that, on May 28, 2020, Lydia Rawlings, while acting within the scope of her employment with FedEx, sustained injury on Hsueh’s property due to their negligence. (See Complaint.) As a result, FedEx has paid out and continues to pay workers’ compensation benefits to Lydia Rawlings.

On June 6, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 22, 2024, Hsueh brought the instant motion to dismiss for failure to prosecute.

On November 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On November 26, 2024, Hsueh filed their reply.

 

II.    LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Procedure § 583.410 provides that an action can be dismissed under appropriate circumstances when made “pursuant to the procedure and in accordance with the criteria prescribed by rules adopted by the Judicial Council.” (CCP § 583.410(a) and (b).) This criteria includes:

 

(1) The court's file in the case and the declarations and supporting data submitted by the parties and, where applicable, the availability of the moving party and other essential parties for service of process;

(2) The diligence in seeking to effect service of process;

(3) The extent to which the parties engaged in any settlement negotiations or discussions;

(4) The diligence of the parties in pursuing discovery or other pretrial proceedings, including any extraordinary relief sought by either party;

(5) The nature and complexity of the case;

(6) The law applicable to the case, including the pendency of other litigation under a common set of facts or determinative of the legal or factual issues in the case;

(7) The nature of any extensions of time or other delay attributable to either party;

(8) The condition of the court's calendar and the availability of an earlier trial date if the matter was ready for trial;

(9) Whether the interests of justice are best served by dismissal or trial of the case; and

(10) Any other fact or circumstance relevant to a fair determination of the issue.

 

Dismissal is also permitted when an action has not been brought to trial within three years of its commencement. (CCP § 583.420(a)(2)(A).) Dismissal is even permitted two years after the action is commenced “for delay in prosecution if the action has not been brought to trial or conditionally settled within two years after the action was commenced against the defendant.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1340(a).)  

“‘The competing considerations to be evaluated are the policies of discouraging stale claims and compelling reasonable diligence balanced against the strong public policy which seeks to dispose of litigation on the merits rather than on procedural grounds.’ [Citation.] ‘However, it is now well established that the policy [of preferring to dispose litigation on the merits] only comes into play when a plaintiff makes a showing of some excusable delay.’ [Citation.]” (Van Keulen v. Cathay Pacific Airways, Ltd. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 122, 131.)

 

III.  ANALYSIS

Hsueh moves to dismiss FedEx’s complaint with prejudice on the grounds that: (1) FedEx did not proceed with reasonable diligence in the prosecution of this action as required by CCP § 583.410; and (2) the action has not been brought to trial or conditionally settled within two years of filing its complaint.

 

Diligence in Seeking to Effect Service of Process

The Court notes that FedEx did not attempt service until February 2023, more than nine months after the complaint was initially filed. (See Dorman October 19, 2023, Decl. at ¶ 7.) FedEx argues that their decision to wait until damages were ascertained before sending the complaint out for service “was not unreasonable under the circumstances.” (Opposition p. 2:25-27.) However, the worker’s compensation matter was resolved on October 4, 2022. (Dorman Decl. ¶ 5.) FedEx does not explain its four-month delay in attempting service thereafter. In October 2023, FedEx stated it would be filing an application for publication of service after being unable to serve Hsueh. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) However, FedEx did not file an application for publication until January 2024. Once again, FedEx does not explain this delay. Therefore, FedEx’s lack of diligence in seeking to effect service of process weighs in favor of dismissal pursuant to CCP § 583.410.

 

Diligence in Pursuing Discovery or Other Pretrial Proceedings

Hsueh answered on March 18, 2024. FedEx has never indicated it is ready for trial, requested a trial date, or sought any other pretrial proceedings or extraordinary relief. (Pugh Decl. ¶ 4.) The parties have not engaged in any settlement discussions or negotiations. (Ibid. at ¶ 5.) FedEx has not propounded any discovery on Hsueh. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.) Therefore, FedEx demonstrates a lack of diligence in pursuing discovery or other pretrial proceedings, weighing in favor of dismissal pursuant to CCP § 583.410.

 

Nature and Complexity of the Case

FedEx’s complaint includes two causes of action: premises liability and general negligence. To succeed on these claims, FedEx must prove whether Hsueh owed a duty of care, whether Rawlings was harmed, whether Hsueh’s negligence was a substantial factor in causing this harm, Rawlings’s damages resulting from the alleged harm, and other fact-dependent determinations. (See Cal. Civ. Jury Instructions Nos. 400, 401, 430, 1000, 1001, and 1003.) As Hsueh notes, “this [action] is a fluid situation that depends on the circumstances on the ground on May 28, 2020 [the date of the underlying incident]. The fact-intensive nature of the case weighs in favor of dismissal.” (Motion p. 20:18-19.)

 

Nature of Any Extensions or Other Delay Attributable to Either Party

Hsueh has not sought any extensions or otherwise delayed this action. Any delay is wholly attributable to FedEx, a fact further weighing in favor of dismissal pursuant to § CCP 583.410.

 

Whether Justice is Best Served by Dismissal or Trial of the Case

Here, the Court considers that the evidence in this case has all but disappeared, substantially prejudicing Hsueh. The Complaint references items on the subject property which have since been removed or stolen. (Pugh Decl. ¶ 11; Lu Decl. ¶ 4.) Here, Hsueh could not avail herself of discovery prior to service, which is the formal method by which to preserve evidence. (Scarzella v. DeMers (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 1762, 1771.) Service was not effectuated until nearly two years after FedEx filed its complaint. Courts may infer prejudice in cases involving unreasonable delays in service. (Luti v. Graco, Inc. (1985) 170 Cal.App.3d 228, 233.)

 

Any Other Fact Relevant to a Fair Determination of the Issue

The Court notes that FedEx filed an untimely opposition to the instant motion. FedEx argues that its untimely filing should not be considered as it resulted from a calendaring error. (Ibid. at p. 4:5-8.) The Court would ordinarily overlook such an error. However, this untimeliness only adds to the mounting evidence of FedEx’s failure to diligently prosecute this action.

In its untimely opposition, FedEx fails to explain its lack of diligence in prosecution. Instead, the opposition claims that Hsueh has failed to show that FedEx’s delay was unreasonable or that it prejudiced the defense. (Opposition p. 1:13-16.) However, FedEx misstates the burden upon a motion to dismiss for failure to prosecute. “[I]t is well established that the policy [of preferring to dispose litigation on the merits] only comes into play when a plaintiff makes a showing of some excusable delay.’ [Citation.]” (Van Keulen v. Cathay Pacific Airways, Ltd. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 122, 131.)

 

IV.  CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, the Court finds that FedEx has failed to diligently prosecute this action pursuant to CCP § 583.130. Therefore, Defendant Hsueh’s motion to dismiss pursuant to CCP § 583.410 is GRANTED and the case is dismissed with prejudice.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiff Marshall Krupp (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Kroenke Sports & Entertainment LLC (“Kroenke”), StadCo LA, LLC (“StadCo”), the Chargers Football Company (“Chargers”), the Contemporary Services Corporation (“Contemporary Services”), and Does 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) premises liability; and (2) negligence.

On October 27, 2023, Defendants Kroenke, StadCo, and the Chargers filed a motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On November 8, 2023, Contemporary Services filed a motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On June 5, 2024, the action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On August 30, 2024, Kroenke, StadCo, and the Chargers’s motion for summary judgment was granted in their favor. On the same date, Contemporary Services’s motion for summary judgment was also granted in their favor.

On November 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for new trial of Kroenke Sports & Entertainment LLC, StadCo LA, LLC, and the Chargers Football Company (hereinafter “Defendants”)’s motion for summary judgment.

On November 27, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition.

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed their reply.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

The determination of a motion for new trial rests so completely within the trial judge’s discretion that it will not be disturbed on appeal unless a manifest and unmistakable abuse of discretion clearly appears. (Schelbaurer v. Butler Mfg. Co. (1984) 35 Cal.3d 442, 452.) The rationale is that the trial judge is familiar with the evidence, witnesses and proceedings of the case and is in the best position to determine whether a new trial should be granted. (Jacoby v. Feldman (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 432, 446.) 

A motion for new trial asks the trial court to reexamine one or more issues of fact or law after a trial and decision by judge or jury. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc §§ 656 and 657.) Since the right to a new trial is purely statutory, exact compliance is required with the procedural steps for making and determining a motion for new trial. Any significant departure from the procedural requirements deprives the trial court of power to grant the motion. The grounds for new trial are also entirely statutory and, thus, the motion must be based on one or more of the following statutory grounds:

(1) Irregularity in the proceedings;

(2) Jury misconduct;

(3) Accident or surprise;

(4) Newly discovered evidence;

(5) Excessive or inadequate damages;

(6) Insufficient evidence;

(7) Verdict or decision “against law;” or

(8) Error in law during trial.

(CCP § 657(1)-(7).)

If a motion for new trial is “made for a cause mentioned on the first, second, third and fourth subdivisions of Section 657, it must be made upon affidavits; otherwise, it must be made on the minutes of the court.” (CCP § 658.) Thus, a motion for new trial based on irregularity in the proceedings, misconduct of the jury, accident or surprise or newly discovered evidence must be supported by affidavits. Other motions based on insufficiency of the evidence, a verdict or decision against law, an error in law or excessive or inadequate damages must be made on the minutes of the court. It is “implicit in the statement of the grounds of the motion whether it will be made upon affidavits or the minutes of the court, or both.” (Nichols v. Hast (1965) 62 Cal.2d 598, 601.)

An order granting summary judgment is properly challenged by a motion for new trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 858.) “Once judgment has been entered . . . the court may not reconsider it and loses its unrestricted power to change the judgment. It may correct judicial error only through certain limited procedures such as motions for new trial and motions to vacate the judgment.” (Passanti v. Williams (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1602, 1606.) Where opposing party's evidence was sufficient to create triable fact issue, summary judgment was “error in law” for the purposes of CCP § 657. (Brewer v. Remington (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 14, 23.)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Plaintiff moves for a new trial on the following grounds: (1) insufficient evidence; and (2) the initial summary judgment was decided by error of law. (Motion p. 2:6-3-6.)

 

Insufficient Evidence

Procedural Deficiencies

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to provide the minutes of the court upon which their motion for new trial for insufficiency of the evidence relies. This provision is a strict statutory requirement pursuant to CCP § 658. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion is denied for procedural deficiencies. However, for both parties’ benefit, the Court now analyzes the motion’s substance.

 

Gross Negligence

Plaintiff argues that there is an insufficiency of the evidence to support granting of summary judgment since Defendants failed to present any evidence negating Plaintiff’s gross negligence claim.

To support a theory of gross negligence, “a plaintiff must allege facts showing either a want of even scant care or an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct. [Citations].” (Anderson v. Fitness Internat., LLC (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 867, 881.) Gross negligence “falls short of a reckless disregard of consequences, and differs from ordinary negligence only in degree, and not in kind. [Citations].” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff argues that Defendants moved for summary judgment on the grounds that Plaintiff waived their liability, yet failed to present evidence that they did not act with gross negligence, which may not be barred by a waiver agreement. (Eriksson v. Nunnik (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 826, 856.) Additionally, Plaintiff argues that they presented facts which could form the basis for a jury’s determination of gross negligence as an issue of fact. (Motion p. 5:14-16.)

However, Plaintiff misstates Defendants’ burden on a motion for summary judgment. Contemporary Services did not need to disprove gross negligence since Plaintiff’s complaint failed to allege facts that, on its face, support a theory of gross negligence. (See Joshi v. Fitness International, LLC (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 814, 828.) Plaintiff’s complaint only pleads facts alleging the existence of a known defect and the failure to cure it, stating, inter alia, that the “[subject premises] was in a dangerous and defective condition because of the unsafe curb grade, face, slope, width, height, depth, color and/or lack of visibility to pedestrians. Defendants . . . created the dangerous and defective condition, and/or knew or had notice of the . . . defective condition . . .” (Complaint ¶ 15.) However, “conduct demonstrating the failure to guard against, or warn of, a dangerous condition typically does not rise to the level of gross negligence.” (Anderson v. Fitness International (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 867, 881). Therefore, Plaintiff’s complaint did not allege facts giving rise to gross negligence and, consequently, Defendants had no duty to defend against such a claim.

 

Trivial Defect

Plaintiff argues that the Court, when finding that the subject curb defect was “trivial,” failed to consider “the extensive evidence presented by Plaintiff which a jury could rely upon” to find that the defect was not trivial in nature. (Motion p. 5:26 and 6:1-2.) However, just because Plaintiff disagrees with how the Court weighed his evidence does not mean there was an insufficiency of evidence for the purpose of CCP § 657. “[A] new trial shall not be granted upon the ground of insufficiency of the evidence . . . unless after weighing the evidence the court is convinced from the entire record, including reasonable inferences therefrom, that the court or jury clearly should have reached a different verdict or decision.” (CCP § 657)(emphasis added.) Here, Plaintiff argues that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the trivial nature of the defect since Plaintiff’s view was obstructed by the large crowd that he was walking with. (Motion p. 6:18-25.) In determining whether a defect is trivial, the court “should consider whether the walkway had any broken pieces or jagged edges and other conditions of the walkway surrounding the defect, such as whether there was debris, grease, or water concealing the defect, as well as whether the accident occurred at night in an unlighted area or some other condition obstructed a pedestrian’s view of the defect. [Citation].” (Stathoulis v. City of Montebello (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 559, 567.) However, a large crowd is not a condition which exacerbates the trivial nature of a defect. In fact, the evidence that there was a large crowd and Plaintiff was the only individual to trip and fall over the curb supports the Court’s initial finding that the defect was trivial in nature. Therefore, Plaintiff fails to present new circumstances, fatcs, or evidence showing that the initial motion for summary judgment was decided on an insufficiency of the evidence.

 

Error of Law

A motion for new trial based on error of law must be made on the minutes of the court. (CCP § 658.) Once again, Plaintiff failed to include the minutes of the court which their motion relies upon. Even assuming Plaintiff’s motion was not procedurally deficient, Plaintiff only supports their argument with a conclusory allegation that there are triable issues of material fact as to Defendants’ gross negligence and whether the defect was trivial in nature. (Motion p. 7:7-9.) As discussed supra, these arguments fail.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion for a new trial pursuant to CCP §§ 656 and 657 is DENIED.

Re:                         22STCV18224 City of Los Angeles vs Gregory

 

 

Plaintiff City of Los Angeles’s Request for Entry of Default

 

Defendant: Long Beach Towing & Storage, Inc.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

           

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED.

           

BACKGROUND

           

Plaintiff City of Los Angeles (“City”) filed a complaint on June 3, 2022, against Defendants Gregory Michael Hanley (“Hanley”), Long Beach Towing & Storage, Inc. (“Long Beach Towing), and Does 1 through 10 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff alleges that, on or about June 3, 2020, Defendants negligently operated or entrusted their vehicle as to cause injuries and damages to Plaintiff.

 

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendant Long Beach Towing.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1800.) 

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff seeks damages in the total amount of $353,916.45, representing $353,294.99 in special damages and $621.46 in costs. (See CIV-100 Item 2.)

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is denied for the following deficiencies:

 

1.      Plaintiff failed to provide a copy of the Statement of Damages. (CRC 3.250(a)(20).)

2.      Plaintiff failed to dismiss or request entry of default for Defendant Gregory Michael Hanley. (CRC 3.1800(a)(7).)

3.       CIV-100 and JUD-100 seek principal damages greater than those specified in Plaintiff’s statement of damages.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff City of Los Angeles’s request for default judgment against Defendant Long Beach Towing & Storage, Inc. is DENIED. Plaintiff is put on notice that further denials for deficiencies risk sanctions or dismissal pursuant to CCP § 538.410.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
