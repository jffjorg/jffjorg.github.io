DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV03965    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 32

PLEASE NOTE:   Parties are encouraged to meet and confer concerning this tentative ruling to determine if a resolution may be reached.  If the parties are unable to reach a resolution and a party intends to submit on this tentative ruling, the party must send an email to the Court at sscdept32@lacourt.org indicating that party’s intention to submit.  The email shall include the case number, date and time of the hearing, counsel’s contact information (if applicable), and the identity of the party submitting on this tentative ruling.  If the Court does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on this tentative ruling and there are no appearances at the hearing, the Court may place the motion off calendar or adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court.  If all parties do not submit on this tentative ruling, they should arrange to appear in-person or remotely.  Further, after the Court has posted/issued a tentative ruling, the Court has the inherent authority to prohibit the withdrawal of the subject motion and adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court. 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

32

HEARING DATE:

February 28, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV03965

MOTIONS: 

(1) Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

(2) Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories

(3) Compel Responses to Demand for Production of Documents

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Andrea Cortez

OPPOSING PARTY:

Unopposed

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

            Defendant Andrea Cortez (Defendant) moves to compel Plaintiff Hector Villegas (Plaintiff) to serve verified responses, without objections, to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)

 

Requests for Production

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.300, if a party fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (Code Civ. Pro § 2031.300 (b).) The party who fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand unless the court finds that the party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance or party’s failure was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (a)(1)- (2).)

 

Courts shall impose a monetary sanction against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection unless the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Here, Defendant served Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, on Plaintiff on March 27, 2023. (Gonzalez Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Extensions to respond, without objections, were granted until July 3, 2023. (Id. ¶ 8.) Since then, no responses have been served. Therefore, because responses have not been served, the motions to compel are granted.

 

Defendant also requests $510.00 in monetary sanctions for each of the three motions, against Plaintiff.  This represents an hourly rate of $150.00 and the $60.00 filing fee. (Id. ¶ 10.) The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Plaintiff has failed to respond. However, the amount requested is excessive due to the type of motions at issue and the fact no opposition was filed. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $855 (1.5 hours of attorney time to file and appear at the hearing, plus the $60 filing fee, for the three motions).

             

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff Hector Villegas shall provide verified responses, without objection, within 14 days.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Hector Villegas the reduced amount of $855. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

Defendant shall provide notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.

Motion to Continue Trial

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant 99 Cents Only Stores, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Unopposed

 

MOTION

 

Defendant 99 Cents Only Stores, LLC (“Defendant”) moves to continue trial. No opposition has been filed.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            The complaint was filed on May 21, 2021. Trial was initially set for November 18, 2022.

 

            The answer was filed on September 29, 2022.

 

            On November 1, 2022, pursuant to stipulation, the Court continued the trial date to October 24, 2023.

 

            On July 24, 2023, pursuant to stipulation, the Court continued the trial to the current date of April 3, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Legal Standard

 

 “Continuances are granted only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring a continuance.”¿ (In re Marriage of Falcone & Fyke (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 814, 823.)¿ A trial court has broad discretion in considering a request for a trial continuance.¿ (Pham v. Nguyen (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 11, 13-18.)¿ California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332 sets forth factors for the Court to consider in ruling on a motion to continue trial. 

 

“To ensure the prompt disposition of civil cases, the dates assigned for a trial are firm. All parties and their counsel must regard the date set for trial as certain.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(a).)

 

“A party seeking a continuance of the date set for trial, whether contested or uncontested or stipulated to by the parties, must make the request for a continuance by a noticed motion or an ex parte application under the rules in chapter 4 of this division, with supporting declarations. The party must make the motion or application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(b).)

 

“Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits. The court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance. Circumstances that may indicate good cause include:

 

(1)   The unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(2)   The unavailability of a party because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(3)   The unavailability of trial counsel because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(4)   The substitution of trial counsel, but only where there is an affirmative showing that the substitution is required in the interests of justice;

(5)   The addition of a new party if:

(A) The new party has not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial; or

(B)  The other parties have not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial in regard to the new party’s involvement in the case;

(6)   A party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or

(7)   A significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which the case is not ready for trial.”

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c).)

 

“In ruling on a motion or application for continuance, the court must consider all the facts and circumstances that are relevant to the determination. These may include:

 

(1)   The proximity of the trial date;

(2)   Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

(3)   The length of the continuance requested;

(4)   The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

(5)   The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance;

(6)   If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay;

(7)   The court’s calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

(8)   Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

(9)   Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

(10)  Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

(11)  Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(d).)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant asserts that the parties have conducted significant discovery in this case and are ready to begin mediation and settlement negotiations. A mediation is scheduled for July 18, 2024. (Anz Decl. ¶ 6.) As a result, the parties have stipulated to continuing the trial and all related dates to at least six months in order to mediate, and if that is unsuccessful, prepare for trial afterward. Therefore, the parties seek a trial date in October 2024 or beyond. (Id. ¶ 12, Exh. A.)

 

Therefore, considering there has only been two prior continuances and the parties are seeking to resolve the matter through mediation, the Court finds good cause to continue.

All discovery and pre-trial motion cut-off dates shall be in accordance with the new trial date.

Compel Deposition of Defendants’ Employee Gilbert Lopez

MOVING PARTY: 

Plaintiffs Phillip Tirman, M.D., Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung

OPPOSING PARTY: 

Defendant The Bicycle Casino, LP

 

 

BACKGROUND 

 

On June 29, 2022, Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Estate of Jonathan Dongin Jung, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung filed a complaint against Defendant The Bicycle Casino, Inc. for the wrongful death of Decedent Jonathan Dongin Jung (“Decedent”). Plaintiffs alleges that Defendant’s security guards attacked Decedent and caused his death.¿¿ 

 

Plaintiffs Phillip Tirman, M.D., Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung (“Plaintiffs”) now move to compel the in-person deposition of Defendant The Bicycle Casino, LP’s (“Defendant”) employee, Gilbert Lopez. Both parties seek monetary sanctions. Defendant opposes and Plaintiff replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARD  

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A party desiring to take an oral deposition shall give a notice in writing which states the specification of reasonably particularly of any materials to be produced by the deponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.220, subd. (a)(4).) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)¿¿ 

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a) provides: “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”¿ Section 2025.450 requires the Court to compel the deposition unless it finds a valid objection was served under section 2025.410.¿ 

¿ 

A motion brought to compel a deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition … by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

If a motion to compel deposition is granted, sanctions are mandatory in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)¿ 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

On January 23, 2024, after receiving Defendant’s objection, Plaintiffs sent a letter to Defendant’s counsel regarding Lopez’s deposition. (Jones Decl. ¶ 37, Exh. J.) Defendant did not respond. Therefore, it appears Plaintiffs attempted to resolve the issue in good faith. 

 

DISCUSSION 

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek the deposition of Gilbert Lopez (“Lopez”), an employee of Defendant, who allegedly was part of the security team that was present during Decedent’s death. (Jones Decl. ¶ 11.) Plaintiffs initially sought to take Lopez’s deposition, but Plaintiffs would not agree to a remote deposition, arguing that Defendant offered no legitimate reason for the request. (Id. ¶ 16–18.) Plaintiffs then sent letters to Defendant on November 16, 2023, December 4, 2023, and December 11, 2023, seeking dates for a deposition. Defendant did not respond. (Id. ¶¶ 25–28.) Then, Plaintiff unilaterally served a deposition notice. In response to Plaintiffs’ notice, on January 9, 2024, Defendant offered three dates in January 2024 for a deposition. (Id. ¶ 30, Exh. G.)

 

On January 11, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendant a Second Amended Notice of Deposition setting an in-person deposition for January 25, 2024. (Id. ¶ 14, Exh. A.) Defendant’s counsel sent a letter on January 19, 2024, insisting that Lopez be deposed remotely because his work schedule makes it difficult to travel. (Id. ¶ 32, Exh. H.) Defendant served a formal objection that day. (Id. ¶ 24, Exh. I.) The deposition did not go forward. In their meet and confer letter, Plaintiffs inquired about whether conducting the deposition in a conference room at Lopez’s work, or at a location 1.3 miles away would be more convenient. (Id. ¶ 43–44.) Defendant did not respond.

 

In opposition, Defendant reveals that Lopez is no longer employed by Defendant, and thus, is under no obligation to present him for deposition. (Opp., 3; Gaddini Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendant also states that every other deposition in this case took place remotely. (Opp., 4.) This conflicts with Plaintiffs’ assertion that three other security guards in this case appeared in-person for their depositions. (Jones Decl. ¶ 5.)

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant never informed them that Lopez was no longer employed, despite seeking to depose him since June 2023. (Reply Jone Decl. ¶ 3.)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1010(b) provides in relevant part, “[a]ny party, other than the deponent, or attorney of record may appear and participate in an oral deposition by telephone, videoconference, or other remote electronic means.” (emphasis added.)  Additionally, before a deposition, any party or deponent may move for a protective order. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420(a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: . . . (4) That the deposition be taken at a place other than that specified in the deposition notice, if it is within a distance permitted by Sections 2025.250 and 2025.260.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420(b)(4).)

 

First, Defendant did not move for a protective order to request a remote deposition. Moreover, Defendant’s argument appears to relate to the costs of an in-person deposition, but does not establish unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. Next, Defendant does not indicate exactly when Lopez ended his employment with Defendant. Defendant attaches no evidence other than an attorney declaration in support. Plaintiffs argue that Lopez may still be employed by the Casino, through Parkwest Bicycle Casino LLC which is also represented by Defendant’s counsel in this matter. (Reply Jones Decl. ¶ 7–9.)

 

Therefore, given this ambiguity, counsel for Defendant is to appear at the hearing and answer whether Lopez is an employee of a party to this case, and if not, when his employment was terminated. If Lopez was not an employee when the notices of deposition were served, then Plaintiff must pursue a subpoena to compel Lopez’s attendance at a deposition and follow the proper procedures for deposing a non-party witness. Otherwise, the Court will compel an in-person deposition of Lopez.

Motion for Protective Order 

MOVING PARTY: 

Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung 

OPPOSING PARTY: 

None 

 

BACKGROUND 

 

On June 29, 2022, Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Estate of Jonathan Dongin Jung, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung filed a complaint against Defendant The Bicycle Casino, Inc. for the wrongful death of Decedent Jonathan Dongin Jung (“Decedent”). Plaintiffs alleges that Defendant’s security guards attacked Decedent and caused his death.¿¿ 

  

 Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung (“Plaintiffs”) now move for a protective order allowing Insoon Jung (“Insoon”) to not respond to written discovery from Defendant TBC, L.P. [The Bicycle Casino LP] (“Defendant”). Plaintiffs argue that Insoon suffers from dementia, Alzheimer’s Disease and would be burdened by responding to the following discovery responses: Special Interrogatories, Set Three, Requests for Admissions, Set Two, Request for Production of Documents, Set Three, Form Interrogatories, Set Two, Supplemental Interrogatories, and Supplemental Requests for Production. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

  

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020, a court shall limit the scope of discovery if the court “determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” A “court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Id.)¿The “motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.” (Id.)¿ 

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(b).)

 

MEET AND CONFER 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel declares that he asked Defendant’s counsel to withdraw its discovery requests regarding Insoon. (Jones Decl. ¶ 43, Exh. A.) Defendant’s counsel did not respond. Therefore, Plaintiffs attempted to resolve the matter in good faith.

 

DISCUSSION

 

As an initial matter, although Plaintiff also moves for a protective order under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420, that section applies to depositions. Here, it appears Plaintiff is only seeking to limit written discovery. Therefore, the Court will only rule based on section 2017.020.  

 

Here, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant is aware of Insoon’s cognitive inability to respond and argue the discovery was intended to harass her. (Jones Decl. ¶ 4–5.) Plaintiffs note they have filed a motion to preclude Insoon from testifying at trial under Evidence Code section 701. Nevertheless, Plaintiffs’ counsel asserts that he directed Insoon’s husband to help gather evidence in response to the written discovery. (Id. ¶ 40.) The responses therefore contain objections and factual assertions. (Id. ¶ 41.)

 

The Court notes that it previously granted a motion for a trial preference based on Insoon’s condition. (Min. Order, 1/30/24.) However, the argument that Insoon is not competent to respond to the discovery suggests the need for a guardian ad litem. (See Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504–05 [A guardian ad litem is allowed to verify discovery responses].) The Court notes that no application to appoint a guardian ad litem in this case has been filed. Plaintiffs also do not address this in their motion. Since a guardian ad litem could verify Insoon’s responses, and seeing that Plaintiffs’ counsel has enlisted her husband in responding, Plaintiffs have not shown that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of the discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Therefore, the motion for a protective order is denied.

 

Seeing as no opposition was filed, the Court declines to impose monetary sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER 

 

Therefore, the Court denies Plaintiffs’ Motion for Protective Order.

MOTIONS: 

(1)   Compel Plaintiffs’ Responses to Form Interrogatories

(2)   Compel Plaintiffs’ Responses to Special Interrogatories

(3)   Compel Plaintiffs’ Responses to Request for Production of Documents

(4)   Deem Admitted Request for Admissions

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant The Bicycle Casino, LP

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 29, 2022, Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Estate of Jonathan Dongin Jung, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung filed a complaint against Defendant The Bicycle Casino, Inc. for the wrongful death of Decedent Jonathan Dongin Jung (“Decedent”). Plaintiffs alleges that Defendant’s security guards attacked Decedent and caused his death.¿¿ 

           

Defendant The Bicycle Casino, LP (“Defendant”) moves to deem admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One and Set Two, to Compel Plaintiffs Vanessa Jung Tirman, Jung Sik Jung, and Insoon Jung (“Plaintiffs”) responses to: Form Interrogatories, Set One, Set Two, and Supplemental Interrogatories; Special Interrogatories, Set One, Two, and Three, and Supplemental Special Interrogatories; Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Set Two, Set Three and Supplemental requests. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs and their counsel. Plaintiffs oppose and Defendant replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Deem admitted

 

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).)  The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) 

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

Compel Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Compel Request for Production

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.300, if a party fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (Code Civ. Pro § 2031.300 (b).) The party who fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand unless the court finds that the party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance or party’s failure was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (a)(1)- (2).)

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c); Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Here, Defendant asserts that it served Request for Admissions, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One on all three Plaintiffs on August 18, 2022. (Derflinger Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1–3.) Defendant granted an extension and on January 13, 2023, Plaintiffs provided responses without verifications. (Id. ¶ 3, Exh. 4–6.)

 

On June 6, 2023, Defendant served Special Interrogatories, Set Two and Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two on all three Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs provided responses without verifications on July 6, 2023.

 

On October 24, 2023, Defendant served Requests for Admission, Set Two; Form Interrogatories, Set Two, and Supplemental Interrogatories; Special Interrogatories, Set Three and Supplemental Interrogatories; and Request for Production of Documents, Set Three and Supplemental Requests on Plaintiffs Insoon Jung and Jung Sik Jung only. (Id. ¶ 4, Exh. 7–10.) The responses were due November 24, 2023, and on November 22, 2023, Plaintiffs Insoon Jung and Jung Sik Jung served responses without verifications. (Id. ¶ 5, Exh. 11–14.) Defendant argues that no verifications to the discovery have been served. Defendant’s counsel acknowledges that it cannot receive verified responses from Vanessa Jung Tirman, who has since passed away, and Insoon Jung, who appears to lack capacity to verify the discovery. (Id. ¶ 9.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs show that Insoon Jung and Jung Sik Jung served verifications to Request for Admissions, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One, on January 23, 2023. (See Jones Decl., Exh. C.) Vanessa Jung Tirman also provided a verification for Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Ibid.) Insoon Jung signed a verification for RFP, Set Two and Special Interrogatories, Set Two. (Id., Exh. D.) Plaintiffs also show verifications from Jung Sik Jung to RFP, Set Two, Special Interrogatories, Set Two, Form Interrogatories, Sets 1–3;[1] Request for Production, Set One, and Supplemental Requests for Production, Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Admissions. (Ibid.) These verifications were signed on January 17, 2024.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs argue the motions are moot since Jung Sik Jung has served verifications. (Jones Decl. ¶ 10.) They also argue the motions are moot since Vanessa Jung Tirman is deceased, and Insoon Jung’s cognition is impaired. (Jones Decl. ¶¶ 7, 20–22; Opp., 3.) Plaintiffs argue sanctions should not be imposed because they were working to retrieve the verifications, and Defendant was aware of Vanessa Jung Tirman’s death and Insoon Jung’s declining health. (Opp., 10.)

 

            In reply, Defendant acknowledges that Vanessa Jung Tirman cannot provide verifications due to her death and that Insoon Jung is not mentally competent. (Reply, 3.) However, it asks the Court to either “affirm that the discovery has no responses and cannot be responded to or that the individual with power of attorney who has the legal ability to provide verifications on her behalf provide verifications.” (Ibid.)

 

While Plaintiffs contend that Jung Sik Jung has served verifications of all outstanding discovery, they did not provide proof of verifications of Defendant’s Request for Admissions, Set Two, Special Interrogatories, Set Three, and Requests for Production, Set Three. (See Jones Decl., Exh. C–D.) Unverified discovery responses are tantamount to no response at all, and are subject to a motion to compel responses (rather than a motion to compel further responses).¿ (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal. App. 3d 632, 635-36.)¿ Therefore, because verifications have not been served, the motion to deem admitted Request for Admission, Set Two is granted. Additionally, the motions to compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Three and Requests for Production, Set Three are granted. The motions to compel the remaining responses are denied as moot since verifications were served prior to this hearing.

 

Similarly, because Vanessa Jung Tirman is deceased, the motions against her are denied as moot.

 

As for Insoon Jung, the Court notes that a party who is incompetent or lacks capacity may have a guardian ad litem appointed, who is allowed to verify discovery responses. (See Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504–05.) Therefore, the motion to compel is granted as to all discovery responses that have not received verifications from Insoon Jung.[2] Plaintiffs may file an application to appoint a guardian ad litem and have the guardian ad litem verify the responses. Additionally, since verified responses were not served, the motion to deem admitted Request for Admissions, Set Two is granted.

 

Defendant requests $1,800 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiffs and their counsel of record for each separate motion, representing an hourly rate of $225.00 and the $60.00 filing fee. (Derflinger Decl. ¶ 12.) The Court finds sanctions are warranted as to the motions against Jung Sik Jung since verifications were served after the motion was filed. However, the amount requested is excessive. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $1,072.50 (4.5 hours of attorney time to file and appear at the hearing, plus the $60 filing fee).      

 

As for Insoon Jung, the Court finds that imposing sanctions would be unjust. However, sanctions are mandatory for the motion to deem admitted. Therefore, the Court will award $60 against Insoon Jung for the filing cost of this motion.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, as to Plaintiff Vanessa Jung Tirman, the motions to compel responses and deem admitted are DENIED as moot.

 

As to Plaintiff Jung Sik Jung, the motions to compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Three and Requests for Production, Set Three are GRANTED. Plaintiff Jung Sik Jung shall serve verified responses, without objections, within 30 days. Additionally, the motion to deem admitted Requests for Admission, Set Two, against Jung Sik Jung is GRANTED.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Jung Sik Jung and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,072.50. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

 The motions to compel the remaining discovery responses and deem admitted Request for Admission, Set One against Jung Sik Jung are DENIED as moot.

 

As to Plaintiff Insoon Jung, the motions to compel responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One and Set Two, Requests for Production, Set One and Set Three and Supplemental Requests,  and Special Interrogatories, Set Three and Supplemental Interrogatories are GRANTED. Plaintiff Insoon Jung shall serve verified responses, without objections, within 30 days. Additionally, the motion to deem admitted Requests for Admission, Set Two, against Insoon Jung is GRANTED.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Insoon Jung and her counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $60. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The remaining motions to compel responses and deem admitted Request for Admission, Set One against Insoon Jung are DENIED as moot.

 

 

Defendant shall provide notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.
