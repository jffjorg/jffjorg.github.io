DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02007    Hearing Date: June 18, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Michele Bidinger,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Ralph, Estate of Shaffer, et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  22VECV02007         107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Motion to Strike

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

Grant as to defendant Abrienne Schaffer; deny as to all other Defendants.

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Michele Bidinger “Plaintiff” resided in an apartment building (the “Apartment”), which was owned by defendant Ralph Shaffer (“Ralph”), for which defendant Abrienne Schaffer (“Abrienne”) was the “acting landlord,” which was managed by defendants Vivian Sypien (“Vivian”) and Andrew Sypien (“Andrew” and, together with, Abrienne, and Vivian, “Opposing Parties”), and for which defendant Isaac Stewart (“Isaac” and, together with Ralph and Opposing Parties, “Defendants”) was “acting resident manager.” (SAC p. 2.) Plaintiff vacated the Apartment on November 15, 2021. (Id. at p. 3.) During her tenancy at the Apartment, she alleges she was subject to various harassment related to her tenancy by Defendants in violation of The City Of Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance, Los Angeles, Cal. Mun. Code §¿45.33, et seq. (Ibid.) 

 

On November 15, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”). Plaintiff subsequently amended the Complaint and then, after this court sustained a motion to strike against the first amended complaint, Plaintiff amended the first amended complaint by filing a second amended complaint (the “SAC”). The SAC alleges four causes of action: (1) statutory violations, (2) breach of implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) negligent supervision.

 

On January 5, 2024, Plaintiff propounded a set of special interrogatories on each of Opposing Parties. (Motion Exh. 1.) On February 22, 2024, Abrienne served Plaintiff with her responses to the special interrogatories. (Id. at Exh. 2.)

 

On April 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel further responses to a special interrogatory (the “Motion”). Opposing Parties opposed the Motion on May 1, 2024, and Plaintiff replied to Opposing Parties’ opposition six days later, on May 7, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

All motions to compel further responses to written discovery must be brought within 45 days of receiving the challenged responses, or as otherwise agreed upon in writing.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (c), 2031.310, subd. (b)(1), 2033.290, subd. (c).)¿ This deadline is jurisdictional, and courts are without authority to grant untimely motions.¿ (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.) Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

 

Plaintiff submits with the Motion a declaration by her attorney signed under penalty of perjury, which states that “[t]he time to bring a motion to compel has been extended by Defendant until April 18, 2024.” (Kurtz Decl. ¶ 4.) The declaration also attaches copies of the e-mails in which Parties make such agreement. (Id. at Exh. 3.) We are satisfied that all filings made in connection with the Motion are timely.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A Motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must always be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration evidencing a “reasonable and good faith attempt an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2016.040, 2030.300, subd. (b).)

 

Plaintiff submits with the Motion a declaration by her attorney signed under penalty of perjury, which attaches copies of e-mails exchanged with Opposing Parties’ counsel in which they discuss potential resolution to the issue raised by the Motion. (Kurtz Decl. Exh. 3.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met in good faith.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action. (Ibid.) 

“Relevant evidence is defined in Evidence Code section 210 as evidence ‘having any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.’ The test of relevance is whether the evidence tends logically, naturally, and by reasonable inference to establish material facts. The trial court has broad discretion in determining the relevance of evidence but lacks discretion to admit irrelevant evidence.” (Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 148, internal quotations and citations omitted; see also Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 97 [“[I]n the context of discovery, evidence is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a¿settlement.”].) 

A court “retains the discretion to weigh the burden of compliance against the likelihood of producing helpful information, to avoid duplicative production, and to narrow demands if appropriate to balance the reasonable concerns of both parties.” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1497.) “[Discovery] statutes must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper by virtue of well-established causes for denial.” (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court In and For Merced County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 377.) But, “discovery, like all matters of procedure, has its ultimate and necessary boundaries.” (Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19.) 

 

A party on whom interrogatories are propounded is required to refer to all sources of information that are under the party's control. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220, subds. (a)–(b).) A responding party’s duty also encompasses the requirement that it make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information sought by the interrogatory, except where that information is readily available to the propounding party. (Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal. App.4th 1496, 1504; Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782 [“If a party is unable to fully answer it should set forth the efforts made to secure the information.”].) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220, subd. (b).)¿¿ 

¿ 

If the responding party responds with inappropriate objections or evasive or incomplete answers, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2030.300.) A motion to compel further is properly brought to challenge “boilerplate” responses to special interrogatories. (Best Products, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1190; see also Hernandez v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.) The responding party bears the burden of justifying any objection or insufficient answer to a special interrogatory. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–21 [“Defendants here had the burden of showing facts from which the trial court might find that the interrogatories were interposed for improper purposes. The statute does not require any showing of good cause for the serving and filing of interrogatories.”].) “Nevertheless, if interrogatories are reasonably subject to objection as calling for the disclosure of matters so remote from the subject matter of the action as disclosed by the issues framed by the pleadings as to make their disclosure of little or no practical benefit to the party seeking the disclosure or if to answer them would place a burden and expense upon the parties to whom the interrogatories are propounded which should be equitably borne by the propounder or if the interrogatories are so framed as to require the disclosure of relevant as well as irrelevant matter, the trial court in the exercise of its discretion may refuse to order such interrogatories answered.” (Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County, supra at 19.)¿ 

 

“The disclosure of the names and addresses of potential witnesses¿is a routine and essential part of pretrial discovery.” (People v. Dixon (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 414, 443.) However, “[d]iscovery may be prohibited where the information violates the right to privacy and is not necessary to the prosecution of the matter.” (Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1254.) “The California Constitution expressly provides that all people have the inalienable right to privacy.” (Johnson v. Superior Court (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 1050, 1068, disapproved on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557, citing Cal. Const., art. I, § 1.) “[T]he California Constitution expressly recognizes a right of privacy [that] is considered broader than the implied federal right to privacy.” (Ibid.)

 

Addresses are private. (Puerto v. Superior Court, supra, at 1254; Lori v. Medical Board (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 462, 468; City of San Jose v. Superior Court (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1017, fn. 5.) There is a privacy interest against disclosure of addresses and telephone numbers. (Planned Parenthood Golden Gate v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 347, 367, disapproved on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court, supra, at 557; Morales v. Superior Court (1979) 99 Cal.App.3d 283, 291; Board of Trustees v. Superior Court (1981) 119 Cal.App.3d 516, 529, disapproved on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court, supra, at 557.) Individuals have a reasonable expectation of privacy in their addresses, and the disclosure of such addresses constitutes a serious invasion of that privacy interest. (Puerto v. Superior Court, supra, at 1252.) However, the privacy interest in contact information like addresses is not strong. (Id. at 1253 [“[T]he requested information, while personal, is not particularly sensitive, as it is merely contact information, not medical or financial details, political affiliations, sexual relationships, or personnel information.”].)

 

Where a party resists discovery on the basis of a constitutional right to privacy, the privacy interests must be balanced against the need for discovery. (Planned Parenthood Golden Gate v. Superior Court, supra, at 357–58.) “The strength of an individual's interest in keeping personal information private depends in large part on the consequences of disclosure.” (Id. at 360.) The existence of a risk of “the infliction of threats, force and violence” to the person whose address is being sought through discovery is a consequence of disclosure that strengthens their privacy interest. (Id. at 360–62.) In Planned Parenthood Golden Gate v. Superior Court, the party resisting discovery, an abortion provider, was not compelled to produce the contact information of its employees and volunteers where the party showed “specific evidence” that the employees and volunteers “could well face unique and very real threats not just to their privacy, but to their safety and well-being if personal information about them is disclosed”; there was evidence that the parties seeking discovery and their attorneys engaged in anti-abortion protest activities; and party resisting discovery had offered to facilitate access to its employees and volunteers as witnesses without having to disclose their personal information, rendering the discovery no longer necessary. (Id. at 360–67.)

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff brings the Motion against all Defendants. (Notice of Motion p. 1.) This includes two Defendants, Ralph and Isaac, on whom the discovery request at issue was not even propounded. While the special interrogatories at issue were propounded on Vivian and Andrew, who join Abrienne in opposing the Motion, it is unclear from the Motion if Vivian and Andrew responded to the special interrogatories and, if so, whether those answers are also contested. Plaintiff clarifies in her reply papers that she seeks to compel further responses to the special interrogatories from only Abrienne. (Reply p. 2.)

 

Accordingly, to the extent that the Motion is brought against any of the Defendants other than Abrienne, it is DENIED.

 

The only special interrogatory at issue in the Motion is Specially Prepared Interrogatory No. 16, which seeks from Abrienne the current residence address of her co-defendant Isaac, who is also the father of her grandchild. (Motion p. 3; Separate Statement p. 2; Opposition p. 2.)

 

Opposing Parties argue in their opposition to the Motion that Abrienne should not be compelled to produce Isaac’s address because she fears that Plaintiff could jeopardize the safety of Isaac and his child. (Opposition pp. 3–4.) However, Opposing Parties do not cite any authority to their point that safety concerns are independent grounds to deny a motion to compel further and Abrienne did not raise this objection in her response to the discovery request. We are not required to consider arguments unsupported by meaningful discussion or legal authority, and we decline to do so here. (Mansell v. Board of Education (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 539, 545–46; In re S.C. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 396, 408 [“When a point is asserted without¿argument¿and authority for the proposition, it is deemed to be without foundation and requires no discussion by the reviewing court,” internal quotation marks omitted.].) However, we will consider these safety concerns when we evaluate the objection Abrienne did raise which was on privacy grounds.

 

Opposing Parties argue in their opposition that “[t]here is a privacy issue at stake here.” (Opposition p. 5.) Abrienne objects in her responses to the special interrogatories on the same grounds. (Separate Statement p. 2.) We agree that Isaac has a constitutionally protected reasonable expectation of privacy in his address, and by seeking its disclosure, Plaintiff seeks to invade that interest. (See Puerto v. Superior Court, supra, 158 Cal.App.4th 1242 at 1252.) We must therefore balance Isaac’s privacy interest against Plaintiff’s need for its disclosure.

 

Ordinarily, Isaac’s privacy interest in his address is a weak one, unless some consequence of disclosure would strengthen it. (See Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242 at 1253.) Following Planned Parenthood Golden Gate v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 347 at 360–67, the existence of a risk, as Plaintiff fears, that Isaac’s address would give Plaintiff “easy access” to Isaac and that “Plaintiff will retaliate against [Isaac] and potentially place [Abrienne’s] young grandchild in harm's way” would be a consequence of disclosure of Isaac’s address that would strengthen his privacy interest. (Abrienne Decl. ¶ 6; Opposition p. 4.) However, the mere assertion of a fear by the party resisting discovery does not, by itself, show the existence of such a risk. Unlike in Planned Parenthood Golden Gate v. Superior Court, there is not, in this case, “specific evidence” that the risk of harm exists, there is no showing that Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s attorney have any affiliations to whom they may disclose Isaac’s address and who might harm Isaac, and Opposing Parties have not offered to facilitate access to Isaac without the need for the disclosure of his address. Opposing Parties submit a declaration by Abrienne in which she states that she “believe[s] there is a substantial reason to fear retaliation by Plaintiff because when she moved out of the my rental property, she appeared to be angry and the male friend she brought with her made me concerned for my own safety,” apparently because the man helping Plaintiff move “was wearing a court ordered ankle bracelet, literally broke down one of the doors and caused damage to the property.” (Abrienne Decl. ¶¶ 7–8.) However, there is no connection expressed between this man and the instant case or Isaac, and no evidence is raised to show that Plaintiff wishes to harm Isaac or Abrienne’s grandchild or that Plaintiff creates a risk of harm. This falls short of “specific evidence” that Plaintiff and the unidentified man who helped her move apartments create a risk of harm to Isaac if Plaintiff is able to discover Isaac’s address. Therefore, Abrienne’s fears of a risk of harm do not strengthen Isaac’s privacy interest in his address.

 

Opposing Parties also contend that Isaac’s address is not discoverable because Plaintiff seeks it for an improper purpose: to serve him with the Complaint and summons rather than to contact him as a witness. (Opposition pp. 4–5.) However, discovery is broad and covers any “relevant” information. (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court In and For Merced County, supra, 56 Cal.2d 355 at 377.) Evidence is relevant if it “might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a¿settlement.” (Kirkland v. Superior Court, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th 92 at 97.) Under such a broad definition of relevance, Isaac’s address is relevant to the instant litigation and discoverable.

 

Thus, we conclude that Plaintiff’s right to discover and need for disclosure of Isaac’s address outweighs Isaac’s privacy interest therein.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion against Abrienne.

I. BACKGROUND

 

  1. Factual

 

Plaintiff Michele Bidinger “Plaintiff” resided in an apartment building (the “Apartment”), which was owned by defendant Ralph Shaffer (“Ralph”), for which defendant Abrienne Schaffer (“Abrienne”) was the “acting landlord,” which was managed by defendants Vivian Sypien (“Vivian”) and Andrew Sypien (“Andrew” and, together with, Abrienne, and Vivian, “Moving Parties”), and for which defendant Isaac Stewart (“Isaac” and, together with Ralph and Moving Parties, “Defendants”) was “acting resident manager.” (SAC p. 2.) Plaintiff vacated the Apartment on November 15, 2021. (Id. at p. 3.) During her tenancy at the Apartment, she alleges she was subject to various harassment related to her tenancy by Defendants in violation of The City Of Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance, Los Angeles, Cal. Mun. Code § 45.33, et seq. (Ibid.)

 

  1. Procedural

 

On November 15, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”). On December 29, 2023, Moving Parties moved to strike punitive damages from the Complaint and on January 5, 2024, Plaintiff served and filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”), thereby mooting the motion to strike.

 

On March 12, 2024, this court granted Moving Parties’ motion to strike punitive damages from the FAC with leave to amend.

 

On March 25, 2024, Plaintiff attempted to file a second amended complaint (the “SAC”), but due to a typographical error in the filing, it was not successfully filed until sometime later. The SAC alleges four causes of action: (1) statutory violations, (2) breach of implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) negligent supervision.

 

On April 18, 2024, Moving Parties filed the instant motion to strike (the “Motion”) punitive damages from the second amended complaint (the “SAC”). On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the Motion, and on May 21, 2024, Moving Parties replied to Plaintiff’s opposition.

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

            A. Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure § 435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Per California Code of Civil Procedure § 1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading.  (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.)

 

Here, the Motion, the Opposition, and the Reply were all timely served and filed.

 

            B. The Meet & Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a motion to strike to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the motion to strike. The Moving Parties’ counsel submits a declaration with the Motion that she conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone prior to the filing of the Motion and that they were unable to resolve the issues raised therein. (Mantovani Decl. ¶¶ 2–3.)

 

We are thus satisfied that the meet and confer requirement of California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) has been met.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of punitive damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.)

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “A claim for exemplary damage may be supported by pleading that the wrong was committed willfully or with a design to injure.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055.) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice. However, malice does not require actual intent to harm. Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)

 

“Punitive damages may not be pleaded generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra, at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in section 3294, it must include specific factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for punitive damages.”].)

 

“[P]unitive damages may be awarded in an action by a residential tenant based on the landlord's interference with peaceful possession. Punitive damages likewise are recoverable for… the infliction of emotional distress.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th 1004 at 1055, internal citations omitted.) However, punitive damages are not recoverable for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment because “breach of the covenant gives rise to an action in contract,” for which punitive damages may not be recovered. (Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 873, 896–97.) But, “[i]f the landlord's acts or omissions affect the tenant's use of the property and compel the tenant to vacate, there is a constructive eviction,” for which punitive damages are recoverable. (Id. at 897.) In Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 920, a residential tenant “pleaded sufficient facts to support her prayer for exemplary damages” when her complaint both “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions” and “alleged that defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance,… acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’”

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

Moving Parties move to strike paragraph 19, including its subparts a through e, of the SAC and the SAC’s prayer for punitive damages. (Notice of Motion p. 2.)

 

Paragraph 19 of the SAC reads, in full,

 

19. Plaintiff is informed and believes and based thereon alleges that Defendants, excluding the Estate of Ralph Shaffer, in doing the things alleged above, have been guilty of oppression, fraud and malice. In particular, the following acts are examples of this oppression, fraud and/or malice

 

a.      Defendants, collectively, conspires to make Plaintiff’s home environment so miserable and unbearable that she was forced to move away, and the conduct described in paragraph 12 and its subparts were acts designed to further that conspiracy and constructively evict Plaintiff from her home.

 

b.      Each of the defendants acted with oppression and malice by failing, refusing or delaying necessary maintenance, repairs, and replacements that, as landlords and an agents of the landlords, they had duty to provide. Examples of this conduct are described in particularity in paragraphs 12(a), 12(g), 12(h), and 12(j).

 

c.      Mrs. Sypien and Ms. Shaffer acted with oppression and malice by creating financial difficulties and uncertainties, which were designed to and had the affect of instilling fear of the initiation of eviction proceedings. Examples of this conduct are described in particularity in paragraphs 12(b), 12(c), 12(d), 12(e), and 12(f).

 

d.      Each of the defendants acted with oppression and malice by encouraging or, at a minimum, permitting Mr. Stewart, who was Mrs. Shaffer’s son in law, to engage in the conduct alleged in paragraphs 12(i), 12(k), 12(l), 12(m), 12(o) to 12(v).

 

e.      Mr. Sapien acted with malice when violating Plaintiff’s privacy and trespassing as described in paragraph 12(n).

 

Moreover, Defendants' conduct, in which they placed their own economic gain and self- interest above their obligations to Plaintiff and attempted to exclude Plaintiff from her home and personal possessions, constitutes fraud, malice and oppression sufficient to justify an award of punitive damages. As a result of Defendants' outrageous conduct, Plaintiff is entitled to recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing Defendants.

 

(SAC pp. 8–9.) Paragraph 12 of the SAC, which the above quoted paragraph 19 makes frequent reference to, lists by date “[s]pecific instances of [Defendants’] violations [of The City Of Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance, Los Angeles, Cal. Mun. Code § 45.33, et seq.].” (Id. at p. 3.)

 

Moving Parties argue that “the allegations in the SAC fail to reflect facts showing Defendants' actions and intentions that may point to oppression, fraud or malice as opposed to some level of negligence” and do “not sufficient[ly] allege punitive damages with the requisite particularity.” (Motion p. 4, emphasis original.)

 

However, each allegation about Moving Parties’ intentions to act with oppression or malice in each subpart of paragraph 19 references all or part of paragraph 12. And so while paragraph 19, read in isolation, may contain only “conclusory characterizations” about Moving Parties’ intentions, does not allege sufficient facts as are required to support a claim for punitive damages, and does not plead punitive damages with the required specificity, when read in conjunction with paragraph 12 and in the context of the SAC as a whole, we reach a different conclusion. Paragraph 12 contains specific factual allegations. Each subpart of paragraph 12 describes a certain incident between Plaintiff and Defendants that Plaintiff alleges constituted harassment, identifies which Defendants were involved in what way, and specifies when such incident occurred. By referencing paragraph 12, each subpart of paragraph 19 anchors and supports its broad allegations about Defendants’ intentions with specific factual allegations about their actions. Thus, we disagree with Moving Parties’ objection that the SAC fails to plead facts pointing to oppression, fraud, or malice, and we disagree with Moving Parties’ objection that the SAC does not plead punitive damages with sufficient particularity.

 

For example, subpart b of paragraph 19 regards maintenance, repairs, and replacements in the Apartment and, in support of its charge of oppression and malice by each of the Defendants, it references subparts a, g, h, and j of paragraph 12. These subparts of paragraph 12 describe (1) an incident in 2018 when Plaintiff was locked out of the garage because her requests for a replacement garage door remote control were ignored, (2) an incident occurring on two occasions in October 2020 when Plaintiff’s requests for service on the fire alarm and carbon monoxide alarm were ignored, (3) an incident in October and November 2020 when Plaintiff’s requests for dishwasher maintenance were ignored, and (4) a similar incident in January 2021 when Plaintiff’s request for dishwasher maintenance was ignored. As far as which Defendants are specifically alleged to be responsible for these incidents, the SAC earlier charges “[all] Defendants individually and collectively” of “[f]ailing to perform necessary repairs and maintenance.” (SAC p. 3.) Thus, the SAC alleges that, and describes examples of specific incidents in which, all Defendants, individually and collectively, failed to, refused to, or delayed maintenance, repairs, and replacements that were needed in the Apartment despite Plaintiff having notified Defendants of the Apartment’s specific service needs and despite their duty to provide such maintenance, repairs, or replacements as Plaintiff’s landlords or as agents of her landlord. The SAC alleges that such conduct evidences oppression and malice. We agree. Like the residential tenant in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, 101 Cal.App.3d 903 at 920, Plaintiff may seek punitive damages based on her specific factual allegations that Defendants had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the Apartment because of her requests for maintenance and based on her more general allegation that, by failing to remedy the defects in spite of this actual knowledge, they “acted with oppression and malice.” (SAC p. 9.) Thus, like the court in Stoiber v. Honeychuck, supra, at 920, we similarly conclude that Plaintiff has adequately pled allegations that may support a claim for punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion.
