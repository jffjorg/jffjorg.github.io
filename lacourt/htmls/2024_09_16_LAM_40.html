DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV26388    Hearing Date: September 16, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

J.P., an individual;

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

EASTERN REVENUE, INC., a California Corporation; EMMANUEL UNIVERSITY, INC., a Georgia Corporation; KYLE SHANAHAN, an individual; MATT MCREE, an individual, JOANN S. HARPER, an individual; and DOES 1-30, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV26388

 Hearing Date:   September 16, 2024

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Quash for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction (RES ID # 3282)

Motion to Quash and for Protective Order (RES ID # 6671)

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff J.P. (Joseph Parker) sues Defendants Eastern Revenue, Inc. (Eastern Revenue), Emmanuel University, Inc. (Emmanuel University), Kyle Shanahan (Eastern Revenue’s President), Matt McRee, Joann S. Harper, and Does 1-30 pursuant to an October 30, 2023, Complaint alleging claims sounding in fraud, unfair business practices, and breach of contract. As alleged by Plaintiff, the case arises from a collection agency (Eastern Revenue) attempting to collect student loan debt from an active duty soldier, in violation of the law.

B. Procedural History

On May 17, 2024, Emmanuel University filed the Motion to Quash for lack of personal jurisdiction.

On May 31, 2024, Emmanuel University filed the Motion to Quash and for Protective Order quashing the Plaintiff’s first set of Requests for Admission and Notice of Deposition of Person Most Knowledgeable.

On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the Motion to Quash for lack of personal jurisdiction.

On July 12, 2024, Emmanuel University replied.

On July 19, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the motion to quash service based on the cyber-attack on all Superior Court systems, which prevented remote appearances or access to the Court files. The matter was rescheduled for hearing on September 16, 2024, the same day as the motion to quash discovery.

Specially Appearing Defendant Emmanuel University’s Motions to Quash are now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Quash for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction: GRANTED as to Defendant Emmanuel University, Inc.

A. Opposition Objections to Declaration of Matthew Edmonds:

Objection Nos. 1-10: OVERRULED.

B. Opposition Objections to Declaration of Brian James:

Objection Nos. 1-13: OVERRULED.

C. Procedural Issue

Plaintiff argues in his opposition that Emmanuel University’s motion should be denied due to defective service. He claims that Emmanuel University improperly served the motion to an incorrect email address, shiloh.bentacourt@icloud.com, instead of Plaintiff’s counsel’s correct address, shiloh@thesabfirm.com. Attorney Fisher, Emmanuel’s lawyer, used OneLegal.com for service but failed to update the email address despite having prior communication with Plaintiff’s counsel and being aware of the correct address. This improper service mirrors a previous situation where the court denied Plaintiff’s request for costs and fees due to similar service issues. Additionally, Plaintiff states he never agreed to electronic service with Emmanuel University. Moreover, Attorney Fisher has provided conflicting email addresses, one with the State Bar of California (eric.Msher@btlaw.com [sic]) and another on his pleadings (efisher@btlaw.com), complicating communication. Plaintiff asserts that Fisher’s failure to update his information with the State Bar violates his duty to ensure accurate contact details, and the court should instruct him to correct this discrepancy.

In reply, Emmanuel University argues that the service of its motion to quash was sufficient, contrary to Plaintiff’s claims. It asserts that the issue of service to the email address shiloh.bentacourt@icloud.com has already been addressed by the Court, and Plaintiff appears to be raising this argument again possibly to justify submitting an opposition brief one day late. Emmanuel University states that the purpose of serving motions is to provide notice to counsel, which was achieved. The motion was served through the Court’s electronic service provider, OneLegal, to the email address shiloh.bentacourt@icloud.com. Emmanuel University points out that Attorney Shiloh Bentacourt and Attorney Shiloh Parker share the same California State Bar Number, implying both email addresses belong to Plaintiff’s counsel. Therefore, serving either email address would effectively serve Plaintiff’s counsel. Additionally, Emmanuel University provides evidence from the OneLegal system showing that Attorney Parker viewed the motion when it was served in May. Furthermore, Emmanuel University highlights that Plaintiff’s counsel has been made aware of the motion through multiple means: it was available on the public docket, referenced in every pleading served since, discussed in correspondence regarding discovery requests, and mentioned during a June 25, 2024 hearing where the Court discussed scheduling related to the motion to quash.

Given the fact that Plaintiff received actual notice and there is no showing of prejudice, the Court will consider the Motion to Quash.

D. Legal Standard

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes: (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a).)

In California, courts are authorized to “exercise jurisdiction over parties on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of [California] or the United States.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.) “Personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific.” (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 445.) The extent to which a California court may exercise jurisdiction over a defendant depends on the nature and quality of that defendant’s contacts with the state.

Nonresident defendants whose commercial activities impact California on a substantial, continuous, and systematic basis are subject to general jurisdiction in California. (See Vons Companies, 14 Cal.4th at pp. 445-446.) However, if the defendant’s activities in California are insufficient to justify the exercise of general jurisdiction over him or her, then the cause of action must arise out of an act done or transaction consummated in the forum such that the defendant may be subject to specific jurisdiction. (Cornelison v. Chaney (1976) 16 Cal.3d 143, 147-148.)

A plaintiff “bears the burden of proof by a preponderance of evidence to demonstrate the defendant has sufficient minimum contacts with the forum state to justify jurisdiction.” (Thomson v. Anderson (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 258, 266.) The Court must assess each defendant’s contacts with the forum state individually. (Anglo Irish Bank, PLC v. Superior Court (Brar) (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 969, 978.)

1. General Jurisdiction

An individual is subject to general jurisdiction in the state of their domicile; while for a corporation it is where it is “fairly regarded as home.” (Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown (2011) 564 U.S. 915, 924 (Goodyear).) This is where the corporation’s contacts are “so ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render [the defendant] essentially at home in the forum state.” (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 139 (Daimler AG).) In Goodyear and Daimler AG, for corporations, the U.S. Supreme Court held that general jurisdiction exists only in the defendant’s (1) state of incorporation and (2) principal place of business. (See Goodyear, supra, 564 U.S. at p. 294; Daimler AG, supra, 571 U.S at p. 137.)

California courts have held that “designation of an agent for service of process and qualification to do business in California alone are insufficient to permit general jurisdiction except for lawsuits arising out of the foreign corporation's business conducted in the state.” (DVI, Inc. v. Superior Court (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1080, 1095.)

Emmanuel University contends that it is not subject to general jurisdiction because Emmanuel University is a Georgia nonprofit corporation with its principal place of business in Georgia. Plaintiff incorrectly claims that Emmanuel University maintains its principal place of business and headquarters in California. However, general jurisdiction can only apply outside of a corporation’s place of incorporation and principal place of business in exceptional cases where its operations in another state are substantial enough to make it at home there. Emmanuel University states that Plaintiff has not made such allegations in this case, nor can he.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Emmanuel University has intentionally misled the court regarding the law on personal jurisdiction. In its moving documents, Emmanuel University cited to Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 449, to argue that “if a defendant’s contacts with the forum state are ‘continuous and systematic’ or ‘substantial,’ the state has a sufficient relationship with the defendant to assert ‘general’ jurisdiction.” Plaintiff contends this citation is a misstatement of the law, that the accurate statement from the case is that personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific, and that a nonresident defendant can be subject to general jurisdiction if their contacts with the forum state are “substantial, continuous, and systematic,” as established in Perkins v. Benguet Consolidated Mining Co. (1952) 342 U.S. 437, 446, 446, and Helicopteros Nacionales de Colombia v. Hall (1984) 46 U.S. 408, 414-415. In such cases, Plaintiff argues, it is not necessary for the specific cause of action to be connected with the defendant’s business relationship to the forum.

Plaintiff argues that the Court has general jurisdiction over Emmanuel University because its contacts with California have been substantial, continuous, and systematic over the past ten years. Emmanuel’s strategy, despite being a smaller private school, involves substantial recruitment and enrollment efforts in California. Emmanuel University conducts annual recruitment and orientations in Northern and Southern California, confirmed by Emmanuel's admissions agent, Matthew Edmonds, during a conversation with Alexis Garcia on May 7, 2024. Mr. Edmonds assured Garcia that Emmanuel University admits students from California and invited her to an orientation in Pasadena. Furthermore, Emmanuel University advertises to students globally, including those in California. Mr. Edmonds waived Garcia's application fee and directed her to the university’s website, which shows that Emmanuel University admits students from California, over 30 states in the US, and more than 22 foreign countries.

Additionally, Plaintiff argues that Emmanuel University actively recruits student-athletes from California, with Mr. Edmonds stating that the university sends recruiters to California yearly to recruit athletes, specifically mentioning the women’s wrestling team. Emmanuel University also provides scholarships to students it recruits from California, including full and partial scholarships, particularly for members of the women’s wrestling team. Emmanuel University has successfully enrolled multiple students from California due to its recruitment efforts, as evidenced by a list of six California student-athletes currently enrolled at Emmanuel University who received scholarships. Emmanuel University has been recruiting and enrolling students from California for the past ten years, with Mr. Edmonds assuring Garcia that many California students had joined the wrestling team in previous years.

According to Plaintiff, the number of students recruited and enrolled from California is significant in comparison to the total student body of around 800 per class. Emmanuel University’s advertising and recruitment strategy targets not only California but students worldwide, resulting in the enrollment of hundreds to thousands of California students and generating millions in tuition dollars. The university’s continuous engagement in these activities over a decade underscores the systemic nature of its contacts with California. As a result, these extensive and ongoing connections are so significant that California can be considered Emmanuel University’s home, fulfilling the criteria for general jurisdiction as outlined in Daimler AG v. Bauman. Plaintiff asserts that denying general jurisdiction would unjustly prevent all current and former California students from pursuing claims against Emmanuel University, which would be contrary to the interests of justice.

In reply, Emmanuel University argues that the court should grant its motion to quash because asserting personal jurisdiction over the university, a Georgia corporation with its principal place of business in Georgia, would violate its constitutional due process rights and be inconsistent with California’s long-arm statute. Emmanuel University contends that Plaintiff misrepresents the facts regarding the university’s contacts with California. While Emmanuel University acknowledges being a Georgia corporation, it disputes Plaintiff's claim of attending recruiting fairs in California. Emmanuel University presents declarations from its employees refuting Plaintiff’s assertions, stating that it does not conduct recruitment sessions or orientations in California and has not done so in the past five years. (See James Decl.) The university confirms that it accepts students from various states, including California, but argues that this alone does not establish personal jurisdiction. Emmanuel University asserts that its contacts with California are insufficient for general personal jurisdiction. General jurisdiction requires a defendant’s contacts with the forum state to be so substantial and systematic that the state becomes akin to a home state, which Emmanuel University argues is not the case here. Merely admitting students from California does not meet this threshold. Emmanuel University also points out that if general jurisdiction were based solely on admitting California students, any out-of-state university could be subject to California jurisdiction, contradicting established legal precedents.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not presented evidence to demonstrate that general jurisdiction of Emmanuel University exists. From the evidence presented to the Court, general jurisdiction of Emmanuel University does not exist because Emmanuel University’s state of incorporation and its principal place of business is in Georgia. (James Decl., ¶ 6; see Goodyear, supra, 564 U.S. at p. 294; Daimler AG, supra, 571 U.S at p. 137.)

2. Specific Jurisdiction

The California Supreme Court has held that three requirements must be met before specific jurisdiction may be exercised: (a) defendant purposefully availed himself of the forum benefits, (b) the controversy is related to or arises from defendant’s contacts with California, and (c) the assertion of jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice. (Pavlovich v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 269.) “A plaintiff opposing a defendant’s motion to quash service has the burden of establishing factor Nos. (1) (the defendant’s purposeful availment) and (2) (lawsuit relates to the defendant’s contacts with state).” (People ex rel. Harris v. Native Wholesale Supply Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 357, 362, citing Bridgestone Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 767, 774].) “If the plaintiff does so, the burden then shifts to the defendant to show factor No. (3), that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.” (Ibid.)

“The nonresident defendant must have purposefully directed its activities at forum residents, or purposefully availed itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum state, thus invoking the benefits and protections of local law.” (Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2017) ¶ 3:226.) When individuals “purposefully derive benefit” from their interstate activities, it is unfair to allow such individuals to escape having to account for their activities in the state from which they purposefully derived a benefit, for the consequences that arise from such activities. (Burger King (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 471-74.) Minimum contacts can be electronic or created through the use of the telephone, mail, or e-mail. (Hall v. LaRonde (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1344, 1346-1347.) What is important “is that the defendant’s conduct and connection with the forum State are such that he should reasonably anticipate being haled into court there.” (World-Wide Volkswagen Corp. v. Woodson (1980) 444 U.S. 286, 297.)

“[E]ven where other parties involved in the action have considerable contacts with California, if the nonresident defendant did not evidently intend to conduct business in California or in any other way directly or indirectly gain from California dealings, the purpose of the other parties cannot be imputed to the nonresident defendant for the purpose of assuming personal jurisdiction over him. [Citation.] Thus, “... jurisdiction over a nonresident must be based on an analysis of the relationship between that defendant and the forum state. [Citation.]” [Citation.]” (Edmunds v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 221, 231.)

“‘The purposeful availment inquiry ... focuses on the defendant’s intentionality. [Citation.] This prong is only satisfied when the defendant purposefully and voluntarily directs his activities toward the forum so that he should expect, by virtue of the benefit he receives, to be subject to the court’s jurisdiction based on’ his contacts with the forum. [Citation.] Thus, the “‘purposeful availment’ requirement ensures that a defendant will not be haled into a jurisdiction solely as a result of ‘random,’ ‘fortuitous,’ or ‘attenuated’ contacts [citations], or of the ‘unilateral activity of another party or a third person.’ [Citations.]” [Citation.] “When a [defendant] ‘purposefully avails itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum State,’ [citation], it has clear notice that it is subject to suit there, and can act to alleviate the risk of burdensome litigation by procuring insurance, passing the expected costs on to customers, or, if the risks are too great, severing its connection with the State.” [Citation.]” (Pavlovich, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 269.)

To determine if the defendant has sufficient forum contacts, a forum selection clause of choice of law provision in the parties’ agreement can be examined. (Halyard Health, Inc. v. Kimberly-Clark Corp. (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 1062, 1076 (holding that Delaware choice of law forum reflected a “deliberate affiliation with Delaware,” the parties’ state of incorporation, and finding insufficient minimum contacts).) Further, courts will look to the totality of the defendant’s contacts with a state, and in controversies arising from the ownership interest in a corporation in the forum state, courts have held that “ownership of a subsidiary alone does not constitute purposeful availment.” (Healthmarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1168.)

“Specific jurisdiction depends on the quality and nature of the defendant’s forum contacts in relation to the particular cause of action alleged. [Citation.]” (Anglo Irish Bank Corp., PLC v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 969, 978.)

“A controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant's forum contacts, so as to satisfy the second requirement for the exercise of specific personal jurisdiction, if there is “a substantial connection between the forum contacts and the plaintiff's claim.” [Citation.] The forum contacts need not be the proximate cause or “but for” cause of the alleged injuries. [Citation.] The forum contacts also need not be “substantively relevant” to the cause of action, meaning those contacts need not establish or support an element of the cause of action. [Citation.] “A claim need not arise directly from the defendant's forum contacts in order to be sufficiently related to the contact to warrant the exercise of specific jurisdiction. Rather, as long as the claim bears a substantial connection to the nonresident's forum contacts, the exercise of specific jurisdiction is appropriate.” [Citation.] Accordingly, in evaluating the quality and nature of the defendant’s forum contacts, we consider not only the conduct directly affecting the plaintiff, but also the broader course of conduct of which it is a part. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 979.)

Emmanuel University argues that the Court lacks specific personal jurisdiction. First, Emmanuel University argues that it has not purposefully availed itself of California. Purposeful availment requires a substantial connection with the forum state through continuous obligations. Emmanuel University neither transacts business in California nor is registered to do so. It does not own, rent, or lease property, maintain bank accounts, enroll distance learning students, employ California residents, or participate in recruiting programs in California. Second, Emmanuel University contends that the controversy does not arise from its contacts with California. Plaintiff’s allegations primarily involve the refusal to provide an agreement with a collection agency and the purported destruction of such an agreement, which do not have a substantial nexus with California. Third, even if minimum contacts were established, Emmanuel University argues that asserting jurisdiction would be unreasonable. The university’s lack of meaningful contacts with California and the absence of any substantial nexus between its actions and the state further support this claim.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the Court has specific jurisdiction over Emmanuel University based on the university’s purposeful availment of doing business in California, the controversy’s relationship to Emmanuel University’s contacts in California, and the reasonableness of exercising jurisdiction. Emmanuel University has deliberately engaged in business in California by conducting recruitment efforts and orientations, advertising to California students, and targeting student-athletes. Over the past ten years, Plaintiff contends that Emmanuel University has made millions of dollars in tuition from California students, demonstrating intentional and substantial business activities in the state. The controversy involves Emmanuel University’s adherence to California laws when interacting with California students and utilizing their resources for financial gain. Plaintiff argues that Emmanuel University’s legal obligations extend beyond graduation, as the university must follow California laws concerning former students’ contractual obligations. Emmanuel University’s refusal to provide key information related to the alleged debt at issue exemplifies its failure to meet these obligations.

Plaintiff asserts that it is reasonable for the Court to exercise jurisdiction over Emmanuel University. If jurisdiction is denied, current and former California students would be unable to pursue claims against Emmanuel University in California, despite being recruited and paying tuition to the university. Emmanuel University benefits financially from its activities in California and should therefore be accountable for its obligations in the state. With remote appearances and depositions available, Emmanuel University would not be unduly burdened by defending itself in California, where it already has legal representation. California has a vested interest in adjudicating claims involving foreign corporations and protecting the rights of its residents. Plaintiff emphasizes the importance of judicial economy, suggesting that claims against both Emmanuel and Eastern should be resolved in a single action. The strength of the reasonableness of exercising jurisdiction over Emmanuel University justifies even a minimal showing of purposeful availment, as denying jurisdiction would deprive former and current California students of their rights to seek redress against Emmanuel for similar issues.

In reply, Emmanuel University contends that Plaintiff’s claims of purposeful availment through advertising and recruiting California students are unsupported. The university highlights that its acceptance of applications from California does not demonstrate purposeful availment. Emmanuel University references cases like Simonelli v. New York University A148786, 2017 WL 5712586 (Cal.Ct.App. Nov. 28, 2017) (an unpublished case which should not have been cited and will not be considered; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.115) and Roman v. Liberty University (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 670, 680, where the court found that admitting students or recruiting an athlete from California did not establish personal jurisdiction. Emmanuel University maintains that Plaintiff has not shown a sufficient nexus between the controversy and California, noting that the debt in question was incurred while Plaintiff attended the university in Georgia.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has failed to carry his burden establishing by a preponderance of the evidence that Emmanuel University has sufficient contacts with California that relate to the claim to justify jurisdiction. Plaintiff has failed to produce evidence to establish that the alleged misconduct in the complaint is related to or arises out of Emmanuel University’s contacts with California. Furthermore, Plaintiff does not explain how this controversy relates to California when Plaintiff was not a resident of California while attending Emmanuel University. This dispute is instead about debt incurred during Plaintiff’s time at Emmanuel University in Georgia. As stated in Emmanuel University’s opening brief, the fact that collection efforts may have occurred in California does not establish a connection to the state or personal jurisdiction over the non-collection-agency defendant.

 Accordingly, the Court finds that Emmanuel University lacks minimum contacts with California. The Court GRANTS Emmanuel University’s motion to quash service of summons for lack of jurisdiction.

 

III. Motion to Quash and for Protective Order: GRANTED as to Defendant Emmanuel University, Inc.

On May 4, 2024, Plaintiff served Emmanuel University with: (1) Plaintiff’s First Set of Requests for Admissions and (2) a Notice of Deposition of the Personal Most Knowledgeable for June 4, 2024.

Emmanuel University argues that the Court lacks jurisdiction in this case.

Based on the Court’s discussion above in the motion to quash for lack of personal jurisdiction, the Court agrees that Emmanuel University lacks minimum contacts with California. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Emmanuel University’s motion to quash Plaintiff’s First Set of Requests for Admission and Notice of Deposition of Person Most Knowledgeable.

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff J.P. (Joseph Parker) sues Defendants Eastern Revenue, Inc. (Eastern Revenue), Emmanuel University, Inc. (Emmanuel University), Kyle Shanahan (Eastern Revenue’s President), Matt McRee, Joann S. Harper, and Does 1-30 pursuant to an October 30, 2023, Complaint alleging claims of fraud, unfair business practices, and breach of contract.

B. Relevant Procedural History

On June 5, 2024, Defendants Eastern Revenue and Shanahan filed a joint opposition.

On June 6, 2024—one court day late—Emmanuel University filed an opposition.

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply addressing the Eastern Revenue and Shanahan opposition but not the untimely Emmanuel University opposition.

On June 18, 2024, the Court ruled on Plaintiff’s motion as it related to Defendants Eastern Revenue and Shanahan but continued the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion as it related to Emmanuel University to June 25, 2024. The Court noted the lack of a reply from Plaintiff responding to Emmanuel University’s opposition and gave Plaintiff until June 21, 2024, to file a reply.

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to the Emmanuel University opposition

The Court heard the motion and issued a ruling on June 25, 2024.

The Court denied Plaintiff’s motion because Plaintiff did not provide a copy of the Summons and Complaint to an individual authorized to accept service on Emmanuel University’s behalf or provide proof that Plaintiff had mailed a copy of the Summons and Complaint to Emmanuel University.

C. Motion Before the Court

On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion for reconsideration of his motion for costs/expenses as it relates to Emmanuel University.

On July 9, 2024, Emmanuel University filed an opposition.

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

On July 19, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the motion to quash service, which was scheduled for July 23, 2024, based on the cyber-attack on all Superior Court systems, which prevented remote appearances or access to the Court files. The matter was rescheduled for hearing on September 16, 2024, the same day as a motion to quash discovery as to defendant Emmanuel.

The Plaintiff’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

A motion for reconsideration is used to ask the court to modify, amend, or revoke its earlier order on a prior motion to the court because of new or different facts, circumstances, or law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) Such an order may involve an interim or final order (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subds. (e), (h)), where an interim order is an intermediate ruling of some kind that requires further proceedings before the suit can be resolved, and where a final order is an order that finally disposes of the suit (see People v. DeLouize (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1223, 1231 [differentiating between interim and final orders]).  

               A motion for reconsideration may be brought by a party or by the court on its own motion. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subds. (a), (c).) The grounds for the motion vary depending on whether a party or the Court is making the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subds. (a), (c), (e).)  

               A party may move for reconsideration based on: (1) new or different facts, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); see e.g., In re Marriage of LaMoure (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1473 [reconsideration motion granted on new evidence]); (2) new or different circumstances, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a)); and (3) new or different law, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); Baldwin v. Home Sav. of Am. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1196.) However, a party cannot move reconsideration based on the court’s erroneous order, i.e., the court’s misinterpretation of facts or law. (See Jones v. P.S. Dev. Co. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 707, 724, disapproved on other grounds by Reid v. Google, Inc. (2010) 50 Cal.4th 512, 531-532, 532 n. 4.)

A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances or law.”  (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1499.)  Motions for reconsideration are restricted to circumstances where a party offers the Court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Ibid.) 

Moreover, there is a strict requirement of diligence, which means the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence or different facts earlier. (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.) The burden under Section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at trial.  (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213.)   

B. Analysis

Plaintiff argues that the June 25, 2024, order should be reconsidered because there is new evidence. Plaintiff states that during the hearing on the original motion to recover expenses, the Court noted that it had to deny Plaintiff’s motion because Plaintiff did not attach proof of service of the notice of acknowledgement. Plaintiff now submits the notice of acknowledgement as new evidence. (See Langer Decl., Ex B)

In opposition, Emmanuel University argues that Plaintiffs new evidence is untimely and insufficient as a matter of law to warrant reconsideration. Emmanuel University argues that in the original motion, the Plaintiff made the argument that he had served Emmanuel University by mail for the first time in his reply brief, and thus the argument was waived. Emmanuel University also argues that the proof of service documents filed with Plaintiffs motion for reconsideration do not include a copy of the notice and acknowledgement.

In reply, the Plaintiff argues that it mailed all the required documents, including the notice of acknowledgement, to Emmanuel University in March 2024. Plaintiff further argues “[s]ince this Court did not request proof of service in granting service fees/costs against Eastern, the Court did not request this at the first hearing in preparation for the second hearing, and the legal standard changed at the last minute during the second hearing, Plaintiff could not have known that this Court would request a proof of service and/or any other document that would help the Court made [sic] a decision.” (Opp. p. 4.)

Here, the proof of service for the notice of acknowledgement does not constitute new or different facts within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 1008. (See New York Times, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at 213.) While the proof of service for the notice of acknowledgement is new evidence to the Court, Plaintiff, by his own admission, had this evidence when he filed the original motion to recover expenses. (See Garcia, supra, 58 Cal.App.4th at 689-690 [motion for reconsideration improperly granted where evidence reflected knowledge plaintiff had from outset of litigation].) Plaintiff contends that he mailed Emmanuel University the notice of acknowledgement on March 15 and March 22, 2024. (Langer Decl., p.2:6-8, and Ex. B.) As such, this evidence was available to Plaintiff when he filed the original motion to recover expenses on April 23, 2024.

Moreover, Plaintiff has not provided a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence earlier (See Garcia, supra, 58 Cal.App.4th at 690.) Plaintiff argues he did not provide the notice of acknowledgment in the original motion to recover expenses against Emmanuel University because he “could not have known” that the Court would request it based on the earlier hearing in the motion to recover expenses against Eastern Revenue. However, this argument lacks merit. In the June 18, 2024, Minute Order this Court explained that the notice of acknowledgment was not necessary as to Defendant Eastern Revenue because Plaintiff’s counsel confirmed with Eastern Revenue’s counsel Richard Perr that Perr represented Eastern Revenue. (Minute Order June 18, 2024, pp. 3-4) Plaintiff had no such confirmation on behalf of Emmanuel University. To the contrary, Daniel Cohen told Plaintiff’s counsel that he did not represent Emmanuel University. (Opp. p. 1:6-9) (See also Foothills Townhome Assn. v. Christiansen (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 688, 692 [plaintiff's belief that certain evidence was not necessary at hearing on summary judgment motion insufficient to justify reconsideration], disapproved on another ground in Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82.)

C. Issues with Service

Finally, in the Reply brief, Plaintiff has a final section in which Plaintiff claims not to have been served with other pleadings (not related to this motion) and asks the Court “to address the issue and order Emmanuel not to serve third parties and to serve Plaintiff at the email address listed on this caption,” [i.e., shiloh@thesabfirm] and if the Court is not inclined to do so, Plaintiff “respectfully requests recusal under CCP 170.3(6)(B).” (Reply, pp. 5-6.)

First, there is no such code section in the Code of Civil Procedure. This Court does not believe there is any basis for disqualification, but to the extent that any party believes that there is a basis for this Court to recuse itself, that party must follow the procedures specified in Code of Civil Procedure section 170.3 subdivision (c)(1), which were not followed here.

            Second, as to the service issue and whether Emmanuel improperly served Plaintiff with its opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion for service costs/fees, their motion to quash summons, and their motion for protective order re: discovery, the Court notes that a reply brief on an unrelated motion is not the ideal place to raise such a concern. However, the Court will inquire with the parties as to whether any documents have been erroneously served to Plaintiff’s counsel’s wrong email address since the hearing on June 18, 2024, in which Plaintiff’s counsel confirmed the proper email address for service, as all of the above pleadings predated that hearing. Counsel for both sides should of course use the other side’s proper email address.

 

III. Conclusion

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of Plaintiff’s Motion for Costs/Expenses Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 415.30(d) is DENIED.

On February 16, 2024, Plaintiffs Leo David Tumanyan (Leo), by and through his guardian ad litem Lilit Tumanyan, and Lilit Tumanyan (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Defendants USC Care Medical Group, Inc, USC Perinatal Group, Keck Medicine of USC, Keck Hospital of USC, Marc H. Incerpi, MD, SoCal OBGYN Associates, Joon Choi, MD, APC, Joon Choi, MD and Does 1-100, (collectively, Defendants) alleging causes of action for (1) Medical Malpractice, (2) Wrongful Life, and (3) Wrongful Birth.

B.    Motion Before the Court

On July 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for trial preference.

On September 3, 2024, Defendants SoCal OBGYN Associates, Joon Choi, MD, APC, and Joon Choi, MD opposed the motion.

On September 9, Plaintiffs replied.

 

II. Motion

A.    Legal Standard

“A civil action to recover damages for wrongful death or personal injury shall be entitled to preference upon the motion of any party to the action who is under 14 years of age unless the court finds that the party does not have a substantial interest in the case as a whole...” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (b).) 

“Unless the court otherwise orders: (1) A party may file and serve a motion for preference supported by a declaration of the moving party that all essential parties have been served with process or have appeared.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (c).) 

“Upon the granting of a motion for preference pursuant to subdivision (b), a party in an action based upon a health provider’s alleged professional negligence, as defined in Section 364, shall receive a trial date not sooner than six months and not later than nine months from the date that the motion is granted.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (g).) 

Priority is mandatory; the trial court has no discretion to refuse the minor’s request for early setting. (Peters v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 218, 223-24.) However, early trial setting may violate a defendant’s due process rights if there is inadequate time to prepare for trial. (Id. at 227.) 

B.    Analysis

During a Case Management Conference on June 25, 2024, Plaintiffs and Defendants agreed to a trial date of May 13, 2025. Plaintiff now brings this motion for an order deeming the May 13, 2025, trial date as a preferential trial date not subject to continuances, pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 36 subdivisions (b), (e), (h) and (g).

 Leo is 21 months old and was born with multiple serious health conditions (Mot. Ex. 1 [detailing Leo’s health conditions]; Ex. 3 [birth certificate showing date of birth as November 28, 2022].) Thus, Leo is under 14 years of age and clearly has a substantial interest in the case as a whole. Plaintiffs argue that the Court should grant this motion because Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivision (b) is mandatory, and doing so serves the interests of justice due to the minor Plaintiff's age and serious health issues.

In opposition, Defendants argue that the motion should be denied because the Plaintiffs have been dilatory throughout the litigation, and because the Defendants will suffer significant prejudice if the trial date is deemed preferential due to the complexity of the case. Defendants also argue that the Plaintiffs’ motion did not include a declaration that all essential parties have been served with process or appeared, as stated in Code of Civil Procedure, section 36 subdivision (c)(1).

In reply, the Plaintiffs argue that they have diligently prosecuted the case and responded to discovery requests. They also state that all necessary parties have been served (Harutyunyan Decl. ¶¶ 3-4.) In addition, the Plaintiffs state that they are willing to set a preferential trial date toward the outer limits of the six-to-nine-month time period outlined in Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivision (g). Currently, the trial is set for May 13, 2025. The hearing on this motion is scheduled for September 16, 2024. Six to nine months from September 2024 is between March 2025 and June 2025. (In their motion, Plaintiffs inadvertently calculate nine months from September 2024 as July 2025.)

Here, the Defendants have failed to provide adequate defenses to mandatory trial preference. Leo is under 14 years of age and has a substantial interest in the case. The Plaintiffs brought this action within a reasonable time after Leo’s health problems were discovered and the Court does not find Defendants’ argument that Plaintiffs have been dilatory to be supported. The Defendants’ argument that the case requires extensive discovery fails because a party’s inability to conduct discovery or other pretrial matters as to a party entitled to a preference is not a basis to deny the motion in its entirety. (Swaithes v. Superior Court¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1082, 1085¿[“Failure to complete discovery or other pretrial matters does not affect the absolute substantive right to trial preference for those litigants who qualify for preference”].) By contrast, the cases cited by Defendants do not support denial of the motion here. (Peters v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 218, 227 [asking, but not answering the question whether the preference statute violates due process]; Roe v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 642, 643, fn. 2 [noting that the due process implications of the preference statute have not been decided but noting that in this case “it may not be possible to bring the matter to trial within the technical limits of Code of Civil Procedure, subdivision (f)” where a request for trial priority was made by the plaintiff after he filed a petition for writ of mandate to compel the lower court to grant a motion to compel discovery]; Landry v. Berryessa (19995) 39 Cal.App.4th 691 [preference statute does not prevent Court from dismissing case for delay in prosecution, where case was dormant for 7.5 months at one point, and another 11 months at another point, and plaintiffs did not move for preference until 4.5 years after case had been filed].)

Finally, although the Plaintiffs did not include a Code of Civil Procedure, section 36 subdivision (c)(1) compliant declaration in their motion, proofs of service on file with the Court show that all parties have been served, and all parties have appeared in the case.

One additional consideration bears mentioning. The Court would like clarity on whether Defendants are arguing that there will not be enough time for them to conduct discovery before the currently scheduled trial date of May 13, 2025. This matter was set for trial at a June 25, 2024 Case Management Conference, wherein both parties agreed to set trial that date, and both sets of Defendants’ CMC Statements checked the Box 6.b. that the case would be ready for trial within 12 months of the date of the filing of the complaint (which was on February 16, 2024). Because of the Court’s schedule and upon stipulation of all counsel, the case was set for trial 15 months from the date of the filing of the complaint, and about 13 months from the date of service on Defendants. Even without the request for preference, this Court is required to follow California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332 regarding continuances of trials. That rule states that “[t]o ensure the prompt disposition of civil case, the dates assigned for a trial are firm. All parties and their counsel must regard the date set for trial as certain.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (a).) A party seeking a continuance must do by way of noticed motion or ex parte motion as soon as practicable once the necessity for the continuance is discovered, and since continuances of trial are “disfavored,” such a request may only be granted upon an affirmative showing of good cause, as further explained in the rule. (Id. at subds. (b) and (c).) Thus, an opposition to a motion for trial preference which apparently objects to the currently scheduled trial date is a concern to this Court.

Accordingly, because the pleadings and evidence show that the minor Plaintiff is under the age of 14 and has a substantial interest in the case as a whole and because Defendants SoCal OBGYN Associates, Joon Choi, MD, APC, and Joon Choi, MD do not provide valid defenses to mandatory preference, the motion for trial preference is GRANTED. Once set, the trial date will not be subject to continuance, except as specified by California Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivision (f). 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Trial Preference is GRANTED.

The Court will entertain at oral argument whether there is good cause to continue the current trial date from May to June, 2025, which would be at the far end of the 6-9 months.
