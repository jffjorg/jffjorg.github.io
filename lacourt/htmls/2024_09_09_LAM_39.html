DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV14800    Hearing Date: September 9, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 9, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV14800

MOTIONS

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant State Farm General Insurance Company

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Noureen Entertainment, Inc.

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Noureen Entertainment, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendant State Farm General Insurance Company (“Defendant”) failed to defend Plaintiff against lawsuits brought by third parties.  Defendant moves to strike portions of the first amended complaint, and Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Defendant moves to strike the prayer for punitive damages. In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.)

In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges, “the subject wrongdoing complained-of herein is being committed by [Defendant], as knowingly approved, expressly or impliedly, and/or ratified, by the corporate officers of [Defendant], including, but not limited to, Michael Tipsord, in his capacity as the President/Chief Executive Officer of State Farm, and Jon Farney, in his capacity as the Senior Vice President/Chief Financial Officer/Treasurer of [Defendant], despicably, in the manner of fraud, and/or malice and/or oppression in the conscious disregard for the rights and welfare of [Plaintiff] justifying the proper imposition of punitive and/or exemplary damages in favor of [Plaintiff] and adverse to [Defendant] . . . .” (First Amended Complaint, p. 3.)  Plaintiff fails to allege specific facts to support these conclusory allegations.  The Court, therefore, grants the motion to strike.

In its February 5, 2024 order, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to strike and provided Plaintiff with the opportunity to amend its complaint to state a claim for punitive damages.  Plaintiff has failed to do so.  Accordingly, the Court considers leave to amend futile at this time.  (See e.g., Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 994-995 [trial court does not abuse its discretion in sustaining demurrer without leave to amend when plaintiff given ample opportunity to cure defect in its pleading but fails to demonstrate it can do so].)  Nonetheless, the Court will consider a motion for leave to amend should Plaintiff uncover evidence to support a claim for punitive damages against Defendant.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPARTMENT

39

September 9, 2024

24STCV05506

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Space Exploration Technologies Corp.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Michelle Dopak

 

MOTION

              Defendant Space Exploration Technologies Corp. (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration of the claims Plaintiff Michelle Dopak (“Plaintiff”) asserts against it.  Plaintiff opposes the motion. 

ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendant has presented an arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed when Plaintiff worked for Defendant.  The arbitration agreement provides Plaintiff and Defendant agree to arbitrate a “Covered Claim,” defines as “any claim (except a claim that by law is non-arbitrable) . . . .”  (Declaration of Lynette Dhillon in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit A.)  Accordingly, Defendant meets its burden to show an agreement to arbitrate exists that may cover Plaintiff’s claims in this action. 

The arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed specifies the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) governs.  (Dhillon Decl., Exhibit A, ¿ 11.)  A choice of law provision in an arbitration agreement is enforceable.  (See Coopers & Lybrand v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 524, 538.)  As such, the FAA applies.  Plaintiff argues the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 (“Ending Forced Arbitration Act”), which became law on March 3, 2022, bars arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.  This law amends the FAA to provide, “at the election of the person alleging conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute or sexual assault dispute, or the named representative of a class or in a collective action alleging such conduct, no predispute arbitration agreement . . . shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.”  (9 U.S.C. § 402(a).)  For purposes of this law, a sexual assault dispute is “a dispute involving a nonconsensual sexual act or sexual contact . . . , including when the victim lacks capacity to consent . . . ,” and a sexual harassment dispute is “a dispute relating to conduct that is         alleged to constitute sexual harassment under applicable Federal, Tribal, or State law.  (9 U.S.C. § 401(3)-(4).) 

Defendant argues the Ending Forced Arbitration Act does not apply because Plaintiff’s claims accrued before the enactment of the law.  The law applies to “any dispute or claim that arises or accrues on or after the date of enactment of this Act.”  (9 U.S.C. § 401 note.)  Plaintiff alleges the sexual harassment continued after March 3, 2022.  (See, e.g., First Amended Complaint, ¶ 67.)  As such, Plaintiff’s claims accrued after the law’s enactment under the continuing violation doctrine.  (Richards v. CH2M Hill, Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 798, 812; see also Blue Fountain Pools & Spas Inc. v. Superior Court (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 239, 254-255.)  Accordingly, the Ending Forced Arbitration Act applies to Plaintiff’s claim for sexual harassment. 

Plaintiff argues all her claims are intertwined with and arise out of her sexual harassment claim and, therefore, none of the claims should proceed to arbitration.  In support of this contention, Plaintiff cites various federal opinions, which do not bind this Court.  (Mesler v.  Bragg Mgt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 299; see also Thomas G. Ferruzzo, Inc. v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 501, 503 [“[n]either federal district court procedures nor decisions are binding on this court”].)  Likewise, “a written trial court ruling has no precedential value . . . .”  (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshe (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831.)  The Court disregards these citations. 

The Court concludes only Plaintiff’s first cause of action for sexual harassment is non-arbitrable.  Under the FAA, the Court must compel arbitration of arbitrable claims, even if some claims are not arbitrable.  (KPMG LLP v. Cocchi (2011) 565 U.S. 18, 22.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff must arbitrate her second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action to the extent they do not relate to Plaintiff’s claim for sexual harassment. The Court concludes those claims are not sufficiently related to the sexual harassment claim to warrant excluding them from being subject to arbitration pursuant to the parties’ agreement to arbitrate such claims.  Thus, Plaintiff’s first cause of action for sexual harassment is stayed pending arbitration of Plaintiff’s other claims. 

Plaintiff also contends the arbitration agreement is unconscionable.  This contention is unsupported.  Pursuant to Civil Code section 1670.5, “If the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract, or it may enforce the remainder of the contract without the unconscionable clause, or it may so limit the application of any unconscionable clause as to avoid any unconscionable result.”  (Civ. Code, § 1670.5, subd. (a).)  Unconscionability is defined as “an absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms which are unreasonably favorable to the other party.”  (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486 (A & M Produce).) 

Unconscionability thus has a procedural and a substantive element.  (A & M Produce, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at p. 486.)  The court considers the factors of oppression and surprise in determining whether a contract is procedurally unconscionable.  (Ibid.)  Oppression refers to inequality of bargaining power, which cuts off meaningful negotiation.  (Ibid.)  Surprise refers to the concealment of the agreed-upon terms in a lengthy form drafted by the party seeking to enforce them.  (Ibid.)  Substantive unconscionability refers to the extent to which a contractual term reallocates the risks of a transaction “in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected or unexpected manner.” (Id. at 487.)  Both substantive and procedural unconscionability must be present for a contract to be unenforceable, and “the greater the unfair surprise or inequality of bargaining power, the less unreasonable the risk allocation which will be tolerated.” (Ibid.)

            The evidence before the Court shows Defendant prepared the arbitration agreement, and Plaintiff had no opportunity to negotiate or discuss its terms.  It is, therefore, a contract of adhesion (Neal v. State Farm Ins. Companies (1961) 188 Cal.App.2d 690, 694), which constitutes some evidence of procedural unconscionability.  (Flores v. Transamerica HomeFirst, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 846, 853). 

Plaintiff has not, however, demonstrated the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable.  She argues substantive unconscionability, asserting, although the agreement does not prevent either party from obtaining injunctive relief, it is one-sided, and the agreement requires her to waive a right to recovery under FEHA.  First, by virtue of the fact both parties can obtain injunctive relief, the provision is bilateral.   Second, the agreement does not require Plaintiff to waive her rights under FEHA.  Rather, the operative provision in the agreement expressly states what claims are covered by the agreement and specifies claims that are not arbitrable under the law.  As such, Plaintiff has not presented evidence of unconscionability.

Plaintiff also argues Defendant waived the right to compel arbitration.  Defendant did not waive the right to compel arbitration by litigating claims unless the litigation prejudiced Plaintiff.  (Saint Agnes Med. Ctr. v. PacifiCare of Calif. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187, 1203.)  As Plaintiff points out, Defendant answered Plaintiff’s complaint.  However, Plaintiff has not explained how this prejudiced her.  Accordingly, Defendant has not waived arbitration.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate is granted, in part.  Plaintiff must arbitrate her second, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action to the extent they do not relate to Plaintiff’s claim for sexual harassment. Plaintiff’s first cause of action is stayed pending arbitration of Plaintiff’s other claims. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
