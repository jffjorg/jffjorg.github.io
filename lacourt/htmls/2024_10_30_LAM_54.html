County of Los Angeles

Plaintiffs,

Hearing Date: October 30, 2024

Department 54, Judge Maurice Leiter

Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint

Moving Party: Cross-Defendant Dariush G. Aldi

Responding Party: Cross-Complainants Ali Salomi and Chadwick Tyner

 

T/R:     CROSS-DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO THE FIFTH CAUSE OF ACTION IS SUSTAINED. THE DEMURRER TO THE REMAINING CAUSES OF ACTION IS OVERRULED.

 

CROSS-COMPLAINANTS TO FILE AND SERVE A SECOND AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT WITHIN 30 DAYS. CROSS-DEFENDANTS TO FILE AND SERVE A RESPONSE WITHIN 30 DAYS THEREAFTER.

 

CROSS-DEFENDANT TO NOTICE.

 

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative, please email the courtroom at SMCdept54@lacourt.org with notice to opposing counsel (or self-represented party) before 8:00 am on the day of the hearing. 

 

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a dispute over legal fees. Plaintiffs Dariush G. Adli (Adli) and ADLI Law Group P.C. (ALG) sued defendants Skypanels, Inc., Ali Salomi, and Chadwick Tyner on November 12, 2020, alleging a single cause of action for breach of contract (retainer agreement) and claiming $33,343.25 in damages.

 

Defendants Ali Salomi and Chadwick Tyner cross-complained against Plaintiffs on May 27, 2021 for intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, unjust enrichment, conversion, unfair business practices, breach of contract (in two iterations), and civil theft (Penal Code § 496). Salomi and Tyner (Cross-Complainants) allege Adli and ALG overbilled them at least $40,000.00 for the services in question.

 

Following Cross-Defendants' motion for judgment on the pleadings, Cross-Complainants filed a first amended cross-complaint on August 22, 2024.

 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Cross-Defendant's request for judicial notice is GRANTED as to the existence of the documents, but not as to the truth of the matters asserted therein.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer to a complaint may be taken to the whole complaint or to any of the causes of action in it.  (CCP § 430.50(a).)  A demurrer challenges only the legal sufficiency of the complaint, not the truth of its factual allegations or the plaintiff's ability to prove those allegations.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High Sch. Dist. (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 726, 732.)  The court must treat as true the complaint's material factual allegations, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  (Id. at 732-33.)  The complaint is to be construed liberally to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Id. at 733.)

 

A. First and Second Causes of Action for Misrepresentation

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) In California, fraud, including negligent misrepresentation, must be pled with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.)

Cross-Defendant Aldi demurs to the first and second causes of action on the ground that Cross-Complainants have failed to allege fraud with the requisite specificity. Cross-Complainants allege that Cross-Defendant Aldi made various misrepresentations in the billing statements, including examples of duplicative billing and inflated hours. Aldi asserts that this is insufficient to state a claim for fraud against him individually. The Court disagrees. Plaintiff alleges Cross-Defendant Aldi made misrepresentations and provides specific examples from the billing statements. This is sufficient to state claims for fraud against Aldi.

 

The demurrer to the first and second causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

C. Third Cause of Action for Unjust Enrichment and Sixth Cause of Action for Breach of Implied Contract

 

Cross-Defendant demurs to the third and sixth causes of action on the ground that quasi-contract claims may only be alleged in the absence of an express contract. Cross-Defendant argues that these claims fail because Cross-Complainants have alleged a valid, express contract. The Court declines to sustain the demurrer on this ground. Cross-Complainants may allege alternative theories of liability.        

 

The demurrer to the third and sixth causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

D. Fourth Cause of Action for UCL Violations

 

The UCL “bars ‘unfair competition’ and defines the term as a ‘business act or practice’ that is (1) ‘fraudulent,’ (2) ‘unlawful,’ or (3) ‘unfair.’ … Each is its own independent ground for liability under the [UCL], but their underlying purpose ‘is to protect both consumers and competitors by promoting fair competition in commercial markets for goods and services’ … .” (Shaeffer v. Califia Farms, LLC (2020) 44 Cal.App.5th 1125, 1135, citations omitted.)

 

Cross-Defendant asserts Cross-Complainants cannot state a claim for UCL violations because they have failed to allege a fraudulent, unlawful, or unfair business practice. As stated, Cross-Complainants have sufficiently alleged fraudulent billing practices. This is sufficient to state a claim for UCL violations.

 

The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

E. Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

Cross-Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action on the ground that he is not a party to the retainer agreement and therefore cannot be liable for its breach. The Court agrees.

 

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

If a party fails to provide a timely response to a request for admission, the party waives any objection to the requests.  (C.C.P. § 2033.280(a).)  Moreover, “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction….”  (C.C.P. § 2033.280(b).) 

 

When timely responses to interrogatories are not received, “[t]he party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.”  (CCP § 2030.290(b).) “If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it. . .  [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. . . .  The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”  (CCP § 2031.300(a)–(b).)
