Case Number: 23STLC07368    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         September 10, 2024

Case Name:                             California Fair Plan v. Leonard Zuniga

Case No.:                                23STLC07368

Motion:                                   Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant California FAIR Plan Association

Responding Party:                   Unopposed

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant California Fair Plan’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.    

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 16, 2023, Plaintiff California Fair Plan (“CFP”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Leonard Zuniga (“Zuniga”) and Does 1 to 20, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) motor vehicle; and (2) general negligence. The complaint arises from Defendant Zuniga negligently operating a Bobcat front loader that collided with the property of CFP’s insured.   

 

On May 10, 2024, Zuniga filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

Also, on May 10, 2024, Zuniga filed a Cross-Complaint against CFP. The cross-complaint is on a Form PLD-PI-002 and indicates that Zuniga is asserting causes of action for: (1) negligence; and (2) declaratory relief. (See Cross-Complaint at pp. 3-4.)

 

On July 30, 2024, CFP filed and served the instant demurrer to the cross-complaint. CFP demurs to the cross-complaint on the grounds that the cross-complaint fails to state a cause of action and the cross-complaint is uncertain. In limited jurisdiction cases, however, special demurrers are not allowed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) The Court therefore OVERRULES the demurrer of CFP to the cross-complaint on the grounds of uncertainty.

 

Thus, the Court’s analysis below will only address whether cross-complaint alleges sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against CFP.

 

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            CFP argues that the cross-complaint does not state a cause of action for either negligence or declaratory relief.  

 

OPPOSITION

 

            None filed as of September 5, 2024.  

 

REPLY

 

            None filed as of September 5, 2024.  

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Demurrer

A.                Meet and Confer Requirement  

            Before filing a demurrer, the moving party must meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41.) “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

            The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met. (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 2.) The parties met and conferred via telephone but were unable to resolve the issues raised in the demurrer. (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 4.)

B.        Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318. “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” Hahn v. Mirda, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.  A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense.  Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50. “Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349. A “[p]laintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” Ibid.

1.         Judicial Notice         

 

In the memorandum of points and authorities, CFP references a request for judicial notice. (See Demurrer at p. 3:26-28.) However, no request for judicial notice was filed with the demurrer. A request for judicial notice “must be made by [a] separate document . . . .” (Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles, Court Rules, Rule 3.8.)

 

Here, CFP did not file a separate document requesting judicial notice. Thus, the Court DENIES CFP’s request for judicial notice as it is procedurally improper.  

 

2.         Negligence Cause of Action  

 

“The elements of a negligence cause of action are duty, breach, causation, and damages.” Coyle v. Historic Mission Inn Corporation (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 627, 634. “Whether a duty exists is a question of law for the courts.” Ibid.

 

The Court finds that the allegations of the cross-complaint are not set forth in the clearest manner; however, from what the Court can ascertain, Zuniga alleges that CFP insured property with defective and unpermitted grading. (Cross-Complaint at p. 4.) CFP failed to install a proper retaining wall which would have prevented the parked Bobcat from sliding three feet into a fence and then “down deep [over four feet] on Plaintiffs property, causing significant property damage to [P]laintiff and [D]efendant[‘s] property.” (Cross-Complaint at p. 4.) Zuniga alleges that he greeted the owner’s parents prior to the incident, and they returned the greeting, but when they awoke after two hours of sleeping, they found the Bobcat on their property. (Cross-Complaint at p. 4.)

 

The Court finds that Zuniga has not stated a cause of action for negligence against CFP as the cross-complaint does not allege the elements of duty, breach, causation, or damages. (Cross-Complaint at p. 4.)

 

Therefore, the Court finds that the negligence cause of action is insufficiently alleged. The Court SUSTAINS the general demurrer of CFP to the first cause of action for negligence with leave to amend.

 

3.         Declaratory Relief Cause of Action           

 

            To state a cause of action for declaratory relief, “a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted. Further, “there is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.” Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, quotation marks omitted.

 

            Here, Zuniga checks a box on the Form PLD-PI-002 indicating that he is alleging a cause of action for declaratory relief. (Cross-Complaint at p. 3.) However, Zuniga has not alleged any facts indicating that there is a proper subject of declaratory relief at issue or that there is an actual controversy between the parties. (Cross-Complaint at p. 4.) Without sufficiently clear allegations, the Court cannot deem the declaratory relief cause of action as sufficient. Zuniga has not alleged any of the elements required to state cause of action for declaratory relief.

 

            The Court therefore SUSTAINS the general demurrer of CFP to the second cause of action for declaratory relief with leave to amend.

 

II.        Conclusion

 

            Based on the foregoing, CFP’s demurrer for uncertainty as to each cause of action in the cross-complaint is OVERRULED. 

 

            CFP’s demurrer to the negligence and declaratory relief causes of action in the cross-complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend. The Court does not find that there is no reasonable possibility of successful amendment.

 

            The Court recognizes that Zuniga is self-represented; however, a self-represented litigant is still held to the same standards as an attorney. Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543. If and when a First Amended Cross-Complaint is filed, Zuniga is ordered to clearly label the causes of action and to set forth the facts in a clear and comprehensible manner for the Court and the parties to understand.

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

Hearing Date: Tuesday, September 10, 2024

Department 25, Commissioner Mkrtchyan

Motion to Set Aside Default

Moving Party: Defendant Soul Housing

Responding Party: Plaintiff Gregory Johnson

 

RULING:       DEFENDANT SOUL HOUSING’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT IS GRANTED; Request to set an OSC Re: Sanctions is DENIED.

 

            DEFENDANT SOUL HOUSING TO GIVE NOTICE.

            The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

           

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiff and Landlord Gregory Johnson (“Plaintiff”) filed the underlying breach of contract complaint against its former tenant, Defendant Socially Oriented United Living, Inc. (“Defendant” or “Soul Housing”) for a single cause of action alleging Defendant Soul Housing breached the terms of their Lease Agreement when it failed to tender the full the monthly rent for the Premises in January 2022.

 

On July 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a CIV-100, requesting entry of default and a POS alleging proof of substituted service. The POS provides that service was made on May 21, 2024. That same day, the Court entered default.

 

On July 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Default, along with the Declarations of Defendant Soul Housing’s CEO, Roman Rozhansky; the former president of Soul Housing, Yigal Keren; and Defendant’s Counsel, Alexander R. Safyan. The instant motion contains an attached Proof of Service, stating service was made on Plaintiff’s Counsel, Lane Nussbaum, via mail.

 

On September 3, 2024, Defendant filed Notice of Plaintiff’s Non-Opposition to the instant motion.

 

On September 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed his tardy Opposition, stating the Opposition was tardy because Defendant failed to serve the instant motion by electronic means to the address identified for that purpose, thus, violating California Rules of Court (“CRC”), Rule 2.251(c) (2).

 

On September 5, 2024, Defendant filed a Reply.

 

MOVING PAPERS:

 

Defendant Soul Housing contends that the default should be set aside for three main reasons and that the Court should accordingly award Defendant sanctions for the costs of having to bring the instant motion.

 

First, Plaintiff failed to timely serve the Complaint and file a proof of service within the period requirement by Rule 3.110(b) of the California Rules of Court (“CRC”). The lack of timeliness is apparent from the Court record without reference to any extrinsic evidence, making the underlying default void on its face under CCP § 473(d).

 

Second, Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to effect proper service on Defendant, making the default void as a matter of law under § 474 (d). Despite Plaintiff purporting to serve Defendant Soul Housing (a corporation) via substituted service, Plaintiff did not comply with the statutory requirements of § 415.20. This is because Plaintiff’s process server left copies of the summon and complaint with a “Maria Doe” at Soul Housing’s former president’s residence which does not constitute valid service on Soul Housing. Defendant asserts this is especially improper considering Defendant’s business address and address for its designated service agent are publicly available on the Secretary of State’s website.   (Safyan Decl., ¶ 6, Exs. D.-E.) Thus, Plaintiff did not even attempt to serve Soul Housing there or mail copies of the summons and complaint to its actual office. (Mot., pg. 5.)

 

Third, Defense Counsel reached out to Plaintiff’s Counsel on June 23, 2024, before Plaintiff requested entry of default, and offered to accept service on Defendant Soul Housing’s behalf. (Id. at pg. 5, Safyan Decl., ¶ 8, Exs. F-G.) However, Plaintiff’s Counsel did not timely respond; instead, counsel filed the request for default nine days later without warning Defense counsel, as he was ethically and legally required to. (Id.) Plaintiff’s Counsel then responded to Defense Counsel’s email three days after filing the request for default. (Id.) Yet, even at that point, Plaintiff’s Counsel did not notify Defendant of the default entered on July 2, 2024. Defense Counsel Safyan claims he did not learn default had been entered until July 9, 2024. Thus, not only is the underlying default void on its face, but it should also be set aside on equitable grounds and Defense Counsel’s improper conduct warrants sanctions.

 

In sum, Defendant argues that the default is void on its face due to both (a) Plaintiff’s untimely service; and (b) improper service. Lastly, (c) Defendant argues Defense Counsel’s failure to warn Plaintiff’s Counsel that he was imminently seeking default was a breach of both a legal and ethical duty as prescribed by the court in LaSalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127.

 

OPPOSITION PAPERS

 

On Opposition, Plaintiff’s counsel argues that pursuant to Superior Court of Los Angeles County, Local Rules, rule 3.4(a) requires—in general civil cases such as the instant action—all documents must be electronically filed. (LASC Local Rule 3.4(a).) Thus, Defendant’s failure to follow Local Rule 3.4(a) is the cause of Plaintiff’s tardy opposition.

 

The Court notes this and accepts Plaintiff’s Opposition accordingly.

 

            Plaintiff further contends that § 474(d) provides that a motion for relief shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party's lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5(b). Thus, Plaintiff argues Defendant possessed both actual notice and the ability to defend the action and, yet, chose not to. Thus, Defendant took no action on his own behalf to protect his interests and this request for the equivalent of a “do-over” must be denied in its entirety. (Opp., pg. 5.)

 

REPLY

 

            In Reply, Defendant asserts that it has served its Motion by U.S. mail and electronic mail to Plaintiff’s counsel.  Furthermore, Plaintiff’s Proof of Service of Summons reflects that service was attempted by substituted service on “Maria, a competent member of the household, authorized to accept service on behalf of Yigal Karen, agent for service of process for Socially Oriented United Living, Inc. dba Soul Housing.”

 

ANALYSIS

 

California Code Civil Procedure § 473(d) provides:

 

(d) The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.

 

“Former landlord had knowledge of residential tenant's address such that service by leaving summons and complaint with a commercial mail receiving agency was not available to landlord under statute providing for service in lieu of personal delivery, in landlord's lawsuit alleging causes of action for breach of lease agreement and negligence; landlord included tenant's address on both a security deposit itemization form and a letter to tenant, which were exhibits to his complaint, and such address also appeared on non-service report regarding attempted service on tenant by personal delivery.” (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 415.20(c).) (Kremerman v. White, 71 Cal. App. 5th 358, 286 Cal. Rptr. 3d 283 (2021).)

 

“Employee of commercial mail receiving agency failed to send summons and complaint to tenant within five-day statutory time period, and thus service was improper in landlord's suit against tenant alleging causes of action for breach of lease agreement and negligence; although employee stated in her declaration that she received summons and complaint from process server and placed them in tenant's mailbox on the same day, she further indicated that she did not send the documents to tenant's home address until 20 days later.” Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17538.5(d)(1); Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 415.20(c). (Kremerman v. White, 71 Cal. App. 5th 358, 286 Cal. Rptr. 3d 283 (2021).)

 

“Given that trial court lacked personal jurisdiction over tenant due to improper service of summons and complaint, court's denial of tenant's motion to vacate void default judgment was abuse of discretion, in landlord's action alleging causes of action for breach of lease agreement and negligence; tenant had no proper notice of action against her and promptly moved to vacate default and default judgment when she learned of the action through a third party.” (Kremerman v. White, 71 Cal. App. 5th 358, 286 Cal. Rptr. 3d 283 (2021).)

 

“[U]nder section 473, subdivision (d), the court may set aside a default judgment [or order] which is valid on its face, but void, as a matter of law, due to improper service.” (Kremerman, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 371.)

 

“[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction” over a defendant. (Id.) “[I]f a default judgment was entered against a defendant who was not served with a summons as required by statute, the judgment is void, as the court lacked jurisdiction in a fundamental sense over the party and lacked authority to enter judgment.” (Id. at p. 370, citing OC Interior Services, LLC v. Nationstar Mortgage, LLC (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 1318, 1330- 1331.)

 

“Whether the lack of jurisdiction appears on the face of the judgment, or is shown by extrinsic evidence for a judgment that appears valid on its face, in either case the judgment is for all purposes a nullity—past, present and future.” (OC Interior Services, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at p. 1331.)

 

Section 415.20, subdivision (a), describes how to serve a corporation with valid substituted service, in lieu of personal service. (§ 415.20, subd. (a).) It requires, among other things, leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in the corporation’s office or, if no physical address is known, at the corporation’s usual mailing address. (Id.)

 

Plaintiff here did not personally serve Soul Housing. (Rozhansky Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff purported to serve Soul Housing via substituted service. (See Safyan Decl., Ex. B.) However, Plaintiff did not leave a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in Soul Housing’s office or at Soul Housing’s usual mailing address. Instead, Plaintiff’s process server performed service at a residence: 7227 Oakwood Ave., Los Angeles, CA 90036. (Id.) Soul Housing’s office address is publicly available on the California Secretary of State’s website: it is 3982 S. Figueroa Street, Suite 210, Los Angeles, CA 90037. (Safyan Decl., Exs. D & E.) Moreover, there is no indication that Plaintiff’s process server ever attempted to serve Soul Housing at its office address.

 

Plaintiff’s process server apparently made several attempts to serve Soul Housing at 7227 Oakwood Ave., but never attempted to serve Soul Housing at its actual business address at 3982 S. Figueroa Street. The mailing that followed leaving a copy of the summons and complaint also was not sent to Soul Housing. (See Safyan Decl., Ex. B.). (Mot., pg. 12.) Moreover, the Oakwood address has no formal connection to Soul Housing because it is the residence of Soul Housing’s former president, Yigal Keren. (Keren Decl. ¶ 6; Mot., pgs. 11-12.) Further, the Oakwood address is not listed on the Secretary of State’s website or on Soul Housing’s Statement of Information. (See Safyan Decl., Exs. D & E.) The proof of service states that copies of the summons and complaint were left with a “Maria Doe,” a competent member of Keren’s household which does not cure the fact that Keren is not the designated agent to receive service. (Mot., pg. 12.) Instead, the Secretary of State’s website indicates Rozhansky is.  (Rozhansky Decl. ¶ 4; Keren Decl. ¶ 3; Safyan Decl., Ex. D.)

 

Because service in this case was improper, there was no personal jurisdiction over Soul Housing. Accordingly, the default entered against Soul Housing is void and should be set aside. (See Kremerman, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at pp. 371-74 [improper service on former tenant by landlord resulted in lack of jurisdiction and rendered default judgment void]; County of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1226-31 [confirming that improper service results in lack of personal jurisdiction and makes the resulting default “void, not merely voidable”].)

 

            The Court finds Plaintiff has failed to establish proper service was effected on Defendant because Plaintiff’s arguments raise many doubts as to the issue. While Plaintiff raises many arguments as to why service on Defendant’s former President, namely leaving the documents with a Maria Doe, could be considered proper, Plaintiff fails to address two key important points. First, Defense Counsel emailed Plaintiff and offered to accept service, and yet, Plaintiff’s Counsel did not respond to this email until after default was entered. Moreover, Plaintiff did not meaningfully respond to Defense Counsel’s offer to accept service. Second, Defense Counsel overstretches legal authority to purport service on Keren was proper by assuming the Maria Doe was Maria Heifetz. This seems unnecessary given two valid means of service were provided to Plaintiff: the first being through Counsel Safyan and the second through the information publicly available on the State Secretary’s website. Thus, the totality of the evidence weighs against finding there was proper service in accordance with statutory law. Moreover, the law favors findings on the merit and in this instance, Plaintiff went to great effort to ensure otherwise. Given the instant facts and Court record evidence of deficiencies in service—both as to its means and its timeliness—the Court finds personal jurisdiction over Defendant was not established and that the default is invalid.

            Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside Default, as Defendant has demonstrated service was improper.

 

 

C. REQUEST TO SET AN ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE: SANCTIONS

 

In 2022, the California Court of Appeals issued a decision that surrounded an unlawful detainer action in Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico’s FAS, Inc. (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 198. In Shapell, the court stated, at the very beginning of its opinion: “An attorney has both an ethical and statutory obligation to warn opposing counsel, if counsel’s identity is known, of an intent to seek a default and to give counsel a reasonable opportunity to file a responsive pleading.” (Id. at p. 203 (emphasis added).) The Shapell court cited and built upon a 2019 decision, LaSalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127, which it said “confirmed that obligation directly, unequivocally, and without qualification.” (Id.) The LaSalle court had observed that it was “well-acknowledged that an attorney has an ethical obligation to warn opposing counsel that the attorney is about to take an adversary’s default.” Cal.App.5th at 135 (emphasis in original).)

            Here, Defense Counsel sent an email to Plaintiff’s Counsel, offering to accept service. Defense Counsel’s June 23, 2024 email exchange provides as follows:

Good afternoon counsel,

I represent Socially Oriented United Living, Inc. dba Soul Housing. I understand that your client, Gregory Johnson, has filed two related breach of contract actions against Soul Housing relating to properties located at 1323 and 1325 W 61st Street in Los Angeles. My client contact just returned from military service in Israel and was told by his wife that someone left copies of the complaints at their house. I’m writing to advise you that this is not proper service on Soul Housing; however, I have been authorized to accept service on Soul Housing’s behalf via Notice and Acknowledgement of Receipt.

Please send me all of the filing documents along with executed NAR forms, and I will sign and return the forms.

Thank you, Alex Safyan

On July 5, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel replied as follows:

Your client, Socially Oriented United Living, Inc., was on active military duty?

That's new.

 

Sincerely,

Lane M. Nussbaum

Nussbaum APC

27489 Agoura Road Suite 102
Agoura Hills, California 91301 lnussbaum@nussbaumapc.com
Tel. 818.660.1919 - Fax. 818.864.3241 www.nussbaumapc.com

 

The Court refers Plaintiff’s Counsel to Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico’s FAS, Inc. (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 198, 221. (“The obligation to advise opposing counsel of an impending default is part of an attorney’s responsibility to the court and the legal profession and takes precedent over the obligation to represent the client effectively [Citations Omitted]”)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Set Aside is GRANTED. The Request to set an OSC Re: Sanctions is DENIED.
