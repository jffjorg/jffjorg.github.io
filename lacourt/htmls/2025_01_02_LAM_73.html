DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV315347    Hearing Date: January 2, 2025    Dept: 73

01/02/2023

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

SOLEDAD VALENCIA v. NORMANDIE/WILSHIRE RETIREMENT HOTEL, INC., et al.

(22STCV31547)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Steven Peck (Peck Law Group, APC)

 

Counsel for Defendant Barlow Respiratory Hospital/moving party: Jeffrey Grass (Davis, Grass, Goldstein & Finlay)

 

MOTION FOR SUMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION BY DEFENDANT BARLOW RESPIRATORY HOSPITAL (filed 10/11/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to the first and second causes of action against Defendant Barlow Respiratory Hospital.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 27, 2023, Plaintiff Soledad Valencia (“Plaintiff”) filed her Complaint against Defendants Normandie/Wilshire Retirement Hotel, Inc. (“CHRC”) and Barlow Respiratory Hospital (“Barlow”).

 

On July 19, 2023, Defendant Barlow filed a demurrer to the Complaint’s first cause of action for elder abuse and a motion to strike portions of the Complaint. On August 22, 2023, this court sustained Defendant Barlow’s demurrer with leave to amend and the motion to strike was moot.

 

On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging claims for (1) Elder Abuse, (2) Negligence, and (3) Violation of Residents Rights against Defendant CHRC only.

 

The FAC alleges the following as it pertains to Barlow.  Plaintiff was an elderly patient admitted to Barlow from July 21, 2023 until September 15, 2021. (FAC, ¶¶ 15-23.) During her hospitalization at Barlow, Plaintiff’s care plan required Barlow staff to monitor Plaintiff’s skin at least two times daily for redness or breakdown, and to reposition Plaintiff at least every two hours to prevent skin breakdown. (FAC, ¶ 17.) As a result of Barlow’s staff’s failure to adhere to Plaintiff’s care plan as well as understaffing of the facility by Barlow’s managing agents, Plaintiff developed pressure injuries to her sacrum. (FAC, ¶¶ 21-22.)

 

On November 19, 2024, Plaintiff added Glendale Adventist Medical Center dba Adventist Health Glendale as DOE 1.

 

On October 11, 2024, Defendant Barlow filed a Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication, arguing:

·       Barlow Respiratory Hospital contends that the care and treatment provided to Ms. Valencia met the standard of care and no act or omission on its part caused injury to Ms. Valencia. Moreover, Barlow Respiratory Hospital contends that there is no triable issue of material fact that it did not abuse or neglect Ms. Valencia. The undisputed evidence is that Barlow Respiratory Hospital met the patient’s care needs and provided the patient with care and treatment as required.

·       Ms. Valencia was admitted to Barlow Respiratory Hospital on July 21, 2021, with a deep tissue injury to her sacrococcygeal area (sacrum). The condition of the injury remained stable during the patient’s admission and was a deep tissue injury at the time of discharge from Barlow on September 15, 2021. Barlow cites to medical records in support.

·       On the issue of standard of care, the Motion is supported by the declaration of Ryan Klein, M.D. It is the expert opinion of Dr. Klein that the care and treatment provided by Barlow Respiratory Hospital to the plaintiff met the standard of care. (Exhibit L, Klein Decl., p.5, lines 2-27.)

 

No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Motions for summary adjudication are procedurally identical to motions for summary judgment. (Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 859.) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code of Civ. Proc., §¿437c,¿subd. (f)(1).)¿

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment/summary adjudications must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.) 

 

B.      First Cause of Action for Elder Abuse and Second Cause of Action for Negligence

 

Defendant Barlow moves for summary adjudication of the first and second causes of action.

 

To plead elder abuse, the plaintiff must allege “facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care [citations]; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs [citations]; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult’s basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness) [citations].”  (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-07.)  “The plaintiff must also allege . . . that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering.” (Id. at p. 407.) “[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.” (Ibid. quoting Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790).)   

 

“‘[N]eglect’ within the meaning of Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.57 covers an area of misconduct distinct from ‘professional negligence.’” (Covenant Care, Inc., supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 783.) “As used in the Act, neglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the ‘failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.’” (Ibid.) Furthermore, in order to distinguish Dependent Adult Abuse from Professional Negligence, there must be a showing of recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression. (Ibid.) “Oppression, fraud, and malice involve intentional, willful, or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature” whereas recklessness requires deliberate disregard of a high degree of probability an injury will occur. (Carter, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at 405 [internal quotation marks omitted].)   

 

In Carter, the court provided examples of cases involving conduct sufficiently egregious to warrant an award of enhanced remedies under the Elder Abuse Act. One such example was the following:  

 

An 88–year–old woman with a broken ankle “was frequently left lying in her own urine and feces for extended periods¿of time” and she developed¿pressure ulcers¿on her ankles, feet and buttocks that exposed bone, “despite plaintiff's persistent complaints to nursing staff, administration, and finally, to a nursing home ombudsman.”  

 

(Carter, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at 405, citing Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 27, 41.) 

 

Additionally, to recover against a corporate defendant for elder abuse, a plaintiff must allege that an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation personally engaged in wrongful conduct, or else had advance knowledge of the conduct, authorized it, or ratified it.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657, subd. (c).)   

 

Here, the FAC alleges the following. Barlow is a hospital that provides general acute care. (FAC, ¶ 3.) At all relevant times, Plaintiff was an elder over 65 years of age who had physical and developmental disabilities. (FAC, ¶ 11.) On July 21, 2021, Plaintiff was admitted to Barlow for long term acute care. (FAC, ¶ 15.) When she was admitted, Plaintiff had no pressure injuries or skin breakdown. (FAC, ¶ 16.) Plaintiff’s skin integrity care plan at Barlow indicated that Barlow staff would monitor her skin at least two times daily for redness or breakdown and report to a physician for further treatment orders in the event of any skin breakdown issues. (FAC, ¶ 17.) Plaintiff’s care plan also required Barlow staff to reposition her at least every two hours to prevent skin breakdown. (Ibid.) However, Plaintiff alleges that on numerous days, Barlow staff failed to inspect her skin every shift and reposition her every two hours. (FAC, ¶ 20.) As a result, Plaintiff developed pressure injuries to her sacrum which she continues to endure pain and suffering for. (FAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff further alleges that Barlow deliberately understaffed its nursing staff, which also caused Barlow staff to fail to inspect her skin every shift and reposition her as required by her care plan. (FAC, ¶ 22.) On September 15, 2021, Plaintiff left Barlow and was admitted to CHRC for skilled nursing care. (FAC, ¶ 23.) According to Plaintiff, Barlow’s neglect of the Plaintiff included its failure to provide medical care, to protect her from health and safety hazards and failure to provide sufficient staff. (FAC at ¶¶ 6, 15 – 30, 36 - 40).

 

Barlow argues it did not abuse or neglect Plaintiff. Barlow contends that its expert witness, Ryan Klein, M.D. ("Dr. Klein"), establishes that it complied with the applicable standard of care in treating Plaintiff. Barlow asserts that when Plaintiff was admitted, her condition was assessed by the nurse, and orders were obtained for wound care management, which appropriately included Optifoam for the sacrococcygeal deep tissue injury, citric acid for the moisture associated damage of the perianal area, pressure reduction mattress, along with turning and repositioning Plaintiff. (Exhibit C – Barlow Records, p.001912 – 001913; Exhibit D – Barlow Records, p.001879 - 001880; Exhibit F – Barlow Records, p.000014 – 000015; Exhibit L – Klein Decl., p.3, lines 12-16.) Barlow also submits Plaintiff’s medical records showing that the orders were carried out, with daily assessments and treatment of the sacrococcygeal deep tissue injury and perianal MASD wound, and repositioning every two hours. (Exhibit G – Barlow Records, p.002861 – 002880; Exhibit H – Barlow Records, p.003356 – 003702; Exhibit L, Klein Decl., p.5, lines 12-18.) Further, Barlow asserts the sacrococcygeal deep tissue injury and the perianal MASD remained stable throughout the Plaintiff’s admission and Plaintiff was provided with adequate hydration, nutrition, and medications, including antibiotics. Dr. Klein states that the fact that Plaintiff’s sacrococcygeal deep tissue injury and perianal MASD did not progress or become infected, and that she was able to be transferred to a lower level of care, as opposed to being readmitted to an acute care hospital, reflects that the patient’s condition did not deteriorate and that her basic needs were met, and she was provided reasonable and appropriate care. (Exhibit L, Klein Decl., p.5, lines 19-27.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Barlow has carried its initial burden. Plaintiff must establish a triable issue of material fact in order to avoid summary adjudication.

 

Here, Plaintiff did not file an opposition. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion or demurrer is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.) Thus, Plaintiff has failed to carry her burden.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to the first and second causes of action against Defendant Barlow Respiratory Hospital.

The Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to the first and second causes of action against Defendant Barlow Respiratory Hospital.

1.      Fraud

2.      Constructive Fraud

3.      Negligence

4.      Conversion

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendant Kathy Hyun Kyung Lee (“Lee”) represented herself as someone who works with banks and could assist Plaintiffs in refinancing mortgage loans. However, Plaintiffs are informed and believe that Lee is not a licensed mortgage/loan broker or agent. Lee did not disclose that she was not a license mortgage/loan broker or agent. Lee, through false representations, convinced Plaintiffs to give her total sum of $ 43,759.04 between 2000 to May 2022. When Plaintiffs demanded the funds in return, Lee wrote a check which had insufficient funds. Plaintiffs have made multiple demands for the return of money without success.

 

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendant Hernandez and Does 1-50.

 

On July 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Request for Default Judgment against Defendant Lee. Plaintiffs seek judgment against Defendant Lee in the total amount of $154,770.52 consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $43,759.05, $110,406.48 in interest, and $605 in costs.

 

On September 19, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ Request for Default Judgment without prejudice.

 

On October 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Request for Default Judgment against Defendant Lee. Plaintiffs seek judgment against Defendant Lee in the total amount of $55,397.62 consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $43,759.05, $11,033.58 in interest, and $605 in costs. 

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.)

Plaintiffs are seeking default judgment against Defendant Lee. Plaintiffs properly dismissed non-parties to the judgment, submitted a CIV-100 form, submitted declarations to support damages, and a proposed judgment. It appears Plaintiffs submitted a proper default judgment application so long as they present adequate evidence of damages. 

 

To prove damages, Plaintiffs provide their own declarations stating:

 

“When plaintiffs needed help with refinancing their loan, they asked Lee and she represented herself as someone who works with banks and could assist Plaintiffs in refinancing mortgage loans. However, at that time, we did not know Lee was not a licensed mortgage/loan broker or agent. Lee convinced plaintiffs to deposit $ 43,759.04 but as time went by without any progress, Plaintiffs couldn't wait any longer and asked for the refund. Lee promised to refund the money and in fact did write a check, but the check was returned for insufficient funds. We want the money back. The $43,759.04 was not intended as a fee but to part of the refinance. For the foregoing reasons, I am entitled to the return of the $ 43,759.04 I deposited with Lee.” (Lee Decl., ¶¶ 4-6; Park Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.)

 

Plaintiffs purportedly attached a copy of the checks from Defendant that were returned for insufficient funds as well as correspondence between Plaintiff and Defendant in support of their assertion that Defendant failed to return their deposit. (Lee Decl., Exs. B-C; Park Decl., Exs. B-C.)

 

However, Plaintiffs have not provided evidence showing that any monies were transmitted from Plaintiffs to Defendant. Exhibit A to Plaintiffs’ declarations provides only a table of the amounts they allegedly deposited to Defendant and the dates. Yet, Plaintiffs do not provide any invoices or bank statements in support. (Lee Decl., Ex. A; Park Decl., Ex. A.)

 

Default judgment may only be granted upon competent evidence proving Plaintiff’s entitlement to the damages sought. (Kim v. Westmoor Partners, Inc. (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 267, 272.) A CCP § 585 declaration must include a proper foundation for the testimony. (CCP § 585(d).) A defaulting defendant admits only the well pled facts concerning liability, not damages. Plaintiff must still introduce admissible prima facie evidence of damages. (Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361.)

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiffs have adequately supported their claims for damages. Accordingly, request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

SUPERSTAR MONITORING LLC, et al. v. SHARP GROUP LLC, et al. (23STCV31024)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Suzanne Rand-Lewis (Gary Rand & Suzanne E. Rand-Lewis, Professional Law Corporations)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Corine Zygelman (Murchison & Cumming LLP)

 

demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE FIRST AMENDED complaint (filed 6/24/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ first and twelfth causes of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s second, third, fourth and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs Superstar Monitoring LLC; Annmarie Baranik (“Baranik”); Raymond Anthony Lerma; Samantha Brown; Adrian Napolitano and Roman Moreno filed a Complaint against Defendants Sharp Group LLC and Rozita Natanzi.

 

On October 2, 2024, the Court sustained in part Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint with leave to amend.

 

On October 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action against the Defendants: (1) Breach of Express and Implied Contract; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (3) Breach of Express and Implied Warranty; (4) Breach of Quiet Enjoyment; (5) Trespass; (6) Nuisance; (7) Fraud, Negligent Misrepresentation, Concealment; (8) Negligence – Premises Liability; (9) Negligence - Negligent Supervision, Negligent Management; (10) Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200; (11) Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; and (12) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiffs were tenants/occupants of the subject premises, at Defendants’ Oakridge Plaza with the street address of 14336 Ventura Boulevard. Plaintiff Baranik was the lessee of the premises. Defendants entered into a written lease with Plaintiff Baranik, which provided for her to have the exclusive use of and to occupy 1,117 square feet of retail space, parking and amenities located at the premises for the use of her business Superstar Monitoring LLC, and her employees, occupants and clientele. Almost immediately after moving in, Plaintiffs allege issues arose relating to the hot water, thermostat, heat, unhoused individuals in front of the building, smoke from other tenants, water intrusion and the elevator. Plaintiffs also allege Defendants breached the lease and their duties to Plaintiffs by failing to remediate water intrusion on the premises which caused the premises to be unfit for use, unhealthy and unsafe.

 

On November 19, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs’ first, second, third, fourth, sixth and twelfth causes of action fail for uncertainty and failure to state a claim.

·       Defendants demur to the first cause of action (Breach of Express and Implied Contract), second cause of action (Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing) and fourth cause of action (Breach of Quiet Enjoyment) as to the “non lessee” Plaintiffs

o   The Non-Lessee Plaintiffs have no standing to allege damages based on contractual claims where they were not parties to the contract.

·       Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for "Breach of Express and Implied Warranty" fails because it is not clear what warranty is actually being alleged.

o   It is presumed for purposes of this demurrer that Plaintiffs are seeking damages for warranty of habitability; however, there is no recognized implied warranty of habitability in a commercial lease.

·       Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action for nuisance is duplicative. The nuisance claim is a mere clone of the negligence cause of action.

·       Plaintiffs’ twelfth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress fails to state facts. Allegations supporting a showing that Defendants acted intentionally or outrageously have not been adequately pled.

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing that the FAC sufficiently sets forth facts to constitute a cause of action.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiffs failed to plead facts to constitute each of its causes of action.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating she sent Plaintiffs’ counsel a letter outlining the alleged issues in the Complaint. (Mantovani Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Defendants’ counsel received an email from Plaintiffs’ counsel stating they were unwilling to amend the Complaint. (Ibid.) While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.      First, Second and Fourth Causes of Action against Non-Lessee Plaintiffs

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action (Breach of Express and Implied Contract), second cause of action (Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing) and fourth cause of action (Breach of Quiet Enjoyment) as to the “non lessee” Plaintiffs.

 

The FAC alleges that “[o]n or about December 21, 2020, Baranik (“Lessee”) and Sharp (“Lessor”) entered into a written lease for the suitable use of the common areas and retail space in Defendants’ Oakridge Plaza commencing February 1, 2021, and ending January 23, 2023.” (FAC, ¶ 3.)

 

While the lease is not attached to the FAC, Defendants assert that the FAC states that only Baranik was the lessee. Therefore, Defendants argue that the other Non-Lessee Plaintiffs have no standing to allege damages based on contractual claims where they were not parties to the contract. "Someone who is not a party to [a] contract has no standing to enforce the contract.’ (Hatchwell v. Blue Shield of California (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 1027, 1034; Republic Indem. Co. v. Schofield (1996) 47 Cal. App. 4th 220, 227.) Defendants assert Non-Lessee Plaintiffs also have no standing as to implied contract terms, including Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing and Breach of Quiet Enjoyment. “The prerequisite for any action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is the existence of a contractual relationship between the parties, since the covenant is an implied term in the contract.” (Smith v. City and County of San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49.) The implied covenant of quiet enjoyment runs between the tenants and the landlord from whom they are renting the Property. (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Ests. (2005) 125 Cal. App. 4th 578, 590.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs first argue that Defendants are barred from raising this argument as to the first and second causes of action because Defendants failed to demur to these causes of action in their first Demurrer.

 

As to the first cause of action for Breach of Express and Implied Contract, the Court agrees. Under CCP 430.41(b), a party demurring to a pleading that has been amended after a demurrer to an earlier version of the pleading was sustained shall not demur to any portion of the amended complaint, cross-complaint, or answer on grounds that could have been raised by demurrer to the earlier version of the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer. Thus, after the first demurrer, a party cannot subsequently challenge a cause of action if the previous demurrer was overruled, or an unchanged cause of action was initially unchallenged. Here, the first cause of action is an unchanged cause of action that was initially unchallenged. The original Complaint’s first cause of action was for Breach of Express and Implied Contract. Defendants’ first Demurrer filed on June 24, 2024, did not demur to the first cause of action. Therefore, Defendants are precluded from challenging this cause of action in the instant Demurrer.  

 

As to the second cause of action for Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing and fourth cause of action for Breach of Quiet Enjoyment, Defendants are not precluded from challenging these causes of action. In Defendants’ first Demurrer, Defendants had demurred to the original Complaint’s second cause of action for “Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing/Breach of Express and Implied Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment, Trespass, Nuisance.” The Court had sustained the Demurrer to this cause of action based on uncertainty. Thus, the FAC’s second cause of action and fourth cause of action were originally challenged and sustained in Defendants’ first Demurrer. Therefore, Defendants are not barred from demurring to the second and fourth causes of action.

 

Plaintiffs next argue that the Non Lessee Plaintiffs have standing as to the Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing and Breach of Quiet Enjoyment because they were employees of the business and intended beneficiaries of the lease. Plaintiffs contend the FAC alleges that the lease conferred rights and benefits on all of the Plaintiffs: “Defendants entered into a written lease with Plaintiff ANNMARIE BARANIK, which provided for her to have the exclusive use of and to occupy 1,117 square feet of retail space, parking and amenities located at Defendants’ Oakridge Plaza with the street address of 14336 Ventura Boulevard (hereinafter the “PREMISES”) for the suitable use of her business SUPERSTAR MONITORING LLC, formerly known as Legal Angels, and her employees, occupants and clientele.” (FAC, ¶ 1.)

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ argument persuasive. As Defendants point out, Plaintiffs provide no details about how the Non Lessee Plaintiffs were intended beneficiaries, nor is it alleged that there was any manifestation in the lease that third parties were intended to be benefitted. The contracting parties must clearly manifest their intent to benefit the third party. (City & County of San Francisco v. Western Air Lines, Inc. (1962) 204 Cal.App.2d 105, 120–121.) Plaintiffs fail to cite to any authority establishing that the Non Lessee Plaintiffs have standing as to the Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing and Breach of Quiet Enjoyment for being employees of the business. The case that Plaintiffs cite in support, Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, is inapposite as it pertains to whether the defendant owed its tenants a right to quiet enjoyment, and does not discuss whether third party beneficiaries or employees are also owed this right.  

 

Since Defendants had not previously demurred to the first cause of action, it is OVERRULED. The Court SUSTAINS the second and fourth causes of action with leave to amend.

 

D.     Third Cause of Action: Breach of Express and Implied Warranty

 

Next, Defendants demur to the third cause of action for Breach of Express and Implied Warranty against all Plaintiffs.

 

To establish a breach of the implied warranty of habitability, Plaintiffs must establish (1) “the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises’ habitability,” (2) “notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant’s discovery of the condition,” (3) “the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and” (4) “resulting damages.”  (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

 

Defendants contend it is well established that there is no recognized implied warranty of habitability in a commercial lease. (See, e.g., Muro v. Superior Court (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 1089, 1098; Fish Construction Co. v. Moselle Coach Works, Inc. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 654, 658; Schulman v. Vera (1980) 108 Cal.App.3d 552, 560–561.)

 

Plaintiffs do not dispute that this cause of action pertains to a commercial lease. However, Plaintiffs argue they were a small business and their tenancy was akin to a dwelling, in which they provided services like a home visit to their customers. Plaintiffs argue “Superstar fka Legal Angels was a monitoring business which provided Court ordered supervision services for families and children with Court ordered visitation needs. The Premises provided a home environment for the family visits to occur. The Premises were more like a home than an office or other retail business.” (Oppos., p. 10.) However, the FAC fails to contain any such allegations.

 

The Court SUSTAINS the third cause of action with leave to amend.

 

E.      Sixth Cause of Action: Nuisance

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action for nuisance fails because it is duplicative of its negligence causes of action.

 

To establish an action for private nuisance, (1) “the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of his property”; (2) “the invasion of the plaintiff’s interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, that is, that it causes the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, but it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.”  (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263, cleaned up.)

 

The FAC alleges:

 

“The dangerous and defective conditions of the PREMISES alleged in the factual allegations above constituted a nuisance. The conditions include, but were not limited to, that Plaintiffs were exposed to unsafe, unfit premises with long standing and ongoing safety, water, mold and smoke intrusion issues at the PREMISES. Defendants refused reasonable access to water. Defendants did not provide hot water. Defendants did not provide heat. The common areas were unclean, unsafe and Plaintiffs had to report unhoused individuals living in front of the business and blocking ingress and egress multiple times. Other tenants used the area in front of the business to smoke, causing smoke to enter the PREMISES and to cause the PREMISES to be unhealthy and unusable. Water intruded from structural defects into the garage parking areas making it unsafe. The elevator was not functional for months causing difficulty access the premises and moving objects necessary for use in the premises. The common areas were wet, slippery and unsafe. Water was permitted to intrude in and remain in the premises causing it to be unsafe and unhealthy. The PREMISES was unfit for use, unhealthy and unsafe, as there was a complete lack of upkeep and maintenance, it was not watertight, its systems were old and run down, which permitted the existence and infestation of the PREMISES with mold, mildew, fungus, insects, in addition to the trespasses by people, smoke, slippery common areas, lack of heat, parking and elevator issues, which were nuisances as stated above.”

 

(FAC, ¶ 61.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ nuisance claim appears to be a negligence claim. Plaintiffs’ nuisance cause of action is premised on the unsafe conditions of the Property. Similarly, the nuisance cause of action is premised on the same defective conditions of the Property that constituted a nuisance (FAC, ¶ 81.) Where negligence and nuisance causes of action rely on the same facts about lack of due care, the nuisance claim is a negligence claim. (El Escorial Owners' Assn. v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal. App. 4th 1337, 1349; Martinez v. Pacific Bell (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1557, 1565.) Further, Plaintiffs seek the same relief for both causes of action.

 

Thus, Plaintiffs fail to distinguish their nuisance cause of action from their negligence causes of action. Accordingly, the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

F.       Twelfth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress cause of action fails to state a cause of action.

 

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, cleaned up.)

 

The “mere allegation that the plaintiffs suffered severe emotional distress, without facts indicating the nature or extent of any mental suffering incurred as a result of the defendant's alleged outrageous conduct, fail[s] to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.” (Pitman v. City of Oakland (1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 1037, 1047.) “The allegation that plaintiff suffered shame, humiliation and embarrassment without further factual explanation does not meet the requirement of specificity….” (Id.)

 

The FAC alleges:

 

“[T]he dangerous and defective conditions of the PREMISES alleged in the factual allegations above were outrageous and intolerable by civil society including Defendants’ depriving Plaintiffs of the basic things necessary for life such as water, heat and fresh air. Their outrageous intolerable conduct included, but was not limited to, that Plaintiffs were exposed to unsafe, unfit premises with long standing and ongoing safety, water, mold and smoke intrusion issues, Defendants refused reasonable access to water, Defendants did not provide hot water, Defendants did not provide heat. The common areas were unclean, unsafe and Plaintiffs had to report unhoused individuals living in front of the business and blocking ingress and egress multiple times. Other tenants used the area in front of the business to smoke, causing smoke to enter the PREMISES and to cause the PREMISES to be unhealthy and unusable. Water intruded from structural defects into the garage parking areas making it unsafe. The elevator was not functional for months causing difficulty access the premises and moving objects necessary for use in the premises. The common areas were wet, slippery and unsafe. Defendants permitted the existence and infestation of the PREMISES with mold, mildew, fungus, insects, in addition to the trespasses by people, smoke, slippery common areas, lack of heat, parking and elevator issues.

 

Because of these conditions Plaintiffs were sick. Defendants knew the conditions were causing Plaintiffs to be sick. They still let them become worse.

 

Plaintiffs suffered extreme distress including fear, worry, anxiety, which affect their daily lives and caused them anguish, pain, suffering and illness.”

 

(FAC, ¶¶ 96-98.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs allegations sufficient at this time. Here, Plaintiffs have alleged the nature of their mental suffering incurred as a result of Defendants’ alleged outrageous conduct. (FAC, ¶ 98.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the twelfth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ first and twelfth causes of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s second, third, fourth and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
