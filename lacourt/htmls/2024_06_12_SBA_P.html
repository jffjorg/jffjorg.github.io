DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00085    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: P

(1) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES SET ONE

(2) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS SET ONE

(3) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES SET ONE

(4) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR ADMISSION SET ONE

The court considered the moving papers. No opposition or reply papers were filed.

RULINGS

The court rules on the motions as follows.

The request to compel plaintiff Aries Global Logistics, Inc.’s further responses to defendant Valley of the Sun Cosmetics, LLC’s Form Interrogatories (Set One), Request for Production of Documents (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), and Request for Admission (Set One) is GRANTED. The plaintiff is ordered to serve further responses to those discovery requests within 30 days of this ruling.

The request for sanctions is GRANTED. Plaintiff Aries Global Logistics, Inc. is ordered to pay defendant Valley of the Sun Cosmetics, LLC total sanctions of $2,840.

BACKGROUND

On 2/5/2021, plaintiff Aries Global Logistics, Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “AGL”) filed this action.

On 5/6/2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Fourth Amended Complaint (“4AC”) against defendants Valley of the Sun Cosmetics, LLC (“VOTS”), Ajmal Shehzad, Sofia Shehzad, Danish Gajiani, Faizan Bakali, Clean Beauty Concepts, LLC, Mad Engine, LLC, and Does 1-50, inclusive, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of written contract, (2) declaratory relief, (3) injunctive relief, (4) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (5) quantum meruit, (6) account stated, (7) fraud, (8) fraudulent conveyance, and (9) intentional interference with contractual relations.

The 4AC alleges that this action arises from VOTS’s failure to pay Plaintiff for services Plaintiff performed (i.e., freight forwarding and storage of hand sanitizer bottles and caps during the COVID-19 pandemic) pursuant to the parties’ written contract.

On February 20, 2024, VOTS filed the instant motions to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to its Form Interrogatories, Set One (“FROG”), Request for Production of Documents, Set One (“RPD”), Special Interrogatories, Set One (“SROG”), and Request for Admission, Set One (“RFA”).

As of June 10, 2024, no opposition or reply has been filed.

LEGAL STANDARD

A. Demand for Production

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: ¶ (1) A statement of compliance with

the demand is incomplete. ¶ (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. ¶ (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a). “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).

B. Interrogatories

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: ¶ (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. ¶ (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. ¶ (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).

C. Request for Admission

“A party to a civil action may propound a written request that another party ‘admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact.’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010; [citation].)” City of Glendale v. Marcus Cable Associates, LLC (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 344, 351–352.

“On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: ¶ (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete. ¶ (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (a).

DISCUSSION

A. Timeliness

The motions must be brought within 45 days of service of the verified response, supplemental verified response, or on a date to which the propounding and responding parties have agreed to in writing; otherwise, the propounding party waives the right to compel further responses. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c); 2031.310, subd. (c); 2033.290, subd. (c); but see Golf & Tennis Pro Shop, Inc. v. Superior Court (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127, 134-136 (suggesting that the 45-day deadline does not apply to objections-only responses). “[T]he time within which to make a motion to compel production of documents is mandatory and jurisdictional just as it is for motions to compel further answers to interrogatories.” Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410. The 45-day deadline “is ‘jurisdictional’ in the sense that it renders the court without authority to rule on motions to compel other than to deny them.” Ibid.

The motions are timely since they were filed on February 20, 2024, within 45 days of January 2, 2024, when Plaintiff served the discovery responses at issue. FROG Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 3; SROG Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 3; RPD Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 3; RFA Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 3.

B. Meet and Confer

The instant motions to compel further responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b)(1); 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).

Here, VOTS has satisfied the meet and confer requirement for each motion. Defense counsel testifies that he sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff, but Plaintiff did not respond. FROG Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 4; SROG Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ r; RPD Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 4; RFA Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 4.

C. Separate Statement

The motions must be accompanied by a separate statement. Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(1), (2), (3), and (5). “A separate statement is a separate document filed and served with the discovery motion that provides all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses to it that are at issue. The separate statement must be full and complete so that no person is required to review any other document in order to determine the full request and the full response. Material must not be incorporated into the separate statement by reference.” Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(c).

Here, VOTS has satisfied the separate statement requirement of each motion.

D. The Discovery Requests at Issue

According to the separate statements, VOTS moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to RPDs Nos. 1 through 25, RFAs Nos. 1 through 39, SROG Nos. 1 through 52 (Plaintiff points out that VOTS did not provide any response to SROG No. 40), and FROG Nos., 1.1, 3.1, 3.2, 3.3, 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, 4.1, 4.2, 7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 12.7, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, 14.2, 17.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, and 50.6.

Plaintiff has not filed any opposition to the motions, disputing VOTS argument that it refused to meet and confer with VOTS or arguing that it later supplemented its responses. Nevertheless, “[t]he failure to file a written opposition [to a motion to compel discovery] … shall not be deemed an admission that the motion was proper or that sanctions should be awarded.” Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(b).

The court has reviewed the separate statements and agrees with VOTS that Plaintiff does not show that it attempted to respond to the authorized discovery methods in good faith. Specifically, most of the objections to the discovery were meritless. For example, RFA No. 3 asked Plaintiff: “Admit that there is no storage contract between YOU and Propounding Party.” RFA Separate Statement, p. 4:6-7. Plaintiff objected to that RFA, arguing that it calls for information protected by the attorney-client privilege and work-product doctrine; it seeks information not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence (even though the RFA seeks relevant information based on the 4AC’s allegations); it is vague as to time (even though the RFA does not need to be narrowed to a specific time period); and it is burdensome/oppressive (even though Plaintiff alleges that this action arises from VOTS alleged breach of an alleged storage contract). Plaintiff also responded to the RFA and other discovery requests, saying that: “Responding Party has not completed his investigation of facts and documents, his discovery, or his preparation for trial.” However, as VOTS argues in its separate statements and pointed out in its meet and confer letter, by using pronouns such as “his” (or “her” in other discovery responses) instead of “it” to describe Plaintiff, an entity, the responses

suggest that Plaintiff simply cut and pasted the objections from another set of discovery responses.

Accordingly, the request to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to the discovery requests at issue is GRANTED.

E. Requests for Sanctions

In each of the four motions, VOTS seeks monetary sanctions. Specifically, VOTS seeks sanctions of $3,310 per motion for the RPD and FROG motions. That amount consists of 3 hours counsel spent on the moving papers, 2 hours counsel anticipated spending on the reply and appearing at the hearing, a total of 5 hours at counsel’s billing rate of $650 per hour, plus a $60 filing fee. FROG Motion, Agege Decl. ¶ 6; RPD Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 6. For the RFA and SROG motions, VOTS seeks sanctions of $3,960 per motion. That amount consists of 4 hours counsel spent on the moving papers, 2 hours he anticipated to draft the reply and attend the hearing, a total of 6 hours multiplied by his billing rate of $650 per hour, and a $60 filing fee. RFA Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 6; SROG Motion, Agege Decl., ¶ 6.

The court finds it proper to impose sanctions given Plaintiff’s deficient responses. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h); 2033.290, subd. (d).

However, the court finds the requested sanctions excessive since no reply (or opposition) papers were filed. The court also finds a billing rate of $650 per hour excessive for these routine motions.

Therefore, the court imposes sanctions of $2,840 total (five hours of attorney time at $500/hr and four filing fees) against Plaintiff.

CONCLUSION

The request to compel plaintiff Aries Global Logistics, Inc.’s further responses to defendant Valley of the Sun Cosmetics, LLC’s Form Interrogatories (Set One), Request for Production of Documents (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), and Request for Admission (Set One) is GRANTED. The plaintiff is ordered to serve further responses to those discovery requests within 30 days of this ruling.

The request for sanctions is granted. Plaintiff Aries Global Logistics, Inc. is ordered to pay defendant Valley of the Sun Cosmetics, LLC sanctions of $2,840.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843; Code Civ. Proc., § 437c (c).) “[Section 437c] requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on¿the¿cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Then, “the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to¿the¿cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.

In a motion for summary judgment, the pleadings serve to “delimit the scope of the issues” while the affidavits function to “disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Flores v. City of San Diego (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 360, 372.) Facts alleged in affidavits of the party against whom the motion for summary judgment is based must be accepted as true. (Blaustein v. Burton (1970) 9 Cal.App.3d 161.)

IV. DISCUSSION

A. Positions of the Parties

a. Moving Party’s Arguments

Plaintiff argues that there are no triable issues of material fact for his first (trespass), second (nuisance), and third (interference with contractual relations) cause of action because all elements were established. (Not. of Mot. for Sum. Jmt. pp. 2,5-7.) Plaintiff provides his own declaration and exhibits in support of his motion.

b. Responding Party’s Opposition

In opposition, Defendants argue that there are triable issues of material facts because Plaintiff has not provided any evidence to support the elements of his causes of action besides his own declaration. (Opp’n at p. 1-3.) Moreover, Defendants also argue that Plaintiff’s claim of damages are not supported by law and states that the trespass cause of action is moot. (Opp’n at p. 5-7.) Defendants provide declarations from Eileen M. Kendall and Dorothy Forba in support of their claims that Plaintiff did not suffer any physical, mental, or emotional injuries and did not engage in the acts alleged by Plaintiff.

c. Moving Party’s Reply

In reply, Plaintiff reasserts his initial arguments and states that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Reply at p. 4.) There is still an ongoing trespass due to the fact that Defendants never removed the additional portion of the 2-step landing and porch on the west side of the Defendants’ property, 723 30th Street, Hermosa Beach, CA. (Reply at p.2.)

B. Legal Analysis

a. Trespass

“‘Trespass is an unlawful interference with possession of property.’ (Internal citation omitted.) The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant’s intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm. (See CACI No. 2000.)” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc., (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 261-62.)

In the complaint, Plaintiff asserts that “Plaintiff had full ownership and possession of the Property.”(Complaint ¶ 24.) “Since approximately May 2017, Defendants intentionally, recklessly, and/or negligently entered and continued entering Plaintiff’s property by and through their encroachments and actions as to Plaintiff’s agents and contractors.” (Complaint ¶ 25.) “Plaintiff never gave permission to Defendants to act in this manner or to enter the Property.” (Complaint ¶ 26.) “Plaintiff is and was actually harmed by Defendants’ actions and intrusion on his Property, and such harm was substantially caused by Defendants’ conduct resulting in economic and non-economic damages, among others.” (Complaint ¶ 27.)

In support of his position, Plaintiff presents his own declaration and other exhibits.

(1) The Plaintiff’s Ownership or Control of the Property

Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff is the owner of 721 30th Street, Hermosa Beach, California 90254 (“Plaintiff’s Property”). (UMF No. 1; Trozak Decl. ¶ 3.) Therefore, Plaintiff satisfied this element.

(2) The Defendant’s Intentional, Reckless, or Negligent Entry onto the Property

Plaintiff declares that after he “had a survey and topography of the property line between [his] property and the neighboring property then owned by Defendants which showed that a portion of Defendants’ property, 723 30th Street, Hermosa Beach, California 90245 was encroaching onto [his] property. This included Defendants’ front entry porch and mailbox.” (Trozak Decl. ¶ 5; Exhibit A.) Plaintiff attached a copy of the survey along with photographs showing the encroaching mailbox and front entry porch. (Id.) However, there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendants affirmatively acted to trespass; such action need not have been intentional, but trespass requires at least a negligent action or failure to act.

(3) Lack of Permission for the Entry or Acts in Excess of Permission

Plaintiff declares that he “never gave permission to Defendants to enter or encroach onto [his] property.” (Trozak Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff does not provide any other evidence. However, since Plaintiff makes the declaration under penalty of perjury, the Court finds that this declaration was sufficient. Therefore, this element was satisfied.

(4) Harm

Plaintiff declares that “based on the current market value, [his] home is worth approximately $5,650,000.00. The square footage of [his] home is 3,931 square feet. Approximately 42 square feet was affected which is a loss of use amounting to $72,105.74 in loss of use damages” and

continues to accrue. (Trozak Decl. ¶¶ 15-16.) However, Plaintiff failed to provide any evidence as to the costs of the home or how the 42 square feet resulted in $72,105.74 in loss of use damages. Therefore, Plaintiff failed to prove there are no triable issues of material facts as to the harm. Assuming this element was met, the Court completes the analysis below.

(5) The Defendant’s Conduct was a Substantial Factor in Causing the Harm

Plaintiff provides his declaration to show that Defendants’ conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm because Defendants “mercilessly harassed” the general contractor throughout the time he was doing construction at his property by “getting in his face, physically blocking his workers, and threatening his workers physically if they continued to work on constructing the fence that [he] hired MROCK Construction to construct. Due to this harassment, portions of the work has been left an incomplete state.” (Trozak Decl. ¶ 13.) As mentioned above, Plaintiff fails to provide any evidence of the alleged harassment rather than his own testimony.

Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that there are no triable issues of material facts, and the burden does not shift to Defendants.

Thus, the Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to the first cause of action for trespass.

b. Private Nuisance

To state a claim for private nuisance, a plaintiff must plead (1) that the defendant interfered with plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the plaintiff’s property; (2) that “the invasion of the plaintiff's interest in the use and enjoyment of the land was substantial, i.e., that it cause[d] the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference was unreasonable, i.e., it must be ‘of such a nature, duration or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” (Chase v. Wizman (2021) 71 Cal. App. 5th 244.)

In the complaint, Plaintiff asserts, “Since approximately May 2017, [Defendants] acted in a manner which was indecent, offensive, obstructed Plaintiff’s free use of the Property and interfered with his comfortable enjoyment of Property, and caused and continues to cause a hazard to the Property through Defendants’ continued intrusions upon the Property and preventing Plaintiff’s agents and contractors from replacing the fence upon rightful and proper boundaries.” (Complaint ¶ 29.) The complaint further alleges “Defendants’ conduct in performing these actions was intentional, unreasonable, reckless, and/or negligent, and substantially interfered and continues to interfere with Plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the Property, where an ordinary person would be annoyed or disturbed by Defendants’ conduct.” (Complaint ¶ 30.)

In support of his position, Plaintiff presents his own declaration. Plaintiff states “Defendants often stared into my yard and home in an intimidating manner. They took photographs of myself and my family while we were in our home where my wife and I have our home offices. This made us extremely uncomfortable as they could have been photographing work we were doing on our computers. These behaviors only stopped because we put up a partial fence. They also continued to photograph me from their balcony as I did regular things at my home like taking out my trash. They yelled profanities at me from their balcony. This was ongoing during the time they lived next door. Defendants would also glare at me through their rotting fence.” (Trozan Decl. ¶ 14.) Plaintiff

did not provide any evidence showing that Defendants engaged in this behavior or show how this behavior would be a private nuisance. The Court also notes that Plaintiff does not cite to any authority showing how this behavior is a private nuisance.

Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that there are no triable issues of material facts, and the burden does not shift to Defendants.

Thus, the Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to the second cause of action for private nuisance.

c. Interference with Contractual Relations

The elements of an action for tortious interference of contract are (1) a valid contract between plaintiff and a third party, (2) defendant's knowledge of this contract, (3) defendant's intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship, (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship, and (5) resulting damage. (Hahn v. Diaz-Barba, (2011) 194 Cal. App. 4th 1177, 1198.)

The complaint alleges, “On or around early 2021, Plaintiff formed and had a contract with his first general contract to perform the work to replace the deteriorated fence between property and Neighbor Property. Defendants knew of the existence of such contract based on the work the first general contractor began performing on the Property and their interaction with Defendants. Defendants’ conduct which included screaming, harassing, unlawfully calling the police, and threatening physical altercation with the first contractor, as well as physically blocking the construction, prevented the first general contractor from performing his contractual duties to Plaintiff. Due to Defendants’ actions, the first general contractor had to end his contractual relations with Plaintiff.” (Complaint ¶¶ 34-37.)

In support of his position, Plaintiff again presents his own declaration and a copy of his contract with MROCK Construction.

(1) A Valid Contract Between Plaintiff And A Third Party

Plaintiff submitted the copy of the contract between MROCK Construction and Plaintiff. (Trozak Decl. ¶ 7; Exhibit B.) Exhibit B shows that the parties David Trozak and Andrea Trozak entered into the contract with MROCK Construction on December 11, 2019. Therefore, Plaintiff satisfied this element.

(2) Defendant's Knowledge of this Contract

Plaintiff states that “[o]n or about April 2020, workers from MROCK Construction were subjected to physical intimidation, threats of physical abuse, and being physically blocked by Defendants which prevented MROCK Construction from doing the work I hired them to do. This work included removing portions of concrete that were trespassing onto Plaintiff’s property to perform construction of the property line fence and Defendants interfered physically to try to stop them.” (Trozak Decl. ¶ 8.) Therefore, Plaintiff argues that Defendants “clearly knew of the existence of the contract because they had numerous interactions with Plaintiff’s general contractor . . ..” (Mot. for Summ. Judgm. at p. 7.) The Court agrees that Defendants could make

an inference that a contract existed when a construction team was working on Plaintiff’s property. Therefore, this element is satisfied.

(3) Defendant's Intentional Acts Designed to Induce a Breach or Disruption of the Contractual Relationship

Plaintiff argues Defendants then intentionally interfered with the contract by engaging in repeated harassing behaviors for more than a year and eventually causing Plaintiff’s contractor to quit. (SSDF Nos. 11, 12; Trozak Decl. ¶¶ 8-13.) However, the Court notes that Plaintiff fails to provide any evidence showing that Defendants intentionally interfered besides his own declaration. Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that there are no triable issues of material facts, and the burden does not shift to Defendants.

(4) Actual Breach or Disruption of the Contractual Relationship

Plaintiff declares that “[d]ue to this harassment, portions of the work has been left an incomplete state. Before we can complete the work, the encroaching mailbox and porch need to be removed. To date, the request to remove the encroachment has been refused by Defendants.” (Trozak Decl. ¶ 13.) Again, Plaintiff failed to include any evidence showing that the refusal resulted in the disruption of the contractual relationship. Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that there are no triable issues of material facts, and the burden does not shift to Defendants.

(5) Resulting Damage

For the same reasons mentioned above, this element fails because of the lack of support as to damages.

Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that there are no triable issues of material facts, and the burden does not shift to Defendants.

Thus, the Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to the third cause of action for interference with contractual relations.

V. CONCLUSION

Therefore, the PLAINTIFF’S NOTICE OF MOTION AND MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT filed by David Trozak on 03/28/24 is DENIED.

RULING

The demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. The motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend. Plaintiff is instructed to file an amended complaint within 20 days of notice of this order.

BACKGROUND

On March 27, 2024, plaintiff Elana T. Mossazadeh (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc. (“Defendant”), alleging the following causes of action: (1) violation of subdivision (d) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (2) violation of subdivision (b) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (3) violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code § 1793.2; (4) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability; and (5) fraudulent inducement – concealment.

The complaint alleges that, on March 5, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a 2021 Honda Odyssey, bearing vehicle identification number 5FNRL6H84MB022473, which was manufactured and or distributed by Defendant. (Compl. ¶ 6.) A written warranty accompanied the sale of the subject

vehicle. (Id. at ¶ 7, Exh. A.) It is further alleged that the subject vehicle possessed a “sensing defect” that were covered by the warranty and substantially impaired the subject vehicle’s use, value and safety. (Id. at ¶¶ 11-12, 14.) Defendant is alleged to have concealed this “sensing defect” prior to Plaintiff’s purchase of the subject vehicle. (Id. at ¶ 64.)

Now, Defendants move to the strike portions of the complaint that support a claim for punitive damages, which consists of Paragraphs 31 and 38 as well as the prayer for punitive and exemplary damages. Plaintiff opposes.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

A. Demurrer

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966–967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face. (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.) “If substantial facts which constitute a cause of action are averred in the complaint or can be inferred by reasonable intendment from the matters which are pleaded, although the allegations of these facts are intermingled with conclusions of law, the complaint is not subject to demurrer for insufficiency.” (Krug v. Meeham (1952) 109 Cal.App.2d 274, 277.)

B. Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The

grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

DISCUSSION

As a preliminary matter, the Court finds that the Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 430.41 and 435.5. (Hernandez Decls. at ¶ 2.)

A. Whether the Fifth Cause of Action for Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment is Time-Barred

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 338(d), a fraud cause of action is “not deemed to have accrued until the discovery, by the aggrieved party, of the facts constituting the fraud or mistake.”

Defendant argues that the fifth cause of action is time-barred because the claim was not brought within the three-year statute of limitations under Civil Code § 338(d), and it reasons that the claim accrued at the date of purchase on March 5, 2021 because it is alleged that the subject vehicle was equipped with the “sensing defect” and Plaintiff would not have purchased the subject vehicle had she known about this defect. (Demurrer at pp. 10-11; Compl. ¶¶ 6, 64, 70-77.) The instant case was not filed until March 27, 2024. Defendant further contends that Plaintiff’s appeal to various forms of tolling are inadequate because sufficient allegations have not been alleged to support such relief.

Notably, Plaintiff’s opposition fails to address this issue. Thus, Plaintiff has effectively conceded this issue. (See Rudick v. State Bd. Of Optometry (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 77, 90.)

Even if Plaintiff had raised argument in opposition to this issue, the Court finds Defendant’s argument to be persuasive because sufficient facts have not been alleged regarding the discovery of the alleged fraud. To rely on the delayed discovery rule, a plaintiff “must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence,” and “conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 808.) The complaint conclusory alleges that the alleged concealment was not discovered until “shortly before filing this Complaint as the Subject Vehicle continued to exhibit symptoms of defects following Defendant's unsuccessful attempts to repair them.” (Compl. ¶¶ 23, 26-29.) However,

this inadequately states the time and manner of the discovery, and why Plaintiff was unable to make this discovery sooner. For instance, there are no allegations to suggest when Plaintiff brought the subject vehicle to repair the alleged “sensing defect” and for how many times.

Additionally, based on the allegations in the complaint, Plaintiff presumes that class action tolling under Am. Pipe & Const. Co. v. Utah, 414 U.S. 538 (1974) automatically applies. (Compl. ¶¶ 24-25.) In order for class action tolling to apply, it must be alleged that (1) the prior class action “sufficiently put defendants on notice of the substance and nature of plaintiff's claims” and (2) tolling would “serve to further economy and efficiency of litigation.” (Jolly v. Eli Lilly & Co. (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1130, 1122.) Upon review of the complaint, Plaintiff has failed to alleged a prior class action put Defendant on notice of the substance and nature of Plaintiff’s claims, and there are no allegations to suggest Plaintiff actively protected her rights or that she delayed in filing suit in reliance of either class action. (American Pipe, supra, 414 U.S. at pg. 561 (conc. opn. of Blackmun, J.) (reasoning that this form of tolling is not meant “to attract and save members of the purported class who have slept on their rights.”).)

Accordingly, demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained on this ground.

B. Whether the Fifth Cause of Action is Pleaded with Specificity to State a Claim of Fraud.

Defendant also argues that the fifth cause of action is subject to demurrer because it has not been pleaded with the requisite specificity. (Demurrer at pp. 4-8.) In particular, Defendant argues that the sufficient facts have not been alleged that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of the alleged defect or that the parties had a direct transaction to trigger a duty to disclose the alleged defect.

To state a claim for fraudulent inducement-concealment, a plaintiff must allege: (1) the defendant “concealed or suppressed a material fact,” (2) the defendant was “under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff,” (3) the defendant “intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff,” (4) the plaintiff was “unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact,” and (5) “as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310–11.)

Absent a fiduciary relationship between the parties, a duty to disclose can arise in only three circumstances: (1) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of the material fact; (2) the

defendant actively concealed the material fact; or (3) the defendant made partial representations while also suppressing the material fact. (Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at 311; LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) Such circumstances “presuppose[ ] the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise.” (LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337.) The California Supreme Court “has described the necessary relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose as a ‘transaction’ between the plaintiff and defendant ….” (Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at p. 311; Warner Construction Corp. v. City of Los Angeles (1970) 2 Cal.3d 285, 294 [“In transactions which do not involve fiduciary or confidential relations”]; Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1187–89 [rejecting concealment claim where plaintiffs “were not involved in a transaction with the parties they claim defrauded them”]; LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337 [“such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties”].)

Defendant contends that it did not have exclusive knowledge of the alleged defect because it is alleged that this alleged defect was publicly disclosed through the NHTSA and there was no direct transaction between the parties. (Demurrer at pg. 6.) In this regard, Defendant argues that sufficient facts have not been alleged to suggest that Defendant’s authorized dealerships are its agents for the purpose of selling cares. (Id. at pg. 7.) Moreover, Defendant asserts that the express warranty does not create a duty to disclose because it is not part of the sale contract. (Id. at pg. 6, relying on Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324 611, 619-620.)

In opposition, Plaintiff maintains that the fifth cause of action has been sufficiently alleged and reasons that a transaction relationship is not required under California law for the manufacturer to have a duty to disclose. Regardless, Plaintiff asserts that for pleading purposes, her allegations are sufficient to establish a transactional relationship. (Opposition at pg. 7, relying on Dhital v. Nissan North America (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828.

The plaintiffs in Dhital sued Nissan for Song-Beverly violations. They alleged the transmission was faulty and unreliable, and that it placed them at risk for an accident though they did not allege they had actually been injured. The pleading included a claim for fraudulent inducement. Plaintiffs alleged that Nissan concealed defects it had a duty to disclose in order to induce the purchase. The trial court sustained Nissan’s demurrer to the fraudulent inducement cause of action on the basis that it was barred by the economic loss doctrine. Within its decision reversing the lower court’s decision, the appellate court found that:

“[a]t the pleading stage (and in the absence of a more developed argument by Nissan on this point), we conclude Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient. Plaintiff alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan’s authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers. In light of these allegations, we decline to hold Plaintiff’s claim is barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring Nissan to disclose known defects.”

(Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at pp. 727-729.)

However, unlike the defendant in Dhital, Defendant here has set forth a satisfactory argument as to why it should not be summarily presumed that an agency relationship existed between Defendant and the dealership that sold the subject vehicle to Plaintiff. “[A] dealer or retailer may in a layman's view be an agent of the [manufacturer], but he is not an agent in the legal sense of that relationship.” (Avalon Painting Co. v. Alert Lumber Co. (1965) 234 Cal.App.2d 178, 184; see also Herremans v. BMW of N. Am., LLC, No. (C.D.Cal. 2014) 2014 WL 5017843, at *6 [“[t]he relationship between automobile manufacturers and their dealers has been examined by a host of courts throughout the country, all of which have agreed that dealers are not ‘agents’ of manufacturers.”].) Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege that Defendant’s authorized dealerships are its agents. Critically, “fraud must be pleaded specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Thus, without sufficiently alleging the existence of a transactional relationship, the complaint fails to sufficiently allege the existence of a duty.

Accordingly, because the complaint does not sufficiently allege a direct transaction between the parties, the fifth cause of action for fraudulent concealment has not been sufficiently alleged. Therefore, the demurrer is sustained on this ground as well. For the sake of judicial economy, the court declines to address Defendant’s remaining argument regarding the economic loss rule.

C. Motion to Strike

Because Defendant’s demurrer has been sustained as to the fifth cause of action, and the motion to strike attacks the punitive damages that would flow only from that cause of action, it is GRANTED.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is SUSTAINED and the motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend. Plaintiff is ordered to file any amended complaint within 20 days of notice of this order.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
