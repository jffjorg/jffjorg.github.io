DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: BC611486    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

GABRILE RUEDA, also known as GABRIEL SALVADOR (an individual),

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

EMMANUEL D. PACQUIAO (an individual); FREDERICK ROACH, (an individual); KEITH M. DAVIDSON (an individual); CBS CORPORATION (a Delaware corporation); SHOWTIME NETWORKS, Inc. (a Delaware corporation); and DOES 1-25,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          BC611486

 Hearing Date:   October 24, 2024

 Trial Date:        January 14, 2025

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s Ninth Cause of Action [RES ID # 5341]

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Joinder to Keith Davidson’s Motion for Summary Judgment [RES ID # 5282]

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Joinder to Defendants Showtime Networks Inc.’s and Paramount Global’s Renewed Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s First, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action [RES ID # 1045]

 

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Gabriel Rueda (Rueda) sues Defendants Emmanuel Pacquiao (Pacquiao), Frederick (Freddie) Roach, Keith M. Davidson, Paramount Global (f/k/a ViacomCBS, f/k/a CBS Corporation), Showtime Networks, Inc. (Showtime), and Does 1-25 pursuant to a February 2, 2024, Third Amended Complaint (TAC). The TAC alleges claims of: (1) Breach of Oral Contract against the Showtime Defendants and Freddie Roach; (2) Omitted [Breach of Implied Contract; Dismissed by the Court]; (3) Common Counts for Labor, Work, and Services against the Showtime Defendants and Freddie Roach; (4) Quantum Meruit against the Showtime Defendants and Freddie Roach; (5) Fraud in the Inducement against Freddie Roach; (6) Fraudulent Misrepresentation against Freddie Roach; (7) Omitted [Unjust Enrichment; Dismissed by the Court]; (8) Omitted [Attempted Extortion; Dismissed by the Court]; and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress against the Showtime Defendants, Freddie Roach, Defendant Pacquiao, and Defendant Davidson.

The claims arise from allegations that Rueda, pursuant to an unpaid finder’s fee, introduced Leslie Moonves, the then-president of CBS Corporation and Showtime Networks, to Defendant Roach, who was the trainer and confidant of boxer Pacquiao. Moonves allegedly used the introduction to broker a bout between Pacquiao and boxer Floyd Mayweather Jr.—dubbed the “Fight of the Century”—that earned Pacquiao, Mayweather, and Showtime Networks more than $430 million. According to Rueda, he is entitled to 2% of the gross proceeds from the fight received by Roach, Pacquiao, and Paramount and Showtime. When Rueda approached Moonves, Roach and Pacquiao for payment after the fight, Defendants directly or indirectly sought to intimidate Rueda to minimize or eliminate the money to be paid to Rueda.

B. Motion Before the Court

On July 9, 2024, Defendant Keith Davidson filed a motion for summary judgment (MSJ) to the TAC’s ninth cause of action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED).

C.    Legal Standard

A motion for summary adjudication (MSA) is nearly identical to a motion for summary judgment except that instead of disposing of the entire case, a summary adjudication disposes of only select causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) All matters that are not resolved by summary adjudication will be resolved at trial. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (n)(1).) 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) To meet this burden, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

D.    Analysis

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress [IIED] exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051].) 

 Roach argues that the IIED claim fails because Roach did not, directly or indirectly, engage in conduct that constitutes actionable IIED.

1.     Direct Liability for IIED

The only facts alleged about Roach’s direct liability for IIED pertain to an interaction at Craig’s restaurant. Rueda alleges that in December 2025, Roach, Liam Vaughan, Richard Palmer and Joseph Abinanti had dinner at Craig’s, where Rueda worked as a waiter. (UMF 62-63; PUMF 83.) Rueda alleges that during this dinner Roach and the others pointed at and taunted Rueda. (UMF 57.) He alleges that Roach called him a “motherfucker” and that in Roach’s presence, Abinanti grabbed Rueda’s wrist and threw it down on the table. (UMF 57, 107; PUMF 86-87.) This instance of insults and threats alone does not amount to the kind of ‘extreme and outrageous conduct’ necessary for an IIED cause of action. (see Hughes, supra, 46 Cal.4th at 1050-1051.) Thus, Rueda has not shown that a triable issue of material fact exists as to Roach’s direct liability for IIED.    

2.     Vicarious Liability for IIED

a.      Conspiracy

“[T]he basis of a civil conspiracy is the formation of a group of two or more persons who have agreed to a common plan or design to commit a tortious act. The conspiring defendants must also have actual knowledge that a tort is planned and concur in the tortious scheme with knowledge of its unlawful purpose.” (Kidron v. Movie Acquisition Corp. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1571, 1582) (cleaned up). “Knowledge of the planned tort must be combined with intent to aid in its commission.” (Ibid.)

However, such knowledge and intent “may be inferred from the nature of the acts done, the relation of the parties, the interests of the alleged conspirators, and other circumstances.” (Wyatt v. Union Mortgage Co. (1979) 24 Cal.3d 773, 785.)

Roach argues that the facts as alleged are not sufficient to show a conspiracy relationship because there are no facts establishing that Roach has ever communicated with any other person about harming or threatening Rueda or has any knowledge of overt acts to harm Rueda. (UMF 49-50, 54-55, 77-78.) Rueda argues that there is sufficient circumstantial evidence for one to infer a conspiracy relationship based on the relationship between Roach and the other alleged co-conspirators including Nick Khan, Keith Davidson, and Leslie Moonves, as well as Roach’s alleged connections to the individuals who threatened and harassed Rueda. (PUMF 76, 80, 82-87.)

Here, there is a genuine issue of material fact as to whether Roach conspired with other individuals to harass and intimidate Rueda out of pursuing his finder’s fee. Rueda provides evidence that he arranged a meeting between Roach and Moonves to coordinate the fight, and that Roach’s talent agent, Nick Khan, coordinated to hire Davidson. (PUMF 15, 30-33.) Rueda further alleges that after he met with Davidson, Roach and other individuals showed up to Rueda’s workplace and threatened and taunted him. (PUMF 79-80.) Rueda also alleges that he was stalked by unidentified individuals that he recognized as members of Roach’s Wildcard Gym. (PUMF 77.)

It is true that “[m]ere association does not make a conspiracy” (Kidron v. Movie Acquisition Corp., (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 1571, 1582), It is also true that a temporal sequence of events alone does not prove causation. (Franklin v. Dynamic Details, Inc. (2004) 116 Cal.Ap.4th 375, 393-394.) However, Rueda has alleged close relationships amongst the named parties with a direct connection between them to the allegedly tortious conduct. Considering the evidence in the light most favorable to the Plaintiff, as the Court must, a reasonable finder of fact could infer a conspiracy. Roach’s close business and personal relationships with the alleged co-conspirators and tortfeasors, plus Roach’s direct involvement in the incident at Craig’s restaurant, coupled with the temporal proximity of events, creates at least a triable issue of fact whether Roach was aware of or aided in a plan to inflict emotional distress upon Rueda. (Novartis Vaccines & Diagnostics, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1284, 1300 [“[A] finder of fact may infer the requisite concurrence and knowledge from the nature of the acts done, the parties’ relations to each other, and the common interest of the alleged conspirators. ‘It is not necessary that the plaintiffs provide evidence showing that the defendants met and actually agreed to undertake the performance of the unlawful act.’”] See also Balistreri v. Turner (1964) 227 Cal.App.2d 236, 241-242 [“Proof of a combination linking two or more defendants may . . . be inferred from indirect and circumstantial evidence, such as the nature of the acts done, the relationship of the parties, their interest and other circumstances.”]; Wyatt v. Union Mortgage Co. (1979) 34 Cal.3d 773, 785.)

Roach argues that the Court should adopt its reasoning from Pacquiao’s motion for summary adjudication, however the events as they relate to Pacquiao are distinguishable. There was no evidence that Pacquiao knew of specific tortious conduct against Rueda, while here Rueda alleges that Roach witnessed and participated in the incident at Craig’s. Additionally, there was no evidence that Pacquiao had any recent communication with the alleged co-conspirators, while here Roach was actively represented by Nick Khan during the relevant period and Roach acknowledges that Davidson acted as his lawyer.

Thus, Rueda has met his burden to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to establishing a cause of action against Roach for IIED based on conspiracy. Accordingly, there is a genuine issue of material fact regarding Roach’s liability for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

b.     Agency

Because the Court has found that a genuine issue of material fact exists as to Roach’s liability for IIED based on a conspiracy theory, the Court need not reach of the issue of whether there is also a genuine issue of material fact under an agency theory.

D.    Analysis

Because Roach’s Joinder raises identical arguments as to those raised in Davidson’s motion for summary judgment, (compare Davidson MSJ, pp. 12-19, with Roach Joinder, p. 2), the Court adopts its reasoning to find that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that Davidson’s conduct is outrageous, that for purposes of this ruling the June 29, 2015 meeting between Davidson and Rueda is protected by the litigation privilege, and that there is a genuine issue of material fact as to whether in that phone call Davidson conspired with the other named individuals to harass and intimidate Rueda from following up on his request for a finder’s fee. (See 9/30/2024 Minute Order.)

IV. Joinder to Showtime Defendants Renewed Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s First, Third and Fourth Causes of Action

A.    Evidentiary Objections

In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court need rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (q).) 

Rueda Evidentiary Objections to Motion:

Not ruled on as not material to disposition of the motion. 

Roach Evidentiary Objections to Opposition:  

Not ruled on as not material to disposition of the motion. 

B.    Legal Standard

See above.

C.    Analysis

Roach joins the Showtime Defendant’s MSA with respect to Issue 2 – no meeting of the minds. No binding agreement is formed when the parties to the agreement fail to “reach a meeting of the minds on all material points.” Cheema v. L.S. Trucking, Inc., (2019) 39 Cal. App. 5th 1142, 1149 (quoting Banner Ent., Inc. v. Super. Ct., (1998) 62 Cal. App. 4th 348, 359).

 In their MSA, the Showtime Defendants argue that the February 27, 2015 Email and Rueda and Marshall’s June 2024 testimony about the email establish that there was no meeting of the minds on the alleged May 2014 oral contract. (Showtime MSA, pp. 14-17.) Specifically, the Showtime Defendants argue that there was no meeting of the minds on the following material terms: (1) whether Rueda was entitled to a finder’s fee on rematches and (2) how many tickets Rueda would receive to the fight. (Showtime MSA, pp. 15-17.) The Showtime Defendants argue that Rueda has testified that compensation for rematches and the number of tickets for the fight were material terms of the agreement, and further testifies that the parties did not reach an agreement on either of these terms. (SUF 6, 8, 11, 92, 93.)

In opposition to the Joinder, Rueda argues that the oral contract between Rueda and Roach was entirely separate from the oral contract between Rueda and CBS. (Opp. to Joinder, p. 6.) He asserts that “[t]he oral contract between Rueda and Roach was for a 2% finder’s fee only (with respect to Roach’s and Pacquiao’s gross proceeds), and did not include any agreement (or discussion) regarding tickets or compensation for future bouts.” (Opp. to Joinder, p. 6:20-22.) Rueda further argues that the February 27, 2015 Email relates only to Rueda’s efforts to get an advance on his finder’s fee contract with the Showtime Defendants and thus is unrelated to his finder’s fee contract with Roach. (Opp. to Joinder, pp. 12:25-13:2.)

The TAC makes no clear reference to either an “advance” or to Rueda’s contention that he is owed two separate finders fees, one pursuant to an oral contract with Roach and another pursuant to an oral contract with the Showtime Defendants. Roach did not file a reply addressing Rueda’s argument about the separate contracts. Rueda has presented evidence that the February 2015 communications only pertained to an advance, not the finder’s fee itself, and that regardless, his separate contract with Roach did not contemplate future fights or tickets. Accordingly, because the Court must view the evidence in the light most favorable to the Plaintiff, Rueda has raised a triable issue of material fact regarding whether there was a meeting of the minds on the finder’s fee agreement.

V. Conclusion

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s Ninth Cause of Action is DENIED.

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Joinder to Keith Davidson’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

Defendant Freddie Roach’s Joinder to Defendants Showtime Networks Inc.’s and Paramount Global’s Renewed Motion for Summary Adjudication of Plaintiff’s First, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action is DENIED.
