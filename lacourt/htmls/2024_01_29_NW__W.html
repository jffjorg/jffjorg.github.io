DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01586    Hearing Date: January 29, 2024    Dept: W

JOHN MORRIS, ET AL. V. MICHAEL HRICAK ARCHITECTS

 

DEFENDANT JOMAR CONSTRUCTION, INC.’S MOTION FOR GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 29, 2024                   Trial Date:       March 25, 2024 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        19VECV01586

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Jomar Construction, Inc.

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs allege they entered into a contract with Defendant Michael Hricak Architects (“Hricak”) with the purpose of remodeling their existing residence (the “subject property”). Plaintiffs allege they hired Hricak in 2015 as their architect. However, it has taken four and a half years and the remodel has not been completed nor have they received approved plans from Hricak or a building permit. Plaintiffs further allege Defendant Hricak intentionally misled Plaintiffs about the status of the project. To date, Plaintiffs allege they have already paid Defendant at least $326,040.00.

 

Plaintiffs also allege they entered into an agreement with Defendant Jomar Construction, Inc. (“Jomar”) for Jomar to provide general contractor services for the remodeling of the subject property. However, Plaintiffs allege Defendant Jomar breached the agreement and its fiduciary duties by failing to obtain the permits and demolish the subject property without Plaintiffs’ knowledge or approval.

 

On September 11, 2020, Plaintiffs filed a SAC against Defendants Michael Hricak Architects and Jomar Construction, Inc. asserting causes of action for: (1) Intentional Misrepresentation; (2) Constructive Fraud (Civil Code §1573); (3) Fraudulent Concealment; (4) Professional Negligence; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (6) Breach of Contract.

 

Plaintiffs named Michael Hricak as Defendant Doe 1.  

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendant Jomar Construction, Inc.’s Motion for Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Jomar Construction Inc. moves this court for an order finding its settlement with Plaintiffs John Morris and Cindy Peterson to be one entered into in good faith. Plaintiff filed a notice of joinder on January 12, 2024.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6(a)(1) provides that a “party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors…” Under subdivision (a)(2), a settling party may give notice of settlement to the parties and court, together with an application for determination of good faith settlement and a proposed order. The application “shall indicate the settling parties, and the basis, terms, and amount of the settlement” and must include a proof of service. (CCP §877.6(a)(2).)

 

In determining the good faith of a settlement, the court may consider the affidavits served with the notice of hearing, and any counter-affidavits filed in response, or other evidence in the court’s discretion. (CCP §877.6(b).) The California Supreme Court, in Tech-Bilt Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, provided factors to be considered in determining the good faith of a particular settlement. The court explained that the policies and intent underlying section 877.6 “require that a number of factors be taken into account,” such as: a rough approximation of a plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability; the amount paid in settlement; the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs; a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. (Tech-Bilt at p. 499.) Other relevant factors include financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, and the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of non-settling defendants. (Id.). This evaluation must be made “on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.” (Id.) The court further explained that settlement by a defendant “must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability to be.” (Id., citations omitted.)

 

If a motion is unopposed, a “barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient.” (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.)

 

Defendant has set forth the grounds of good faith and the background of the case. Around March 6, 2016, Jomar was approached by Co-Defendant Michael Hricak of Hricak Architects to assist in completing a project of Mr. Hricak's, consisting of a contract he had with Plaintiffs to completely rebuild their home. (Miller Decl. ¶3.) In approximately mid-May of 2019, Plaintiffs reached out to Jomar to get the selective demolition done on the residence and on May 29, 2019, Plaintiffs entered into a direct contract with Jomar to perform the demolition on the residence. (Miller Decl. ¶5.) According to Jomar, the agreement provided that Jomar would have no responsibility to obtain permits. (Miller Decl. ¶5.) It also provided that the residence would be taken down to the ground floor except for one wall so that the project could be considered a remodel rather than a full rebuild. (Miller Decl. ¶9.) Both parties signed the agreement. (Miller Decl. ¶¶10, 11.) Jomar completed the work under the contract with no incident or problems and Plaintiffs paid for the work in full. (Miller Decl. ¶¶12, 13.) To Defendant Jomar’s surprise, Defendant Jomar was served with the First Amended Complaint in January 2020. (Miller Decl. ¶16.)     During litigation, the court adjudicated several causes of action in Defendant’s favor except the sole claim for negligence with respect to the permit issue. (Miller Decl. 17, Exh. A.)

 

Counsel for Defendants attest there have been extensive settlement discussions with all of the parties in this case. (Gamboa Decl. ¶2.) There have also been several motions for summary judgment and summary adjudication. (Gamboa Decl. ¶2.) As noted above, Jomar’s motions have resulted in the causes of action for Intentional Misrepresentation, Fraudulent Concealment and Professional Negligence be adjudicated in Jomar’s favor. (Gamboa Decl. ¶2, Exh. A.) The only cause of action alleged in the complaint that remains against Jomar is a negligence cause of action over a permit issue. (Gamboa Decl. ¶3.) However, there is no insurance coverage available to Jomar as Jomar tendered the claims alleged in the complaint twice and coverage was twice denied. (Gamboa Decl. ¶3.) Jomar attaches the settlement as Exhibit B. Jomar has settled with the Plaintiffs for a full dismissal, release, and the payment of $30,000. (Gamboa Decl., Exh. B.)

 

No party has opposed the instant motion[1]. As a result, Defendant Jomar herein does not have the burden to produce such evidence in support of a good faith determination. The court notes, however, Defendant Jomar’s motion satisfies the Tech-Bilt factors. Based on the record presented and the lack of any objection, the court GRANTS the application, finding this settlement was made in good faith. All claims against Defendant Jomar for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault, are barred pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6(c).

 




Case Number: 23VECV04267    Hearing Date: January 29, 2024    Dept: W

MARSHALL R. COLE vs KAMRAN FATTAHI, et al.

 

DEFENDANTs’ demurrer to plaintiff’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        January 29, 2024                               Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04267

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Kamran Fattahi, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust and Kathy Hosseini, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Marshall R. Cole, as Trustee of the Marshall and Jennifer Cole Trust 2020 Revocable Trust

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Brenner Decl. ¶¶3-5.)  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 27, 2023, Plaintiff Marshall R. Cole, as Trustee of the Marshall and Jennifer Cole Trust 2020 Revocable Trust filed a complaint against Defendants Kamran Fattahi, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust and Kathy Hosseini, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust for (1) Negligence; (2) Trespass; (3) Private Nuisance; and (4) Wrongful Discharge of Water.

 

Defendants’ Property is located uphill of Plaintiff’s Property and situated between Plaintiff’s Property and Defendants’ Property is unimproved natural ground that slopes downward towards a retaining wall abutting Plaintiff’s backyard. Plaintiff alleges sometime between the evening of January 9, 2023 and the morning of January 10, 2023, a large landslide originating on Defendants’ Property occurred which traveled downward onto Plaintiff’s Property. Plaintiff alleges Defendants failed to maintain their property and as a result of the landslide, an immense amount of dirt, rock, trees, and other debris rushed downhill and came onto Plaintiff’s Property, completely destroying a major portion Plaintiff’s backyard and continuing to the front of the house causing significant damage.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Kamran Fattahi, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust and Kathy Hosseini, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Kamran Fattahi, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust and Kathy Hosseini, individually and as Trustee of the Fattahi Family Trust demur to the complaint of Plaintiff Marshall R. Cole, as Trustee of the Marshall and Jennifer Cole Trust 2020 Revocable Trust on the grounds that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for trespass, third cause of action for nuisance and fourth cause of action for negligent discharge of water fail to allege sufficient facts to constitute said causes of action pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e).

 

Negligence and Trespass

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for negligence and second cause of action for trespass on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state facts against Defendant sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

To plead a cause of action for negligence, one must allege (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.) “In order to state a cause of action for negligence, the complaint must allege facts sufficient to show a legal duty on the part of the defendant to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 614, 619.)  In California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) 

 

“The elements of a common law trespass are (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant’s intentional, reckless, or negligent entry on the property; (3) lack of permission to enter the property, or acts in excess of the permission; (4) actual harm; and (5) the defendant’s conduct as a substantial factor in causing the harm.” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. United Food and Commercial Workers Union Local 8 (2010) 113 Cal.Rptr.3d 88, 93, reversed on other grounds by 55 Cal.4th 1083 (2012).)  

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s conclusory assertions, without factual support, that Plaintiff acted reasonably to avoid and minimize any damage to Plaintiff’s Property and/or that Defendants failed to properly maintain their own property are uncertain. Moreover, Plaintiff’s allegations are speculative as the allegations themselves establish Plaintiff’s lack of any personal knowledge as to how the claimed event occurred and the complaint reflects that no less than three properties abut Plaintiff’s Property, each with the unimproved natural ground that slopes downward towards Plaintiff’s Property. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues both causes of action are sufficiently pled. Plaintiff notes Plaintiff does not have a duty to prove its negligence claim in its initial pleading nor does Plaintiff have to satisfy a heightened pleading as is required when pleading causes of action for fraud - Plaintiff merely needs to apprise Defendants of the nature of the facts supporting the claim. Moreover, Defendants' application of trespass law is misguided. A "[t]respass may be 'by personal intrusion of the wrongdoer or by his failure to leave; by throwing or placing something on the [and; or by causing the entry of some other person.' A trespass may be on the surface of the land, above it, or below it. The migration of pollutants from one property to another may constitute a trespass, a nuisance, or both." (Martin Marietta Corp. v. Insurance Co. of North America (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1132.)

 

The court agrees Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the first and second causes of action for negligence and trespass. As noted above, in California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros, supra, 17 Cal.3d at pp. 407-408.) As such, Plaintiff need not allege the exact time or day the landslide occurred or what Defendants could have done to avoid or minimize the damage to Plaintiff’s property. On demurrer, the court looks at the pleading alone, and not on the evidence or facts alleged. (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) Here, the complaint sufficiently alleges the landslide and debris originated from Defendant’s property and Defendants failed to properly maintain their property in order to avoid or minimize the damage done. Moreover, Plaintiff need not allege Defendants personally entered Plaintiff’s property or personally caused another person to enter Plaintiff’s property. As noted by Plaintiff, “[t]migration of pollutants from one property to another may constitute a trespass, a nuisance, or both.” (Martin Marietta Corp. v. Insurance Co. of North America (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1132.)

 

Nuisance

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for nuisance on the grounds it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

“The essence of a private nuisance is an interference with the use and enjoyment of land.” (Friends of H Street v. City of Sacramento (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 152, 160.) Thus to allege a cause of action for private nuisance, the plaintiff must allege injury specific to the use and enjoyment of his land. (See Adams v. MHC Colony Park Limited Partnership (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 601, 610.) 

 

Defendant argues the complaint alleges a claim for nuisance based on the same allegations as Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligence and trespass. As such, the complaint contains no allegations as to “conduct” on the part of Defendants. Moreover, Courts have made clear that “liability for nuisance does not hinge on whether the defendant owns, possesses or controls the property, nor on whether he is in a position to abate the nuisance; the critical question is whether the defendant created or assisted in the creation of the nuisance.” (City of Modesto Redevelopment Agency v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 28, 38.)

 

As noted above, the court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged Defendant’s failed to maintain their property and as a result, dirt, rock, trees, and other debris rushed downhill and came onto Plaintiff’s Property during a landslide. The complaint further alleges their "use and enjoyment" of its property was interfered with because Defendant affected Plaintiff’s quiet enjoyment of the property and caused Plaintiffs to be displaced from their home as repairs occurred to their backyard. This is sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Wrongful Discharge of Drainage Water

 

Defendant demurs to the fourth cause of action for wrongful discharge of drainage water on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

“Under our civil law rule the owner of an upper, or dominant, estate is entitled to discharge surface water from his land as the water naturally flows. As a corollary to this, the upper owner is liable for any damage he causes to adjacent property by the discharge of water in¿an unnatural manner. In essence, each property owner's duty is to leave the natural flow of surface water undisturbed.”  (Keys v. Romley¿(1966) 64 Cal.2d 396, 405–406.)

 

Defendant argues the complaint proffers no allegations as to any conduct on the part of Defendants in using or altering Defendants’ Property in a manner which affected/affects the discharge of surface waters onto Plaintiff’s Property, or at all. Moreover, the complaint provides no facts showing unreasonableness in the use of Defendants’ property. Defendant contends under the modern rule governing landowner liability for surface water runoff and drainage, it is no longer simply a rule of property law dependent upon the existence of rights, servitudes, or easements.  Rather, today a landowner’s conduct in using or altering the property in a manner which affects the discharge of surface waters onto adjacent property is subject to a test of reasonableness.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues they have s pled sufficient facts to allege a cause of action for wrongful discharge of drainage water. Plaintiff alleges facts demonstrating that Defendant's property is adjacent to and situated uphill of Plaintiffs property. Moreover, the photographs attached to the complaint show the flow of surface water from Defendants' property to Plaintifls property and the resulting damage.

 

The court agrees Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a claim for wrongful discharge of water for the purposes of a demurrer. Again, Plaintiff has alleged Defendant failed to properly maintain Defendant’s own property which caused and contributed to the unsafe land movement that is the subject of this action. Reading the complaint as a whole, Plaintiff has alleged Defendant’s failure to maintain their property did not leave the natural flow of surface water undisturbed..

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV04358    Hearing Date: January 29, 2024    Dept: W

ELLEN A. SENTIMORE v. SHREE RAM KRISHNA HARI, LLC., et al.

 

Motion TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF plaintiff’s COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 29, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        23VECV04358

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Ellen A. Sentimore

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case involves a claim of bed bug infestation in a hotel. On October 2, 2023, Plaintiff Ellen A. Sentimore filed a complaint against B&M Capital Group, LLC and Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC for (1) Negligence - Premises Liability/Failure to Warn/ Negligence Per Se; (2) Nuisance; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (4) Breach of Contract; and (5) Fraudulent Concealment. Plaintiff seeks damages from Defendants for their alleged wanton failure to properly maintain, control or repair Plaintiff’s rented hotel room number 501.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC moves this court for an order striking portions of Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds the complaint fails to plead specific facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages and not based on ultimate fact.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.)

 

To sufficiently plead a claim for punitive damages pursuant to Civil Code §3294, a plaintiff must satisfy circumstances of “malice, oppression, or fraud,” supported by facts alleged with sufficient particularity. (G.D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29.) These allegations are held to a heightened pleading standard: a plaintiff may not state a mere conclusion of law to support a cause of action. (Perkins v. Sup. Ct. (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6.) More importantly, the plaintiff may not simply "plead . . . a claim for damages in the language authorizing such damages." (Id.) While some conclusory statements may be permitted, they must make sense in the context of the Complaint taken as a whole. (Id.)

 

“An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages ..., based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct.... With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, [or] ratification ... must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civil Code § 3294.) The California Supreme Court interpreted the “latter statement as requiring the officer, director, or managing agent to be someone who ‘exercise[s] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.’” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 577.) 

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff does not allege, nor can she allege, that Defendant intended to injure her. Rather, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant failed to repair and maintain the premises in a reasonably safe condition and failed to properly maintain pest control. While the circumstances alleged in the Complaint may arise to the level of negligence, mere negligence, and even gross negligence, is not a sufficient basis to justify an award of punitive damages. Defendant further argues Plaintiff has done nothing more than set forth blanket conclusions without supporting facts which give rise to malice, oppression or fraud. Moreover, Plaintiff has not named any managing agent of Defendant who engaged in malice, fraud or oppression, nor are there any facts demonstrating advance knowledge of the unfitness of an employee of Defendant, with authorization or ratification by a managing agent.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues she has sufficiently made clear Defendant’s intentionality, malice, and oppressive behavior. Plaintiff contends the basis for her prayer for punitive damages is the Defendants’ relationship with Plaintiff (innkeeper and guests), Defendants’ failure to notify Plaintiff of the bedbugs despite knowing about them, Defendants’ failure to act in a timely and effective manner once Plaintiff notified Defendants of the bedbugs, Defendants’ failure to hire effective pest control, their failure to abate the infestation, and their collection of rent monies despite all this.  Plaintiff further argues the complaint sufficiently alleges the concealment of the bed bugs was by an officer or managing agent of Defendants or Defendants authorized, ratified, or approved the conduct of these officers or managing agents of the Defendants and has named an individual alleged to be a managing agent or otherwise authorized by the management, known to her as “Zagar.”

 

The court finds Plaintiff has alleged more than mere negligence. The complaint alleges Defendant knew or had constructive notice of the infestations as numerous prior hotel guests have written reviews about subject property and hotel rooms contaminated with bed bugs infestation over years. (Compl. ¶31.) Defendant failed to disclose the material facts or warn Plaintiff of the presence of these filthy infestations at the subject property and hotel room. (Compl. ¶31.) Plaintiff complained directly to Defendant’s manager, employee and/or agent, Zagar about the uninhabitable conditions at the hotel room and despite this, knowingly rented out the bug infested room.  (Compl. ¶¶28, 50.) This conduct could certainly be found to be so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.  However, the court agrees Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged Zagar was an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation or that an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. The complaint generally alleges he was a manager, employee, or agent.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.
