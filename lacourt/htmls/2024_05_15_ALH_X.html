DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22GDCV00252    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: X

   Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

 

 

HEARING DATE:      May 15, 2024                                      TRIAL DATE: May 6, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         GC-8, LLC, a California limited liability company; SAN CLEMENTE BEACH COUNTRY CLUB, a California corporation; SHORECLIFFS GOLF MANAGEMENT, INC, a California corporation; SEAN LEONG, an individual; and ALLSION LEE, an individual; v, ROBERT D. HEINRICH, CPA, an individual; ROBERT D. HEINRICH, C.P.A., INC., a California corporation, MMAP SERVICES, INC., a California corporation; and DOES 1-30, inclusive.  

 

CASE NO.:                 22GDCV00252

 

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Robert Heinrich

 

RESPONDING PARTY:      Plaintiffs GC-8, LLC and San Clemente Beach Country Club

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

 

            Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiffs GC-8, LLC and San Clemente Beach Country Club to provide further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Request for Admission, Form Interrogatories, and Special Interrogatories, Sets One, and for monetary sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND

 

             This case arises out of Plaintiffs claim that Defendant Robert Heinrich engaged in professional negligence related to accounting and bookkeeping services. Plaintiffs filed a third amended complaint on September 29, 2023, alleging four causes of action for (1) professional negligence, (2) negligent supervision, (3) common counts, and (4) violations of Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Defendant’s motions to compel further responses to special interrogatories, set one, is DENIED as to San Clemente Beach Country Club and GRANTED as to GC-8 LLC.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents, set one, is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to request for admissions, set one, is DENIED.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories, set one, is GRANTED.

 

            Sanctions are denied.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, subdivision (a), Section 2031.310, and 2033.290 subdivision (a), parties may move for a further response to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, or requests for admission where an answer to the requests are evasive or incomplete or where an objection is without merit or too general. A motion to compel further response to requests for production has an additional requirement to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(1).) 

 

Notice of motions to compel further responses to discovery must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c), 2031.310, subd. (c), 2033.290, subd. (c).)  The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b), 2031.310, subd. (b), 2033.290, subd. (b).) 

 

Finally, Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).

 

Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (h), 2030.300, subd. (d); 2033.290, subd. (d).) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiffs GC-8, LLC (“GC-8”) and San Clemente Beach Country Club (“Country Club”) (“Plaintiffs”) to provide further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Request for Admission, Form Interrogatories, and Special Interrogatories, Sets One, and for monetary sanctions.

 

Timeliness

 

            It appears the last supplemental responses to requests for admission, form interrogatories, and request for production were served on February 19, 2024, and to special interrogatories on February 22, 2024. Defendant agreed to extend the deadline for Plaintiffs to file motions to compel to April 30, 2024. (Wu Decls., ¶23, Exh. O.) As such the motions are timely.

 

Meet and Confer

 

Defendant has shown that the parties have extensively met and conferred regarding the instant discovery requests. While Plaintiffs argue that Defendant would not meet and confer in person, this is not required and Defendant attempted to meet and confer by other means multiple times. It does not appear that the parties are able to resolve this matter informally and the motions are opposed on the merits.

 

 

Service of Motion

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendant did not provide sufficient notice of the motions based on CCP §§ 1105 and 1010.6 based on the initial May 14, 2024 hearing date. The motions were served by email on April 19, 2024.

 

CCP § 1005(b) provides “all moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing. … However, if the notice is served by mail, the required 16-day period of notice before the hearing shall be increased by five calendar … if the notice is served by facsimile transmission, express mail, or another method of delivery providing for overnight delivery, the required 16-day period of notice before the hearing shall be increased by two calendar days.” 

 

CCP § 1010.6(a)(3)(B) Provides “Any period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days.”¿ 

 

Defendant argues that email service is a method of overnight delivery under CCP § 1005(b), and thus he only needed to serve the motions 16 court days, plus 2 calendar days, prior to the hearing date.

 

Cole v. Superior Court (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 84, 87 is instructive. In Cole, the appellate court faced the same issue in relation to a summary judgment motion and, since CCP Section 437c does not expressly state any extension of the notice period for electronic service, it looked to CCP Section 1010.6, which addresses service by electronic transmission, to determine if any extension applies to such service. CCP § 1010.6(a)(3)(B) provides for a two-day notice extension for electronic service. Here, too, Section 1005(b) does not expressly state an extended notice period, so we look to Section 1010.6. Accordingly, Plaintiffs needed to be served the motions 18 court days before the hearing.

 

The hearing date is now May 15, 2024, and 18 court days prior to this date is April 19, 2022, when Defendant served notice. Plaintiffs argue that the continuing of the May 14 hearing date to May 15 does not remedy this but does not provide any authority for such. In any event, Plaintiffs have fully opposed the motions, and it does not appear that a further continuance would provide Plaintiffs anything more as they have already opposed. Thus, there does not appear to be any denial of due process rights.

 

A court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP § 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected. (CRC 3.1300(d); CCP § 475.) The appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on the merits is a waiver of any procedural defects or irregularities in a motion. (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.)

 

Accordingly, the Court turns to the merits of the motions.

 

Special Interrogatories:

 

            As an initial matter, Plaintiff Country Club objected to Defendant’s separate statement on the basis that it does not reference the requests propounded upon it or its responses. The Court observes that only the discovery as to GC-8 is reflected in the separate statement. The requests are not identical and identify the party it is directed towards. Accordingly, the motion is DENIED as to Country Club for failure to provide a separate statement as to it.

 

            As to GC-8, the Court agrees with Defendant that Plaintiff’s responses are not straightforward. “Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (CCP § 2030.220(a).) Plaintiff provides the same 7-page narrative to each of the 35 interrogatories and does not specifically answer each of the requests. Further, Plaintiff has only glossed over its objections in opposition, and the Court finds that the objections are without merit.

 

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to GC-8.

 

Request for Admissions

           

Plaintiffs’ responses consist of objections and statements of “admit” or “deny.”

 

Plaintiffs’ opposition focuses on procedural arguments with little to no merit rather than justifying their objections. As to the separate statement, while only one is filed, Defendant represents that they are identical in request and response. The Court cannot tell which Plaintiff they are directed towards and observes that they in fact appear identical. Thus, there is no need to file an identical separate statement.

 

Although Plaintiffs do not justify their objections, Plaintiffs have also responded to the subject requests notwithstanding the objections. It is unclear what more Defendant is seeking from the request for admissions that state “deny.” Defendant references Form Interrogatory No. 17 and Plaintiffs’ lack of details and documents in response to it. While the interrogatory is related, it is not part of this discovery set, and Defenant’s request for admissions only seek admission or denial, which Plaintiffs have provided. 

 

Based on the foregoing, the motion is DENIED.

 

Requests for Production

 

            Plaintiffs’ responses to the requests at issue consist of objections and statements of compliance. Defendant argues that Plaintiffs have yet to comply with production.

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiffs have failed to justify their objections in opposition, and instead focus on meritless procedural arguments again. Further, Plaintiffs are ordered to comply with their statements of compliance within 30 days of this order. The motion is GRANTED.

 

Form Interrogatories 

 

In the responses at issue, Plaintiffs object to the term “incident” and provide substantive responses notwithstanding said objection. Plaintiffs also object to some interrogatories on the basis that they are only for personal injury cases, but there is no support for this objection. The Court likewise does not find merit in the objection to the term “incident,” which is defined as “The Wrongdoing by Defendants which is alleged in the Second Amended Complaint filed in this litigation.” Plaintiffs object on the basis that vague, ambiguous, overly broad, and unduly burdensome because there are 5 plaintiffs and 3 defendants in the SAC. However, it is common sense that Defendant is asking about the wrongdoings in relation to the present parties.

 

            Again, Plaintiffs focus their opposition on meritless procedural arguments. Further, Plaintiffs have not provided justification for the above objections, or any other objections set forth in the responses. Thus, Plaintiffs must provide substantive responses.

 

            As to the substantive responses provided, Plaintiffs have not answered all subparts and appear to qualify their responses based on the term “incident.” Thus, the responses are not complete and straightforward.

 

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

            The Court declines to award sanctions to either party as the rulings go in favor of both parties and imposition of sanctions would be futile to compensate either side.

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Defendant’s motions to compel further responses to special interrogatories, set one, is DENIED as to San Clemente Beach Country Club and GRANTED as to GC-8 LLC.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents, set one, is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to request for admissions, set one, is DENIED.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories, set one, is GRANTED.

 

            All ordered verified, code complaint further responses are to be served on the defendants with 7 days of notice of this ruling.

 

            Sanctions are denied.

BACKGROUND 

 

This case arises out of Plaintiff Justin Whiter’s lemon law claim for a 2019 Ford Fusion, Vehicle Identification Number 3FA6P0HD4KR174062 (the “Subject Vehicle”). Plaintiff filed his complaint on March 7, 2023. Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel further responses to his request for production of documents, set one (the “Motion”) issued to FMC. Plaintiff does not request sanctions in connection with the Motion.

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to compel further responses is granted in part. FMC is ordered to produce supplemental verified, code-compliant responses to the RFPs and produce all responsive documents which are not part of the public record within 30 days. RFP Nos. 45, 68, 73, and 79 are granted subject to the following limitations: RFP Nos. 68 and 73 are limited to documents from 2019; and RFP Nos. 45 and 79 are limited to documents concerning the Subject Vehicle.

  

 

OBJECTIONS TO EVIDENCE 

 

A trial court may decline to rule on evidence that is not material to the disposition of the motion.

  

LEGAL STANDARD 

 

On receipt of a response to discovery requests, the party requesting may move for an order compelling further responses for interrogatories (Code Civ. Proc. 2030.300), requests for admission (Cod. Civ. Proc. section 2033.290), and request for production (Code Civ. Proc. section 2031.310). “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the requesting party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the requesting party waives any right to compel further response to the requests for admission.” (Code Civ. Proc. section 2033.290, subd. (c). 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met. (Smith Decl. ¶¶ 26-34, Exs. 7-12).

Plaintiff seeks further responses to RFPs 1, 3, 12, 15, 17, 19, 31, 36, 39, 43, 45, 46, 56, 57, 58, 68, 69, 73, 76, 77, 78, 79, 80, 82 and 83.  Plaintiff argues that FMC’s responses are not code-compliant and contain boilerplate objections.  

 

Plaintiff characterizes the RFPs as falling into five categories: (1) documents concerning the Subject Vehicle (Nos. 1, 3, 12, and 15); (2) documents concerning FMC’s internal knowledge and investigation in other vehicles regarding the engine defects in other vehicles with the same engine as the Subject Vehicle (Nos. 17, 19, 31, 36, and 39); (3) documents concerning summaries, memorandum, PowerPoints regarding the engine defects in other vehicles with the same engine as the Subject Vehicle (Nos. 43, 45 and 46); (4) documents concerning FMC’s lemon law policies and procedures (Nos. 56, 57, 58, 68, 69 and 73); and (5) documents concerning communication with governmental agencies and suppliers (Nos. 76, 77, 78, 79, 80, 82 and 83). Plaintiff asserts that there is good cause to seek these documents because they are relevant to Plaintiff’s claims and various issues presented in the Complaint, including whether FMC knew of the alleged defects. 

 

In the opposition (the “Opposition”), FMC argues its existing responses are code-compliant and its privilege objections are proper and merited.  FMC argues that Plaintiff has not established good cause to compel further responses or production. FMC also contends that Plaintiff’s definition of “ENGINE DEFECT(S)” does not adequately describe the information sought, and the requests are overly broad and burdensome. FMC additionally argues cost shifting is appropriate if the Court compels FMC to produce discovery.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has shown good cause that the RFPs seek documents relevant to the action. FMC’s current objections are stated in boilerplate and conclusory language and its responses are not code compliant under CCP section 2031.210. As to FMC’s argument regarding Plaintiff’s Engine Defect definition, Plaintiff provides the following definition: “[t]he term ‘ENGINE DEFECT(S)’ shall be understood to mean such defects which result in symptoms, including, loss of power; rough running; engine misfire(s); loss of coolant and/or oil; low coolant and/or oil; failure and/or replacement of the engine, cylinder, long block, and/or engine components; and/or any other similar concern identified in the repair history for the SUBJECT VEHICLE.” The Court finds that this definition adequately describes the information sought as it is based on Plaintiff’s repair orders.

 

Notwithstanding, the Court finds that RFP Nos. 68 and 73 should be limited to documents from 2019, whereas RFP Nos. 45 and 79 should be limited to documents concerning the Subject Vehicle. Further, as to RFP Nos. 76, 78, and 80, the Court limits the requests to documents that are not publicly available NHTSA documents.

 

Sanctions are not requested and the Court declines to impose sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION 

 

 The Court GRANTS the Motion in part and orders FMC to produce supplemental verified, code-compliant responses to the RFPs and produce all responsive documents which are not part of the public record within 30 days. RFP Nos. 45, 68, 73, and 79 are granted subject to the limitation described above.
