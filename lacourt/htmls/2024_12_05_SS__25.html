ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 10, 2022, Plaintiff Nicholas Howell (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendants Eduardo Cabral and Manuel Cabral (“Defendants”) and Does 1 through 100. (Compl., p. 1.)

On October 20, 2022, Defendants filed an Answer.

 

On July 28, 2023, the Court granted the parties’ stipulation to continue trial and all trial-related dates from September 7, 2023 to March 8, 2024.  (7/28/23 Order.)

 

On December 28, 2023, the Court granted the parties’ stipulation to continue trial and all trial-related dates from March 8, 2024 to May 31, 2024. (12/28/23 Order.)

 

On March 15, 2024, the Court granted the parties’ stipulation to continue trial and all trial-related dates from May 31, 2024 to December 10, 2024. (3/15/24 Order.)

 

On September 19, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ unopposed Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition and ordered Plaintiff and/or Plaintiff’s counsel to meet and confer with Defendants’ counsel within five days from the service of the Court’s order to set a mutually agreed upon date for Plaintiff’s deposition for which Plaintiff is ordered to appear. (9/19/24 Order.)

 

On September 20, 2024, the Notice of Ruling regarding Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition was served by mail and electronic service on Plaintiff’s counsel. (Motion, 9/20/24 Proof of Service.)

 

On September 30, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Terminating Sanctions against Plaintiff (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Defendants move for terminating sanctions on the grounds “Plaintiff has repeatedly demonstrated disregard for the discovery process, their obligations under the Code of Civil Procedure, and this Court’s Order. (Motion, p. 5.)

 

Most recently, Defendants contend Plaintiff have avoided Defendants’ request to “set Plaintiff’s deposition and meet and confer with the Defendant regarding scheduling the deposition,” as was ordered by this Court on September 19, 2024. (Motion, p. 5; 9/19/24 Order.) Defendants’ counsel served the Notice of Ruling on Plaintiff on September 20, 2024 and followed up via email on September 25, 2024 to no avail. (Motion, p. 4; Goodwin Decl.) As of September 30, 2024, Defendants have not received a response. (Motion, p. 4; Goodwin Decl.)

 

On motion, if a party “fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction” as provided in Section 2030.010, et seq. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (h).)  The “court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after an opportunity for hearing, may impose” monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions “against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.030; 2023.030, subds. (a)-(d).) Failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are misuses of the discovery process.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010, subds. (d) and (g).)

 

The court should look to the totality of the circumstances in determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a “willful” discovery order violation “preceded by a history of abuse,” and evidence showing that “less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.” (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.” (Brown v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)

 

“The court may impose a terminating sanction by one of the following orders:  

 

(1)   An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process. 

(2)   An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed. 

(3)   An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party. 

(4)   An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).) 

 

As an initial matter, the Court finds this Motion to be untimely. “Except as otherwise provided in this chapter, any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)

 

The Court finds Defendants are not entitled to an order granting terminating sanctions against Plaintiff. Defendants have not submitted evidence suggesting Plaintiff and/or Plaintiff’s counsel failed to obey the Court’s order compelling continued meet and confer discussions and Plaintiff’s attendance at a deposition in a manner that warrants terminating sanctions. While Defendants allege that Plaintiff’s counsel has not complied with the Court’s order and was unresponsive from September 20, 2024, when Plaintiff was served with the Notice of Ruling, to September 30, 2024, when the instant Motion was filed and served, the imposition of terminating sanctions is unwarranted and/or premature. (See Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793-794 [“The sanction of dismissal or the rendition of a default judgment against the disobedient party is ordinarily a drastic measure which should be employed with caution. [Citation.] However, there is no question that a court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations. [Citation.] The refusal to reveal material evidence is deemed to be an admission that the claim or defense is without merit. [Citations.]”].) Defendants submit no evidence of their meet and confer attempts or of another deposition following the Court’s Order where Plaintiff failed to appear. Thus, the Court finds there is insufficient evidence that Plaintiff’s and/or Plaintiff’s counsel failure to comply with the Court order was willful or in bad faith.

 

Plaintiff and/or Plaintiff’s counsel are ordered to meet and confer with Defendants’ counsel to set a mutually agreed upon date for Plaintiff’s deposition for which Plaintiff is ordered to appear per prev. Plaintiff is ordered to appear for deposition before trial on December 10, 2024.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ Motion for Terminating Sanctions is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to appear for the continued deposition prior to trial on December 10, 2024.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Subsequently, the Court continued the hearing on the Motions to allow Defendant to file a proper answer/responsive pleading. As the Court noted, the one-page document filed by Defendant on June 12, 2023 was “an improper response to Plaintiff’s Complaint as it is not answer nor a demurrer.” (9/12/24 Order.)

 

On October 15, 2024, the Motions were again called for hearing. Defendant had not yet appeared. The Court stated it was “advised that attorney M. Reza Lajevardi was previously present via LACourtConnect and represented he was appearing on behalf of the Defendant. II.                 Legal Standard

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

 

A party must respond to requests for admissions within 30 days after service of such requests. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).) “If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response…(a) [that party] waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)

Interrogatories and Request for Admissions

 

Plaintiff contends that Defendant failed to timely respond to Form Interrogatories and Request for Admissions by the statutory deadline. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260, subd. (a), 2033.250, subd. (a).)

As an initial matter, the Court notes Plaintiff has submitted evidence of 31 discovery requests made upon Defendant – 19 Form Interrogatories and 12 Requests for Admissions. As of August 15, 2024, Plaintiff had not received any responses. (ROGs Tapper Decl., p. 2; RFAs Tapper Decl., p. 2.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff’s Motions are GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.250(a), 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].)

 

            Here, Defendant failed to serve a timely response to Plaintiff’s Interrogatories and Request for Admissions by the statutory deadline. Thus, sanctions are mandatory against Defendant, “whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.250(a), 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a).)

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions of $2,330.00, based on $1,165.00 for each motion, reflecting 2 hours incurred on bringing each Motion at an hourly rate of $225.00 per hour, plus three hours of travel and attendance per Motion at $225.00 per hour, plus $80.00 in filing fees. (ROGs Tapper Decl., ¶ 6; RFAs Tapper Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Given the similarity of the Motions, the shared hearing date, and the straightforward nature of the Motions, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions against Defendant is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $980.00, reflecting 4 hours incurred at $225.00 an hour, plus $80.00 in filing fees.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff Kenneth Forbes (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendant Gregory Allen Pedone (“Defendant”) alleging Common Counts, Breach of Contract, Conversion, Fraud, and Punitive Damages causes of action. (Compl., p. 1.) The dispute arises out of an oral contract (the “Agreement”) for the packing of collectible ornaments into a cargo container. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

On October 28, 2024, Defendant filed a Demurrer to the Complaint (the “Demurrer”) and Motion to Strike (the “Motion”) as to portions of the Complaint. While the Demurrer and Motion were filed in the same document, the Court will reference the Demurrer and Motion separately.

 

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition as to both the Demurrer and Motion.

 

No Reply has been filed.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes the Demurrer and Motion are untimely as they were filed more than 30 days after service of the Complaint. (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 430.40; 435.) However, given the parties’ stipulation to extend the time to respond to the Complaint in attempts to resolve their differences without court involvement (Grismer Decl., ¶ 3), the Court exercises its discretion to hear the Demurrer and Motion on the merits.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

“As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). (Demurrer, Grismer Decl.) According to the declaration, Defendant’s counsel sent met and confer correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel on September 13, 2024 that addressed the merits of the Demurrer and Motion. (Grismer Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)  The parties then “met and conferred and agreed to an extension in which to respond to the complaint up to and including October 28, 2024, in order to allow the parties to further discussed resolution.” (Grismer Decl., ¶ 3.) However, Defendant’s counsel then states that “at this juncture, the Defendant wishes to defend this matter.” (Grismer Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Thus, the Court finds the meet and confer requirement satisfied.

 

B.     Demurrer

 

Moving Party Position

 

Defendant demurs to the third and fourth causes of action regarding Conversion and Fraud. (Demurrer, p. 2.) As to the third cause of action regarding Conversion, Defendant contends the Complaint fails to state sufficient facts to support a Conversion claim because “the Complaint fails to identify the personal property Defendant took possession of, whether that be possession of money or any other tangible object or prevented plaintiff from having access to the item of personal property or destroying the personal property or refusing to return the item of personal property after plaintiff demanded its return. Further, the complaint fails to state how Defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor causing plaintiff’s harm.” (Demurrer, p. 5.)

 

As to the fourth cause of action regarding Fraud, Defendant contends Plaintiff’s Complaint is uncertain and fails to satisfy the heightened pleading standard required for allegations of fraud. (Demurrer, p. 5.) Specifically, Defendant contends the Complaint is vague and uncertain as to whether the allegations of fraud are based on negligent misrepresentation, intentional misrepresentation, or intentional concealment. (Demurrer, pp. 6-7.) Defendant alleges that “[b]ecause Plaintiff fails to clearly indicate the legal theory under which she is operating and because her allegations in this cause of action might arguably fall under three distinct theories of fraud it is difficult if not impossible for Defendant to respond.” (Demurrer, p. 7.)

 

            Opposition

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff addresses the third and fourth causes of action in turn. First, Plaintiff contends each element for a Conversion cause of action is pled because the Complaint alleges that (1) Plaintiff possessed money in the sum of $1,800 that he had obtained from the sale of the container (Complaint, p. 21, Exh. C); (2) Defendant interfered with the $1,800 paid by Plaintiff and refused to return the $1,800 to Plaintiff (Complaint, ¶¶ 2-5, 17, Exhs. B-D); (3) Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant’s failure to return the $1,800 (Complaint, ¶¶ 3-5, 7, 17, Exhs. B-D); and (4) Plaintiff was harmed in the amount of $1,900 as a “direct actual and proximate result of Defendant’s dilatory conduct.” (Opp., pp. 2-3.)

 

            As to the Fraud cause of action, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s Demurrer is unclear as it references inapt code sections and contains conflicting language. (Opp., p. 4.) Plaintiff then contends that the Complaint is clear as to the fraud allegations since it is explicitly alleged that “Defendant engaged in a proximate and actual deceit by representing to Plaintiff that he fulfilled his contractual obligation to pay for the container.” (Opp., p. 4.) Plaintiff contends the Complaint states facts that “support a fraud to obtain property of another by false pretenses,” and “[t]here may be other possible theories which may well be pursued during the course of discovery in this case.” (Opp., p. 4.)

 

            Allegations in the Complaint

           

            The Complaint alleges the parties entered into the Agreement on or about December 21, 2023 for the packing of collectible ornaments into a cargo container. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Pursuant to the terms of the Agreement, Plaintiff agreed “to pack and store … collectible ornaments into the container on a pro bona basis for [his] services. Defendants, and each of them, agreed to pay for the cost of the Container,” which cost $1,900. (Compl., ¶ 1, Exh. A.) A month or so later, the Container was sold for $1,800 and this “sum of money was returned to Defendants,” who each assured Plaintiff that they had paid for the Container as initially agreed. (Compl., ¶ 2.) An invoice from Defendant also confirmed payment for the costs of the Container. (Compl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) After Defendants received the $1,800 payment for the Container, Plaintiff received information from the container company that Defendant’s credit card was not going through, and the container company had not received any payment of the $1,900 owed. (Compl., ¶ 4, Exh. B.)

 

            On or about March 9, 2024, Plaintiff demanded that Defendants “return the $1,800 that [Plaintiff] had returned to him from the sale of the Container… and/or pay for the Container.” (Compl., ¶ 4.) On or about March 12, 2024, Plaintiff called each Defendant to determine if they would agree to return the $1,800 earned for the sale of the Container; however, Defendants refused and stated they would not pay for the Container. (Compl., ¶ 5.) A couple days later, Plaintiff paid $1,900 to the container company, Container Alliance, as “full and final payment of the charges due for sale of the Container … so as to avoid a lawsuit against [Plaintiff] by the Container Company.” (Compl., ¶ 6, Exh. E.)

 

            Defendants “continue to refuse to return of the $1,800 and refuse to reimburse [Plaintiff] for the cost of the Container therein.” (Compl., ¶ 7.)

 

            Analysis

 

3rd Cause of Action – Conversion

 

“ ‘ “ ‘Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.’ ” ’ ” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) 

 

Here, the Court finds Plaintiff has pled the elements of conversion. Plaintiff pleads he is entitled to the $1,900 Defendant failed to pay the container company as part of the Agreement; Defendant wrongfully defrauded Plaintiff of the $1,900; and Plaintiff suffered damages in the amount of $1,900 as a result of Defendant’s conversion by having to pay the container company instead of Defendant. (Compl., ¶¶ 1-7.)

 

To the extent Plaintiff contends he is owed the $1,800 from the sale of the Container, separate and apart from the $1,900 owed to reimburse the Container, the Court agrees Plaintiff has failed to plead the elements of conversion as to this sum of money. Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman, supra, 18 Cal.3d at p. 348.)

 

Thus, the Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the claim of $1,900 and SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the claim of $1,800.

 

                        4th Cause of Action – Fraud

 

The elements of fraud are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184, citing Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)  A cause of action for fraud must be “ ‘pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. … “ ‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which “ ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’ ” ’ ” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184, citing Lazar, supra, 12 Cal.4th at p. 645.)

 

Here, Defendant’s contentions rest entirely on the ground of uncertainty. However, a demurrer brought on the ground of uncertainty is a special demurrer, and special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).

Thus, the Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the fourth cause of action.

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part as to the third cause of action and OVERRULED as to the fourth cause of action.

 

C.     Motion to Strike

 

Moving Party Position

 

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages in its entirety or, in the alternative, the dollar amount listed in the Complaint. (Motion, p. 2.) Specifically, Defendant alleges Plaintiff improperly identifies a cause of action for punitive damages and contends the Complaint merely asserts conclusory allegations as to the conduct serving as the grounds for punitive damages. (Motion, pp. 8-9.) Finally, Defendant alleges the Complaint “impermissibly and arbitrarily requests punitive damages in the sum of $25,000” in contravention of Civil Code section 2395(e), which states “no request for exemplary damages may state an amount or amounts.” (Motion, p. 9.)

 

                        Opposition

 

            In Opposition, Plaintiff contends the Motion should be denied because punitive damages are properly pled. Indeed, Plaintiff states the Complaint alleges that “Defendant engaged in a scheme to defraud and to enrich himself by falsely tendering a credit card which he knew would not go though and/or which Defendant stopped payment on the tender of the credit card to deceive the Plaintiff herein.” (Opp., p. 5.)

 

                        Analysis

 

California law authorizes a party’s motion to strike matter from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false, or improper. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436.) Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings that are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules, or orders. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (b).)

 

However, motions to strike in limited jurisdiction courts may only challenge pleadings on the basis that “the damages or relief sought are not supported by the allegations of the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 92, subd. (d).) The Code of Civil Procedure also authorizes the Court to act on its own initiative to strike matters, empowering the Court to enter orders striking matter “at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.)

 

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires that “[b]efore filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).) (Emphasis added.)

 

Punitive damages are authorized by Civil Code section 3294 in non-contract cases “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied . . . .”  (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

 

As an initial matter, the Court finds Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement of Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5. Indeed and as previously mentioned, the Motion is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). (Grismer Decl.) According to the declaration, Defendant’s counsel sent met and confer correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel on September 13, 2024 that addressed the merits of the Demurrer and Motion. (Grismer Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)  The parties then “met and conferred and agreed to an extension in which to respond to the complaint up to and including October 28, 2024, in order to allow the parties to further discussed resolution.” (Grismer Decl., ¶ 3.) Defendant’s counsel then states that “at this juncture, the Defendant wishes to defend this matter.” (Grismer Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Here, the allegations of breach of contract, common counts, and conversion of Plaintiff’s monetary property, without more, do not support a claim for punitive damages as they do not demonstrate that Defendant intended to injure Plaintiff or engaged in despicable conduct or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subds. (a), (c).)

 

However, the fraud cause of action supports the request for punitive damages. Indeed, Civil Code section 3294 defines fraud as an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civil Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).) Here, the Complaint alleges Defendant misrepresented or concealed from Plaintiff the material fact that the tendered credit card for payment under the Agreement would not work for payment to deprive Plaintiff of his monetary property. (Compl., p. 6.) Since fraud is an acceptable ground for recovering punitive damages, Plaintiff may plead a punitive damages request.

 

Accordingly, the Motion is DENIED as to the request to strike punitive damages in its entirety.

 

However, the Court agrees Plaintiff improperly included an amount with the punitive damages request (Civ. Code, § 3295), and punitive damages are a form of relief, not a cause of action. Thus, the Motion is GRANTED as to Plaintiff’s request to strike the sum of $25,000 from the punitive damage request as well as the punitive damages cause of action.

 

D.    Conclusion & Order

 

Defendant Gregory Allen Pedone’s Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the fourth cause of action and OVERRULED IN PART and SUSTAINED IN PART WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the third cause of action.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. Plaintiff is ordered to strike the punitive damages cause of action and mention of the specific sum in relation to his request for punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint to be served and electronically filed within 30-days of this Court’s order.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
