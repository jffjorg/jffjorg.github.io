DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV01501    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

JERRY LYLES, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV01501

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

JOHN MCINTYRE, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          September 6, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants John McIntyre, The Le June Group, and JM Sunshine Group LLC

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Jerry Lyles and Cassandra Wagoner

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint and for Award of Fees and Expenses

 

            The Court considered the moving papers, late-filed opposition, and defendants’ objection.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED in part.  The first and second causes of action in the complaint are STRICKEN.  The motion is DENIED as to paras. 30, 31, 37, 48, 51, and 159.

            Defendants are awarded reasonable attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $2310.

BACKGROUND

            On May 2, 2024, plaintiffs Jerry Lyles and Cassandra Wagoner filed a complaint against John McIntyre, The Le June Group, and JM Sunshine Group LLC for (1) retaliatory eviction, (2) malicious prosecution, (3) elder abuse, (4) violation of Civil Code §789.3, (5) violation of Civil Code §1946.2, (6) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (7) breach of the implied warranty of habitability, (8) constructive eviction, (9) assault, (10) battery, (11) IIED, (12) NIED, and (13) injunctive relief.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Under CCP §425.16(b), “(1) A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.  (2) In making its determination, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.”

“The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.”  Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal. 5th 376, 393.  “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech.  It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.”  Id. at 384.  “Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps.  First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16.  If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.”  Id. (citation omitted).  The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’  The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.  Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment.  It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law.  ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’”  Id. at 384-85 (citations omitted). 

“At the first step, the moving defendant bears the burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity, and the claims for relief supported by them.  When relief is sought based on allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the unprotected activity is disregarded at this stage.  If the court determines that relief is sought based on allegations arising from activity protected by the statute, the second step is reached.  There, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate that each challenged claim based on protected activity is legally sufficient and factually substantiated.”  Id. at 396.

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to §425.16, defendants request that the Court strike the following:  paras. 30, 31, 37, 48, 51, first cause of action for retaliatory eviction, second cause of action for malicious prosecution, and para. 159.

In ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, the trial court engages in a two-step process.  “First, the court decides whether the defendant has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity.  The moving defendant’s burden is to demonstrate that the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken ‘in furtherance of the [defendant]’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue,’ as defined in the statute.  If the court finds such a showing has been made, it then determines whether the plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim. . . . Only when a defendant shows that a cause of action is based on protected conduct and the plaintiff fails to show a likelihood of success on that claim is it subject to dismissal.”  Wong v. Jing (2010) 189 Cal. App. 4th 1354, 1360 (citation omitted).

            The complaint alleges that plaintiffs signed a lease and moved into their residence at 16531 S. Denver Avenue, #2, Gardena on May 19, 2019.  Complaint, ¶14.  Throughout their tenancy, plaintiffs’ rent has been subsidized by the Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing (VASH) Program.  Id., ¶15.  From May 19, 2019 to January 3, 2024, plaintiffs peacefully lived in their residence, paid their rent on time, and had a great relationship with their neighbors at the subject property.  Id., ¶16.  On January 3, 2024, plaintiffs contacted the LA Department of Housing to notify them that they had a faulty electrical box that could potentially become dangerous.  Id., ¶17.  On January 4, 2024, when Cassadra pulled into the driveway of the property, a man that Cassandra had never seen before, dressed in a motorcycle vest with patches to signify what motorcycle gang or club he is a member, walked up to her vehicle and yelled, “get your motherfucking shit out of the laundry room.”  Id., ¶18.  In response, Cassandra asked, “who are you?” and the man said, “don’t worry about it, just get your shit out of the laundry room.”  Appalled and fearful of her safety, she got out of her vehicle, went into the residence, and informed Jerry about what just transpired.  Id., ¶19.

            The complaint further alleges that while Cassandra was telling Jerry what transpired, there was a knock on their door.  When Cassandra opened the door, she encountered defendant John McIntyre who identified himself as the new owner of the subject property and his agent Rhonda Willis, who identified herself as the new manager.  John informed Cassandra that he needed plaintiffs to sign some documents.  Id., ¶20.  Plaintiffs had never received notice that the property was being sold or had been sold.  Id., ¶21.  Jerry stated to Rhonda that he would have to take a look over the document before he signed it.  As he turned his back to return to his residence and look at the papers, Rhonda suddenly and unexpectedly rushed from behind and yelled, “give me back them papers.,” and knocked Jerry to the ground while attempting to snatch the documents from him.  Id., ¶22.  Cassandra called the police.  Id., ¶23.  After the police left, John yelled out to Cassandra from outside to come and get her clothes out of the laundry room.  He removed her clothes, mixed it with the neighbor’s clothes, and dumped them at the front door of her residence.  Id., ¶24.  Shortly after, plaintiffs observed John changing the lock to the laundry room. As of the time that the complaint was filed, John still had not provided plaintiffs with a key to the laundry room.  Id., ¶25.

            The complaint further alleges that on January 7, 2024, plaintiffs attempted to pay their rent to defendants on the Zelle account that defendants provided them.  Defendants subsequently cancelled that transaction.  Id., ¶29.  On January 8, 2024, defendants posted a three-day notice to quit premises, falsely accusing plaintiffs of blocking the driveway preventing defendants’ workers from doing their job on January 3, 2024.  Id., ¶30.  The notice also falsely accused plaintiffs of physically assaulting defendants’ agent on January 4 and falsely accusing Jerry of waving a hammer at defendants and their agents in a threatening manner.  Id., ¶31.  On January 13, 2024, defendants called LAPD officers and falsely accused Jerry of pulling a gun on him.  Jerry was subsequently arrested because he would not allow officers to search his home without a search warrant.  The District Attorney’s office declined to file charges against him.  Id., ¶33.  On January 14, 2024, defendants placed a Ring camera directly in front of their residence to monitor plaintiffs’ comings and goings.  Id., ¶34.  On January 17, 2024, defendants shut off the hot water to their residence for two days, which prompted plaintiffs to file a complaint with the LA Department of Housing.  Id., ¶35.  On that same day, Jerry filed a Request for a Civil Harassment Restraining Order against John due to the ongoing harassment.  Id., ¶36.  On January 18, 2024, defendants filed another three-day notice to quit premises.  In that notice, they repeated the same false allegations about incidents that did not occur on January 4 and 6.  Id., ¶37.  Defendants included the false allegation that Jerry pulled a gun on John, an allegation that the District Attorney’s office declined to file charges.  Id., ¶38. 

            The complaint further alleges that defendants continued terrorizing plaintiffs by calling parking enforcement to issue tickets for parking in locations that they had been accustomed to parking for the previous four years.  Id., ¶39.  In the beginning of February 2024, plaintiffs had to travel to Texas due to a death in the family.  While they were gone, defendants falsely informed the usual mail carrier that plaintiffs no longer lived at their residence, and that the mail could no longer be delivered there.  Id., ¶40.  On February 16, 2024, plaintiffs’ son went to the property to check in on plaintiffs’ residence.  When he arrived, he was shocked to see that plaintiffs’ garage door had been removed, exposing plaintiffs’ residence and personal items stored inside and in the garage to potential thieves as well as the elements during a rainstorm.  Id., ¶41.  Plaintiffs returned to their residence on February 26, 2024.  They called a garage door installation company to replace their door.  Id., ¶43.  When the garage door installation company arrived and their workers were about to install a new garage door, John immediately approached them and threatened to have them arrested for trespassing.  They left.  Id., ¶44.  On February 28, 2024, plaintiffs contacted another garage door installation company.  When the company arrived, John and one of his agents approached the workers.  LAPD officers were called onto the scene.  The officers informed John that his action of removing the garage door was illegal and informed the garage door employees that they could install the new garage door.  Id., ¶45.  One of John’s agents threw a stack of papers at Cassandra and said, “you’ve been served.”  Id., ¶46.  She discovered that on January 29, 2024, defendant JM filed an unlawful detainer against plaintiffs based on the three-day notice dated January 18, 2024 “that was filled with lies.”  Id., ¶47.  Cassandra also saw that she was being served with a Request for a Civil Harassment Restraining Order filed by John on February 5, 2024, and that the hearing had been conducted on February 27.  Plaintiffs later learned that John also filed one against Jerry on February 5, but that he was never served.  Id., ¶48.  Plaintiffs are of the belief that because their rent is subsidized by VASH, and their rent is below market value, defendants are trying to evict them based on false pretenses in order to circumvent the Tenant Protection Act of 2019 and avoid paying them relocation fees.  Id., ¶49.

            The first cause of action for retaliatory eviction alleges that plaintiffs hold a leasehold interest in and have been tenants of the subject property before and while defendants have owned and/or managed the property.  Id., ¶56.  Civil Code §1942.5 prohibits a landlord from raising tenants’ rent or causing the tenants to quit the premises voluntarily within 180 days after the tenants exercise their rights under the relevant chapter of the Civil Code.  Id., ¶57.  The 180 days would run from January 17, 2024, the date on which plaintiffs filed a Tenant Complaint Form with the Los Angeles Housing Department due to defendants shutting off their hot water and locking them out of the laundry room.  In the alternative, the 180 days would run from February 21, 2024, seven days before defendants served plaintiffs with the unlawful detainer, which is the date that the Los Angeles Housing gave defendants a notice of violation and ordered them to make repairs.  Id., ¶58. 

            The second cause of action for malicious prosecution alleges that defendants’ false claims caused law enforcement to arrest Jerry and charge him with assault with a deadly weapon.  Id., ¶64.  The District Attorney’s Office were able to determine that defendants were not truthful and therefore, they declined to file charges against Jerry.  Id., ¶65.  No reasonable person in defendants’ circumstances would have believed that there were grounds for causing plaintiff to be arrested.  Id., ¶66.  It is also plaintiffs’ belief that defendants acted primarily for a purpose other than to bring plaintiff to justice, which in this case, was to circumvent The Tenant Protection Act of 2019 in order to file a three-day notice to quit premises in order to file an unlawful detainer and evict them.  Id., ¶67.  Plaintiffs were harmed by Jerry being arrested for no reason and having to face the prospect of being incarcerated due to defendants false claims.  Id., ¶68. 

 

Step One:  Conduct in furtherance of right of petition or free speech

            CCP §425.16(e) states, in relevant part:  “As used in this section, ‘act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ includes:  (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.”

“The phrase ‘arising from’ . . . has been interpreted to mean that ‘the act underlying the plaintiff’s cause’ or ‘the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff’s cause of action’ must have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’”  Schwarzburd v. Kensington Police Protection & Community Services Dist. Bd.  (2014) 225 Cal. App. 4th 1345, 1350 (citation omitted).  “What nexus must a defendant show between a challenged claim and the defendant’s protected activity for the claim to be struck?  . . . [A] claim is not subject to a motion to strike simply because it contests an action or decision that was arrived at following speech or petitioning activity, or that was thereafter communicated by means of speech or petitioning activity.  Rather, a claim may be struck only if the speech or petitioning activity itself is the wrong complained of, and not just evidence of liability or a step leading to some different act for which liability is asserted.”  Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal. 5th 1057, 1060 (considering the relationship a defendant must show between a plaintiff’s claim and the sorts of speech on public matters the Legislature intended to protect).  “’[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’  ( . . . see City of Cotati [v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal. 4th 69], at p. 78 [suit may be in “response to, or in retaliation for,’ protected activity without necessarily arising from it].)  Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’  ‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy the [arising from] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . . . .’  In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.”  Id.

            Defendants argue that the first and second causes of action arise out of a right to petition—the prosecution of a prior unlawful detainer action.

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that their claims are based on defendants’ wrongful acts and not their right to petition.  Plaintiffs assert that the retaliatory cause of action is based on defendants attempting to evict them after plaintiffs made complaints about uninhabitable conditions and before plaintiffs served them with an unlawful detainer complaint, including by serving plaintiffs with three-day notices based on false allegations.  Further, plaintiffs contend, the “substance” of the cause of action for malicious prosecution is based on defendants calling law enforcement and making a false claim that plaintiff Lyles threatened them with a handgun, which led to his arrest, activity which is wrongful and therefore is not “protected.” 

            The Court rules as follows as to the first prong:  “The prosecution of an unlawful detainer action indisputably is protected activity within the meaning of section 425.16.”  Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal. App. 4th 1467, 1479 (citation omitted).  “Service of the three-day notice to quit in this case was also protected activity within the meaning of section 425.16.”  Id. (citation omitted). 

“Although an unlawful detainer action itself is protected activity under section 425.16, terminating a lease is not.  A complaint arising out of or based on the dispute or conduct underlying the unlawful detainer action is not subject to a special motion to strike.  ‘[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’  When, as in this case, the challenged lawsuit follows service of a three-day notice to quit, ‘[t]he pivotal distinction’ is whether an actual or contemplated unlawful detainer action by a landlord (unquestionably a protected petitioning activity) merely ‘preceded’ or ‘triggered’ the tenant's lawsuit, or whether it was instead the ‘basis’ or ‘cause’ of that suit.”  Copenbarger v. Morris Cerullo World Evangelism (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 1237, 1245 (citations and internal quotations omitted).  See, e.g., Clark v. Mazgani (2009) 170 Cal. App. 4th 1281, 1290 where although the unlawful detainer action triggered the tenant’s lawsuit, that lawsuit was based on claims the landlord fraudulently invoked the family occupancy exemption of the rent control ordinance and failed to fulfill her obligations under that ordinance.

“We are mindful that the lines drawn in these cases are fine ones.  However, we are reminded by our Supreme Court . . . , that the ‘focus’ of the statute ‘is not the form of plaintiff's cause of action but, rather, the defendant's activity that gives rise to his or her asserted liability . . . .’”  Feldman, supra, at 1483 (citations omitted). 

As to the first cause of action for retaliation, plaintiffs are alleging that defendants are liable because they served plaintiffs with three-day notices to quit (based on “false claims”) in an attempt to evict them within 180 days after plaintiffs filed a complaint with the Los Angeles Housing Department.  The three-day notices are the cause or basis of this cause of action, and it is the challenged activity.  Service of a three-day notice is protected activity.  See Feldman, supra.

As to the second cause of action for malicious prosecution, plaintiffs allege that defendants are liable for making a false claim to law enforcement that Jerry had pointed a gun at defendant McIntyre and that he was arrested and that such claim was also made on the three-day notice to quit, which formed the basis of the unlawful detainer action.  Jarrow Formulas, Inc. v. LaMarche (2003) 31 Cal.4th 728, 735 states that “[b]y definition, a malicious prosecution suit alleges that the defendant committed a tort by filing a lawsuit. . . . [a]ccordingly, every Court of Appeal that has addressed the question has concluded that malicious prosecution causes of action fall within the purview of the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Citations omitted.)  Further, communications and complaints to police fall within the anti-SLAPP Statute.  See Comstock v. Aber (2012) 212 Cal. App. 4th 931, 941-42 (“The law is that communications to the police are within SLAPP.”). Thus, the three-day notices and complaint to the police are covered under CCP §425.16(e)(1) and/or (e)(2). 

Accordingly, the Court finds that defendants have met their burden under the first prong that plaintiff’s first and second causes of action fall under CCP §425.16(e).  As to the individual paragraphs (as to three-day notices and civil restraining orders), they are allowed as context and background and are not “claims” as contemplated by CCP §425.16.

 

            Step Two:  Plaintiff’s Responding Burden

            “To establish a probability of prevailing, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.  For purposes of this inquiry, the trial court considers the pleadings and evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant (§ 425.16, subd. (b)(2)); though the court does not weigh the credibility or comparative probative strength of competing evidence, it should grant the motion if, as a matter of law, the defendant's evidence supporting the motion defeats the plaintiff's attempt to establish evidentiary support for the claim.”  Hawran v. Hixson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 256, 273-74.  However, the court must accept as true the evidence favorable to plaintiff.  Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 291.  Plaintiff need only establish that the claim has minimal merit.  Id.  

Defendants argue that plaintiffs cannot meet their burden based on the litigation privilege.  The Court notes that the litigation privilege does not apply to either cause of action.  There is an exception to the litigation privilege for retaliatory eviction cases under Civil Code §1942.5; if it were otherwise, the litigation privilege would effectively immunize the very conduct that the statute prohibits.  Winslett v. 1811 27th Avenue, LLC (2018) 26 Cal. App. 5th 239, 254.  Further, under Civil Code §47(b), “(5) This subdivision does not make privileged any communication between a person and a law enforcement agency in which the person makes a false report that another person has committed, or is in the act of committing, a criminal act or is engaged in an activity requiring law enforcement intervention, knowing that the report is false, or with reckless disregard for the truth or falsity of the report.”

 

            First cause of action for retaliatory eviction

            In the opposition, plaintiffs assert that they have demonstrated a reasonable probability of prevailing, citing only to their complaint.  Plaintiffs fail to present any admissible evidence.  There are no declaration.  See also defendants’ evidentiary objections, which the Court SUSTAINS.

            The Court finds that plaintiffs have not met their burden of showing a reasonable probability of prevailing under the first cause of action.

 

            Second cause of action for malicious prosecution

The elements of a claim for malicious prosecution include (1) a prior action commenced by or at direction of defendant, (2) that lacked probable cause, (3) was initiated with malice, and (4) resulted in a termination of the action in plaintiff’s favor.  Lanz v. Goldstone (2015) 243 Cal. App. 4th 441, 458.

            Defendants argues that the unlawful detainer action and restraining order were brought with probable cause and that neither of the proceedings terminated in plaintiffs’ favor.

            In opposition, plaintiffs contend that the basis for the cause of action is the false police report.

            The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action for malicious prosecution because filing a false police report is not a “prior action commenced by defendants” and there was no proceeding.  Further, “[s]imply establishing that an action was dismissed is insufficient to support a malicious prosecution claim.”  Eells v. Rosenblum (1995) 36 Cal. App. 4th 1848, 1855.  Also, plaintiffs have not shown a probability of prevailing as they do not provide any evidence that defendants lacked probable cause.  Also, of note, the unlawful detainer action was pursued to a legal termination favorable to defendants.

            The motion is thus GRANTED as to the first and second causes of action.

 

            Attorney’s Fees

Under CCP §425.16(c), “In any action subject to subdivision (b), a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover his or her attorney’s fees and costs.”

 “[B]y its terms, CCP section 425.16 permits the use of the so-called lodestar adjustment method.”  Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal. 4th 1122, 1131.  The lodestar figure is calculated by multiplying the amount of attorney time reasonable spent with the hourly prevailing rate for private attorneys in the local community conducting noncontingent litigation of the same type.  Id. at 1122.

Defendants request $3210 ($450/hr. x 7.5 hrs., $60 filing fee) in attorney’s fees and costs.  In light of the ruling on the motion, defendants are the prevailing party and entitled to reasonable attorney fees and costs.  The Court finds that $2310 ($450/hr. x 5 hrs., $60 filing fee) is a reasonable amount.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED in part.  The first and second causes of action in the complaint are STRICKEN.  The motion is DENIED as to paras. 30, 31, 37, 48, 51, and 159.

            Defendants are awarded reasonable attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $2310.

            Defendants are ordered to give notice of the ruling.
