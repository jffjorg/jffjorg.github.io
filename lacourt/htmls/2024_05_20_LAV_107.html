DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV00045    Hearing Date: May 20, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

 

Mathew Gleizer, et al.,

 

 

Plaintiffs.

 

   v.

 

Matthew Safapour,

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   24VECV00045                 107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

(1) Plaintiffs’ Motion to Strike

(2) Defendant’s Demurrer

(3) Defendant’s Motion to Strike

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

On January 22, 2022, plaintiff Mathew Gleizer (“Gleizer”) was operating a vehicle with plaintiff Daniel Tubi (“Tubi” and, together with Gleizer, “Plaintiffs”) as a passenger, which was involved in a collision with a vehicle being operated by defendant Matthew Safapour (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiffs, “Parties”). (Complaint p. 4.)

 

On January 4, 2024, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) negligence and (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On March 28, 2024, Defendant filed a declaration of his counsel in support of a 30-day extension of time in which to file a demurrer or motion to strike, to which Plaintiffs objected the same day.

 

On April 9, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (“Defendant’s Motion”). The same day, Plaintiffs filed a motion to strike the Demurrer (“Plaintiffs’ Motion”). Defendant opposed Plaintiffs’ Motion on April 23, 2024, and Plaintiffs replied to Defendant’s opposition on May 10, 2024. Plaintiffs opposed the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion on May 7, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

A.   Timeliness

 

Plaintiffs have not filed a proof of service of the Complaint in this case. Plaintiffs state that they served Defendant by personal service on February 26, 2024. (Plaintiffs’ Objection to Volk Decl. p. 2.) Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should similarly be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Thus, the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion should have been filed by March 27, 2024.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41, subdivision (b) states, in relevant part,

 

The parties shall meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. If the parties are not able to meet and confer at least five days prior to the date the responsive pleading is due, the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension of time within which to file a responsive pleading, by filing and serving, on or before the date on which a demurrer would be due, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer.

 

If Plaintiffs served Defendant by personal service on February 28, 2024, then in order to qualify for the 30-day extension of time in which to file a demurrer or motion to strike, Defendant must have filed and served his declaration under California Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41, subdivision (b) by March 27, 2024. Since Defendant did not file and serve this declaration until March 28, 2024, it is untimely and therefore, the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion are similarly untimely.

 

However, without a proof of service of the Complaint, we cannot confirm the date on which Defendant was served by what means. Additionally, judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).) As we are unable to confirm that the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion are untimely, we exercise our discretion to decline to strike the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion. We therefore consider the Demurrer and Defendant Motion on their merits.

 

B.   The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)

 

Defendant submits with each of the Demurrer and Defendant’s Motion a declaration by his counsel explaining that she reached out to Plaintiffs’ counsel by e-mail in an attempt to meet and confer and that Plaintiffs’ counsel responded by e-mail declining to amend the Complaint. (Volk Decl. ¶¶ 2–3.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendant thus admits to having met and conferred. However, an e-mail exchange is insufficient to meet this requirement.

 

Parties have not met and conferred. We remind both Parties of their obligation to meet and confer with each other in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

A.   Plaintiffs’ Motion to Strike

 

Plaintiffs moves to strike the Demurrer, Defendant’s Motion, and “related papers.” (Plaintiffs’ Notice of Motion p. 2.)

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) California Code of Civil Procedure § 435, subdivision (a)(2) defines a “pleading” as “a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint.”

 

Thus, Plaintiffs may not move to strike Defendant’s Motion but may move to strike only the Demurrer.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion raises the same arguments that Plaintiffs raised in their objection to the declaration of Defendant’s counsel in support of a 30-day extension of time in which to file a demurrer or motion to strike. Plaintiffs contend that the Parties met and conferred before Defendant’s counsel’s declaration, that Defendant’s counsel interchanges the words “demurrer” and “motion to strike,” and that her declaration was untimely. We consider two of these arguments above and decline to strike the Demurrer on those grounds.

 

Plaintiffs point out that “[Defendant’s counsel’s] Declaration states under penalty of perjury that she intends to file a ‘demurrer.’ However, [Defendant’s counsel’s] meet and confer letter dated March 14, 2024, only contemplated a motion to strike.”

 

Defendant’s counsel’s declaration in support of a 30-day extension of time in which to file a demurrer or motion to strike extends the time in which Defendant may file a “responsive pleading.” Moreover, these errors are not grounds on which a motion to strike may be granted under California Code of Civil Procedure § 436.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Plaintiffs’ Motion.

 

B.   The Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to both of the Complaint’s causes of action on the grounds that neither “sets forth [any] factual allegations to show [Plaintiffs] are entitled to an allegation of punitive damages.” (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

A motion to strike, not a demurrer, is the appropriate means to attack punitive damages in a pleading. (Grieves v. Superior Court¿(1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 163 [“Since a demurrer¿does not lie to a¿part¿of a cause of action, petitioners' punitive damage allegations were not subject to real parties' demurrers. There is no cause of action for punitive¿damages. Punitive or exemplary damages are remedies available to a party who can plead and prove the facts and circumstances. Punitive damages are merely incident to a cause of action, and can never constitute the basis thereof."].)

 

The Demurrer states that it is brought on the grounds that “the Court does not have jurisdiction over the claims asserted; that [the Complaint] does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, or which contains allegations rendering the cause of action uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible.” (Demurrer p. 6.) However, the Demurrer does not argue any of these grounds beyond its challenge to the Complaint’s prayer for punitive damages, which is not properly the subject of a demurrer.

 

Defendant’s reply to Plaintiffs’ opposition to the Demurrer contends that “Plaintiffs failed to provide facts or information to support their causes of action” and that “[t]here are no facts supporting either legal theory or especially the claims against Defendant.” (Reply pp. 2–3.) However, “the general rule of motion practice is that new evidence is not permitted with reply papers” and “[a] trial court has discretion whether to accept arguments or evidence made for the first time in reply.” (Jack v. Ring LLC (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1186, 1210, 1212.) As raising new arguments in reply papers deprives the opposing parties a fair opportunity to respond, we decline to consider new arguments Defendant raises in his reply.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Demurrer.

 

C.   Defendant’s Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike from the Complaint Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages and certain paragraphs containing factual allegations about “lies and misrepresentations” that Plaintiffs allege Defendant made to his insurance company following Parties’ vehicle collision. (Defendant’s Motion pp. 7–9.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.) 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”  (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)  

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves, supra at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of ‘oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].) 

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant “intentionally misrepresented facts regarding the collision to his insurance carrier” and “attempted to shift his responsibility and liability to Plaintiffs by lying about the facts of the cause of the collision to his insurance carrier.” (Complaint p. 5.) The Complaint also alleges that Defendant’s “lies and misrepresentations were made with a discriminatory intent to cause, or with a reckless disregard for the probability to cause, Plaintiffs humiliation, mental anguish, and substantial and enduring emotional distress.” (Id. at p. 6.) The Complaint alleges that Defendant acted “with the knowledge that Plaintiffs’ emotional and physical distress would thereby increase, and with a wanton and reckless disregard for the deleterious consequences to Plaintiffs.” (Ibid.) The Complaint thus alleges that Defendant committed the intentional tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress with either intent to harm Plaintiffs or the knowledge that Plaintiffs’ harm would result from his actions. Following Angie M. v. Superior Court, supra, 37 Cal.App.4th 1217 at 1228 and Taylor v. Superior Court, supra, 24 Cal.3d 890 at 895–96, these allegations adequately plead malice. These allegations about Defendant’s intentions are not conclusory in context because they follow allegations that Defendant’s “lies and misrepresentations” in his report to his insurance carrier contradicted earlier statements he made to Plaintiffs immediately after the vehicle collision, which are facts from which one may infer Defendant’s knowledge or intentionality. (Complaint p. 5.) Thus, the Complaint adequately pleads a basis for punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Defendant’s Motion.
