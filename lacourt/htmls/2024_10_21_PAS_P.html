DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22GDCV01128    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT MARIA RAQUENEL PORTILLO JIMENEZ’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a personal injury action arising from the alleged childhood sexual abuse, sexual battery, assault, and molestation that Plaintiffs Jane Doe K.C. 1 and Jane Doe A.H. 2 (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) suffered at the hands of Defendants Gloria De Los Angeles Trevino Ruiz aka Gloria Trevi (“Trevi”), an individual; Sergio Gustavo Andrade Sanchez aka Sergio Andrade (“Andrade”), an individual; Maria Raquenel Portillo Jimenez aka Mary Boquitas and Mary Raquenel (“Boquitas”), an individual; and DOES 4 through 50 (collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants on March 16, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) Sexual Battery – Civil Code § 1708.5 (Plf. Jane Doe K.C. 1); (2) Sexual Assault (Plf. Jane Doe K.C. 1); (3) Violation of Penal Code § 647.6(a)(1) – Annoying and Molesting a Minor (Plf. Jane Doe K.C. 1); (4) Sexual Battery – Civil Code § 1708.5 (Plf. Jane Doe A.H. 2); (5) Sexual Assault (Plf. Jane Doe A.H. 2); and (6) Violation of Penal Code § 647.6(a)(1) – Annoying and Molesting a Minor (Plf. Jane Doe A.H. 2).

         

          Plaintiffs allege at the age of fifteen (15) and thirteen (13), Jane Doe K.C. 1 and Jane Doe A.H. 2, respectively were groomed, manipulated, exploited, and coerced to engage in sexual contact with Defendants Trevi and Andrade, who are a well-known Mexican pop star and a famous music producer, while touring in California. (FAC ¶2.) Plaintiff Jane Doe K.C. 1 worked as a background dancer for Defendant Trevi beginning in or about November 1991. (Id. at ¶28.) From 1992 until 2000, Defendants Trevi and Andrade coerced Plaintiff Jane Dor K.C.1 to have sexual intercourse with Defendant Andrade and other sexual acts with other girls. (Id. at ¶¶40-53.) In or 1989, Defendant Andrade began sexually assaulting Plaintiff Jane Doe A.H. 2. (Id. at ¶¶126-130.)

 

          On September 1, 2023, Defendant Jimenez filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) against Defendant Andrade, Plaintiffs, ROE J.P. 1, ROE J.P. 2, ROE S.P. 3, ROE J.C.4, ROE S 5, and ROES 6 through 50, inclusive, alleging causes of action for (1) Indemnity; (2) Contribution; (3) Defamation – Libel and Libel Per Se; (4) False Light; and (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. The FACC alleges the allegations made in the FAC about her are false and libelous. (FACC ¶¶98-99.)

 

          On November 16, 2023, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants Jane Doe K.C. 1 and A.H. 2 filed a Special Motion to Strike (Anti-SLAPP) as to the FACC. The motion was fully briefed and came for hearing on December 22, 2023. The Court granted the motion and dismissed with prejudice as to Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants.

 

          On December 27, 2023, Defendant Trevi filed an Answer to the FAC and Cross-Complaint against Defendant Andrade, Plaintiffs, Jane ROE J.P. 1, Conexiones Americanas, S.A. de C.V., and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Sexual Assault; (2) Aiding and Abetting Sexual Assault; (3) Sexual Battery; (4) Aiding and Abetting Sexual Battery; (5) Battery; (6) Aiding and Abetting Battery; (7) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (8) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (9) Indemnity.

 

          On January 12, 2024, the Court found that the Case No. 23GDCV02700 is related to this case and designated this case as the lead.

 

          On January 30, 2024, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed a Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint. The matter was fully briefed and came for hearing on May 1, 2024. The Court overruled the demurrer. On May 13, 2024, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

 

          On April 15, 2024, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed a Motion for Attorney Fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16(c). The matter was fully briefed and came for hearing on July 11, 2024. The Court granted the motion and ordered Defendant Jimenez to pay $37,408.37 in attorney fees to Plaintiffs/Cross-defendant within 30 days of the order.

 

          On July 23, 2024, Defendant Jimenez filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration. On October 8, 2024, Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants filed an Opposition. On October 10, 2024, Defendant Jimenez filed a Reply.

         

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008, subdivision (a) provides in pertinent part, “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

 

As it relates to new or different facts, circumstances, or law under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a), “the moving party must provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Shiffer v. CBS Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 246, 255.) Furthermore, “facts of which the party seeking reconsideration was aware at the time of the original ruling are not “new or different.” (In re Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1468.) 

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          Defendant Jimenez moves for this Court to reconsider and revoke its July 11, 2024, in which Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (c) was granted in the amount of $37,408.37. The motion is made on the grounds that Plaintiffs’ failed to serve and file their fees/costs motion within the 60 days after Notice of Entry of the Order granting the anti-SLAPP motion per California Rules of Court, rule 3.1702, subdivision (b). Defendant Jimenez contends she did not discover this rule until after July 11, 2024.

         

          In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Defendant Jimenez improperly asserts an alternative legal argument without establishing either new or different law or meeting the diligence requirement applicable to motions for reconsideration under Section 1008, subdivision (a). Moreover, Plaintiffs contend this present motion is untimely because it was filed eleven days after service of the notice of entry of the July 11, 2024 order. Plaintiffs also contend their fees/costs motion was not untimely under rule 3.1702 because the first and second causes of action of the Cross-Complaint are still pending against the other Cross-Defendants.

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that the instant motion is untimely. A motion for reconsideration of the July 11, 2024 order was supposed to be filed by July 22, 2024 because the tenth day fell on July 21, 2024, which is a Sunday. Even if the motion was timely, it would still fail for the substantive reasons set forth below.

 

In Baldwin v. Home Sav. of America (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, the court held “the diligence requirement applicable to motions to reconsider on the basis of different facts is also applicable to motions to reconsider on the basis of different law.” (Baldwin v. Home Sav. of America (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1200.) Here, Defendant Jimenez does not provide a satisfactory explanation as to why the argument regarding the timeliness of Plaintiffs’ fees/costs motion under rule 3.1702, subdivision (b) was not presented earlier such as in her original opposition. Defendant Jimenez merely asserts the timing requirements of rule 3.1702 were not apparent at the time of the original hearing.

 

Next, Plaintiffs’ fees/costs motion was timely pursuant to rule 3.1702, which states, “A notice of motion to claim attorney’s fees for services up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court--including attorney’s fees on an appeal before the rendition of judgment in the trial court--must be served and filed within the time for filing a notice of appeal under rules 8.104 and 8.108 in an unlimited civil case or under rules 8.822 and 8.823 in a limited civil case.” (CRC, Rule 3.1702, subd. (b).) Furthermore, California Rules of Court, rule 8.104, subdivision (a)(1) states: “Unless a statute or rules 8.108, 8.702, or 8.712 provide otherwise, a notice of appeal must be filed on or before the earliest of: (A) 60 days after the superior court clerk serves on the party filing the notice of appeal a document entitled “Notice of Entry” of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, showing the date either was served; (B) 60 days after the party filing the notice of appeal serves or is served by a party with a document entitled “Notice of Entry” of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, accompanied by proof of service; or (C) 180 days after entry of judgment.” (CRC, Rule 8.104, subd. (a)(1).)

 

Neither party disputes that Plaintiffs’ fees/costs motion concerned fees incurred in connection to their special motion to strike (anti-SLAPP motion). The July 11, 2024 order granted Plaintiff’s fees/costs motion. “A motion for attorney fees incurred in connection with a prejudgment appealable order, such as an order granting or denying a special motion to strike, is a “claim for services” rendered before “the rendition of judgment.” It is not a claim “for services up to and including the rendition of judgment,” and therefore does not fit within the plain language of rule 3.1702.” (Carpenter v. Jack in the Box Corp. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 454, 464.)

 

As such, “the time limits imposed by rules 3.1702 and 8.104 for filing a motion for attorney fees under section 425.16, subdivision (c) do not commence to run until entry of judgment at the conclusion of the litigation. This interpretation of rule 3.1702 is consistent not only with the rule's history and underlying policy, but also with existing law and practice.” (Id. at 468.) The Cross-Complaint which was the subject of the special motion to strike is still pending as to the first and second causes of action, thus there has been no rendition of judgment or conclusion of litigation. Defendant Jimenez does not cite to any published case law or statutory authority indicating otherwise.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, Defendant Maria Raquenel Portillo Jimenez’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On November 9, 2023, respective defaults were entered against Defendants Li and Chang.

          On March 27, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Margaret L. Oldendorf to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.

          On April 3, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to continue prove up hearing. (04/03/24 Minute Order.) The Court continued the hearing from April 15, 2024, to June 24, 2024. (04/03/24 Minute Order.) Plaintiff was “directed to bring a proposed Judgement and all supporting documents to the next hearing.” (04/03/24 Minute Order.)

          On June 10, 2024, the Court, on its own motion, continued the default prove up hearing from June 24, 2024 to July 23, 2024.   

          On July 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a prove-up hearing brief.

          On July 23, 2024, the Court continued the default prove up hearing to October 21, 2024 due to a county wide network outage. (07/23/24 Minute Order.)  

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD     

A party seeking default judgment must comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1800. In support of a default judgment application, a party must file the following: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney fees if allowed by statute or by agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1800(a)(1)-(9).)  

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          Plaintiff has not filed a default judgment application. Plaintiff has not filed: (1) a signed memorandum of costs and disbursements; (2) a declaration of non-military status; (3) a request for court judgment form on CIV-100; (4) a proposed form of judgment; (5) a declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; (6) interest computations; (7) properly authenticated exhibits; or (8) a summary of the case. Plaintiff has also not dismissed any of the Doe defendants. The Court cannot grant default judgment until Plaintiff files a sufficient default judgment application. Plaintiff has not satisfied the requirements of Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800. A default prove-up brief is not a default judgment application.

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, the Court CONTINUES the default-prove up hearing to Friday, December 13, 2024, at 8:30 AM in this department. Plaintiff is to file a complete default judgment application at least five court days prior to the continued hearing date.

Case Number: 23AHCV02226    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION ATTENDANCE OF A PERSON MOST QUALIFIED AND CUSTODIAN OF RECORDS OF DEFENDANT AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC., AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS


I.        INTRODUCTION

          On October 9, 2018, Plaintiff Jose Garcia Ruiz (“Plaintiff”) purchased a new 2019 Honda Pilot (the “Vehicle”), which was subject to a warranty by Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”).

On September 26, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint alleging causes of action for: (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act—breach of express warranty; and (2) fraudulent concealment.

On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) violation of Song-Beverly Act—breach of express warranty; and (2) fraudulent inducement—concealment. Plaintiff alleges that the Vehicle suffered from a transmission defect, which caused the Vehicle to “judder” and caused the Subject Vehicle to display issues with acceleration. (FAC, ¶ 13.)  

          On March 27, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Margaret L. Oldendorf to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.   

          On July 10, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC and denied Defendant’s motion to strike. (07/10/24 Minute Order.)

          Also, on July 10, 2024, Defendant filed its answer to the complaint.

          On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified (PMQ) and Custodian of Records for deposition and for the production of documents at deposition. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record in the sum of $3,120.00.

          On October 7, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition to the motion, to which Plaintiff replied on October 14, 2024.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD     

In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” (John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.) “When discovery requests are grossly overbroad on their face, and hence do not appear reasonably related to a legitimate discovery need, a reasonable inference can be drawn of an intent to harass and improperly burden.” (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.) 

“The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)  

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)   

A motion made pursuant to Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450 must “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) A court may limit the frequency or extent of discovery of electronically stored information if “[t]he likely burden or expense of the proposed discovery outweighs the likely benefit, taking into account the amount in controversy, the resources of the parties, the importance of the issues in the litigation, and the importance of the requested discovery in resolving the issues.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (f)(4).) “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

         

A.   Defects in the Notice of Motion

Defendant contends that the notice of motion does not indicate that Plaintiff seeks

the production of documents at deposition. Plaintiff argues that the notice of motion is not deficient.

          “A notice of motion must state in the opening paragraph the nature of the order being sought and the grounds for issuance of the order.” (Cal. R. Ct., rule 3.1110(a).) “[D]ue process requires a party to be fully advised of the issues to be addressed and be given adequate notice of what facts it must rebut in order to prevail.” (Fenn v. Sherriff (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1481.)

          Plaintiff’s counsel, Timothy Lupinek (“Lupinek”), states that on May 2, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with an Amended Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records (the “Deposition Notice”). (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. A.) The Deposition Notice identified 53 matters for examination and 53 requests for document production.[1] (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. A.) Defendant objected to the Deposition Notice and indicated that it would not produce a witness on the July 8, 2024, noticed deposition date. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. C.) Defendant did not appear at the July 8, 2024, deposition. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 8.)

          Plaintiff is seeking to compel the testimony of Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records, as well as the production of documents at deposition. (See Memo. of Ps and As at p. 8:12-14; see also Separate Statement at pp. 64-135.) However, the notice of motion only indicates that Plaintiff is seeking to compel Defendant to produce a PMQ and Custodian of Records at deposition. (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:5-7.) The notice of motion does not set forth that Plaintiff is seeking to also compel the production of documents at deposition. (Not. of Mot. at p. 1.) The Court finds that the notice of motion is deficient as it concerns Plaintiff’s request to compel the production of documents at deposition. Plaintiff should have specifically indicated that it sought to compel production of documents at deposition in the notice of motion.

          Even if Plaintiff had requested to compel the production of documents in a procedurally compliant manner, such request would have been denied as the declaration of Mr. Lupinek is void of any facts showing good cause to compel the production of documents at deposition.

          The Court therefore DENIES Plaintiff’s request to compel the production of documents at deposition pursuant to the Deposition Notice.

 

B.          Plaintiff Has Shown that Defendant Did Not Appear for Examination

In its second and final argument in opposition to the motion, Defendant contends

that Plaintiff’s motion is improperly brought under CCP § 2025.450 because Defendant did not fail to appear for examination. (Opp’n at p. 3:4-20.)

          The Court rejects Defendant’s argument and finds that the motion is properly brought under CCP § 2025.450. Mr. Lupinek has provided a declaration attesting that Defendant did not appear at the July 8, 2024, deposition. Further, CCP § 2025.450 does not require a declaration from Plaintiff’s counsel as to the taking of a non-appearance as Defendant contends in the opposition brief. (Opp’n at p. 3:10-14.) Plaintiff’s counsel inquired about Defendant’s non-appearance at the deposition. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 8; Ex. B.) Plaintiff has therefore met the requirements of CCP § 2025.450(b)(2) and the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

          The Court will now address whether Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records should be deposed on the matters for examination set forth in the Deposition Notice.

 

C.         The Matters for Examination in the Deposition Notice are Relevant 

Plaintiff seeks to examine Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records on 53

categories. Categories No. 1-27 seek information related to the torque converter lock-up clutch defect in the Vehicle. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at pp. 5:4-7:21.) Categories No. 28 and 29 seek information as to Defendant’s efforts to preserve discoverable information and efforts to search for documents and information responsive to Plaintiff’s discovery requests in this matter. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at p. 7:22-28.) Categories No. 30-38 seek information pertaining to the Vehicle such as warranty claims, repairs, and requests for repurchase. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at p. 8:1-14.) Category No. 39 seeks all documents produced by Defendant thus far in this litigation. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at p. 8:15.) Categories No. 40-50 seek information as to Defendant’s policies and procedures concerning matters such as repair attempts, vehicle replacement eligibility, and training for evaluating a vehicle repurchase. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at p. 8:16-9:12.) Categories No. 51, 52, and 53 seek information about databases for vehicle warranty data, methodology for categorizing and tracking such data, and the searchability of such databases. (Lupinek Decl., Ex. A at p. 9:13-17.)

          The Court finds that the matters for examination seek relevant information as such information might assist Plaintiff in evaluating his case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546 (“information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement . . . .”).) The matters on which the PMQ and Custodian of Records are to be deposed go to Plaintiff’s claims in this action. Although it objected to the Deposition Notice, Defendant has not justified any of its objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

          The Court therefore finds that Defendant should be compelled to produce a PMQ and Custodian of Records to testify at deposition on the matters for examination, nos. 1-53, listed in the Deposition Notice.

 

D.         Monetary Sanctions 

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary

sanction . . . in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

          Mr. Lupinek declares that he spent a total of seven (7) hours in connection with the instant motion at the rate of $450.00 per hour. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 10.) A $60.00 filing fee was incurred for the motion. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 10.) Plaintiff therefore requests monetary sanctions of $3,120.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record, Nelson Mullins Riley & Scarborough LLP. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 10.)

          The Court finds that monetary sanctions are appropriate. Defendant did not produce its PMQ and Custodian of Records for deposition. The Court GRANTS IN PART Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions and AWARDS Plaintiff reasonable monetary sanctions in the amount of $960.00 (representing two (2) hours of work on the motion at the rate of $450 per hour plus the $60 filing fee). The Court reduces the hours given the straightforward nature of the motion and the lack of complexity. Monetary sanctions are to be paid by Defendant to Plaintiff as CCP § 2025.450(g)(1) does not authorize the imposition of monetary sanctions against Defendant’s counsel.

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records of Defendant, and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request to compel the production of documents at deposition.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to compel Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records to be deposed on matters for examination, nos. 1-53, set forth in the Deposition Notice. The Court ORDERS that the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ and Custodian of Records occur within 45 days of the date of notice of this order.

Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART. Defendant is ORDERED to pay monetary sanctions to Plaintiff in the amount of $960.00 within 30 days of the date of notice of this order.

          Plaintiff Jose Garcia Ruiz to give notice of this order.
