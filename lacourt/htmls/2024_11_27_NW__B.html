DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV27671    Hearing Date: November 27, 2024    Dept: B

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DISMISSAL

Date of Hearing: November 27, 2024 Trial Date: None

Department: B Case No.: 19STCV27671

Moving Party: Advanced Fresh Concepts Franchise Corp.

Responding Party: None

BACKGROUND

On August 7, 2019, Plaintiff Victor Naing (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Ralph’s Grocery Company (“Ralph’s”), Kroger Company (“Kroger”), and Does 1 to 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for premises liability. The complaint alleges that, on or about August 12, 2017, Plaintiff slipped and fell on an icy patch caused by Defendants’ negligence, resulting in injuries.

On August 8, 2022, Ralph’s filed a cross-complaint against Advanced Fresh Concepts Franchise Corp. (“Fresh Concepts”) and Roes 1 to 10 for indemnity, contribution and apportionment, express indemnity, and declaratory relief.

On February 6, 2024, Fresh Concepts filed a cross-complaint against Michelle Chen Naing (“Michelle”) and Roes 1 to 20 for express and equitable indemnity.

On May 9, 2024, Michelle entered default.

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On July 18, 2024, Fresh Concepts reached a settlement with Plaintiff.

On August 30, 2024, the Court set a case management conference (“CMC”) and order to show cause (“OSC”) re: dismissal for November 6, 2024. On October 9, 2024, the Court issued a notice of continuance moving the OSC up one week to October 29, 2024.

Counsel for Fresh Concepts did not appear at the October 29, 2024, hearing. As a result, the Court dismissed Fresh Concept’s cross-complaint without prejudice.

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to set aside the dismissal.

LEGAL STANDARD

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) An application for relief under this section must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Additionally, an application for relief under this section “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed herein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.” (CCP § 473(b).) Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) When dismissal is the attorney’s fault, the six-month period starts to run from the date of the entry of dismissal. (CCP § 473(b); Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 295.)

DISCUSSION

Here, based on the declaration of Fresh Concepts’s counsel, Fresh Concepts failed to appear at the October 29, 2024, hearing due to a calendaring error by Counsel Linwood Warren Jr. (Warren Decl. ¶ 8.) Counsel declares this was an excusable mistake based on the Court advancing the OSC hearing date. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7 and 8.) Thus, Fresh Concepts argues that mandatory relief is warranted under CCP§ 473(b). (Motion at p. 2:2-5.)

Under the circumstances, the Court finds that Fresh Concepts’s argument has merit. Courts have found excusable neglect where the attorneys relied on a member of their staff “to perform certain tasks, including calendaring deadlines, and the staff member errs.” (Renteria v. Juvenile Justice, Dept. of Corrections and Rehabilitation (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 903, 911; Bergloff v. Reynolds (1960) 181 Cal.App.2d 349, 358-359.) Therefore, the Court finds that Fresh Concepts is entitled to relief under the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b).

Accordingly, the Court grants the instant motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b).

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Fresh Concepts’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is GRANTED. Court to sign proposed order.

Case Number: 21STCV40027    Hearing Date: November 27, 2024    Dept: B

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

Date of Hearing: November 27, 2024 Trial Date: December 16, 2024

Department: B Case No.: 21STCV40027

Moving Party: Rabin Saidian

Responding Party: None

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a motor vehicle accident. On November 1, 2021, Plaintiffs Romero Brigarde (“Brigarde”) and Benjamin Wilson (“Wilson”)(collectively “Plaintiffs”), filed a complaint against Defendants Felix Trinidad (“Trinidad”), Mike’s Performance Exhaust (“MPE”), Miguel A. Vaca (“Vaca”), and Does 1 to 50 alleging three causes of action: (1) negligence (as to all defendants); (2) battery (as to Trinidad only); and (3) assault (as to Trinidad only).

On June 7, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 29, 2024, Rabin Saidian, Esq. (“Counsel”) filed and served the instant unopposed Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff Benjamin Wilson.

[TENTATIVE] RULING

The Court DENIES the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel filed by Rabin Saidian, Esq.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 284(2).) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.)

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1362(a)), MC-052 (Declaration) (CRC Rule 3.1362(c)), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) (CRC Rule 3.1362(e)). The proposed order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. (CRC Rule 3.1362(e).)

Further, the requisite forms must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (CRC Rule 3.1362(d).) The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (CRC Rule 3.1362(e).) The Court has discretion on whether to allow an attorney to withdraw, and a motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

DISCUSSION

Counsel declares that irreconcilable conflicts have arisen between lawyer and client. (MC-052 Item 2.) Counsel has filed and served Plaintiffs with the required documents under CRC Rule 3.1362. However, the final status conference and trial are set for December 6 and December 16, 2024, respectively. Plaintiffs would be severely prejudiced by Counsel’s relief as trial is just a few weeks away. Therefore, Counsel’s motion to be relieved is denied.

CONCLUSION

The Court DENIES the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel filed by Rabin Saidan, Esq.

Case Number: 22STCV05553    Hearing Date: November 27, 2024    Dept: B

BACKGROUND

On March 28, 2024, Plaintiff Glendis Beker (“Plaintiff”) initially noticed the deposition of Defendant Hertz Corporation (“Hertz”)’s employee, Darren Bibbins (“Bibbins”), for April 11, 2024. (Movsesian Decl. ¶ 6; Exh. C [notice of deposition].) On April 8, 2024, Hertz served an objection, which Plaintiff claims was untimely (Ibid. at ¶ 7; Exh. D [Hertz response].) On April 11, 2024, neither Hertz nor Bibbins appeared for the noticed deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 8; Exh. E [notice of non-appearance].)

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Hertz a meet and confer correspondence regarding Bibbins’s non-appearance. (Movsesian Decl. ¶ 9; Exh. F [correspondence].) Hertz’s counsel responded that their objection to the deposition was timely and that Bibbins was an inappropriate witness. (Ibid.; Exh. G [Hertz response].)

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff sent Hertz another meet and confer correspondence, explaining why their objection was untimely and why Bibbins was an appropriate witness. (Movsesian Decl. ¶ 10; Exh. H [correspondence].) On multiple occasions thereafter, counsels for Plaintiff and Hertz meet and conferred as to the legal basis for Bibbins’s deposition. To date, Hertz refuses to submit Bibbins to a deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 15.)

LEGAL STANDARD

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

CCP § 2025.450 provides in pertinent part the following:

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

CCP § 1987.1(a) further states, “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.” (CCP § 1987.1(a).) “[U]pon motion reasonably made by the party, judges may rule upon motions for quashing, modifying or compelling compliance with, subpoenas.” (Lee v. Swansboro Country Property Owners Ass'n (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 575, 582-583.)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff’s Argument

Plaintiff argues that good cause exists to depose Bibbins because Hertz’s responses to Plaintiff’s first set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production, and Requests for Admissions were verified by Bibbins. (Movsesian Decl. ¶¶ 3 and 4; Exh. A [Plaintiff’s discovery requests]; Exh. B [Hertz’s responses].) This verification included Bibbins’s declaration that the “matters stated in the foregoing document are true of [his] own knowledge except as to those matters which are stated on information and belief . . .” (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)

Most responses were not made upon information and belief. Therefore, Plaintiff argues, Bibbins has information that is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence pursuant to CCP § 2017.010, et seq, making Bibbins’s deposition both appropriate and necessary. (Movsesian Decl. ¶ 5.)

Hertz’s Argument

In its November 14, 2024, opposition, Hertz argues that Plaintiff’s motion should be denied because Bibbins is not a party to the action, nor is he an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party. (Opposition p. 2:3-4.) Therefore, Hertz argues, Plaintiff has no authority to depose Bibbins pursuant to CCP § 2025.450.

Hertz notes that Plaintiff erroneously identified Bibbins as an employee of Defendant, when he is employed as a claims representative by ESIS TPA, an insurance adjuster. (Tafarella Decl. ¶ 4.) Therefore, Hertz argues, Bibbins can only be properly deposed through a notice of deposition of Hertz’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) or Person Most Qualified (“PMQ”). (Opposition pp. 3:26-27 and 4:1-2.)

Additionally, Hertz argues that their opposition to Plaintiff’s March 28, 2024, deposition notice was timely. Hertz electronically served their objection three calendar days prior to the noticed deposition date. (Opposition p. 5:2-4.)

Timeliness

CCP § 2025.410 states that objections to noticed depositions must be served at least three calendar days prior to the noticed deposition date. (CCP § 2025.410.) Pursuant to CCP § 1010.6, electronic service of a document is “deemed complete at the time of the electronic transmission of the document or at the time that the electronic notification of service of the document is sent.” (CCP § 1010.6.) Hertz electronically served their objection on April 8, 2024,

three days prior to the deposition date of April 11, 2024. (Opposition p. 5:2-4.) Therefore, Hertz timely served their objection pursuant to CCP § 2025.410.

Good Cause

The Court now addresses whether Plaintiff has good cause to depose Bibbins pursuant to CCP § 2025.450.

Bibbins is not a party to the action nor an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under CCP § 2025.230. (CCP § 2025.450.) Plaintiff is certainly entitled to seek the deposition of the person most knowledgeable as to Hertz’s discovery responses, or, in the alternative can serve a deposition subpoena on Bibbins but cannot compel his deposition pursuant to CCP 2025.450.

CCP § 2025.230 states, in pertinent part:

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

CONCLUSION

Hertz timely served an objection to Plaintiff’s notice of deposition for Mr. Bibbins. Additionally, Plaintiff improperly noticed that deposition pursuant to CCP § 2025.450.

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.
