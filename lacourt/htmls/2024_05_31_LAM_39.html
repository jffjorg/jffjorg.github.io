DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV20862    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

May 20

May 31, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

20STCV20862

MOTION: 

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

MOVING PARTIES:

Plaintiff Outsource, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiff Outsource, LLC (“Plaintiff”) prevailed on its breach of contract claim against Defendant Horizon Communications Technologies, Inc. (“Defendant”).  Now, Plaintiff seeks an award of attorney fees.  The court previously considered the matter, and in its order of March 7, 2024, continued the matter to this date.

 

ANALYSIS

 The prevailing party must seek an award of attorney fees by way of a noticed motion and bears the burden of proof to justify the amount sought.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5)(A). Plaintiff claims it is entitled to attorney fees based on the payment plan into which the parties entered.  The plan provides that if Defendant defaults in making payments, Plaintiff is “entitled to recover the reasonable attorney’s fees and costs incurred in enforcing its rights under this Agreement.”  (Declaration of Dayton B. Parcells, III, Exhibit 2, Para. 2D.)  Plaintiff is, therefore, entitled to recover fees Plaintiff incurred in prevailing on Plaintiff’s contract claims against Defendant.  (See Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 615.)  However, Plaintiff’s billing records include substantial fees Plaintiff incurred in defending against the claim of third-party claimant Sunwest Bank.  Plaintiff is not entitled to recover those fees from Defendant, as those fees do not stem from the contract claims between Plaintiff and Defendant.  In its order of March 7, 2024, the Court ordered Plaintiff to lodge supplemental evidence in support of its motion, in which it indicates which fees Plaintiff incurred in prosecuting its breach of contract claims against Defendant.  Plaintiff provided no supplemental filing.  Additionally, at no point has Plaintiff contended the contract into which it entered with Defendant provides for an award of attorney fees for work its counsel performs related to defending a claim made by a third party not bound by the subject contract.  As this court is unable to discern what amount of attorney fees would be appropriate based on the work Plaintiff’s counsel performed with respect to Plaintiff’s contractual claim against Defendant, the motion is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 20STCV25567    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

May 31, 2024

CASE NUMBER

20STCV25567

MOTIONS

Motion to Set Aside Default

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Paul Kozlov

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff the Hakala Law Group, P.C.

 

MOTION

Defendant Paul Kozlov (“Defendant”) moves to set aside the default and default judgment that Plaintiff Hakala Law Group, P.C. (“Plaintiff”) took against Defendant.  In its order of February 9, 2024, the Court continued the matter to this date, and construed the motion as a motion to enforce settlement under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.  Per this Court’s order of February 9, 2024, the parties filed further briefing, which the Court now considers.

 

ANALYSIS

            Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides that “[i]f parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6.)  In ruling on a motion to enter judgment, the court acts as a trier of fact.  The court must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement agreement.  To do so, the Court may receive oral testimony in addition to declarations.  (Kohn v. Jaymar-Ruby, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1530, 1533.)

            Defendant submits the declaration of his counsel, Fahim Farivar (“Counsel”).  Counsel states Plaintiff and Defendant reached a settlement of this action and finalized a written agreement reflecting that settlement on April 6, 2022.  The settlement agreement provides Defendant would pay $32,717.56 to Plaintiff in multiple payments.  As of the settlement date, Defendant had already paid $17,717.56 to Plaintiff.  Defendant agreed to pay the remaining balance in a series of nine monthly installments of $1,666.67 for the first eight months, and then one payment of $1,666.64.  The first payment was due within seven calendar days of the settlement agreement, or by April 13, 2022, and thereafter no later than the 13th of each consecutive month.  The parties agreed if Plaintiff contended Defendant missed any payments, Plaintiff would notify Defendant in writing, and Defendant would have five business days to cure any missed payment.  If Defendant failed to make timely payments on three occasions, Defendant would automatically be in default of the agreement and owe $118,007.35, less any payments Defendant had already made.  In exchange, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss all claims against Defendant with prejudice.  (Declaration of Fahim Farivar, Exhibit A.)  Plaintiff claims Defendant missed the first payment, but acknowledged Defendant cured this failure.  (Declaration of Fahim Farivar, Exhibit B.)  Defendant presents evidence another payor made the May 2022 payment on his behalf on May 16, 2022, which was again untimely.  However, Defendant eventually made all monthly payments by December 2022.  

            Plaintiff states Defendant’s September 2022 payment was also untimely.  (Declaration of Brad A. Hakala, ¶ 8.)  As such, Plaintiff contends Defendant defaulted on the settlement agreement.  However, the evidence before the court reflects Plaintiff likewise failed to satisfy his obligations set forth in the settlement agreement.  If Defendant defaulted under the settlement agreement, Plaintiff was to file the stipulated judgment the parties executed in connection with the settlement.  (Declaration of Fahim Farivar, Exhibit A.)  But Plaintiff failed to advise the court of the settlement agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant and sought to collect on the default judgment against Defendant as if the settlement agreement did not exist, thereby violating Plaintiff’s duties to the court. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1385, subd. (a)(1).)  The court finds, by failing to proceed according to the terms of the settlement agreement, Plaintiff waived the right to hold Defendant in default of the settlement agreement.   (Zamora v. Lehman (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1, 12.)  Thus, the court grants Defendant’s motion to enforce the settlement agreement and Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant are dismissed with prejudice.

            The settlement agreement provides Defendant is entitled to recover his fees and costs in enforcing the agreement.  Defendant has provided a detailed accounting of such fees and costs. (See Exhibits E and F attached to the Declaration of Fahim Farivar in support of Plaintiff’s Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement and Request for Attorney’s Fees and Costs.)  Plaintiff has offered no opposition to Defendant’s request. The court grants Defendant’s request for attorney fees and costs in the amount of $22,098.50. 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Associated Ready Mixed Concrete, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Xavier Sosa Duran

 

MOTION

              Defendant Associated Ready Mix Concrete, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration of the claims Plaintiff Xavier Sosa Duran (“Plaintiff”) asserts against Defendant.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

 

ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotation omitted.) 

Defendant advances multiple arbitration agreements Plaintiff signed while working for Defendant.  The most recent agreement is dated April 27, 2018.  The Court assumes this agreement supersedes prior agreements, as the parties do not argue otherwise.  While Plaintiff points out that Plaintiff did not sign an arbitration agreement that Defendant presented to Plaintiff after the April 27, 2018 agreement, Plaintiff’s failure to sign a later arbitration agreement has no impact on whether the April 27, 2018 arbitration binds Plaintiff.  The April 27, 2018 arbitration agreement provides Plaintiff and Defendant agree to arbitrate “any legally actionable dispute . . . which cannot be resolved by discussion . . . .”  (Declaration of Jaret M. Ramirez in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit 4.)  Accordingly, Defendants meet their burden to show an agreement to arbitrate covers Plaintiff’s claims. 

The arbitration agreement specifies the arbitration will be under “the Federal Arbitration Act . . . if applicable . . . .”  (Declaration of Jaret M. Ramirez in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit 4.)  The party that contends the Federal Arbitration Act applies bears the burden of demonstrating the arbitration agreement is in a “‘contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce’ . . . .” (Woolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211; 9 U.S.C. § 2.)  Defendant advances no evidence showing the Federal Arbitration Act applies, other than its misleading quotation of the arbitration agreement to omit the qualification that the Federal Arbitration Act applies if applicable.  As such, Defendant fails to demonstrate the Federal Arbitration Act governs.  Accordingly, California law applies. 

As California law applies, the arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant must meet the rules set forth in Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83 (Armendariz).  Statutory claims an employee brings against an employer are arbitrable if the arbitration agreement provides for a neutral arbitrator, discovery, a written decision from the arbitrator, and limitations on the expenses.  (Id. at p. 102.)  The arbitration agreement involving these parties specifies the employment arbitration rules of the Judicial Arbitration and Mediation Services, Inc., or JAMS, apply.  It further states if the parties cannot agree on an arbitrator, the parties will select an arbitrator by the JAMS rules.  (Declaration of Jaret M. Ramirez in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit 4.)  It therefore provides for a neutral arbitrator.  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 15, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Pursuant to JAMS rules, the parties are entitled to depositions and written discovery.  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 17, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Likewise, as the arbitration agreement states JAMS rules apply, it provides for a written decision.  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 24, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Further, Plaintiff will not be responsible for more in fees than Plaintiff would be in Court.  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 31, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Therefore, the  arbitration agreement complies with Armendariz. 

Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is unconscionable.  “If the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract, or it may enforce the remainder of the contract without the unconscionable clause, or it may so limit the application of any unconscionable clause as to avoid any unconscionable result.”  (Civ. Code,  1670.5, subd. (a).)  Unconscionability is defined as ‘“an absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms which are unreasonably favorable to the other party.”’  (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corporation. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486 (A & M Produce).) 

Unconscionability thus has a procedural and a substantive element.  (A & M Produce, supra, 135 Cal.App.3d at p. 486.)  The court considers the factors of oppression and surprise in determining whether a contract is procedurally unconscionable.  (Ibid.)  Oppression refers to inequality of bargaining power, which cuts off meaningful negotiation.  (Ibid.)  Surprise refers to the concealment of the agreed-upon terms in a lengthy form drafted by the party seeking to enforce them.  (Ibid.)  Substantive unconscionability refers to the extent to which a contractual term reallocates the risks of a bargain “in a[n] objectively unreasonable or unexpected or unexpected manner.” (Id. at 487.)  Both substantive and procedural unconscionability must be present for a contract to be unenforceable, and “the greater the unfair surprise or inequality of bargaining power, the less unreasonable the risk reallocation which will be tolerated.” (Ibid.)

            The evidence before the court shows Defendant prepared the arbitration agreement, and Plaintiff had no opportunity to negotiate or discuss its terms.  The agreement is, therefore, a contract of adhesion.  (See Neal v. State Farm Insurance Companies (1961) 188 Cal.App.2d 690, 695.)  This constitutes some evidence of procedural unconscionability.  (Flores v. Transamerica HomeFirst, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 846, 853.)  Further, Defendant incorporated the JAMS rules by reference, which is further evidence of procedural unconscionability.  (See Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1246.)

Plaintiff has not, however, demonstrated the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable.  Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable because it requires Plaintiff to arbitrate claims but permits Defendant to seek relief in court.  The arbitration agreement excludes certain claims, including claims that Plaintiff may bring.  (Declaration of Jaret M. Ramirez in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit 4.)  As such, this is not evidence of unconscionability.

Plaintiff points out the waiver of Plaintiff’s ability to bring a class action or representative action is unenforceable.  (Muro v. Cornerstone Staffing Solutions, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 784, 788; Iskanian v. CLS Transp. Los Angeles, LLC (2014) 59 Cal.4th 348, 360.)  However, Plaintiff does not assert class action or representative claims.  As such, the enforceability of the waivers is irrelevant.

Plaintiff further argues the discovery rules are lopsided in favor of Defendant.  But they are not.  Pursuant to the JAMS rules, each party is entitled to one deposition as a matter of right.  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 17, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Plaintiff argues that this rule favors Defendant, as Defendant could use that deposition to depose Plaintiff, whereas Plaintiff must “make the difficult decision as to who he would depose.”  (Opposition to Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Action, p. 15.)  If Plaintiff notices Defendant’s deposition, however, Defendant must produce a witness knowledgeable to testify about pertinent issues.  Further, Plaintiff may request the arbitrator “grant a request for additional depositions, based upon the reasonable need for the requested information, the availability of other discovery and the burdensomeness of the request on the opposing Parties and the witness.”  (Employment Arbitration Rules & Procedures (June 1, 2021) Rule 17, at <https://www.jamsadr.com/rules-employment-arbitration/english> [as of May 16, 2024].)  Likewise, Plaintiff may obtain “discovery of third parties with the approval of the Arbitrator.”  (Ibid.)  “[A]rbitration is meant to be a streamlined procedure.  Limitations on discovery, including the number of depositions, is one of the ways streamlining is achieved.  [D]iscovery limitations are an integral and permissible part of the arbitration process.”  (Dotson v. Amgen, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 975, 983.)  As Plaintiff fails to show the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable, the agreement is enforceable.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate is granted.  This case is stayed pending arbitration.  The Court will set an order to show cause regarding the status of arbitration on a date to be specified. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of same.
