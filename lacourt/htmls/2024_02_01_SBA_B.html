[Tentative] RULING – Carruth Motion

 

 

ROBERT CARRUTH, et al.,

Hearing Date:                          February 1, 2024

Tentative Ruling:                  Carruth Motion

Moving Parties:                     Defendants Robert Carruth, et al.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Joseph Loh

Motion to Enforce Terms of Settlement (filed on September 13, 2023)

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers and notice of errata.  The hearing was continued from October 27, 2023 for oral argument.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The court orders that plaintiff Joseph Loh pay $22,500 in penalties to defendants for violation of Para. L of the settlement agreement.  The court orders that plaintiff Loh comply with Para. E within five days by removing “personal items,” including sandbags and pavers from the compost area.

            Plaintiff Loh is ordered to pay $8471 in attorney’s fees to defendants.

BACKGROUND

            On September 25, 2019, Joseph Loh and Joseph Loh as trustee of The Loh Family Trust filed a complaint against Robert Carruth, Marianne Carruth, The Seager Family Trust, Susan E. Seager as trustee, and Ray F. Seager as trustee for (1) quiet title, (2) trespass, (3) private nuisance, (4) malicious prosecution, and (5) IIED.  Plaintiff argues that it is the owner of the property at 18002 Faysmith Avenue, Torrance and purchased it in 2010.  Defendants are owners of property at 18008 Faysmith Avenue and moved in in May 2012.  The parties share a fence between the two properties.  In summer 2017, R. Carruth approached plaintiff Loh about replacing the fence.  Plaintiff related that due to cost issues, he was not in a position to do so.  In summer 2018, R. Carruth approached plaintiff again.  He relayed that he was not financially able to pay for a new fence.  Thereafter, Loh discovered Carruth removing a 16” by 16” pillar on plaintiff’s property with a jackhammer.  The next day, Carruth installed a gate onto plaintiff’s property by approximately 14”.  On March 22, 2019, Carruth had poured cement on plaintiff’s property.  On March 27, 2019, Loh found damage to his fence.  To date, the Carruths continue to take actions to intimidate, harass, scare, and otherwise bully Loh and his sons.  On June 18, 2019, plaintiff had a topographical survey conducted, which confirmed that a portion of defendants’ driveway, gate, and yard is encroaching onto plaintiff’s property.

            On December 17, 2019, Marianne Carruth filed a cross-complaint against Joseph Loh for (1) easement, (2) vandalism, and (3) Good Neighbor Act.

            On September 23, 2020, the court granted cross-complainant Marianne Carruth’s motion for leave to amend cross-complaint and to join party.

            On October 1, 2020, the FACC was filed.

            On December 1, 2020, the parties stipulated to allow filing of a SACC.

            On December 23, 2020, Marianne Carruth filed the SACC against Joseph Loh, Amy Prout, and Chris Degroot for (1) easement, (2) vandalism, (3) Good Neighbor Act, (4) breach of contract, and (5) fraud.

            On August 17, 2022, plaintiff filed a notice of conditional settlement.

            On July 21, 2023, the court denied without prejudice plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement agreement.

            On October 2, 2023, plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §664.6 states:  “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” 

“A trial court, when ruling on a section 664.6 motion, acts as a trier of fact.  Section 664.6’s ‘express authorization for trial courts to determine whether a settlement has occurred is an implicit authorization for the trial court to interpret the terms and conditions to settlement.’”  Skulnick v. Roberts Express, Inc. (1992) 2 Cal. App. 4th 884, 889 (citation omitted).

            CCP §187 states:  “When jurisdiction is, by the Constitution or this Code, or by any other statute, conferred on a Court or judicial officer, all the means necessary to carry it into effect are also given; and in the exercise of this jurisdiction, if the course of proceeding be not specifically pointed out by this Code or the statute, any suitable process or mode of proceeding may be adopted which may appear most conformable to the spirit of this Code.”

The court’s inquiry in ruling on a motion for judgment pursuant to CCP §664.6 is generally limited to a determination of whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement, and the material terms thereof.  Viejo Bancorp, Inc. v. Wood (1989) 217 Cal. App. 3d 200, 209 n.4.  “Although a judge hearing a section 664.6 motion may receive evidence, determine disputed facts, and enter the terms of a settlement agreement as a judgment, nothing in section 664.6 authorizes a judge to create the material terms of a settlement, as opposed to deciding what terms the parties themselves have previously agreed upon.”  Hernandez v. Board of Educ. of Stockton Unified School Dist. (2004) 126 Cal. App. 4th 1161, 1176.  “The court is powerless to impose on the parties more restrictive or less restrictive or different terms than those contained in their settlement agreement.”  Id.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants Robert Carruth, Marianne Carruth, and David Seager as trustee of The Seager Family Trust request an order to enforce the settlement agreement pursuant to CCP §664.6 as to Paragraph E of the settlement agreement and to enter judgment for penalties under Paragraph L.

            As background, the parties entered into a settlement agreement dated August 15, 2022, whereby the defendants [Carruths] agreed to pay plaintiff Loh $25,000 with the final payment due on August 30, 2023.  Sherry Anne Lear decl., Exh. 1, at Paragraph A.  The Carruths contend that they have paid the full amount.  Further, the Carruths agreed to install a 6’ vinyl fence in their backyard with the correct property line (as established by survey obtained by Loh) at their cost by December 31, 2022.  Id., at Paragraph C. 

            Paragraph E

Under Paragraph E, “No later than December 31, 2022, Joseph Loh will remove the compost area that was installed along the property line and adjacent to the driveway at 18008 Faysmith and remove items of personal property stored in that location.”

Robert Carruth states in his declaration that “we received notification on December 6, 2022 that Joseph Loh had cleaned upon the “compost” area where he used to have box bushes.  . . . At the time, items of personal properly still remained in the area including stacks of cement pavers.”  R. Carruth decl., ¶2, Exh. 2 (picture dated September 6, 2023).  He states that Loh has continued to use the formerly designated “compost” area to store personal items, and that the amount and types of items have increased over time, and that there are still stacks of unused pavers and sandbags along with plastic buckets, empty and filled.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the terms “stacked,” “items,” “compost area,” and “personal items” are not defined in the settlement agreement.  Plaintiff also asserts that he is in compliance with this provision as reflected in the October 9, 2023 photo.  Justin Otten decl., Exh. B; Loh decl.  Plaintiff contends that the only remaining items in question are several sandbags, and that they are neither within the bounds of the compost area or personal items.

The court finds that plaintiff is in violation of Paragraph E to the extent the sandbags, cement pavers, and buckets remain.

    

Paragraph L

            Defendants contend that Loh has breached the settlement agreement under paragraph L, which states:   

“Effective immediately, Joseph Loh agrees that he will not stack any items against the existing vinyl fence in the middle portion of the [Carruth] driveway.  Once the backyard vinyl fence has been installed, Joseph Loh agrees that he will not stack any items against it.  Joseph Loh will not paint, deface, or otherwise cause any damage to the existing vinyl fence on the backyard vinyl fence.  Should action be necessary to enforce this provision, then the Carruths shall be entitled to payment of the sum of $500 per day for each day that items remain stacked against the vinyl fence.” 

            Defendants request a judgment in the amount of $22,500, consisting of $500/day x 38 days (August 18, 2022 through September 24, 2022) and $500/day x 7 days (January 7, 2023 to January 13, 2023).

Defendants assert that Loh violated this material provision and attorney assistance was required (on multiple occasions) to stop Loh’s violations.  Accordingly, they argue, they are entitled to penalties because action was necessary to enforce the terms of the agreement.

Marianne Carruth states in her declaration that after August 15, 2022, Loh did not comply with Paragraph L, and that as of September 13, 2022, he “still had the wooden gate held in pace with dirt, sticks and leaves pushed up forcefully against our fence.”  M. Carruth decl., ¶¶9, 11, Exh. E (September 15, 2022 picture).  She states that after their attorney made a demand to remove the gate, on September 16, 2022, Loh moved the wooden gate so that it was “entirely behind our vinyl fence but still leaning against it.”  Id., ¶12.  Their attorney again demanded that the gate be removed so it was not against defendants’ fence.  Id., ¶13, Exhs. F (September 21 and 22 pictures).  She states that it “was only sometime around” September 25, 2022 when Loh finally moved the wooden gate so that it was not touching “our fence in any way.”  She also asserts that they installed a new vinyl fence in their backyard area in December 2022, and that from January 7 to January 13, 2023, Loh “leaned up his old termite-infested wooden fence against the backyard fence.”  Id., ¶14, Exh. G (January 9 picture),

R. Carruth states in his declaration that after defendants installed the required fencing in their backyard as of December 26, 2022, Loh took the old wooden fence, which was full of termite damage, and leaned it up against “our brand-new fence” and that it was in place from January 7 to January 13, 2023, and it was removed only after their attorney made a demand that it be removed.  R. Carruth decl., ¶6, Exh. 4 (photos of fence on January 7, 2023 and January 9, 2023).

Defense counsel states in her declaration that on September 13, 2022, she sent an email to plaintiff’s counsel stating “Mr. Loh was to ‘effective immediately’ stop stacking or leaning items against the vinyl fence in the middle portion of the driveway.  . . . Please remind Mr. Loh that my client can seek $500 per day for each day that this condition remains in place.”

In a September 14, 2022 email, plaintiff’s counsel states that “[w]e considered it to be part of the compost area” but that “[i]f you contend the board must be removed before 12/31, I will discuss with Mr. Loh further so we can come to a resolution.” 

On September 15, 2022, defense counsel sent a demand for compliance as to Paragraph L, stating that “Mr. Loh needs to remove the wooden gate from its place leaning up against the fencing . . . . Allowing for two days for Mr. Loh to remove all items stacked against the fence, including the still remaining wooden gate, this item needed to be taken care of no later than August 18, 2022.  . . . The condition remains in place and the Carruths, as is their right under the Settlement Agreement, are demanding the immediate removal of the wooden gate along with the required $500 pers day penalty from August 18, 2022, until the removal of the gate.”

In an email dated September 20, 2022, defense counsel states that “[t]he gate and compost are both up against the fence. . . . Mr. Loh remains out of compliance with Paragraph L.”

In an email dated September 21, 2022, plaintiff’s counsel states that she drove by the property and took her own photographs, and that the “white wooden gate is not touching the Carruth’s fence.  No items are stacked against it at all.”  She also states that she was provided photos from Loh on [September 16] and “there is nothing ‘stacked’ against the fence.”

In a letter dated September 27, 2022, defense counsel states that her “clients went out of town on Friday, September 23, 2022.  Before they left, the wooden gate was still resting against their fence . . . . On September 25, 2022, they found the wooden gate was moved so that it no longer touches the fence.”  Defendants requested penalties in the amount of $19,000 for 38 days.

On January 9, 2023, defense counsel sent an email to plaintiff’s counsel stating that “Mr. Loh has again violated the settlement agreement.  He has placed the much discussed wooden gate up against the Carruths new fence.  He has also added the prior, termite infested wood fence that was removed and replaced when the Carruths put in a vinyl fence.  . . . Each day he continues this is a $500 penalty.”

On January 15, 2023, defense counsel sent another email stating that Loh “continues to violate the settlement agreement” and that according to the photos taken on January 11, 2023, they “show that the old termite-filled fence is still leaning against the new vinyl fence. . . . Again, each day and each violation a $500 penalty.”

Defendants also request attorney’s fees in the amount of $8460 ($300/hr.) plus $71.12 in filing fees, citing to Paragraph 5, which states, in part:  “The party obtaining enforcement of this settlement agreement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §664.6 shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees.”

In opposition, Loh argues that the motion is unwarranted and premature because all issues concerning Paragraph L were resolved in September 2022 and January 2023, as reflected in communications by defendants’ counsel to plaintiff’s prior counsel.  Loh also contends that no “action,” as defined by CCP §22, has been necessary to enforce the settlement agreement.  Loh further asserts that the settlement agreement fails to define what constitutes “stacking,” “items,” or the “stacking of items.”  Loh also argues that the attorney’s fees requested by defendants are excessive and unwarranted because the motion is unnecessary.

The court rules as follows:

The court finds that Loh is in violation of Paragraph E as sandbags, pavers, and buckets remain in the compost area, according to plaintiff’s photos.  The terms “stacked” and “items” and “personal items” are not ambiguous.  Further, both parties understand the boundaries of the “compost area.”

As to Paragraph L, as the court previously stated in its ruling on plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement, the agreement is not clear and explicit as to what falls under “[s]hould action be necessary to enforce this provision,” but the provision does not state “an action” and thus, plaintiff’s counsel’s interpretation is too narrow.  The parties do not dispute that defense counsel took action in sending multiple communications and demand letters to obtain compliance.

The court finds that Loh violated the terms of agreement in paragraph L and defendants are thus entitled to penalties.  The evidence indicates that Loh was non-compliant from August 18, 2022 through September 24, 2022 (38 days) and from January 7, 2023 to January 13, 2023 (7 days).  Thus, the court awards penalties to defendants in the amount of $22,500 against plaintiff.

            The court also finds that because defendants obtained enforcement of the settlement agreement, they are entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees.  The court finds that $8,471 ($300/hr. x 28 hrs.; $71 filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be awarded in favor of defendants and against plaintiff Loh.

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

Plaintiff Joseph Loh is ordered to pay $22,500 in penalties to defendants for violation of Para. L of the settlement agreement.

Plaintiff Loh is ordered to comply with Para. E within five days by removing “personal items,” including sandbags and pavers from the compost area.

BACKGROUND

Seager as trustee, and Ray F. Seager as trustee for (1) quiet title, (2) trespass, (3) private nuisance, (4) malicious prosecution, and (5) IIED.  Plaintiff argues that it is the owner of the property at 18002 Faysmith Avenue, Torrance and purchased it in 2010.  Defendants are owners of property at 18008 Faysmith Avenue and moved in in May 2012.  The parties share a fence between the two properties.  In summer 2017, R. Carruth approached plaintiff Loh about replacing the fence.  Plaintiff related that due to cost issues, he was not in a position to do so.  In summer 2018, R. Carruth approached plaintiff again.  He relayed that he was not financially able to pay for a new fence.  Thereafter, Loh discovered Carruth removing a 16” by 16” pillar on plaintiff’s property with a jackhammer.  The next day, Carruth installed a gate onto plaintiff’s property by approximately 14”.  On March 22, 2019, Carruth had poured cement on plaintiff’s property.  On March 27, 2019, Loh found damage to his fence.  To date, the Carruths continue to take actions to intimidate, harass, scare, and otherwise bully Loh and his sons.  On June 18, 2019, plaintiff had a topographical survey conducted, which confirmed that a portion of defendants’ driveway, gate, and yard is encroaching onto plaintiff’s property.

            On September 23, 2020, the court granted cross-complainant Marianne Carruth’s motion for leave to amend cross-complaint and to join party.

            On October 1, 2020, the FACC was filed.

            On December 1, 2020, the parties stipulated to allow filing of a SACC.

            On December 23, 2020, Marianne Carruth filed the SACC against Joseph Loh, Amy Prout, and Chris Degroot for (1) easement, (2) vandalism, (3) Good Neighbor Act, (4) breach of contract, and (5) fraud.

            On July 21, 2023, the court denied without prejudice plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement agreement.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §664.6 states:  “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Loh requests that the court enforce the terms of the settlement agreement and order defendants to install a delineation to designate the location of the property line along the front portion of their driveway.  Plaintiff also seeks attorney’s fees in the amount of $3210 for the first motion to enforce (which the court notes was denied without prejudice on July 21, 2023) and $3185 for the herein motion to enforce.

            As background, after a MSC, the parties entered into a written settlement agreement on August 15, 2022.  Pursuant to the terms of the settlement agreement, the parties requested the court to retain jurisdiction to enforce the terms of the agreement under CCP §664.6. 

            As it relates to this motion, the settlement agreement states that “[w]ithin 30 days of the removal of the compost area, [defendants] will install some delineation to mark the location of the property line on their property along the front portion of the driveway (where there is not already a fence installed) and where the hedges were previously located.”  Settlement Agreement, para. F.

            Plaintiff argues that defendants have not complied with this term of the agreement.  Plaintiff states in his declaration that it was his understanding at the settlement conference that something tangible would be installed along the property line so as to avoid future conflicts, including the Carruths’ continued trespass upon his property.  Joseph Loh decl., ¶31.

Plaintiff asserts that instead of installing something tangible along the property line, R. Carruth “simply drew a line (twice now as the first one washed away).”  Plaintiff states in his declaration that on September 15, 2023, defendants sprayed the line, as depicted in photographs taken on December 30, 2023.  Plaintiff’s decl., ¶32.

            In opposition, defendants argue that they have complied fully with the settlement agreement and that plaintiff is seeking to rewrite or modify the agreement.  According to the declaration of defendant Robert Carruth, defendants “accepted the survey obtained by Loh as the correct property line.”  R. Carruth decl., ¶2.  He states that “[w]e were not required, nor would we have agreed to, the installation of a physical barricade.  To do so on our side of the property line could be dangerous to those using the driveway.”  Id., ¶3.  He states that he is employed as a construction manager and has “extensive experience in performing and supervising construction on both large and small scale projects.”  Id., ¶4.  He states that he complied with the terms of the settlement by placing a delineation along what was noted by Loh’s survey as one in large-scale construction.  He performed this installation by using the marker placed by the surveyor on the sidewalk as well as the survey marker in front of the gatepost.  He marked point-to-point to follow the property line as established by the survey.  He installed this delineation as a permanent marking of the property line in January 2023.  Id., ¶5.  He states that the initial delineation was clearly visible “even after Loh started his new habit of watering our driveway . . . with his hose on full blast.”  Id., ¶6.  He states that after many high-pressure washings by Loh, the original delineation faded but did not wash “away.”  He reinstalled the delineation marker as before.  Id., ¶8.  In September 2023, he undertook the effort of scoring the concrete driveway along the property line.  He also painted the line with bright white concrete paint.  The scored line is permanent because it is a shallow cut into the concrete itself.  Id., ¶9.  Defendant Marianne Carruthers states in her declaration that the “original delineation was designed to be permanent and the newest installed delineation, which was accomplished by scoring the concrete, is certainly permanent.  The scored delineation and white concrete paint are still in place and visible.  She provides photographs that were purportedly taken in January 2024, which she states show the current status of the delineation and that although there is dirt and leaves which have covered some of the line, it has not faded or eroded in any way.  Id., ¶4.  

            In reply, plaintiff argues that he is not seeking to rewrite or modify the agreement.  Rather, the parties dispute the interpretation of “install” and the “form of the delineation.”  Plaintiff asserts that he only seeks enforcement of this requirement in conformity with the manner in which plaintiff believes it was intended to be implemented, that a clear-cut boundary be installed.  Plaintiff contends that the current delineation does not comply with “installed” and is subject to deterioration, fading, and erosion. 

            The court rules as follows:  The court notes that it previously found that the settlement agreement was not clear and explicit that a “delineation” is to be a physical barrier, although the agreement refers to “install.”  Plaintiff agreed that as drafted “the term is a bit vague.”  Further, plaintiff had not shown that defendants breached the agreement by failing to install a delineation.  And, defendants had not shown that they did comply.  Defendant had represented that he “placed” a delineation but the court could not ascertain from the photo the “delineation” or whether it was adequate and complied with the agreement.  See July 21, 2023 minute order.

            As to this present motion, the parties do not dispute that the settlement agreement requires “some delineation,” that the property line be “marked,” and that the property line is delineated currently by scoring and paint.  Rather plaintiff’s position is that the current delineation was not “installed” and that it is thus not adequate.  As a verb, “install” means “to set up for use or service,” such as “had an exhaust fan installed in the kitchen” or “install software.”  It also means “to establish in an indicated place, condition or status.”  See Merriam-Webster Dictionary.  In the context of the settlement agreement, the other key words are “some,” “delineation,” and “mark,” none of which infer a physical barrier.  The current paint and scoring fall under “some delineation,” which marked the property line.  Although it is not unreasonable for plaintiff to have expected a physical barrier of some sort, the court finds that defendants have complied with the settlement agreement.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

            Attorney’s fees

            Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees is DENIED as to the first motion.  The court denied the motion without prejudice as it was prematurely filed. 

As to the parties’ request for attorney’s fees as to the herein motion, the requests are DENIED. 

 

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On October 19, 2023, The Roosk Group Inc. filed a TAC against Jamie McDaniel, Friendly Llama, Inc., Diane Zane, Zane Consulting, Inc., and Telcolynx, LLC for (1) breach of oral contract (against Friendly Llama), (2) breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing (against Friendly Llama), (3) breach of fiduciary duties (against Friendly Llama), (4) breach of fiduciary duties (against McDaniel), (5) fraud (against Friendly Llama and McDaniel), (6) rescission of contract (against Zane Consulting), (7) breach of contract (against Zane and Zane Consulting), and (8) unjust enrichment (against Zane and Zane Consulting).

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467; CCP §437c(c).

DISCUSSION

Defendant Zane Consulting requests summary judgment on the Third Amended Complaint.  Alternatively, defendant requests summary adjudication as to the 6th cause of action for rescission of contract, 7th cause of action for breach of contract, and 8th cause of action for unjust enrichment. 

The court notes that the “Facts Common to All Causes of Action” in the TAC encompass 23 pages; thus, the court will not attempt to summarize them.

In opposition to the motion, plaintiff requests a continuance to complete discovery.  Plaintiff argues that the motion is premature because discovery is far from complete in this case, with numerous unresolved disputes and outstanding issues.  Plaintiff contends that the motion asserts lack of evidence on various points, which are still the subject of ongoing discovery.  As an example, defendant Zane Consulting has agreed to supplement its responses to plaintiff’s requests for production, nos. 18-20, 23, 27, and 30, which relate to fraud.  Plaintiff argues that the evidence is crucial in demonstrating the harm caused by the alleged fraudulent conduct.  Further, plaintiff contends, there are outstanding documents that are essential to establishing defendants’ knowledge of Roosk’s rights as a member of Telcolynx and the historical agreements and course of dealings between Roosk and Friendly Llama and documents that evidence any contracts or other agreements between Zane and Telcolynx, for example.

In reply, defendant argues that plaintiff is not entitled to a continuance as it failed to comply with CCP §437c(f) because counsel’s declaration does not explain how the outstanding discovery is necessary for the opposition.

The court rules as follows:  Under CCP §437c(h), “If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just.  The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.”

“A declaration in support of a request for continuance under section 437c, subdivision (h) must show:  ‘(1) the facts to be obtained are essential to opposing the motion; (2) there is reason to believe such facts may exist; and (3) the reasons why additional time is needed to obtain these facts.’ . . . ‘It is not sufficient under the statute merely to indicate further discovery or investigation is contemplated.  The statute makes it a condition that the party moving for a continuance show “facts essential to justify opposition may exist.”’”  Cooksey v. Alexakis (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 246, 254 (citations omitted).  See also A & B Painting & Drywall, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 25 Cal. App. 4th 349, 356-57 (“a continuance for the purpose of taking depositions was not warranted when the declaration submitted in support of the continuance did not “explain what efforts were made to the necessary depositions or why they could not have been taken earlier.”).

Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration states that the ongoing discovery process remains incomplete, and that there are several unresolved disputes between the parties, “which the parties have been working diligently to resolve.”  Counsel states that the awaited supplemental responses have not been received to date and that the proposed confidentiality order has not been finalized.  Counsel further states that all depositions in this matter are not completed and are pending finalization of written discover responses, “as are being supplemented pursuant to the agreements of the parties.”  Brian Williamson decl. (and attached copies of email correspondence between counsel re discovery and requests for production of documents).  The November and December correspondence (after the motion for summary judgment was filed) appear to show that counsel have been working diligently in attempting to resolve any discovery disputes and that defendants are purportedly supplementing their responses.  Although not reflected in the declaration, the attached copy of the requests for production of documents indicates requests that are relevant to the issues raised by moving party.

The court finds that a continuance is warranted.

The motion is thus CONTINUED.

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th causes of action and OVERRULED as to the 6th cause of action in the complaint.

BACKGROUND

            On April 18, 2023, plaintiffs Nazanin Soleimany, Connor Mackey, and Ziba Soleimany filed a complaint against Abdul J. Sheriff, C On Adams, LLC, and Eagle Eye Home & Sewer Line Inspection Group LLC for (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of fiduciary duties, (3) intentional misrepresentation, (4) negligent misrepresentation, (5) fraud by concealment, (6) negligence, and (7) breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Eagle Eye Home & Sewer Inspection demurs to the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, and 6th causes of action on the ground that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that on August 11, 2022, plaintiffs entered into an agreement to buy the subject property located at 2916 W. 83rd St., Inglewood.  Complaint, ¶8.  Plaintiffs followed the Sellers’ Disclosures and the advice and recommendations of professionals, specifically, defendant Eagle in making the decisions that are the subject of this complaint and which have proved to result in substantial loss.  Id., ¶9.  In early August 2022, defendant Abdul and defendant Adams accepted plaintiffs’ offer of purchase for the property.  Id., ¶10.  On August 17, 2022, defendant sellers gave/made certain disclosures in the Real Estate Transfer Disclosure Statement regarding the condition of the property, including the plumbing and the windows.  Defendant sellers indicated, in those disclosures, that there were no non-compliant plumbing fixtures; that there were no material defects; and that there were no defects with the water, sewer, or septic system; and roof.  Id., ¶11.  Defendant sellers, in the Real Estate Transfer Disclosure Statement, stated that the defendant sellers were not aware of any “[f]looding, drainage, or grading problems.”  Id., ¶12.  Defendant sellers also made the following disclosure representations in the Seller Property Questionnaire:  a. there were no plumbing fixtures that were non-compliant plumbing fixtures as defined by Civil Code §1101.3; b. there were no material facts or defects affecting the property not otherwise disclosed to buyer; c. there were no defects in any of the following (including past defects that have been repaired):  . . . . plumbing (including the presence of polybutylene pipes), water, sewer, waste disposal or septic system, sump pumps, . . . , drainage, . . . , windows . . . .; d. defendant sellers were not aware of any past or present known material facts or other significant items affecting the value or desirability of the property not otherwise disclosed to buyer; e. no issues of water intrusion into any part of the physical structure; and f. that there were no defects/malfunctions in the roof, windows, foundation, plumbing, and other structural components.  Id., ¶13.  Defendant sellers made significant improvement on the property in order to sell the property at a profit.  Id., ¶14.

The complaint further alleges that defendant sellers knew or should have known that the representations made in the Seller Disclosure were false and that any reliance on those representations would be to the detriment of the purchaser.  Id., ¶15.  After moving in, plaintiffs learned that the plumbing was non-compliant and/or defective.  Id., ¶16.  Defendant sellers also represented that the windows installed on the property were brand new and the roof was new or newer.  Id., ¶17.  After moving into the property, plaintiffs had the windows investigated after a significant leak occurred and were forced to install and remedy the defects in the windows.  Id., ¶18.  Plaintiffs also had inspection performed on the roof and the entire roof needed replacing and there was water intrusion in the attic spaces, which established that the roof was poorly constructed/renovated.  Id., ¶19.

The complaint further alleges that on August 20, 2022, defendant Eagle performed an inspection and submitted to plaintiffs and defendant sellers an inspection report.  The inspection report contained material representations/recommendations that were not up to industry standards and outlined items that should have been identified as needing repair.  Id., ¶21.  Defendant Eagle owed a duty to plaintiffs to accurately and properly identify certain items in the inspection report and failed to comply with the duty of care owed to plaintiffs.  Id., ¶22.  After moving in, plaintiffs learned that the plumbing was non-compliant and/or defective, contrary to the inspection report.  Id., ¶23.  Defendant Eagle represented in the inspection report that the windows were “serviceable” which means that “it was the opinion of the inspector that the item is doing the job for which it was intended and exhibits normal wear and tear.”  Id., ¶24.  After moving into the property, plaintiffs noticed that the windows were leaking, and had the windows investigated, and were forced to reinstall and remedy the defects in the windows that should have been indicated in the inspection report as in needing attention or repair.  Id., ¶25.  Defendant Eagle inspected the roof and the attic, and the inspection report only mentioned that there were scattered items in the attic that should have been removed.  Eagle should have seen the water intrusion and evidence that the roof was not in good condition and needed repair.  Id., ¶26.  Since the purchase, plaintiffs have found numerous issues surrounding the property, including water intrusions in the crawl space, roof damage, leaky windows, and outdated plumbing, which led to sewage spilling into the crawl space.  Id., ¶28.

            2nd cause of action for breach of fiduciary duties

“To establish a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, a plaintiff must demonstrate the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of that duty and damages.”  Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal. App. 4th 170, 182.  “A fiduciary relationship is any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is in duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.  Such a relation ordinarily arises where a confidence is reposed by one person in the integrity of another, and in such a relation the party in whom the confidence is reposed, if he voluntarily accepts or assumes to accept the confidence, can take no advantage from his acts relating to the interest of the other party without the latter's knowledge or consent . . . .”  Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 625, 29-30 (citation omitted) (internal quotations omitted).

Plaintiffs allege that defendants had a fiduciary duty with plaintiffs as a result of the nature and extent of the transaction.  Complaint, ¶34.

Defendant argues that plaintiffs have failed to allege a fiduciary relationship.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to show a fiduciary relationship or that defendant owed a fiduciary duty to plaintiffs.  Plaintiffs have not alleged “the trust, confidence, integrity and fidelity which are the hallmarks of a fiduciary relationship” between plaintiffs and the inspector company.  See Thompson v. Cannon (1990) 224 Cal. App. 3d 1413, 1418.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

3rd cause of action for intentional misrepresentation

The elements of fraud are:  (1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) damages.  See Civil Code §1709.  A fraud claim must be pled with specificity.  Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal. App. 4th 821, 837.  “The particularity requirement demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal. App. 4th 1462, 1469.

Plaintiffs allege that defendants made a false representation, which ultimately caused plaintiffs harm.  Complaint, ¶45.  Defendants made a clear representation to plaintiffs that the property was clear of certain defects.  Id., ¶46.  Defendants’ representation of the service provided was false.  Id., ¶47.  Plaintiffs believed defendants’ representation of the business’ professionalism and promises; as such, plaintiffs reasonably relied on defendants’ representation.  Id., ¶48.

Defendants argues that plaintiffs fail to plead with the required specificity.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  They are conclusory and lack specificity.  Further, plaintiffs do not allege facts to show knowledge of falsity and intent to defraud or to induce reliance. 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

4th cause of action for negligent misrepresentation

The elements of negligent misrepresentation are: (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed, and (5) damages. Where the defendant makes false statements, honestly believing that they are true, but without reasonable ground for such belief, he may be liable for negligent misrepresentation, a form of deceit.  B.L.M. v. Sabo & Deitsch (1997) 55 Cal. App. 4th 823, 834.  A plaintiff must allege what was said, by whom, in what manner (i.e., oral or in writing), when, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, under what authority to bind the corporation.  See Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal. App. 4th 772, 782.

Defendant argues the same as under the 2nd cause of action.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  They are conclusory and lack specificity.  Further, plaintiffs do not allege facts to show that defendant made misrepresentations without reasonable grounds for believing them to be true.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

6th cause of action for negligence

The complaint alleges that Eagle had a duty to exercise ordinary care with respect to its obligations under the contracts and warranties.  Complaint, ¶65.  Eagle’s duty to exercise ordinary care included the duty to avoid foreseeable injury to plaintiffs by virtue of plaintiffs’ anticipated reliance on defendants’ compliance with the contracts and warranties in a professional and workmanlike manner.  Id., ¶66.

Defendant argues that plaintiffs fail to allege what legal duty of care defendant owed plaintiffs, asserting that it does not have a duty under a tort theory.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that an inspector’s independent duty arises from tort law.  Plaintiffs also cite to Bus. & Prof. Code §7196, which states, “It is the duty of a home inspector who is not licensed as a general contractor, structural pest control operator, or architect, or registered as a professional engineer to conduct a home inspection with the degree of care that a reasonably prudent home inspector would exercise.” 

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient that defendant had a duty of care (based other than in contract) to plaintiffs.  A home inspector may be sued in tort for the negligent failure to disclose material defects in a home.  Leko v. Cornerstone Building Inspection Service (2001) 86 Cal. App. 4th 1109.  See also Bus. & Prof. Code §7196. 

The demurrer is OVERRULED.

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd, 3rd, and 4th causes of action.  The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 6th cause of action.

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
