DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV11209    Hearing Date: November 8, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CALEX ENGINEERING CO,

 

         vs.

 

WENDELL BENIGA, et al.

 

 Case No.:   24STCV11209

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: November 8, 2024

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for preliminary injunction.

 

            On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendants Wendell Beniga and Ironmark Earthworks, Inc.

 

            On October 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Wendell Beniga and Ironmark Earthworks, Inc., alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (4) Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (5) Inducing Breach of Contract; (6) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets - Cal. Civ Code § 3426; (7) Violation of Business and Professions Code Section 17200; (8) Intentional Interference with Economic Relations; (9) Negligent Interference with Economic Relations; and (10) Violation Of Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act – Cal. Penal Code § 502.

 

            On May 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for preliminary injunction. On July 1, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition. On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

            On July 15, 2024, the Court ordered parties to meet and confer to narrow the scope of the preliminary injunction and file supplemental briefing.

 

            On September 12, 2024, Defendants filed supplemental briefing. On the same date, Plaintiff filed a supplemental briefing. On September 13, 2024, Defendants filed a further response. On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a further response.

 

            On September 26, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the motion for preliminary injunction to November 8, 2024, pursuant to written stipulation.

 

            On October 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed supplemental briefing. On October 28, 2024, Defendants filed a supplemental brief. On November 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to Defendants’ supplemental brief.

 

            Plaintiff seeks a preliminary injunction as follows:

 

1.      Enjoining and restraining Defendants herein, and each of them, from using, disclosing, misappropriating, or profiting from Calex’s trade secrets which are in Defendants’ possession or of which Defendant Beniga had access at any time during his employment, including without limitation, customer identification information, bidding opportunities, bidding strategies, bidding information, bid proposals, project reports, and customer contracts.

2.      Enjoining and restraining Defendants herein, and each of them, from solicitating business from, or initiating any further communication with any customer of Plaintiff whom Defendant Beniga submitted bids to or had direct contact with while employed by Plaintiff, including specifically, Walton Construction, AECOM, Benchmark Contractors Inc., CIM Group, CW Driver, Del Amo Const., Fassberg Construction, Frymer, Icon West, Krismar Construction, Level 3 Construction, LT Building, Matt Construction, Milender White Inc., Suffolk Construction, Swinerton Builders, Turner Construction Co., W.E. O’Neil Construction, and WPIC Construction and from using or disclosing any confidential information, documents or records pertaining to those clients.

3.      Enjoining and restraining Defendants herein, and each of them, from solicitating business from, or initiating any further communication with any customer which Defendant Beniga worked on any project while employed by Plaintiff and had access to Plaintiff’s trade secrets and confidential information, specifically including those listed in Section B of Exhibit 1 to the Rossi Declaration filed and received under seal with the Court.

4.      Enjoining and restraining Defendants herein, and each of them, from directly or indirectly altering, destroying, or disposing of any evidence, in any form, relating to this action, including without limitation, emails and paper and electronic documents, including current or archived electronic logs, metadata, and directories.

5.      Directing Defendants herein, and each of them, to deliver immediately to Plaintiff all copies of documents, whether in paper form or stored in an electronic medium, containing Plaintiff’s confidential information that Defendants currently possess or have in their custody or control, including without limitation, customer identification information, bidding opportunities, bidding strategies, bidding information, project reports, and customer contracts.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a preliminary injunction is to preserve the status quo pending a decision on the merits.  (Major v. Miraverde Homeowners Ass’n. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 618, 623.)  In deciding whether or not to grant a preliminary injunction, the court looks to two factors, including “(1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative balance of harms that is likely to result from the granting or denial of interim injunctive relief.”  (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 553-54.) The factors are interrelated, with a greater showing on one permitting a lesser showing on the other.  (Dodge, Warren & Peters Ins. Services, Inc. v. Riley (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1414, 1420.)  However, the party seeking an injunction must demonstrate at least a reasonable probability of success on the merits.  (IT Corp. v. County of Imperial (1983) 35 Cal.3d 63, 73-74.)  The party seeking the injunction bears the burden of demonstrating both a likelihood of success on the merits and the occurrence of irreparable harm.  (Savage v. Trammell Crow Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1562, 1571.) Irreparable harm may exist if the plaintiff can show an inadequate remedy at law.  (CCP § 526(a).)

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff states that despite the Court’s order to meet and confer to narrow the scope of the injunction, the parties could not agree to narrow the scope.

 

Plaintiff seeks an injunction against Defendants for allegedly stealing Plaintiff’s trade secrets and confidential information, such as customer details and bidding strategies, and using them to solicit Plaintiff’s clients. While employees may leave to join competitors, they may not use confidential information from a previous employer to gain a competitive advantage. Plaintiff argues that Beniga misused client information to divert business to Ironmark and requests the court to prevent further solicitation of its clients and any use of its trade secrets.

 

Probability of Prevailing

 

Plaintiff argues that the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA) supports injunctive relief, as Plaintiff’s customer information and bidding strategies are trade secrets. Under Civil Code § 3426.2(a), an injunction is proper when a trade secret is misappropriated or there is a threat of misappropriation. Plaintiff claims these trade secrets have independent economic value and are confidential, satisfying the Act’s two-prong test for trade secret status. (Civil Code § 3426.1(d)(1).) The confidential information, including client data and bidding strategies, is entitled to trade secret protection, as demonstrated in Courtesy Temporary Service, Inc. v. Camacho (1990) 222 Cal. App. 3d 1278, 1288, where the court found customer information like key contacts and specialized requirements satisfies trade secret definition. Similarly, in American Credit Indemnity Co. v. Sacks (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 622, 630-631, the court held that a customer list allowing a competitor to target specific clients qualifies as a trade secret. Plaintiff states that the UTSA’s protection extends to Plaintiff’s confidential bidding data, including pricing and profit margins, per Whyte v. Schlage Lock Co. (2002) 101 Cal. App. 4th 1443, 1455 (noting independent economic value of confidential pricing data for competitive positioning). Plaintiff alleges Defendant Beniga shared confidential pricing and bid information with Ironmark, enabling Ironmark to undercut Plaintiff’s pricing. (Seitz Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. B.)

 

Plaintiff states its efforts to maintain secrecy meet UTSA requirements, including confidentiality agreements and password protections, are supported by Morlife, Inc. v. Perry (1997) 56 Cal. App. 4th 1514; American Credit Indemnity Co. v. Sacks (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 622; and Mai Systems Corp. v. Peak Computer (9th Cir. 1993) 991 F.2d 511.  Plaintiff asserts Defendants’ unauthorized solicitation of customers and use of Plaintiff’s bidding information constitutes misappropriation under Civil Code § 3426.1(b) (disclosure or use without consent under duty of confidentiality). Greenly v. Cooper (1978) 77 Cal. App. 3d 382, 392 further confirms an employee's duty to maintain confidentiality, even without an explicit agreement.

 

Regarding unfair competition, Plaintiff argues injunctive relief is also proper under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17200 and 17203, which allow actions against unfair or unlawful business practices. The California Supreme Court in Aetna Building Maintenance Co. v. West (1952) 39 Cal. 2d 198, 205 emphasized that solicitation of former clients using confidential customer preferences constitutes unfair competition. Even without trade secret status, Plaintiff’s client data warrants protection under unfair competition law, as recognized in Whitted v. Williams (1964) 226 Cal. App. 2d 52, 57 and Courtesy Temporary Services, Inc. v. Camacho (1990) 222 Cal. App. 3d 1278, 1292.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant Beniga breached his duty of loyalty by using confidential information and soliciting Plaintiff’s clients for a competitor, Ironmark. An employee’s duty of loyalty requires “undivided loyalty” during employment. (Fowler v. Varian Assoc., Inc. (1988) 193 Cal.App.3d 34, 41, obligating them not to harm their employer’s interests (Bancroft-Whitney Co. v. Glen (1966) 64 Cal.2d 327, 345-354. Beniga allegedly breached this duty by retaining confidential information and using it for Ironmark’s benefit.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits for its claims and therefore does not meet the standard required for injunctive relief. Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s claims, including misappropriation of trade secrets, rely on speculative and conclusory allegations rather than substantive evidence.

 

Regarding the misappropriation of trade secrets, Defendants assert that Plaintiff has failed to specifically identify any trade secrets, which is necessary under cases like Altavion, Inc. v. Konica Minolta Systems Laboratory, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 26, 43 and Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal. App.2d 244, 253. They argue that general contractor information and client identities are public knowledge and therefore not protected as trade secrets. (American Paper & Packaging Products, Inc. v. Kirgan (1986) 183 Cal.App.3d 1318, 1325.) Further, Defendants claim there is no evidence of improper use of any trade secrets or confidential information, with declarations from Beniga and Boyer specifically refuting Plaintiff’s assertions of solicitation of Plaintiff’s clients, including Walton.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff’s additional claims, such as those for unfair competition and breach of duty of loyalty, are preempted by the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (CUTSA) because they stem from the same alleged facts underlying the trade secret claim. (Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.7(b)(2); K.C. Multimedia, Inc. v. Bank of America Technology & Operations, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 939, 954-955.) According to Defendants, CUTSA’s preemption provision displaces these additional theories of liability, rendering them inapplicable. Thus, Defendants conclude that Plaintiff has not sufficiently shown a likelihood of success, lacks specific evidence of misappropriation or harm, and that any additional claims are preempted by CUTSA.

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ declarations fail to directly deny the misappropriation or use of its trade secrets and confidential information, and instead contain evasive language. Plaintiff highlights inconsistencies in Defendants’ claims, noting that while Defendants assert Ironmark does not directly compete with Calex, they admit to bidding on overlapping projects, undermining their claim. Plaintiff further argues that Defendants do not categorically deny accessing and downloading trade secret files, such as those related to the Fairfield apartments project. Plaintiff also contends that it has adequately identified its trade secrets, including its bidding strategies, customer lists, and client preferences. Defendants’ reliance on outdated case law to argue otherwise is flawed, as recent cases and the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (UTSA) protect such proprietary information. Plaintiff asserts that Calex’s investment in developing customer relations and pricing strategies justifies trade secret protection and that confidentiality agreements and security measures were in place to safeguard this information. Plaintiff further argues that its claims are not preempted by CUTSA, as they are based on Defendants’ actions beyond trade secret misappropriation, including breaches of fiduciary duty, contractual obligations, and unfair competition. Plaintiff claims that Defendants diverted Calex’s business opportunities to Ironmark, violating California’s unfair competition laws. Even if no protectable trade secrets are found, Plaintiff asserts it is still entitled to injunctive relief under the unfair competition claim, as case law supports injunctions against former employees using confidential information to solicit former clients.

 

Balance of Harms

 

Plaintiff claims irreparable harm from Defendants’ potential continued use of trade secrets, citing Campbell Soup Co. v. ConAgra, Inc. (3d Cir. 1992) 977 F.2d 86, 92-93, which holds that trade secret misappropriation threatens irreparable harm, especially for customer lists. Courts need not wait for actual loss to grant an injunction (Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.2.) Plaintiff also states the balance of hardships favors Plaintiff, as the injunction only restricts Defendants from soliciting Plaintiff’s customers and using confidential information, obligations they already agreed to fulfill. Failure to grant the injunction would allow Defendants to use Plaintiff’s trade secrets unfairly, potentially leading to irreparable harm. Plaintiff also argues that an injunction supports public interest by protecting trade secrets, critical for a competitive market (Morlife, Inc. v. Perry (2014) 56 Cal.App.4th 1514, 1520; Kewanee Oil Co. v. Bicron Corp. (1974) 416 U.S. 470.) Finally, Plaintiff requests that any bond required should be minimal, given Plaintiff’s strong likelihood of success and the limited burden on Defendants, who are only required to refrain from misappropriation already prohibited by law.

 

            In opposition, Defendants argue that the balance of equities favors them and that Plaintiff cannot demonstrate that denial of the injunction would cause more harm than its issuance. They assert that without proof of irreparable harm or inadequacy of legal remedies, an injunction should not be granted. Defendants highlight that Plaintiff lacks evidence of threatened or lost clients or business, whereas the injunction would prevent Ironmark from pursuing projects with any general contractor associated with Calex, resulting in substantial harm to Defendants. Additionally, Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s proposed injunction is overbroad and lacks specificity, violating requirements for clear conduct standards in injunctions. (People ex rel. Dept. of Transportation v. Maldonado (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1234; Pitchess v. Superior Court (1969) 2 Cal.App.3d 644, 651.) They argue that the injunction would restrict Defendants from engaging in business without any clear boundaries on affected clients or trade secrets, which would violate public policy favoring free competition. (Aetna Bldg. Maint. Co. v. West (1952) 39 Cal.2d 198, 204.) Defendants further assert that Plaintiff has not shown why monetary damages would be insufficient, arguing that if any part of the injunction is granted, Plaintiff should be required to post a substantial bond to cover potential damages if the injunction is later deemed wrongful. (Code. Civ. Proc. § 529.)

 

            In reply, Plaintiff argues that the harm it faces from the denial of an injunction far outweighs any harm Defendants might suffer if it is granted. Plaintiff cites evidence suggesting irreparable harm, including potential loss of customers and confidential information misappropriated by Defendant Beniga, who downloaded sensitive data just before resigning and has since bid on the same projects as Plaintiff. Plaintiff asserts that an injunction is necessary to prevent both current and future misuse of this information, as authorized by the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (CUTSA), which allows injunctive relief for both actual and threatened misappropriation. (Civ. Code § 3426.2.)

 

Plaintiff also contends that the proposed injunction is not overly broad, as it merely aims to protect Calex’s existing customer base that Beniga became familiar with during his employment. Plaintiff references Morlife, Inc. v. Perry (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1519, where a similar injunction was upheld, restricting former employees from soliciting clients they had serviced while at their previous employer. The proposed order here, Plaintiff argues, is focused solely on preventing Defendants from using or soliciting customers they became familiar with during Beniga’s tenure with Calex, and thus is reasonable and enforceable.

 

Regarding the bond, Plaintiff argues that any bond requirement should be minimal, as Defendants have not identified any specific costs or damages that would result from the injunction. Plaintiff emphasizes that the injunction simply requires Defendants to refrain from using Calex’s confidential information, which they are not entitled to use. Plaintiff notes that courts have previously waived bond requirements in cases involving similar restrictions on confidential information. (Extreme Reach, Inc. v. Spotgenie Partners, LLC, 2013 WL 12081182.)

 

Supplemental Arguments

 

In Plaintiff’s supplemental brief, filed on October 17, 2024, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant Beniga engaged in further wrongful conduct by taking confidential information during his last days at Calex, with evidence showing he downloaded over 35 files, including trade secrets, onto his computer and used multiple USB devices to transfer data. Plaintiff argues that Beniga and Ironmark have already used Calex’s confidential information, as shown by the similar bid proposal submitted to Walton Construction and other clients. Plaintiff claims that Beniga retained documents with confidential details, including pricing and key contacts, which qualify as trade secrets under California law. Plaintiff seeks an injunction to prevent Defendants from soliciting or conducting business with specific clients Beniga engaged with while at Calex, including Walton Construction, CW Driver, and others. Plaintiff contends that similar injunctions have been upheld in cases where misuse of trade secrets created a risk of losing established customers. (Stuhlbarg Int’l Sales Co. v. John D. Brush & Co. (9th Cir. 2001) 240 F.3d 832, 841.)

 

Plaintiff argues that the proposed injunction is narrowly tailored, targeting only clients Beniga worked with and protecting Calex’s confidential information without being overbroad. Plaintiff asserts that such relief is reasonable, as it prevents Defendants from benefitting from misappropriated information while preserving Calex’s customer relationships. Plaintiff believes this injunction is essential to avoid further harm and protect its trade secrets.

 

In supplemental opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s request for a preliminary injunction is overly broad, as it would prevent Defendants from accepting business from clients with whom Ironmark had established independent relationships, without any involvement from Beniga. Defendants claim that equity does not support enjoining a former employee from receiving business that is fairly and legally obtained. They contend that Ironmark’s revenue from several clients, including AECOM, CW Driver, and Turner Construction, stems from pre-existing relationships and should not be restricted. Defendants emphasize that Beniga’s role at Calex was limited to technical tasks, with no client-facing or decision-making responsibilities. They assert that his duties at Ironmark similarly do not involve client interactions or business solicitation, and they deny misappropriation of trade secrets, arguing that the evidence Plaintiff provides, such as the Walton Construction bid proposal, does not substantiate this claim.

 

Additionally, Defendants highlight distinctions between this case and those cited by Plaintiff, noting that in Morlife Inc. v. Perry, Peerless Oakland Laundry Co. v. Hickman, and similar cases, the defendants had direct customer relationships and founded competing businesses, unlike Beniga, who merely joined another company without client-related duties. Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s proposed injunction would create an impractical restriction, barring them from receiving unsolicited business from clients who independently choose to work with Ironmark. Finally, Defendants note that Ironmark and Calex operate in different markets, as Ironmark does not provide shoring services, further weakening Plaintiff’s claim of competitive overlap.

 

In supplemental reply, Plaintiff argues that an injunction is necessary to prevent ongoing harm from Defendant Beniga’s misappropriation of its confidential information and trade secrets. Plaintiff attempted to meet with Defendants to narrow the scope of the injunction as per the Court’s request, but claims Defendants were unwilling to negotiate, refusing any form of injunction. Plaintiff asserts that Defendants’ allegations regarding the client list’s validity are baseless, as the list includes past and current clients compiled from Calex’s records and verified by Calex’s COO. Plaintiff highlights that Defendants do not dispute that Beniga downloaded confidential Calex documents and communicated with overlapping clients like CW Driver and Walton Construction after joining Ironmark. Plaintiff argues that this confirms the need for an injunction covering at least these clients to prevent further misuse of confidential information. Plaintiff further contends that the fact Ironmark had pre-existing relationships with some of these clients does not justify denying an injunction, as Beniga’s access to confidential client information creates a competitive threat. Plaintiff dismisses Defendants’ argument that an injunction should only cover clients Ironmark has not previously worked with, asserting this would inadequately protect Calex from unfair competition.

 

In response to Defendants’ downplaying Beniga’s role at Calex and Ironmark, Plaintiff maintains that Beniga’s duties gave him access to valuable proprietary information that could harm Calex’s competitive position if misused. Plaintiff notes that courts have granted similar injunctions in cases where former employees took confidential information to benefit new employers, regardless of whether the new position directly involved client contact. Plaintiff emphasizes that further discovery has uncovered additional confidential documents on Beniga’s USB drives, showing that Defendants possess sensitive bid, budget, and labor proposals. Given this evidence, Plaintiff requests a narrowly tailored injunction to prevent Beniga and Ironmark from exploiting Calex’s confidential information with overlapping clients. Plaintiff reserves the right to seek additional relief if further discovery reveals more misconduct.

 

Analysis

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated a likelihood of prevailing on the merits of its claims, particularly regarding the misappropriation of trade secrets and confidential information. Plaintiff has provided sufficient evidence through declarations that Defendant Beniga accessed and downloaded sensitive information shortly before his departure from Calex. Specifically, in the Declaration of Matthew Gallegos, it is noted that on May 22, 2024, two days prior to his resignation, Beniga downloaded over 35 files, including confidential bid proposals and client-specific strategies. (Gallegos Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. A at rows 19-43.) Additionally, the Declaration of David Rossi confirms that these files contained proprietary details such as pricing information, client contacts, and detailed project information, which are important to Calex’s competitive positioning. (Rossi Decl. ¶¶ 4-6.) Furthermore, the evidence indicates that Beniga used multiple USB drives to transfer these confidential files, one of which he has allegedly lost or destroyed. (Braun Decl. ¶ 16.) The remaining USB drives, produced in discovery, reveal files pertaining to clients such as CW Driver, Walton Construction, and CIM Group, whom Beniga has since communicated with while at Ironmark. (Rossi Decl. Exs. 2-10; Shackelford Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. B. [SROG 1].) This conduct suggests improper use of confidential information, further supported by Beniga’s own admissions in his supplemental declaration that he engaged with some of these clients in his new role at Ironmark. (Supp. Beniga Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

The balance of hardships also weighs in Plaintiff's favor. Plaintiff is at significant risk of irreparable harm if an injunction is not granted, as the continued unauthorized use of its proprietary information could lead to the loss of established clients and a weakened competitive position. This harm is particularly acute given that trade secrets and client relationships are critical to Plaintiff’s business success and cannot easily be restored through monetary damages alone. In contrast, Defendants’ hardship is minimal; the injunction merely prevents them from utilizing information that does not rightfully belong to them. Any legitimate business activities by Defendants, independent of Plaintiff’s trade secrets, remain unaffected by the scope of this injunction.

 

Thus, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion for preliminary injunction.

         vs.

 

HAMIDEH MOAYEDPARDAZI, M.D., an individual; DE LA PENA EYE CLINIC, A MEDICAL GROUP, INC., a California Corporation; MED-LASER SURGICAL CENTER, LLC, a California Limited Liability Company; and DOES 1-50, Inclusive,

            The Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer to the second and third causes of action with leave to amend.

 

            The Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

Background

 

This is a medical malpractice action. On May 21, 2024, Plaintiff Ramon Arias Moreno (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Hamideh Moayedpardazi, M.D. (“Dr. Moayedpardazi”), De La Pena Eye Clinic (“DLP”), A Medical Group, Inc., and Med-Laser Surgical Center, LLC (“Med-Laser” or “Defendant”) (collectively, “Defendants”). The Complaint alleges, on January 9, 2023, Plaintiff underwent an examination at DLP, and it was determined that Plaintiff should undergo a selective laser trabeculoplasty on his left eye, with a follow-up procedure on his right eye a few weeks after the initial procedure. On February 23, 2023, Plaintiff arrived at Med-Laser for the procedure and underwent pre-operative protocols for his left eye. After Plaintiff was cleared for surgery, Dr. Moayedpardazi allegedly began operating on Plaintiff’s right eye, and because it was not prepped, Plaintiff experienced sharp pain. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action against all Defendants: 1) Professional Negligence, 2) Medical Battery, and 3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED).

 

On September 20, 2024, Defendant Med-Laser filed this Demurrer to the second cause of action for battery and the third cause of action for IIED on the basis they fail to allege facts sufficient to state a claim. Defendant also filed the Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages sought in connection to the second and third causes of action.

 

On October 28, 2024 Plaintiff filed the oppositions.

 

On October 31, 2024, Defendant filed the replies.

 

Legal Standard

 

i.                    Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245) 

 

ii.                 Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Discussion

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), and section 435.5, subdivision (a), require meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” On September 9, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent a meet and confer correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel explaining the Complaint’s defects and to discuss Defendant’s contemplated Demurrer and Motion to Strike. (Lee Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) Defendant’s counsel received a voicemail from Plaintiff’s counsel. (Lee Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel emailed three times requesting a return call, but Plaintiff’s counsel never responded. (Id.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met because the parties never communicated in person or by telephone. Nevertheless, in the interest of judicial efficiency, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the merits of Defendant’s demurrer, but it notes that subsequent failures to comply with statutory obligations may result in a continuance of the hearing on the subject motion. 

 

B.    Demurrer

 

i.                    Second Cause of Action – Medical Battery

 

“A typical medical battery case is where a patient has consented to a particular treatment, but the doctor performs a treatment that goes beyond the consent. ‘When an action is based upon the theory of surgery beyond consent, the gist of such action is the unwarranted exceeding of the consent.’ For example. . . the patient consents to an operation on his right ear, but the doctor operates on the left ear. ” (Conte v. Girard Orthopaedic Surgeons Medical Group, Inc. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1267 (citations omitted).)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff consented to the pre-operative treatment procedures by Defendant for treatment to his left eye. Defendant argues that there are no allegations that any member of Defendant’s staff touched Plaintiff’s right eye or that any harm resulted from the pre-operative treatment. Plaintiff argues in opposition that all Defendants contributed to medical battery when they only performed pre-operative treatment on his left eye and performed surgery on his right eye. Defendant argues in response that Plaintiff conflates all allegations of medical battery against all named defendants without explaining how Defendant Med-Laser specifically contributed to medical battery.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant. Plaintiff consented to receiving treatment to his left eye on January 9, 2023. (Compl., ¿ 13.) On February 23, 2023, Plaintiff arrived at  Defendant’s office, received pre-operative treatment, and was cleared for the procedure. (Compl., ¿ 14.) The Complaint contains no allegations that Defendant’s staff performed a nonconsensual procedure on Plaintiff. The Complaint only alleges that Defendant failed to obtain informed consent for surgery on the right eye and failed to prep the right eye for surgery. (Compl., ¿ 30-31.) The Court finds that these allegations do not arise to medical battery—Defendant prepped the correct eye for treatment, which was within the scope of Plaintiff’s consent. Further, the allegations of a nonconsensual touching are aimed at Dr. Moayedpardazi: “Defendant Dr. MOAYEDPARDAZI intentionally performed a medical procedure on Plaintiff MORENO without his consent.” (Compl., ¿ 30.) But the Complaint does not allege that Defendant was vicariously liable for Dr. Moayedpardazi’s conduct. The Court thus finds that the Complaint fails to allege facts to state claim for medical battery as to this Defendant.

 

The Court sustains the Demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

ii.                 Third Cause of Action – IIED

 

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.) 

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff fails to state a claim for IIED against it. The Complaint alleges that, following the procedure on the incorrect eye, Plaintiff “tried to communicate his distress to the nurse (hereinafter referred to as Defendant “Doe I”) who had initially marked his left eye and administered the numbing drops. Unfortunately, Doe I also left the room following Defendant Dr. MOAYEDPARDAZI, disregarding Plaintiff MORENO's pleas for assistance.” (Compl., ¿ 16.) Defendant argues the nurse’s actions neither arises to extreme or outrageous conduct nor evidences an intent to cause emotional harm. Defendant argues the Complaint fails to allege that the nurse even knew of the alleged mistake. Plaintiff argues in opposition that all Defendants intentionally inflicted emotional distress through their conduct. In response, Defendant argues Plaintiff fails to distinguish Med-Laser’s conduct from the other two defendants. Further, Plaintiff’s argument that the nurse’s failure to prep the right eye does not arise to extreme and outrageous conduct because the nurse was acting within the scope of Plaintiff’s consent.

 

The Court finds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a claim for IIED. IIED claims usually involve intentional offensive touching or harsh words. (See Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, 1 Cal. Med. Malprac. L. & Prac. § 10:6 (2024 ed.).) The Court finds that the nurse’s act of merely ignoring Plaintiff does not arise to such extreme and outrageous conduct. Further, as with the second cause of action, the Complaint neither alleges that Defendant may be vicariously liable for Dr. Moayedpardazi’s conduct.

 

The Court sustains the Demurrer to the third cause of action with leave to amend. 

 

C.    Motion to Strike

 

Because the second and third causes of action were sustained, the Motion to Strike punitive damages sought in connection with those causes of action is moot.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer to the second and third causes of action with leave to amend.

 

            The Motion to Strike is MOOT.
