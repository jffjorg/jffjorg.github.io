DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV27584    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 26, 2023

CASE NUMBER

23STCV27584

MOTION

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiffs Jane LA Doe and Jane MA Doe

OPPOSING PARTIES

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Jane LA Doe and Jane MA Doe (“Plaintiffs”) move to compel further responses from Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set one (“RPD”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310.)  Plaintiffs move to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 7, 8, 14, 26, 51, and 53. 

In RPDs Nos. 7, 8, 14, and 26, Plaintiffs requests documents about themselves and about complaints of misconduct against Defendant Corey Hogan (“Hogan”), an employee of Defendant.  Plaintiffs contend Hogan sexually abused Plaintiffs while Plaintiffs were students at Defendant’s school district.  In RPD Nos. 51 and 53, Plaintiffs seek documents relating to complaints or allegations of misconduct against any of Defendant’s employees. 

Defendant states it failed to serve a supplemental response to RPD No. 14 in error and represents it will serve that supplemental response.  Defendant concedes it must produce documents about Hogan that reflect complaints prior to the incidents of which Plaintiffs complain in this case.  That is insufficient because Plaintiffs are also entitled to discovery of Defendant’s conduct after the allegations made in Plaintiffs’ complaint.  (See Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 593 [“An entity’s actions and the intent with which the party engaged in such actions ‘may be inferred from evidence of [its] subsequent conduct’”].)

Defendant’s responses to the RPD consist of objections.  As Plaintiff timely filed this motion to compel further responses, Defendant has the burden to justify those objections.  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  Defendant fails to do so.  Rather, Defendant argues the California Public Records Act, or CPRA, precludes disclosure.  CPRA permits anyone to obtain, subject to certain exemptions, “any writing containing information relating to the conduct of the public's business prepared, owned, used, or retained by any state or local agency regardless of physical form or characteristics . . . .”  (Gov. Code, § 7920.530.)  CPRA is, therefore, an alternative procedure to obtain records from a public entity and is not pertinent to discovery in civil litigation.  For the same reason, Defendant’s reliance on the Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act of 1974, or FERPA, is misplaced.  FERPA is not applicable to civil discovery.  (Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1438.)

Defendant also interposes objections under Evidence Code section 1040.  Under that statute, a public entity has a privilege to refuse to disclose “official information.”  (Evid. Code, § 1040, subd. (b).)  Official information is information a public employee acquired in confidence in the course of his or her official duties, which has not been disclosed to the public.  (Evid. Code, § 1040, subd. (a).)  The privilege applies to information that cannot be disclosed under an act of Congress or a California statute or that should not be disclosed “because there is a necessity for preserving [its] confidentiality ... that outweighs the necessity for disclosure in the interest of justice.”  (Evid. Code, § 1040, subd. (b).)  In the instant case, as discussed above, Defendant fails to show any statutes prevent the disclosure of the records at issue.  Further, the public interest in maintaining confidentiality does not outweigh the interest of justice in disclosing pertinent information in this case.  “[P]rotecting children from harm is a compelling interest[.]”  (Burt v. County of Orange (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 273, 285.)  It is not in the public’s interest to shield otherwise discoverable information relevant to mistreatment of children.

Defendant also contends the RPD call for disclosure of documents that implicate the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product protection.  To the extent Defendant withholds any documents based on the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product protection, Defendant must provide Plaintiffs with a privilege log.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.240, subd. (c)(1).)  Without a privilege log, Plaintiffs cannot evaluate such claims of privilege.

Lastly, Defendant argues the RPD call for inadmissible character evidence.  This objection is invalid.  (See Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1490-1491 [“A ‘claim that discovery is not warranted because the evidence disclosed would not itself be admissible is untenable.  It is settled that admissibility is not prerequisite to discovery’”].)  Thus, Plaintiffs need not demonstrate the admissibility of evidence to obtain it in discovery.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses to the RPD are granted. 

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Defendants Nicholas Phipps White (“Nicholas”), Mirga Phipps White (“Mirga”), and the Hollywood Land Development Company, LLC (“Hollywood Land Development Company,” collectively, “Defendants”) move to dismiss Plaintiff 594 Mapleton LLC’s (“Plaintiff”) complaint.  In their notice of motion, Defendants cite no authority under which the Court could dismiss the complaint.  As such, the Court cannot grant this relief.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(a); People v. American Surety Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 719, 726.)  The motion is denied.  Defendants are to file responsive pleadings within 30 days.[1]

The Court notes Mirga purports to represent Nicholas and Hollywood Land Development Company.  Hollywood Land Development Company cannot represent itself and must retain an attorney licensed to practice law.  (Gamet v. Blanchard (2001) 91 Cal. App. 4th 1276, 1284, fn. 5.)  As such, the Court strikes Hollywood Land Development Company’s as a party to this motion to dismiss.  Further, Mirga cannot represent Nicholas, as that would constitute an unauthorized practice of law.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6125.)  Nicholas must represent himself or retain a licensed attorney to do so.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
