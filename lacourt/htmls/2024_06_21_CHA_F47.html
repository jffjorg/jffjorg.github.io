DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 23CHCV03185    Hearing Date: June 21, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Case #23CHCV03185

 

DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer filed on 4/29/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Yusheng Shew and Jill J. Van

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs Gevork Voskanian; Mary Keryan and KSJV3, Inc.

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 6th, 9th and 10th causes of action in the First Amended Complaint:

            1.  Breach of Contract

            2.  Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

            3.  Fraud and Deceit

            4.  Negligent Misrepresentation

            5.  Accounting

            6.  Declaratory Relief

            7.  Intentional Interference

            8.  Negligent Interference

            9.  Assault

            10. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

RULING: The demurrer is sustained with 30 days leave to amend. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of the sale of a fencing company, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant KSJV3, Inc. dba Five Star Fence (Five Star), from Defendants/Cross-Complainants Yusheng Shew (Shew) and Jill Van (Van) to Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants Gevork Voskanian (Voskanian) and Mary Keryan (Keryan). 

 

On 10/20/23, Voskanian, Keryan and Five Star (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed this action against Shew and Van (collectively, Defendants) for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Accounting, (6) Declaratory Relief, (7) Unjust Enrichment, (8) Conspiracy, (9) Intentional Interference and (10) Negligent Interference.

 

On 12/18/23, Shew and Van filed and served a cross-complaint against Voskanian, Keryan and Five Star for: (1) Fraud – Promise Without Intent to Perform, (2) Breach of Stock Purchase Agreement, (3) Breach of Secured Promissory Note, (4) Breach of Guaranty, (5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (6) Judicial Foreclosure of UCC-1 Lien, (7) Interference With Contract, (8) Accounting and (9) Declaratory Relief.   

 

On 12/26/23, Shew and Van/Defendants filed and served a demurrer, with a 3/8/24 hearing date, to the 1st – 5th and 7th and 8th causes of action in the complaint.  On 2/22/24, Plaintiffs filed the subject First Amended Complaint for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Fraud and Deceit, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Accounting, (6) Declaratory Relief, (7) Intentional Interference, (8) Negligent Interference, (9) Assault and (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues Defendants had with the First Amended Complaint, on 4/29/24, Defendants served and filed the instant demurrer to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 6th, 9th, and 10th causes of action in the First Amended Complaint.

 

On 5/1/24, the Court overruled Five Star, Voskanian and Keryan’s demurrer as to the 1st, 7th and 9th causes of action in the cross-complaint and sustained the demurrer with 30 days leave to amend as to the 5th cause of action.  A First Amended Cross-Complaint has not been filed.

 

On 6/10/24, Defendants filed and served a Notice of Non-Opposition to the instant demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.  On 6/14/24, 5 court days late and 1 day after a reply was due and without explanation, Plaintiffs filed and served an opposition to the demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.  CCP 1005(b).  On 6/17/24, Defendants filed and served a reply to the opposition.

 

Despite the late filing and service of the opposition and reply papers, they were considered by the Court in ruling on the merits of the demurrer.  See CRC 3.1300(d).

 

ANALYSIS

 

Among others, a demurrer may be based on the grounds that the complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action; the complaint fails to allege whether the contracts sued on are written, oral or implied by conduct and/or that the pleading is uncertain and ambiguous.  CCP 430.10(e), (f), (g).

 

1st cause of action – Breach of Contract

 

As noted above, a demurrer lies when “in an action founded upon a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct.”  CCP 430.10(g).  While Plaintiffs allege that Voskanian and Keryan entered into a written “Stock Purchase Agreement” with Shew and Van, they also allege that they entered into 5 ancillary agreements: (1) Promissory Note, (2) Security Agreement, (3) Guaranty, (4) Non-Competition Agreement and (5) Escrow Agreement without any indication of whether these “ancillary agreements” are written oral or implied.  (See FAC ¶7).  Plaintiffs also allege that Defendants entered into “various agreements memorialized in a number of writings, some signed, others perhaps not.”  (FAC ¶55).  Plaintiffs do not attach and incorporate any agreements to the First Amended Complaint and have failed to set forth the terms of the “ancillary agreements.”

 

Within the breach of contract cause of action itself, Plaintiffs refer to “the Agreement” and “the contract;” however, it is not clear if Plaintiffs are referencing the “Stock Purchase Agreement” and/or one of the “ancillary agreements.” (FAC ¶30).  As such, it is uncertain which contract(s)/agreement(s) forms the basis(es) for the breach of contract claim, the terms of such contract, etc.

 

To state a claim for breach of written contract, the plaintiff must plead the existence of a contract, its terms which establish the obligation at issue, the occurrence of any conditions precedent to enforcement of the obligation, and the breach of that obligation.  FPI Development, Inc. (1991) 231 CA3d 367, 383.  A written contract may be pled by setting forth the terms, verbatim, in the complaint, by attaching a copy of the contract to the complaint and incorporating it by reference, or by pleading its legal effect (i.e., allege the substance of its relevant terms).  See McKell (2006) 142 CA4th 1457, 1489.  As noted above, while Plaintiffs may have pled terms of the “Stock Purchase Agreement,” they have not done so with the other agreements referenced in the First Amended Complaint and, also as noted above, it is not clear which contract, or contracts, form the basis/es for the claim. 

 

2nd cause of action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

If the allegations of a breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cause of action merely restate a breach of contract claim and seek the same damages, such a claim is superfluous and may be disregarded.  See Careau & Co. (1990) 222 CA3d 1371, 1395. 

 

Here, the breach of implied covenant claim appears to rely on the same facts as the defective breach of contract claim.  As such, this claim fails for the same reasons as the breach of contract claim.  Additionally, it appears that the breach of implied covenant claim merely restates the allegations and damages sought in the breach of contract cause of action.  (See FAC ¶¶35-36).  As such, it appears that the cause of action is unnecessarily duplicative.   

 

3rd cause of action – Fraud and Deceit

 

The elements of a fraud cause of action are: (1) false representation by defendant, (2) intent to deceive or induce reliance, (3) justifiable reliance by plaintiff, (4) resulting damages.  Service by Medallion, Inc. (1996) 44 CA4th 1807, 1816.  Fraud claims must be pled with specific facts showing how, when, where, to whom and by what means the misrepresentations were made.  Lazar (1996) 12 C4th 631, 645; Stansfield (1990) 220 CA3d 59, 73. 

 

Plaintiffs do not include any specific allegations within the 3rd cause of action itself.  Rather, Plaintiffs incorporate the previous and subsequent allegations and allege that “Defendants made certain [false] representations of fact as described above related to the operations, financial condition, and value of Five Star Fence.”  (See FAC ¶¶37-40).  It is not the job of Defendants and/or the Court to determine which allegations in the many preceding and subsequent paragraphs of the First Amended Complaint, Plaintiffs contend support their fraud claim.  Even considering the other allegations, Plaintiffs fail to specify which of the two Defendants made which specific misrepresentation, where, to whom and/or by what means. 

 

4th cause of action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

A claim for negligent misrepresentation has the same specific pleading requirements as a fraud claim.  Small (2003) 30 C4th 167, 184.   As with the fraud and deceit claim, Plaintiffs have failed to plead the negligent misrepresentation cause of action with the requisite factual specificity. 

 

6th cause of action – Declaratory Relief

 

In a declaratory relief cause of action, a plaintiff may seek a judicial declaration of the plaintiff’s rights or duties with respect to another in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties.  Alborzian (2015) 235 CA4th 29, 40.

 

Here, Plaintiffs allege that they and Defendants “entered into various agreements memorialized in a number of writings, some signed, others perhaps not. The Plaintiff and Defendants dispute the terms and/or enforceability of those agreements.”  (FAC ¶55).  Plaintiffs further allege that they are “requesting a judicial determination of the validity of all of the aforementioned agreements and a determination of the parties’ rights, title and interest in the action.”  (FAC ¶56).  As noted above, Plaintiffs have alleged the existence of a “Stock Purchase Agreement” and 5 “ancillary agreements” without specifying whether such ancillary agreements are written, oral or implied and without pleading the terms of same.  As such, it is not clear which of the various agreements mentioned in the First Amended Complaint form the basis(es) of Plaintiffs’ declaratory relief cause of action and/or how the Court can determine the parties’ rights, title and interest therein.

 

9th cause of action – Assault &  10th cause of action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Contrary to Defendants’ assertion, Plaintiffs were entitled to include these additional causes of action as they amended their complaint as a matter of right pursuant to CCP 472(a).  Unlike in Harris (2010) 185 CA4th 1018, 1023 relied on by Defendants, this Court has not issued an order sustaining a demurrer with leave to amend.  However, these claims fail for other reasons. 

 

The elements of an assault cause of action are: (1) defendant acted, intending to cause harmful or offensive contact, (2) plaintiff reasonably believed that plaintiff was about to be touched in a harmful or an offensive manner or that defendant threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; that it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; that plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct, (4) plaintiff was harmed and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  CACI 1301. 

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) defendant’s conduct was outrageous, (2) defendant intended to cause plaintiff emotional distress or defendant acted with reckless disregard of the probability that plaintiff would suffer emotional distress, knowing that plaintiff was present when the conduct occurred, (3) plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress and (4) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s severe emotional distress.  CACI 1600. 

 

These two causes of action are alleged solely by Voskanian against Shew and incorporate all of  the previous and subsequent paragraphs.  (See FAC ¶¶67, 72).  However, Voskanian fails to specify which of the allegations form the bases of these claims.  It is unclear how allegations regarding alleged breaches of contract and/or fraud regarding a business transaction could support these claims.  Again, it is not the responsibility of Shew or the Court to determine which allegations Voskanian intended to support his claims.     

 

CONCLUSION

 

The demurrer is sustained.  Due to the liberal policy of allowing leave to amend and because this is only the First Amended Complaint, Plaintiffs are given the opportunity to try to cure the defects in their pleading.  A Second Amended Complaint is due to be filed and served within 30 days.

 

The parties are reminded to comply with the filing deadlines and are warned that failure to timely file and/or serve papers in the future may result in the late papers not being considered by the Court.
