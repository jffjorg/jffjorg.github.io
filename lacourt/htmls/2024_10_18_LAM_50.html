DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV19947    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

MARCO DURAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

MERCEDES-BENZ USA, LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

22STCV19947

Hearing Date:

October 18, 2024

Hearing Time:

2:00 p.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES AND COSTS PURSUANT TO CIVIL CODE SECTION 1794(d)

 

 

Background   

Plaintiff Marco Duran (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on June 20, 2022 against Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“Defendant”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) breach of implied warranty of merchantability and (2) breach of express warranty.

Plaintiff indicates that he accepted an Offer to Compromise in this action on May 23, 2024. (Barry Decl., ¶¶ 11, 12, Ex. 4.) Paragraph 5 of the Offer to Compromise provides that “MBUSA will pay Plaintiff’s attorney’s fees, expenses and costs in the amount of $10,000.00 in full satisfaction of any attorney’s fees, expenses and costs owed to Plaintiff in this action. Alternatively, should the $10,000.00 be refused, MBUSA will allow the Court to determine, in a noticed motion filed pursuant to Civil Code Section 1794(d) and/or by Plaintiff’s filing of a memorandum of costs, the attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses recoverable under Civil Code Section 1794(d). There is no admission of liability by this Offer. However, if this Offer is accepted, MBUSA will stipulate that Plaintiff is the prevailing party for purposes of any motion for attorney’s fees, expenses and costs and/or memorandum of costs.” (Barry Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. 4.) Page 4 of the Offer to Compromise provides, inter alia, that “[f]or fees, costs and expenses, I hereby elect to file a motion and/or a memorandum of costs pursuant to paragraph 5.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff now moves for an order awarding him attorney fees, costs, and expenses. Defendant opposes.[1]

Discussion

The Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act grants car buyers the right to sue a manufacturer or retail seller for failure to comply with implied or express warranties. (Civ. Code, § 1794, subdivision (a).) “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794, subdivision (d).)

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate….The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work. The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” ((PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 [internal citations omitted]); (see Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 818 [applying the lodestar method to determine attorneys’ fees in Song-Beverly action].)

The Hourly Rate of Counsel

In his supporting declaration, Plaintiff’s counsel David N. Barry states that “[a] total of Five (5) attorneys were involved at various junctures in this case: Logan Pascal Esq., Elizabeth Quinn, Esq., Otis R. Hayes III, Esq., Debora Rabieian, Esq., and myself. Attorney Logan Pascal, Esq., an associate at TBLF[2] billing at $400 per hour, was the primary handling attorney in this matter.” (Barry Decl., ¶ 17, emphasis omitted.) Mr. Barry seeks an hourly rate of $675.00/hour. (Barry Decl., ¶ 22.) Mr. Barry’s background and experience is discussed in his supporting declaration. (Barry Decl., ¶¶ 23-25.) In addition, Mr. Pascal’s background and experience is discussed in his supporting declaration. (Pascal Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.)

Ms. Quinn seeks an hourly rate of $600 per hour. (Quinn Decl., ¶ 11.) Mr. Hayes III and Ms. Rabieian seek hourly rates of $500 per hour. (Hayes III Decl., ¶ 5; Rabieian Decl., ¶ 5.) The background and experience of Ms. Quinn, Mr. Hayes III, and Ms. Rabieian are set forth in their respective supporting declarations. (Quinn Decl., ¶¶ 4-11; Hayes III Decl., ¶¶ 3-5; Rabieian Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.)  

In the opposition, Defendant asserts that Mr. Barry’s claimed rate of $675/hour is excessive. The Court disagrees and finds that the hourly rate requested for Mr. Barry is reasonable and commensurate with rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and experience.[3] Defendant also states that it “suggests…a rate of $250/hr for Mr. Pascal and $300/hr for all other attorneys on this case.” (Opp’n. at p. 7:5-6.) However, the Court finds that the hourly rates requested for Mr. Pascal, Ms. Quinn, Mr. Hayes III, and Ms. Rabieian are reasonable and commensurate with rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and experience. Thus, the court declines to reduce these attorneys’ requested hourly rates.

 

Reasonableness of the Requested Fees

“[T]he court’s discretion in awarding attorney fees is … to be exercised so as to fully compensate counsel for the prevailing party for services reasonably provided to his or her client.” ((Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 395.) The trial court may reduce the award where the fee request appears unreasonably inflated, such as where the attorneys’ efforts are unorganized or duplicative. ((Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635, fn. 21.) “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.” (Horsford v. Bd. of Trustees of California State Univ., supra, 132 Cal.App.4th at p. 396.)

Plaintiff’s counsel attaches as Exhibit 5 to his supporting declaration a “copy of [his] firm’s time entries.” (Barry Decl., ¶ 19, Ex. 5.) The time entries show that Plaintiff seeks $69,030.00 in attorney’s fees for a total of 156.7 hours billed. (Barry Decl., ¶ 19, Ex. 5.)

In her declaration in support of the opposition, Defendant’s counsel states that “[a]ttached hereto as Exhibit I, is a spreadsheet setting forth MBUSA’s objections to and positions on Plaintiff’s counsel’s billing, addressing every billing entry made and recommending a reasonable adjustment for each.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.)[4]

In the spreadsheet, Defendant’s counsel objects to certain billing entries based on the asserted use of templated documents. (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) However, the Court does not find that the entries Defendant objects to reflect excessive or unreasonable amounts of time for the particular tasks specified. Although the use of forms and templates help with efficiency, counsel is still expected to review and modify each document for each individual case, and time spent to ensure that each document is appropriately modified is reasonable.

Defendant also objects to billing entries from 11/07/22, 7/17/23, 7/27/23, 7/28/23, 7/31/23, 8/01/23, 8/24/23, 2/16/24, 3/12/24, 3/21/24, and 4/08/24, which concern time spent by Plaintiff’s counsel to draft and revise memos “to the file” “re case status.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) Defendant asserts that such entries are “clerical work” and “internal comms,” and that the fees incurred for such work is thus “not recoverable.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) The Court notes that Defendant does not appear to cite any legal authority to support this proposition. Defendant does not appear to request that the Court deduct any portion of the time requested for these tasks.   

In addition, Defendant objects to a billing entry from 1/27/23 to “[r]eview file in preparation for the deposition of MBUSA’s Person(s) Benz USA, LLC - Pascal Most Knowledgeable” (2.50 hrs.); a billing entry from 4/4/23 to “Review File: in preparation for Telephonic Meet and Confer re: MBUSA’s Discovery Responses” (0.40 hrs); a billing entry from 7/13/23 to “Review File: in preparation of further telephonic meet and confer re: MBUSA’s discovery responses in advance of IDC” (0.70 hrs); a billing entry from 7/20/23 to “Review File: in preparation for Plaintiff’s deposition” (1.40 hrs); a billing entry from 7/26/23 to “Review file in preparation for Informal Discovery Conference” (1.50 hrs.); a billing entry from 8/04/23 to “Review file in preparation for deposition of Armando Recinos” (0.60 hrs.); a billing entry from 8/22/23 to “Review file in preparation for deposition of A. Recino” (1.00 hr.); a billing entry from 9/11/23 to “Review file in preparation for the deposition of Mercedes-Benz of Los Angeles’ Person Most Knowledgeable” (1.50 hrs); a billing entry from 9/25/23 to “Review file in preparation of telephonic meet and confer re MBUSA’s Supplemental responses and document production” (0.40 hrs.); a billing entry from 2/15/24 to “Review file in preparation of hearing on Plaintiffs Ex Parte…” (0.70 hrs.); a billing entry from 3/11/24 to “Review file in preparation for Status Conference re ADR” (0.50 hrs.); a billing entry from 3/20/24 to “Review file in preparation for hearing on MBUSA’s Motion for Summary Adjudication” (1.60 hrs.); and a billing entry from 4/05/24 to “Review file in preparation for hearing on Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application for an Order Shortening Time for hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions” (0.50 hrs.). (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) Defendant’s counsel argues that “there is no reason the handling attorney needs to review the file he has been consistently working on.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) However, the above-referenced billing entries pertain to time spent by Plaintiff’s counsel to review the file in preparation for certain specified events. The Court does not find that the above-referenced billing entries are excessive or unreasonable.

Defendant also objects to certain billing entries as “exaggerated,” “not necessary,” and “duplicative.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) The Court does not find that Defendant has shown that the billing entries objected to on these grounds reflect excessive or unreasonable amounts of time for the particular tasks specified, or that tasks are “duplicative.” The Court also notes that Defendant objects to time spent by Plaintiff’s counsel to review the file in preparation for depositions of Mercedes-Benz of Los Angeles Technicians as duplicative. (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I; see billing entries from 09/11/23.) However, these billing entries appear to refer to multiple Mercedes-Benz of Los Angeles Technicians with different numbers assigned to each technician. (Ibid.) Thus, the Court does not find that Defendant has shown that the time spent preparing for each deposition is duplicative.

Defendant also objects to a billing entry from 7/25/23 to “[r]eview MBUSA’s Notice of Change of Attorney,” on the grounds that “[t]here is no record of any such document filed at this time.” (Gallagher Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. I.) However, the Court notes that on July 25, 2023, Defendant filed a “Notice of Change of Handling Attorney” in this case.

In addition, Defendant asserts that “Plaintiff’s counsel also billed 8 hours of anticipated time for tasks related to fee motion and bills for tasks that have not been performed, billing hours for their anticipated reply and attendance at fee motion hearing. Only fees actually incurred are recoverable and because it has not yet occurred, this anticipated work is not recoverable.” (Opp’n at p. 5:8-11, emphasis omitted.) But Defendant does not appear to cite to any legal authority to support this proposition. In the motion, Plaintiff cites to McKenzie v. Ford Motor Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 695, 703, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he fees incurred in preparing a motion for fees are properly includable in the award. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133 [104 Cal. Rptr. 2d 377, 17 P.3d 735] [‘an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee’].).” (Emphasis omitted.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court declines to reduce or strike the time billed by Plaintiff’s counsel as requested by Defendant.

Lastly, the Court notes that Defendant argues that “Plaintiff is not entitled to a lodestar multiplier.” (Opp’n at p. 7:9.) However, Plaintiff does not appear to seek a lodestar multiplier. Thus, the Court does not see why Defendant’s opposition includes this argument.

Costs

Plaintiff seeks reimbursement for costs in the amount of $10,801.88. (Barry Decl., ¶ 21, Ex. 7.) In the opposition, Defendant states that it “intends to challenge Plaintiff’s costs via a properly noticed motion, which will be filed based on the time allowed per statute.” (Opp’n at p. 8:1-2.) Defendant “requests the Court to decline to award any costs until MBUSA’s motion to strike/tax costs is heard.” (Opp’n at p. 8:2-3.)

In the reply, Plaintiff asserts that “MBUSA did not file a timely motion to tax costs, pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(4), the Court should award all costs sought in Plaintiffs Motion.” (Reply at p. 9:20-22.) Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1700, subdivision (b)(1), “[a]ny notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6 (a)(4).” On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a “Proof of Service (CCP 1011)” indicating that the “Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Costs; Memorandum of Costs; Proposed Order/Judgment” was served on September 25, 2024. The Court notes that 15 days after September 25, 2024 is October 10, 2024. The docket does not appear to show that Defendant has filed any motion to strike and/or tax costs.

In light of the foregoing, the Court denies Defendant’s request that the Court “decline to award any costs until MBUSA’s motion to strike/tax costs is heard.” (Opp’n at p. 8:2-3.) Defendant does not indicate that it filed any motion to strike and/or tax costs. Defendant does not appear to otherwise challenge Plaintiff’s claimed costs. The Court finds that the $10,801.88 in requested costs is reasonable. (Barry Decl., ¶ 21, Ex. 7.)  

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs is granted in the total amount of $79,831.88 ($69,030.00 in attorney’s fees + $10,801.88 in costs.) 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Legal Standard

In a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party must prove by a preponderance of evidence the existence of the arbitration agreement and that the dispute is covered by the agreement. The burden then shifts to the resisting party to prove by a preponderance of evidence a ground for denial (e.g., fraud, unconscionability, etc.). ((Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413-414.)  

Generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues. ((Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2); (Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.)

“California has a strong public policy in favor of arbitration and any doubts regarding the arbitrability of a dispute are resolved in favor of arbitration.” ((Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.” ((Ibid. [internal quotations omitted].) This is in accord with the liberal federal policy favoring arbitration agreements under the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”), which governs all agreements to arbitrate in contracts “involving interstate commerce.” (9 U.S.C. section 2, et seq.; (Higgins v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1247.)

 

            Discussion

A.    Existence of Arbitration Agreement

In the FAC, Plaintiffs allege, inter alia, that “[a]t all relevant times, Plaintiffs were covered under the terms of a group health plan arranged by Margit’s employer, the Cascade County Union Elementary School District, a public school district. This group health plan is self-funded through the California Valued Trust (‘CVT’), a public schools trust. At all relevant times Margit and her family, including her son Nathaniel, were covered under the terms of this plan.” (FAC, ¶ 10.)

Defendants submit the Declaration of Kymberly Gilpin, the Operations Manager for California’s Valued Trust (“CVT”). (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 1.) Ms. Gilpin states that “[o]n September 1, 2022, Plaintiff Margit Stevens enrolled in a PPO healthcare plan that covered her and her family, including Plaintiff Nathaniel Stevens, for the 2023 calendar year. Anthem was the Claims Administrator for the plan.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 4.) Ms. Gilpin states that “[t]o obtain benefits under this healthcare plan, Plaintiff Margit Stevens’ completed and submitted an online enrollment form. Attached as Exhibit A is a true and correct copy of screenshots showing the online enrollment form Plaintiff Margit Stevens would have completed and submitted to obtain healthcare plan benefits for her and her family.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 5.)

Defendants note that Exhibit “A” to Ms. Gilpin’s declaration, the “online enrollment form,” provides, inter alia, that “I acknowledge that legal action to resolve any benefit dispute will be through arbitration.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. A, p. 10.)  

But as noted by Plaintiff, Exhibit “A” to Ms. Gilpin’s declaration appears to be a blank enrollment form. As Plaintiff notes, Ms. Gilpin “states that Margit ‘would have’ completed and submitted the online enrollment form but does not describe any electronic signature process.” (Opp’n at p. 3:14-16.) Defendants do not appear to provide any evidence that Margit Stevens signed any online enrollment form with the above-referenced arbitration language. In her declaration in support of the opposition, Margit Stevens states that “I have reviewed the screenshots attached as Exhibit A to the Gilpin Declaration. While I generally recall scrolling through the enrollment website, I do not recall the single arbitration sentence buried in Exhibit A, page 13 of the screenshots, nor do I recall clicking an ‘I agree’ or ‘submit’ button with respect to such arbitration language. In fact there is no ‘submit’ button or other mechanism for agreement on the screenshot page containing the arbitration language.” (Stevens Decl., ¶ 4.)

            The parties cite to Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 843-844, where the Court of Appeal noted that “Civil Code section 1633.9 addresses how a proponent of an electronic signature may authenticate the signature—that is, show the signature is, in fact, the signature of the person the proponent claims it is. The statute states: ‘(a) An electronic record or electronic signature is attributable to a person if it was the act of the person. The act of the person may be shown in any manner, including a showing of the efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.’ (Civil Code section 1633.9, subdivision (a), italics added.) Main summarily asserted in her initial declaration that Ruiz was the person who electronically signed the 2011 agreement ‘on or about September 21, 2011,’ but she did not explain how she arrived at that conclusion or inferred Ruiz was the person who electronically signed the 2011 agreement. To be sure, ‘Ernesto Zamora Ruiz (Electronic Signature)’ and ‘9/21/2011 11:47 AM’ appear in print on signature and date lines of the 2011 agreement, and Main apparently retrieved the proffered ‘true and correct copy’ of the 2011 agreement from Moss Bros.’s personnel records. But Main never explained how Ruiz’s printed electronic signature, or the date and time printed next to the signature, came to be placed on the 2011 agreement. More specifically, Main did not explain how she ascertained that the electronic signature on the 2011 agreement was ‘the act of’ Ruiz. (Civ. Code, § 1633.9, subd. (a).).” Here, Defendants do not appear to even argue that Margit Stevens’s electronic signature, or any other “act” of Margit Stevens appears on any online enrollment form.[1]

In addition, Defendants do not appear to assert that they are parties to the subject “online enrollment form” attached as Exhibit “A” to Ms. Gilpin’s declaration. As noted by Plaintiffs, “[t]he screenshot sentence does not state that it is an agreement to arbitrate with the claims administrator or, specifically, Anthem.” (Opp’n at p. 6:13-14.)

Ms. Gilpin also states in her supporting declaration that “[a]s part of the enrollment process, CVT sent Plaintiff Margit Stevens a welcome packet confirming that her healthcare plan was effective as of September 1, 2022…The packet…included a plan benefit summary that included the statement ‘This summary is for comparison purposes only. Please refer to the actual benefit booklet for complete benefits at www.cvtrust.org/plan- documents.’” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 7, emphasis omitted.) Ms. Gilpin states that “[t]he Benefit Booklet detailed the particular terms of Plaintiffs’ healthcare plan. A true and correct copy of this Benefit Booklet is attached as Exhibit C.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 8.) Defendants note that the “Benefit Booklet” provides, inter alia, as follows:

 

“BINDING ARBITRATION

 

Any dispute or claim, of whatever nature, arising out of, in connection with, or in relation to this plan or breach or rescission thereof, or in relation to care or delivery of care, including any claim based on contract, tort, or statute, must be resolved by arbitration if the amount sought exceeds the jurisdictional limit of the small claims court. Any dispute or claim within the jurisdictional limits of the small claims court will be resolved in such court.

 

The Federal Arbitration Act will govern the interpretation and enforcement of all proceedings under this Binding Arbitration provision. To the extent that the Federal Arbitration Act is inapplicable, or is held not to require arbitration of a particular claim, state law governing agreements to arbitrate will apply.

 

The member and CVT agree to be bound by this Binding Arbitration provision and acknowledge that they are each giving up their right to a trial by court or jury.

 

The member and CVT agree to give up the right to participate in class arbitration against each other. Even if applicable law permits class arbitration, the member waives any right to pursue, on a class basis, any such controversy or claim against CVT and CVT waives any right to pursue on a class basis any such controversy or claim against the member.

 

The arbitration findings will be final and binding except to the extent that state or Federal law provides for the judicial review of arbitration proceedings.

 

The arbitration is begun by the member making written demand on CVT. The arbitration will be conducted by Judicial Arbitration and Medication Services (‘JAMS’) according to its applicable Rules and Procedures. If, for any reason, JAMS is unavailable to conduct the arbitration, the arbitration will be conducted by another neutral arbitration entity, by mutual agreement of the member and CVT, or by order of the court, if the member and CVT cannot agree. The arbitration will be held at a time and location mutually agreeable to the member and CVT.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, p. 155, emphasis in original.)

            Defendants contend that “Plaintiffs did not initiate arbitration as required, but instead filed suit in this Court.” (Pet. at p. 5:7.)

            As an initial matter, in the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that Defendants are “not a party to the terms of Plaintiffs’ Booklet,” and that “[c]onsistent therewith, the Booklet’s Binding Arbitration provision limits the arbitration agreement to members and CVT.” (Opp’n at p. 5:7; 5:9-10.) Indeed, as set forth above, the subject arbitration provision provides, inter alia, that “[t]he member and CVT agree to be bound by this Binding Arbitration provision and acknowledge that they are each giving up their right to a trial by court or jury.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, p. 155, emphasis in original, underline added.) As noted by Plaintiffs, “[b]ecause arbitration is a matter of contract, generally one must be a party to an arbitration agreement to be bound by it or invoke it.” ((DMS Services, LLC v. Superior Court (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1346, 1352 [internal quotations omitted].) Here, Defendants do not appear to contend that the subject “Benefit Booklet” is an agreement between Defendants and Plaintiffs. 

            In the petition, Defendants argue that “under the equitable estoppel doctrine, a plaintiff cannot circumvent an arbitration provision in a contract by suing non-signatories to the contract.” (Pet. at p. 10:17-18.) Defendants cite to JSM Tuscany, LLC v. Superior Court (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1222, 1237, where the Court of Appeal noted that under the doctrine of equitable estoppel, “as applied in both federal and California decisional authority, a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are intimately founded in and intertwined with the underlying contract obligations. By relying on contract terms in a claim against a nonsignatory defendant, even if not exclusively, a plaintiff may be equitably estopped from repudiating the arbitration clause contained in that agreement.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.)

            Defendants assert that here, “Plaintiffs’ claims are premised upon and inextricably tied to their health plan contract with CVT—and in particular, the terms in the Benefit Booklet. They are estopped from opposing arbitration on the ground that Anthem is a non-signatory to that contract, when they are pursuing Anthem for claims under the very same contract.” (Pet. at p. 12:7-10.) In the FAC, Plaintiffs allege, inter alia, that “[t]he contract form setting forth the plan benefits under Plaintiffs’ plan is called the Benefit Booklet (the ‘Booklet’).” (FAC, ¶ 12.) Plaintiffs allege that “[i]n a letter dated September 6, 2023, Anthem denied Plaintiffs’ request to authorize G-POEM[2] for Nathaniel stating that it was ‘investigational’ per SURG.00047.” (FAC, ¶ 27.) “On September 17, 2023, Nathaniel sent a letter to Anthem’s Grievances & Appeals Department appealing the denial of his request for G-POEM.” (FAC, ¶ 28.) “In a letter dated November 16, 2023, Anthem advised that it was denying Plaintiffs’ appeal.” (FAC, ¶ 29.) Plaintiffs allege that “[a]lthough Anthem’s original denial and appeal denial letters quote from the Booklet’s definition of an investigational service (‘not widely accepted as proven and effective within the organized medical community’), neither references any view of ‘the organized medical community’ regarding G-POEM.” (FAC, ¶ 30.)

            Plaintiffs further allege that “Anthem on behalf of Anthem Life assumed all duties of claims administration, claims adjudication, and appeals. By assuming these duties, Anthem assumed a duty of due care to Nathaniel as a covered member to avoid physical harm to him resulting from its claims administration of benefits under the Booklet.” (FAC, ¶ 44.) Plaintiffs allege, inter alia, that “Anthem did not perform the promises made in the Booklet. When Plaintiffs appealed the denial of Nathaniel’s care, Anthem’s Grievances and Appeals Department did not conduct a review of its investigational position and ignored the substance of Nathaniel’s appeal, including the referenced medical article and Clinical Practice Update (CPU) from the American Gastroenterology Association. Rather, Anthem relied on SURG.00047 which Anthem knew contained false statements regarding G-POEM.” (FAC, ¶ 58.)

            In the opposition, Plaintiffs do not appear to dispute that their allegations against Defendants involve the “Benefit Booklet.” However, Plaintiffs argue that equitable estoppel does not apply here. Plaintiffs indicate that the deposition of Kristyn Nelms, Defendants’ Person Most Qualified, was taken on April 23, 2024. (Barrio Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs note that Ms. Nelms testified as follows: “Q. Okay. So who is responsible for creating the benefits booklet?... THE WITNESS: We work collaboratively with the CVT team to finalize the benefit booklet documents.” (Barrio Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 4 (Nelms Depo.) at pp. 42:24-43:4.) Ms. Nelms further testified that “I think I mentioned earlier, CVT has been a client for 40 years, so I don’t know how the document was initially created. But as I suggested earlier, or stated earlier, I should say, that every year we look at the summary plan description or the benefit booklet, incorporate any new benefit changes that have been made since the prior document was finalized, and then add those to the newer document and send those to CVT for their final review and blessing before they became finalized.” (Id. at p. 43:12-22.) Plaintiffs also cite to the following testimony from Ms. Nelms’ deposition: “Q. So now I’m going to direct your attention to Bates 5 in Exhibit 2. So I’m on page 5 of -- of the -- of this exhibit…. Q. It says (as read): On behalf of trust, Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health shall produce and maintain a master copy of the benefits booklet and make changes and amendments to the master copy of the benefits booklet and incorporate any approved exchanges or amendments pursuant to Article 18A of this agreement.” (Id. at pp. 40:24-41:17.)

            Plaintiffs assert that Defendants accordingly “drafted and agreed to the language of the Booklet omitting it from the arbitration agreement.” (Opp’n at p. 9:5-6.) Defendants assert that “[t]here…would be no fairness in allowing Anthem to draft an arbitration clause excluding itself then invoke arbitration through the backdoor with equitable estoppel when it was advantageous for it to do so…” (Opp’n at p. 9:22-24.) Plaintiffs cite to Goldman v. KPMG, LLP (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 209, 235, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[o]ne may recall that the linchpin for equitable estoppel is equity—fairness, and that the application of the doctrine is fact specific.” (Internal quotations and citation omitted.)

            Plaintiffs also asserts that the circumstances “compare[] to the kind of lack of mutuality that constitutes substantive unconscionability.” (Opp’n at p. 9:24-25.) Plaintiffs cite to Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 120, where the California Supreme Court noted that “an arbitration agreement imposed in an adhesive context lacks basic fairness and mutuality if it requires one contracting party, but not the other, to arbitrate all claims arising out of the same transaction or occurrence or series of transactions or occurrences. The arbitration agreement in this case lacks mutuality in this sense because it requires the arbitration of employee--but not employer--claims arising out of a wrongful termination. An employee terminated for stealing trade secrets, for example, must arbitrate his or her wrongful termination claim under the agreement while the employer has no corresponding obligation to arbitrate its trade secrets claim against the employee.”

Similarly here, Defendants could argue that they are not required to arbitrate their claims against a member per the terms of the Benefit Booklet’s arbitration provision, while also arguing that a member must arbitrate their claims against Defendants pursuant to the doctrine of equitable estoppel. As discussed above, the subject arbitration provision in the Benefit Booklet provides that it pertains to the “member” and “CVT,” not Defendants. Specifically, the arbitration provision provides, inter alia, that “[t]he member and CVT agree to be bound by this Binding Arbitration provision and acknowledge that they are each giving up their right to a trial by court or jury.” (Gilpin Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, p. 155, emphasis in original.) Moreover, Defendants do not appear to dispute that they assisted in drafting the Benefit Booklet containing the above-referenced arbitration provision that only references “CVT.” Now, however, they seek to compel Plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims against Defendants, who are not referenced in the subject arbitration provision.

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs that under the facts of this case, it would be inequitable to apply the doctrine of equitable estoppel, which, as set forth above, is grounded in principles of fairness. (Goldman v. KPMG, LLP, supra, 173 Cal.App.4th at page 235, “the ‘linchpin’ for equitable estoppel ‘is equity—fairness’.”) As noted in Metalclad Corp. v. Ventana Environmental Organizational Partnership (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1705, 1714, cited by Defendants, the equitable estoppel doctrine “prevents a party from playing fast and loose with its commitment to arbitrate, honoring it when advantageous and circumventing it to gain undue advantage.”

In light of the foregoing, the Court does not find that Defendan8ts have demonstrated that an arbitration agreement exists between Plaintiffs and Defendants. In addition, under the circumstances, the Court does not find that Defendants may invoke the arbitration provision of the Benefit Booklet under the doctrine of equitable estoppel to compel Plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims against Defendants.

            Defendants also argue that “the arbitration provisions’ plain language provides that gateway questions of arbitrability—such as interpretation and operation of the arbitration provisions themselves—are for the arbitrator, and not the courts, to decide.” (Petition at p. 8:25-27.) As an initial matter, as set forth above, the Court does not find that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties. Plaintiffs thus note that “Anthem’s arbitrability argument puts the proverbial cart before the horse because it presupposes there is an agreement between the parties to arbitrate…” (Opp’n at p. 11:2-3.)[3]

As the Court does not find that Defendants have demonstrated that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties, the Court need not analyze Plaintiffs’ arguments pertaining to unconscionability.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ petition to compel arbitration is denied.
