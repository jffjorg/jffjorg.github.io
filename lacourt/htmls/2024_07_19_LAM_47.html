DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV32007    Hearing Date: July 19, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     July 19, 2024                          JUDGMENT: September 1, 2023

                                                          

CASE:                         Diane Bradley, et al. v. Swagg Media, Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV32007           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH SUBPOENA DUCES TECUM; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs and Judgment Creditors Diane Bradley and DAB Promotions, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 7/16/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         08/21/20: Complaint filed.

·         09/01/23: Judgment entered.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a breach of contract and fraud action. Plaintiffs allege that the parties entered into a joint venture agreement in 2019 regarding the promotion and production of an event called the Fall Ball All Star Event. Plaintiffs contend that Defendants misrepresented that the event was scheduled for March 21, 2020, and that certain celebrities would appear.

 

Plaintiffs, as judgment creditors, move to compel Judgment Debtor Craig Nobles to comply with a subpoena duces tecum, and for sanctions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Compliance with Subpoena Duces Tecum is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiffs, as judgment creditors, move to compel Judgment Debtor Craig Nobles to comply with a subpoena duces tecum, and for sanctions.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1(a) states, in relevant part:

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion . . . may make an order . . .  directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1(a).) There is no meet and confer requirement in section 1987.1. There is also no requirement for a separate statement when no response has been provided to the request for discovery. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(b)(1).) The moving party need not show good cause for production of documents in connection with a deposition subpoena. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2020.510(b); Terry v. SLICO (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 352, 358.)

 

Analysis

 

            Judgment Creditors served Judgment Debtor Craig Nobles with a subpoena duces tecum for financial records via personal service on October 22, 2023. (Declaration of Robert S. Crowder ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-3, Exhs. A-B.) Judgment Creditors state that the Judgment Debtor produced certain documents but did not make a complete disclosure. However, Judgment Creditors have not provided a Separate Statement, as is required where there has been a response to a request for discovery. (See Cal. Rules of Court 3.1345(b)(1).) In fact, Judgment Creditors do not provide any indication of what has been produced and what has not been produced beyond the arguments in their Memorandum of Points and Authorities, which are entirely unsupported by documentary evidence or sworn statements by any individual with personal knowledge. (See Motion p.8:10-20.) This is not a sufficient showing to demonstrate what further compliance should be ordered by the Court.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Compliance with Subpoena Duces Tecum is DENIED.

 

            Moving Parties to give notice.

//

IT IS SO ORDERED.

 

Dated: July 19, 2024                           ___________________________________

CASE:                         Gladys Navarro v. South Bay Safety Inc., et al

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV38140           

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES AND COSTS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Gladys Navarro

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 07/16/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         10/05/20: Complaint filed.

·         10/06/20: First Amended Complaint Filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for wrongful termination. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants terminated her employment in retaliation for taking sick days.

 

Plaintiff moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs as against the remaining Defendants, South Bay Safety, Inc. 46 and South Bay Safety, Inc.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is CONTINUED to Friday, August 2, 2024 at 9:00 AM.

 

            Plaintiff is directed to serve and file any supplemental evidence supporting its request for attorney’s fees by Friday, July 26, 2024.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs as against the remaining Defendants, South Bay Safety, Inc. 46 and South Bay Safety, Inc.

 

//

Entitlement to Fees

 

Plaintiff requests an award of attorney’s fees as the prevailing party against the remaining Defendants—who have defaulted—on claims under the Labor Code.  Labor Code section 218.5 authorizes an award of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs to the prevailing party in any action brought “for the nonpayment of wages, fringe benefits, or health and welfare or pension fund contributions.” (Labor Code. § 218.5.) Plaintiff brought individual wage and hour claims under Labor Code sections 245 through 246.5, as well as a claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy rooted in the same facts. (See generally FAC.) Plaintiff is therefore entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees under Labor Code section 218.5 as the prevailing party.

 

Reasonableness of Fees

 

            Plaintiff’s counsel requests an award of $52,425 in attorney’s fees.

 

Reasonable attorney fees are ordinarily determined by the Court pursuant to the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096; Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004 [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].) “[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….”  (Ibid.) In setting the hourly rate for a fee award, courts are entitled to consider the “fees customarily charged by that attorney and others in the community for similar work.” (Bihun v. AT&T Info. Sys., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 976, 997 [affirming rate of $450 per hour], overruled on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 664.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney’s fees to prove the reasonableness of the fees. (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 615.) 

 

The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Indus. Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-275.) 

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s counsel requests an award for fees actually incurred in the amount of $52,425, based on a total of 83.5 hours of work spent by the firm on this case by four attorneys: 50 hours billed by Tielle Shu at $600 per hour, 4.5 hours by Vincent Granberry at $750 per hour, 22 hours by Danielle Montero at $375 per hour, and 27 hours by Melanie Rodriguez at $400 per hour.  (Declaration of Tielle Shu ISO Mot. ¶ 49.) All four attorneys attest to their training, experience, and hourly rates. (Shu Decl. ¶ 36; Declaration of Danielle Montero ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2, 4; Declaration of Vincent Granberry ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-4; Declaration of Mielanie Rodriguez ISO Mot.¶¶ 2-3.) Attorneys Shu and Rodriguez also provide itemized billing records showing the tasks performed and the hours billed. (Shu Decl. Exh. 1., Rodriguez Decl. Exh. 1.) Attorney Granberry does not provide itemized records, but states that he performed 4.5 hours of work on “initial case review, initial demand [he] drafted and sent to defendants, and reviewing this Motion for Attorney’s Fees.” (Granberry Decl. ¶ 4.) Although Plaintiff states that Attorney Montero billed 22 hours of attorney time, no itemized record nor explanation of how those hours were billed is included in the motion. Such an exhibit is referenced in Attorney Montero’s Declaration at paragraph 6, but it was not included in the papers. Without this evidence, the Court cannot approve the fee award in its entirety. That said, since the absence of this exhibit appears to be an inadvertent omission, the Court will permit Plaintiff to submit that evidence as a supplemental filing before ruling on the fee request.

 

Costs

 

            Plaintiff also moves for an award of $4,157.37 in costs pursuant to the Memorandum of Costs filed and served on May 29, 2024. Because the Court is permitting supplemental briefing on the fee award, the Court declines to rule on the award of costs at this time. However, the Court notes that there has been no challenge to the Memorandum of Costs.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

For the reasons above, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is CONTINUED to Friday, August 2, 2024 at 9:00 AM.

 

            Plaintiff is directed to serve and file any supplemental evidence supporting its request for attorney’s fees by Friday, July 26, 2024.

 

            Moving party to give notice.

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     July 19, 2024              TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Jose Vallerivas v. Staffmark Investment LLC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV05902           

 

(1)   MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO INTERROGATORIES

(2)   MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)(2) Plaintiff Plaintiff Jose Vallerivas

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1)(2) Defendant CEVA Logistics LLC.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an employment discrimination and wage and hour action that was filed on March 16, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that he was discriminated against and ultimately terminated because he took medical leave to recuperate from a back injury.  

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production propounded to Defendant CEVA Logistics LLC.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED. This ruling is conditioned on Plaintiff’s payment of $60 in filing fees to the Court within 5 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED.

 

            Defendant’s requests for sanctions are DENIED.

 

//

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form and Special Interrogatories

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to form and special interrogatories propounded to Defendant CEVA Logistics LLC.

 

Two Motions in One

 

            Multiple motions should not be combined into a single filing.¿(See¿Govt. Code,¿§ 70617(a)(4) [setting forth the required filing fee for each motion, application, or any other paper or request requiring a hearing];¿see¿also¿Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011)¿[“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”].)

 

Here, Plaintiff improperly combined two Motions to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories into a single filing. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions to avoid payment of separate filing fees deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect. The Court therefore conditions its ruling on this motion on the payment of an additional $60 in filing fees within 5 calendar days of this order.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to an interrogatory when the court finds that: “(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[;] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[; or] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Timing:

 

            A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

            Plaintiff served Defendant with the interrogatories at issue on this motion on January 15, 2024. (Declaration of Melad N. Haddad ISO Mot. ¶ 4.) Defendant served responses on March 15, 2024. (Id. ¶ 7.) During the parties’ attempts to informally resolve this dispute, the parties agreed to several extensions of the deadline to bring this motion, with the final deadline being May 30, 2024. (¶¶ 8-13, Exhs. 11-12.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that the motion was filed on May 30, 2024, but was rejected. Defendant’s counsel concedes that a motion to compel further responses was served on May 30, 2024 as part of a set of discovery motions, but the notice of motion at that time set hearings for July 8 and July 15, 2024. (Declaration of Orlando Arrellano ISO Opp. ¶ 12.) Because the motions were not filed, those hearings were not set and did not take place. Defendant thus argues that the motion before the Court is untimely because the original notice was invalid and the current motion was served on June 18, well after the agreed-upon deadline.

 

In reply, Plaintiff asks the Court to exercise its inherent authority to control its processes to permit this motion to be heard. The Court lacks jurisdiction to grant such leave if the motion was not timely filed.  A failure to comply with the 45-day notice requirement robs the Court of jurisdiction to hear a motion to compel further responses. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) Moreover, even if the Court could hear the motion under its inherent authority, nothing in this record demonstrates that such relief should be granted. Plaintiff offers no explanation for a three-week delay in re-serving the motion with a corrected notice of hearing, nor does Plaintiff account for the additional week’s delay in filing the motion. Plaintiff’s motion was not timely filed nor served and must therefore be denied.

 

Sanctions

 

            Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel for bringing an untimely motion to compel further responses.

 

            Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subd. (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.300(d).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Id.)

 

            Here, Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,000 for fees incurred opposing both this motion and the accompanying motion to compel further responses to requests for production, discussed below. Defendant does not provide any explanation of how this amount was reached beyond a bare assertion that Attorney Arrellano incurred 5 hours at $400 per hour opposing Plaintiff’s motions. (Arellano Decl. ¶ 17.) This statement is not sufficient to meet Defendant’s burden to demonstrate the reasonableness of the fees sought. The Court therefore declines to award sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED.

 

            Defendant’s request for sanctions is also DENIED.

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

 

Legal Standards

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to a demand for inspection when the court finds that: “(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the moving party to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) These facts must also be set forth in a separate statement filed by the moving party. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(c).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Timeliness

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)  The timeline for service of and response to the discovery, extensions of the deadline for moving to compel, and the service and filing of this motion are the same as for the motion to compel further responses to interrogatories.  For the reasons explained above, the Court rules Plaintiff’s motion was neither timely filed nor served and must therefore be denied.

 

Sanctions

 

            Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel for bringing an untimely motion to compel further responses.

 

            Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subd. (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to requests for production against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.310(h).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Id.)

 

Here, Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,000 for fees incurred opposing both this motion and the accompanying motion to compel further responses to interrogatories, discussed above. Defendant does not provide any explanation of how this amount was reached beyond a bare assertion that Attorney Arrellano incurred 5 hours at $400 per hour opposing Plaintiff’s motions. (Arellano Decl. ¶ 17.) This statement is not sufficient to meet Defendant’s burden to demonstrate the reasonableness of the fees sought. The Court therefore declines to award sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED. 

 

            Defendant’s request for sanctions is also DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED. This ruling is conditioned on Plaintiff’s payment of $60 in filing fees to the Court within 5 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production from Defendant CEVA Logistics, LLC is DENIED.

 

            Defendant’s requests for sanctions are DENIED.

 

            Moving party to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     July 19, 2024                                      TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         David Oday, et al. v. 118 Wadsworth Avenue Homeowners Association, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV24766           

 

MOTION TO DISSOLVE PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant 118 Wadsworth Avenue Homeowners Association

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs David Oday and Lakota Patrick Ford

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         10/11/23: Complaint filed.

·         01/10/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         03/01/24: Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a breach of contract and habitability defect action. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants refused to replace the roof of Plaintiff’s condominium, causing extensive water damage and mold throughout the unit. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants improperly issued special assessments in violation of the operative covenants, conditions, and restrictions on the property, and retaliated against Plaintiffs for raising these issues.

 

Defendant 118 Wadsworth Avenue Homeowner’s Association moves to dissolve the preliminary injunction entered on February 22, 2024 because of Plaintiffs’ failure to post the bond required.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant’s Motion to Dissolve Preliminary Injunction is DENIED on the condition that Plaintiffs serve and file an agreement authorizing the deposit to be applied to enforce the liability of the principal, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 995.710(c), within 10 days of this order.

 

If the above condition is not satisfied, Defendant’s Motion to Dissolve Preliminary Injunction is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant 118 Wadsworth Avenue Homeowner’s Association moves to dissolve the preliminary injunction entered on February 22, 2024 because of Plaintiffs’ failure to post the bond required.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 533 states:

 

In any action, the court may on notice modify or dissolve an injunction or temporary restraining order upon a showing that there has been a material change in the facts upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted, that the law upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted has changed, or that the ends of justice would be served by the modification or dissolution of the injunction or temporary restraining order.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 533.) Modification of a preliminary injunction “rests in the sound discretion of the trial court upon a consideration of all the particular circumstances of each individual case.” (Union Interchange, Inc. v. Savage (1959) 52 Cal.2d 601, 606.) The party seeking modification has the burden of proving that the request is justified. (See Loeffler v. Medina (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1504.)

 

Analysis

 

Defendant 118 Wadsworth Avenue Homeowner’s Association moves to dissolve the preliminary injunction entered on February 22, 2024 on the grounds that Plaintiffs failed to post the undertaking of $70,416.09 ordered by the Court. (See February 22, 2024 Minute Order.)

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition, state that payment in the amount specified by the Court’s order was deposited with the Court on June 26, 2024, with notice given to Defendant the next day. (Declaration of Scott J. Kalter ISO Opp. Exhs. 1-2.) In reply, Defendant contends that Plaintiffs’ deposit of the funds to the Court is effective as a posting of a bond under Code of Civil Procedure section 995.710, but only if the deposit is accompanied “by an agreement executed by the principal authorizing the officer to collect, sell, or otherwise apply the deposit to enforce the liability of the principal on the deposit.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 995.710(c).) Defendant contends that Plaintiffs have not executed such an agreement, and, therefore, that the injunction should be dissolved.

 

The Court concurs with Defendant that an authorization to dispose of the funds to satisfy liability is required for Plaintiffs’ deposit to be considered posting of the bond under section 995.710. However, because Plaintiffs have demonstrated an attempt to comply with the spirit of the Court’s order, the Court is not inclined to dissolve the injunction for a procedural violation without first providing an opportunity to cure the violation. The Court will therefore order Plaintiffs to serve and file an agreement authorizing the deposit to be applied to enforce the liability of the principal, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 995.710(c), within 10 days of this order.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Dissolve Preliminary Injunction is DENIED on the condition that Plaintiffs serve and file an agreement authorizing the deposit to be applied to enforce the liability of the principal, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 995.710(c), within 10 days of this order.

 

If the above condition is not satisfied, Defendant’s Motion to Dissolve Preliminary Injunction is GRANTED.

 

Moving party to give notice.
