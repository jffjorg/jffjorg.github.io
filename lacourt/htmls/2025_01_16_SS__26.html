Case Number: 22STLC08314    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 26

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant Hillcrest Renaissance Associates, LP’s Motion to Quash Writ of Execution and Sheriff’s Levy is GRANTED.

ANALYSIS:

On December 14, 2022, Plaintiff Alejandro Diaz (“Petitioner”) filed the instant action for civil rights violations against Defendants Arif Hussain Siddiqui (erroneously sued as Arif Hussain Siddiqvi aka Arif Hussain Siddiqi aka Arif H. Siddiqi aka Arif Siddiqi) (“Defendant Siddiqui”), Hillcrest Renaissance Associates, LP (“Defendant Hillcrest”), and Alhambra Renaissance, LLC (“Defendant Alhambra”). Defendant Siddiqui filed an answer on February 6, 2023. On February 14, 2023, the Court entered Defendant Alhambra’s default. Plaintiff added Aneesa Arif Siddiqi (“Defendant Siddiqi”) to the Complaint via doe amendment on April 4, 2023. Defendant Siddiqi answered on June 5, 2023. Defendants Siddiqui, Siddiqi, and Hillcrest were dismissed on April 23, 2024. Default judgment was entered against Defendant Alhambra on February 29, 2024.

On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a writ of execution with respect to Defendant Alhambra. Defendant Hillcrest filed the instant Motion to Quash Writ of Execution and Sheriff’s Levy on November 21, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

Discussion

Defendant Hillcrest moves to quash the writ of execution and Sheriff’s levy on the grounds that the real property sought pursuant to the levy is not owed by Defendant Alhambra, but by Defendant Hillcrest. The Motion is brought pursuant to case law set forth in Wilkins v. Wilkins (1950) 95 Cal.App.2d 605, 610-611, which holds “While the creditor is, as a matter of right, entitled to an execution, the affidavit, order and execution issued thereon are subject to review and the execution is subject to recall, upon motion, timely made, at which time the trial court may exercise its judicial discretion in determining whether the facts set forth in the affidavit are true, and whether there has been a misrepresentation of the facts or wilful suppression of facts which should have been related to the court in the first instance . . . .” (Wilkins v. Wilkins (1950) 95 Cal.App.2d 605, 610.) Even earlier cases have held the same of the Court’s authority: “The right of the court to recall the execution cannot be doubted if the issuance was improperly or inadvertently made or authority therefore revoked.” (Creditors' Adjustment Co. v. Newman (1921) 185 Cal. 509, 511.) Also, any action that follows from a recalled writ is likewise subject to recall. (Wellborn v. Wellborn (1942) 55 Cal.App.2d 516, 525.)

The Motion attaches evidence showing that the levy seeks to attach property commonly known as 1 E Main Street, Alhambra, California, 91801. (Motion, Exh. C, ¶1B.) Defendant Hillcrest submits the declaration of its managing member, Thomas Slaught, who declares that it owns the subject property, not Defendant Alhambra. (Motion, Slaught Decl., Exh. D.) The declaration is supported by a title report showing title to the subject property is vested in the name of Hillcrest Renaissance Associates, LP. (Ibid.) Based on this evidence, to which there is no opposition, the Court finds grounds to quash the writ of execution, and the subsequently issued levy.

Conclusion 

Defendant Hillcrest Renaissance Associates, LP’s Motion to Quash Writ of Execution and Sheriff’s Levy is GRANTED.

Case Number: 23STLC02269    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 26

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant Taochih Peng’s Motion to Vacate Default and Default Judgment is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

On April 6, 2023, Plaintiff United Financial Casualty Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this action for automobile subrogation against Defendant Taochih Peng (“Defendant”). Following Defendant’s failure to file a responsive pleading, default was entered on September 30, 2024. The case was then called for trial on October 3, 2024, at which time Plaintiff appeared and represented that a request for default judgment had been filed. (Minute Order, 10/03/24.) The Court set an order to show cause regarding entry of default judgment for March 25, 2024. (Ibid.) Defendant appeared after the case had been called and was informed that notice of the order to show cause would be mailed. (Ibid.) Default judgment was entered against Defendant on October 4, 2024.

On December 18, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Default and/or Default Judgment (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed an opposition on December 20, 2024.

Discussion

Defendant moves to vacate the entry of default and default judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). An application for discretionary relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) must be made within a reasonable time, no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought, and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the party’s excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) The motion must also be accompanied by a copy of the moving defendant’s proposed pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) This can be corrected if the defendant submits a proposed responsive pleading by the hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403.)

The Motion is supported by Defendant’s declaration, which does not provide an explanation as to their failure to file a responsive pleading upon service of the Summons and Complaint. (Motion, Peng Decl.) Instead, Defendant admits that they were served in this action on April 28, 2023. (Id. at ¶8.) The declaration addresses why Defendant was late to appear for the trial date, but this has no bearing on the default entered on September 30, 2024. Also, as Plaintiff points out, the Motion is not accompanied by a copy of Defendant’s proposed responsive pleading, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). Therefore, relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) cannot be granted.

Conclusion

Defendant Taochih Peng’s Motion to Vacate Default and Default Judgment is DENIED.

Case Number: 23STLC06528    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 26

Avilez v. The Guarantee Company of North America USA et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiff Jose Avilez’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED. JUDGMENT IS ENTERED BY DEFAULT AGAINST DEFENDANT RAYMOND SHARIFPOUR DBA WHITE DIAMOND AUTO SALES. PLAINTIFF IS ALSO AWARDED SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,860.00. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. 

ANALYSIS:

On October 11, 2023, Plaintiff Jose R. Avilez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Raymond Sharifpour dba White Diamond Auto Sales (“Defendant Sharifpour”) and The Guaranty Company (“Defendant Guaranty”). Defendants filed an answer on December 12, 2023. On June 26 and 27, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Minute Orders 06/26/24, 07/26/24.) Defendant Sharifpour was ordered to serve verified responses and pay sanctions within 20 days’ service of the order. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff filed the first Motion for Terminating and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant Sharifpour on August 30, 2024, seeking an order striking their answer and entering default. Defendant Sharifpour opposed the first Motion, which came for hearing on November 5, 2024. Following oral argument, the Court denied the first Motion, as follows:

Despite Defendant’s failure to timely comply with the Court’s orders, verified discovery responses have now been served. Though Plaintiff did not clearly indicate in their reply how the responses were inadequate, at the hearing he claims that witness addresses, presumably known to him, were not. Also, previous sanctions remain unpaid. The threat of terminating sanctions has been sufficient to ensure Defendant Sharifpour’s substantial if imperfect compliance, such that an award of terminating sanctions is not necessary. The Court agrees that Defendant Sharifpour’s failure to comply with the discovery orders was not excusable, and will not tolerate further delay in the payment of sanctions but it exercises its discretion to deny the Motion under these circumstances. The Court retains jurisdiction to impose evidentiary sanctions, additional monetary sanctions, or even terminating sanctions should Defendant not immediately remedy the indicated shortfalls.

(Minute Order, 11/05/24, p. 2.) Plaintiff now brings a second Motion for Terminating Sanctions. No opposition has been filed to date.

Legal Standard

Where a party willfully disobeys a discovery order, courts have the discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidence or monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010, subds. (d), (g); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.) The court should look to the totality of the circumstances in determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.” (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.) “The court may impose a terminating sanction by one of the following orders:

(1)   An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

(2)   An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

(3)   An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

(4)   An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.”

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).)

Discussion

On June 26 and 27, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant’s Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶9 and Exh. 6.) Defendant Sharifpour was ordered to serve verified responses and pay sanctions within 20 days’ service of the order. (Ibid.) Notice of the ruling was mailed to Defendant Sharifpour on June 28, 2024. (Id. at ¶¶10-11 and Exhs. 7-8.) 

Following the filing of Plaintiff’s first Motion for Terminating Sanctions, Defendant Sharifpour served an opposition and responses to the discovery that failed to include the necessary witness information. (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶¶15-16 and Exhs. 9-10.) Following review, Plaintiff’s counsel determined that the responses intentionally withheld material witness information. (Id. at ¶17.) Also, the responses did not comply with Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.280 because they failed to identify which document responds to which request. (Id. at ¶19.) Following the hearing on the first Motion, Plaintiff again sought to meet and confer to obtain complete responses to the discovery requests and indicating that another motion for terminating sanctions would be filed. (Id. at ¶22.) As of the filing of the instant Motion, Defendant Sharifpour has not served responses with the missing information nor paid the complete sanctions as ordered. (Id. at ¶¶21-23.)  

Plaintiff again moves for terminating sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 by striking Defendant Sharifpour’s answer and entering default. The Court finds that terminating sanctions are now warranted for Defendant’s non-compliance with the discovery orders and in light of the Court’s warning. Plaintiff has shown that Defendant Sharifpour’s responses were inadequate and that they have not corrected the inadequacies as ordered by the Court on November 5, 2024. This demonstrates that Defendant Sharifpour’s compliance with the Court’s orders cannot be achieved through lesser sanctions. Although terminating sanctions are a harsh penalty, “[t]he court [is] not required to allow a pattern of abuse to continue ad infinitum.” (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 280.)

Nor will the Court allow Defendant Sharifpour to avoid this result by now serving further responses. The Court’s initial order compelling service of code-compliant responses set a deadline six months ago. The time to comply has long since passed such that corrective action cannot now be taken.

The Court further grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,860.00 based on 4.4 hours or attorney time billed at $650.00. (Motion, Sadr Decl., ¶¶25-27.) The request for costs of $160.00 is denied for lack of supporting evidence.

Conclusion

Plaintiff Jose Avilez’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED. JUDGMENT IS ENTERED BY DEFAULT AGAINST DEFENDANT RAYMOND SHARIFPOUR DBA WHITE DIAMOND AUTO SALES. PLAINTIFF IS ALSO AWARDED SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,860.00. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: 24STLC00190    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 26

Alliance United ins. Co. v. Myles, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiff Alliance United Insurance Company’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE AND SERVE THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. 

ANALYSIS: 

Plaintiff Alliance United Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence against Defendant Lenora Myles (“Defendant”) on January 11, 2024. On October 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint adding Defendants MV Transportation, Inc., individually and dba MV Transportation LLC, MV Transportation LLC, and MV Public Transportation Inc. Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint on October 31, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date. 

Discussion 

The Complaint and First Amended Complaint are set forth on Judicial Council Form PLD-PI-001 and indicate that causes of action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence are to be alleged against Defendant. (Compl., ¶10; FAC, ¶10.) The necessary attachments to provide the factual allegations for these causes of action, however, are not attached to the First Amended Complaint.

Plaintiff now moves for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint with the attachments under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) and section 576. A motion for leave to amend a pleading must comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines of the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).) The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified. . . .” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.) However, “[a] different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff’s Motion is supported by a declaration explaining that the First Amended Complaint did not include the required attachments and seeking to add attachments that thoroughly detail the subject motor vehicle accident, “including the date and place, and roles of defendants.” (Motion, Cordova Decl., ¶10.) The amendments are necessary to provide the parties with a clear understanding of the causes of action. (Id. at ¶11.) The declaration explains that Plaintiff attempt to file the Second Amended Complaint was rejected by the clerk’s office on grounds that it did not have leave to amend the pleadings. (Id. at ¶8 and Exh. C.) Plaintiff brings this Motion within weeks of the rejection. (Id. at ¶12.) Here, Plaintiff seeks leave to amend the First Amended Complaint to make necessary corrections. Nor is there any indication of excusable delay nor prejudice to Defendant, who has not been served in this action nor appeared. All the requirements for leave to amend, therefore, are satisfied.

Conclusion

Plaintiff Alliance United Insurance Company’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE AND SERVE THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.
