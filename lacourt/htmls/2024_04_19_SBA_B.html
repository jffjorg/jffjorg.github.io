Case Number: 23TRCV03647    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

PHEBE SHIN KWON,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03647

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

TESLA MOTORS, INC.,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 19, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Phebe Shin Kwon

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED and the action is STAYED.

BACKGROUND

On November 2, 2023, Phebe Shin Kwon filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. LC for violations under Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. requests that the court compel arbitration and to stay the case.

The complaint alleges that on March 7, 2023, plaintiff purchased a 2023 Tesla Model S.  Complaint, ¶5.  After plaintiff took possession of the vehicle and during the warranty period, the vehicle contained defects that substantially impair the use, safety, and/or value of the vehicle.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff provided defendant with sufficient opportunity to service or repair the vehicle.  Id., ¶13.  Defendant was unable and/or failed to service or repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts.  Id., ¶14.

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant Tesla presents evidence that on March 7, 2023, upon initially placing the order for the subject vehicle, plaintiff electronically accepted the terms and conditions of the Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”).  Defendant asserts that the MVOA contains an arbitration provision, which states, in part:  “Please carefully read this provision, which applies to any dispute between you and Tesla, Inc. and its affiliates. . . . [Y]ou agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the [AAA] under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. . . .”  The provision contains an opt out clause and that if “you do not opt out, this agreement to arbitrate overrides any different arbitration agreement between us, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract.”  Raymond Kim decl., Exh. 1.

Defendant also contends that plaintiff took delivery of the subject vehicle on March 7, 2023, at which time plaintiff executed a Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) for the purchase of the subject vehicle on credit.  Defendant asserts that the RISC also contains an arbitration agreement.  It states in part:  “1.  Either you or we may choose to have any dispute between us decided by arbitration and not in court or by jury trial. . . . Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action. […]”  “We” and “us” are defined as the seller, Tesla Motors, Inc.  See Kim decl., Exh. 2.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that her claims are independent from the order/purchase agreement, citing to Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324 (“Ochoa”), and that her claims do not rely on contract terms derived from the purchase agreement.

            In reply, defendant argues that Ochoa is irrelevant case law because defendant is the direct contracting party and does not have third-party dealerships that handle the ordering and selling of its vehicles.

The court finds that arbitration agreements exist between plaintiff and defendant Tesla and that plaintiff did not opt-out.  Plaintiff does not dispute that she entered into both agreements.  The Ochoa case (which addresses whether a nonsignatory manufacturer can enforce the arbitration agreement) is distinguishable because, here, Tesla is both the manufacturer and seller of the vehicle and is a party to the agreements, and not a nonsignatory.

Scope of agreement

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims against it fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clauses as they arise directly out of the relationship between plaintiff as purchaser/consumer and Tesla as the manufacturer/retailer/warrantor/repair facility and relate to the “purchase or condition” of the vehicle.

The court finds that the scope of the arbitration agreements covers plaintiff’s claims against Tesla as they relate to the condition of the vehicle and arise out of the relationship.  Further, plaintiff’s citation to Ochoa is unavailing.

Enforceability

            Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the agreements are procedurally unconscionable because they are an adhesion contract drafted by defendant.  Plaintiff contends that the “opt out” provision “demonstrates an abuse of power, creates confusion and an undue burden to consumers” because it is “extremely easy for consumers to ‘opt-in’” with a simple click of a button but to opt-out requires that the consumer send a letter, within 30 days.  Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration agreements are substantively unconscionable because the agreement provides that Tesla will pay all AAA fees for any arbitration, and that the arbitrators will not remain neutral because “repeat business creates an incentive for arbitrators to give favorable decisions for corporations that are paying them.”  Plaintiff also asserts that there is no appeals process and that an arbitration is private so consumers would be denied the opportunity to learn about other consumers with similar claims.  Moreover, plaintiff argues, requiring that plaintiff submit dispute solely to AAA because the AAA Rules restricts discovery.  Plaintiff states that if the court grants the motion, then the AAA provision should be stricken.

In reply, defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are not unconscionable because plaintiff’s decision to order and purchase the subject vehicle was entirely willful and that she has not shown that she lacked bargaining power, citing to Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899.  Defendant also points out that AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules allow for the exchange of information between the parties, and are more efficient.

The court finds that even if the arbitration provisions are adhesion contracts, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration clause.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  

As to substantive unconscionability, the court finds little to none as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.  The discovery rules apply equally to both parties, for example.  Moreover, the court does not find a basis to strike the provision requiring AAA as arbitrators and plaintiff’s argument that they are not neutral because defendant pays for the arbitration costs is purely speculative.  In any event, plaintiff could offer to pay for half the costs.

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

The action is STAYED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

ALICIA CAMARENA MILLAN, et al. vs AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR COMPANY, INC., et al.

Tentative Ruling on Demurrer and Motion to Strike

 

This is a Song-Beverly lawsuit, although it contains a Fifth Cause of action for fraudulent inducement-concealment that is not predicated on Song-Beverly.  Before the Court is the demurrer of American Honda Motor Company.  On January 10, 2024 Defendant filed its demurrer attacking only the non-Song-Beverly claim, the Fifth Cause of Action.  On January 26, 2024 Defendant filed its Motion to Strike seeking to strike the punitive damages allegations.  Plaintiff has filed oppositions to both motions.  Reply briefs have been filed.  Both are on calendar for April 19, 2024.

On April 16, 2024 Defendant filed a Moton to Transfer Venue scheduled for hearing on July 3, 2024.

“Defendant, American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“AHM”) will and hereby does move this Court, pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. §397(a) for an order transferring the venue of this action to Superior Court of California, County of Bernardino due to the fact that this action was not commenced in a proper venue. The 2020 Honda Pilot (“Subject Vehicle”) was purchased and primarily repaired in San Bernardino, County, and the majority of the witnesses reside in San Bernardino, County. AHM further moves the Court for an order that Plaintiffs pay to AHM its transfer costs and fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §399.”

In light of the Motion to Transfer Venue, the Court believes that it is appropriate to continue the Demurrer and Motion to Strike to a date after the change of venue motion is decided.  However, the Court also shall not delay this matter by having this change of venue motion heard in July.  This Court has many available dates that are much more timely than July.

The Motion to Transfer Venue is advanced to May 13, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

The Demurrer and Motion to Strike are continued to May 15, 2024 at 8:30.

In as much as the Demurrer and Motion to Strike do not implicate Song-Beverly, the Court Orders Plaintiff to note in counsel’s time records the time devoted to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike as the time devoted to these two motions is not compensable under Song-Beverly and must be separately accounted for should there ultimately be a claim for attorney’s fees under Song-Beverly.
