DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV20363    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Hearing on Defendants’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

Defendants’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint is granted.

                                                                                

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Joseph Cohen and Janet Cohen, filed this action against Mendy Ezagui and Mayta Malamud (“Defendants”), alleging that Defendants breached the lease agreement they had with Plaintiffs by failing to pay rent for the years 2020, 2021, and 2022 for the property located at 1115 S. Sherbourne (“Property”). Defendants’ Cross-Complaint alleges claims related to habitability issues with the Property.

The motion now before the Court is Defendants’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047. Ordinarily, the court will not consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading in ruling on a motion for leave since grounds for a demurrer or motion to strike are premature. The court, however, does have discretion to deny leave to amend where a proposed amendment fails to state a valid cause of action as a matter of law and the defect cannot be cured by further amendment. See California Casualty General Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 281, overruled on other grounds by Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)

A motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).

A separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

Additionally, if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490. Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery. Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.

 

ANALYSIS

Defendants seek to add an eighth cause of action to their Cross-Complaint for Declaratory Relief, establishing the parties' respective rights to attorney’s fees under the lease.  Defendants intend to seek summary adjudication of this claim in the hopes of helping the parties understand the relative value of their cases and potentially facilitate settlement. 

Contents of Motion

Defendants have included, with their motion, a copy of the proposed First Amended Cross-Complaint. Declaration of Joshua Greer ¶ 4, Ex A. Defendants state that no allegations are changed or deleted and that the additional cause of action for declaratory relief is to be added at paragraphs 99-114. Motion at p. 5:22-23; Greer Decl. ¶ 3. The motion, therefore, complies with the requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).

 

Supporting Declaration

The declaration of Joshua Greer, Defendants counsel, states that the effect of the proposed amendment does not change or delete any allegations, but only adds an eighth cause of action for declaratory relief, seeking to clarify the parties' rights for attorney’s fees under the lease.  Greer Decl. ¶¶ 2-3. The declaration identifies why the amendment is necessary and proper, because it “is likely to facilitate more fruitful settlement discussion in the coming months.” Greer Decl. ¶ 5. Finally, the declaration explains that the amendment was not made earlier because the parties were previously unaware of their dispute regarding the proper interpretation of the lease agreement. Greer Decl. ¶ 6. Accordingly, Defendants have complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

Plaintiff’s Opposition

In opposition, Plaintiffs claim that leave to amend should not be permitted because Defendants unreasonably delayed in seeking leave. According to Plaintiffs, Defendants have been aware of the disputed interpretations of the attorney fee provision since a conversation between counsel in the fall of 2023. Declaration of Jonathan Kintzele ¶ 7. In a reply declaration, attorney Greer reasserts that the need for declaratory relief only became apparent following mediation but does not deny the earlier conversation regarding limits on the attorney’s fees. Greer Reply Decl. at ¶ 8. Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendants engaged in a delay in seeking leave to amend. 

Plaintiffs further argue that the proposed amendment prejudices them because they have already conducted significant discovery and depositions, and the amendment will require them to incur additional fees and costs for the defense and investigation of the cause of action. Opposition at p. 5:10-14. The Court finds this argument unpersuasive. The proposed amendment contains no new factual allegations necessitating additional discovery or investigation, merely a straightforward issue of contract interpretation. Moreover, both parties requested attorney’s fees in their operative complaints, and Plaintiffs have been aware of their differing interpretations of the lease attorney fee provision for over a year. Kintzele Decl. ¶ 7. The proposed amendment only changes when the interpretation is resolved, before rather than after trial. Accordingly, the Court finds that, even if Defendants delayed in seeking amendment, Plaintiffs are not prejudiced by adding the claim for declaratory relief regarding the parties' rights to attorney’s fees.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint is granted. Defendants shall file and serve the FACC within 10 days of this order.


Case Number: 23STCV30751    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Hearing on Defendants’ Demurrer with Motion to Strike (CCP 430.10)

 

Defendants’ Demurrer is overruled. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is denied.

                                                                                                                                       

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Aisha Jeter, Daniel Ernan Jacob Jeter, and Elijah David Massey (by and through his guardian ad litem) filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against defendants 6325 8th Avenue, LLC and Malin Asset Management, Inc. (Defendants) on August 16, 2024, for (1) negligence, (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (3) breach of the implied warranty of habitability, (4) tortious breach of same, (5) private nuisance, (6) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (7) violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, (8) unfair business practices, and (9) premises liability.

As alleged in the FAC, Defendants own or manage a residential rental property located at 6325 8th Ave., Los Angeles 90043 (“the Property”). Plaintiffs have rented Apartment 5 on the Property (“the Unit”) since September 23, 2020. During Plaintiffs’ tenancy, their Unit has suffered from vermin infestation, dampness, moisture, and mold, defective plumbing, defective smoke detectors, unsealed windows, and crumbling walls, floors, etc. Plaintiffs complained to Defendants’ agents, but Defendants refused to correct the poor conditions.

The motion now before the Court is Defendants’ Demurrer with Motion to Strike (CCP 430.10)

 

LEGAL STANDARD

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not on the evidence or facts alleged.” E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315. As such, courts assume the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. Ibid. However, it does not accept as true deductions, contentions, or conclusions of law or fact. Stonehouse Homes LLC v. City of Sierra Madre (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 531, 538.

Further, the court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a). The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. Id., § 436(b). The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. Id. § 436. The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. Id. § 437.

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 (court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”); Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1037 (“A demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint, liberally construed, can state a cause of action under any theory or if there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured by amendment.”). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.

ANALYSIS

Demurrer

Defendants demur to the third, fourth, and sixth cause of action as to Massey on the grounds that, as a minor, he was not a party to the lease contract and cannot state claims based on that contract.

Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

Defendants argue that Massey cannot maintain a cause of action for breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability because such a claim is on the lease, which Massey is not a party to. 

“[A] warranty of habitability is implied by law in residential leases in this state…” Green v. Superior Court of San Francisco (1974) 10 Cal.3d 616, 637. 

Defendants rely on Family Code section 6701, which states in relevant part, “A minor cannot … [m]ake a contract relating to real property or any interest therein.”

Plaintiffs argue that Massey is entitled to bring a claim for breach of the implied warranty of habitability because he was an intended third-party beneficiary of the lease. 

‘“California law permits third party beneficiaries to enforce the terms of a contract made for their benefit.” Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1021-1022 (Spinks). (quoting, Principal Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Vars, Pave, McCord & Freedman (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 1469, 1485. (Principal Mutual). “That authority is codified in Civil Code section 1559, which states: ’A contract, made expressly for the benefit of a third person, may be enforced by him at any time before the parties thereto rescind it.’” Ibid. “[C]hildren as third party beneficiaries may enforce the provisions therein made expressly for their benefit in the same manner as can a party to the agreement.” Reliance Life Ins. Co. v. Jaffe (1953) 121 Cal.App.2d 241, 244.   

“The test for determining whether a contract was made for the benefit of a third person is whether an intent to benefit a third person appears from the terms of the contract. If the terms of the contract necessarily require the promisor to confer a benefit on a third person, then the contract, and hence the parties thereto, contemplate a benefit to the third person.” Johnson v. Holmes Tuttle Lincoln-Mercury, Inc. (1958) 160 Cal.App.2d 290, 297 (citation omitted). “Nevertheless, the third person need not be named or identified individually to be an express beneficiary. A third party may enforce a contract if it can be shown that he or she is a member of the class for whose express benefit the contract was made.” Kaiser Eng’rs v. Grinnell Fire Prot. Sys. Co. (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 1050, 1055.

Here, the lease states that “Occupancy is for 2 Adults and 1 Minor Only.”  FAC ¶ 17, Ex. A at p. 29. Plaintiffs allege that they informed Defendants that Massey would be living with them when they entered into the lease agreement. FAC ¶ 16. The lease terms require Defendants to confer on Massey the benefit of allowing him to occupy the property.  Moreover, Family Code section 6701, subdivision (b), concerns only a minor’s ability to make a contract; it says nothing about their ability to enforce a contract to which they are a third-party beneficiary. Accordingly, the Court concludes that Massey has properly alleged that he was a third-party beneficiary to the lease. 

Furthermore, Defendants ignore that in addition to a contractual claim, breach of warranty of habitability can also be a statutory claim. Civil Code section 1941 provides, “The lessor of a building intended for the occupation of human beings must, in the absence of an agreement to the contrary, put it into a condition fit for such occupation, and repair all subsequent dilapidations thereof, which render it untenantable, except such as are mentioned in section nineteen hundred and twenty-nine.” For a unit to be “untenantable under section 1941.1 … means that the condition of the premises ‘are not fit for occupancy or rental.’ McNairy v. C.K. Realty (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1500, 1506 (quoting Black’s Law Dict. (6th ed. 1990) p. 1540, col. 1). Conditions rendering a property untenantable are further enumerated in Civil Code section 1941.1 which includes violations of Health and Safety Code section 17920.3. Nothing in these statutory provisions limits their scope to those authorized occupants who signed the lease.

Because the Court finds that Massey has properly pleaded that he is a third-party beneficiary, and because his statutory rights do not depend on privity of contract, the Court finds that Massey can state a claim for breach of the implied warranty of habitability. Accordingly, the demurrer as to the third cause of action is overruled.

Tortious Breach of Implied Warranty

Defendants make substantially the same argument as to the tortious breach of implied warranty of habitability claim.

For the reasons given above, the Court finds that Massey properly pleaded that he is an intended third-party beneficiary to the lease.

Additionally, a claim for tortious breach of implied warranty sounds not in contract but in tort. In Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903 (Stoiber), the court recognized a cause of action for tortuous breach of implied warranty of habitability. The court explained that in Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.3d 108, “the Supreme Court repudiated the traditional trespasser-licensee-invitee classification of duties of an owner or possessor of land, and substituted in place of these rigid classifications the basic rule of foreseeability of injury to others.” Stoiber, supra,101 Cal.App.3d at p. 917. Thus, the issue is whether Massey’s injury was foreseeable, not whether he had a contract with Defendants.

Plaintiffs have pled facts sufficient to allege that Massey was an authorized occupant of the property and that there were habitability issues with the property due to the unsanitary and unsafe conditions. As such, Massey may maintain a cause of action for tortious breach of the warranty of habitability, notwithstanding the fact that he was not a party to the lease. Accordingly, the demurrer as to the fourth cause of action is overruled. 

Quiet Enjoyment

Again, Defendants argue that Massey may not bring an action for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment because he is not a party to the contract. 

“In the absence of language to the contrary, every lease contains an implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, whereby the landlord impliedly covenants that the tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession of the premises.”  Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal. App. 4th 578, 588.  “The implied covenant of quiet enjoyment implies a term in a contract, and a breach of the covenant gives rise to an action in contract.”  Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal. App. 4th 873, 896. A third-party beneficiary is entitled to sue for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment. Spinks, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1035-36.

In Spinks, the court reversed summary adjudication of a plaintiff’s breach of quiet title, holding that whether the plaintiff was a third-party beneficiary to the lease presented a triable issue of fact. Id. at p. 1036. There, the lease was between the plaintiff’s employer and the defendant landlord, but evidence showed that the parties intended the plaintiff to occupy the property. Id. at 1027.

Defendants argue that the lease granted Massey only a “license” interest – not a tenancy interest. The Court finds this unpersuasive. By the terms of the lease, all three Plaintiffs were entitled to occupancy, and no additional language was included, otherwise limiting Massey’s interest in the property. Massey was not a mere licensee, whom the Defendants could eject at their discretion. While Defendants are correct that Massey is not, and legally could not be, a signatory to the lease, the parties’ clear intent, as alleged in the FAC, was that he occupy the property with his mother and uncle. As in Spinks, the fact that Massey’s right is derivative of the parties who signed the lease on his behalf is not dispositive. Spinks, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1035-36.  Accordingly, the Court overrules the demurrer as to the sixth cause of action for quiet enjoyment.

 

 

 

Motion to Strike

Punitive Damage

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs fail to allege sufficient facts to support their claims for punitive and exemplary damages. Defendants further argue the FAC fails to specify which defendant engaged in the alleged conduct.

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code Section 3294. College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721. These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a). “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” Civ. Code, § 3294 subd. (c)(1).

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth. In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.” Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255. (citations omitted). “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 (fn. omitted) (citations omitted). A tenant’s habitability action against the owners for failure to remedy slum living conditions may be sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. See, e.g., Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 921 (failure to correct defective living conditions sufficient for punitive damages claim; Rivera v. Sassoon (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1046 (affirming general, special, and punitive damages jury award for breach of the warranty of habitability, nuisance, and other torts). Punitive damages may be assessed against an employer where the employer authorized or ratified a malicious act. College Hospital Inc., supra,8 Cal.4th at. p 723.

Plaintiffs pleaded sufficient facts to support their prayer for punitive damages. They allege that Defendants had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises, including a severe and persistent cockroach infestation, chronic water leaks that caused damage and mold, a rodent infestation, and a number of other issues, such as missing window sealant, defective plumbing, and cracked walls, ceilings, and flooring. FAC ¶ 29. Plaintiffs specifically allege that Defendants’ conduct was “willful, wanton, intentional, despicable malicious and initiated with malice and with the intent to knowingly take advantage of, oppress, and injure Plaintiffs.” FAC ¶¶ 42, 49, 72, 81, 89. While conclusory allegations are not entitled to a presumption of truth, a jury could conclude that the alleged conduct was done with malice. Plaintiffs make these allegations against each Defendant and allege that they authorized, instructed, and ratified this conduct by their agents and employees. FAC ¶¶ 6, 7, 15, 25, 34. Whether Plaintiffs can prove that each Defendant engaged in such conduct is not an issue for demurrer. Accordingly, the Court denies the motion to strike punitive damages.

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Demurrer is overruled.  Defendants’ Motion to Strike is Denied. Defendants have twenty days to answer.
