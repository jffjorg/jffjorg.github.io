DEPARTMENT 72 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV29695    Hearing Date: October 26, 2023    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JONATHAN DORTCH, et al.  

 

                                  Plaintiffs,

 

         v.

 

 

JOSHUA GOLSEN, et al.

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  21STCV29695

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 26, 2023

 Calendar Number:  5

 

          Plaintiffs and Cross-Defendants Jonathan Dortch (“Dortch”) and Sly Spectrum LLC (“Sly Spectrum”) (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”) demur to the cross-complaint of Defendant and Cross-Complainant Part Four, LLC (“Part Four”).  Cross-Defendants also move to strike the punitive damages claim from the cross-complaint.

 

          Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer is OVERRULED and the Motion to Strike is DENIED.  Cross-Defendants shall file an answer in 20 days.

 

Background

 

          The operative Second Amended Complaint in this action involves claims by Dortch and Sly Spectrum as plaintiffs against Joshua Golsen, Barry Golsen, Rebel Bass, LLC, GOL Capital LLC and Part Four as defendants.  The claims in the Second Amended Complaint generally involve issues of fraud, breach of fiduciary and other matters concerning the formation and operation of Part Four.

 

          Dortch and Sly Spectrum assert that Part Four was a company formed by Plaintiff Dortch and one of the other defendants, Joshua Golsen.  Plaintiff Sly Spectrum is a member of Part Four.  Plaintiff Dortch is the sole member and manager of Sly Spectrum.

 

          On May 31, 2023, the defendants filed answers to Dortch’s and Sly Spectrum’s Second Amended Complaint.  On May 31, 2023, defendant Part Four also filed a cross-complaint (the “Cross-Complaint”) against Dortch and Sly Spectrum alleging a single claim for Violation of the Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act (15 U.S.C. §1125(d). 

 

          On June 30, 2023, Cross-Defendants filed this demurrer and motion to strike.   Part Four opposed on October 13, 2023 and Cross-Defendants replied on October 19, 2023.

 

Legal Standard

 

          A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) The court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.)

 

Discussion

 

          An individual may be held liable under the Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act if the person (1) registers, traffics in, or uses a domain name identical or confusingly similar to a distinctive mark, and (2) has a bad faith intent to profit from use of the mark as a domain name. (See 15 U.S.C. § 1125(d); Bosley Medical Institute v. Kremer (2005) 403 F.3d 672, 680.)  “[C]ybersquatting occurs when a person other than the trademark holder registers the domain name of a well known trademark and then attempts to profit from this by either ransoming the domain name back to the trademark holder or by using the domain name to divert business from the trademark holder to the domain name holder.”  (Bosley Medical Institute, Inc., supra, 403 F.3d at 680.

Cross-Defendants demur to Part Four’s sole claim for violation of the Anti-Cybersquatting Consumer Protection Act.  Cross-Defendants make the following arguments.  The only potential cybersquatting alleged is Cross-Defendants’ registration of certain domain names.  The allegations of the Cross-Complaint establish that Cross-Defendants registered the protectible domain names for the benefit of Part Four and at its behest. The Cross-Complaint alleges that Sly Spectrum was a member of Part Four and pursuant to the articles of organization, Sly Spectrum had the authority to enter into contracts based on its status as a member.  There are assertedly no allegations of bad faith intent to profit from the acquisition of these protectible trademarks.

          Cross-Defendants’ arguments on demurrer do not track or take account the actual allegations in the Cross-Complaint.  Part Four alleges the following.  Part Four registered multiple distinct trademarks.  (Cross-Complaint, ¶35.).  Dortch registered infringing domains and withheld other domain names while he was an employee of Part Four but after he realized he would be terminated.  (Id. at ¶37.)  Dortch and Sly Spectrum’s conduct in registering the domain names was done without Part Four’s explicit or implicit authorization, consent, approval, or knowledge. (Id. at ¶38.)  Dortch and Sly Spectrum’s registrations of the domains was done with the bad faith intent to profit from the registration.  (Id. at ¶40.)  Dortch and Sly Spectrum used the misappropriated domains and registered the infringing domains to hold them maliciously for ransom from Part Four.  (Id. at ¶42.)  Dortch demanded $4 million for the domain names after he was terminated.  (Id. at ¶1.)  These are sufficient facts to state a claim for violation of 15 U.S.C. 1125(d). 

          Contrary to Cross-Defendants’ position, Part Four does not allege that their acquisition of the domain names was done at the instruction and with the permission of Part Four.  Part Four’s Cross-Complaint alleges the exact opposite. (Id. at ¶38.)  The mere fact that Dortch was an employee when he acquired the domain names does not establish that he acquired them with Part Four’s permission.  Likewise, it is not clear from the face of the Cross-Complaint that Sly Spectrum had authority to purchase the domain names, that Dortch was doing so on behalf of Sly Spectrum as a member of Part Four or that Sly Spectrum did not purchase the domain names for the bad faith purpose of holding them hostage.  Cross-Defendants challenge the truth of the Cross-Complaint’s allegations, which is not grounds for demurrer.

Cross-Defendants’ arguments also improperly conflate various allegations by Part Four.  Specifically, Cross-Defendants conflate allegations about what the Cross-Complaint refers to as the “Misappropriated Domains”—four domains that Dortch obtained at Part Four’s direction but then wrongfully withheld from Part Four (Cross-Complaint at ¶¶16-19)—with allegations about what the Cross-Complaint refers to as the “Infringing Domains”—eighteen domains that Dortch obtained without authority for his own benefit when he realized his time at Part Four was coming to an end.  (Id. at ¶¶20-26.)  The Court considers all allegations in the Cross-Complaint when making its rulings.

          Cross-Defendants also move to strike the punitive damages from the Cross-Complaint. Cross-Defendants argue there are no allegations to support a finding of malice, oppression, or fraud.  Cross-Defendants argue there are also no allegations that would support imposition of punitive damages against Sly Spectrum based on the conduct of its employee, Dortch. 

The Court denies the motion to strike for reasons similar to its denial of the demurrer.  Part Four’s  allegations support a finding of malice based on Cross-Defendants’ purchase of the domain names with the bad faith intent of ransoming them to Part Four.  Sly Spectrum is liable for punitive damages based on the acts of Dortch, its sole member and manager, on its behalf.  (Hartman v. Shell Oil Co. (1977) 68 Cal.App.3d 240, 248.)

                                  Plaintiffs,

 

         v.

 

 

GENERAL MOTORS LLC, et al.

 Hearing Date:  October 26, 2023

          Plaintiff Markus Pinon (“Plaintiff”) moves to and to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) Nos. 16-21 propounded to Defendant General Motors LLC (“Defendant”). 

         

          Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP Nos. 16-21 is GRANTED as set forth below.  Defendant shall produce within 30 days further written responses and documents in accordance with the Court’s guidelines for production set forth below.

 

          Plaintiff shall give notice.

 

Background

 

          Plaintiffs purchased a 2021 GMC Sierra 1500 AT4 on October 15, 2021.  Plaintiff alleges the car suffered from a defective engine system, safety system, transmission system, braking system, electrical system.  Plaintiff filed a complaint on November 16, 2022 pursuant to the Song Beverly Act alleging (1) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(d); (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(b); (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(a)(3); (4) breach of express warranty Civil Code §1791.2(a), 1794; (5) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability Civil Code §1791.1, 1794

 

Legal Standard

 

Song-Beverly Act

 

          The Song Beverly Act “is a remedial measure intended for protection of consumers and should be given a construction consistent with that purpose.”  (Id.)  To

succeed on a claim brought under the Act, the plaintiff bears the burden of proving, by a

preponderance of the evidence, several elements, including nonconformity of a vehicle that substantially impaired its use, value or safety; presentation of vehicle to manufacturer or authorized representative for repair; and failure to repair the defect after a reasonable number of attempts.  (Ibrahim v. Ford Motor Co. (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 878, 886-887.)  A buyer may also be entitled to a civil penalty of up to two times the actual damages upon a showing that the manufacturer willfully failed to abide by any of its obligations under the Act.  (Civ. C. §1794(c).)

 

          Scope of Discovery and Discovery Obligations in Song-Beverly Act litigation

 

CCP § 2017.010

 

Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.

 

CCP §2031.220

 

A statement that the party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling has been directed will comply with the particular demand shall state that the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and related activity demanded, will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.

 

CCP §2031.230

 

A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.

 

CCP § 2031.310

 

(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

          (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

          (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

          (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with each of the following:

          (1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.

          (2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

          (3) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.

 

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.

 

… (h) Except as provided in subdivision (j), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

          The burden is on the moving party to show both relevance to the subject matter and specific facts justifying discovery.  (Glenfed Develop. Corp. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.)  Once good cause is established by the moving party, the burden then shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure.  (Hartbrodt v. Burke (1996, 2nd Dist.) 42 Cal.App.4th 168, 172-174.)         

 

          “Relevant evidence is defined in Evidence Code section 210 as evidence ‘having any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.’ The test of relevance is whether the evidence tends logically, naturally, and by reasonable inference to establish material facts.  The trial court has broad discretion in determining the relevance of evidence, but lacks discretion to admit irrelevant evidence.”  (Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 148 (evidence of post-warranty repairs relevant to action based on warranty repairs because they could demonstrate that vehicle was not repaired to conform to warranty during warranty period.)

 

          In Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, plaintiff alleged violation of the Song Beverly Act based on the defendants’ failure to repair, repurchase, or replace a Bentley that had an obnoxious odor in the interior.  (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at 971.).  The following discovery requests were deemed “highly relevant” and failure to readily produce them prior to trial should have resulted in terminating sanctions: 

 

“all documents relating to (1) customer complaints concerning the rust inhibitor used on the 2002 Bentley Arnage; (2) all notices to Bentley dealers for the period 2001 to date concerning the rust inhibitor used on the Bentley Arnage; (3) all warranty repairs during the period of January 2002 to date of the Bentley Arnage related to the rust inhibitor used on the  vehicle; (4) all customer complaints of a wax oil smell caused by the rust inhibitor on the 2002 Bentley Arnage; and (5) vehicle tests conducted on the 2002 model year Arnage to confirm whether there was a wax oil smell arising from the vehicle's rust inhibitor.”

         

Discussion

          Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause for production.  The RFPs pertain to the Engine Defect, which was allegedly present in Plaintiff’s vehicle, and seek documents (1) relating to internal investigations into the Engine Defect in cars of the same year, make and model as Plaintiff’s; (2) relating to communications regarding the Engine Defect in vehicles of the same year, make and model as Plaintiff’s; (3) relating to any decision to issue any notices, letters, campaigns, warranty extensions technical service bulletins and recalls concerning the Engine Defect in vehicles of the same year, make and model as Plaintiff’s; (4) concerning customer complaints, claims, reported failures, and warranty claims relating to the Engine Defect in vehicles of the same year, make and model, including databases; (5) concerning failure rates of vehicles of the same year, make and model as Plaintiff’s due to the Engine Defect; and (6) concerning or relating to any fixes for the Engine Defect in vehicles of the same year, make and model as Plaintiff’s. 

          The documents requested could reasonably lead to admissible evidence regarding Defendant’s knowledge of the Engine Defect, any attempts to fix or address the defect, the nature of the defect.  As Plaintiff argues, the Song Beverly Act allows for a civil penalty of up to two times the actual damage upon showing that the manufacturer willfully failed to abide by its obligations thereunder.  The Act requires a manufacturer to replace a vehicle or make restitution to the buyer.  (Lukather v. General Motors, LLC (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1050.)  Plaintiff argues the RFPs could produce information demonstrating Defendant’s knowledge of the defect, the inability to fix that defect and Defendant’s willful failure to repurchase Plaintiff’s vehicle despite knowledge that the defect was ongoing in vehicles of the same year, model and make as Plaintiff’s car.

          On the other hand, as the requests are currently phrased, they do impose an unnecessary and undue burden on Defendant.  This problem, however, is a narrow one.  The burden problem may be remedied by limiting the scope of the requests as follows: 

Request for Production No. 16:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents including recall notices and technical services bulletins.  Defendant shall not be required to search for emails.

Request for Production No. 17:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents including recall notices and technical services bulletins.  Defendant shall not be required to search for emails.

Request for Production No. 18:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents including recall notices and technical services bulletins.  Defendant shall not be required to search for emails.

Request for Production No. 19:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents limited to complaints within the state of California.

Request No. 20:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents except that Defendant shall not be required to search for emails.

Request No. 21:  Defendant shall produce responsive documents except that Defendant shall not be required to search for emails. 

          Finally, any concerns regarding trade secrets and confidential information involving third parties would be sufficiently addressed by a standard protective order. Plaintiff executed the protective order proposed by Defendant during the parties’ meet and confer efforts.  (Sue Dec., ¶¶18-19, Ex 9.)  Based on the evidence, the protective order is fully executed, and Defendant need only file the protective order with the Court. 

          Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP Nos. 16-21 (Set One) is GRANTED within the limitations described above.  Defendant shall file the protective order executed on June 7, 2023 by Plaintiff’s counsel and attached as Exhibit 9 to the Sue declaration.
