Department 71: Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff  via the Department's email: SMCdept71@lacourt.org before the set hearing time.  See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).   All parties are otherwise encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect for all matters.

Case Number: 21STCV30154    Hearing Date: October 23, 2023    Dept: 71

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

BILLIE JEAN WHITTENBERG, 

 

         vs.

 

APARTMENT MANAGEMENT GROUP, LLC, et al.

 Case No.:  21STCV30154

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 23, 2023

 

Defendant Malin Asset Management, Inc.’s unopposed demurrer to Plaintiff Billie Jean Whittenberg’s first amended complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend as to the entire pleading.

 

Defendant Malin Asset Management, Inc.’s unopposed motion to strike is denied as moot.

 

Defendant Malin Asset Management, Inc. (“Malin Asset”) (“Defendant”) demurs unopposed to Plaintiff Billie Jean Whittenberg’s (“Whittenberg”) (“Plaintiff”)[1] first amended complaint (“FAC”).  (Notice of Demurrer, pgs. 1-2; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f).)  Defendant also moves to strike portions of the FAC.  (Notice of MTS, pgs. 1-2; C.C.P. §§435, 436.)

 

Background

Plaintiff filed her initial Complaint on August 16, 2021.  On February 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed an amendment to her Complaint designating Doe No. 1 as Crystal Property Management, Inc. (“Crystal Property”).  On February 22, 2022, Plaintiff dismissed Dan Zuckerman and Adrian Malin from her Complaint, with prejudice.  On February 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed the operative FAC against Eloise Williams, Gaylord Williams, Chloes Apartments LLC (“Chloe’s”), and Apartment Management Group, LLC (“AMG”) (collectively, “Defendants”), alleging thirteen causes of action: (1) constructive eviction; (2) breach of contract; (3) violation of Civil Code §1942.4; (4) violation of Civil Code §1950.5; (5) violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code §151.09; (6) breach of the warranty of habitability; (7) private nuisance; (8) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment; (9) negligence; (10) violation of unfair business practices; (11) battery; (12) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (13) fraud.  On May 25, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the FAC designating Doe No. 2 as Malin Asset.  On September 18, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Eloise Williams and Gaylord Williams from this action, with prejudice.

This action arises out of Plaintiff’s entry into a written lease agreement (“Lease”) in 2015 with Eloise Williams and Gaylord Williams to rent real property located at 3105 West 27th Street #32, Los Angeles, CA 90018 (“Subject Property”).  (FAC ¶¶5, 12.)  Plaintiff alleges Eloise Williams and Gaylord Williams owned the Subject Property until approximately February 2018, when the Subject Property was sold to Chloe’s.  (FAC ¶13.)  Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that Crystal Property was the property management company for the Subject Property during the Williams’ ownership period.  (FAC ¶14.)  Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that that AMG was the property management company for the Subject Property during Chloe’s ownership.  (FAC ¶15.)  Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that Defendants are or have been, under the respective ownership dates noted above, the owners and managers of the Subject Property, and have exercised real or apparent authority regarding it, and have been legally responsible for maintaining the Subject Property in a lawful and habitable condition but have failed to do so.  (FAC ¶16.) 

Plaintiff alleges the Subject Property is a rent control unit under the Rent Stabilization Ordinance (“RSO”).  (FAC ¶18.)  Plaintiff alleges that throughout her tenancy and since its inception, the Subject Property lacked most of the basic characteristics necessary for human habitation.  (FAC ¶19.)  Plaintiff alleges substantial habitability defects existed at the Subject Property during Plaintiff’s tenancy that constitute violations of applicable housing laws, including but not limited to the Los Angeles Department of Building and Safety Code, and the Defendants failed to take corrective actions.  (FAC ¶19.)  Plaintiff alleges because of the volume and severity of the habitability problems with multiple units at the building which houses the Subject Property, many tenants sent in complaints to the Housing and Community Investment Department of Los Angeles (“HCIDLA”).  (FAC ¶19.)

Plaintiff alleges on January 1, 2016, an HCIDLA investigation occurred and HCIDLA notified the Williamses that the Subject Property’s smoke detector was not secure, there was a broken light switch at the Subject Property’s entry, and that they needed to install an approved trap under the kitchen sink.  (FAC ¶20.)  Plaintiff alleges the roof of the common area of the Subject Property was noted to not be properly weatherproofed, which constituted a failure to maintain the premises free of structural hazards under Health and Safety Code §17920.3(b).  (FAC ¶21.) 

Plaintiff alleges two months later, on March 11, 2016, HCIDLA noted that the Williamses did nothing to remedy the Subject Property’s unsecure smoke detector.  (FAC ¶22.)  Plaintiff alleges notice of the Subject Property’s continued need of a smoke detector and lack of habitability was provided by the city of Los Angeles, yet the Williamses still did not respond.  (FAC ¶22.)  Plaintiff alleges HCIDLA also had to notify the Defendants a second time that the roof of the Subject Property’s common area was still not properly weatherproofed.  (FAC ¶22.) 

Plaintiff alleges on July 10, 2017, HCIDLA noted that the Subject Property had unsanitary stairways and walkways, the exterior areas needed to be painted because they were not weather protected, there was evidence of water damage, and the exterior overhangs near the second floor were deteriorated.  (FAC ¶23.)  Plaintiff alleges when Defendants, and each of them, abated some of the habitability problems with the building, Defendants did so without the proper permits, which constitutes illegal construction (91.8105 L.A.M.C), (161.601 L.A.M.C).  (FAC ¶24.)  Plaintiff alleges the unpermitted work led to multiple problems for the Plaintiff and other tenants as well.  (FAC ¶24.) 

Plaintiff alleges a further complaint was filed with HCIDLA on March 8, 2018, for the illegal construction which had been performed on the building containing the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶25.)  Plaintiff alleges on April 30, 2018, HCIDLA found that Chloe’s and AGM’s illegal construction was still ongoing and on July 25, 2018, HCIDLA issued a Failure to Comply notice.  (FAC ¶25.)  Plaintiff alleges this HCIDLA complaint ultimately led to a General Manager’s meeting being held, to make a determination if the building should be placed into the Rent Escrow Account Program (“REAP”) because Chloe’s and AGM had failed to remediate the habitability problems with the building by the time allowed.  (FAC ¶26.)  Plaintiff alleges thereafter, the General Manager hearings were continued three times to allow Chloe’s and AGM the opportunity to retroactively attain the permits which Defendants negligently failed to acquire before commencing work on the building.  (FAC ¶26.) 

Plaintiff alleges during the construction, Defendants failed to provide Plaintiff with mandated relocation funds.  (FAC ¶27.)  Plaintiff alleges she was forced to live in an unsafe and uninhabitable building due to the construction, the building’s ongoing habitability violations, and Defendants not providing any temporary relocation funds to Plaintiff.  (FAC ¶27.)  Plaintiff alleges she was forced to depart through constructive eviction.  (FAC ¶27.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ failure to provide relocation assistance constituted violations of L.A.M.C. 151.09, which indicates that failure will make a landlord such as the Defendants liable to a civil action and substantial damages.  (FAC ¶27.) 

Plaintiff alleges she was also exposed to dilapidated and contaminated conditions, including the presence of rodents, an infestation of cockroaches and other insects, and bed bugs.  (FAC ¶28.)  Plaintiff alleges these vermin made the property decidedly unsafe.  (FAC ¶28.)  Plaintiff alleges these conditions exacerbated Plaintiff’s suffering and anguish and was in blatant violation of the Williamses’ and Chloe’s duty, as the landlord, to maintain premises free of bedbugs and other vermin pursuant to L.A.C.C 11.30.010, 91.8104 LAMC and Health & Safety Code 17920.3(j)).  (FAC ¶28.) 

Plaintiff alleges Defendants Chloe’s and AGM’s constant construction also created excessive amounts of noise, unsanitary common areas, safety hazards throughout the premises, and caused debris and dirt to enter the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶29.)  Plaintiff alleges throughout her tenancy these issues became progressively more severe as Chloe’s and AGM continued their renovations despite the illegal conditions it created and the burden it placed on their tenants.  (FAC ¶29.)  Plaintiff alleges the Subject Property had a hole in its roof and the building containing the Subject Property cracks throughout its roof that would often leak.  (FAC ¶30.)  Plaintiff alleges when Defendants did make repairs, they were highly insufficient, and the unit’s issues remained until Plaintiff vacated the premises.  (FAC ¶31.)  Plaintiff alleges she complained about the above conditions to the Defendants, but the defects were not remedied.  (FAC ¶32.)  Plaintiff alleges she contacted the Defendants via telephone, in writing, e-mail, and in person regarding the deplorable conditions at the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶32.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants repeatedly failed to address or abate the endemic problems plaguing the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶32.)  Plaintiff alleges when Defendants abated some of the habitability problems at the Subject Property, they did so without proper permits.  (FAC ¶33.)  Plaintiff alleges the work was not performed in a workmanlike manner and in accordance with all building codes.  (FAC ¶33.) 

Plaintiff alleges in or around 2019, Chloe’s and AGM continued to collect Section 8 rent on behalf of the tenant; however, they refused to accept Plaintiff’s portion of the rent.  (FAC ¶34.)  Plaintiff alleges Chloe’s and AGM then set out on a path to evict Plaintiff for non-payment of rent through an unlawful detainer process.  (FAC ¶35.)  Plaintiff alleges Chloe’s and AGM fraudulently represented that they had not received rental payments which they knew was untrue.  (FAC ¶36.)  Plaintiff alleges Chloe’s and AGM failed to disclose that they had in fact received the Section 8 rental payments and had refused Plaintiff’s additional rent payments in an effort to force Plaintiff out of the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶36.)  Plaintiff alleges due to the Defendants’ material breaches of the terms of the Lease which resulted in substantial interferences with Plaintiff’s use of the Subject Property, Plaintiff was constrictively evicted in or around July 2019.  (FAC ¶37.)

On September 1, 2023, Malin Asset filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike.  As of the date of this hearing no oppositions have been filed.

 

A.   Demurrer

Summary of Demurrer

Malin Asset demurs on the basis that Plaintiff’s entire complaint is uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible, and the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, 6th, 7th, 8th, 9th, 10th, 11th, 12th, and 13th causes of action failed to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action against Malin Asset.  (Demurrer, pg. 6; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f).)

 

          Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a).)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Defendant’s counsel’s declaration fails to indicate he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel in person or by telephone; instead, he declares he sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel.  (Decl. of Carpenter ¶3, Exh. A.)  Defendant’s counsel’s declaration does not demonstrate a proper meet and confer per C.C.P. §430.41(a).  However, the failure to sufficiently meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(4).)  Accordingly, the Court will consider Malin Asset’s demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Uncertainty

Entire Complaint

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

If the complaint contains enough facts to apprise defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, failure to label each cause of action is not ground for demurrer: “Although inconvenient, annoying and inconsiderate, the lack of labels . . . does not substantially impair [defendant’s] ability to understand the complaint.” (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 n.2.)

Plaintiff fails to allege which actions of Malin Asset are attributable to each cause of action.  Plaintiff merely alleges with regard to Does 2 through 25, inclusive, that she “will amend this complaint to allege their true names, capacities, or basis for liability when the same has been ascertained. Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereon alleges that defendants DOES 2 through 25, inclusive, and each of them, are in some manner liable to Plaintiff, or claim some right, title, or interest in the subject property that is junior and inferior to that of Plaintiff, or both” and “[a]t all times relevant to this action, each defendant, including those fictitiously named, was the agent, servant, employee, partner, joint venturer, alter ego, or surety of the other defendants and was acting within the scope of said agency, employment, partnership, venture, or suretyship with the knowledge and consent or ratification of each of the other defendants in doing the things alleged herein.”  (FAC ¶¶7, 8.)  Plaintiff’s allegations do not specify Malin Asset’s relationship to the other named Defendants such that they have sufficient notice to respond to the FAC.

Accordingly, Malin Asset’s demurrer on the basis of uncertainty is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Malin Asset’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

B.    Motion to Strike

In light of the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, Malin Asset’s motion to strike is denied as moot.
