Case Number: BC583713    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendant Shara Koplowitz’s Motions (1) for Attorney Fees; (2) to Strike or Tax Costs; (3) for Sanctions Under CCP § 128.5

Plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC’s Motions (1) for Attorney Fees; (2) to Strike or Tax Costs

Motions for Attorney Fees and Motions to Tax Costs

            Both parties move for attorney fees and move to tax one another’s memoranda of costs.  The four motions all turn on the same question: who was the prevailing party?  Defendant/cross-complainant Shara Koplowitz is the prevailing party for two reasons.

First, the arbitrator already decided Koplowitz is the prevailing party.  Plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC’s argument that it prevailed constitutes an improper collateral attack on the arbitration award and on this court’s order confirming the award.  In the arbitration proceeding, plaintiff moved for attorney fees.  The arbitrator denied both motions.  (Shtofman Decl. 5/2/23, Ex. 2, Arb. Order [describing ACMO Nos. 9 & 12].)  Courts have limited authority to vacate an arbitration award.  “A court’s power to correct or vacate an erroneous arbitration award is closely circumscribed.” (Heimlich v. Shivji (2019) 7 Cal.5th 350, 367.)  Plaintiff moved to vacate the arbitration award.  The court denied that motion.  The court instead granted defendant’s motion to confirm the arbitration award.  Even if the arbitrator’s decision was incorrect, plaintiff failed to show a basis for vacating the award.  The arbitrator’s decision that plaintiff was not the prevailing party stands. 

Second, defendant is the prevailing party under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1032(a)(4) and 1293.2.  Plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC argues it is the prevailing party under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032(a)(4) as “a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant.”  That portion of subdivision (a)(4) does not apply because both parties cannot be the prevailing party.

Under these circumstances, the applicable provision is that “in situations other than as specified, the ‘prevailing party’ shall be as determined by the court, and by the court, and under those circumstances, the court, in its discretion, may allow costs or not.”  (CCP § 1032(a)(4).)  When a case “falls within the ‘in situations other than as specified’ prong” of the statute, the court must “ ‘ “exercise its discretion both in determining the prevailing party and in allowing, denying, or apportioning costs.” ’ ”  (Friends of Spring Street v. Nevada City (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1104.)  “[T]he trial court determines whether the party succeeded at a practical level by realizing its litigation objectives [citation] and the action yielded the primary relief sought in the case.”  (Ibid.)

Here, defendant succeeded at a practical level because she successfully moved to confirm the arbitration award, including an award to her of over $150,000 in attorney fees.  The parties arbitrated their substantive disputes against one another.  The remaining proceedings in this court were the parties’ competing motions to confirm or vacate the arbitration award under Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.  The California Arbitration Act provides, “The court shall award costs upon any judicial proceeding under this title as provided in” Code of Civil Procedure section section 1021, et seq.  (CCP § 1293.2.) 

Even if there were no prevailing party in the underlying arbitration or the substantive dispute, defendant achieved a complete victory in the postarbitration proceedings in this court.  “Postarbitration proceedings are distinct from arbitration proceedings, and it may be that the prevailing party in the arbitration is not the prevailing party in postarbitration proceedings.”  (Cohen v. TNP 2008 Participating Notes Program, LLC (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 840, 878 (Cohen).)  In postarbitration proceedings, parties who “successfully defeated the petition to vacate the award and successfully confirmed the monetary awards” in their favor are “were the prevailing parties.”  (Id. at p. 879.)  Defendant successfully moved to confirm the arbitration award and successfully opposed plaintiff’s motion to vacate the award. 

Defendant is the prevailing party.  Plaintiff is not entitled to attorney fees or other costs.  The court therefore will (1) deny plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees and (2) grant defendant’s motion to strike plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

Amount of Defendant’s Attorney Fees

Defendant moves for $39,325 in attorney fees.  Defendant is the prevailing party and is entitled to recover attorney fees pursuant to the parties’ contract.  Plaintiff argues defendant seeks to recover attorney fees this court already denied when ruling on prior motions for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.  Such sanctions are discretionary.  “A trial court may order a party … to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith.”  (CCP § 128.5(a).)  “If warranted, the court may award to the party prevailing on the motion the reasonable expenses and attorney’s fees incurred in presenting or opposing the motion.”  (Id., subd. (f)(1)(C).) 

In contrast, defendant now moves for attorney fees under a contract that provides for the prevailing party’s recovery of attorney fees.  As discussed above, defendant prevailed in these proceedings, including those under the California Arbitration Act.  “The award of costs pursuant to [Code of Civil Procedure section] section 1293.2, including attorney fees when authorized by contract, is mandatory.”  (Cohen, supra, 31 Cal.App.5th at p. 878.)  Courts are “required to award [a party], as the party prevailing on the contract, reasonable attorney fees and costs for post-arbitration judicial proceedings, pursuant to the statutory scheme governing arbitration.”  (Carole Ring & Associates v. Nicastro (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 253, 260.)  The court therefore will award attorney fees based on defendant’s lodestar, “the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)

Plaintiff argues this motion is an improper motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.  It is not.  Defendant has not previously sought these fees as the prevailing party in this proceeding.

Plaintiff also challenges several billing entries.  The court finds all claimed attorney fees were reasonably incurred.  Defendant’s counsel Robert Gentino’s hourly rate of $650 reflects the reasonable market value of his services.  The court will therefore grant defendant’s motion for attorney fees in its entirety.

Amount of Defendant’s Other Costs

Defendant’s memorandum of costs claimed a total of $362.75, comprising $339.75 in filing and motion fees and $23 in other costs.  Plaintiff argues defendant failed to justify those expenses.  A “verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show they were not reasonable or necessary.”  (612 South LLC v. Laconic Limited Partnership (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1285.)  These nominal costs are presumptively proper.  Plaintiff does not meet its burden of showing the costs were not reasonable or necessary. 

Assuming plaintiff met its initial burden of doing so, defendant establishes these costs were proper.  Defendant’s opposition authenticated copies of receipts substantiating all costs.  (Gentino Decl., ¶¶ 1-2, Exs. 1-2.)  The court will therefore deny plaintiff’s motion to strike or tax defendant’s memorandum of costs.

Defendant’s Motion for Sanctions

            Defendant moves for $3,062.82 in sanctions against plaintiff and its counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.  CCP § 128.5 applies to “ ‘actions or tactics’ which are both frivolous and in ‘bad-faith.’ ”  (Summers v. City of Cathedral City (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1047, 1070.)  “[A] showing that a party’s action or tactic is totally and completely without merit does not settle the issue of whether that action or tactic was in bad faith.  …  [T]he trial court may not be willing to draw that inference if it is convinced that, despite the complete lack of merit, the party was acting in the good faith (albeit erroneous and even unreasonable) belief that the action was meritorious.”  (Id. at p. 1073.)  Sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 “requir[e] subjective bad faith” (In re Marriage of Sahafzadeh-Taeb & Taeb (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 124, 128), meaning they were “committed with an improper motive, such as to harass or manipulate opposing counsel or the court” (id. at p. 141).

Defendant moves for sanctions for plaintiff’s action or tactic of filing a memorandum of costs on August 14, 2023.  Filing that memorandum of costs was frivolous.  The parties already litigated the issue of whether plaintiff was the prevailing party—several times.  Plaintiff lost each time.  Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs was “totally and completely without merit.”  (CCP § 128.5(b)(2).)  The court, however, does not find sufficient evidence of plaintiff’s counsel’s subjective bad faith.  The court will therefore not impose sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.

Disposition

            Plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC’s motion for attorney fees is denied.

Defendant Shara Koplowitz’s motion to strike or tax plaintiff’s memorandum of costs is granted.  The court hereby strikes the memorandum of costs plaintiff filed on August 14, 2023.  Plaintiff shall recover nothing from defendant.

Defendant Shara Koplowitz’s motion for attorney fees is granted.  Defendant shall recover $39,325 in attorney fees from plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC.

Plaintiff’s motion to strike or tax defendant’s memorandum of costs is denied.  Defendant shall recover $362.75 in costs from plaintiff 7219-7225 West Sunset LLC.

Defendant Shara Koplowitz’s motion for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 is denied.
