DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV32625    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: W

URI PEREZ v. GEORGE ANDERSON

 

motion for additional discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        October 31, 2024                                          Trial Date:       11/4/24

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        19STCV32625

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Uri Perez

Responding Party:     George Anderson

 

BACKGROUND

 

On September 13, 2019, plaintiff Uri Perez filed a complaint against defendant George Anderson for injuries arising from a motor vehicle accident.  This case has been set for trial and continued multiple times.  Plaintiff and his former counsel have been sanctioned several times for discovery abuses and violations of court orders.   Prior counsel substituted out on August 8, 2024, and new counsel took over the case.  While new counsel had believed that expert discovery deadlines would be based on the new trial date of November 4, 2024, he subsequently learned that defendant was taking the position that all discovery deadlines were to be based on an earlier trial date of December 13, 2023.    Plaintiff’s new counsel moved swiftly to file this motion for additional discovery after entering the case.   Defendant opposes the motion. 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s motion for additional discovery is denied.  

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff moves this court for additional discovery pursuant to C.C.P. Section 2024.050, which provides:

 

 (a) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

 

(b) In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following:

 

(1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery.

 

(2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.

 

(3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party.

 

(4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.

 

            The court finds that plaintiff cannot establish a basis for relief under Section 2024.050.  While expert discovery is obviously necessary for any case in which experts have been designated, the rules relating to the disclosure and discovery of expert opinions are rigorous and must be complied with.   Prior counsel’s declaration that he did not know that he had stipulated to a discovery cut-off based on the December 13, 2023 trial date is belied by his conduct.  He would have received multiple deposition notices and substantial checks for the appearance of plaintiff’s experts all set for the end of November 2023 – the discovery cut-off date set by the court in its order of November 1, 2023.   He did not appear at those depositions or even contact defense counsel to ask why they were being set at that time and not based on a later trial date.  He also did not set any depositions of his own.  Defense counsel showed up at each deposition and took and paid for a notice of non-appearance. 

 

There is also a history of discovery abuses by plaintiff and his prior counsel.  Critically, Plaintiff’s counsel did not produce plaintiff for a court-ordered neurological exam on November 14, or for the court ordered and stipulated neurological exam on February 1, 2024.  It was not until some time in February that plaintiff’s former counsel claims to have realized that there was a court order that all discovery was cut-off and then only when defendant brought a motion to compel the mental health examination and for sanctions.   By the time the court ruled on that motion on April 18, 2024, plaintiff knew that it was the court and defendant’s position that discovery was cut-off in November 2023.  Despite this information, plaintiff’s counsel did nothing to request relief or to reopen discovery.

 

 In short, plaintiff’s counsel has displayed a remarkable lack of diligence in obtaining expert discovery, complying with defendant’s requests for discovery and seeking relief from his failure to cooperate.   New counsel’s efforts to reopen these issues, while well-meaning and pursued in good faith, do not erase the years of discovery abuses by former counsel. 

 

            Further, if the court were to permit discovery of the parties’ eight retained experts, the court would need to continue the current trial date of November 4.  This court is heavily impacted by other cases and cannot easily move the current trial date without interfering with the expectations of other parties, many of whom are also approaching the five-year deadline for getting their cases to trial.   The court further finds that defendants will be severely prejudiced should the court reopen expert discovery.  Defendants reasonably expected that discovery would be completed by the end of 2023. Plaintiff ignored their requests for expert discovery.  Defendants have planned their trial strategy accordingly.  To require them to now expend time and money to depose plaintiff’s experts and to incorporate those findings and opinions into their trial planning would be disruptive and costly.  

 

            Accordingly, the motion to allow additional discovery is denied.

motion for additional discovery

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Uri Perez

BACKGROUND

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s motion for additional discovery is denied.  

 

discussion

(2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.

 

(3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party.

Case Number: 24VECV00052    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: W

SAJJAD TAKALLOU vs WALMART INC.

 

defendant walmart inc.’s motion for summary judgment on plaintiff’s complaint for damages

 

Date of Hearing:        October 31, 2024                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV00052

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Walmart, Inc.

Opposing Party:        No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a premises liability case. Plaintiff Sajjad Takallou alleges while walking down the shopping aisle at a Walmart location, an employee operating a metal commercial cart made contact with Plaintiff, causing Plaintiff to fall into a clothing rack while carrying a child. As a result of the incident, Plaintiff has suffered injuries. On January 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant Walmart, Inc. for premises liability.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Walmart Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment on Plaintiff’s Complaint for Damages is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Walmart, Inc. moves this court for an order granting Defendant’s summary judgment on Plaintiff Sajjad Takallou’s Complaint for damages.

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) A moving defendant need not conclusively negate an element of plaintiff’s cause of action. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.) It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence. (Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

 

“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that while carrying a child and walking down a shopping aisle at a Walmart located in West Hills, California, a Walmart associate hit him repeatedly with a cart, causing him to fall into a clothing rack. (SSUMF No. 1, 2.) To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  Here, Defendant Walmart presents evidence that Plaintiff was not carrying a child at the time of the Incident, nor did he fall into a clothing rack. (SSUMF No. 3.) The video footage also shows that a Walmart associate also did not strike him with a cart; rather, Plaintiff shoved a cart out of his way as he ran past it to chase after one of his daughters. (SSUMF No. 4.) Moreover, based on Plaintiff’s discovery responses, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s deemed admissions establish that he cannot prove that Defendant caused his injuries. “[A] deemed admitted order establishes, by judicial fiat, that a nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein.”  (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979.)  Requests for admissions “differs fundamentally from the other five discovery tools (depositions, interrogatories, inspection demands, medical examinations, and expert witness exchanges) . . . [because] admission requests seek to eliminate the need for proof: ‘The purpose of the admissions procedure . . . is to limit the triable issues and spare the parties the burden and expense of litigating undisputed issues.’  Sometimes, the admissions obtained will even leave the party making them vulnerable to summary judgment.”  [Citations.]  Matters that are admitted or deemed admitted through RFA discovery devices are conclusively established in the litigation and are not subject to being contested through contrary evidence.  [Citation.]”  (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 775.) 

 

On February 16, 2024, Defendant served Requests for Admissions, Set One on Plaintiff. (SSUMF 10, Exh. H.) Plaintiff’s responses to the Requests for Admissions were due no later than March 22, 2024. (SSUMF 12.) Defendant did not receive responses by that date, and therefore filed a Motion to Deem Admitted on March 29, 2024.  (SSUMF 13, Exh. J.) After hearing on June 5, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion, deeming the Requests for Admissions, Set One, admitted. (SSUMF 15, Exh. L.) Thereafter, Defendant received Plaintiff’s responses to written discovery requests on August 2, 2024. (SSUMF 16, Exh. M.) In this response, Plaintiff states he was not injured because of the accident. (SSUMF 18, Exh. M.) Moreover, Defendant asked Plaintiff to admit that the Incident was not caused by a dangerous condition on the Defendant’s premises, that he was not injured because of the Incident, Defendant did not breach a duty to him on the date of the Incident, and Plaintiff could have avoided this Incident using reasonable care. (SSUMF 17-20, Exh.s L, H, M.) As noted above, the court ordered Defendant’s requests for admission deemed admitted after Plaintiff failed to serve responses.  Therefore, Plaintiff has admitted that he cannot establish essential elements for his premises liability claim.

 

Plaintiff did not oppose this motion and failed to raise a triable issue of material fact.  Accordingly, Plaintiff did not meet his burden to show why summary judgment should not be granted.   

 

In light of the foregoing, the Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.
