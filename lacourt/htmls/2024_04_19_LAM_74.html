Case Number: 22STCV41032    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 74

Richard Gonzalez v. 2013 Madrid Family Irrevocable Trust Dated May 31, 203, et al.

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One) and Request for Sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a landlord-tenant dispute.

            On December 30, 2022, Plaintiff Richard Gonzalez (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendant 2013 Madrid Family Irrevocable Trust Dated May 31, 2013 (Defendant) and Alfredo Madrid Jr.

            In the complaint, Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: (1) breach of contract, (2) tortious breach of the implied warranty of habitability, (3) violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, (4) unlawful and unfair business practices, (5) violation of Civil Code section 1942.5, (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (7) violation of Civil Code section 51.

            On July 6, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with special interrogatories (set one).

            However, Defendant never provided responses.

            On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel Defendant’s responses to the special interrogatories and request for sanctions.

LEGAL STANDARD

¿¿            If a party to whom interrogatories or an inspection demand were directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses without objections. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).) Moreover, failure to timely serve responses waives objections to the requests. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).) Failure to verify a response is equivalent to no response at all. (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) 

DISCUSSION 

            The court grants this motion because Defendant has failed to provide timely responses to the special interrogatories. Moreover, sanctions are warranted because Defendant has not demonstrated that it acted with substantial justification. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) Plaintiff’s counsel proposed hourly rate is reasonable and he has provided a breakdown of work performed. (Alatorre Decl., ¶ 8.) Accordingly, the court grants Plaintiff’s counsel’s request for sanctions in the amount of $800.

CONCLUSION 

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s responses to special interrogatories (set one). Defendant shall provide verified, Code-compliant responses, without objections, within 30 days of this order. The court grants Plaintiff’s counsel request for sanctions. Defendant shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel $800 within 30 days of this order.

Plaintiff shall give notice.

(a), 2031.300, subd. (a).) Failure to verify a response is equivalent to no response at all. (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) 

DISCUSSION 

            The court grants this motion because Defendant has failed to provide timely responses to the form interrogatories. Moreover, sanctions are warranted because Defendant has not demonstrated that it acted with substantial justification. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) Plaintiff’s counsel proposed hourly rate is reasonable and he has provided a breakdown of work performed. (Alatorre Decl., ¶ 8.) However, this motion largely duplicates work from Plaintiff’s other discovery motion. Accordingly, the court will award Plaintiff’s counsel sanctions in the amount of $400.

CONCLUSION 

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s responses to form interrogatories (general). Defendant shall provide verified, Code-compliant responses, without objections, within 30 days of this order. The court grants Plaintiff’s counsel request for sanctions. Defendant shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel $400 within 30 days of this order.

Case Number: 23STCV19069    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 74

People of the State of California v. Ultimate Host, LLC, et al.

Defendants’ Demurrer to Complaint

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from allegedly unruly parties at properties that serve as short-term rentals.

            On August 10, 2023, the People of the State of California (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Ultimate Host, LLC and Mokhtar Jabli (Defendants), in addition to others.

            The complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) violation of California’s unfair competition law and (2) public nuisance.

            On October 2, 2023, Defendants filed this demurrer.

LEGAL STANDARD

            A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (¿¿Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318¿¿.) “¿To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.¿” (¿¿C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872¿¿.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (¿Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967¿.) A demurrer “¿does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.¿” (¿¿Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713¿¿.)

DISCUSSION 

            In support of their demurrer, Defendants first argue Defendant Jabli cannot be liable in his individual capacity. That is, Plaintiff’s allegations do not indicate that Defendant Ultimate Host is an alter-ego of Defendant Jabli. According to Defendants, the court should dismiss the claims against Defendant Jabli on this basis.

            However, Plaintiff has alleged facts that, if true, support Defendant’s Jabli’s personal liability for violations of California’s Unfair Competition Law, which prohibits unlawful business practices. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) Under the allegations, Defendant Jabli is a tenant at the Birds Villa. (Complaint, ¶¶ 48, 51.) Defendant Jabli has subleased the property for short-term rentals, even though the property is not registered for home sharing. (Complaint, ¶¶ 48, 51, 75; LAMC, § 12.22, subd. (A)(d)(1)-(2).) In addition, the allegations suggest Defendant Jabli has subleased the property for the purposes of parties that have violated the Los Angeles House Party Ordinance. (Complaint, ¶ 2, 48, 51, 83; see LAMC, §§ 41.58.1, 116.01.)

            The foregoing allegations also support Plaintiff’s public nuisance claim against Defendant Jabli. Under Los Angeles Municipal Code section 11.00, subdivision (l), any violation of the municipal code is declared to be a public nuisance. Moreover, “where the law expressly declares something to be a nuisance, then no inquiry beyond its existence need be made and in this sense its mere existence is said to be a nuisance per se.” (People v. ConAgra Grocery Products Co. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 51, 114.) Thus, the court will not dismiss Defendant Jabli from the action.

            Defendants go on to argue that Plaintiff’s complaint contains inaccurate allegations. But for the purpose of testing the sufficiency of causes of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (¿Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist., supra, Cal.4th at pp. 966-967¿.) The court will not sustain Defendants’ demurrer on this basis, either.

CONCLUSION 

The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer.

Defendants shall file and serve their answer within 20 days.

Defendants shall give notice.

The People of the State of California v. Ultimate Host, LLC, et al.

Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of the Complaint

The court considered the moving papers and opposition.

BACKGROUND

            This action arises from allegedly unruly parties at properties that serve as short-term rentals.

            On August 10, 2023, the People of the State of California (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Ultimate Host, LLC and Mokhtar Jabli (Defendants), in addition to others.

            The complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) violation of California’s unfair competition law and (2) public nuisance.

LEGAL STANDARD

            A court may strike any “¿irrelevant, false or improper matter¿inserted in any pleading¿” or any part of a pleading “¿not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 436¿.) “¿The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437¿.)¿           

DISCUSSION 

            Defendants first argue the court should strike inaccurate facts from the complaint. But in ruling on a motion to strike, the court presumes the allegations in the complaint to be true. (Clauson v. Sup.Ct. (Pedus Services, Inc.) (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)[1] Next, Defendants contend the court should strike the request for costs because Plaintiff cites no statute in support. However, under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b), a prevailing party is entitled to costs in any action as a matter of right, unless otherwise expressly provided by statute. Last, the court notes that Defendants have not identified the portions of the complaint they seek to strike in the notice. (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1322.)

CONCLUSION 

            The court denies Defendants’ motion to strike in its entirety.

            Defendants shall give notice.

[1] The court notes that judicially noticed matters may provide grounds for a motion to strike. However, Defendants have not filed a request for judicial notice.
