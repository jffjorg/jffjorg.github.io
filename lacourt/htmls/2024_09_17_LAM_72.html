DEPARTMENT 72 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV41597    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

YEHUDA ELMAKIAS, et al.,

 

                                  Plaintiffs,

 

         v.

 

 

ILAN KENIG, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  21STCV41597

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 17, 2024

 Calendar Number:  3

 

 

 

Defendants Ilan Kenig (“Kenig”), FMB Development, LLC (“FMB”), and Nehorai Construction, Inc. (“Nehorai”) (collectively, “Defendants”) move for judgment on the pleadings as to the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) filed by Plaintiffs Yehuda Elmakias (“Elmakias”) and Cloud Nine Builders (“Cloud Nine”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”).

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motion.

 

Background

 

This is a construction contract case.

 

Defendants retained Plaintiff Elmakias to perform project management on approximately 41 construction projects. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to pay Plaintiffs, converted Plaintiffs’ property, fraudulently used Plaintiffs’ contractor license, and created fake contracts, checks, and invoices to obtain improper funding.

 

Plaintiffs recorded a number of mechanic’s liens against Defendant FMB relating to reimbursement of the fees and costs advanced on six of the properties.

 

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiffs and FMB entered into a settlement agreement (the “Settlement”). (Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. A (“Settlement”).) The Settlement provided that Cloud Nine would release the mechanics’ liens and would not record further liens against Defendants’ properties. (Settlement at pp. 2-3.) The Settlement provided that FMB would dismiss all of the petitions that it had filed for expungement of the liens. (Settlement at pp. 2-3.) The Settlement included reciprocal claim releases by both parties. (Settlement at p. 3.) The releases explicitly excluded all claims which result from any act other than the fact that mechanics liens were recorded on the six properties in question. (Settlement at p. 3.)

 

Plaintiffs filed this action against Defendants on November 12, 2021.

 

The operative complaint is now the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), which raises claims for (1) breach of contract; (2) conversion; (3) fraud; (4) implied or equitable indemnity; (5) temporary restraining order; (6) preliminary injunction; and (7) permanent injunction.

 

Kenig and FMB (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiffs on April 11, 2022.

 

The operative cross-complaint is now the First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”), which raises claims for (1) breach of contract; (2) negligence; (3) defamation per se; and (4) conversion.

 

On May 17, 2024, a non-jury trial commenced in this action. Kenig was individually present, representing himself. FMB and Nehorai did not appear because they were not able to do so without counsel. Kenig requested a continuance, which was at first denied. Kenig participated in the trial. Plaintiff conducted direct examination of Elmakias. Plaintiff sought provisional admission of eighteen exhibits, which the Court granted, pending any objections by Defendants. When the Court determined that there were problems with the notice on Kenig, however, the Court continued the trial to July 17, 2024.

 

On July 11, 2024, Kenig filed an ex parte application to continue the trial. On July 12, 2024, the Court granted the ex parte application and continued trial to October 7, 2024. At the hearing, Kenig contended that trial in this matter should be a jury trial. The Court set an order to show cause on whether trial should be a jury trial for August 22, 2024.

 

Defendants filed an initial brief on this issue. Plaintiffs filed an opposition and Defendants filed a reply.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

“Judicial notice may be taken of the following matters to the extent that they are not embraced within Section 451:

 

(a) The decisional, constitutional, and statutory law of any state of the United States and the resolutions and private acts of the Congress of the United States and of the Legislature of this state.

 

(b) Regulations and legislative enactments issued by or under the authority of the United States or any public entity in the United States.

 

(c) Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.

 

(d) Records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States.

 

(e) Rules of court of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States.

 

(f) The law of an organization of nations and of foreign nations and public entities in foreign nations.

 

(g) Facts and propositions that are of such common knowledge within the territorial jurisdiction of the court that they cannot reasonably be the subject of dispute.

 

(h) Facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”

 

(Evid. Code, § 452.)

 

            Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of the Settlement because its existence and contents are not in dispute.

 

            Plaintiffs do not dispute the existence or contents of the Settlement. However, Plaintiffs dispute the proper interpretation of the Settlement’s terms and argue that judicial notice of it is therefore improper.

 

            “If the language of the agreement is unambiguous and no disputed extrinsic evidence bears upon its meaning, interpretation of the document is a legal issue for the court.” (Chacon v. Union Pacific Railroad (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 565, 573.) “Where, as here, judicial notice is requested of a legally operative document—like a contract—the court may take notice not only of the fact of the document and its recording or publication, but also facts that clearly derive from its legal effect.” (Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 754.)

 

            Here, Plaintiffs do not dispute the existence of the Settlement agreement or the text of its terms. Nor do Plaintiffs dispute the meaning of the Settlement’s terms using extrinsic evidence. Rather, Plaintiffs argue (as discussed below) that the plain terms of the Settlement show that it only released claims relating to the acts of recordation of the mechanic’s liens, and not the entire universe of the parties’ actual or potential claims against each other. Thus, the Court may properly take judicial notice of the Settlement agreement and interpret its terms as a matter of law.

 

            The Court therefore grants the request for judicial notice and takes notice of the Settlement between Plaintiffs and Defendant NMB.

 

Legal Standard

 

Either prior to trial, but after the time to answer or demur has passed, or at the trial, the plaintiff or the defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings and the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense. (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791; See also Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586 [The non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for a general demurrer are never waived.], see also Code Civ. Proc., §438(f).)  

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. (See, e.g., Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (TRG 1998) §§ 7:275, 7:322; Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.) Both a demurrer and a motion for judgment on the pleadings accept as true all material factual allegations of the challenged pleading, unless contrary to law or to facts of which a court may take judicial notice. (Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677; Edwards v. Centex Real Estate Corp, (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 15, 27.) 

 

The motion may be made only after one of the following conditions has occurred: (1) If the moving party is a plaintiff, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired; (2) If the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(f).) The motion provided for in Code of Civil Procedure section 438 may be made even though either of the following conditions exist:  (1) The moving party has already demurred to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section and the demurrer has been overruled, provided that there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer; (2) The moving party did not demur to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(g).) No motion may be made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438 if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(e).)

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants argue that the Settlement fully resolved the parties’ claims against each other and that Plaintiffs’ claims must be dismissed as a result.

 

            By its express terms, the Settlement only provides for the release of claims stemming from the act of recordation of the mechanics liens on FMB’s properties. (Settlement at p. 3.) The relevant paragraph of the Settlement is paragraph 5.  While the first sentence of this paragraph contains what appears to be a broad release of FMB by Cloud Nine, the paragraph goes on to state as follows: 

 

“The parties expressly agree and acknowledge that this release is limited to and does not extend to or impact any actual or potential claims, liability or damages resulting from any act or omission by FMB and/or Ilan Kenig or any of its principals other than the fact that Mechanic’s Liens were recorded on the Properties.”

 

(Settlement at p. 3, paragraph 5.)

 

            The FAC includes a number of claims that do not stem from the act of recordation of liens or security interests. The FAC alleges that Plaintiffs worked on 41 projects – more than the six properties covered in the Settlement. (FAC ¶ 15.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to pay a weekly management fee and a year-end bonus. (FAC ¶¶ 19, 24.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to return tools and equipment to Plaintiffs and failed to pay the rental rates for them. (FAC ¶ 26.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants fraudulently used Plaintiffs’ general contractor license on multiple projects. (FAC ¶ 28.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants created fake contracts, invoices, and checks which exposed Plaintiffs to damages. (FAC ¶ 31.) Plaintiffs seek damages for this conduct.

 

            Defendants argue that the claims in the FAC are inherently bound up in the same business relationship and disputes resolved by the Settlement. (Reply at p. 4.) Defendants argue that Plaintiffs filed this lawsuit under the same core theory under which Plaintiffs filed the mechanic’s liens.

 

            The Settlement, however, did not extend its release to the full business relationship. It only extended its release to the fact that the liens were recorded. Plaintiffs seek recovery for different acts – acts which were allegedly committed by Defendants, and not by Plaintiffs, who recorded the liens. The Court therefore finds that the Settlement does not bar the action as a whole.

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiffs implicitly concede that there are some claims in this case which relate to the recordation of the mechanics’ liens, and that the Court should therefore grant the motion in part. (Reply at p. 1:19-22.) However, Defendants do not explain which of Plaintiffs’ claims they believe relate to the act of recording the liens – outside, that is, of Defendants’ argument that the answer is “all of them.” Furthermore, Defendants’ notice does not split out Plaintiffs’ various claims and explain different grounds for their dismissal; rather, it simply seeks dismissal of the entire FAC pursuant to the Settlement. The Court therefore finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that dismissal of any specific claim is proper.

 

            The Court therefore denies the motion.


Case Number: 23STCV28232    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 72

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ROSALIE GENUS,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

JANET HAYWOOD, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  23STCV28232

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 17, 2024

 Calendar Number:  4

 

 

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Rosalie Genus (“Plaintiff”) moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the Cross-Complaint filed by Defendants and Cross-Complainants Janet Haywood (“Haywood”) and 8424 Western Plaza, LLC (“Western Plaza”) (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”).

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion without leave to amend.

 

Background

 

This is a real property dispute.

 

Allegations of the Complaint

 

The following facts are taken from the allegations of the Complaint. The Court does not accept them as true for the purposes of the motion for judgment on the pleadings.  The Court recites them for context only.

 

Plaintiff and Haywood are sisters. Haywood owned the real property located at 10427 S. Denker Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90047 (the “Property”).

 

In 2007, Plaintiff moved into the Property as a tenant.

 

On January 27, Haywood formed the Western Plaza as an LLC. On April 7, 2017, Haywood transferred title to the Property to Western Plaza.

 

Plaintiff alleges that on January 25, 2018, Plaintiff and Western Plaza entered a written agreement whereby Plaintiff would purchase the property from Western Plaza for $325,000.00. Plaintiff alleges that she complied with the terms of the purchase agreement. Plaintiff alleges that Western Plaza, acting through Haywood, refused to sign the necessary documents to close escrow and thus transfer the property.

 

What followed was a series of lawsuits resulting in stipulated judgements, each of which provided that Defendants Haywood and Western Plaza would transfer the property to Plaintiff, and each of which Defendants Haywood and Western Plaza violated by refusing to sign the transfer documents.

 

Most recently, in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 19STCV39523, the Court ordered on November 29, 2022 that the court clerk would be appointed as an elisor to execute sale documents on behalf of Haywood and Western Plaza. Plaintiff brought an ex parte application on May 18, 2023 for an order directing the court clerk to sign the escrow instructions for the transfer (the “Escrow Instructions”), which the Court granted. The court clerk signed the Escrow Instructions.

 

The Escrow Instructions provide for the duties and obligations of New Era Escrow, Inc. (“New Era”), which include engaging Lawyers Title Company (“Lawyers Title”) to prepare and deliver to Plaintiff a policy of title insurance.

 

Plaintiff alleges that she provided the signed Escrow Instructions to the escrow officer, New Era and to the title company, Lawyers Title.

 

Plaintiff alleges that Haywood and the Western Plaza contacted New Era and demanded that New Era refrain from closing escrow to allow Haywood and Western Plaza to appeal the judgment in Case No. 19STCV39523. The Escrow Instructions do not provide a grace period for appeal, nor do they permit a party to unilaterally cancel the agreement. Haywood and Western Plaza have not posted bond.

 

Plaintiff alleges that New Era refused to comply with the Escrow Instructions and close escrow.

 

Plaintiff alleges that Lawyers Title has not delivered a title insurance policy to Plaintiff.

 

Allegations of the Cross-Complaint

 

The following facts are taken from the allegations of the Cross-Complaint, which the Court accepts as true for the purposes of the motion.

 

In 2003, Haywood acquired ownership of the Property and conveyed ownership to Western Plaza. Haywood is the sole member and manager of Western Plaza.

 

The Cross-Complaint alleges that Plaintiff, who is Haywood’s sister, became a tenant at the Property prior to 2017 and that the parties entered a purchase agreement under which Haywood would sell the Property to Plaintiff.

 

The key point on which the Cross-Complaint differs from the Complaint is the purchase price. Cross-Complainants allege that the appraised value of the Property was $500,000.00. Cross-Complainants allege that the purchase contract reflects a purchase price of $325,0000, but that Plaintiff and Haywood actually agreed to a sale price of $500,000.00. Cross-Complainants allege that Plaintiff promised to pay Haywood an additional $175,000.00, on top of the stated price of $325,000.00, after escrow closed. Cross-Complainants allege that the written contract contained a mistake because it did not include the additional $175,000.00, and is thus subject to reformation.

 

Cross-Complainants allege that Plaintiff requested this arrangement because she did not want to take out additional financing to purchase the property. Cross-Complainants allege that Plaintiff had no intention to honor the promise to pay the additional $175,000.00.

 

Cross-Complainants allege that prior to the close of escrow, Haywood learned from another family member that Plaintiff had told other family members that she was not going to pay the $175,000 to Janet once title transferred to Plaintiff’s name. Cross-Complainants allege that Haywood asked for reassurance from Plaintiff that Plaintiff would pay the additional $175,000.00, but that Plaintiff ignored the request and demanded that escrow close.

 

Cross-Complainants allege that on February 1, 2018, Cross-Complainants and Plaintiff agreed to a set of sale escrow instructions for the Property. Cross-Complainants attach a copy of escrow instructions dated January 25, 2018. (Cross-Complaint, Ex. 1.) Cross-Complainants allege that Haywood would not have signed Exhibit 1 if Plaintiff had not promised to pay the additional $175,000.00.

 

Cross-Complainants allege that, by seeking to compel Cross-Complainants to sell the Property for $325,000.00, Plaintiff has caused Cross-Complainants to suffer damages.

 

Procedural Background

 

Plaintiff filed this action on November 16, 2023. The operative complaint is now the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), which is filed against Defendants Haywood, Western Plaza, New Era, and Lawyers Title. New Era and Lawyers Title were not parties to the previous actions. The FAC raises claims for (1) declaratory relief (against all Defendants); (2) injunction (against New Era and Lawyers Title); (3) injunction (against Haywood and Western Plaza); and (4) specific performance (against New Era and Lawyers Title). Plaintiff seeks damages from Haywood and Western Plaza, and an order compelling New Era to comply with the Escrow Instructions and close escrow.

 

Lawyers Title was dismissed from this action on September 5, 2024 pursuant to Plaintiff’s request.

 

On June 17, 2024, Haywood and Western Plaza filed the Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, raising claims for (1) reformation of contract; (2) declaratory relief (as to the sale price of the Property); and (3) fraud.

 

On August 5, 2024, Plaintiff moved for judgment on the pleadings as to the Cross-Complaint. No party filed an opposition.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of the following documents:

 

A. Complaint filed in Rosalie Genus v 8424 Western Plaza, LLC, LA Superior Court Case No. BC706314;

 

B. Settlement Agreement filed in Rosalie Genus v 8424 Western Plaza, LLC, LA Superior Court Case No. BC706314;

 

C. Order granting ex parte application directing 8424 Western Plaza, LLC to pay all liens secured against the property in Rosalie Genus v 8424 Western Plaza, LLC, LA Superior Court Case No. BC706314;

 

D. Complaint filed in Rosalie Genus v K. Janet Genus Haywood et.al., LA Superior Court Case No. 19STCV39523;

 

E. Stipulated judgment filed in Rosalie Genus v K. Janet Genus Haywood et.al., LA Superior Court Case No. 19STCV39523;

 

F. Cross-Complaint filed in Rosalie Genus v Janet Haywood et.al., LA Superior Court Case No. 23STCV28232.

 

            The Court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice and takes notice of the above documents as public records.

 

Legal Standard

 

Either prior to trial, but after the time to answer or demur has passed, or at the trial, the plaintiff or the defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings and the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense. (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791; See also Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586 [The non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for a general demurrer are never waived.], see also Code Civ. Proc., §438(f).)  

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. (See, e.g., Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (TRG 1998) §§ 7:275, 7:322; Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.) Both a demurrer and a motion for judgment on the pleadings accept as true all material factual allegations of the challenged pleading, unless contrary to law or to facts of which a court may take judicial notice. (Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677; Edwards v. Centex Real Estate Corp, (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 15, 27.) 

 

The motion may be made only after one of the following conditions has occurred: (1) If the moving party is a plaintiff, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired; (2) If the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(f).) The motion provided for in Code of Civil Procedure section 438 may be made even though either of the following conditions exist:  (1) The moving party has already demurred to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section and the demurrer has been overruled, provided that there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer; (2) The moving party did not demur to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(g).) No motion may be made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438 if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(e).)

 

Discussion

 

Statute of Limitations

 

Plaintiff argues that Cross-Complainants’ claims are time-barred.

 

Reformation of Contract – First Claim

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 338 provides for a three-year statute of limitations for “[a]n action for relief on the ground of fraud or mistake.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) “The cause of action in that case is not deemed to have accrued until the discovery, by the aggrieved party, of the facts constituting the fraud or mistake.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).)

 

Cross-Complainants’ reformation claim is grounded in allegations that the sale contract contains a mistake as to the sale price. It is therefore subject to a three-year statute of limitations.

 

Plaintiff filed her first lawsuit regarding the sales contract against Western Plaza on May 15, 2018. (Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. A.) Plaintiff filed her second lawsuit, against both Haywood and Western Plaza, on November 4, 2019. (Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. D.) These lawsuits specifically alleged that the sales price in the parties’ transaction was $325,000. Thus, Western Plaza had notice by May 15, 2018 at the latest, and Haywood had notice by November 4, 2019 at the latest.

 

The Cross-Complaint was filed on June 17, 2024, more than three years after the latest possible accrual date. This claim is therefore untimely.

 

The Court therefore grants the motion as to this claim.

 

Declaratory Relief – Second Claim

 

Cross-Complainants seek declaratory relief as to the true terms of the sale of the Property. This claim stems from Cross-Complainants’ allegation that the written contract of sale states the wrong sale price. The claim is therefore grounded in mistake and is subject to a three-year statute of limitations.

 

As discussed above, Cross-Complainants had notice of this claim by 2019 at the latest, but did not file the Cross-Complaint until 2024. This claim is therefore untimely.

 

The Court therefore grants the motion as to this claim.

 

Fraud – Third Claim

 

Claims for fraud are subject to a three-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).)

 

As discussed above, Cross-Complainants had notice of this claim by 2019 at the latest, but did not file the Cross-Complaint until 2024. This claim is therefore untimely.

 

The Court therefore grants the motion as to this claim.

 

 


Case Number: 24STCV04204    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

KATHERINE KELLER,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

ROBERT H. BISNO, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  24STCV04204

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 17, 2024

 Calendar Number:  5

 

 

 

Defendants Robert H. Bisno (“Robert”); Jeanette Ann Bisno (“Jeanette”); Law Office of Robert H. Bisno (“Law Office”); Bisno Management Company, LLC (“Bisno Management”); Bisno Development Enterprise, LLC (“Bisno Development”); The Bisno Family Limited Partnership (“Bisno Family”); and Bisno Real Estate Group, LLC (“Bisno Real Estate”) (collectively, “the Bisno Parties”) move for relief from default.

 

The Court GRANTS the motion.  The Bisno Parties shall file their response within five (5) days.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff Katherine Keller (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against the Bisno Parties on February 20, 2024, raising claims for (1) malicious prosecution, and (2) violation of the Bane Act. Plaintiff subsequently named a number of additional corporate defendants via Doe amendments.

 

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff served Robert, Jeanette, Bisno Management, Bisno Development, and Bisno Real Estate via personal service.

 

On May 6, 2024, Bisno Management, Bisno Development, and Bisno Real Estate (collectively, the “Anti-SLAPP Entities”) filed an anti-SLAPP motion (the “First Anti-SLAPP”), noticing the hearing for May 28, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on May 9, 2024 arguing, inter alia, that the motion did not provide an adequate notice period.

 

On May 17, 2024, default was entered against Law Office and Bisno Family.

 

On May 20, 2024, the Anti-SLAPP Entities withdrew the First Anti-SLAPP, stating that they would re-file it with an adequate notice period.

 

On May 21, 2024, default was entered against Robert, Bisno Management, Bisno Development, and Bisno Real Estate.

 

On May 23, 2024, Robert, Bisno Management, Bisno Development, and Bisno Real Estate filed a Notice of Intent to File Anti-SLAPP Motion.

 

On May 28, 2024, the Anti-SLAPP Entities filed the pending anti-SLAPP motion (the “Second Anti-SLAPP”).

 

On May 31, 2024, default was entered against Jeanette.

 

On June 3, 2024, Robert and Jeanette filed a notice of joinder to Second Anti-SLAPP.

 

On July 30, 2024, the Court denied the Second Anti-SLAPP on the basis that all of the moving parties had been defaulted.

 

On August 2, 2024, the Bisno Parties moved for relief from default. Plaintiff filed an opposition and the Bisno Entities filed a reply.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

            The Court has reviewed the parties’ evidentiary objections.

 

            The Court overrules the Bisno Parties’ objections.

 

            The Court sustains Plaintiff’s objection to the reply declaration of Robert Bisno on the basis that it is impermissible evidence submitted with reply papers. The Court notes that even if it did not sustain this objection, the reply Bisno declaration would not change the outcome of this motion, because it consists of legal argument impermissible in a declaration, rather than declarations of fact.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

The Court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice and takes notice of the requested docket items in this case as public records.

 

Legal Standard

 

Permissive Relief Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473 (b)

 

The trial court has discretion to “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 (b).) Such an application shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed in replacement of the pleading being set aside and shall be made within six months after the judgment, dismissal, order, or other pleading for which dismissal is being sought. (Ibid.)

 

Mandatory Relief under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473 (b)

 

Where a defendant in default moves for relief from default, judgment, or other dismissal against them entered as the result of her attorney’s mistake and “no more than six months” have passed since the entry of judgment, the court shall grant such relief as is requested, provided the motion satisfies all procedural requirements and the court does not find that the entry of default was caused by something other than the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 (b).)

 

Discussion

 

Service

 

The Bisno Parties argue that Plaintiff had a practice of refusing to serve the Bisno Parties as required by California Rules of Court, rule 2.254(b) and Los Angeles Superior Court General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil, filed November 5, 2018. The Bisno Parties argue that this was a material cause of their default.

 

California Rules of Court, rule 2.254(b) provides that a court may provide for mandatory electronic filing and service of documents. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.254(b).) The Los Angeles Superior Court has adopted such a rule: “[p]ursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.253(b), represented litigants will be required to electronically file documents with the Court through an approved Electronic Filing Service Provider.” (Los Angeles Superior Court General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil, November 5, 2018 at p. 2:16-20 (“General Order”).)

 

The Bisno Parties argue that Plaintiff was required to serve them electronically pursuant to the General Order. This argument is without any legal basis and does not accord with the language of the General Order.  The General Order requires that a represented party electronically file documents with the Court. Plaintiff has done so – her documents have been timely filed electronically with the Court and are in the Court’s electronic document system. The General Order does not prescribe a manner of service that must be effected on parties. Parties should not make arguments that rely on a blanket misreading of the language of an order.

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff has provided proofs of service as to the Bisno Parties showing that the complaint and summons were served on each of the Bisno Parties via personal service on Robert, the person authorized to receive service (and attorney for the Bisno Parties, other than himself). (Willoughby Decl. ¶ 3-6, Ex. A.) Personal service is a statutorily authorized method of service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)

 

Failure to Notify

 

“The obligation to advise opposing counsel of an impending default is part of an attorney’s responsibility to the court and the legal profession and takes precedence over the obligation to represent the client effectively.” (Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico's FAS, Inc. (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 198, 213, citing Lasalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127, 134, 137.) “The ethical obligation to warn opposing counsel of an intent to take a default is now reinforced by a statutory policy that all parties ‘cooperate in bringing the action to trial or other disposition.’ [Citation.] Quiet speed and unreasonable deadlines do not qualify as ‘cooperation’ and cannot be accepted by the courts.” (Lasalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127, 137.)

 

The Bisno Parties argue that Plaintiff’s counsel improperly failed to notify their counsel, Robert (who represents himself in pro per, and the remaining Bisno Parties as their counsel of record), prior to seeking entry of default.  This argument is without merit.

 

On March 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Robert an email that stated as follows: “Furthermore, please consider this a warning under Lasalle v. Vogel, (2019) 36 Cal. App. 5th 127. File your responsive pleading on time! Your response is due April 10, 2024. Have a great Easter weekend.” (Willoughby Decl. ¶¶ 7-9.)

 

On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Robert again, stating: “If you have a motion to file, I suggest you file it. Please consider this your second warning under Lasalle v. Vogel, (2019) 36 Cal. App. 5th 127. If you do not file your responsive pleading on time, we will file a request for entry of default. Your response is due April 10, 2024.” (Willoughby Decl. ¶¶ 10-12, Ex. C.)

 

On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel again emailed Robert, stating: “This is your final warning pursuant to Lasalle v. Vogel, (2019) 36 Cal. App. 5th 127. If you do not file your responsive pleading on time, we will file a request for entry of default. Your response is due April 10, 2024. You have exhausted your good will with our office.” (Willoughby Decl. ¶¶ 13-15, Ex. D.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s counsel satisfied their duty to notify opposing counsel of their intent to seek entry of default.

 

Mandatory Relief under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473 (b)

 

Relief from default is mandatory where the default was entered as a result of the party’s attorney’s mistake. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 (b).)

 

Here, Robert has provided a declaration stating that his mistake in withdrawing the First Anti-SLAPP and failing to fire the Second Anti-SLAPP on time was the cause of the entry of default against his clients, the other Bisno Parties.

 

Relief from default is therefore mandatory as to all of the Bisno Parties other than Robert.

 

Permissive Relief Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473 (b)

 

            Permissive relief remains available as to Robert, because the default entered against him appears to be a result of his mistake or excusable neglect. Robert withdrew the First Anti-SLAPP in order to remedy defects raised in Plaintiff’s opposition and failed to re-file it in time. While this does appear to be some type of neglect, it is hardly inexcusable. The Court therefore grants permissive relief from default in light of the policy in favor of determining actions on their merits.
