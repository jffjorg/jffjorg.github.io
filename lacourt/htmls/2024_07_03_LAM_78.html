DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV09195    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
 
BRIAN NEMAN,
Plaintiff, 
vs. 
FARHAD YAGHOUBI, et al.,
Defendant. Case No.: 19STCV09195
Hearing Date: July 3, 2024
 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:   
MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT FILED BY DEFENDANTS/CROSS-COMPLAINANTS FARHAD AND ELHAM YAGHOUBI 



The motion to enforce settlement filed by Farhad and Elham Yaghoubi is GRANTED
The request for attorney’s fees is DENIED.
Moving parties must file a proposed judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement agreement within five court days after the date of this order.
Moving party to give notice.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND   
This is an action for breach of contract, fraud, rescission, and conversion. Plaintiff Brian Neman alleges that in April 2017, he entered into a written agreement with Defendants Farhad and Elham Yaghoubi to buy a commercial building for $1,000,000. (FAC ¶9.) Although Plaintiff delivered $1,000,000, Defendants failed to deliver the property by the promised date and refused to return the funds according to the agreement. (FAC ¶¶10-14.) 
On June 6, 2024, the Yaghoubis filed this motion to enforce settlement. The motion is not opposed.
DISCUSSION
The Yaghoubis move to enforce settlement against Cross-Defendants Alfonso Guerrero, Albert Arana, and VIP ENT. 
Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, “if parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.”  “Section 664.6 was enacted to provide a summary procedure for specifically enforcing a settlement contract without the need for a new lawsuit.”  (Weddington Productions, Inc. v. Flick (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 793, 809.)    
To be enforceable under Code Civ Proc., section 664.6, any written settlement agreement outside of court must be signed by the parties and their counsel. Although the Court may adjudicate disputes over the terms of the settlement agreement, the Court may not modify terms from what was agreed to by the parties. (Machado v. Myers (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 779, 797.)
Here, the Yaghoubis and Cross-Defendants executed a settlement agreement on March 27, 2024. (Motion, Exh. 1.) The Yaghoubis agreed to accept, and Cross-Defendants agreed to pay, $125,000, some paid by insurance, in exchange for a mutual release of claims. (Motion, pdf pp.10-11.) Arana, Alfonso, Guerrero, and VIP ENT, Inc. agreed to pay $50,000 within 10 days and $25,000 within 30 days of execution of the settlement agreement for a total of $75,000. (Id.) Cross-Defendants thereafter failed to make the first payment and instead offered to make $5,000 monthly payments. (Kohrs Decl., ¶3; Motion, Exh. 2.) 
The settlement agreement provided that the Court would retain jurisdiction over this action and that a settling party could move to enforce settlement under Code Civ. Proc., section 664.6. (Motion, pdf p.12.) 
Here, the Court has jurisdiction to grant a motion under Code Civ. Proc., section 664.6. Additionally, Cross-Defendants are in breach of their settlement agreement with the Yaghoubis because they failed to make payment according to the agreement. Cross-Defendants did not oppose this motion and thus fail to dispute that they are in breach of the settlement agreement. Therefore, the motion to enforce settlement filed by the Yaghoubis is granted.
The Yaghoubis also move for attorney’s fees, arguing that the agreement only stated that each party would handle their own attorney’s fees up through the execution of the agreement. The Yaghoubis argue that they should be entitled to collect attorney’s fees because they were the prevailing party and attorney’s fees are authorized by the underlying contract. (Motion, pdf p. 5.) However this request falls outside the scope of Code Civ. Proc., section 664.6. The settlement agreement does not state that any party seeking to enforce the agreement would be entitled to attorney’s fees. Although the contract underlying this action authorizes attorney’s fees, section 664.6 only empowers the Court to enter judgment according to the terms of the settlement. Because there is nothing in the settlement agreement authorizing fees for a party seeking to enforce the agreement, the Yaghoubis’ request for attorney’s fees is denied.

DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that he propounded the first set of written discovery on SCG on August 31, 2023. (Osorio Decl., ¶3.) Responses were due by October 3, 2023. (Id., ¶4.) On October 2, 2023, SCG’s counsel requested an extension to November 3, 2023 which Plaintiff’s counsel granted. (Id., ¶5.) SCG requested a second extension on October 31, 2023 which Plaintiff denied. (Id., ¶6.) As of the date this motion was filed, SCG has not served RFA responses. (Id., ¶7.) At the last status conference in this case, SCG’s counsel represented that the reason SCG has not responded to the discovery requests was because SCG has not been communicating with its counsel. (Id., ¶8.)
In opposition, SCG’s counsel testifies that his office began losing contact with SCG around September 11, 2023. (Soleimanpour Decl., ¶7.) SCG’s counsel contacted SCG’s principal about the missing responses and received no response. (Id., ¶8.) After receiving no response to phone calls and emails, SCG’s counsel requested an extension from Plaintiff to November 3, 2023. (Id., ¶¶9-10.) SCG’s counsel successfully contacted SCG on October 9, 2023. (Id., ¶11.) However, SCG failed to provide the information its counsel requested. (Id., ¶¶11-12.) On November 6, 2023, SCG’s counsel contacted SCG again and represented he would withdraw as counsel due to lack of communication. (Id., ¶14.) SCG and its counsel elected to attempt to continue the attorney-client relationship. (Id.) However, SCG again failed to provide the information necessary to complete the discovery responses. (Id., ¶¶15-16.) On March 7, 2024, SCG’s counsel filed a motion to withdraw as counsel.  (Id., ¶18.) SCG’s counsel attempted unsuccessfully to contact SCG throughout April and May 2024. (Id., ¶¶22-26.) SCG’s counsel then hired a private investigator to contact SCG to obtain verifications so that counsel could serve supplemental responses on June 18, 2024. (Id., ¶29.)
Because SCG served verified responses before the hearing on this matter, Plaintiff’s motions to compel SCG’s responses are denied. Although Plaintiff argues that the responses are not code compliant, such issues will have to be pursued through  motions to compel further.
SCG’s repeated failures to serve timely discovery responses constitute discovery abuse. The Court will award a reduced amount of $2,180 in sanctions for five hours of attorney time at a rate of $400 per hour and filing fees for three motions against SCG.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

DISCUSSION 
Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 2030.300, the Court may order a responding party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) the answer to a particular interrogatory is incomplete or evasive, (2) the exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is improper or inadequate, or (3) that an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. Such a motion “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040,” which “shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., sections 2016.040, 2030.300(b)(1).) 
Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.220 provides:
“(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.
(b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.
(c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”
Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subdivision (d) authorizes a mandatory issuance of sanctions against “any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subd. (d).) 
Here, Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s further responses to SROG numbers 14, 20, 23, 25, 26, 39, 41, and 43.

Legendary will not be prejudiced if this motion is granted. The remaining hearings in this matter include a final status conference set for January 27, 2025. Trial is set for February 10, 2025. There is sufficient time for Legendary to secure other counsel.
