DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 23CMCP00201    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCP00201 In the Matter of:  Edith Gabriela Gallegos Nuñez and Jose De Jesus Gallegos Nuñez, by and through their mother, Edith Luna

 Tuesday, January 16, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING THE HEARING ON PETITION FOR CHANGE OF NAME

 

      Both Petitioners, who are 14 years old, request an order changing their last name from Gallegos Nuñez to Luna. Both birth parents must consent to and sign the petitions. If only one parent consents to the name change, Petitioners are required to serve the other parent with the Order to Show Cause at least 30 days before the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1277(a)(4).) The OSC shall be personally served on the other parent if that parent resides in California. (Id.) Service outside California may be accomplished by mail, return receipt requested. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.40.) Petitioner shall file the proof of service with the court prior to the hearing date.

      Petitioners are required to make diligent efforts to locate the non-consenting parent. If that person cannot be located, Petitioners shall file a declaration stating what attempts were made to locate the other parent and why service cannot be reasonably accomplished. The declaration shall be filed no less than 10 court days before the continued hearing date. Additionally, Petitioners must comply with the requirements for publication of the Order to Show Cause. Code Civ. Proc., § 1277.

      Accordingly, the court continues the hearing to March 5, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. in Department A of the Compton Courthouse. Petitioner is ordered to comply with publication requirements prior to the date of the hearing.

 


Case Number: 23CMCV00700    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCV00700 Evelin Quintero v. Wily Oris Barrios, et al.

Tuesday, January 16, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART AND DENYING IN PART AMENDED MOTION BY DEFENDANT, WILY OSIRIS BARIOS, TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND  REQUEST FOR IMPOSITION OF MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART AND DENYING IN PART TWO AMENDED MOTIONS BY DEFENDANT, W.B.& E. PROPERTIES, TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM AND SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR IMPOSITION OF MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

I.        BACKGROUND

      The first amended complaint filed on October 24, 2023, alleges that Plaintiff suffered injury as the result of the uninhabitable conditions of leased premises owned by Defendants. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant breached a Lease-to-Buy Agreement granting Plaintiff exclusive rights to the property with an option to purchase for one year.  

II.      ARGUMENTS

      Defendant, Wily Osiris Barios (“Barios”) served Plaintiff with special interrogatories on September 6, 2023. Defendant, W.B.& E. Properties, (“WBE”) served Plaintiff with form and special interrogatories on September 13, 2023. Both Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s responses were evasive, incomplete, and contained boilerplate objections. The parties have not been able to resolve the issues despite numerous attempts to meet and confer informally and with the Court.

      In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the parties met with the Court for an informal discovery conference on November 17, 2023. Defendants filed these motions without further attempt to meet with Plaintiff, who provided amended responses on November 28, 2023. The “amended” motions do not mention Plaintiff’s amended responses, and Defendants refused to provide a copy of the “amended” motions. Defendants did not meet and confer with respect to Plaintiff’s amended responses.

      In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff refused to extend the deadline for Defendants to file motions to compel further responses, then sent amended responses on November 28, 2023, after Defendant filed these motions and after the IDC with the Court. Plaintiff did not inform Defendants that Plaintiff intended to serve amended responses.

 

III.    DISCUSSION

A party can move to compel a further response to interrogatories where the party deems an answer to be evasive or incomplete or an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a).) The parties are required to meet and confer prior to making the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(b).) A notice of motion to compel further responses must be given “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories. Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(c).

Plaintiff served responses to Barrios’ and WBE’s written discovery on October 5, 2023. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 2. The 45th day (increased by five calendar days for service by mail) to file motions to compel further was November 24, 2023. (Decl. of Lopez, Ex. 1.) The parties met and conferred informally between October 17 and October 30, 2023. Defense counsel, Steven Lopez, requested an extension to file a motions to compel by December 4, 2023. (Lopez Decl., Ex. 7.) Plaintiff refused to do so citing defense counsel’s failure to agree to meet and confer, for making disparaging remarks, and defense counsel’s failure to articulate the purported deficiencies. (Lopez Decl., Ex. 8.)

Plaintiff again refused to extend the 45-day deadline because Defendants refused to commit to an IDC, although Mr. Lopez offered to reserve an IDC in his email of October 24, 2023, wherein Plaintiff’s counsel purported to be unaware of what was at issue. (Lopez Declaration, Ex. 6, Ex. 10.) Mr. Lopez again requested an IDC by email dated October 31, 2023. (Lopez Decl., Ex. 9.)

Mr. Lopez set an IDC for November 17, 2023, in which Plaintiff participated. However, while Plaintiff “represented” that she intended to provide amended responses, there is no documentary evidence of that fact although there are 40-50 other emails as Plaintiff’s counsel admits that were exchanged in efforts to meet and confer. (Spegler Decl., ¶ 8.) Nor is there evidence that Plaintiff agreed to extend the motion deadline given the parties’ agreement to participate in an IDC seven days before the deadline.

Defendants were forced to file the motions to compel by November 24, 2023, given Plaintiff’s refusal to extend the 45-day deadline, which was unreasonable given the parties’ extensive attempts to resolve their issues. [1] Moreover, if Plaintiff had indicated she intended to supplement responses, this would have avoided the need for Defendants to file these motions since the 45-deadline would have re-started from the day the supplemental responses were served.

The consequence of these failures to meet and confer in good faith with respect to motion deadlines or to communicate as to whether or not Plaintiff would voluntarily supplement without motion was to cause undue burden and costs for Defendants and the Court; additionally, the motions do not address the supplemental responses since Defendants were unaware they would be forthcoming.  

Contrary to Plaintiff’s oppositions, the motions are not made moot by her service of supplemental responses which she served nine days after the IDC. (Opp. 4:18-19.) Without allowing an extension of the 45-day motion deadline which expired one week after the IDC, or informing that she would supplement, Defendants were forced to engage in needless motion practice.

      Abuse of the discovery process include employing a discovery method that causes unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense; making unmeritorious objections or evasive responses to discovery; opposing a motion to compel without substantial justification; and failing to meet and confer in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010. Plaintiff has not shown substantial justification for any of the conduct previously identified.   

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

 

      An order compelling Plaintiff’s further response is no longer necessary since Plaintiff opted to voluntarily amend the responses without informing anyone. However, Plaintiff’s abuse of the discovery process warrants imposition of monetary sanctions. The Court finds that $375 per hour, three hours to draft each of three motions, and one hour to prepare a reply brief and appear at the hearing, for a total of 10 hours are reasonable. Defendants incurred $60 in filing fees per motion. Accordingly, the Court imposes sanctions of $3,930, against Plaintiff, Evelin Quintero, and her counsel, Robert Spelger, jointly and severally, payable to Defendants within 10 days.  


[1] Defendants’ attempt to reserve motion dates for November were rejected because dates were not available for one year. (Reply Decl. of Sinai Franco.) Regardless, the 45-day deadline is not at issue given Plaintiff’s amended responses to discovery. The 45-day clock begins anew from the date of service of the amended responses.
