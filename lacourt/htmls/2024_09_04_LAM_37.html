DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV37953    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Wednesday, September 4, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV37953

CASE NAME:                        Jacob Shaw v. Honda Performance Development, Inc., et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Jacob Shaw

OPPOSING PARTY:             Defendant Honda Performance Development, Inc.

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion to Strike Fifth Amended Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        01  August 2024

SUR-REPLY                           27 August 2024

 

REPLY:                                  29 August 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Plaintiff’s request for further responses to RPD Nos. 54, 55, and 56 is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On December 5, 2022, Jacob Shaw (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Honda Performance Development, Inc.; Experis US, Inc.; Experis US, LLC; Manpower Group US, Inc. (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 50.  

 

On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint. On July 7, 2023, the parties stipulated to allow Plaintiff to file the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).  On October 4, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”).

 

The TAC alleges fifteen causes of action: (1) disability discrimination, (2) failure to engage in the interactive process, (3) failure to accommodate disability, (4) retaliation in violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2), (5) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (6) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (7) violation of Labor Code section 233 and 234, (8) wrongful termination in violation of public policy; (9) failure to pay overtime wages; (10) failure to provide meal periods, (11) failure to authorize and permit rest periods, (12) failure to pay all wages due upon separation, (13) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements, (14) failure to maintain required records, and (15) unfair and unlawful business practices.  

On November 15, 2023, the Court sustained Honda’s demurrer to the first to seventh and ninth through fifteenth cause of action was sustained with leave to amend.

 

On December 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Fourth Amended Complaint (4AC) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC. On June 18, 2024, the court sustained Honda’s demurrer and granted Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

Plaintiff has now filed the the operative Fifth Amended Complaint (“5AC”) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC and 4AC.

 

Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses to the request for production of documents (“RPD”) Nos. 54, 55, and 5. Defendant Honda opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS 

 

Under CCP § 2031.310(a), parties may move for a further response to request for production where an answer to the requests was evasive or incomplete or where an objection is without merits or too general.¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2031.310(c).)¿ The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2031.310(b).)¿ 

¿¿ 

Finally, CRC, rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (CRC, rule 3.1345(a)(3)).¿ 

 

motion to compel further[1]

 

I.         Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks: (1) an order compelling Defendant Honda to produce all documents responsive to Demand for Production of Documents (“RPD”), Set Three, Nos. 54, 55, and 56 served by Plaintiff on Honda on April 18, 2024; (2) an order compelling Honda to provide further verified written responses that comply with the Code of Civil Procedure; and (3) an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel in the amount of $6,000.00.

 

 

 

RPD No. 54 seeks:

 

  • “Any and all DOCUMENTS evidencing the reasons for the decision to offer Gwendolyn Hashimoto a direct hire position.”

 

RPD No. 55 seeks:

 

·       “Any and all DOCUMENTS evidencing the available headcount for positions at Honda at or around the time that Gwendolyn Hashimoto was offered a direct hire position.”

 

RPD No.  56 seeks:

 

  • “Any and all DOCUMENTS evidencing the selection process for the engineer position ultimately filled by Gwendolyn Hashimoto, including any evidence of candidates considered.”

 

Plaintiff asserts the documents requested are to show evidence of pretext related to Honda’s stated reasons for its treatment of Plaintiff and refusal to directly hire Plaintiff as an employee.

 

Plaintiff alleges that immediately after being informed of Plaintiff’s inability to travel due to his health, Honda’s supervisors Masatsugu Endo and Pierre Descamps became angry and decided to terminate Plaintiff’s employment by not renewing his contract. (5AC, ¶ 35.) Instead, Gwen Hashimoto was selected as a direct hire despite having less tenure and experience than Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 40.) Plaintiff believes it was because Hashimoto was outside of Plaintiff’s protected class. (Id.) Plaintiff believes he should not have been terminated and instead been a direct hire given his hard work, excellent performance, and experience. (Id., ¶ 48.)

 

Plaintiff asserts that RPD Nos. 54, 55, and 56 are aimed at obtaining circumstantial evidence of discrimination by showing that despite his qualifications, Honda’s failure to directly hire Plaintiff constituted an adverse action due to discrimination based on Plaintiff’s actual or perceived disability. The documents requested seek to understand whether Honda’s proffered reasons for not hiring Plaintiff, and instead hiring Hashimoto for the same engineer position that Plaintiff was qualified for, have merit. This includes evidence about whether Honda had the available headcount, including for direct hire employees, and if such evidence would tend to disprove Honda’s reason that Plaintiff was not selected due to budget reasons rather than because of discrimination. Moreover, any privacy concerns are assuaged by the fact that the employee records would be produced pursuant to the Parties’ protective order.

 

Defendant Honda asserts that employment records are private, and Plaintiff is not entitled to the requested information. A party has a right of privacy in his or her employment records. (See Alch v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1426.) “But the privacy right is not absolute and at times must yield to other important interests.” (Grafilo v. Soorani (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 497, 507.) A party seeking discovery of private information need not always establish a compelling interest or compelling need without regard to other considerations articulated in Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, including the strength of the privacy interest itself, the seriousness of the invasion, and the availability of alternatives and protective measures. (See Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557 (Williams).)  “Courts must instead place the burden on the party asserting a privacy interest to establish its extent and the seriousness of the prospective invasion, and against that showing must weigh the countervailing interests the opposing party identifies, as Hill requires.” (Id.).

 

Defendant Honda cannot use the fact that employee records are private as an absolute shield to deny Plaintiff’s requested discovery. While the court agrees that employee records are private, the burden is on Defendant Honda to show that discovery as to those records is extensive and serious and not outweighed by Plaintiff’s countervailing interests in that information. “The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. A court must then balance these competing considerations.”  (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.)

 

The fact that Plaintiff does not plead a disparate treatment claim does not mean that the information sought is not relevant to proving discriminatory animus because the information goes directly to Honda’s employment practices when it comes to decisions to directly hire employees. “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action ... if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP., § 2017.010; see Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301 [“discovery is not limited to admissible evidence”].)

 

Defendant Honda asserts that Plaintiff’s request must be denied because there are alternative means to obtain the information requested that are less burdensome to obtain the information and protect employee records from unnecessary disclosure. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff could submit an interrogatory or deposition question requesting specific information about Honda’s reasons for hiring Hashimoto and Honda’s section process for hiring engineers. Defendant asserts that interrogatories or deposition questions are less burdensome than producing “any and all documents” related to employees and third party candidates. Defendants further assert that to protect privacy interests, private information would need to be redacted, making the request more burdensome. Plaintiff’s opposition fails to explain why interrogatories and deposition questions are insufficient to provide the Plaintiff with the information he seeks or why these means of discovery are more intrusive than document production.

 

The court agrees that there appears to be less burdensome means and alternatives to obtain the requested information. Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion for further RPD and request for sanctions is denied.

 

Lastly, Plaintiff’s request to strike the sur-reply is granted. “Points raised for the first time in a reply brief will not be considered.” (Malmstrom v. Kaiser Aluminum & Chemical Corp. (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 299, 320.)

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff’s request for further responses to RPD Nos. 54, 55, and 56 is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV37953

CASE NAME:                        Jacob Shaw v. Honda Performance Development, Inc., et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Honda Performance Development, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Jacob Shaw

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion to Strike Fifth Amended Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        21 August 2024

REPLY:                                  27 August 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         The Court denies Defendant Honda’s motion to strike.  Defendant to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On December 5, 2022, Jacob Shaw (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Honda Performance Development, Inc. (“Honda”); Experis US, Inc.; Experis US, LLC; Manpower Group US, Inc. (“Manpower”) (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 50.  

 

On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint.  On July 7, 2023, the parties stipulated to allow Plaintiff to file the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).  On October 4, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”). The TAC alleges fifteen causes of action: (1) disability discrimination, (2) failure to engage in the interactive process, (3) failure to accommodate disability, (4) retaliation in violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2), (5) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (6) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (7) violation of Labor Code section 233 and 234, (8) wrongful termination in violation of public policy; (9) failure to pay overtime wages; (10) failure to provide meal periods, (11) failure to authorize and permit rest periods, (12) failure to pay all wages due upon separation, (13) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements, (14) failure to maintain required records, and (15) unfair and unlawful business practices.  

 

On November 15, 2023, the Court sustained Honda’s demurrer with motion to strike to the first to seventh and ninth through fifteenth cause of action with leave to amend. The Court also sustained Manpower’s demurrer to the eighth cause of action with leave to amend.  On December 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Fourth Amended Complaint (4AC) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC. On June 18, 2024, the court sustained Defendant Honda’s demurrer and granted Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

Plaintiff has now filed the the operative Fifth Amended Complaint (“5AC”) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC and 4AC.

 

Defendant Honda moves to strike the 5AC’s request for punitive damages. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS 

 

A.        Motion to Strike 

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

  

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿¿¿ 

 

motion to strike fifth amended complaint[1]

 

I.         Discussion

 

Defendant Honda seeks to Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages  in the 5AC as follows:

 

1)     Page 40, Prayer for Relief, Paragraph 5;

2)     Pages 13-14, 16, 18-19, 21, 23-24, 25-26, 27-28, 30-31, and 32-33 at Paragraphs ¶¶ 66, 79, 93, 104, 117, 127, 136, 152, and 163;

3)     Pages 14, 16-17, 19, 21-22, 24, 26, and 28 at Paragraphs ¶¶ 67, 80, 94, 105, 118, 128, and 137; and

4)     Pages 33-34, Paragraph ¶ 165.

 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civ. Code § 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.)¿ The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Ibid.)¿¿“Malice” is defined in Civ. Code § 3294 (c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.”¿ (Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 847, 891.)¿ 

 

“In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) Taking Plaintiff’s allegations as true, the court finds that the 5AC sufficiently alleges facts to support a claim for punitive damages. The 5AC alleges that Defendants Masatsugu Endo (“Endo”) and Pierre Descamps (“Descamps”) were Plaintiff’s supervisors and Defendant Honda delegated to them the authority to engage in the interactive process, grant accommodations, and make decisions about terminating employees such as ending their contracts or declining to hire employees into “direct-hire” positions. (5AC, ¶ 28.)

 

Since early December 2021, Endo and Descamps were aware that Plaintiff suffered from stomach ulcers and had requested a flexible start time as an accommodation. (5AC, ¶¶ 30, 32.) On or about February 3, 2022, Plaintiff’s symptoms became severe, and Plaintiff advised Defendants Endo, Descamps, and Honda that he could not travel for work and took sick leave. (Id.,  ¶ 33.) “Plaintiff requested an accommodation of not traveling and the opportunity to support offsite work from Defendant Honda’s worksite or from home.” (Id., ¶ 34.)

 

Rather than engage in the interactive process to see if the request was reasonable or if there were alternatives, Endo and Descamps immediately decided not to renew Plaintiff’s contract. (5AC, ¶¶ 34, 35.) On or about April 27, 2022, Linda Palafox, Honda’s Human Resources contact, emailed Joanne Aglubat, Honda’s Human Resources Manager, that Descamps and Endo were upset by Plaintiff’s requested accommodations. (Id., ¶¶ 26, 40, 41.) The 5AC alleges that despite knowing about Descamps and Masatsugu’s conduct, Honda did nothing and made no prompt corrective measures to protect Plaintiff from discrimination, engage in the interactive process, or accommodate Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 41) On May 4, 2022, Aglubat reported that Descamps and Masatsugu called Plaintiff “lazy” and had decided not to renew Plaintiff’s contract. (Id., ¶ 42.)

 

Defendant Endo told Plaintiff his contract was not renewed due to budget reasons, but Plaintiff saw that Defendant Honda posted advertisements for Plaintiff’s position. (5AC, ¶ 50.) For this reason, Plaintiff believes his contract was not renewed due to illegal reasons, including, his disabilities and/or perceived disability and/or Plaintiff's requested accommodations and/or because he took sick leave. (Id., ¶ 49.)

 

Based on the allegations above, the court finds that malice or oppression may be found in the fact that Descamps and Endo acted with willful and conscious disregard for Plaintiff’s employment rights by opting to not renew Plaintiff’s contract rather than attempt to engage in the interactive process or offer Plaintiff a reasonable accommodation. (5AC, ¶¶ 34, 35.) Moreover, the 5AC alleges that Defendant Honda, through its human resources, knew about Endo and Descamps conduct and derogatory comments about Plaintiff being “lazy” due to his disabilities and requested accommodations, and instead of correcting such conduct, ratified it by agreeing to not renew Plaintiff’s contract. (5AC, ¶ 42.)

 

Moreover, while the court agrees that Plaintiff cannot recover both punitive damages and statutory penalties, Plaintiff may recover punitive damages for FEHA violations. (See De Anza Santa Cruz Mobile Estates Homeowners Assn. v. De Anza Santa Cruz Mobile Estates (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 890, 912 [A “

plaintiff cannot recover both punitive damages and statutory penalties, as this would constitute a prohibited double penalty for the same act.”].) Here, the tenth to fifteenth causes of action based on Labor Code violations and the UCL, do not seek punitive damages and there is no threat of double recovery.

 

Furthermore, in Commodore Home Systems, Inc. v. Superior Court (1982) 32 Cal.3d 211, the California Supreme Court held that “in a civil action under the FEHA, all relief generally available in noncontractual actions, including punitive damages, may be obtained.” (Id., p. 221.) As Plaintiff brings FEHA-related causes of action, and Defendant Honda has failed to allege that those causes of action are insufficiently pled, Plaintiff may pursue a claim for punitive damages as to those claims.

Therefore, Defendant Honda’s motion to strike is denied.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Honda’s motion to strike is denied. Defendant to give notice.
