[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for order deeming truth of matters admitted in requests for admission

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Dulce Garcia

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC

Motion for Order Deeming Truth of Matters Admitted in Requests for Admission

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Dulce Garcia (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) deeming the truth of the matters set forth in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, served on defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“Defendant”), admitted, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $2,904.

If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the court shall, upon motion by the propounding party, order that the matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, unless the court finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response that is in substantial compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.220.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (b), (c).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As a threshold matter, the court notes that the parties dispute whether the subject discovery was served on Defendant at the correct email address.  The court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that they served the subject Requests for Admission at the correct electronic service address and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.

Plaintiff has submitted evidence establishing that they served counsel for Defendant with their Requests for Admission, Set One, by electronic service at the email addresses associated with individual attorneys Brian Hom, C. Brian Wagner, and Vanessa Dao.  (Serrano Decl., Ex. 1, Requests for Admission, pp. 7-8 [proof of service].)  Defendant has opposed this motion, asserting that service on these email addresses was improper.  (Opp., p. 4:3-4.)

In reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendant consented to electronic service at the email addresses for attorneys Brian Hom, C. Brian Wagner, and Vanessa Dao pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.251 since Defendant’s answer manifested consent to receive service at those email addresses.  Specifically, Plaintiff contends that service was proper because (1) rule 2.251 provides that a party may indicate that the party agrees to accept electronic service by, inter alia, manifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court or the court’s electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party’s electronic service address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service, and (2) Defendant, in filing its answer electronically, affirmatively consented to electronic service at the email addresses listed on its answer.  The court disagrees.

 First, the court acknowledges, as Plaintiff points out, that Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (b)(2) states that “[a] person represented by counsel, who has appeared in an action or proceeding, shall accept electronic service of a notice or document that may be served by mail, express mail, overnight delivery, or facsimile transaction.”  However, subdivision (b) of that statute states that it “applies to mandatory electronic service.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (b)(1) [emphasis added].)  Plaintiff has not presented evidence or authority establishing that service of their Requests for Admission was required to be served by electronic service within the meaning of section 1010.6, subdivision (b).  (Ibid.)  Further, although the court notes that Plaintiff states that section 1010.6 provides that documents filed by represented parties in all limited, unlimited, and complex civil actions “must be filed electronically and [must] allow for service electronically,” Plaintiff did not cite the specific provision of that statute containing that language, and the court has not located that provision of section 1010.6.  (Reply, p. 4:25-26 [emphasis omitted].)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant manifested affirmative consent to service at the email addresses listed on Defendant’s answer pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.251, subdivision (b)(1)(B).  (Reply, p. 5:17-28.)

“A party or other person indicates that the party or other person agrees to accept electronic service by: [¶¶] (B) Manifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court or the court’s electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party’s electronic service address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service.  A party or other person may manifest affirmative consent by serving notice of consent to all parties and other persons and either: [¶] (i) Agreeing to the terms of service with an electronic filing provider, which clearly states that agreement constitutes consent to receive electronic service; or [¶] (ii) Filing Consent to Electronic Service and Notice of Electronic Service Address (form EFS-005-CV).”  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.251, subd. (b)(1)(B).)

The court acknowledges that Defendant electronically filed its answer on December 19, 2023, on which Defendant listed the email addresses of its counsel.  (Answer filed Dec. 19, 2023, p. 1:1-6.)  However, Plaintiff has not cited authority establishing that Defendant, by electronically filing its answer, manifested affirmative consent to receive electronic service at those email addresses.  As set forth above, a party may manifest affirmative consent “by serving notice of consent to all parties and other persons and either” taking one of two actions.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.551, subd. (b)(1)(B) [emphasis added].)  Plaintiff did not submit evidence showing that Defendant (1) “serv[ed] [a] notice of consent” on Plaintiff, and (2) either (i) agreed to the terms of service with an electronic filing service provider clearly stating that agreement thereto constitutes consent to receive electronic service (rule 2.251, subdivision (b)(1)(B)(i)), or (ii) filed a Consent to Electronic Service and Notice of Electronic Service Address on form EFS-005-CV (rule 2.251, subdivision (b)(1)(B)(ii)).  Moreover, there does not appear to be such a notice of consent filed with the court.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that they properly served Defendant with the subject Requests for Admission on Defendant at the correct email address for electronic service.  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Dulce Garcia’s motion for order deeming truth of matters admitted in requests for admission.

            The court orders defendant Mercedes-Benza USA, LLC to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s demurrer to first amended complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Michael Waldinger

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff Justin Cato

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this demurrer.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court grants defendant Michael Waldinger’s requests for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Justin Cato (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative First Amended Complaint in this action on January 16, 2024, against, inter alia, defendant Michael Waldinger, Deputy Alternate Public Defender (“Defendant”). 

Defendant now moves the court for an order sustaining his demurrer to each cause of action alleged against him in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action because they do not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because Plaintiff has not alleged facts establishing the element of actual innocence.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Genis v. Schainbaum (2021) 66 Cal.App.5th 1007, 1015 [“In any action for legal malpractice against criminal defense counsel, the plaintiff, as the former criminal defendant, must necessarily allege and prove actual innocence of the criminal charges”].)

First, as a threshold matter, the court finds that the first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action are all based on the alleged legal malpractice of Defendant in representing Plaintiff in the underlying criminal matter, such that Plaintiff must plead the element of actual innocence.  (Genis, supra, 66 Cal.App.5th at p. 1015 [“The actual innocence requirement applies to any legal malpractice in the course of defending a client accused of a crime [citation] or, put another way, arising out of a criminal proceeding”] [internal quotations and citation omitted].)

The first cause of action for legal malpractice alleges that Defendant breached his duties to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as members of the legal profession by, inter alia, failing to share discovery with Plaintiff, failing to interview and question percipient witnesses, failing to make important evidentiary objections during witness testimony, and failing to object to prosecutorial misconduct.  (FAC ¶¶ 119, 56-57, 59.)  The third cause of action for restitution is based on Plaintiff’s allegations that (1) Defendant’s legal work in the underlying criminal matter was deficient, thereby causing Plaintiff to retain private successor counsel to bring a motion for new trial (FAC ¶ 149), such that (2) Defendant has benefited from Plaintiff’s obtaining of the post-trial services of private counsel (FAC ¶ 150) and therefore is liable to Plaintiff for restitution (FAC ¶ 155).  Thus, Plaintiff’s claim for restitution is, as pleaded, based on the alleged tort of legal malpractice and the damages that resulted from Defendant’s wrongful acts or omissions.  

Similarly, the fourth cause of action for prima facie tort seeks damages based on the alleged malpractice committed by Defendant in failing to obtain Plaintiff’s written waiver of the existing conflict of interest (FAC ¶¶ 159, 162, 99-100).  The fifth cause of action for common counts – work and labor done is also based on the allegations that (1) Defendant’s malpractice in the underlying criminal matter required Plaintiff to obtain private counsel to preserve his rights (FAC ¶ 165), such that (2) Defendant has become indebted to Plaintiff for the amounts paid by him to private counsel (FAC ¶¶ 165-166).

            Thus, the court finds that each cause of action alleged against Defendant is based on his alleged legal malpractice in the underlying criminal matter.

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has not alleged facts establishing actual innocence.

“‘When a former criminal defendant sues his or her attorney for legal malpractice resulting in conviction, the former defendant’s actual innocence of the underlying criminal charges is a necessary element of the cause of action.  [Citation.]  Moreover, the “plaintiff must obtain postconviction relief in the form of a final disposition of the underlying criminal case—for example, by acquittal after retrial, reversal on appeal with directions to dismiss the charges, reversal followed by the People’s refusal to continue the prosecution, or a grant of habeas corpus relief—as a prerequisite to proving actual innocence in a malpractice action against former criminal defense counsel.”  [Citation.]’”  (Jones v. Whisenand (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 543, 550-551 [internal citations omitted]; Coscia v. McKenna & Cuneo (2001) 25 Cal.4th 1194, 1201 [“an individual convicted of a criminal offense must obtain reversal of his or her conviction, or other exoneration by postconviction relief, in order to establish actual innocence in a criminal malpractice action”].)

The court acknowledges that Plaintiff has alleged that he “is innocent of all charged offenses” and that he “did not commit any of the charged offenses” (FAC ¶ 38).  But (1) Plaintiff has also alleged that the prosecution offered him a plea bargain for a plea to manslaughter, rather than the homicide charges for which he was originally convicted (FAC ¶¶ 104-105), and (2) the court has taken judicial notice of the docket in the underlying criminal action, which establishes that (i) Plaintiff was found guilty in connection with charges 3 and 5 (i.e., violations of Penal Code sections 246 and 192, subdivision (a)) after pleading nolo contendere to those charges,       (ii) Plaintiff admitted the gun allegation pursuant to Penal Code section 12022.5(A), and (iii) on October 24, 2023, Plaintiff was sentenced to 21 years in prison.  (RJN Ex. 1, Docket in People v. Cato (Case No. BA432098) pp. 65, 67.)

Thus, the court finds, based on the allegations of the First Amended Complaint and the court docket in the underlying criminal matter, that Plaintiff (1) was convicted in the underlying matter, (2) has not obtained reversal of his conviction or other exoneration by postconviction relief, and therefore (3) has not alleged facts sufficient to plead the element of actual innocence.  (Coscia, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 1201; Jones, supra, 8 Cal.App.5th at p. 551; Wilkinson v. Zelen (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 37, 46 [finding that the plaintiff could not allege factual innocence because “she entered no contest pleas to two misdemeanors in order to compromise the charges which resulted in her conviction by jury, a result inconsistent with factual innocence in that ‘defendant has in fact committed a crime’”].)

            Third, the court notes that Plaintiff has argued, in his opposition, that “there is a disputed factual issue regarding the probative value of a plea bargain by a defendant (Mr. Cato) who was represented by an attorney (Mr. Waldinger) with whom he had an unwaived actual conflict of interest.”  (Opp., p. 7:21-24; FAC ¶ 106 [alleging that the case law presupposes that criminal defendants are represented by successor counsel in retrial proceedings].)  However, Plaintiff has not cited authority establishing that he is relieved of the requirement to plead facts establishing the element of actual innocence under such circumstances.

            Fourth, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant is equitably estopped from asserting Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the actual innocence requirement.  Plaintiff has neither (1) cited authority establishing that the doctrine of equitable estoppel may be applied to relieve him of pleading the element of actual innocence, nor (2) alleged facts satisfying the elements of equitable estoppel, including because (i) for equitable estoppel to apply, the asserting party “must be ignorant of the true state of facts[,]” but (ii) Plaintiff has alleged that he was aware of the alleged conflict of interest, and lacked confidence in Defendant because of that conflict (FAC ¶ 102), but chose not to protest because he felt it would be futile (FAC ¶ 103).  (Krolikowski v. San Diego City Employees’ Retirement System (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 537, 564-565 [elements of equitable estoppel]; FAC ¶ 112 [alleging that Defendant is equitably estopped from asserting the absence of acquittal or dismissal of criminal charges as a result of Defendant’s wrongful acts and omissions, including the “purposeful failure to address the actual conflict of interests”].)

            Thus, for the reasons set forth above, the court finds that Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to plead the element of actual innocence in support of each cause of action alleged against Defendant.

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  In his opposition, Plaintiff did not set forth facts that he may allege to establish the element of actual innocence.  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to articulate how he can amend his pleading to render it sufficient and therefore sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Complaint without leave to amend.  (Genis, supra, 66 Cal.App.5th at p. 1015 [“When it is clear the plaintiff cannot in good faith plead factual innocence or exoneration, leave to amend the complaint should not be granted”].)

ORDER

            The court sustains defendant Michael Waldinger’s demurrer to plaintiff Justin Cato’s First Amended Complaint without leave to amend.

The court orders defendant Michael Waldinger to prepare, serve, and lodge a proposed judgment of dismissal within 10 days of the date of this order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 581, subd. (f)(1).)

The court vacates (1) the trial set for January 22, 2025, and (2) the Final Status Conference set for January 10, 2025.

The court sets an Order to Show Cause re entry of judgment of dismissal (after sustaining demurrers without leave to amend) for hearing on September 4, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. 

 

   

            The court orders defendant Michael Waldinger to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories – general

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Emilio Reyes

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories – General

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Andrew Salas (“Defendant”) to serve further responses, without objections, to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories – General, Set One, number 2.5, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $109.34.

The court finds that the discovery motion cutoff date has passed and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)

Except as otherwise provided, “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)  “Thus, if a party properly notices a discovery motion to be heard on or before the discovery motion cutoff date, that party has a right to have the motion heard.  By negative implication, a party who notices a discovery motion to be heard after the discovery motion cutoff date does not have a right to have the motion heard.”  (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1586 [emphasis in original].)

On March 6, 2023, the court issued a minute order setting this case for trial on May 15, 2024.  (Mar. 6, 2023 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Thereafter, on May 2, 2024, the court issued a minute order (1) continuing trial from May 15, 2024 to July 10, 2024, and (2) stating that “[a]ll discovery cut-off and discovery motion cut-off dates, and deadlines for the exchange of information concerning expert trial witnesses, shall be based on the date initially set for trial of this action (5/15/24) and shall not be reopened or based on the new trial date, except that Defendants may take the deposition of Plaintiff Emilio Reyes pursuant to the Court’s 5/1/24 order.”  (May 2, 2024 Minute Order, pp. 1-2.) 

Thus, the court finds that the discovery motion cutoff date in this action was April 30, 2024, i.e., the 15th day before the May 15, 2024 trial date.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); May 2, 2024 Minute Order, pp. 1-2.)  However, Plaintiff (1) noticed this motion for hearing on June 27, 2024 (i.e., following the discovery motion cutoff date), and (2) did not (i) request that the court extend the discovery motion cutoff date pursuant to section 2024.050, or (ii) show that the considerations set forth in that statute support extending the discovery motion cutoff date.  (Notice of Mot., pp. 1:26-2:2.)  The court therefore finds that, because Plaintiff noticed this motion to be heard after the discovery motion cutoff date passed, Plaintiff does not have a right to have this motion heard.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc., supra, 165 Cal.App.4th at p. 1586.)

            The court finds that the circumstances presented would make the imposition of sanctions unjust and therefore denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories.

            The court orders defendant Andrew Salas to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
