DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV26682    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            Defendant Valley Brake Supply moves for summary judgment of the claims of Plaintiffs Darren Hargrove and Kevin Hargrove that brakes sold by Defendant exposed Patricia Bacer Wilson to asbestos. 

            Plaintiffs’ Objection Nos. 1-5:  Overruled.

            Plaintiffs’ Objection No. 6:  The court did not rely on this exhibit.

Defendant’s Objections:  The court did not rely on the material objected to.

A defendant seeking summary judgment must “conclusively negate[] a necessary element of the plaintiff’s case, or . . . demonstrate[] that under no hypothesis is there a material issue of fact that requires the process of trial.”  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 334.)  To show that a plaintiff cannot establish an element of a cause of action, a defendant must make the initial showing “that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)  “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence – as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.”  (Id. at p. 855.)

Defendant attempts to conclusively negate a necessary element of Plaintiffs’ case, arguing Defendant had a different owner before October 14, 1992, during the period of exposure.  Defendant submits evidence that Plaintiffs’ parents went to Defendant’s store from 1977 to 1991.  (Undisputed Material Fact “UMF”) 11, 13.)  Frank Cho purchased Defendant in October 1992.  (UMF 14.)  Defendant argues that because it had a different owner during the pre-1992 period of exposure, the current owner is not liable.

Generally, “the purchaser does not assume the seller’s liabilities unless:  (1) there is an express or implied agreement of assumption, (2) the transaction amounts to a consolidation or merger of the two corporations, (3) the purchasing corporation is a mere continuation of the seller, or (4) the transfer of assets to the purchaser is for the fraudulent purpose of escaping liability for the seller’s debts.”  (Ray v. Alad (1977) 19 Cal.3d 22, 28.)  Defendant attached sales documents showing no agreement of assumption of the prior liabilities.  To the contrary, the agreement states that the seller will indemnify the buyer for all liability “due to matters connected with Seller’s ownership and operation of the business prior to close of escrow.”  (UMF 18.)

Plaintiffs argue that Defendant has not submitted facts showing the Ray v. Alad exceptions do not apply.  (Opposition at pp. 8-9.)  Plaintiff argues that Defendant under its current owner is a mere continuation of the business under its prior owner.  (Opposition at p. 9.)  It operates under the same name; the current owner purchased all of the goodwill, lease, fixtures, and equipment; the current owner kept on some of the same employees; the business operated at the same phone number and location for 18 years after the purchase; and the business performed at least some of the same services before and after the sale.  (Plaintiffs’ Disputed Material Facts (“DMF”) 17, 18, 21, 22, 23, 25, 26, 30.

In Ray v. Alad, the court recognized strict tort liability for defective products when a party acquires a manufacturing business and continues the output of its line of products “holding itself out to potential customers as the same enterprise,” thereby exploiting the prior business’ “established reputation as a going concern manufacturing a specific product line.”  (Ray, supra, 19 Cal.3d at p. 34.)  Plaintiffs showed facts suggesting Defendant held itself out to potential customers as the same enterprise thereby exploiting the prior business and reputation.  Thus, there are disputed issues of material fact regarding Defendant’s liability for pre-1992 exposure.

Defendant also contends Plaintiffs have no evidence that Patricia Bacer was exposed to asbestos from Defendant’s products, citing Plaintiffs’ interrogatory responses.  (Motion at pp. 4-5.)  In response to interrogatories asking for all facts, witnesses, and documents to support their claims, Plaintiffs responded that Bacer was exposed to asbestos from the 1960s to the 1990s when her first husband and sons worked on cars in her presence.  (Goldberg Decl., Ex. E at p. 2.)  Also Bacer launder their clothing that allegedly was coated with asbestos-containing debris.  (Id., at pp. 2-3.)  Bacer’s husband purchased brakes and clutches from Valley from Defendant.  (Id., at p. 3.)  

Assuming Defendant shifted the burden, Plaintiffs showed disputed facts.  Plaintiffs filed additional excerpts from Darren Hargrove’s deposition where he testified Bacer was present during automotive repair work, including work with brakes, from 1977 to 2001.  (Plaintiffs’ Index, Ex. A at pp. 25-26.)  She assisted her sons and husband by handing them tools.  (Ibid.)  She was present for at least 35 brake jobs.  (Id. at pp. 32-33.)  She was responsible for washing the clothing allegedly covered in asbestos dust.  (Id. at pp. 86, 93.)

The motion is DENIED.  The parties are to meet and confer about how the issue of successor liability is to be resolved.

The moving party is to give notice.


Case Number: 23STCV16061    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES AND DOCUMENTS

In the September 7, 2023 order on Defendants IMI Fabi’s, LLC, IMI Fabi (USA), Inc.’s, and IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC’s motions to quash for lack of personal jurisdiction, the court continued the hearing to allow jurisdictional discovery on “Defendants’ activities in and contacts with California and its sale of talc for those particular products [referring to Mary Kay, Revlon, Max Factor, and Johnson & Johnson products] during the time Plaintiff was using those products.”

On October 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents from IMI Fabi, LLC.  In the December 8, 2023 order on that motion, the court ruled:  “Plaintiff failed to file a separate statement.  If Plaintiff fails in that regard in the future, the court will deny the motion on that ground alone.  Also, because Plaintiff did not file a separate statement and did not address each request separately in its motion, the court cannot determine the relevance of many of the requests, as discussed below.”  In other words, the court gave Plaintiff one “freebie” for not complying with the California Rules of Court separate statement requirement.

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed another motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents and deposition questions from Defendants IMI Fabi, LLC, IMI Fabi (USA), Inc. and IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC.  Again, Plaintiff did not file a separate statement as required by California Rule of Court, rule 3.1345, subdivision (a).  In the December 8, 2023 order, the court warned Plaintiff that a separate statement is required and that the court would not consider a future motion to compel further discovery responses without a separate statement.  (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409 n. 14 [“the court rule requiring a separate statement on a motion to compel further responses . . . would permit the trial court to continue or deny a motion to compel when no separate statement is provided”]; Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893 [court “was well within its discretion to deny the motion to compel discovery” where moving party failed to file proper separate statement].) 

Because Plaintiff ignored Rule 3.1345 a second time, despite the court’s December 8, 2024 warning, the motion to compel further responses is DENIED.

The moving party is to give notice.
