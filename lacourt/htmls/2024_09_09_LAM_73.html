DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV34229    Hearing Date: September 9, 2024    Dept: 73

09/09/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

ANIMAL PROTECTION AND RESCUE LEAGUE, INC., et al. v. GINOR, et al. (20STCV34229)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Bryan Pease (Pease Law, APC); David Tenenbaum (Law Offices of G. David Tenenbaum); John Maher (Law Offices of John Maher)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Michael Tenenbaum (The Office of Michael Tenenbaum); Thomas Rickeman (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

 

(1)   DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STAY AND QUASH DEPOSITION NOTICES AND SUBPOENAS AND FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER ENFORCING DISCOVERY CUT-OFF (filed 02/13/2024)

(2)   PLAINTIFFS’ Motion to COMPEL (filed 03/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Motion to Stay and Quash Deposition Notices and Subpoenas and for Protective Order Enforcing Discovery Cut-Off is GRANTED and DENIED in part. Defendants’ request to stay and quash all third-party notices of deposition and deposition subpoenas served after the June 26, 2023, discovery cut-off is GRANTED. Defendants’ request for a Protective Order enforcing the discovery cut-off and request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 4, 2020, Plaintiffs Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“APRL”), and Dina Kourda (“Kourda”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint.  The operative pleading is the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) which alleges causes of action for (1) Consumer Legal Remedies Act, (2) unfair business practices, and (3) declaratory relief against Defendants Michael Ginor, Izzy Yanay, HVFG LLC, D’Artagnan, Inc., D’Artagnan, LLC, Ariane Daguin, Andy Wertheim, Bella Bella Gourmet Foods LLC, La Belle Farm, Inc., Herman Lee, French Laundry Partners, L.P., Thomas Keller, Michael’s Gourmet Pantry, Inc.,  MGP Specialty Foods, J. Michael Stamm, and Cathy Stamm, Tyler Charman, Brian Redzikowski, Matthew Spencer, Kettner House LP, SDCM Inc., and Petrossian, Inc. 

 

Plaintiffs filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras, i.e., fattened duck liver, which is obtained from force-fed duck and geese, and which violates Health & Safety Code section 25982.  Plaintiffs also seek to enjoin false and misleading statements made by Defendants regarding the foie gras.  

 

On June 28, 2024, the Court continued Plaintiff APRL’s Motion to Compel Discovery for failure to meet and confer.  

 

On September 6, 2024, APRL filed a notice of compliance with order to adequately meet and confer.

 

On February 13, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Stay and Quash Deposition Notices and Subpoenas and for Protective Order Enforcing Discovery Cut-Off, arguing:

·       Defendants ask that the Court: (1) stay and quash all third-party notices of deposition and deposition subpoenas served after the June 26, 2023, discovery cut-off; (2) issue a protective order enforcing the cut-off by directing that APRL not take any further discovery in this matter in violation of the Code of Civil Procedure, and directing that APRL immediately provide to Defendants copies of all deposition subpoenas and that APRL promptly notify all subpoena recipients of the Court’s order; and (3) award monetary sanctions to Defendants in an amount, to be determined in a separate motion, sufficient to compensate Defendants and deter APRL’s continuing violations.

·       Discovery in this case closed last summer, on June 26, 2023 — i.e., 30 days before this case was set for trial. Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 2024.020.

·       The Court’s order of January 9, 2024, setting trial for July 31, 2024, expressly states, “Absent stipulation of the parties and a further court order, no other dates or deadlines are continued or revived.”

·       On February 2, 2024, APRL served Defendants with both a Notice of Deposition of J.W. Strauss, LLC, D/B/A Pamplemousse Grille and a Notice of Deposition of La Bastide, Inc., D/B/A Bistro du Marche, each with a deposition date of February 13, 2024. (Declaration of Michael Tenenbaum (“Tenenbaum Decl.”), Exs. 1 and 2.)

 

In opposition, APRL argues:

·       The parties and the Court discussed the issue of the discovery cutoff as it relates to subpoenas at the March 7, 2024 hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to enforce a prior discovery order.

·       Given the Court’s position on the discovery cutoff, Plaintiff has stated it is not taking any action to enforce the subpoenas.

·       As there is no pending dispute regarding the subpoenas that were issued, the motion should be denied if Defendants do not take it off calendar.

 

In reply, Defendants argue:

·       Without a protective order from the Court, Defendants have no assurance that APRL will not continue to coerce Defendants’ customers into providing documents via threats of lawsuits.

·       The relief Defendants seek has been necessary, this motion should be granted, and the Court should award Defendants’ attorney’s fees as monetary sanctions against APRL for its discovery abuses in an amount to be determined by separate motion or submission.

 

APRL filed a response to Defendants’ reply, arguing:

·       Defendants’ reply contained new arguments and misstated evidence. Nevertheless, APRL asserts Defendants’ motion is moot since March 7, 2024 when the Court confirmed discovery was closed.

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff APRL filed the Motion to Compel, arguing:

·       APRL moves for an order compelling production of documents and further deposition of HVFG, LLC vice president Marcus Henley regarding those document, and for HVFG, LLC to be required to pay the cost of the continued deposition

·       Discovery is open as to this deposition by agreement of the parties

o   As stated in the February 13, 2024 Declaration of Michael Tenenbaum, paragraph 8: “the parties agreed that these party depositions — as well as depositions of identified representatives of Defendants — would proceed after the cut-off.” (Declaration of Attorney Bryan Pease (“Pease Decl.”), ¶6, Ex. 5.)

o   discovery is still open as to this particular deposition, which was noticed prior to the discovery cutoff and continued by agreement of the parties.

·       The motion is timely

·       Defendants did not serve valid objections to the document request

o   Defendants served an objection to the original deposition notice, but not to the second amended deposition notice.

·       There is good cause to require production of documents

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that:

·       APRL Failed to Meet-and-Confer with Defendants

·       APRL’s Motion is Time-Barred

o   APRL’s Motion was Filed more than 60 Days After the Completion of the Record of Mr. Henley’s Deposition

o   APRL’s Motion was Filed After the Discovery Motion Cutoff

o   APRL Fails to Show Good Cause for the Production of Documents

o   HVFG’s Objections Are Meritorious

o   APRL Should Be Ordered to Pay Sanctions

In reply, APRL maintains that the Court should grant the instant motion.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Defendants’ Motion to Stay and Quash Deposition Notices and Subpoenas and for Protective Order Enforcing Discovery Cut-Off

 

Defendants request that the Court: (1) stay and quash all third-party notices of deposition and deposition subpoenas served after the June 26, 2023, discovery cut-off; (2) issue a protective order enforcing the cut-off by directing that APRL not take any further discovery in this matter in violation of the Code of Civil Procedure, and directing that APRL immediately provide to Defendants copies of all deposition subpoenas and that APRL promptly notify all subpoena recipients of the Court’s order; and (3) award monetary sanctions to Defendants in an amount, to be determined in a separate motion, sufficient to compensate Defendants and deter APRL’s continuing violations.

 

Discovery in this matter closed on June 26, 2023, 30 days before this case was set for trial. (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 2024.020.) By order dated January 9, 2024, the Court ruled: “Absent stipulation of the parties and a further court order, no other dates or deadlines are continued or revived.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020, subdivision (a), “any party shall be entitled . . . to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.”

 

Defendants bring this instant motion to stay and quash the third-party Notice of Deposition of J.W. Strauss, LLC, D/B/A Pamplemousse Grille, the third-party Notice of Deposition of La Bastide, Inc., D/B/A Bistro du Marche, and all other deposition subpoenas, including but not limited to deposition subpoenas for the production of business records, that Plaintiff Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“APRL”), has served on other third parties since June 26, 2023.

 

APRL asserts that Defendants’ motion is moot. APRL states that the parties discussed the issue of the discovery cutoff at the March 7, 2024 hearing. In the Court’s March 7, 2024 order, the Court reiterated that the discovery deadline has passed. Thus, APRL contends it has not taken any action to enforce the subpoenas.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Defendants’ request to stay and quash all third-party notices of deposition and deposition subpoenas served after the June 26, 2023, discovery cut-off. Since APRL states it is not seeking to enforce the outstanding subpoenas nor issue any new ones, the Court is not inclined to grant Defendants’ request for a protective order or monetary sanctions.

 

B.      Plaintiff APRL’s Motion to Compel

 

a.       Legal Standard

 

If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.230, fails to appear for examination, to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, without having served a valid objection under Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.410, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2025.450(a).)

 

b.      Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain 'an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016 (internal quotations and citations omitted) (emphasis added).)

 

On September 6, 2024, APRL filed a notice of compliance with order to adequately meet and confer. APRL’s counsel states that on September 5, 2024, the parties discussed the motion by telephone and the parties were unable to resolve their differences.

 

The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

c.       Discussion

 

APRL moves to compel production of documents and further deposition of HVFG, LLC vice president Marcus Henley regarding documents that were not produced at his deposition.

 

APRL states that although the discovery deadline has passed, the parties had agreed that depositions of identified representatives of Defendants would proceed after the discovery cut-off. (Pease Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 5.) APRL contends that pursuant to Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 2016.030, “unless the court orders otherwise, the parties may by written stipulation modify the procedures provided by this title for any method of discovery permitted under Section 2019.010.”

 

As discussed above, discovery in this matter closed on June 26, 2023, 30 days before this case was set for trial. (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 2024.020.) By order dated January 9, 2024, the Court ruled: “Absent stipulation of the parties and a further court order, no other dates or deadlines are continued or revived.” (emphasis added.) Further, on March 7, 2024, the Court denied APRL’s Motion to Enforce Prior Discovery Order, citing its January 9th order requiring Court approval for any further discovery motions and noting that APRL had failed to receive such approval. (Court’s 3/7/24 Minute Order [“[The deadline for discovery motions] has long passed in this action. There is no order allowing this motion to be heard. Accordingly, the court will not entertain the motion.”].)

 

Here, APRL has not moved to reopen discovery nor has the Court issued an order reopening discovery. Thus, APRL’s motion to compel is denied.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion to Stay and Quash Deposition Notices and Subpoenas and for Protective Order Enforcing Discovery Cut-Off is GRANTED and DENIED in part. Defendants’ request to stay and quash all third-party notices of deposition and deposition subpoenas served after the June 26, 2023, discovery cut-off is GRANTED. Defendants’ request for a Protective Order enforcing the discovery cut-off and request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel is DENIED.

DOMINGUEZ HILLS ESTATES v. WILLIAM J. BEVERLY, et al. (23STCV02685)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  John Manzano (Tacsis Law APC); Jack Henningsen (Turner Henningsen Wolf & Vandenburg LLP)

Counsel for Defendants:  Stephen Goldberg (Spierer, Woodward, Corbalis & Goldberg)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO CONTINUE THE FINAL STATUS CONFERENCE, TRIAL AND ALL APPLICABLE DISCOVERY AND MOTION CUTOFF DATES (filed 05/24/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Continue the Final Status Conference, Trial, and all Applicable Discovery and Motion Cutoff Dates is GRANTED.

 

The new Trial Date is set for March 17, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. The Final Status Conference is continued to March 3, 2025, at 10:00 a.m. All case-related deadlines are to follow the new trial date.

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 7, 2023, Plaintiff Dominguez Hills Estates (“DHE”) filed this action against Defendants William J. Beverly, William J. Beverly, as Trustee under Declaration of Trust by William Mertz and Joelene Mertz, William J. Beverly dba Mertz Management Company, Mertz Management Company, form of entity unknown, and Does 1 through 50.

 

Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on April 14, 2023, alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Contract;

2.      Breach of Fiduciary Duty;

3.      Constructive Fraud; and

4.      Conversion.

 

The FAC alleges the following. William J. Beverly is a former General Partner of DHE in his capacity as Trustee under a Declaration of Trust by William Mertz and Joelene Mertz. William J. Beverly also was doing business under the fictitious business name of Mertz Management. Defendant William J. Beverly is sued in both his capacity as trustee and individually. Beverly was prohibited, under the express terms of the partnership agreement, from receiving compensation for the management of the partnership affairs. In breach and violation of the express terms of the partnership agreement, Beverly collected a management fee from the operation of the partnership and concealed this fact from the partnership and the other partners. Furthermore, Beverly conducted himself as the managing general partner against the interest of the partnership and in his own self-interest in such a manner that DHE was prevented from receiving the benefits under the contract. By doing so, Beverly did not act fairly and in good faith.

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Continue the Final Status Conference, Trial, and all Applicable Discovery and Motion Cutoff Dates.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A party seeking a continuance of the date set for trial must make the request for a continuance by a noticed motion or an ex parte application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332(b).)¿ The request for continuance may be granted on an affirmative showing of good cause.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332.)¿

 

The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial. (See generally, Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (d)(1)-(11).) Additionally, factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; the proximity of the trial date; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c), (d).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order continuing the September 9, 2024 Final Status Conference, the September 23, 2024 Trial, and all applicable discovery and motion cutoff dates for a period of 180 days or any date thereafter.

 

Plaintiff asserts that the parties are “engaging in good faith settlement discussions to resolve both this Action as well as its sister matter, Los Angeles County Superior Court case number 22STCV32334.” (Manzano Decl., ¶ 4.) Thus, due to the complexity of the contemplated settlement, additional time is needed to both negotiate settlement terms as well as set up the financial aspects that will very likely be needed in order to facilitate any such settlement, and alternatively, to prepare for trial in the event that settlement is not consummated. (Manzano Decl., ¶ 4.) Further, Plaintiff asserts that a new party—Defendant CHARLES GIOVINO aka CHUCK GIOVINO, individually and as Trustee under Declaration of Trust by William Mertz and Jolene Mertz (hereinafter, “Giovino”)—was recently added to the Action, and additional time will also be needed in order to conduct discovery and prepare for trial, should settlement not be consummated.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to continue trial. The addition of a new party to the action, the fact that there has only been one previous trial continuance and the relatively short length of the requested continuance all weigh in favor of a trial continuance. Further, Plaintiff asserts that the complex nature of the contemplated settlement leaves no alternative means to address the need for a continuance. Moreover, no opposition has been filed alleging prejudice. Plaintiff asserts that all parties have stipulated to a continuance. Thus, the Court finds the interests of justice are best served by a trial continuance.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Motion to Continue the Final Status Conference, Trial, and all Applicable Discovery and Motion Cutoff Dates.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Continue the Final Status Conference, Trial, and all Applicable Discovery and Motion Cutoff Dates is GRANTED.

 

The new Trial Date is set for March 17, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. The Final Status Conference is continued to March 3, 2025, at 10:00 a.m. All case-related deadlines are to follow the new trial date.


Case Number: 22STCV18602    Hearing Date: September 9, 2024    Dept: 73

09/09/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

AVERY v. BELANGER, et al. (22STCV18602)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Jason Stone (Stone & Sallus, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  R. Steven Derryberry (Derryberry & Associates LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT CATHERINE BELANGER TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSE TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET TWO, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 03/11/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, Nos. 50, 51 and 57 is DENIED. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 7, 2022, Plaintiff Veronica Avery filed the instant action against Defendants Catherine Belanger (“Catherine”), Benjamin Belanger, Kannella, Inc. dba Re/Max All-Pro (Re/Max), and Does 1-10.

 

On October 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Elder Financial Abuse

2.      Breach of Fiduciary Duty

3.      Fraud

4.      Violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200

5.      Declaratory Relief and Injunctive Relief

The FAC alleges that Plaintiff is an elder with dementia and cognitive decline, and that Plaintiff suffered damages when Defendants took her home against her will and forged her signature to complete the sale of her home to themselves at a discounted price.

 

On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Defendant Catherine Belanger’s (“Defendant” or “Catherine”) Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Request Nos. 50, 51 and 57.

·       Defendant’s responses do not properly specify whether the documents ever existed or not. Further, Defendant’s response does not identify the party who may have the requested items. For example, if Defendant did have insurance, then she needs to identify the insurance company and the other parties who would have information regarding the policies. Defendant must provide code compliant responses and produce any withheld documents.

·       Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the amount of $3,212.50.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendant’s responses are code compliant.

o   Plaintiff’s basis for the subject motion is that Defendant’s responses to the three enumerated items were improper because they did not specify “whether the documents ever existed or not.” This misstates the requirements of § 2031.230. Defendant’s original response properly stated that the requested documents “are not in Responding Party’s custody or control, and in fact such documents, if they ever existed, [iv] have never been in Responding Party’s custody or control.” As such there is nothing in the Defendant’s original response which is improper. Further, Defendant’s supplemented response to item 57 clearly stated that the “documents/items do not exist.”

·       Plaintiff failed to properly meet and confer

·       Sanctions should not be imposed

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       Defendant’s counsel failed to respond to Plaintiff’s prior meet and confer correspondence before this motion was filed because she told Plaintiff’s counsel that she did not see the prior meet and confer correspondence because she was out of town and that it was “her bad.” (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 4).

·       In Defendant’s further response to RFP 51, Defendant says that her insurance information is possessed by her broker, RE/MAX, but Defendant is still working as an agent under RE/MAX and did not obtain the requested insurance documentation from them.

·       Plaintiff does not dispute that Defendant’s further responses to RFP 50 and 57 are code compliant, but Plaintiff asserts it was untimely submitted after Plaintiff had already filed this motion. Therefore, Defendant is subject to monetary sanctions.

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel a further response to a request for production must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the response, or any supplemental response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further response to the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c); see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)  The motion must also be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of any issue presented by it. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).) Finally, a motion to compel further responses to inspection demands must be accompanied by a separate document that sets forth each item to which further response is requested and the factual and legal reasons for compelling it. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a).)  

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiff’s counsel states they sent a detailed meet and confer letter to Defendant's counsel regarding the discovery responses at issue and a deadline to provide further responses. (Carpinelli Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.) However, Defendant did not provide further responses by the deadline. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff failed to properly meet and confer because counsel’s letter did not give proper consideration to Defendant’s supplemented responses.

 

Nevertheless, the Court will consider the merits of the motion.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Request Nos. 50, 51, and 57.

 

Plaintiff asserts that on or about November 21, 2023, Plaintiff served her second set of Request for Production of Documents to Defendant Catherine. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 4, Exhibit A.) Catherine served her responses to the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two on or about December 22, 2023. Upon Plaintiff’s review, these responses are deficient. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 5, Exhibit B.) On or about January 22, 2024, Plaintiff served her meet and confer letter to Catherine. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 6, Exhibit C.) On or about February 9, 2024, Catherine served her response to the meet and confer letter. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 7, Exhibit D.) Catherine also served her supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s second set of Request for Production of Documents. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 8, Exhibit E.)

 

Plaintiff served her second meet and confer letter on February 23, 2024. The letter provided a deadline for Catherine to provide further responses by March 1, 2024. (Carpinelli Declaration ¶ 9, Exhibit F.)

 

On August 8, 2024, Catherine served further responses. (Rose-McCaslin Decl., ¶ 5, Exhibit A.)

 

a.       Request No. 50

 

Request No. 50 states “Please produce all DOCUMENTS RELATED to errors and omissions insurance that YOU had at the time of the SUBJECT TRANSACTION.”

 

Defendant’s further response states “Responding Party has made a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry for the documents/items but is unable to comply with the demand because the documents/items never existed.”

 

The Court finds Defendant’s response is code compliant. In Plaintiff’s reply separate statement, Plaintiff concedes that Defendant’s further response is code compliant.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Request No. 50.

 

b.      Request No. 51

 

Request No. 51 states “Please produce all DOCUMENTS RELATED to any insurance policies that may provide YOU with coverage for the SUBJECT TRANSACTION.”

 

Defendant’s further response states “Responding Party has made a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry for the documents/items but is unable to comply with the demand because the documents/items, to the extent they ever existed, are not in Responding Party’s custody or control, and in fact such documents, if they ever existed, have never been in Responding Party’s custody or control.

If the documents exist they would be in the possession of:

KANNELLA, INC. DBA RE/MAX ALL-PRO

c/o Cheyanne Terracciano

43832 20th Street West Lancaster, California 93534

Phone: (661) 945-9461.”

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not made a diligent and good faith inquiry into obtaining the sought information because Defendant still works for RE/MAX. (Reply, Ex. G.)

 

However, the Court finds Defendant’s response is code compliant. Defendant provided the name and address of the possible location of the requested documents.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Request No. 51.

 

c.       Request No. 57

 

Request No. 57 states “Please produce any and all COMMUNICATIONS related to title for the PROPERTY being placed only in BENJAMIN BELANGERS name as his sole and separate property.”

 

Defendant’s further response states “Responding Party has made a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry for the documents/items but is unable to comply with the demand because the documents/items do not exist.”

 

The Court finds Defendant’s response is code compliant. In Plaintiff’s reply separate statement, Plaintiff concedes that Defendant’s further response is code compliant.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Request No. 57.

 

d.      Sanctions

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(h), monetary sanctions shall be imposed where a party unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses unless the Court finds the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

Here, the Court will not impose sanctions. Defendant served further responses and as discussed above, the further responses are code compliant.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, Nos. 50, 51 and 57 is DENIED. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.
