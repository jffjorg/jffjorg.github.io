DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02503    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Carlos Bernal,

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

 

Ali Mehranrazi, et al.

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV02503              107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

  Motion for Summary Judgment

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:  Denied.

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On December 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Ali Mehranrazi (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 25, alleging causes of action for: (1) general negligence; and (2) premises liability.

 

The complaint alleged that Defendant and Does 1 to 25 owned, managed, operated, controlled, inspected, maintained, cleaned, repaired, guarded, supervised, renovated, designed, monitored, constructed, and/or secured a wooden joist in an attic at a property located at 963 Deep Wood Drive, Westlake Village, CA 91362 (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint p. 4.) On October 11, 2022, Plaintiff Carlos Bernal (“Plaintiff”) slipped and fell from the Subject Property’s attic. (Complaint pp. 4-5.)

 

On November 14, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”). On January 7, 2025, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On January 13, 2025, Defenant filed a reply.

 

II.              PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of Defendant’s Notice of Motion and Motion for Summary Judgment; or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication, filed and served on Plaintiff on November 6, 2024. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.) Plaintiff also requests this court take judicial notice of Defendant’s Notice of Hearing Date Regarding Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication, filed and served on Plaintiff on November 25, 2024. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h) states that the court may take judicial notice of “Facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” Defendant filed a document with this court stating “Defendant Ali Mehranrazi filed and served his Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication. The Motion for Summary Judgement inadvertently listed a hearing date of January 21, 2024. The correct hearing date is January 21, 2025.” (Notice of Hearing Date filed 11/25/24.) Accordingly, this court finds that Defendant’s Notice of Motion and Motion for Summary Judgment; or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication, filed and served on Plaintiff on November 6, 2024, is not reasonably subject to dispute and grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.  (The specific document cannot be found in the court’s file. This document has the wrong hearing date on the first and second page of the notice of motion. The notice of motion filed by Defendant with the court on November 14, 2024 has the correct hearing date on the first page of the notice of motion, and the wrong date on the second page. (Plaintiff Ex. 5.))

 

This court need not take judicial notice of Defendant’s Notice of Hearing Date Regarding Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication, filed and served on Plaintiff on November 25, 2024, because it is part of the case file.

 

III.           DEFENDANT’S OBJECTIONS

 

Defendant raises five objections to Ryan J. Radebach’s declaration. While this decision does not rely on Radebach’s declaration and it is not necessary to address the objections, this court this court overrules Objections 1 and 2, and sustains Objections 3, 4, and 5. Regarding the portions of Radebach’s declaration that relate to Objection Nos. 3 through 5 - Radebach makes statements such as “The dilapidated condition of the floor joist was a concealed hazard that was not reasonably detectable and even though Defendant knew about this water damage and potential pest he failed to warn the Plaintiff. . .”, “The Defendant knew or should have known about the issues identified in the reports from the inspection and should have warned the Plaintiff that there could be damage from water and/or pest possibly decreasing the integrity of the floor joists in the attic . . .”., and “There were enough indicators that latent safety hazards were likely present in the attic to where it was reasonable to expect the Defendant to recognize this and warn people prior to entering the attic space.” Radebach’s statement Defendant knew about the damage lacks foundation.

Regarding the other statements -that Defendant knew or should have known and Defendant’s duty to warn are conclusions - and they are not relevant.

 

IV.            TIMELINESS

 

A.    Trial Date

 

A motion for summary judgment “shall be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(3).)

 

Trial in this case is set for February 19, 2025. Thirty days before February 19, 2025, was January 20, 2025. The hearing on this Motion is set for January 21, 2025.

 

Defendant filed multiple ex parte applications seeking to allow the Motion to be heard within 30 days of the current trial date, or, in the alternative, seeking to continue the trial date. (Ex Parte Application filed 01/06/25; Ex Parte Application filed 12/19/24; Ex Parte Application 12/09/24.) This court denied Defendant’s applications. (12/10/24 Minute Order; 12/20/24 Minute Order; 01/07/25 Minute Order.) Accordingly, this court has not found good cause to hear the Motion less than 30 days before trial.

 

Further, Defendant filed the Motion on November 14, 2024, but did not file the first ex parte application seeking an order allowing the Motion to be heard within thirty days of trial until December 9, 2024.  In Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1268, the defendants argued the notice was invalid. First, the defendants argued that the plaintiffs served the defendants with the notice by mail 76 days before the hearing, which was invalid because “the hearing had to be set at least 80 days after service by mail – the standard 75 days plus five days if mailed to a location in California.” (Id.) The defendants also argued that the hearing date was set for only 18 days before trial, and “the motion had to be heard no later than 30 days before the trial date unless the trial court ruled otherwise for ‘good cause.’” (Id.) On April 12, 2007, the trial court continued the hearing for four days and “advised defendants to file a declaration setting forth the reasons why the motion should be heard within 30 days of trial[.]” (Id. at p. 1261.) At the April 16, 2007 hearing, the trial court found “good cause for the motion being brought within 30 days of trial” and considered the motion’s merits. (Id.) The Court of Appeal reversed. (Id. at p. 1269.) First, the Court of Appeal found that the plaintiffs did not receive the required 75-day notice. (Id. at p. 1268.) The Court also found that the April 12, 2007 hearing was invalid, stating “Defendants noticed their motion for hearing within 30 days of the trial date without first obtaining a determination of good cause from the trial court. [Citation]. Unless and until the trial court found good cause, the notice of the hearing was invalid.”

 

Like Robinson, Defendant noticed the Motion for hearing within 30 days of the trial date, without first obtaining a determination of good cause from this court. Further, this court never found good cause to permit the Motion to be heard within 30 days of the trial date. Accordingly, the notice of hearing was invalid.

 

B.    Minimum Notice Period

 

A motion for summary judgment “may be made at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(1).)

 

Effective from January 1, 2017, to December 31, 2024:

 

(2) Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing. If the notice is served by mail, the required 75-day period of notice shall be increased by 5 days if the place of address is within the State of California, 10 days if the place of address is outside the State of California but within the United States, and 20 days if the place of address is outside the United States. If the notice is served by facsimile transmission, express mail, or another method of delivery providing for overnight delivery, the required 75-day period of notice shall be increased by two court days.

 

(Former Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(2), enacted 2016)

 

Effective January 1, 2025:

 

(2) Notice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 81 days before the time appointed for hearing. If the notice is served by mail, the required 81-day period of notice shall be increased by 5 days if the place of address is within the State of California, 10 days if the place of address is outside the State of California but within the United States, and 20 days if the place of address is outside the United States. If the notice is served by facsimile transmission, express mail, or another method of delivery providing for overnight delivery, the required 81-day period of notice shall be increased by two court days.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a)(2).)

 

Courts have no authority to shorten the minimum notice period for a summary judgment motion. (See Urshan v. Musicians’ Credit Union (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 758, 764 [“[A] trial court has discretion to shorten the initial 60–day period to bring a motion for summary judgment on a showing of good cause. Similarly, a trial court has discretion to shorten the 30–day period in which a motion for summary judgment must be heard before trial where circumstances warrant. However, the Legislature did not similarly authorize a trial court to shorten the minimum notice period for hearings on summary judgment motions. Moreover, the statutory language regarding minimum notice is mandatory, not directive.”]

 

The Motion is set for hearing on January 21, 2025. Seventy-five days before January 21, 2025, was November 7, 2024.

 

Defendant attaches two proofs of service to the Motion.

 

The first proof of service states Defendant served Plaintiff with the Motion and supporting documents, by email, on November 14, 2024. (Motion pdf p. 18.) The notice of motion served November 14, 2024, is clearly untimely.

 

The second proof of service states Defendant served Plaintiff with the Motion and supporting documents on November 6, 2024, “(BY PERSONAL SERVICE) I placed such envelopes for collection and to be mailed on this date following ordinary business practices.”

 

Although the November 6, 2024, proof of service state it was served “BY PERSONAL SERVICE”, the description of such service states Defendant “placed such envelopes for collection and to be mailed on [November 6, 2024.]” Plaintiff’s counsel’s address is in California. Per Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (a)(2), enacted 2016, “If the notice is served by mail, the required 75-day period of notice shall be increased by 5 days if the place of address is within the State of California[.]” Five days before November 7, 2024 was November 2, 2024. Defendant mailed the documents on November 6, 2024. Thus, the Motion is untimely because Defendant did not provide Plaintiff with the minimum notice period required under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c.

 

Further, the notice of motion served on November 6, 2024, was defective for a second reason. The notice of motion served on November 6, 2024, listed the hearing date as “January 21, 2024.” (Plaintiff Ex. 5.) The hearing in this case is set for January 21, 2025. “Notices must be in writing, and the notice of a motion, other than for a new trial, must state when, and the grounds upon which it will be made, and the papers, if any, upon which it is to be based.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1010.)  Accordingly, the notice served on November 6, 2024, was defective because it did not list the correct hearing date.

 

Defendant attempted to correct this issue by serving Plaintiff with an amended notice on November 25, 2024, which stated “On November 6, 2024, Defendant Ali Mehranrazi filed and served his Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication. The Motion for Summary Judgement inadvertently listed a hearing date of January 21, 2024. The correct hearing date is January 21, 2025.” (Defendant’s Notice of Hearing Date Regarding Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication filed 11/25/24.)

 

In Golf & Tennis Pro Shop, Inc. v. Superior Court (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127, 138-139, a petitioner filed a defective notice relating to a motion to compel, and then attempted to cure the defect by serving an amended notice. On appeal, the petitioner argued “it filed and served amended notices for the motions ‘well ahead’ of the . . . hearing date.” (Id.)  The Court of Appeal rejected that argument, stating “[w]e are glad petitioner got around to it, but it was still too little, too late. Section 2030.300, subdivision (c) required adequate notice to be given to plaintiffs and the court 45 days after service of the verified response, not in some nebulous timeframe that was ‘well ahead of the hearing date.’” (Id.)  In that case “petitioner's notice of motion was ineffective in doing what a notice is meant to do. It stated petitioner's intent to move to compel further responses by Layus to the first set of special interrogatories, but it did not identify the specific interrogatories, leaving both Layus and the court with the potential impression that all interrogatories were at issue. It did not cite any statutory authority for the motion, or attempt to state what, if anything, was deficient about Layus's responses to the interrogatories. It also failed to identify the papers upon which it was to be based.” Accordingly, Golf & Tennis Pro Shop, Inc. had a different defect than the defect at issue here. However, it is still analogous to the defect here because both cases involve notices that did not contain the information required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1010. [“Notices must be in writing, and the notice of a motion, other than for a new trial, must state when, and the grounds upon which it will be made, and the papers, if any, upon which it is to be based.”]

 

Likewise, here, Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (a)(2), enacted 2016, requires a party moving for summary judgment serve the notice of motion and supporting papers on the opposing party at least 75 days before the hearing, plus additional time depending on the method of service. Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 1010 requires that the notice “state when, and the grounds upon which it will be made, and the papers, if any, upon which it is to be based.” (Italics added.) Defendant served Plaintiff with the notice that contained the correct hearing date on November 25, 2024. November 25, 2024, was only 57 days before the hearing on this Motion, so it did not provide Plaintiff with the required minimum notice under section 437c.

 

In the reply, Defendant cites Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1119, 1125, and states that the notice was sufficient because Plaintiff’s counsel received actual notice of the hearing date, “even if there was a typographical error on the caption page regarding the year of the hearing, the reservation page attached to the Motion indicated the correct hearing date”, “Plaintiff was provided with sufficient notice for the hearing”, and “given that the Motion was filed in November, and the hearing date was noticed for February, it would be an impossibility that the hearing would take place in 2024.” (Reply pp. 6-7.) In Luri v. Greenwald, supra, 107 Cal.App.4th at p. 1125, the Court of Appeal stated that “An omission in the notice may be overlooked if the supporting papers make clear the grounds for the relief sought. [Citations.] The purpose of these requirements is to cause the moving party to ‘sufficiently define the issues for the information and attention of the adverse party and the court.” In Luri, Luri argued that “even though her section 473 motion only referred to discretionary relief, the trial court erred by not treating the motion as though it sought relief under the mandatory provision of section 473 because the motion was accompanied by a declaration of an attorney indicating his fault.” (Id. at p. 1124.) The Court of Appeal noted that this argument “is not consistent with procedural rules governing motions” including section 1010’s requirement “that a notice of motion must state ‘the grounds upon which it will be made.’” (Id. at p. 1125.) The Court in Luri also noted that “Because Luri’s motion did not include a notice of motion stating ‘the grounds upon which the motion will be made’ [citation] or an opening paragraph stating ‘the nature of the order being sought and the grounds for issuance of the order’ [citation] the trial court could reasonably have rejected the entire motion as defective and noncompliant with California rules and statutes. The trial court overlooked these defects and reviewed the documents submitted to determine the specific basis upon which Luri sought relief . . .” (Id. at p. 1127.) Thus, the Luri Court acknowledged that a trial court may reject a motion when the notice of motion does not comply with California rules and statutes. Further, Luri did not involve the same issue as this case.

 

This court applies the version of section 437c in effect in 2024. However, this court also notes that applying the version of section 437c in effect in 2025 would not change the outcome here, because the 2025 version increased the required notice period from 75 days to 81 days.

 

Thus, Defendant’s amended notice was untimely and insufficient to cure the original defective notice of hearing. This court has no authority to shorten the minimum notice period under section 437c, subdivision (a)(2). (Urshan v. Musicians’ Credit Union, supra, 120 Cal.App.4th at p. 764.)

 

V.              CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion is denied.

II.             PROCEDURAL ISSUES

 

A.    Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿430.40(a).)¿ Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)¿¿

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. “Any period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

 

On October 17, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendants with the first amended complaint by electronic transmission. (Proof of Service filed 11/15.24.) Thirty days after October 17, 2024, was November 16, 2024. Accordingly, with the extension for electronic service, Defendants’ demurrer with motion to strike was due by November 19, 2024. Defendants filed the demurrer with motion to strike on December 10, 2024.

 

Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281.) This court will exercise its discretion and consider the demurrer with motion to strike.

 

The demurrer with motion to strike, oppositions and replies were timely filed under section 1005.

 

B.    Meet and Confer

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)¿ California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435.5, subdivision (a) also requires parties to meet and confer before filing a motion to strike.¿¿¿ 

 

Defendants’ counsel submits declarations showing Defendants’ counsel telephonically met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel, but the parties were unable to reach an agreement. (Latson Demurrer Declaration ¶¶ 6-7; Latson Motion to Strike Declaration ¶¶ 9-10.)

 

This court finds that Defendants satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

 

III.           DEMURRER

 

A.    Legal Standard

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)¿

 

B.    Discussion

 

1.     Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

“’The [implied] covenant of good faith and fair dealing [is] implied by law in every contract.’ [Citation.] The covenant is read into contracts and functions ‘as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party's rights to the benefits of the contract.’ [Citation.] The covenant also requires each party to do everything the contract presupposes the party will do to accomplish the agreement's purposes. [Citation.] A breach of the implied covenant of good faith is a breach of the contract [citation], and ‘breach of a specific provision of the contract is not…necessary’ to a claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing [citation].” (Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230, 1244.)

The plaintiff's allegations must show “that the conduct of the defendant, whether or not it also constitutes a breach of a consensual contract term, demonstrates a failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities, prompted not by an honest mistake, bad judgment or negligence but rather by a conscious and deliberate act, which unfairly frustrates the agreed common purposes and disappoints the reasonable expectations of the other party thereby depriving that party of the benefits of the agreement. Just what conduct will meet this criteria must be determined on a case by case basis and will depend on the contractual purposes and reasonably justified expectations of the parties.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.) “If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated.” (Id.)

Plaintiffs allege a contract exists. (FAC ¶ 17.) Plaintiffs also allege Defendants demonstrated a failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities, prompted by a conscious and deliberate act, by alleging Defendants “exerted undue influence to pressure Plaintiff, JAN WEISSMAN, to continue paying on the contract and pressuring her to pay even more money via unnecessary and/or excessive change orders.” (FAC ¶ 27.)  Plaintiffs allege this “frustrat[ed] the agreed common purpose of the parties and disappoint[ed] the reasonable expectations of Plaintiffs thereby depriving Plaintiffs of the benefits of [the Contract].” (FAC ¶ 51.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages resulting from Defendants’ conduct. (FAC ¶ 52.)

 

Defendants argue the allegations referenced above are insufficient because Plaintiffs “merely refers to ‘the conduct as set forth herein’ and have “not plead the specifics of [the] alleged conduct which allegedly frustrated the agreed upon common purpose of the parties and disappointed Plaintiff’s reasonable allegations, but the allegations set forth under the Third Cause of Action require speculation as to what conduct Plaintiff references.” (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

The specific allegations referencing exerting pressure on Plaintiffs to continue paying for unnecessary and/or excessive orders and removing property are alleged under the first amended complaint’s general allegations section, not the section on the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. (FAC ¶ 27.)  However, the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing section states “Plaintiffs hereby incorporate by reference each of the preceding paragraphs in this First Amended Complaint as if fully set forth herein.” (FAC ¶ 49.)  In reviewing a demurrer, the court “do[es] not read passages from a complaint in isolation” but instead “read[s] the complaint ‘as a whole and its parts in their context.’” (West v. JPMORGAN Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 804.) Upon reviewing the first amended complaint as a whole, Plaintiffs sufficiently allege a breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing cause of action.

 

2.     Elder Abuse

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action for financial elder abuse fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

Elder abuse includes any of the following:  

 

1.     Physical abuse, neglect, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering. 

 

2.     The deprivation by a care custodian of goods or services that are necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering. 

 

3.     Financial abuse, as defined in¿Section 15610.30. 

 

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.07(a).) 

 

Financial elder abuse occurs when a person or entity: “(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both. (3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).) 

 

“A person or entity shall be deemed to have taken, secreted, appropriated, obtained, or retained property for a wrongful use if, among other things, the person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains the property and the person or entity knew or should have known that this conduct is likely to be harmful to the elder or dependent adult.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(b).) “For purposes of this section, a person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property when an elder or dependent adult is deprived of any property right, including by means of an agreement, donative transfer, or testamentary bequest, regardless of whether the property is held directly or by a representative of an elder or dependent adult.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(c).)  “Undue influence” is defined as “excessive persuasion that causes another person to act or refrain from acting by overcoming that person's free will and results in inequity.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.70(a).) An “’[e]lder’ means any person residing in [California], 65 years of age or older.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.27.) 

 

“[S]tatutory causes of action must be pleaded with particularity.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790; see also Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 404 [stating in a cause of action for elder abuse based on neglect “the facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.’”])

 

Defendants argue “Plaintiff did not plead with particularity how the Vandagriffs exerted undue influence over her and/or intended to defraud her[.]” (Demurrer p. 8.) Plaintiffs state “Defendants have not cited one case that requires the particularity that they state Plaintiffs must allege.” (Opposition p. 5.) As noted, an elder abuse cause of action must be pleaded with particularity.

 

Plaintiffs allege that Plaintiff Jan Weissman was 66 years old when Plaintiff entered into the Contract with Defendants and that Plaintiff Jan Weissman resides in West Hills, California. (FAC ¶¶ 59, 1.) Thus, Plaintiffs allege Plaintiff Jan Weissman was an elder adult.

 

Plaintiffs also allege that Defendants “learned that Plaintiff, JAN WEISSMAN, suffered from a medical condition called ‘concussion syndrome’ that causes her to sometimes lose balance and easily get confused.” (FAC ¶ 60.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants “exerted undue influence on Plaintiff, JAN WEISSMAN, to continue paying for work that was not complete or was defective.” (FAC ¶ 61.) And Plaintiffs allege that Defendants “exerted undue influence on Plaintiff, JAN WEISSMAN, to sign and pay for change orders for unnecessary and/or excessive work.” (FAC ¶ 62.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants “knowing [Plaintiff Jan Weissman] did not have legal counsel and unfamiliar with the legal process, filed a Demand for Arbitration to attempt to pressure her to pay an additional $21,962.00.” (FAC ¶ 63.)

 

A plaintiff’s “use of . . . terminology [such] as fraudulently and recklessly cannot cure the failure to point out how or in what manner the [defendant] has transgressed.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 410.) Plaintiffs allege Defendants “exerted undue influence on Plaintiff . . . to continue paying for work that was not complete or was defective” and “to sign and pay for change orders for unnecessary and/or excessive work.” (FAC ¶¶ 61-62.) These paragraphs lack specific allegations showing how or in what manner the Defendants exerted undue influence or pressured Plaintiffs to pay the additional funds. These paragraphs also lack specific allegations showing Defendants took, secreted, appropriated, obtained, or retained Plaintiffs’ property for a wrongful purpose or with an intent to defraud.

 

Plaintiffs argue the first amended complaint states a cause of action for financial elder abuse because it complaint alleges Defendants “stole equipment and material[.]” (Opposition p. 6.) Plaintiffs allege that “[i]n the beginning of December of 2022” Defendant Craig Vandagriff Construction, Inc. “removed HVAC equipment and rebar that [Plaintiff] JAN WEISSMAN had already paid for” and Plaintiff “JAN WEISSMAN told CRAIG VANDAGRIFF to stop taking what belonged to her and to get off her property.” (FAC ¶ 29.) This could constitute a taking. However, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged how this taking was done “for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud” or through “undue influence.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).) 

 

Plaintiffs also argue the allegation Defendants filed a demand for arbitration is a sufficient “factual allegation . . . regarding specific ‘conduct.’” (Opposition p. 2.) As Defendants point out, the contract attached to the first amended complaint contains a dispute resolution provision, which provides that “The parties will attempt to resolve any dispute arising out of or relating to this Contract through friendly negotiations between the parties. If the matter is not resolved by negotiation, the parties will resolve the dispute using the below Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) procedure.” (Ex. A pdf p. 35.) By filing the demand for arbitration, Defendants appear to have been following the contract’s terms. Plaintiffs have not plead sufficient facts showing that, by filing the demand for arbitration, Defendants were engaging in undue influence by using “excessive persuasion that cause[d Plaintiff] to act or refrain from acting by overcoming [Plaintiffs’] free will[.].” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.70(a).)

 

Moreover, section 15610.70, subdivision (a) requires the excessive persuasion to “result[] in inequity.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.70(a).) And section 15610.30 states that financial elder abuse involves taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, “real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence . . .” or one of the other means listed in subdivision (a). Plaintiffs allege that Plaintiff Jan Weissman received notice that Defendant Craig Vandagriff Construction, Inc. filed a demand for arbitration and Plaintiff Jan Weissman specially appeared in the arbitration and “objected to jurisdiction on the grounds that there are parties in the present case that are not privy to the contract between Plaintiffs and Defendant, CRAIG VANDAGRIFF CONSTRUCTION, INC.” (FAC ¶ 36.) And Plaintiffs allege that Defendants filed the demand for arbitration “to pressure [Plaintiff Jan Weissman] to pay an additional $21,962.00.” (FAC ¶ 63.) Based on these allegations, it appears that the arbitration proceeding is pending. These allegations do not indicate that Defendants have, through filing the demand for arbitration, taken, secreted, appropriated, obtained, or retained, Plaintiffs’ real or personal property for a wrongful use, with the intent to defraud, or by undue influence.

 

Thus, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged a financial elder abuse cause of action.

 

IV.           MOTION TO STRIKE

 

A.    Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike “any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike “all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿¿ 

 

B.    Discussion

 

1.     Punitive Damages

Defendants seek an order striking the references and prayer for relief relating to punitive damages in the first amended complaint. (Motion to Strike p. 5.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) To justify a punitive damages award, the defendant must be “guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”¿(Civ. Code., § 3294.)

 

Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c)(2).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).)¿ 

¿ 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And “’conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿

 

Plaintiffs allege that Defendants “exerted undue influence to pressure Plaintiff, JAN WEISSMAN, to continue paying on the contract and pressuring her to pay even more money via unnecessary and/or excessive change orders.” (FAC ¶ 27.) This allegation lacks specificity and is, therefore, insufficient to sustain a request for punitive damages.

Plaintiff also alleges that “[i]n the beginning of December 2022 [Defendant] CRAIG VANDAGRIFF CONSTRUCTION, INC. began removing property from the Subject Property that [Plaintiff] JAN WEISSMAN had already paid for” including “HVAC equipment and rebar[.]” (FAC ¶ 29.) This allegation relates only to Defendant Craig Vandagriff Construction, Inc. and does not mention Defendants Craig Vandagriff and Jody Vandagriff. Moreover, the allegation is insufficient to show Defendants acted with malice, fraud, or oppression.

Finally, Plaintiffs allege that, on or about December 29, 2022, Plaintiff Jan Weissman was notified that Defendant Craig Vandagriff Construction, Inc. filed a demand for arbitration. (FAC ¶ 30.) As noted, the contract attached to the complaint contains an arbitration provision. (See Ex. A pdf p. 35.) Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged that, by attempting to resolve the dispute in a manner provided for by the Contract, Defendants acted with malice, fraud, or oppression.

Accordingly, the first amended complaint’s allegations are insufficient to support Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages.

2.     Treble Damages

Defendants also seek an order striking the references and prayer for relief relating to treble damages. (Motion to Strike p. 12.)

Plaintiffs seek treble damages for the fifth cause of action for financial elder abuse. (FAC p. 27.) This court sustained Defendants’ demurrer as to the fifth cause of action. Thus, this issue is moot.

V.             LEAVE TO AMEND

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, ‘leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.’” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.)¿¿¿ 

 

This court finds there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiffs can cure the defects in the complaint and grants Plaintiffs leave to amend.¿ 

 

VI.           CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer, in part, and overrules the demurrer, in part. This court also grants the motion to strike, as it relates to punitive damages, and finds that the motion to strike, as it relates to treble damages, is moot. Plaintiffs are granted leave to amend.
