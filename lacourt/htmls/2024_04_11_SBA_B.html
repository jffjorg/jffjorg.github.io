DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02275    Hearing Date: April 11, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

CONCEPTUAL MANAGEMENT, INC.,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV02276

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

MW RESIDENTIAL SOCAL CORP., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 11, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant MW Residential Socal Corp.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Compel Arbitration and to Stay Proceedings

 

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The action is STAYED pending arbitration.

BACKGROUND

On July 14, 2023, plaintiff Conceptual Management, Inc., dba Triton Construction filed a complaint against MW Residential Socal Corp., ST Market D3, Inglewood Market Gateway, LLC, Thomas Safran & Associates, Inc., and First Republic Bank for (1) breach of contract (against MW Residential), (2) reasonable value (against MW Residential), and (3) foreclosure of mechanic’s lien (against all defendants).

On September 27, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC against MW Residential Socal Corp., ST Market D3, LLC, Inglewood Market Gateway, LLC, and Fidelity and Deposit Company of Maryland.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant MW Residential Socal Corp. (“MW Residential”) requests an order compelling arbitration of the FAC and to stay any further proceedings.       

The FAC alleges that on August 19, 2020, plaintiff and defendants entered into a written contract wherein plaintiff agreed to provide specific labor and material for metal framing and drywall at the Inglewood Market Gateway project.  Defendants agreed to pay plaintiff $5 million for such labor and material.  Thereafter, the parties entered into several modifications of the contract whereby plaintiff agreed to furnish certain labor, material and equipment different from and/or in addition to that originally agreed to be furnished and defendants agreed to pay plaintiff the additional sum of $1,008,063 for such additional work.  FAC, ¶6.  On January 31, 2023, defendants breached the contract by failing to pay plaintiff pursuant to the terms of the contract.  Plaintiff has suffered damages in the amount of $1,033,873.  Id., ¶9

            Existence of an Enforceable Agreement

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant MW Residential contends that defendant and plaintiff executed a subcontract (FAC, Exh. A), which sets forth the general terms and conditions under which plaintiff provided labor, materials, and services to defendant for the Inglewood Market Gateway project.  Defendant asserts that Section 15 (“Claims Resolution”) of the subcontract states, in part, “A. Arbitration.  Subcontractor/Vendor agrees to binding arbitration as the agreed from of dispute resolution for any claims arising under this Agreement. . . .”  Defendant also explains that it is engaged in arbitration against ST Market D3, LLC before the AAA, and involves the same set of facts and circumstances on the same project.  See Todd Bressler decl. and FAC, Exh. A.

There is no opposition.

The court finds the existence of an arbitration agreement covering the causes of action in the complaint, and that no grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.

The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion to compel arbitration and to stay proceedings is GRANTED.  The action is stayed. 

            Moving defendant is ordered to give notice.

RULING

            The demurrer to first and second causes of action in the complaint is OVERRULED.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

            On November 6, 2023, plaintiff Ketria M. Land, successor trustee of The Louil P. Land, Jr. Revocable Living Trust dated July 14, 2000, filed a complaint against Edna Hamilton, in her capacity as the conservator of the Estate of Angela Land, and all persons claiming any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest in the property.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

This demurrer begins and ends with the well established rule that a demurrer tests the sufficiency of the pleading, but does not permit the introduction of other facts outside the pleading.  In other words, since defendant claims that there are additional facts implicating the divorce, that somehow invalidates the complaint.  Not so.  The Court shall not entertain the introduction of “alternative facts” at this point to attack the validity of the pleading.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant demurs to the first and second causes of action in the complaint on the grounds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action and plaintiff lacks standing/capacity to sue.

            The complaint alleges that this is a garden variety partition action following the death of one of the two co-owners of real property located at 3533 West 79th Street, Inglewood, CA 90305 (and a legal description).  Complaint, ¶1.  The property is currently occupied by co-owner Angela Land.  Id., ¶2.  She has failed to pay the mortgage, property taxes, and insurance premiums, which are all delinquent.  Id., ¶5.  Title to the property is currently vested in the name of Angela D. Land and Louil P. Land, Jr., Trustee, The Louil P. Land, Jr. Revocable Living Trust, Dated July 14, 2000 (“Trust”), tenants in common.  Id., ¶6.  On July 21, 1998, one-time husband and wife, Louil and Angela took title to the property as joint tenants with each party owing a one-half interest.  Id., ¶7.  On July 14, 2000, Louil created the Trust, which severed the joint tenancy when he recorded a trust transfer deed transferring his one-half interest in the property to the Trust.  The property is the only trust asset.  Id., ¶¶8-9. 

            The complaint further alleges that on December 5, 2003, Louil and Angela divorced when the court entered a default judgment for dissolution in case no. BC388894.  The judgment provides “there are no community or quasi-community assets or debts subject to disposition by the court in this matter.”  Id., ¶11.  On November 20, 2017, Louil died and his son David Land assumed successor trustee.  Id., ¶12.  On December 19, 2017, David commenced a probate proceeding to administer Louil’s probate estate in case no. 17STPB11292.  On March 2, 2018, David was appointed the administrator of Louil’s probate estate and letters of administration issued to him on April 10, 2018.  Id., ¶13.  On August 7, 2018, Angela was conserved, and defendant was appointed the conservator of the Estate of Angela Land in case no. 19STPB04481.  Letters of Conservatorship issued to defendant.  The property is the conservatorship estate’s sole asset.  Defendant’s Letters of Conservatorship expressly grant her the power to commence and maintain an action for partition.  Id., ¶14.  On June 30, 2020, defendant caused to be filed an Inventory and Appraisal in Angela’s conservatorship confirming that Angela owns one-half interest in the property.  Id., ¶15. 

            The complaint further alleges that on November 14, 2020, David died survived by his wife plaintiff and their children in common.  Plaintiff then petitioned to succeed David as Louil’s administrator, which was granted on April 26, 2021, and letters of administration issued to her on January 20, 2022.  Id., ¶16.  On March 22, 2021, plaintiff petitioned the probate court for the appointment of successor trustee of the Trust given the vacancy of trusteeship in case no. 23STPB01258.  Id., ¶17.  On April 20, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s petition for the appointment as successor trustee of the Trust.  The order was filed on November 6, 2024.  Id., ¶18.

                        First cause of action for partition

            Under Civil Code §872.230, “[t]he complaint shall set forth:  (a) A description of the property that is the subject of the action. . . . In the case of real property, the description shall include both its legal description and its street address of common designation, if any.  (b) All interests the plaintiff has or claims in the property.  (c) All interests of record or actually known to the plaintiff that persons other than the plaintiff have or claim in the property and that the plaintiff reasonably believes will be materially affected by the action, whether the names of such persons are known or unknown to the plaintiff.  (d) The estate as to which partition is sought and a prayer for partition of the interests therein.  (e) Where the plaintiff seeks sale of the property, an allegation of the facts justifying such relief in ordinary and concise language.”

The complaint alleges that plaintiff seeks partition by sale.  Partition is more equitable than a division in kind of the property under the circumstances, including the nature of the property and disagreement and dissension among all parties.  Complaint, ¶26.  The parties have no agreement relative to the administration of the sale or buyout by one of the other’s interests in the property.  Id., ¶27. 

Defendant contends that plaintiff has no standing because her “position as beneficiary is predicated upon the validity of the purported divorce” and that the “purported divorce was procured by fraud on its face and therefore is void.”  She contends that in the dissolution, the decedent made a false statement that there was no community property when at the filing date of the purported divorce, the conservatee was earning retirement credits.  Plaintiff also asserts that the trust was predicated on fraudulent dissolution.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations meet the elements for a cause of action for partition.  Further, plaintiff point out, nowhere in the demurrer does defendant challenge the sufficiency of the allegations for partition.  As to defendant’s argument regarding the invalidity of the divorce, plaintiff contends that contesting the facts in an otherwise well pled complaint is not grounds for demurrer, and that defendant’s definition of “community property” is irrelevant, and that defendant cannot attack a divorce judgment by demurrer.

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient to meet the elements for partition.  The question of whether the “divorce was procured by fraud” or whether the “trust was predicated on fraudulent dissolution” is not at issue at the pleading stage.  Further, plaintiff properly alleges standing as successor trustee based on the probate court’s orders.

            The demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

                        Second cause of action for declaratory relief

            Declaratory relief is available to a party “who desires a declaration of his or her rights or

duties with respect to another, or in respect to, in, over or upon property . . . may . . . bring an original action . . . for a declaration of his or rights and duties in the premises . . . .”  CCP §1060. A complaint for declaratory relief is legally sufficient if it sets forth facts showing the existence of an actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the parties and requests that the rights and duties of the parties be adjudged by the court.  If these requirements are met and no basis for declining declaratory relief appears, the court should declare the rights of the parties whether or not the facts alleged establish the plaintiff is entitled to favorable declaration.  See Ludgate Ins. Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2000) 82 Cal. App. 4th 592, 606.

The complaint alleges that an actual controversy has arisen and now exists between the parties concerning their respective rights and duties in that plaintiff seeks partition of the property and defendants refuse to cooperate in the administration of the sale or buyout by one or more parties of the other’s interests in the property.  Complaint, ¶31.  Plaintiff also seeks a declaration of rights and duties and a judicial determination that upon the sale and/or partition of the property, (a) the parties be paid in accordance with their fractional interest, (b) the court find it more equitable for defendants to pay all costs of partition, (c) the court find it more equitable for defendants to pay all of plaintiff’s attorney’s fees and costs, (d) any applicable party be paid any expenses incurred to preserve and protect the parties’ interest in the property, (e) defendants’ reimburse plaintiff for 100% of the money she advanced for the common benefit and protection and preservation of title, and (f) recovery of damages and compensatory adjustments from defendants for the fair market value of the property.  Id., ¶32.

            See defendant’s and plaintiff’s arguments above.

            The court finds that the allegations meet the elements for a cause of action for declaratory relief and that plaintiff has standing.

            The demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

ORDER

            The demurrer to the first and second causes of action in the complaint are OVERRULED.

            Defendant is ordered to file an answer within 20 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.
