DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00327    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: S27

Plaintiff filed this motion to compel Meta Development LLC to comply with a deposition subpoena for production of business records on 8/04/23, setting it for hearing on 10/05/23.  On 9/27/23 and 12/08/23, the parties filed stipulations to continue the hearing on the motion.  Both stipulations indicated the parties wished to have the hearing on the motion continued because they were working to resolve the issues without the need for a court order. 

 

Additionally, while not directly relevant to this motion, on 1/10/24, the parties submitted a stipulation continuing the hearing on a motion for summary adjudication and motion to compel deposition testimony of Michael Burwell, noting that the parties were set to close escrow on the property that forms the basis of this action on 2/08/24, which would render all ongoing issues moot. 

 

To date, no party has filed opposition to the motion to compel compliance with the Meta deposition subpoena, but it is not clear whether any issues remain unresolved.  The Court is therefore continuing the hearing on the motion to 4/23/24, to be heard with the motion for summary adjudication, motion to compel deposition testimony of Michael Burwell, and CMC.  If issues relating to this motion remain unresolved, the parties must file timely opposition and/or reply papers clarifying what remains outstanding for court resolution. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative.  If the parties do not submit on the tentative, they should arrange to appear remotely.


Case Number: 22LBCV00602    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jose Antonio Torres Aldaba and Veronica Barragan Figueroa filed this action against Defendant, Julie Lee Laven for damages arising out of a two-car accident.  The accident occurred on 10/31/20, and Plaintiff filed the lawsuit on 10/14/22.  On 3/17/23, both parties appeared for a CMC.  At the CMC, the Court set the FSC for 11/22/23 and the trial for 11/27/23. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Non-Party Deposition

Defendant moves to compel the PMK of Ridge Surgical Center, LLC to appear for deposition and produce documents.  The motion is denied without prejudice due to service issues.  Exhibit A to the moving papers is the Notice of Deposition and Deposition Subpoena.  The last page of the Notice of Deposition is a proof of service of the Notice on Plaintiff, which is acceptable.  The second page of the deposition subpoena is the proof of service of the subpoena.  It is, however, entirely blank.  There are no details about who was served, when, or how. 

 

CCP §2020.220(b) and (c) require personal service of a deposition subpoena on a non-party in order to compel that party to appear and testify.  Absent proof of service, the Court cannot grant the motion. 

 

This ruling is without prejudice to Defendant’s right to renew the motion with a sufficient showing that Defendant personally served the deponent with the deposition subpoena. 

 

The Court notes that it received, on 2/22/24, a proof of personal service of the motion on the non-party.  This proof of service does not, however, establish proper service of the deposition subpoena itself.

 

The Court also notes that it appears the issue between the parties relates to payment of the PMK’s claimed expert deposition fee.  Defendant, in the moving papers, provides evidence that the deponent has not been named as an expert in this case.  There is no opposition to the motion, either by Plaintiff or by the deponent.  The Court asks the parties to continue to attempt to work together to resolve any issues relating to the deposition without the need for additional law and motion practice.

Case Number: 22LBCV00808    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Ginger Andre Dolle filed this action against Defendants, City of Long Beach and Ali Assef for damages arising out of a series of sexual assaults perpetrated by Officer Assef on Plaintiff when she was a minor.  Plaintiff’s operative complaint is her First Amended Complaint, which she filed on 12/28/22.  It includes causes of action for childhood sexual abuse, sexual battery, violation of 42 USC §1983, violation of Civil Code §52.1, and violation of Civil Code §51.7.

 

  1. Motion for Summary Adjudication

a.     Request for Relief

            The City seeks summary adjudication of two issues.  First, it asks the Court to adjudicate whether it is vicariously liable for a sexual assault committed by Assef when he was off duty.  Second, it asks the Court to adjudicate whether any direct liability claims against it have been sufficiently pled and, if so, whether the City has direct liability for Plaintiff’s claimed injuries.    

 

b.     Threshold Issue

i.              Issue Presented

The threshold issue on this motion is whether either or both of the issues presented by Defendant are proper issues for summary adjudication.  Plaintiff contends they are not.

 

ii.             Statutory Language

CCP §437c(f)(1) provides, “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”

 

iii.            Vicarious Liability for Third Alleged Act

Plaintiff alleges Officer Assef assaulted her on three separate occasions.  On the third occasion, he was off duty.  The first stated issue is whether the City is vicariously liable for Assef’s actions perpetrated when he was off duty.  Plaintiff argues the issue cannot be adjudicated because (a) it would not dispose of an entire cause of action, and (b) vicarious liability is not an issue of “duty.”

 

Defendant relies on Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848 to support its position that summary adjudication of a distinct count within a cause of action is proper, even if the ruling would not dispose of the entire cause of action.  In Lilienthal, the plaintiffs sued the defendant for negligence and legal malpractice.  The defendant had represented the plaintiffs in two entirely separate and distinct matters, and each matter was at issue in each cause of action.  The defendant sought adjudication of the claims relating to one of the two representations, and the trial court denied the motion, finding that, even if granted, it would not dispose of an entire cause of action.  The Court of Appeals reversed, holding that the claims were so separate and distinct that, even though they were pled in the same causes of action, they really amounted to entirely distinct legal “counts,” or causes of action within the cause of action. 

 

By way of contrast, in Hindin v. Rust (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1247, 1258, the Court of Appeals held that summary adjudication was not proper when the complaint alleged numerous acts allegedly constituting malicious prosecution, and the defendant contended he had probable cause as to some of the acts, but not all of the acts.  The Court held that the cause of action was one whole and undivided cause of action, and the statute did not permit the trial court to parse out the various allegations to determine whether each allegation would ultimately support the relief sought.

 

This case falls somewhere in between.  The three acts are distinct, but so were the various acts of malicious prosecution in Hindin.  The Court finds this case is more similar to Hindin than it is to Lilienthal, and summary adjudication of one of the three acts is not appropriate.

 

This finding is made more certain when the Court turns to the next issue – whether the Court can adjudicate vicarious liability on a summary adjudication motion.  The Court finds Plaintiff’s position is correct; vicarious liability is not “duty”-based, but instead is a theory of liability.  A finding that an employer is vicariously liable for an employee’s torts does not rest on any duty running between the employer and the injured person, but instead of a philosophy of law that passes liability for the torts of employees onto their employers.  In Hinman v. Westinghouse Elec. Co. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 956, 950, the Court explained that the justification is in part based on “deep pockets”:  As between employer and employee, it is felt that the employer is more likely to be able to respond in damages to an innocent third person injured by the employee's tortious conduct (i.e., by working for the employer, the employee has increased the employer's profits). Also, employers are generally better able to protect against such risk by insurance, the cost of which can be spread over the entire business (and passed on to the public).

 

Thus, both because the motion is directed only to one of three acts that forms the basis of the complaint, and also because the motion does not address an issue of “duty,” the motion for summary adjudication of the issue of whether the City is vicariously liable for Officer Assef’s third assault on Plaintiff is denied. 

 

iv.            Direct Liability

Defendant argues, at page 18, lines 20-23, “Here, Plaintiff cannot establish any basis for direct liability against a public entity in California.  Plaintiff alleges that the City is vicariously liable for her Childhood Sexual Abuse, Battery, Ralph Act, and Bane Act claims against Assef.  Yet, none of these claims provide any statutory basis for a direct liability claim against the City.”

 

The Court is not clear on why Defendant is seeking adjudication of this issue.  The Court has reviewed the operative First Amended Complaint, and finds it never mentions direct liability.  At ¶38, found in the first cause of action, Plaintiff alleges the City is vicariously liable for Assef’s conduct.  Similarly, ¶¶44 (second cause of action), ¶55 (fourth cause of action), and ¶61 (fifth cause of action) all contain similar allegations.  The third cause of action is not pled against the City; it is only pled against Assef.

 

Because the FAC never mentions direct liability against the City, the Court finds the motion for summary adjudication is unnecessary and moot.  Additionally, it does not appear direct liability is an issue of “duty” such that it could properly be adjudicated.  The Court need not, however, make that determination, as no direct liability assertion is found anywhere in the FAC.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff argues, at page 22, lines 10-11, that she can prove both a vicarious and a direct liability theory against the City in connection with her Bane Act cause of action.  Again, however, she has not pled any such claim.  To the extent she intends to do so, she needs to seek leave to amend to assert such a claim.  The Court will determine the propriety of any such claim in connection with a motion for leave to amend, if one is filed.

Case Number: 23LBCV01617    Hearing Date: March 19, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Harvey Smith filed this action against Defendant, Sunrise Operations, LLC for damages arising out of a back injury he sustained while employed by Defendant.  He alleges he was working on the M/V Horizon Spirit, which is a vessel, and that his injuries are subject to the Jones Act.  He sues for maintenance and cure, punitive damages, and pre- and post-judgment interest. 

 

  1. Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he met and conferred multiple times with Plaintiff’s attorney prior to filing the demurrer. 

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant contends Plaintiff’s complaint is barred by the rule of res judicata and the rule against splitting a cause of action because Plaintiff previously brought the same claims in Federal Court, and then claims were tried by jury, resulting in a verdict in Plaintiff’s favor.  Plaintiff has appealed the verdict because the jury only apportioned 5% of Plaintiff’s damages to Defendant. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the demurrer.  He argues maritime causes of action require a standard of review that is deferential to the preservation of a seaman’s right to maintenance and cure.  He contends Defendant has not shown the federal, as opposed to state, elements of res judicata exist here.  He contends the federal judge presiding over the federal case excluded evidence related to the maintenance and cure claim, and suggested it should not be tried with the negligence and unseaworthiness claims; he contends neither the judge nor the jury ever made findings concerning the maintenance and cure claims.  He contends the rule against claim splitting does not apply here because the rule does not apply to the federal case, which is governed by federal law.  He contends, alternatively, that this court cannot apply a standard more strict than the federal law standard, and the federal law standard would not preclude his claims. 

 

In reply, Defendant contends both federal and state law bar claims that are precluded by res judicata, and that both federal and state law bar a plaintiff from splitting his claims.  Defendant contends nothing in the judicially noticeable documents submitted by Plaintiff show some sort of agreement, tacit or explicit, between Plaintiff and the federal judge permitting Plaintiff to withdraw his maintenance and cure claim and present it in a subsequent state court lawsuit. 

 

d.     Requests for Judicial Notice

Both parties seek judicial notice of various documents from the companion federal case.  The RJNs are granted, and the Court has considered all evidence from the federal case in ruling on this demurrer. 

 

e.     Facts

The basic relevant background facts are not disputed.  Plaintiff filed a federal lawsuit where he sought, among other damages, “maintenance and cure.” 

 

At the pretrial conference, the transcript of which is attached as Exhibit 2 to the RJN in opposition to the motion, the federal trial judge discussed maintenance and cure with the parties.  The specific issue was whether the parties could bring up insurance at the trial.  The purpose of bringing up insurance would be to show that the defendant has insurance, but the context was whether the defendant personally paid past maintenance and cure or whether the defendant’s insurance paid past maintenance and cure. 

 

The judge asked the parties about past maintenance and cure and also future maintenance and cure.  The judge indicated past maintenance and cure appeared to be moot because the defendant had paid it all, and future maintenance and cure did not appear to be at issue because the elements were not met.  Again, the real issue was whether insurance could be brought up, and the judge ruled it could not.  The judge noted, “If you’re objecting to them even saying they paid anything, then I guess where we’re at is that you should not be making a claim for maintenance and cure, and then they won’t be saying they paid it.” 

 

The Court then discussed the jury verdict form.  The plaintiff’s attorney, Brian Beckcom, stated, at page 742 of the transcript, that past maintenance and cure would probably be dropped, and that he couldn’t tell the Court there would be a future cure claim, so, “at least from my perspective, that the maintenance and cure, both past and future, goes away.”  The Court said OK, and Mr. Beckcom stated, “And we just have negligence and unseaworthiness.”  The Court said that would make a cleaner verdict form. 

 

f.      Analysis

Plaintiff contends the Court must apply the federal, rather than state, test for res judicata.  For purposes of this analysis, the Court is using the federal standard; the Court uses this standard without making an explicit determination of whether federal or state res judicata law applies, because the federal standard is more difficult to meet, and therefore the state standard would necessarily also be met if the federal standard were met.

Under federal law, the doctrine applies if the earlier litigation (1) reached a final judgment on the merits, (2) involved the same claim or cause of action as the later lawsuit, and (3) involved the same parties or their privies.  GP Vincent II v. Estate of Beard (9th Cir. 2023) 68 F.4th 508, 514.  Plaintiff contends the second element is not met here.  There are four factors to be considered to determine whether the second element is met.  They are:

 

1) whether rights or interests established in the prior judgment would be destroyed or impaired by prosecution of the second action; 

2) whether substantially the same evidence is presented in the two actions; 

3) whether the two suits involve infringement of the same right; and 

4) whether the two suits arise out of the same transactional nucleus of facts.

GP Vincent II v. Estate of Beard, supra, 68 F.4th at 515.

 

While Plaintiff twists the facts and analysis relating to the prior lawsuit into all manner of contortions, at the end of the day, it is impossible to see how this test is not met.  Plaintiff filed a prior lawsuit for maintenance and cure.  Plaintiff indicated, during the pretrial hearing, that maintenance and cure was no longer at issue.  The case then went to trial, and resulted in a final jury verdict. 

 

Plaintiff had many options other than conceding maintenance and cure was not at issue.  Plaintiff could have argued, at the pretrial hearing, that maintenance and cure had not been resolved and needed to be tried.  Plaintiff could have insisted on a ruling on the issue.  Plaintiff could have appealed any determination that maintenance and cure was no longer at issue.

 

Plaintiff argues Brooks v. Raymond Dugat Co. (5th Cir. 2003) 336 F.3d 360, 362 is persuasive.  In Brooks, the Court held that a plaintiff can sue a second time for maintenance and cure if the second lawsuit represents ongoing medical damages incurred after the conclusion of the first lawsuit.  Plaintiff admits, however, that this is not the case here, and that he is not suing for new and different medical damages, but instead for the same damages that already existed when he pursued his first lawsuit.

 

Additionally, the parties discuss the portion of Brooks holding that a plaintiff can NOT file a second lawsuit for maintenance and cure damages incurred prior to resolution of the prior lawsuit if the plaintiff voluntarily dismisses the prior lawsuit.  Plaintiff argues this holding is not relevant here because he never dismissed his maintenance and cure claim in the prior lawsuit.  He did, however, expressly disclaim the suit for maintenance and cure during the pretrial conference, indicating it was not at issue.  This is tantamount to dismissing the claim, even if there was never a formal dismissal. 

 

As Defendant correctly notes in reply, under federal law, res judicata acts to bar claims that were previously submitted and also claims that arise from the same transactional nucleus of facts.  See Turtle Island Restoration Network v. U.S. Dept. of State, 673 F.3d 914, 918 (9th Cir. 2012).  Plaintiff’s claims asserted in this case clearly, at a minimum, arise from the same transactional nucleus of facts as the claims he asserted in his prior federal lawsuit. 

 

The demurrer is sustained on the ground that res judicata bars the lawsuit.  Because this is a purely legal issue, leave to amend is denied.  The Court declines to rule on the issue of whether the complaint bars the rule against splitting a claim, as doing so is not necessary to a resolution of the merits of the demurrer.

 

  1. Motion for Sanctions

Defendant served Plaintiff with a motion for sanctions on 10/10/23.  When more than 21 days passed, as required by CCP §128.7(c), Defendant filed the motion with the Court.  The motion is made on the ground that Plaintiff’s lawsuit is clearly barred by the doctrine of res judicata and the law against splitting a claim, such that the lawsuit is frivolous and sanctions are appropriate.

 

The Court has read Plaintiff’s opposition to the demurrer and motion for sanctions and finds that, while the demurrer must ultimately be sustained, the issues are complex and Plaintiff took his position in good faith, such that the request for sanctions is denied. 

 

  1. Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.  The motion for sanctions is denied.  All future hearing dates are taken off calendar.
