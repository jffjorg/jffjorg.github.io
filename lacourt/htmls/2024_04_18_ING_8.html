DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00666    Hearing Date: April 18, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling 

 

HEARING DATE:   April 18, 2024

 

CASE NUMBER:   21TRCV00666 

 

CASE NAME: Sebastian Medvei v. Dominique Nikko, et al. 

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Sebastian Medvei  


RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant, Dominique Nikko Westmoreland 

 

TRIAL DATE: March 24, 2025  

 

MOTION: (1) Motion to Quash Deposition Subpoena 

 

Tentative Rulings: (1) GRANTED.  

(2)  The Court will discuss ordering the parties into a mandatory settlement conference given the fact that both parties appear to have engaged in host of litigation conduct disproportionate to the limited civil jurisdictional amount in controversy  

 


 

I. BACKGROUND

 

A. Factual 

 

On September 10, 2021, Plaintiff, Sebastian Medvei (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Dominque Nikko Westmoreland, and DOES 1 through 100. The Complaint alleges a cause of action for Malicious Prosecution.  

 

Plaintiff, as a lawyer of Medvei Law Group, APC, represented a party that sued one of Defendant’s clients, Brandi Linton, in Case No. 20STCV42456. Thereafter, in a subsequent case, 21STCV07905, Defendant, via his client Brandi Linton, filed a Complaint against Plaintiff, who is not an immigration consultant as defined in the Immigrations Consultants Act (“ICA”), for violating the ICA. Plaintiff informed Defendant that the ICA does not apply to attorneys. Plaintiff repeatedly asked Defendant to dismiss the case and Defendant refused. Eventually, Plaintiff served a motion for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 on Defendant. In response to that motion, Defendant voluntarily dismissed Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for Malicious Prosecution. 

 

Plaintiff’s moving papers assert that on February 23, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant, ordering the Defendant to sit for the deposition on March 5, 2024. Plaintiff notes that in opposition to that motion, Defendant had argued that the action was stayed and that he was unavailable on March 5, 2024 due to a lengthy trial he was involved in commencing on March 1, 2024. At that time, Plaintiff contends that Defendant had not provided a case number or any case information for the case that was set for trial on March 8, 2024.  

 

Per the moving papers, on February 27, 2024, Defendant filed an ex parte application seeking a stay of the action. The application was denied, except that the Court, based on Defendant’s affirmative representation to the Court that he was commencing a lengthy trial on March 1, 2024, changed the deposition date from March 5, 2024 to March 22, 2024. Plaintiff explained that the Court encouraged the parties “to discuss an earlier date for deposition if the trial defendant represents he will be engaged in does not go forth 3/1/24 or is delayed.” 

 

However, Plaintiff’s moving papers further assert that unbeknownst to the Court or to his counsel, on February 26, 2024, Defendant had already stipulated with his opposing counsel to continue the trial that was supposed to commence on March 1, 2024 to April 24, 2024. Plaintiff’s counsel discovered this after the February 27, 2024 hearing, and in keeping with the Court’s order to try and get the deposition done earlier, Plaintiff’s counsel contacted Defendant about proceeding with the deposition in person on March 8, 2024.  The moving papers claim that Defendant responded as follows: “You are stalker. Im [sic] going to get a restraining order against you.” (Declaration of Adrian Medvei (“AMED Decl.”), Exhibit 2.) Plaintiff further notes that ten minutes later, Defendant further provided: “You are a really loser [sic] tracking me. I will be filing a restraining order against you and your stalker son. And I feel as though I should report you both to the bar.” (AMED Decl., Exhibit 2.)  

 

Now, Plaintiff notes that as part of the aforementioned communications, Defendant issued the subpoena in question on this motion. Per the moving papers, the subpoena seeks to depose Non-Party Medvei Law Group, APC (the “Deponent”), the law corporation of Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel. Plaintiff notes that the subpoena seeks the personal appearance of the person most qualified (“PMQ”) at the Deponent and the production of the following documents: “Payroll records & statements pertaining to Sebastian Medvei.” The subpoena also asked the Deponent to produce a PMQ to testify as to “Sebastian Medvei [sic]… employment at Medvei Law Group, wages, job duties.” Plaintiff further notes that the subpoena was not personally served on an authorized agent or officer for the Deponent (AMED Decl., ¶ 4.) 
 

As such, Plaintiff has filed this Motion to Quash the Subpoena as to Medvei Law Group, APC.  

 

B. Procedural


On March 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Quash. On April 4, 2024, Defendant Westmoreland filed an opposition brief. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.  

 

II. ANALYSIS 

 

  1. Legal Standard  

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 1987.1 grants the trial court authority to quash a subpoena when necessary. Code of Civil Procedure § 1987.1 provides: “If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1987.1(b), “[t]he following persons may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a): (1) A party; (2) A witness; (3) A consumer described in Section 1985.3; (4) An employee described in Section 1985.6; and (5) A person whose personally identifying information, as defined in subdivision (b) of Section 1798.79.8 of the Civil Code, is sought in connection with an underlying action involving that person’s exercise of free speech rights.  

 

  1. Discussion   

 

Service 

 

First, Plaintiff argues that the subpoena was not personally served on the Deponent, non-party, Medvei Law Group, APC. Plaintiff notes, as does the process server, that the subpoena was served to Elena Stepanyan, who is a front desk/receptionist. Plaintiff argues that Ms. Stepanyan is not an employee of Medvei Law group and is not, in any event, an employee who is authorized to accept service of a subpoena on Medvei Law Group’s behalf. As such, Plaintiff argues that pursuant to the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2020.220(b)(2), which states: “[a]ny person may serve the subpoena by personal delivery of a copy of it as follows: (2) if the deponent is an organization, to any officer, director, custodian of records, or to any agent or employee authorized by the organization to accept service of a subpoena.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.220(b)(2). Because Ms. Stepanyan is none of these people identified, Plaintiff argues that service was improper.  

 

Here, Defendant attempts to argue that service of this subpoena was proper as Medvei Law Group is a party-affiliate pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.280(a). However, the Defendant fails to establish how this is. Specifically, Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.280(a) states, “The service of a deposition notice under section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280.) The Court is unaware how a law firm could be identified as an “officer, director, managing agent, or employee” of Plaintiff, Sebastian Medvei.  

 

As such, this Court finds that service of the subpoena was improper, without reaching the issue of the propriety of Defendant’s seeking to depose the opposing counsel of record in apparent retaliation for seeking to re-schedule Defendant’s own Court-ordered deposition.  

 

Information Sought by the Subpoena  

 

Although failure to properly serve a subpoena would be grounds alone for granting this motion to quash, this Court also discusses the information sought by said subpoena in order to give Defendant Westmoreland guidance for a potential future subpoena. As noted in the moving papers, the subpoena requests from the Deponent, Medvei Law Group, to produce payroll records and statements pertaining to the Plaintiff, Sebastian Medvei. Plaintiff argues that this is a case for malicious prosecution as against Defendants, and that he is not seeking damages for lost wages, and thus, the items sought would be irrelevant.  

 

However, as noted by Plaintiff in his reply brief, he is not seeking “business losses” and has even conceded in discovery that he was not aware of any. Plaintiff also argues that his wages and other financial data are irrelevant to his claim for noneconomic damages in the form of inconvenience and annoyance. As argued by Plaintiff, “’[n]oneconomic damages compensate an injured plaintiff for nonpecuniary injuries. . . . [Citation.]’” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal. App. 5th 276, 300.) Here, the Court agrees. The Court is not persuaded that the items sought by the subpoena would amount to relevant evidence as to the noneconomic damages sought by Plaintiff. Without further persuasive argument, the Court would be inclined to find that the subpoena may be quashed because it does not seek relevant information that would outweigh privacy information.   

 

Moreover, the Court recently granted Defenant’s motion to reclassify this case, which rested in large part on Plaintiff’s discovery responses that essentially disclaimed and economic damages aside from the attorneys fees allegedly paid to resolve the underlying action.  Just as Plaintiff will be bound by those discovery responses to the limited scope of economic damages claimed here (should this case proceed to trial), the scope of Defendant’s discovery to Plaintiff will similarly be limited.    

 

Sanctions 

 

In Plaintiff’s moving papers, he seeks to recover his expenses for filing this motion. In opposition, Defendant also argues that they are entitled to monetary sanction for Plaintiff’s allegedly filing a frivolous motion.  At the time this motion was filed, the Court had not yet considered or ruled on the motion to reclassify or reviewed the Plaintiff’s verified discovery responses addressing the categories of claimed damages in this matter.  As of earlier this week, this case is now reclassified as a limited civil matter.  The Court does not find good cause to award monetary sanctions to either side at this time, and gives notice to each side that the Court will look with substantial skepticism at future requests or cross-requests for monetary sanctions on discovery matters.  No party will profit from making or opposing a discovery motion and the Court will limit its future consideration of sanctions requests by the stringent “substantial justification” standard in the Discovery Act.   


For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion is GRANTED, but monetary sanctions are not awarded to either party. Plaintiff is ordered to give written notice of this ruling, to include attaching this tentative ruling.

HEARING DATE: April 18, 2024


CASE NUMBER: 22TRCV01128


CASE NAME: Maurice Stephenson v. Leo Ramsey, et al.

ATTORNEY NAME: Defendants, Federal and State Income Tax Services, LLC and Leo Ramsey’s attorney, Robert M. Moss

TRIAL DATE: February 24, 2025


MOTION: (1) Motion to be Relieved as Counsel


Tentative Rulings: (1) GRANTED. In giving notice of the Court’s ruling, Mr. Moss must notify his clients that an LLC cannot represent itself in California courts without a lawyer

I. Background

On November 3, 2022, Plaintiff, Maurice Stephenson (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants, Leo Ramsey, Federal and State Income Tax Services, LLC, and DOES 1 through 50. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Breach of Oral Contract; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Intentional Misrepresentation; (5) Intentional Misrepresentation; (6) Unjust Enrichment; (7) Negligence; (8) Declaratory Relief; and (9) Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq.

On March 19, 2024, Defendants, Federal and State Income Tax Services, LLC and Leo Ramsey’s attorney, Robert M. Moss (“Moss”) filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for both of the Defendants.

II. Legal Standard & Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure § 284 states that “the attorney in an action…may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination, as follows: (1) upon the consent of both client and attorney…; (2) upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 284; CRC 3.1362.) The withdrawal request may be denied if it would cause an injustice or undue delay in proceeding; but the court's discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably. (See Mandell v. Superior (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4; Lempert¿v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)

In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court 3.1362. The motion must be made using mandatory forms: Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – Civil (MC-051); Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the

attorney-client relationship” reasons the motion was brought (MC-052); and a Proposed Order (MC-053). (Ibid.) The forms must be filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case. (Ibid.)

Here, Defendants’ counsel, Moss, moves the Court to relieve him as attorney of record for Defendants, Federal and State Income Tax Services, LLC and Robert M. Moss properly filed a Notice of Motion, Motion to be Relieved as Counsel, Declaration, and Proposed Order in accordance with Cal. Rules of Court 3.1362. On March 19, 2024, all forms for the pending motion were served on Defendants by mail. Additionally, the moving papers demonstrate that Moss has confirmed via telephone that Defendants’ addresses were current in the past thirty (30) days. Further, on March 19, 2024, proof of service for said documents were filed with the Court.

In the declaration Moss notes that “there has been an irreparable breakdown in communication between the undersigned attorney (Robert M. Moss of the Law Offices of Robert M. Moss, Inc.) and Defendants Leo Ramsey and Federal & State Income Tax Services LLC (the defendants in the within action). This breakdown in communication has made it impossible for the undersigned attorney to continue to represent Defendant Leo Ramsey [and Federal & State Tax Services in this action].”

Since Defendants’ attorney has complied with all procedural requirements in filing a motion to be relieved as counsel and because the withdrawal would not cause an injustice or undue delay in proceedings, the Court finds that withdrawal of Robert M. Moss as attorney of record for Defendants, Federal & State Income Tax Services LLC and Leo Ramsey can be accomplished without undue prejudice to their interests.

III. Conclusion & Order

For the foregoing reasons, Robert M. Moss, Motion to Be Relieved As Counsel is GRANTED and the Order will be signed at the hearing. “After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).) The Order on this Motion will not be effective “until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on Plaintiff and Defendant has been filed with the court.” (Id.)

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

 

This lawsuit is based on Plaintiffs alleged suffering years of mistreatment at the hands of their uphill-neighbor in Rolling Hills, Nourit Korzennik, Korzennik’s alleged misconduct towards the Nakamuras included, but was not limited to: spitting on Mrs. Nakamura; mooning Mrs. Nakamura and her family; performing sexually offensive and lewd gestures toward the Nakamuras; illegally trespassing onto the Nakamura’s property twice and chopping down their trees that spanned 365 feet; trapping the Nakamuras in their car; photographing and recording the Nakamuras and their family, along with workers and visitors without their consent, including by the use of drones; harassing and preventing utility and other workers from entering the Nakamura property – going so far as to hit a worker with her car – promoting a call to the Los Angeles County Sherriff’s Office on this and many other occasions; Sherriff’s Officers advising the Nakamuras to obtain a restraining order on multiple occasions to protect their personal safety; Korennik’s multiple allegedly frivolous lawsuits against the Nakamuras; and claimed abuse of the administrative process to block the rebuilding of the Nakamura family home that burned in a fire over a decade ago.

 

On December 13, 2023, this Court heard Plaintiffs’ Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement, which required Defendant Nourit Korzennik to move out of 26 Chinchring Rd., Rolling Hills, CA 90274. This Court GRANTED that motion, stayed the liquidated damages through January 1, 2024, and ordered that Defendant vacate by January 1, 2024, or be subject to the liquidated damages amount per violation, and $2,500 in reasonable attorney’s fees. This order, in reference to the liquidated damages, noted that for violating the settlement agreement, Defendant would be required to pay $1,000 per violation to Plaintiffs commencing January 2, 2024. Prior to the matter being dismissed and pursuant to the settlement, the parties filed with the Court a stipulation to retain jurisdiction pursuant to Code fo Civil Procedure section 664.6.

 

As of the filing of the moving papers more than 60 days into calendar year 2024, Plaintiffs assert that Defendant Korzennik remains at 26 Cinchring. The declaration of Jeffrey Lewis, counsel for Plaintiffs, notes that on January 11, 2024, he received an email from Defendant’s counsel indicating that she had not vacated 26 Cinchring. (Declaration of Jeffrey Lewis (“Lewis Decl.”), ¶ 8.) As such, Plaintiffs have brought this motion re: contempt for violation of the prior order on the parties’ settlement agreement.

 

            B. Procedural

 

            On March 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this Motion for Contempt against Defendant Nourit Korzennik. On April 5, 2024, Defendant Korzennik filed an opposition brief. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply brief.

 

II. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

IV. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

Civil Contempt procedures are set forth in Code of Civil Procedure §§ 1209 through 1222. (See Allison v. County of Ventura (1977) 68 Cal. App. 3d 689, 702.)  Under these rules, a proceeding for adjudication of constructive contempt is customarily initiated by the issuance of an Order to Show Cause.  (In re Rose (1949) 90 Cal. App. 2d 299, 305.)  The proceeding is also initiated by the filing of an affidavit or declaration, stating the facts constituting the alleged contempt, in order to confer jurisdiction upon the court to exercise its contempt powers over the accused.  (In re Liu (1969) 273 Cal. App. 2d 135, 140-41.) 

 

Since a contempt proceeding is quasi-criminal in nature, the accused person is entitled to due process, or the procedural safeguards available to a person accused of a criminal charge, including the right to know the exact nature of the charge against him/her so that he/she can prepare and present his/her defense.  (Ibid. at 141.)  The accused must be shown, beyond a reasonable doubt, that he/she had the ability to comply with the subject court order; the burden of proof on this issue lies with the party seeking to have the accused held in contempt.  (Ibid. at 141-42.) 

 

Contempt is any act, in or out of court, “which tends to impede, embarrass or obstruct the court in the discharge of its duties.”  (In re Shortridge (1893) 99 Cal. 526, 532.)  An “indirect contempt” occurs out of court but is equally subject to the summary punishment set forth in Code of Civil Procedure § 1211: “When the contempt is not committed in the immediate view and presence of the court, or of the judge at chambers, an affidavit shall be presented to the court or judge of the facts constituting the contempt, or a statement of the facts by the referees or arbitrators, or other judicial officers.”  (Arthur v. Sup.Ct. of Los Angeles County (1965) 62 Cal.2d 404, 407-408.)  Thereafter, an order to show cause must be issued and the court must hold a hearing on the facts.  (Ibid. at 408.) Since the acts involved in the alleged contempt in this action did not occur in the Court’s presence, the affidavit must cover each element of the commission of the contempt.  (Code Civ Proc. § 1211.5.)  If necessary, the court may order or permit amendment of the affidavits at any stage of the proceedings.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1211.5(b).)  

 

The elements of contempt are: (1) facts establishing court’s jurisdiction (e.g., personal service of subpoena, validity of court order allegedly violated, etc.); (2) the party’s knowledge of the order disobeyed; (3) the party’s ability to comply; and (4) the party’s willful disobedience of the order.  (Koehler v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1153, 1169; In re Jones (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 879, 881.)   Furthermore, the order must be personally served on the party to establish jurisdiction.  (Cedars-Sinai Imaging Medical Group v. Superior Court, (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1281.) 

 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiffs assert that they have brought this motion because as of January 11, 2024, counsel for Plaintiff received an email from Defense counsel indicating that Defendant had not vacated her home, as required by the parties’ settlement agreement that carried with it the imprimatur of the Court’s December 13, 2023 order. Thus, Plaintiffs are seeking to enforce the liquidated damages in $1,000 per day starting on January 2, 2023.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that she vacated her home prior to Plaintiffs’ filing of this motion and thus, Plaintiffs did not meet their burden for a contempt order. Defendant’s opposition notes that as of February 6, 2024, Defendant Korzennik no longer resides in her home at 26 Cinchring. Defendant notes that Korzennik began the move out process, upon selecting a residential room and board care home for elderly people that had availability by December 20, 2023, with good faith deposits for the accommodations made by December 31, 2023. However, Defendant notes the facility required medical and financial assessments, reports from Korzennik’s physician and an assessment to be conducted by the facility’s nurse, in addition to refurbishing the room. Defendant asserts that these steps took the month of January 2024, resulting in the move out beginning on February 2, 2024.  Korzennik asserts she completed her move into her new home as of February 6, 2024. Defense counsel notes that he advised Plaintiffs’ attorney, in the January 11, 2024 email that the move out would be completed, but because of what The Canterbury residential care facilityneeded, it would not be completed until the end of January, but forwarded to Plaintiffs’ attorney a payment of $1,000 for not meeting the January 2, 2024 deadline, in addition to attorney’s fees of $2,500 as ordered by the Court’s December 13, 2023 court order.

 

Further, Defendant notes in the opposition papers that more than a month after Korzennik moved out, Plaintiffs, on March 7, 2024, filed this current motion for an order to show cause (OSC) for contempt. Defendant highlights that Plaintiffs did not move for an order to enforce the settlement agreement contract, or for statutory sanctions. Instead, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ motion seeks an injunction, plus contractual damages, based on the issuance of an OSC and contempt hearing. The Court is confused as to Defendant’s arguments and discussion around Code of Civil Procedure section 1218 as Plaintiffs have not moved under section 1218.

 

As noted above, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1211.5, Plaintiffs were required to provide an affidavit meeting all of the following elements of contempt: (1) facts establishing court’s jurisdiction (e.g., personal service of subpoena, validity of court order allegedly violated, etc.); (2) the party’s knowledge of the order disobeyed; (3) the party’s ability to comply; and (4) the party’s willful disobedience of the order.  (Koehler v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1153, 1169; In re Jones (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 879, 881.) Here, as noted in the Lewis Declaration, the parties agreed that this Court would maintain jurisdiction (Lewis Decl., ¶ 5.) Next, Defendant and Defense counsel were aware of the order to move out by December 31, 2023 as defense counsel attended the December 13, 2023 order and as evidenced by the declarations from Defendant herself in opposition to the Motion to Enforce Settlement. Third, the issue of the Defendant’s ability to comply may be argued. Compliance with this Court’s order did not hinge on whether Defendant was able to relocate to The Canterbury facility, which Defendant has informed took a month or so to get into. The order required Defendant to leave the home by December 31, 2023. As such, the issue of whether Defendant should have stayed somewhere else while The Canterbury was approving her is in question as to the ability to comply issue.

 

Lastly, as to the willful violation issue, this Court notes that Defendant argues she did not willfully violate the Court order as she and her son took immediate steps to have her move out, but that the process took more time than expected. Here, as the Court noted in the December 13, 2023 Minute Order, it is not persuaded by the argument that Defendant be permitted to find a suitable home before vacating the premises. In fact, the Court expressly stated that even if into a temporary lodging circumstance, Plaintiff was to move out pursuant to the binding and enforceable settlement agreement. Here, this Court finds that even if Defendant was waiting for approval to move to The Canterbury, she could have stayed in a hotel, a short-term Airbnb, with a family member, etc. Failure to do so expressly violated the binding and enforceable settlement agreement between the parties.

 

As to the issue of contempt and Plaintiffs filing this after Defendant had already moved out, Plaintiffs note in their reply brief that they were not put on notice that Defendant had vacated the premises and that in advance of this motion, which was served on February 15, 2024, the Plaintiffs asked Defense counsel for specifics about whether, and what date Defendant moved out. However, after receiving no answer about those specifics, Plaintiffs contend that this motion was filed. In the interest of judicial efficiency, this Court does not require Plaintiffs to file a separate motion to enforce their settlement agreement again. Also, even if not for the move-out of Defendant, the contempt hearing can also flow from the fact that this Court ordered $1,000 in liquidated damages to be paid each day of Defendant’s violation. The Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement that was GRANTED by this Court included the parties’ fully-signed agreement to pay daily liquidated damages of $1,000. As such, the parties both noting that on January 11, 2024, $1,000 was paid to Plaintiffs’ counsel is already a violation of the Court order as by January 11, 2024, Defendant had already accrued $10,000 in liquidated damages to be paid to the Plaintiffs for failure to move out sooner.

 

This Court will not enforce the liquidated damages clause, or impose contempt sanctions for the violations for the months of September, October, and November as requested for the first time in the reply brief. However, the Court will allow oral argument as to the $1,000 per day breach since January 2,2024. The Court’s tentative ruling is to GRANT the motion, either as an enforcement of the parties’ settlement agreement or as a civil contempt, in the amount of $1,000 per day for Defendant’s failure to move out by January 1, 2024 and liquidated damages beginning on January 2, 2024.  The tentative amount is $35,000 in liquidated damages between January 2, 2024 and February 6, 2024, less the $1,000 already paid. Defendant made an economic decision, with the advice of counsel, to pay $1,000 per day for each day she would not relinquish possession of the premises.  The Court’s tentative ruling is to enforce this portion of the settlement agreement per the agreed upon terms, and was not persuaded by Defendant’s argument that she must have located suitable alternative living arrangements before moving out of 26 Cinchring Rd.  Because of the dollar amount involved, the Court will entertain discussion of a payment plan or extension of time for Defendant to make the sanctions / contempt / breach of settlement payment. 

 

Attorney’s Fees

 

As for attorneys’ fees, counsel for Plaintiffs contends that since the December 13, 2023 hearing, he has spent one hour drafting this motion and declaration, and anticipates spending an additional one hour reviewing the opposition and preparing a reply brief. He also contends that he plans to spend three (3) hours to attend the hearing. As noted in the Court’s December 13, 2023 minute order, the Court finds that the amount of hours to attend the hearing to be excessive and tentatively reduces the number of hours claimed from 5 to 3.  The Court also tentatively reduces Mr. Lewis’ hourly rate to $700. 3 times $700 is $2,100. As such, the Court GRANTS attorneys fees in the lowered amount of $2,100 against Korzennik to be paid to Plaintiffs’ counsel Mr. Lewis by May 9, 2024.
