DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20LBCV00504    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Tokio Marine America Insurance Company filed this action against Defendant, Prestig, Inc. for carrier liability under the Carmack Amendment.  Plaintiff filed its original complaint on 11/19/20 and its operative First Amended Complaint on 1/19/21. 

 

Plaintiff alleges it insured Panasonic Corporation.  Defendant received a shipment of 1024 cartons of lithium ion batteries from Panasonic in Carson and was charged with taking the shipment to Kansas City.  However, the rig carrying the batteries was involved in a single vehicle accident, resulting in the vehicle over-turning and damaging the batteries.  Plaintiff alleges it suffered loss in the amount of $620,199.27, as it had to pay its insured for the damage to its goods.

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

  1. Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant, with its moving papers, seeks judicial notice of ten various documents.  The Court takes judicial notice of the fact that these documents are what they purport to be, but not of the truth of the matter asserted in any of the documents. 

 

  1. Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff filed evidentiary objections with its opposition papers.  The first objection is overruled, but again, the Court is considering the document only as evidence that it is what it purports to be, and not as evidence of the truth asserted in the document. 

 

The remaining objections are overruled. 

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

Defendant moves for summary judgment on the complaint, contending (a) the Coverage of Goods at Sea Act (“COGSA”) and not the Carmack Amendment governs the present action, and thus the sole cause of action pled under the Carmack Amendment cannot support imposition of liability, and (b) any potential COGSA claim would be barred by the contractual claim presentation provision in the subject waybill and the statute of limitations in COGSA.  Alternatively, it seeks summary adjudication of the issue of liability, specifically what constitutes a “package” under the subject waybill.

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, argues the moving papers erroneously rely on waybill ONEDL9CK0425400, when the waybill that governs the parties’ case is ONEDL9CK0425401.  Plaintiff contends Defendant has not submitted admissible evidence showing that waybill ONEDL9CK0425400 applies, and even if it has, there are triable issues concerning whether ONEDL9CK0425401, as opposed to ONEDL9CK0425400, applies.  It contends the request for summary adjudication is procedurally improper because it would not fully dispose of a cause of action, issue of duty, or affirmative defense.

 

Defendant, in reply, contends it set forth sufficient evidence to establish ONEDL9CK0425400 applies, and Plaintiff’s evidence to the contrary fails to raise a triable issue of material fact.  Alternatively, it contends that, even if ONEDL9CK0425401 applies, liability under that waybill is also governed by COGSA and cannot support a claim under Carmack. 

 

  1.  ONEDL9CK0425400 or ONEDL9CK0425401

The threshold question before the Court is whether waybill ONEDL9CK0425400 or ONEDL9CK0425401 applies to this action.  Defendant contends ONEDL9CK0425400 applies, and Plaintiff contends ONEDL9CK0425401 applies. 

 

The Court finds there are triable issues of material fact in this regard.  As Plaintiff correctly notes in opposition, Defendant’s own president, Lubomir Anguelov, on 2/18/21 and in connection with this action, provided a declaration stating that the subject goods were delivered pursuant to waybill ONEDL9CK0425401.  Defendant, in reply, provides extensive analysis of why ONEDL9CK0425401 is not the correct waybill; this analysis, however, only further shows why there are issues of material fact to be determined in this regard, which issues are not suitable for summary judgment. 

 

  1. ONEDL9CK0425401

Defendant, in reply, argues that ONEDL9CK0425401, even if it does apply, contains the same language as ONEDL9CK0425400, and therefore can only support liability pursuant to COGSA, not the Carmack Amendment.  It cites Clause 26.1 of ONEDL9CK0425401 in support of its position.  The Court, in searching through the parties’ evidence, cannot locate Clause 26.1 in connection with waybill ONEDL9CK0425401.  The only place the Court can locate Clause 26.1 is at page 188 of the document entitled Declaration of Glazier.  Clause 26.1 is cut off.  Additionally, it is not clear that Clause 26.1 is part of waybill ONEDL9CK0425401.  Clause 26.1 is part of Exhibit F to the Anguelov Declaration, dated 2/17/21, which is Exhibit H to the Declaration of Glazier.  Anguelov declares, at ¶7, that Exhibit A is a copy of waybill ONEDL9CK0425401, which forms the basis of this lawsuit.  He then authenticates Exhibits B, C, D, and E to his declaration.  He never mentions Exhibit F anywhere in his declaration.  There is, therefore, no evidence submitted to establish that Clause 26.1 is a part of waybill ONEDL9CK0425401. 

 

  1. Conclusion

Defendant’s entire argument is that the parties’ relationship is governed by waybill ONEDL9CK0425400, which contains clause 26.1, and/or that even if it is governed by waybill ONEDL9CK0425401, that waybill stills contains clause 26.1, and therefore the COGSA, not the Carmack Amendment, applies.  Because there are triable issues of material fact concerning whether waybill ONEDL9CK0425400 or ONEDL9CK0425401 governs the parties’ relationship, and because Defendant failed to meet its burden to show waybill ONEDL9CK0425401 contains clause 26.1, the motion for summary judgment is denied.

Defendant seeks summary adjudication in the alternative.  Defendant’s notice of motion indicates it seeks adjudication as follows:

If for any reason summary judgment cannot be granted, Defendant PTI further moves separately or as an alternative to its Motion for Summary Judgment, for an order granting its Motion for Summary Adjudication that establishes in favor of PTI that: PTI is entitled to a summary adjudication on the issue of liability, to wit, what constitutes a ‘Package’ under the ONE Sea Waybill. Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, correctly notes that Defendant’s stated issue is not the type of issue that can be adjudicated pursuant to CCP §437c(f), which provides, “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”

 

Defendant contends, in reply, that Plaintiff failed to address the issue and therefore waived any response.  Defendant is incorrect.  Plaintiff addressed the issue, correctly noting that it is not appropriate for summary adjudication per Code.  The alternative summary adjudication motion is denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV00209    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Babken Gabrielyan filed this action against Defendants, SAI Long Beach B, Inc. dba Long Beach BMW, Brooke Ashley Baker, and Rita Regalado for damages arising out of an automobile accident.  There are cross-complaints and a consolidated action arising out of the same accident. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

  1. Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

Regalado filed a request for judicial notice of Plaintiff’s complaint and Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file an amended complaint.  The RJN is unnecessary, as the Court looks to all papers filed in the action when ruling on a summary judgment motion.

 

  1. Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff filed an objection to SAI’s separate statement.  The objection is overruled.  Both opposing parties filed objections to SAI’s submitted evidence with the moving papers.  SAI filed objections to both parties’ evidence submitted in opposition to the motion.  The ruling on the motion does not turn on a ruling on the objections, and therefore the Court declines to rule on them.  CCP §437c(q).

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

SAI moves for summary judgment on the complaint, contending its employee, Defendant Baker, was on a break at the time of the incident and doing a personal errand such that vicarious liability does not apply. 

 

Plaintiff and Co-Defendant Regalado oppose the motion.  They argue (a) the complaint alleges both vicarious and direct liability, and SAI did not defeat the direct liability claim, (b) to the extent the complaint cannot be read to state a direct liability claim, Plaintiff has a pending motion for leave to amend to add a direct liability claim, and (c) SAI is vicariously liable for Baker’s accident because Baker was moving the car at the direction or and/or for the benefit of SAI. 

 

SAI, in reply, argues direct liability is not pled, and Plaintiff cannot amend the complaint at this late stage of the proceedings to avoid summary judgment.  It contends the undisputed facts show Baker was moving her car for her own personal purposes and not for its benefit or at its direction. 

  1.  Undisputed Facts

The basic facts relating to this case are not in dispute.  Baker was an employee of SAI at the time of the subject collision.  Baker was provided employee parking in the SAI parking lot, but the parking was tandem, and sometimes it was difficult to leave in the evening due to the nature of the parking.  She was not required to park in the employee lot, and was allowed to park on the street.  On the morning of the incident, she parked in the employee lot, but she used her ten-minute break to move her vehicle to the street because she was planning to leave early that day for vacation and did not want to be blocked into the lot by another employee’s vehicle.  While moving the vehicle, the parties were involved in the subject collision.    

 

  1. Vogt v. Herron Construction, Inc.

The Court will consider the last issue first – whether Plaintiff’s claim for respondent superior liability presents factual issues that should go to the jury.  The parties discuss the holding of Vogt v. Herron Construction, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App4th 643 in connection with this issue.  Vogt is a comprehensive opinion and the Court has reviewed it at length. 

 

In Vogt, there were subcontractors working on a construction project.  Cruz worked for a framing contractor and parked his personal car at the worksite.  Nobody told him where to park.  Vogt worked for a concrete subcontractor.  Vogt asked Cruz to move his truck so they could pour concrete.  Cruz did so, and in the process ran over Vogt.  Cruz’s employer, Herron, argued there could be no respondent superior-based liability because it did not direct Cruz to move his truck and it did not benefit from Cruz moving his truck. 

 

The trial court granted summary judgment, but the court of appeals reversed.  It held that acts necessary to the comfort, convenience, health, and welfare of an employee while at work, though strictly personal and not acts of service, would not take the employee out of the scope of employment.  It held that, when an employee’s act benefits both the employee and also the employer, the employee is not outside the scope of employment. 

 

It distinguished the facts of the case from the facts in Bailey v. Filco, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1552, the primary case upon which Moving Defendant SAI relies in this case.  In Bailey, the employee used her 10- to 15-minute break to go and purchase cookies to bring back to work.  The jury found the employee was not in the scope of her employment, and the court of appeals affirmed.  The court of appeals found the deviation substantial because the employee did more than just stop her work for a minute to take a bite of a cookie, but instead, during a break, got in her own car and drove off premises into a four-lane thoroughfare to purchase cookies to eat back at work. 

 

The Cruz Court went on, however, to discuss and distinguish Avila v. Standard Oil Co. (1986) 167 Cal.App.4th 441, 444-445 from Bailey.  In Avila, two employees worked at a gas station.  One employee had supervisorial permission to keep his motorcycle at the station, and to fix it up there on days off; the employees were not, however, permitted to work on the motorcycle during work days.  During work, one of the employees left the station to buy wire to work on the motorcycle.  The employee got into an accident.  The trial court granted summary judgment in favor of the employer, but the court of appeals reversed.  It held the test was foreseeability, and noted that the employee’s conduct was not so unusual or startling as to preclude employer liability.

 

The instant case is not on all fours with any of the above cases.  The Court finds, however, that it is more closely analogous to Cruz and Avila than it is to Bailey.  The employee was acting for her own comfort, but was doing so in a way that was foreseeable in light of the fact that her car could otherwise be blocked in at the end of the day when she needed to leave quickly.  The tandem parking clearly served a benefit to the employer, because it could save more convenient parking spaces for customers.  Importantly, Bailey was decided not on summary judgment, but instead after a jury trial.  The Court finds it is for the jury to determine whether Baker, in this case, so substantially deviated from her job duties when she moved her car as to preclude liability, or whether her acts were sufficiently foreseeable and related to her work duties so as to impose liability. 

 

  1. Direct Liability

Because the Court finds there are triable issues of material fact concerning vicarious liability, the Court declines to determine whether Plaintiff pled a direct liability claim and/or should be permitted leave to do so.  The Court notes that Plaintiff has a motion for leave to amend scheduled for hearing on 9/17/24, which is after the trial date of 9/03/24.  The Court is not making any ruling relating to the motion for leave to amend at this time. 

 

  1. Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication

SAI seeks summary adjudication in the alternative.  Specifically, it seeks summary adjudication of each of the two causes of action (negligence and negligence per se) pled in the complaint.  It does not provide any additional argument in support of his MSA over and above the argument in support of its MSJ.  The alternative motion for summary adjudication is therefore also denied.

 

  1. Final Note

SAI sought summary judgment/adjudication not only on the complaint, but also on Regalado’s consolidated complaint and on the cross-complaints of Regalado, Gabrielyan, and Navasardyan. 

 

In its reply, it contends the only oppositions filed were by Gabrielyan in his capacity as a plaintiff and Regalado in her capacity as a co-defendant.  It therefore argues the motion should be granted as to Regalado’s consolidated complaint, as well as all cross-complaints.  The Court finds the triable issues of material fact presented in the two sets of opposition papers filed by Gabrielyan and Regalado apply equally to all complaint and cross-complaints against SAI, and therefore the motion is denied in its entirety.

2.     Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

            Defendant seeks to recover fees in the amount of $6336.80.  He supports his request with the Declaration of Philip C. Zvonicek, one of the attorneys who has represented him in his defense of this action.  Zvonicek attaches, as Exhibits C and D respectively, the billing statements and summary of billing in this matter.  The Court has examined the billing statements and finds the hours billed and hourly rates are prima facie reasonable.  In the absence of opposition, the Court finds Defendant is entitled to recover the full amount of fees sought by way of this motion. 

 

d.     Conclusion

Defendant’s motion for attorneys’ fees is granted.  Defendant is awarded his fees incurred in the requested amount of $6336.80.  The Court will interlineate the judgment to reflect the award of fees.

Case Number: 23LBCV00552    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Marcus Miller and Angie Muhamad filed this action against Defendants, Brand Scaffold Services, Inc. and The AES Corporation for damages arising out of injuries sustained in a workplace accident at AES’s power plant in Puerto Rico.  Miller, who is a California resident and employee of a California corporation, was injured in the incident. 

 

2.     Motion to Quash

The Court was previously scheduled to hear this motion on 5/02/24.  Prior to the hearing, the Court issued a tentative ruling denying the motion.  After oral argument, the Court continued the hearing on the motion to permit the parties to brief (a) Defendants’ waiver of the statute of limitations, and (b) Plaintiff’s medical treatment in California.  The purpose of this further briefing was to assess the forum non conveniens aspect of the motion.

 

Defendants submitted a further brief on 5/28/24, wherein they argue that imposition of personal jurisdiction over them is improper.  They indicate they will not waive the one-year statute of limitations in Puerto Rice on personal injury claims.  Plaintiff submitted a further brief on 6/07/24, wherein they correctly note that Defendants were NOT asked to brief the Court’s analysis re: imposition of personal jurisdiction, but were ONLY asked to brief the two limited forum non conveniens issues.  Plaintiff contends that, in light of Defendants’ overt statement that they will not waive the one-year statute of limitations in Puerto Rico, the Court cannot dismiss the claim based on forum non conveniens.  Additionally, Plaintiff provides evidence re: medical treatment in California.  On 6/14/24, Defendants submitted another brief, wherein they again argue the Court’s analysis re: imposition of personal jurisdiction was wrong.  They again indicate they will not waive the statute of limitations in Puerto Rico, and they concede the case cannot be dismissed based on forum non conveniens grounds in light of their refusal.  On 6/21/24, Plaintiff submitted a reply to the 6/14/24 brief, again correctly noting that Defendants focused on issues the Court did not ask them to brief.

 

The Court incorporates its 5/02/24 analysis re: the motion to quash due to lack of personal jurisdiction into this order in full and denies the motion to quash.  The Court also incorporates its analysis re: forum non conveniens into this order in full; because Defendants will not agree to waive the one-year statute of limitations in Puerto Rico, Puerto Rico is not a suitable alternative forum, and the Court denies the alternative motion to dismiss based on the doctrine of forum non conveniens.  The motion is denied in its entirety.

Case Number: 23LBCV00678    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: S27

Plaintiffs filed this action against Defendants for damages arising out of an automobile accident.  The parties have agreed to a global settlement of the entire lawsuit for the total amount of $30,000.  If the settlement is approved, $7500 will be allocated to each of the two minor plaintiffs, and the remaining $15,000 will be allocated to their father and GAL. 

 

With respect to Mason’s proposed settlement, $393.65 would be used for medical expenses, $1766.11 for attorneys’ fees, and $435.53 for costs of suit, leaving $4904.71 to be placed in a blocked account.  Mason suffered from headaches, chest pain, nausea, back pain, and shoulder pain as a result of the accident, but he has completely recovered from all injuries.

 

With respect to Broden’s proposed settlement, $356.56 would be used for medical expenses, $1762.70 for attorneys’ fees, and $449.18 for costs of suit, leaving $4931.56 to be placed in a blocked account.  Broden suffered from headaches, dizziness, tinnitus, nausea, knee pain, and chest pain as a result of the accident, but he has completely recovered from all injuries.

 

The Court has two minor concerns with the petitions, each of which must be addressed at the hearing before the Court will grant the petitions.  First, ¶11 in each petition appears to include erroneous information.  It indicates Petitioner is not a plaintiff in the action and would not receive money under the settlement.  Petitioner/GAL, Brian Bundies, however, IS a plaintiff in the action and would receive $15,000 as a result of the settlement.  Related, and more importantly, Attachment 11b(6) requires Petitioner to state the reasons for the allocation of the settlement proceeds.  Attachment 11b(6) does not meaningfully do so.  It does not explain why Petitioner, the adult who will receive immediate access to his funds, is receiving more of the proceeds of the settlement than the two minors.  There is no indication that his injuries were more severe or that the settlement is proportionate to medical expenses, injuries, etc.  The Court wishes to hear from Petitioner and Counsel about the allocation of settlement proceeds at the hearing.

 

CRC 7.952 requires Petitioner and the minors to appear at the hearing before the Court grants the petitions.  The minors are 13 and 15, so the Court will require their appearance at the hearing.  If the Court is satisfied with the testimony received, it will grant the applications and issue the proposed orders on the applications.
