DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV16321    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: 73

8/13/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

THOMAS WESLEY PENTZ v. MARCHEL GABRIELLE AUGUSTE (21STCV16321) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff Pentz/opposing party: Bryan Freedman; Brian Turnauer; Tamar Yeghiayan (Freedman Taitelman + Cooley, LLP)

Counsel for Defendant Auguste/moving party:  Allison Schulman; Christina Coleman 

 

MOTION FOR AN ORDER TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT MARCHEL GABRIELLE AUGUSTE (filed 03/14/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Pentz’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Marchel Gabrielle Auguste is DENIED without prejudice.  

BACKGROUND

 

On April 30, 2021, Plaintiff Thomas Wesley Pentz (“Pentz”) filed a Complaint (the “Pentz Lawsuit”) against Defendant Marchel Gabrielle Auguste, aka Shelly Auguste (“Auguste”). On September 16, 2021, Pentz filed a First Amended Complaint for (1) Stalking; (2) Trespass; and (3) Distribution of Private Materials (Revenge Pornography) Civ. Code section 1708.85.

 

Pentz alleges, among other things, in his Complaint that Auguste stalked Pentz online, showed up at his residence uninvited and repeatedly sent sexually explicit photographs and videos of Pentz to his friends and family.

 

On June 25, 2021, Marchel Gabrielle Auguste filed a Complaint (the “Auguste Lawsuit”) against Thomas Wesley Pentz. In Auguste’s First Amended Complaint filed on August 10, 2022, she alleges causes of action for (1) Sexual Battery; (2) Gender Violence; (3) Violation of the Ralph Civil Rights Act; (4) Violation of the Tom Bane Civil Rights Act; (5) Action Based on Civil Code § 1708.85; (6) Intentional Intrusion Into Private Affairs; (7) Battery; (8) Assault; (9) Defamation; (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (11) Fraud.  

  

Auguste has sued alleging, among other things, the following: (i) Pentz communicated with her when she was a minor; (ii) groomed her; (iii) gave her an STD; (iv) ejaculated inside her; (v) raped her; (vi) defamed her and (vii) committed revenge porn against her. Auguste alleges that all the aforementioned acts caused her severe emotional distress.

 

On August 1, 2021, Pentz filed a Notice of Related Case. On September 14, 2021, the Court ruled the Pentz Lawsuit 21STCV16321 and the Auguste Lawsuit 21STCV23647 were related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 21STCV16321, the Pentz Lawsuit, is the lead case.  

 

On March 14, 2024, Pentz filed the instant Motion to Compel Deposition of Marchel Gabrielle Auguste aka Shelly Auguste. Pentz also requests sanctions.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

On August 8, 2024, Pentz filed a notice of non-opposition.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A party desiring to take an oral deposition shall give a notice in writing which states the specification of reasonably particularly of any materials to be produced by the deponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.220, subd. (a)(4).) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action … without having served a valid objection … fails to appear for examination, … or to produce for inspection any document, … described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)   

 

A motion brought to compel a deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition … by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  

 

B.      Discussion

 

Pentz moves the Court for an order compelling Auguste to appear at deposition.

 

Pentz asserts in his motion that Pentz served a notice of deposition on Auguste on February 22, 2024 for a deposition date of March 13, 2024. (Yeghiayan Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) On March 12, 2024, a day before the noticed deposition, Pentz’s counsel inquired as to whether Auguste would be appearing. (Yeghiayan Decl., ¶ 4.) Auguste did not respond to the email and did not serve objections prior to the deposition. (Ibid.) Auguste did not appear for her March 13, 2024 deposition and a notice of non-appearance was taken. (Yeghiayan Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

However, the Court notes that Pentz’s counsel’s declaration does not state that “the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance” pursuant to Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2). A motion brought to compel a deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition … by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).) Here, the deponent failed to attend the deposition. It does not appear that Pentz’s counsel attempted to contact the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance or has made a good faith attempt to resolve the deposition issue prior to filing this motion. Thus, the Court does not find Pentz has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (b)(2).

 

Accordingly, the Court denies Pentz’s motion without prejudice. Since the Court denies Pentz’s motion, requests for sanctions are also denied.

 

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff Pentz’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Marchel Gabrielle Auguste is DENIED without prejudice.

DONAHUE v. 427 S MARIPOSA PARTNERS, LP, et al. (24STCV02046)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Armand Jaafari (Grand Park Law Group)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party: David Canter (Nicolson Law Group, PC)

 

DEFENDANT 427 S MARIPOSA PARTNERS, LP’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUEST FOR ADMISSIONS, SET NO. ONE, ADMITTED; REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed on 07/10/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted is GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $710.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff Claire Donahue brought this action against Defendants 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP; LA Urban Living LLC; Jeclim LLC; Jeremy Miller; Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff was an occupant of real property located at 427 S Mariposa, Unit 511 Los Angeles, CA (the “Property”) as a lessee/tenant under a written lease with Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that on April 6, 2020, Plaintiff reported an active leak and water damage in the bedroom ceiling to management, who acknowledged the issue but failed to conduct repairs despite multiple follow-ups. On January 25, 2022, Plaintiff discovered additional mold growth in the bedroom after heavy rains. Plaintiff informed the building manager and requested repairs. During Plaintiff’s tenancy, Defendants failed to maintain the Property in a safe and habitable condition. Plaintiff was eventually forced to vacate the Property due to health issues while living in the Property. Defendants constructively evicted Plaintiff from the Property—having refused to make the required repairs to the Property and ensure the Property be made safe and habitable, and after Plaintiff told Defendants that Plaintiff suspected that the fungal growth at the Property was making Plaintiff sick. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of the implied warranty of habitability;

2.      Nuisance;

3.      Breach of contract;

4.      Negligence;

5.      Negligent misrepresentation;

6.      Constructive eviction;

7.      Premises liability;

8.      Fraud; and

9.      Intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On July 10, 2024, Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP filed the Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted and Request for Sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

 When a party fails to serve a timely response to a request for admission, the party propounding the request for admission may move for an order to deem the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.280, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction… on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

On March 22, 2024, Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP served Plaintiff electronically with Requests for Admissions, Set One. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A). Plaintiff’s responses were due on April 24, 2024, 30 days following service, plus an additional two court days for electronic service. (Cal. Code Civ. Pro. §§ 2030.260, subd. (a) and 1013, subd. (a).) However, Plaintiff failed to provide responses by April 24, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

The parties met and conferred regarding Plaintiff’s failure to respond. On April 25, 2024, Defendant sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel to confer about Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendant’s written discovery requests. Defendant allowed Plaintiff until May 3, 2024 to serve verified answers without objections to Defendant’s discovery requests. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 4.) On April 25, 2024, in response to Defendant’s meet and confer correspondence, Plaintiff’s counsel claimed that Defendant failed to properly serve their written discovery requests in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(b)(3). (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 5.) On April 25, 2024, Defendant offered to grant Plaintiff an extension of time to respond as another sign of good faith. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 6.) On April 29, 2024, after receiving no response, Defendant followed up with Plaintiff’s counsel to determine how long Plaintiff will need to provide Defendant with her responses. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 7.) On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel stated that he would accept service electronically from April 30, 2024, making Plaintiff’s responses due on or before June 4, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 8.) On June 7, 2024, after still not receiving Plaintiff’s responses, Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel a second email to meet and confer, extending Plaintiff until June 14, 2024 to provide verified answers, without objection. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 9.) As of the date of the filing of the instant motion, Defendant has not received any communication from Plaintiff’s counsel regarding Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Request for Admissions, nor has Defendant received Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Request for Admissions. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 10.)

 

Here, Defendant offered Plaintiff multiple extensions to provide Defendant with her responses. However, Plaintiff has failed to serve responses. Therefore, the motion is granted. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

Defendant requests monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on its Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Plaintiff. Defendant requests a total of $1,360.00, representing 5 hours of attorney work at an hourly rate of $260, plus $60 for the filing fee. (Id. ¶¶ 12-13.)  

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Plaintiff has failed to respond. However, since the motion is unopposed, the Court will not award Defendant’s anticipated fees for preparing a Reply. The Court awards Defendant attorney’s fees for the 1.5 hours spent preparing the motion, 1 hour of the anticipated fees for Defendant to prepare for and attend the motion hearing and the filing fee. Therefore, the Court awards Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the reduced total amount of $710.00.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted is GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $710.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s within 30 days of the date of this order.

EDALATINIYA v. DARREN ANTHONY PORTER, et al. (24STCV07140)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Mitra Chegini (California Lawyers Group, Inc.)

Counsel for Defendant Enterprise Rent-a-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC/moving party: Adam Miller (Miller Miller Gerber LLP)

 

Demurrer TO complaint (filed 06/27/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court SUSTAINS Defendant Enterprise’s demurrer to the Complaint with 20 days leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 21, 2024, Plaintiff Farhad Edalatiniya filed his motor vehicle negligence Complaint against Defendants Darren Anthony Porter (“Porter”); EAN Holdings, LLC (“EAN”); and Does 1 to 50. This matter arises out of a vehicle collision that occurred on or about August 18, 2023 at Northbound 1-405 at or near National Boulevard, Los Angeles, California.

 

On May 21, 2024, EAN was dismissed.

 

On May 22, 2024, EAN’s affiliate, Enterprise Rent A Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC (“Enterprise”), was named as Doe 1.

 

On June 27, 2024, Defendant Enterprise filed a demurrer to the Complaint on the following grounds:

·       The Complaint fails for uncertainty.

o   Based on conversations with Plaintiff's counsel, Enterprise believes that when Plaintiff dismissed EAN and DOE'd in its affiliate Enterprise, that Plaintiff intended the allegations previously made against EAN to be currently made against Enterprise. However, Plaintiff did not correctly plead and effectuate this presumed intent leading to great uncertainty with the Complaint as applied to Enterprise.

o   For example, the Complaint alleges defendant Porter and Does 1 to 50 operated the subject vehicle at the time of the incident. (Exhibit A at MV-2(a).) However, it is impossible that both Enterprise and Porter operated the same vehicle at the same time or that Enterprise, a corporate entity, operated a vehicle at all.

o   As such, it is impossible to discern the scope of the allegations being made against Enterprise in order to provide it with sufficient notice of the basis for liability alleged against it.

·       The Complaint fails to state a cause of action against Defendant Enterprise.

o   Plaintiff’s complaint also fails to state a cause of action against Enterprise under 49 U.S.C. section 30106, otherwise known as the Graves Amendment, under which it is clear that a rental car company and its affiliates cannot be held vicariously liable for the negligent acts of its customers. Under that law, for a claim against Enterprise to be actionable, it must include material allegations of an independent act of negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of Enterprise.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues:

·       Plaintiff has stated sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action

·       Case law involving 49 U.S.C. § 30106, the "Graves Amendment", offers plaintiff the opportunity to prove negligence on part of the vehicle's owner to overcome the protection that 49 U.S. Code § 30106 may provide

·       In this case, Plaintiff overcomes the Graves Amendment because he is asserting negligence on the part of Enterprise, in that Enterprise did not inspect for a valid driver's license before renting the vehicle to Porter, and in violation of Vehicle Code section 14608. Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant Porter was in fact driving Enterprise’s vehicle with a suspended license at the time of the accident.

·       Plaintiff is not disputing that Enterprise is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles.

·       If Enterprise’s demurrer is sustained, Plaintiff requests leave to amend.

 

In reply, Defendant Enterprise maintains that the Complaint fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against Defendant Enterprise.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant Enterprise submitted a declaration stating he discussed the grounds of the demurrer with Plaintiff’s by telephone on June 25, 2024 but an agreement was not reached between the parties. (Miller Decl., ¶ 9.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

C.      Judicial Notice

 

Defendant Enterprise requests the Court take judicial notice of the following facts:

 

1.      That the Exhibit D attached to the Declaration of Adam I. Miller, served and filed concurrently herewith, is a true and correct copy of the most recent Statement of Information for Enterprise filed with the California Secretary of State.

2.      As stated on the Statement of Information attached as Exhibit D to the Declaration of Adam I. Miller, Enterprise is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles.

3.      Enterprise is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles.

 

Evidence Code §452(h) provides that a court may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”  Additionally, “a court may take judicial notice of the fact of a document’s recordation, the date the document was recorded and executed, the parties to the transaction reflected in a recorded document, and the document’s legally operative language, assuming there is no genuine dispute regarding the document’s authenticity.”  (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 265.) Moreover, “[f]rom this, the court may deduce and rely upon the legal effect of the recorded document, when that effect is clear from its face.” (Id.)

 

The Court grants Defendant Enterprise’s unopposed requests for judicial notice.

 

D.     Uncertainty

 

Defendant Enterprise demurs to the entire Complaint arguing that the Complaint fails for uncertainty.

A demurrer for uncertainty lies where the pleading is uncertain, including where the pleading is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (f); Landau v. Salam (1971) 4 Cal.3d 901, 909.) To survive demurrer, a plaintiff must set forth the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action. (See Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 719.)¿Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored. (Chen v. Benjamin (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822.) Nonetheless, a defendant must have information clear and specific enough to fully comprehend a plaintiff’s allegations and respond with appropriate denials and defenses. (See Committee On Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp., supra, 35 Cal.3d at pp. 211–212.) 

The Court agrees that Plaintiff’s Complaint fails for uncertainty. The Court finds Plaintiff’s Complaint does not satisfy the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure or the Rules of Court and does not put Enterprise on sufficiently clear notice of the facts on which their claims are based – or, indeed, of the precise nature of their claims. The Complaint only contains boilerplate, vague allegations and it is not clear what Plaintiff is specifically alleging against Enterprise. As Enterprise asserts in its motion, for example, the Complaint alleges Defendant Porter and Does 1 to 50 operated the subject vehicle at the time of the incident. (Miller Decl., Exhibit A, p. 4 at MV-2(a).) However, it is impossible that both Enterprise and Porter operated the same vehicle at the same time or that Enterprise, a corporate entity, operated a vehicle at all.

Further, while in Plaintiff’s motion he argues that the Complaint states a cause of action because Plaintiff “is asserting negligence on the part of Enterprise, in that Enterprise did not inspect for a valid driver's license before renting the vehicle to Porter,” this is not alleged anywhere in the Complaint. (Oppos., p. 4.)

As such, the Court finds it is impossible for Enterprise to discern the scope of the allegations being made against Enterprise in order to provide it with sufficient notice of the basis for liability alleged against it.

 

Thus, the Court SUSTAINS Defendant Enterprise’s demurrer to the Complaint with 20 days leave to amend.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court SUSTAINS Defendant Enterprise’s demurrer to the Complaint with 20 days leave to amend.

MACDONALD, et al. v. B.V. GENTRY, et al. (24STCV14183)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs:  Jason Roberts (Resnick & Louis, PC)

Counsel for Defendants: Joseph Wilson (Wilson Rowley)

 

MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT (filed on 07/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Neil Macdonald, individually and as trustee of the Macdonald Scheerer Family Trust; and Gina Scheerer, individually and as trustee of the Macdonald Scheerer Family Trust; filed this action against Defendants Carl Gentry, individually and as representative of the Estate of Sharon Gentry and as representative of the Estate of Margaret Gentry; Ian Gentry; and Patricia Webber.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about August 5, 2023, an incident occurred in the State of California, County of Los Angeles, City of Long Beach. Thereafter, Defendant Carl Gentry, individually and as representative of the Estate of Sharon Gentry and representative of the Estate of Margaret Gentry brought a claim related to the incident against Plaintiffs. In December of 2023, a settlement of the claim was reached wherein Plaintiffs’ insurance carrier agreed to settle and resolve Defendants’ claim. To establish certainty of legal rights and duties of the parties under the settlement agreement, Plaintiffs are seeking declaratory relief from this court.

 

On July 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion to Determine Good Faith Settlement stating that the parties reached a settlement. No opposition was filed.

 

DISCUSSION

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6, subdivision (a)(1), provides that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .” “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 877.6(c).) Although determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿ (Id. at (c).)¿ 

 

In City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261, the Court provided guidance on an unopposed motion for a good faith settlement determination:¿ 

 

This court notes that of the hundreds of motions for good faith determination presented for trial court approval each year, the overwhelming majority are unopposed and granted summarily by the trial court. At the time of filing, in many cases the moving party does not know if a contest will develop. If each motion required a full recital by declaration or affidavit setting forth a complete factual response to all of the¿Tech-Bilt¿factors, literally thousands of attorney hours would be consumed and inch-thick motions would have to be read and considered by trial courts in an exercise which would waste valuable judicial and legal time and clients' resources. It must also be remembered that¿Tech-Bilt¿was decided on a contested basis. We are unaware of any reported decision which has reversed an¿uncontested¿good faith determination and we, therefore, conclude that only when the good faith nature of a settlement is disputed, it is incumbent upon the trial court to consider and weigh the¿Tech-Bilt¿factors. That is to say, when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient. 

 

((1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.) 

 

The instant motion is unopposed. Plaintiffs have provided evidence that the parties reached a settlement. (Roberts Decl., ¶¿9.) The settlement is the result of a series of arms’ length negotiations between the parties, starting with a policy limit demand from Defendants’ counsel. (Ibid.)

 

On or about December 18, 2023, Defendants, via counsel, made a policy limit settlement demand to Plaintiffs and their insurance carrier, State Farm General Insurance Company. (Roberts Decl., ¶¿5.) The demand was subsequently accepted and the settlement between the parties reduced to writing in the form of a Settlement Agreement and Release of All Claims. (Ibid.)

 

The essential settlement terms provide as follows: 1. Defendants agree to release all claims against Plaintiffs in exchange for payment from Plaintiffs’ insurance carrier in the amount of Plaintiffs’ policy limits of $1,000,000; and 2. The settlement is contingent upon a determination of good faith pursuant to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §877.6. (Roberts Decl., ¶¿6, Ex, A.)

 

In Plaintiffs’ motion, they provided a background of the case and the parties’ positions relative to their dispute. All parties to this action have been notified of the subject settlement and the settlement amount. Plaintiffs assert there is no collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct that occurred between the settling parties and good faith is presumed. (Roberts Dec., ¶ 8.)

 

The foregoing is sufficient for an unopposed motion for good faith settlement determination. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion is granted. 

 

DISPOSITION 

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.
