Case Number: 21STCV03338    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: B

BERMAN V. CITY OF LOS ANGELES, ET AL.

 

Motion for Summary judgment or in the alternative summary adjudication

 

Date of Hearing:  August 19, 2024                              Trial Date:  December 2, 2024

Department:   B                                                          Case No.: 21STCV03338

 

Moving Party:             Defendants City of Los Angeles and City of Los Angeles acting by and through the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power

Responding Party:       None

                                   

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 27, 2021, plaintiff Thomas Berman filed this action against defendants City of Los Angeles and Does 1 through 100 for damages arising from an alleged dangerous condition of public property. Plaintiff filed amendments to complaint, naming Los Angeles Department of Water & Power as Doe 1, Northwest Excavating, Inc. as Doe 2, Skansa, Inc. as Doe 3, Skansa USA, Inc. as Doe 4, and Regional Connector Constructors, Inc. as Doe 5.

 

On December 20, 2021, cross-complainants City of Los Angeles and City of Los Angeles acting by and through the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (the “City”) filed a cross complaint against cross-defendant Northwest Excavating Inc. alleging equitable indemnity, contribution, apportionment, declaratory relief, and express indemnity.

 

On November 16, 2023, City filed the instant motion for summary judgment.

 

On June 22, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed the complaint as to Traylor Brothers, Inc. as Doe 6, Skanska, Inc. as Doe 7, and Skanska, USA, Inc. as Doe 8.

 

On May 3, 2024, cross-complainant Regional Connector Constructors, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against cross-defendant Northwest Excavating, Inc. alleging equitable indemnity, declaratory relief, express indemnity, and implied indemnity.

 

On July 24, 2024, City requested to dismiss the action with prejudice as to all parties and all causes of action, but this was rejected because the request requires signatures of the cross-complainants’ attorneys of record.

 

On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff requested to dismiss the action with prejudice as to all parties and all causes of action, but this was rejected because the request to dismiss entire action/all causes of action must be signed by all parties.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

GRANT

 

Factual Allegations

 

The incident in question occurred on October 23, 2020, at approximately 1:15 p.m., near the intersection of S. Flower Street and 7th Street in Los Angeles, California. (UMF, 1.) Before the incident, Plaintiff and his business partner, Melvin Kim, decided to have lunch together and rented Lyft motorized scooters to travel to a restaurant. (UMF, 2-3.) Plaintiff had previously operated similar scooters on City streets over 50 times and was familiar with the area. (UMF, 4.)

 

While traveling southwest on S. Flower Street, Plaintiff’s front tire caught a steel plate in the roadway, causing him to be ejected from the scooter and land on the roadway. (UMF, 5.) Mr. Kim, who was following a similar path, did not experience any issues with the steel plate. (UMF, 6.) Plaintiff attempted to continue walking to the restaurant but had to stop due to pain. (UMF, 7.) He then used a rideshare service to return home, where his wife took him to Kaiser for emergency medical treatment. (UMF, 8.) Plaintiff was diagnosed with fractures in his left knee and right arm. (UMF, 10.) However, Plaintiff no longer suffers from any pain related to this incident. (UMF, 11.)

 

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal. App. 4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal. App. 4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.)

 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal. App. 4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)    

 

LADWP is Not a Proper Party

 

The City argues that the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (“LADWP”) is not a proper defendant in this case and should be dismissed. LADWP is not a separate legal entity from the City of Los Angeles but is instead a department created by the City. Citing several cases, the City contends that similar to how a sheriff’s department is considered a subunit of the county that created it, LADWP is merely a department within the City of Los Angeles. As such, naming LADWP as an independent defendant is improper, and therefore, it should be dismissed from the case.

 

Dangerous Condition

 

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are identical to those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) Property owners, possessors, or controllers generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property to prevent exposing others to unreasonable risks of harm. (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.) The existence and scope of this duty are legal questions for the court. (Id. at 36.) If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner has a duty to exercise ordinary care to either make the condition reasonably safe or provide an adequate warning to help others avoid harm. (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)

 

A plaintiff alleging injuries from a dangerous condition must prove that the defendant either: (1) created the dangerous condition, or (2) knew or should have known about it. (Peralta v. Vons Companies, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1030, 1036; Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.) A defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law if the plaintiff cannot demonstrate that the dangerous condition existed long enough to be discovered through ordinary care and inspection. (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at 1207.) The question of whether a dangerous condition has existed long enough for a reasonably prudent person to have discovered it is a matter of fact for the jury. (See id.) Property owners are required to inspect the premises or take other appropriate actions to determine their condition, and if they could have discovered the condition through reasonable care, they are liable for failing to correct it. (See id.) A store owner exercises ordinary care by conducting reasonable inspections of the areas open to customers. (See id. at 1205.) For business invitees, frequent inspections should be conducted. (See id. at 1207.)

 

The City argues that the Plaintiff's claim for a dangerous condition of public property fails as a matter of law under Government Code section 835, which outlines the exclusive conditions under which a public entity can be held liable for such conditions. To establish a prima facie case under this section, a plaintiff must prove four elements: (1) the property was in a dangerous condition, (2) the injuries were proximately caused by this condition, (3) the condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk even with due care, and (4) the governmental entity had actual or constructive notice of the condition sufficient to take protective measures.

 

The City contends that there is no evidence showing that it either created the dangerous condition or had notice of it prior to Plaintiff’s fall. (UMFs, 1-11, 15-23.) The steel plate that allegedly caused the Plaintiff's accident was likely placed by a third party, Regional Connector Constructors (RCC), as part of a transportation construction project by the Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (Metro). The City asserts that it had no knowledge of the steel plate’s existence, and there are no records of complaints or reports related to it. Moreover, the City emphasizes that even if it had notice or had placed the steel plate, the steel plate does not constitute a dangerous condition under the law. A dangerous condition is defined as one that creates a substantial risk of injury when used with due care. The City argues that the steel plate was not inherently dangerous and that there is no evidence of prior complaints or reports regarding the plate. (UMFs, 17-18.). Additionally, the fact that Plaintiff’s companion had no issues traversing the plate supports the argument that the plate was not a dangerous condition. (UMF, 6.) Furthermore, the City asserts that the steel plate, even if considered a defect, is trivial and does not impose liability. The law does not require public entities to repair trivial defects, and courts have consistently held that minor defects do not create a substantial risk of injury. The City argues that the steel plate was a minor condition that a reasonably careful user would have navigated without issue. (UMF, 21.) The City also argues that the steel plate was open and obvious, meaning that the condition itself served as a warning, and the City had no further duty to remedy or warn about it. The steel plate was clearly visible, and there were no obstructions that would have prevented Plaintiff from seeing it. (UMF, 23.)

 

The City also argues that Plaintiff’s claim is barred by the doctrine of assumption of the risk. This doctrine applies when a defendant owes no legal duty to protect the plaintiff from risks inherent in an activity. Under the primary assumption of the risk doctrine, a defendant has no duty to eliminate inherent risks in the activity that caused the injury, and this acts as a complete bar to the plaintiff's recovery. The City contends that riding a motorized scooter, like skateboarding or jet skiing, falls under this doctrine because it is an activity involving inherent risks that cannot be eliminated without altering its fundamental nature. (UMFs, 1, 3, 5, 26.) Additionally, the City argues that Plaintiff expressly assumed the risk of injury when he agreed to Lyft’s Terms of Use. The Terms included a clear waiver of liability, where Plaintiff acknowledged the risks involved in using the scooter and agreed to take full responsibility for any resulting injuries. This express assumption of risk further precludes the Plaintiff from holding the City liable for his injuries. (UMFs, 27-29.) Finally, the City maintains that even though the City was not specifically named in the waiver, the Release benefits the City because Lyft’s operations, including the use of motorized scooters, depend on City permits and approvals. Therefore, the City falls within the class of entities protected by the waiver, effectively barring the Plaintiff's claim against the City.

 

Plaintiff provides no arguments in opposition.

 

The Court finds that summary judgment should be granted in favor of the City. First, there is no evidence that the City created or had notice of the steel plate that allegedly caused Plaintiff’s injury, as the plate was placed by a third party and the City received no prior complaints or reports about it. Thus, Plaintiff’s claim under Government Code section 835 fails. Additionally, even if the City had notice, the steel plate does not constitute a dangerous condition under the law, as Plaintiff’s companion safely navigated the plate, indicating it was not inherently dangerous. Plaintiff also provides no evidence to rebut this showing. Moreover, the steel plate, at most, represents a trivial defect that does not create a substantial risk of injury, and Plaintiff has not provided evidence of any aggravating factors that would elevate the plate from a minor condition to a dangerous one. Furthermore, the steel plate was an open and obvious condition, clearly visible to any reasonably careful person using the roadway, thereby negating any duty on the part of the City to warn or remedy it. Lastly, Plaintiff’s claim is barred by the doctrine of assumption of the risk, as riding a motorized scooter inherently involves risks that Plaintiff voluntarily assumed, and Plaintiff expressly assumed these risks when agreeing to Lyft’s Terms of Use, which included a waiver of liability. For these reasons, the Court concludes that there are no triable issues of fact as to Plaintiff’s dangerous condition claim.

 

Therefore, the City’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

Case Number: 21STCV21153    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: B

farideh raubitschek V. County of Los Angeles, et al.

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:         July 19, 2024                          Trial Date:       October 16, 2024

Department:                B                                             Case No.:        21STCV21153

 

Moving Party:             Defendant County of Los Angeles

Responding Party:      Plaintiffs Farideh Raubitschek

 

BACKGROUND

           

Factual and Procedural Background

            Farideh Raubitschek (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint on June 4, 2021 against the County of Los Angeles (COLA or Defendant) for (1) premises liability and (2) negligence after suffering a trip and fall on August 11, 2020. Plaintiff alleges that she tripped and fell over a hole in the sidewalk located at or near 4600 Via Marina, California 90292 (the Property) (Complaint, ¶13), a sidewalk that she alleges is owned, maintained, and controlled by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶¶23-25.) The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion, Defendant files a reply.

 

Objections

            Concurrently filed with their reply, Defendant files an objection specifically to Exhibit 5 of the Declaration of Lisa Saperstein, which shows an image taken from Google Maps of the alleged defect from November 2017 and November 2018. The objection is sustained.

 

Tentative Ruling

            The County of Los Angeles’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Legal Standard

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

            “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Analysis

            Plaintiff alleges that Defendants negligently failed to maintain the Property that allowed a dangerous condition to exist and injure Plaintiff. Defendants contend that (1) Defendant, and its employees did not create a dangerous condition, (2) that there was no actual or constructive notice, and (3) Plaintiff cannot establish causation. The Court agrees and grants the Motion.   

 

A.    Premises Liability

            “The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.) “While a landowner is not the insurer of a visitor's safety, a landowner must exercise ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the premises to ascertain whether any dangerous conditions exist on the property.” (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.4th 1200, 1208.)

 

                                i.            Defendants did not create the dangerous condition

            The burden is on Defendant to present facts that negate an essential element or establish a defense. The first contention Defendant makes is that neither Defendant nor its employees created a dangerous condition. The primary evidence put forth is the Declaration of Loren Tiede (Tiede Decl.), who is currently employed by the Los Angeles County Department of Public Works (LACDPW) as a Senior Civil Engineer. Tiede has worked for LACDPW since 2007. In the Tiede Decl., Tiede states that as part of the Defendant’s routine inspection on August 3, 2020, an inspection occurred of the subject Property and no conditions were noted that required repair. (Tiede Decl., ¶15.) The subject incident occurred on August 11, 2020. Additionally, Plaintiff in her own deposition attested to walking by this alleged dangerous condition every morning without incident. (Declaration of Kelly A. Ward, Esq., Exh. 2, pg. 18 lines 10-11 and pg. 22 lines 17-19.)

           

            Plaintiff in her opposition papers contends that just because no repairs were scheduled does not equate to no repairs being needed. This may be true, however, in this instance the Court is not persuaded. Principally because this argument ignores the fact that an employee had inspected the Property prior to Plaintiff’s trip and fall and observed no dangerous condition. As aforementioned, Plaintiff walked this route several times before without incident. (Id.)

 

                              ii.            There was no actual or constructive notice

            Plaintiff alleges that Defendant knew or should have known that a dangerous condition existed on its property. Although Plaintiff does not allege a violation of Gov. Code §835, the statute articulates that if the government entity had constructive notice of the dangerous condition, the entity may be held liable. As previously stated, Defendant notes that an inspection of the Property eight days prior to the trip and fall showed no dangerous condition nor a need for repair. Countering this argument, Plaintiff provides a summary of a site inspection of the Property conducted by Philip Rosecu, a Senior Forensic Engineer at Aperture (formerly Wexco International). (Declaration of Philip L. Rosecu, Exh. A.)

 

            The summary provides photos and measurements of the alleged defect, noting that the hole is 1.5 inches at its deepest point, 5 inches long, and 4.5 inches wide (Id), arguing that the defect was not trivial as Defendant contends. Plaintiff also argues that whether the hole was trivial is for the jury to decide. The Court disagrees. First, regarding the summary, the summary was completed in April of 2021, several months after the subject incident. Second, regarding whether the alleged defect is trivial the Court is well within its discretion to determine whether the defect is insignificant. (Caloroso v. Hathaway, 122 Cal.App.4th 922, 928 […“there is no need for expert opinion. It is well within the common knowledge of lay judges and jurors just what type of a defect in a sidewalk is dangerous.”].) If the photos Plaintiff provides are accurate, they show nothing outside the typical sidewalk lay persons encounter daily. The Court deems that not only was there no actual nor constructive notice, but that the alleged defect was trivial.      

           

                            iii.            Defendant has negated the element of causation

            Finally, Defendant demonstrates that causation is absent. In Plaintiff’s deposition, Plaintiff gives conflicting statements. When asked what occurred after stepping to her right to avoid pedestrians, Plaintiff states that she slipped and fell by stepping on an uneven place on the sidewalk. However, when asked specifically what happened as she stepped to the right, Plaintiff responds that she doesn’t know what happened. (Declaration of Kelly A. Ward, Esq., Exh. 2, pg. 24 lines 8-22 and pg. 26 lines 11-14.) Here, it is unclear what caused Plaintiff’s fall, and Plaintiff’s own testimony does nothing to support the element of causation.  

 

 

 

CONCLUSION

            Accordingly, the County of Los Angeles’ Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.
