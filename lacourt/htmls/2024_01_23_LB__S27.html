DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV42736    Hearing Date: January 23, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Todd Bertrang (“Todd”) and Ophie Bertrang (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants, Lido Sailing Club, Inc. (“Lido”), Ivory Holdings, LLC (“Ivory”), and Scott Volero (“Volero”) (collectively, “Defendants”) based on injuries Plaintiffs allege they sustained from Todd’s exposure to hazardous chemicals. 

 

Plaintiffs filed their original complaint on 11/18/21, and filed a First Amended Complaint on 5/04/22.  Notably, Plaintiffs did not serve any party prior to filing their FAC, and no party responded to the original complaint.  On 6/13/22, Lido Sailing Club, LLC filed a demurrer to the FAC, setting it for hearing on 9/08/22.  On 9/08/22, after receiving opposition and reply papers, the Court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend.

On 9/30/22, Plaintiffs filed their Second Amended Complaint.  On 10/05/22, Lido filed a demurrer to the SAC.  Shortly thereafter, the personal injury hub court found the case complicated and transferred it to Long Beach for all further proceedings.  On 1/12/23, Lido re-filed its demurrer to the SAC in Department S27.  On 6/27/23, the Court continued the hearing on the demurrer, finding the parties had not adequately met and conferred prior to filing their papers.  On 7/27/23, the Court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend.  On 9/20/23, rather than amending the SAC, Plaintiffs dismissed Lido from the case. 

 

2.     Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

3.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel, Paul D. Rasmussen wrote a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff, wherein Counsel detailed the arguments that form the basis of the demurrer.  The correspondence concluded by indicating that a demurrer would be filed if the parties did not resolve the issues.  Counsel then discussed the issues with Plaintiff by phone, but the parties were unable to agree on a resolution.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for determination on its merits. 

 

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiffs seek judicial notice of a notice that the premises are being classified as hazardous, substandard, or a nuisance.  As the Court noted previously in connection with its ruling on Lido’s demurrer, while a demurring party can use a judicially noticeable document to show the complaint is deficient, there is no authority permitting the pleading party to supplement its pleading with a judicially noticeable document to render the otherwise deficient pleading sufficient.  At most, the Court would use the document to determine whether leave to amend should be granted. 

 

  1. Second Cause of Action, Strict Liability for Ultrahazardous Activity

Defendants demur to the second cause of action, contending Plaintiffs failed to allege Defendants have ever engaged in any ultrahazardous activity.  Defendants’ argument is that storing or releasing toxic chemicals on a property does not constitute ultrahazardous activity.  They argue handling, storage, treatment, and disposal of hazardous waste happens every day across Los Angeles, California, and the United States, and is not an ultrahazardous activity.  They cite cases concerning what is considered ultrahazardous activity and what is not; notably, none of the cases address improper disposal of hazardous waste.  Additionally, none of the examples they list have case citations, so the Court cannot review them. 

 

The Court finds Defendants failed to meet their moving burden to show disposing of hazardous waste is not an ultrahazardous activity.  The demurrer is overruled.  The ruling is without prejudice to Defendants’ right to raise the issue at further stages in the proceedings with proper case citations and authorities.

  1. Third Cause of Action, Violation of HSC §25359.7

Defendants contend Plaintiffs cannot state a claim for violation of Health and Safety Code §25359.7 because the code section provides for strict liability when a defendant “releases” or has knowledge of the release of hazardous substances on the property.  Defendants contend Plaintiffs have alleged only that hazardous substances are present on the property, but not that they were released.  Plaintiffs, in opposition, contend the substances would not be present on the property if they had not been released.

 

It appears the parties are arguing semantics.  Defendants appear to take the word “present” to mean have been present at any time, such that even if the chemicals were properly disposed of, they would have been “present” on the property.  This interpretation, however, does not make sense.  The entities alleged to have caused the waste to be “present” on the property ceased occupying the property prior to 2014, and Plaintiffs allege they learned of the hazardous substances in August of 2019.  If the substances were never “released,” they would no longer be on the property in August of 2019.  The demurrer is therefore overruled.

 

  1. Fourth Cause of Action, NIED

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, contending it is redundant with the first cause of action for negligence (to which they do not demur).  In Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984-985, the Court held that there is no separate tort for NIED; it is merely the tort of negligence.  It then went on to explain whether and under what circumstances emotional distress damages can be recovered in connection with a negligence cause of action. 

 

Plaintiffs do not meaningfully oppose the demurrer.  The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted to permit Plaintiffs to add emotional distress allegations to their first cause of action for negligence. 

 

  1. Fifth Cause of Action, IIED

Defendants demur to the cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress, contending Plaintiffs have not alleged extreme and outrageous conduct that would support liability under this cause of action.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argue that knowing hazardous substances are present on the property and failing to inform them before they take possession is extreme and outrageous, such that liability is appropriate.  The Court finds this is a jury issue that cannot be decided at the pleading stage.  The demurrer is overruled.

 

  1. Alter Ego Liability

All claims pled against Volero are pled on an alter ego theory.  Volero demurs to all claims pled against him, contending the allegations of alter ego are deficient.  Plaintiffs’ alter ego allegations are found at ¶6 of the SAC, and merely allege that each defendant was acting as the alter ego of each other defendant.  As Defendants correctly note in their demurrer, Rutherfold Holdings, LLC v. Plaza del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 236 sets forth the pleading standard for pleading alter ego.  In Rutherford, the Court discussed the allegations of the plaintiff’s complaint as follows:

Rutherford alleged that Caswell dominated and controlled PDR; that a unity of interest and ownership existed between Caswell and PDR; that PDR was a mere shell and conduit for Caswell's affairs; that PDR was inadequately capitalized; that PDR failed to abide by the formalities of corporate existence; that Caswell used PDR assets as her own; and that recognizing the separate existence of PDR would promote injustice. These allegations mirror those held to pass muster in First Western Bank & Trust Co. v. Bookasta (1968) 267 Cal.App.2d 910, 915–916, 73 Cal.Rptr. 657. As in First Western, *236  “[a]ssuming these facts can be proved, [Caswell] ... may be held liable ... under the alter ego principle.” (Id. at p. 916, 73 Cal.Rptr. 657.)

 

The Court went on to discuss the pleading standard governing a plaintiff’s allegation of alter ego against a defendant, holding:

Defendants argue that Rutherford failed to allege specific facts to support an alter ego theory, but Rutherford was required to allege only “ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550, 67 Cal.Rptr.3d 330, 169 P.3d 559.) Moreover, the “less particularity [of pleading] is required where the defendant may be assumed to possess knowledge of the facts at least equal, if not superior, to that possessed by the plaintiff,” which certainly is the case here. (Burks v. Poppy Construction Co. (1962) 57 Cal.2d 463, 474, 20 Cal.Rptr. 609, 370 P.2d 313.) Therefore, we affirm the trial court's ruling that Rutherford sufficiently pled an alter ego theory of liability.

 

Plaintiffs herein failed to allege any of the ultimate facts showing alter ego liability.  They failed to allege unity of interest, domination and control, inadequate capitalization, etc.  Volero’s demurrer is therefore sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiffs must allege ultimate facts showing imposition of liability against Volero would be proper.

 

4.     Motion to Strike

Defendants move to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages and related allegations.  Plaintiffs’ only punitive damages allegations are found at ¶45 of the SAC, which is in the cause of action for NIED.  Because the demurrer to the NIED cause of action is sustained, the motion to strike is granted.  Because a negligence cause of action cannot support a prayer for punitive damages, leave to amend is denied.

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained in part and overruled in part as set forth fully above.  Plaintiffs are ordered to file a Third Amended Complaint within twenty days.  Defendants are ordered to file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.  Defendants are ordered to give notice.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Demoni Henderson filed this action against Defendants, City of Long Beach, City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, and California Department of Public Transportation for damages arising out of a trip and fall on an unmarked bench.  Plaintiff’s complaint contains a sole cause of action for premises liability – dangerous condition of public property. 

 

Plaintiff filed his complaint on 2/28/23.  The County filed an answer to the complaint on 7/20/23.  Plaintiff dismissed the County on 7/26/23.  The City of Long Beach filed an answer to the complaint on 7/27/23.  CalTrans filed an answer to the complaint on 7/28/23.  Plaintiff dismissed CalTrans on 7/28/23. 

 

The City of Los Angeles filed this demurrer and motion to strike on 8/10/23.  Plaintiff filed timely opposition papers, and Defendant filed timely reply papers. 

 

2.     Demurrer to Complaint

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel, Jason R. Gianvecchio wrote a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff, wherein Counsel detailed the arguments that form the basis of the demurrer.  The correspondence concluded by indicating that a demurrer would be filed if the parties did not resolve the issues and inviting Plaintiff to discuss the issues.  Counsel declares Plaintiff’s attorney did not respond to the correspondence.  The Court will rule on the demurrer on the merits, and orders Plaintiff’s attorney to promptly respond to meet and confer correspondence in the future in connection with this and other actions.   

 

 

  1. Analysis

Defendant demurs to the sole cause of action in the complaint for dangerous condition of public property, correctly noting that all claims against governmental entities must be pled with specificity.  See Searcy v. Hemet Unified School District (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 802.  Plaintiff’s complaint alleges Plaintiff was injured because he encountered an unmarked bench that was in disrepair.  As Defendant correctly notes in the demurrer, Plaintiff provides no facts stating what “unmarked bench” means, how the bench was in a state of disrepair, or how the bench injured Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff’s lengthy opposition spends a great deal of time arguing points not raised in the demurrer.  Plaintiff also cites authorities inapplicable to a public entity.  What Plaintiff’s opposition fails to do is address or show how alleging an unmarked bench in a state of disrepair injured him.  The demurrer is therefore sustained.  If Plaintiff amends, he must plead his complaint with the level of particularity required when pleading a claim against a public entity. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

a.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant seeks judicial notice of Plaintiff’s claim for damages.  Plaintiff opposes the RJN.  The Court takes judicial notice of the fact that the document is what it purports to be and says what it purports to say.  The Court does not take judicial notice of the truth of any fact stated in the document. 

 

b.     Analysis

Plaintiff’s complaint, at Count 3(b), alleges the subject condition was created by a governmental employee.  Defendant moves to strike that allegation, contending it conflicts with the government tort claim Plaintiff submitted prior to filing the suit. 

 

Pursuant to Plata v. City of San Jose (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 736, 748, a complaint is subject to dismissal if it alleges a factual basis for recovery that is not “fairly reflected” In the government tort claim. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He first argues Defendant is improperly using a motion to strike as a demurrer.  Plaintiff is incorrect.  The motion to strike seeks to strike a specific paragraph from the complaint.  Indeed, a demurrer would be an improper vehicle to achieve this goal, as it must be used to challenge an entire cause of action.

 

Plaintiff next argues Defendant is improperly attempting to make argument outside the four corners of the complaint.  Plaintiff is again incorrect.  Defendant has properly sought judicial notice of Plaintiff’s government tort claim.  The Court can review the claim and determine what it does and does not say.  Again, per Plata, the claim must fairly reflect Plaintiff’s legal theories in order to support those theories in the complaint. 

 

Plaintiff’s final argument is that, pursuant to Stockett v. Association of Cal. Water Agencies Joint Powers Ins. Authority (2004) 34 Cal.4th 441, 446, his government tort claim is sufficient to give Defendant notice of all theories ultimately pled in the complaint against it.  Specifically, he cites to a portion of the opinion clarifying that a plaintiff need not include an allegation as to the legal cause of the accident in the tort claim.  The Court has reviewed Stockett and finds Plaintiff is correct.  Stockett makes clear that the claim need only give a general description of the circumstances of the injury and damages incurred, and that variances between the claim’s general description and the underlying facts or causes of action ultimately pleaded in the complaint are not necessarily fatal; so long as the cause of action pleaded alleges a factual basis for recovery that is “fairly reflected” in the written claim, the complaint will be deemed sufficient. 

 

Plaintiff’s tort claim asserted he was injured when he tripped on a bench.  A variance between whether the City created the dangerous bench or failed to warn of the dangerous bench is the type of variance Stockett held will not defeat the complaint ultimately pled.  By way of example, in Blair v. Superior Court (1990 218 Cal.App.3d 221, 226, the Court of Appeals found a tort claim indicating the defendant entity had negligently maintained and constructed the highway surface and failed to sand and care for the highway was sufficient to support additional claims that the entity defectively placed guardrails, negligently sloped the road, provided inadequate warning signs, and allowed hazards to exist in the vicinity of the highway. 

 

The motion to strike is denied.    

 

4.     Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  Its motion to strike is denied.  Plaintiff is ordered to file an amended complaint within twenty days.  Defendant is ordered to file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.

Case Number: 23LBCV00523    Hearing Date: January 23, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Claudia Lankford, individually and as successor-in-interest to Valentina Timascu, filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Steven J. Appleby, M.D., and Maurice A. Bell, M.D., as well as Sorin Musat as a nominal defendant, for medical malpractice and wrongful death arising out of Defendants’ care and treatment of Decedent, Timascu. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

a.     Moving Argument

At this time, Defendant, LBMMC moves for summary judgment, contending it complied with the standard of care at all times, and nothing it did caused or contributed to Decedent’s death. 

 

b.     Law Governing Standard of Care

The standard of care against which the acts of health care providers are to be measured is a matter within the knowledge of experts.  Elcome v. Chin (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 310, 317.  Unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman, the standard of care in a malpractice action can only be proved by an expert’s testimony.  Id.  If the “common knowledge” exception does not apply to a medical malpractice action, expert evidence is conclusive and cannot be disregarded.  Id. 

 

A medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because he chooses one medically acceptable method of treatment or diagnosis and it turns out that another medically accepted method would have been a better choice.  CACI 506.  Likewise, a medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because his efforts are unsuccessful or he makes an error that was reasonable under the circumstances.  CACI 505.

 

Whether the standard of care in the community has been breached presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by opinion testimony unless the medical question is within the common knowledge of laypersons.  See Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App4th 836, 844. “‘When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.’“ (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985.)

 

c.     Law Governing Causation

In order to establish that defendant's negligence was a “substantial factor” in causing injury or death, the plaintiff must prove the negligence was of itself sufficient to bring about that harm.  “The law is well settled that in a personal injury action causation must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based upon competent expert testimony.  Mere possibility alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case.  [Citations.]  That there is a distinction between a reasonable medical ‘probability’ and a medical 'possibility' needs little discussion.  There can be many possible ‘causes,’ indeed, an infinite number of circumstances which can produce an injury or disease. A possible cause only becomes ‘probable’ when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action.  This is the outer limit of inference upon which an issue may be submitted to the jury.” Bromme v. Pavitt (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1498; citing Jones v. Ortho Pharmaceutical Corp. (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 396, 402 403.

 

d.     Evidentiary Objections

Defendant, with its reply papers, filed objections to the Declaration of Jeffery Brown, M.D.  Defendant’s objections are sustained.  Defendant correctly notes that Dr. Brown fails to lay a foundation for his testimony, as he fails to set forth his expert credentials and also fails to detail which records he reviewed to form his expert opinions.  Defendant also correctly notes that Dr. Brown’s declaration repeatedly discusses what physicians did or did not do, but never specifically opines concerning what the standard of care is that governs Defendant’s nurses and non-physician staff, or how any of those persons breached the standard of care.  The objections are therefore sustained. 

 

e.     Moving Burden

Defendant supports its motion with the expert declaration of Ryan Klein, M.D.  Dr. Klein sets forth his expert credentials, states what records he reviewed, details Defendant’s care and treatment of Decedent (through its nursing staff and non-physician personnel), and ultimately concludes that Defendant’s care and treatment of Decedent complied with the standard of care and did not cause or contribute to Decedent’s death.

The Expert Declaration of Dr. Klein is sufficient to meet Defendant’s moving burden to establish it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  Notably, Plaintiff does not object to Dr. Klein’s declaration or articulate any argument, in opposition to the motion, contending Defendant failed to meet its moving burden. 

 

The burden therefore shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact concerning breach of the standard of care and causation. 

 

f.      Opposing Burden

Plaintiff filed opposition to the motion on 1/10/24.  Plaintiff supports the opposition with the Declaration of Jeffery Brown, M.D.  As noted above, Dr. Brown’s declaration is deficient in numerous respects, and the objections to the declaration are sustained.  Because expert testimony is necessary to defeat a summary judgment motion in the medical malpractice context, and because Plaintiff’s expert declaration is objectionable and therefore not admissible, Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact.  The summary judgment motion is granted.

1.     12/12/23 Hearing

The Court was originally scheduled to hear this motion on 12/12/23.  Prior to the hearing, the Court issued the following tentative ruling, which was ultimately adopted after hearing:

Plaintiff, Sandra Morris filed this action against Defendants, Blythe/Windsor Country Park Healthcare Center, LLC, S&F Management Company, LLC, Estegenet Ayele, M.D., and MemorialCare Medical Group on 5/05/23 for elder abuse and neglect, violation of HSC §1430(b), negligence, and wrongful death.  Plaintiff filed proof of service of the summons and complaint on Blythe, S&F, and MemorialCare on 8/17/23.    

 

On 8/21/23, Plaintiff filed this motion for order for filing complaint for damages nunc pro tunc, setting it for hearing on 2/27/24.  Plaintiff filed proof of service of the moving papers on the agents for service of process on S&F, Blythe, and MemorialCare.  Plaintiff did not file proof of service of the moving papers on Ayele.  Notably, Ayele is the only defendant who has filed an answer to date; Ayele did so on 9/11/23.  Plaintiff dismissed MemorialCare on 9/11/23.    

 

On 10/05/23, the Court held a Case Management Conference.  Plaintiff appeared through Counsel, Kyle Schneberg.  Ayele appeared through Counsel, Sara M. Greco.  The two remaining entity defendants, S&F and Blythe, did not appear.  The Court advanced the hearing on the 2/27/24 motion to today’s date, 12/21/23.  The Court ordered Plaintiff to give notice of the ruling. 

 

To date, there is no notice of the ruling in the Court’s file.  There is also no opposition to the motion.  This is concerning, because Ayele filed a demand that defense of statute of limitations be tried separately, and this motion concerns the statute of limitations. 

 

The Court is continuing the hearing on the motion for one month, to Tuesday, 1/23/24.  Plaintiff is ordered to give notice that the hearing on the motion is continued. 

 

2.     Continued Hearing

Plaintiff appeared at the 12/12/23 hearing through her attorney.  Defendant, Ayele appeared at the hearing through her attorney, Robert Fessinger.  At the conclusion of the hearing, the Court ordered Plaintiff to give notice of the continuance. 

 

On 12/21/23, Plaintiff filed a notice of ruling.  The notice of ruling indicates, in pertinent part, “The Status Conference RE Bankruptcy Status and/or Default of Defendants BLYTHE/WINDSOR COUNTRY PARK HEALTHCARE CENTER, LLC; S&F MANAGEMENT COMPANY LLC, is scheduled for February 14, 2024…”

The Court has reviewed its online docket, and finds there has never been a notice of bankruptcy stay filed in this case.  CRC 3.650 required the debtors to “immediately” serve and file notice when the stay was initiated.  However, pursuant to 11 USC §362(a), the stay arises automatically, with or without a court order activating the stay.  Pursuant to §362(a)(1), all pending actions against a bankruptcy debtor are stayed pending completion of bankruptcy proceedings.   

 

The Court finds it cannot make any order that would affect the proceedings against the debtors while there are ongoing bankruptcy proceedings.  The hearing on this motion is therefore continued to 2/14/24, to be heard concurrently with the status conference re: bankruptcy and/or default. 

 

If Plaintiff wishes to have a substantive ruling on this motion on 2/14/24, Plaintiff must file a declaration and/or points and authorities concerning the status and effect of Defendants’ bankruptcy proceedings at least seven court days prior to the hearing.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
