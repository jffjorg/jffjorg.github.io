DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03098    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV03098

MOTION

Motion to Compel Discovery of Peace Officer Personnel and Other Documents

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Rosa Gonzalez

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant County of Los Angeles

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Rosa Gonzalez (“Plaintiff”) works as a police sergeant at the Sherrif’s Department of Defendant County of Los Angeles (“Defendant”).  Plaintiff contends Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff for reporting deputy gang activity, timecard fraud, fraud in the lieutenant’s exam process, and discriminatory hiring practices.  Plaintiff seeks disclosure of: Defendant’s files relating to Plaintiff’s October 26, 2021 grievance; Plaintiff’s November 12, 2021 supplemental grievance; Internal Affairs Bureau investigation No. IAB 244923, which was an investigation about Plaintiff; Defendant’s inquiry memoranda regarding allegations Yvonne O’Brien (“O’Brien”) and Carmen Arballo (“Arballo”) rigged the 2021 lieutenant promotion exam; and Defendant’s documents regarding O’Brien’s access to the lieutenant’s exam; Plaintiff’s report O’Brien and Arballo committed timecard fraud; an Internal Affairs Bureau investigation that Vanessa Chow filed against Sheriff Alex Villanueva (“Villanueva”) in October 2022; any investigation into Villanueva for discrimination and retaliation against Inspector General Max Huntsman; Cynthia Maluto’s report of O’Brien’s timecard fraud; Internal Affairs Bureau investigation No. IAB 2540083 into Cynthia Maluto; and any records of O’Brien’s fraud and dishonesty.  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Penal Code section 832.7 provides peace officer personnel records are confidential and are not to be disclosed in a civil proceeding except pursuant to Evidence Code sections 1043 and 1046.  (Pen. Code, § 832.7, subd. (a).)  Evidence Code section 1043 sets forth a two-step procedure for disclosure of peace officer personnel records.  The party seeking disclosure must file a motion identifying the peace officer, the agency in possession of the records, a description of the records, who is seeking the records, and the time and place of the hearing.  (Evid. Code, § 1043 subd. (b)(1).)  The moving party must submit a declaration showing good cause for disclosure of the records, setting forth the materiality of the records, and stating upon reasonable belief  the governmental agency has the requested documents.  (Evid. Code, § 1043, subd. (b)(3).)  The moving party need only show “a plausible scenario of officer misconduct . . . that might or could have occurred.  Such a scenario is plausible because it presents an assertion of specific police misconduct that is both internally consistent and supports the defense proposed to the charges.”  (Warrick v. Superior Court (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1011, 1026.)  This is a “relatively low threshold for discovery . . . .”  (City of Santa Cruz v. Municipal Court (1989) 49 Cal.3d 74, 83.)  The moving party must give notice of the motion to the parties and to the governmental agency with custody of the records.  (Evid. Code, § 1043, subd. (a).)

In the instant case, Plaintiff’s request for Defendant’s files noted above is relevant to Plaintiff’s claim Defendant, through its supervisory employees, retaliated against Plaintiff for complaining about unlawful activity.  Accordingly, Plaintiff has good cause to obtain these records.  Nonetheless, Plaintiff is not entitled to discover “the portions of the Internal Affairs report in which the investigating officer states his analysis and conclusions regarding the incident.”  (Haggerty v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1088.)  Therefore, the  Court would have redacted such portions of the file before disclosure of the file to Plaintiff.

Plaintiff fails to articulate a theory under which Defendant’s documents relating to an Internal Affairs Bureau investigation that Vanessa Chow filed against Alex Villanueva in October 2022, any investigation into Villanueva for discrimination and retaliation against Inspector General Max Huntsman, or an Internal Affairs Bureau investigation No. IAB 2540083 into Cynthia Maluto are pertinent to this action.  Accordingly, the Court denies the motion as to those requests.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion is granted, in part.  Pursuant to Evidence Code section 1045, the Court will hold an in camera review of Defendant’s files relating to Plaintiff’s October 26, 2021 grievance, Plaintiff’s November 12, 2021 supplemental grievance, Internal Affairs Bureau investigation No. IAB 244923, Defendant’s inquiry memoranda regarding allegations O’Brien and Arballo rigged the 2021 lieutenant promotion exam, and Defendant’s documents regarding O’Brien’s access to the lieutenant’s exam, Plaintiff’s report O’Brien and Arballo committed timecard fraud, Cynthia Maluto’s report of O’Brien’s timecard fraud, and any of Defendant’s records regarding O’Brien’s fraud and dishonesty

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 22STCV13023    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV13023

MOTIONS: 

Motion to Compel Answers to Judgment Debtor Examination Questions;

Motion for Protective Order

           

MOTION

Petitioners Porch.com, Inc. and Matthew Ehrlichman (“Petitioners”) participated in an arbitration of claims with Respondent Kandela, LLC (“Respondent”).  In its order of October 12, 2022, the Court confirmed the arbitration award and entered judgment pursuant to its terms.  Petitioners subsequently assigned their rights in the judgment to Assignee Scottsdale Indemnity Company (“Judgment Creditor”).  Judgment Creditor moves to compel Respondent’s principal Eli Gordon (“Gordon”) to answer questions at his judgment debtor’s examination.  In response, Respondent filed a motion for protective order regarding judgment debtor examinations in this action.  As the motions substantially overlap, the Court considers the motions together.

ANALYSIS

A judgment debtor examination is the post-judgment equivalent of a deposition.  (Imperial Bank v. Pim Elec., Inc. (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 540, 546-547.)  Service on the judgment debtor of an order for appearance at a judgment debtor’s examination obligates the judgment debtor to appear in court “to furnish information to aid in enforcement of the money judgment.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.110, subd. (a).)  After service of such an order, the judgment debtor may move the court for a protective order to quash or modify the order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1; see Lee v. Swansboro Country Property Owners Ass'n (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 575, 582-583.)

Because the purpose of the examination is to “compel the judgment debtor to give information concerning his property . . . ,” the judgment creditor has “the widest scope for inquiry concerning property and business affairs of the debtor . . . .”  (Young v. Keele (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1090, 1093.)  The judgment creditor may “leave no stone unturned in the search for assets which might be used to satisfy the judgment.”  (Jogani v. Jogani (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 158, 172, internal quotations omitted.)  However, the judgment debtor is entitled to claim the same privileges as trial witnesses.  (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1002-1003.)  If a judgment debtor refuses to answer a question without invoking an applicable privilege, the judgment creditor may move for an order for the judgment debtor to respond.  (Ahart, Cal. Practice Guide: Enforcing Judgments and Debts The Rutter Group 2024)  ¶ 6:1335.10, p. 28.)

In the instant case, counsel for Respondent instructed Gordon not to answer questions during the debtor examination.  Counsel should not instruct an examinee not to answer any question unless the question implicates a privilege.  (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1014.)  In opposition, Respondent makes no showing that questions about Respondent’s past assets or its board members implicate any privilege.  Accordingly, Gordon should have answered those questions. 

While Respondent argues questions regarding the payment of Respondent’s legal expenses implicate the attorney-client privilege, Respondent is incorrect.  A client has a privilege to refuse to disclose, and to prevent another from disclosing, a confidential communication between the client and the lawyer.  (Evid. Code, § 954.)  “‘[C]onfidential communication between client and lawyer means information transmitted between a client and his or her lawyer in the course of that relationship and in confidence by a means which, so far as the client is aware, discloses the information to no third persons other than those who are present to further the interest of the client in the consultation or those to whom disclosure is reasonably necessary for the transmission of the information or the accomplishment of the purpose for which the lawyer is consulted, and includes a legal opinion formed and the advice given by the lawyer in the course of that relationship.”  (Evid. Code, § 952.)  The payment of attorney fees is not a confidential communication between Respondent and its counsel.  Therefore, the attorney-client privilege is inapplicable in this instance. 

As Respondent points out, its “written fee contract” with its counsel is “a confidential communication” for purposes of the attorney-client privilege.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6149.)  However, the privilege does not extend beyond the written fee agreement to such information as invoices for concluded matters.  (See Los Angeles County Bd. of Supervisors v. Superior Court (2016) 2 Cal.5th 282, 298-299 [invoices for legal services are not categorically privileged].)  In this matter, Respondent does not seek specific information regarding the nature of the work Respondent’s counsel performed on Respondent’s behalf.  Instead, Respondent seeks information regarding the source of funds by which Respondent pays its Counsel, which is pertinent as Respondent claims it is insolvent.  Gordon must respond to all questions to which Counsel instructed Gordon not to respond.  The motion to compel responses is granted as to all such questions.

Judgment Creditor also moves to compel Gordon to provide different responses to questions Gordon answered that he could not recall, or otherwise failed to provide answers that Judgment Creditor deemed satisfactory.  The Court cannot compel Gordon to provide specific answers in response to Judgment Creditor’s questions, even if the answers are obvious.  The motion to compel Gordon to respond to questions to which Gordon already responded is denied.

Judgment Creditor seeks sanctions in connection with its motion.  The Court declines to award sanctions.  Judgment Creditor admittedly did not attempt to resolve this dispute without resorting to motion practice.  As such, the Court considers sanctions unwarranted.  Likewise, the Court does not consider appointment of a referee warranted at this time.  However, given what the Court has observed thus far with respect to this examination, the request is reasonable, and despite the Court’s hesitancy at this time to grant the request, if the parties do not show a greater amount of cooperation moving forward, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 639, the Court will entertain a properly filed motion for appointment of a referee or appoint one on the Court’s own motion.

Respondent moves for a protective order to limit Judgment Creditor’s questions at the judgment debtor examination to questions about Respondent’s current assets.  The basis for Respondent’s motion is that the parties are also engaged in another matter: Porch.com, Inc., et al., v. Kandela, LLC, et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2023, No. 23STCV01162 (case No. 23STCV01162)).  Respondent contends Judgment Creditor is using the judgment debtor examination to seek evidence that is pertinent to that matter.  Despite Respondent’s persistent refrain in this regard, Respondent cites no authority under which this Court could severely curtail Judgment Creditor’s rights to take a judgment debtor examination because of other pending litigation.  Further, Respondent cites no authority that Judgment Creditor’s rights to obtain discovery in case No. 23STCV01162 are circumscribed such that any information Judgment Creditor might obtain in its judgment debtor examination would not otherwise be discoverable in that case.  Likewise, Respondent fails to show the other parties in case No. 23STCV01162 do not have the right to pursue complete discovery in that case. 

While Respondent argues Judgment Creditor triggered a stay in case No. 23STCV01162 by filing a motion to compel arbitration, there appear to be no such motion pending in that matter.  Rather, Judgment Creditor filed a motion for summary adjudication and argues, in the alternative, the Court should compel arbitration.  That motion did not stay litigation of case No. 23STCV01162, as the Court granted Judgment Creditor’s motion for leave to amend in that matter after Judgment Creditor filed its motion for summary adjudication.  (See October 30, 2024 Minute Order in case No. 23STCV01162).  The motion for protective order is denied.

Judgment Creditor requests sanctions for opposing Respondent’s motion for protective order.  Again, the Court declines to award sanctions.  By Judgment Creditor’s admission, Judgment Creditor made no effort to informally resolve the dispute between the parties regarding the judgment debtor examination.  The Court, therefore, considers sanctions inappropriate.

Judgment Creditor is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV23191    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV23191

MOTIONS

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant McDonald’s Restaurants of California, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Liam Carballo

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Liam Carballo (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendant McDonald’s Restaurants of California (“Defendant”), contending Defendant served Plaintiff contaminated milk.  Defendant demurs to the sixth cause of action for fraud/deceit/intentional misrepresentation in the first amended complaint.  Defendant also moves to strike the punitive damages allegations from the first amended complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motions. 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action is for fraud, which must be alleged with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) 

To state a case of action for fraud and deceit based on concealment, Plaintiff must allege Defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact Defendant was under a duty to disclose to Plaintiff with the intent to defraud Plaintiff; Plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted in the same way if Plaintiff knew of the concealed fact; and the concealment caused damages to Plaintiff.  (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 868.)  In this regard, Plaintiff alleges Defendant was “aware of various health code violations, such as but not limited to: 1.) Not certifying employees with mandatory food handling training; 2.) Not properly packaging the food products, including the subject product; 3.) Not properly refrigerating the food products, including the subject product; 4.) Not properly using temperature thermometers; 5.) Improper food storage, including the subject product; and 6.) Inadequate food storage space. In spite of this knowledge, [D]efendant[] intentionally concealed this information from the public, including Plaintiff.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 62.)  Plaintiff does not allege material facts, which Defendant had a duty to disclose to Plaintiff.  Instead, Plaintiff speculates about improper practices. 

To state a case of action for intentional misrepresentation, Plaintiff must allege Defendant made misrepresentations of facts to Plaintiff with knowledge of their falsity and intent to defraud Plaintiff; Plaintiff justifiably relied on Defendant’s misrepresentations; and Plaintiff sustained damages as a result.  (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant “advertise[s] [its] food, including the subject product and expressly misrepresented through [its] website that [its] food, including the subject product, [is] ‘quality, great-tasting’ and ‘refreshing’” (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 60) and  Defendant knew these misrepresentations were false because Defendant knew or should have known that its milk was contaminated (id., Complaint, ¶ 61.)  These allegations do not support a claim for intentional misrepresentation in that Plaintiff does not allege he relied on any misrepresentations on Defendant’s website, and he  does not allege misrepresentations of fact.  General statements Defendant’s food is palatable are not statements of fact but rather of opinion. 

Accordingly, the Court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

To the extent it is not moot, the Court likewise grants the motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages with leave to amend. 

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (College Hospital, supra,  8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 (Grieves), internal citations and footnotes omitted.) 

Plaintiff does not allege any specific facts that constitute malicious conduct, which could support an award of punitive damages.  (See, e.g., First Amended Complaint, ¶ 71.)  Accordingly, to the extent it is not moot, the Court grants the motion to strike with leave to amend.

The Court has already provided Plaintiff with an opportunity to state a claim for fraud and a claim for punitive damages against Defendant, and Plaintiff has failed to do so.  As such, leave to amend is likely futile. (Grieves, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 168.)  However, in an abundance of caution, the Court will provide Plaintiff with one more opportunity to amend to state these claims.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action in Plaintiff’s complaint is sustained with leave to amend.

To the extent it is not moot, Defendant’s motion to strike is granted with leave to amend. 

Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 20 days of his receipt of notice of this order. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Motion for Order of Discharge

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Cypress Escrow and Settlement Services, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Cypress Escrow and Settlement Services, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) moves for discharge from this action.

ANALYSIS

A stakeholder which claims no interest in funds held may apply to the Court for permission to deposit the money or property with the court clerk, and for an order discharging the stakeholder from further liability to the adverse claimants.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 386.5.) 

In the instant case, Plaintiff held $50,000 in escrow in connection with a real property sale.  After a dispute arose between the parties concerning the sale as to the disposition of the funds, Plaintiff filed this complaint in interpleader.  Pursuant to this Court’s August 20, 2024 order, Plaintiff deposited the funds with the Court.  Under these circumstances, discharge of Plaintiff is warranted (Pacific Loan Management Corp. v. Superior Court (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 1485, 1489-1490), and the Court discharges Plaintiff from this action.

A stakeholder may seek reimbursement for costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred, and the Court may order payment thereof out of the funds deposited by the stakeholder.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 386.6.)  The award of attorney fees is limited to “costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred in [the interpleader] action”  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff seeks attorney fees in the amount of $8,562.  Plaintiff relies on the declaration of its attorney, Courtney L.  Puritsky (“Counsel”) in support of its request.  Counsel states he has incurred attorney fees and costs in the amount of $8,562 in litigating this action.  (Declaration of Courtney L. Puritsky, ¶ 6.)  Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees and costs.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV18650    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV18650

MOTION

Motion to Stay Action

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Simply Good Foods USA, Inc., and Quest Nutrition, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Zachary Kazarian, Kyle Kazarian, Jake Jessa, Christopher Quemena, Chris Van de Polder, and Richie Fiene

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Zachary Kazarian, Kyle Kazarian, Jake Jessa, Christopher Quemena, Chris Van de Polder, and Richie Fiene (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Simply Good Foods USA, Inc., and Quest Nutrition, LLC (“Defendants”) seeking declaratory relief concerning their rights under employment agreements Plaintiffs entered into with Defendants.  Plaintiffs’ current employer, Legendary Foods, LLC (“Legendary”), separately filed an action against Defendants in federal court: Legendary Foods, LLC v. Simply Good Foods USA, Inc., et al. (C.D. Cal., 2024, No. 2:24-cv-06337 (Legendary Foods)).  Defendants move to stay this action pending resolution of the Legendary Foods matter.  Plaintiffs oppose the motion.   

ANALYSIS

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.”  (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.)  “It is black letter law that, when a federal action has been filed covering the same subject matter as is involved in a California action, the California court has the discretion but not the obligation to stay the state court action . . . .  In exercising its discretion the court should consider the importance of discouraging multiple litigation designed solely to harass an adverse party, and of avoiding unseemly conflicts with the courts of other jurisdictions.  It should also consider whether the rights of the parties can best be determined by the court of the other jurisdiction because of the nature of the subject matter, the availability of witnesses, or the stage to which the proceedings in the other court have already advanced . . . .  The California Supreme Court also has isolated another critical factor favoring a stay of the state court action in favor of the federal action[:] the federal action is pending in California not some other state . . . .”  (Caiafa Prof. Law Corp. v. State Farm Fire & Cas. Co. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 800, 804.)

Defendants argue Legendary filed the Legendary Foods action approximately 30 minutes before Plaintiffs filed this case.  That contention is not accurate.  Legendary filed the complaint in Legendary Foods on July 26, 2024 at 1:57 PM, which was 20 minutes after Plaintiffs filed the complaint in this case.  (See Declaration of Shara Evaskovich in Support of Plaintiff’s Opposition to Motion to Stay, ¶¶ 4-7, Exhibit A; Complaint for Declaratory Relief, p. 1 (file stamp).)  As “the principle of comity may call for a discretionary refusal of the court to entertain the second suit pending determination of the first-filed action . . . ,” this Court is not required to stay this case in favor of Legendary Foods, as Plaintiffs filed this case first.  (Gregg v. Superior Court (1987) 194 Cal.App.3d 134, 136 (Gregg).) 

Further, Plaintiffs are not parties in the Legendary Foods case.  Consequently, this Court cannot preclude Plaintiffs “from pursuing an action because other litigants are suing the same defendants over similar grievances in another forum.”  (Gregg, supra, 194 Cal.App.3d at p. 138.)  The motion is denied.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
