DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00956    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: P

Motion for Summary Judgment or in the Alternative Summary Adjudication

Moving Party: Defendants IHG Management LLC and HPT TRS IHG-2-Inc

Responding Party: Plaintiff Beth Hurewitz

Ruling: Defendants IHG Management LLC and HPT TRS IHG-2-Inc’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED. The Court GRANTS summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s first, fourth, and seventh cause of action for negligence, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and private nuisance in favor of the Defendants as they are barred under the statute of limitations.

Moving party is to give notice.

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

BACKGROUND

On October 14, 2022, Plaintiff Beth Hurewitz (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant IHG Management LLC and TRS IHG-2 Inc. (“Defendants”) for (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Failure to Disclose Latent Defect; (3) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (4) Breach of Contract; (5) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (6) Private Nuisance; and (7) Unfair Business Practices under Business and Professions Code §§ 17200, et seq., 17500.

On November 18, 2022 Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) for (1) Negligence; (2) Fraud — Negligent Misrepresentation; (3) Fraud — Intentional Misrepresentation; (4) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (5) Breach of Contract; (6) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (7) Private Nuisance; and (8) Unfair Business Practices under Business and Professions Code §§ 17200, et seq., 17500. — Negligent Misrepresentation.

Defendants jointly filed their Answer to the FAC on March 6, 2023

On March 7, 2023, Defendants filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff for (1) Implied Equitable Indemnity; (2) Express Contractual Indemnity; (3) Implied Contractual Indemnity; (4) Contribution; (6) Appointment of Fault; and (7) Declaratory Relief.

Plaintiff filed her Answer to the Cross-Complaint on April 28, 2023.

On February 28, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion for summary judgment and in the alternative summary adjudication. Plaintiff filed her opposition and a declaration on May 8, 2024. Defendant’s reply and objections were filed May 17, 2024.

Defendants object to the declaration and various exhibits attached to the declaration of Carlos De La Paz:

1) Exhibit 1 (Authentication of Entire Exhibit): Sustained

2) Exhibit 6

a. Page 37:20-25 (Hearsay): Overruled

b. Page 38:1-2 (Hearsay, Relevance): Overruled

c. Page 37:20-25: Duplicative

3) Declaration

a. Paragraph 2: Overruled

STATEMENT OF FACTS

Plaintiff Beth Hurewitz (hereinafter “Hurewitz”) entered into a commercial lease for space within the Crowne Plaza Hotel in Redondo Beach, California on September 5, 2019. (Undisputed Material Fact (“UMF”) 1.) The lease provided that Hurewitz would use the space for a business that would provide spa and massage services. (Defendant’s Compendium of Exhibits, Exh. A (hereinafter “Lease”), section I(B). The owner of the Hotel is HPT TRS IHG-2, Inc. (hereinafter “HTP”), and the Hotel is managed by IHG Management (Maryland) LLC.

Hurewitz took possession of the space on or about September 5, 2019 and maintained exclusive possession of the space for the periods of time relevant to this lawsuit. (UMF 5.) After taking possession, Hurewitz began to experience symptoms of illness in December of 2019. (UMF 6.) She became aware that there was mold in the heating, ventilation and air conditioning ducts on March 11, 2020. (UMF 6.) Hurewitz believes she became ill because of the mold in the ducts. (UMF 8.)

Hurewitz testified that she spoke with “Winnie Kwok” in April 2019 about operating a spa within the Hotel. (De La Paz Decl. Exh. 5.) Ms. Kwok told Hurewitz that the Hotel “will be

inspecting” or “will be cleaning” the space Hurewitz was interested in leasing. (Id. at Exh. 6.) Hurewitz did not negotiate or sign a lease agreement with Ms. Kwok. (Id.)

The Lease signed by Hurewitz had the following language in Section VII (titled “Indemnification”):

Lessee further releases Lessor and Manager from liability for any damages sustained by Lessee or any other person claiming by, through or under Lessee due to the Leased Premises, the Hotel, or any part thereof or any appurtenances thereto becoming out of repair, or due to the happening of any accident, including by ont limited to any damage caused by water, snow, windstorm, tornado, gas, steam, electrical wiring, sprinkler system, plumbing, heating and air conditioning apparatus and from any acts or omissions of tenants or other occupants of the Hotel…Lessee hereby expressly releases and discharges Lessor and Manager from any and all liability and responsibility for any loss, injury, or damage incurred or claimed by reason of damage to property located on the Leased Premises that is owned, leased, managed, or controlled by Lessee, regardless whether such loss, injury, or damage is caused by the negligence of the Manager, Lessor or Lessor’s agents or other tenants. (Lease, p. 7.)

Hurewitz testified that someone told her that there was mold in the HVAC cabinet and that “Somebody has peeled away the outer layer of drywall.” (De La Paz Decl. Exh. 10, 48:11-17.) Hurewitz further testified that she hired a mold testing company to test both her apartment and the leased space. However, all the evidence submitted in support of this assertion is hearsay. As such, it is not admissible evidence which can be considered in defense of this motion.

DISCUSSION

Defendants pray for an order for summary judgment and in the alternative for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s FAC on the grounds that Defendants have complete defenses to each cause of action alleged in the FAC.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the Motion fails because there are triable issues of fact as to Plaintiff’s causes of action as alleged in the FAC, and Defendants cannot establish a complete defense.

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c).) The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742- 743.) Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) When a defendant seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence that either one or more elements of a cause of action cannot be established or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).) The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions, or ‘ultimate’ facts” and the courts must construe the evidence in support of the opposing party, resolving any doubts in favor of the opposing party. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639; Scalf, 128 Cal.App.4th at 1519; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial movant’s burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.) Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p).) The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.) Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

As to any alternative request for summary adjudication of issues, such alternative relief must be clearly set forth in the Notice of Motion and the general burden-shifting rules apply but the issues upon which summary adjudication may be sought are limited by statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(f)(1).) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Id.)

Exculpatory Clause

Defendants contend that the exculpatory provisions contained in Section VII of the lease constitute a complete defense to all causes of action alleged in the Complaint.

Defendants provide evidence that Plaintiff contracted to release IHG its agents and employees, as well as IHG’s Manager and its Manager’s employees from any liability to Plaintiff for “personal injury, death, property damage or otherwise arising out of [Plaintiff’s] use and occupation of the Lease Premises.” (UMF#2; COE Ex. A) The Lease Agreement released the Defendants “from any and all liability and responsibility for any loss, injury or damage incurred or claimed by reason of damage to property located on the Leased Premises.” (UMF#3,4 COE Ex. A) Defendants argue that each of the negligence actions are based on Plaintiff’s claims she suffered personal injury when allegedly exposed to mold in the leased unit. Finally, Plaintiff cannot establish a breach of duty because Defendants’ alleged duty to inspect was waived by Plaintiff in the contract. Plaintiff took the unit “as is” and acknowledged no promises regarding the condition of the premises were made to her. (Id.) Based on this, the Court finds that Defendants have satisfied their burden under Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(p)(2).

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that there is material evidence that Defendants knew about or should have known about the HVAC problem, the water leak, and the mold growth before leasing the property to Plaintiff. Plaintiff provides evidence that once she suspected that she was being exposed to mold, she hired a mold investigator to test the rental unit for mold. (De La Paz Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1; Molony Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. D.)

“[I]t is well established that that the tenant under a commercial lease may agree to limit the scope of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, whether express or implied, as well as the implied covenant of fair dealing. Frittelli, Inc. v. 350 N. Canon Drive, LP (2011) 202 Cal. App. 4th 35, 43 (internal citations omitted). “Courts have affirmed lease terms that exempted the landlord from

liability arising from conduct by the landlord and neighboring, as well as lease terms that limited the tenant's remedies for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment.” Id. (internal citations omitted). However, to the extent a contract seeks to “shield the lessor and its agents from liability for negligence, the exemption is subject to the public policy disfavoring attempts by contract to limit liability for future torts.” Id. A commercial lease between business entities does not implicate the “public interest;” however, an exculpatory clause within such a lease is “subject to special scrutiny, insofar as it purports to exempt the lessor from liability from ordinary negligence.” Id. at 43-44.

When assessing the impact of an exculpatory clause in the context of a motion for summary judgment, the court must look “at the plain language of the clause, viewed within the lease as a whole and examine whether the clause clearly discloses an intent to exempt the lessor from liability for ordinary negligence.” Garcia v. D/AQ Corporation (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 902, 908. If a lessee alleged that the lessor committed “ordinary, passive negligence [such as] the failure to discover a dangerous condition or to perform a duty imposed by law” an exculpatory clause shields the lessor from liability. Id. at 910. That shield is enforceable “when the parties knowingly bargain for the protection.” Id.

In this case, Hurewitz asserts that there was mold in the space she leased and asserts that Defendants had an obligation to inspect and remediate it. However, she provides no admissible evidence to support her assertion that Defendants knew or should have known about the issue. Even if the court were to conclude that Defendants had a duty to learn of the existence of mold, the lease provides Defendants a shield to liability. This case is not legally distinguishable from Frittelli or Garcia – at best, Defendants committed “ordinary, passive negligence” and the Lease “expressly releases and discharges [Defendants] from any and all liability [for damage to Plaintiff]…caused by the negligence [of Defendants].” Lease at VII.

Given the above, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the causes of action alleging ordinary negligence is GRANTED.

Statute of Limitations

Defendants argue that the first cause of action for Negligence, the fourth cause of action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, the sixth cause of action for Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment, and the seventh cause of action for Private Nuisance are all time-barred by the applicable two-year statutes of limitation.

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1, an action for assault, battery, or injury to, or for the death of, an individual caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another must be brought within two years. (Code Civ. Proc. § 335.1)

Judicial Council Emergency Rule 9, subdivision (a), states, “Notwithstanding any other

law, the statutes of limitations and repose for all civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Appendix I, Emergency rule 9.)

Here, Defendants meet their burden by providing evidence that Plaintiff discovered the presence of mold and its alleged impact on her health on March 11, 2020. (UMF 6 COE Ex. B, Ex. C) Defendants note that the statute of limitations was tolled by the Covid-19 pandemic and the

Judicial Counsel Emergency Rule 9. The last day for Plaintiff to file a negligence action was September 8, 2022. Plaintiff, however, did not file this action until October 14, 2022. Thus, Defendants have met their evidentiary burden under Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(p)(2).

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that her claims for Quiet Enjoyment and private nuisance actions are not barred under the statute of limitations because they are subject to a four-year and three-year statute of limitations, respectively.

Here, since the sixth cause of action for breach of quiet enjoyment stems from a breach of the party's lease agreement, the statute of limitations for the claim would be four years. (Kumar v. Ramsey, (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 1110.) The Court disagrees, however, with the Plaintiff’s contention that a three-year statute of limitation applies to the seventh cause of action for private nuisance. A private nuisance action claiming injury to real property is subject to a three-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure § 338. A private nuisance claiming a personal injury is subject to the two-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure § 335.1.

Plaintiff’s nuisance action arises from the injuries she sustained to her health due to the mold. Thus, the nuisance claim would be subject to the two-year statute of limitations. Therefore, the Court finds that Defendants are entitled to summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s first cause of action for Negligence, fourth cause of action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and seventh cause of action for private nuisance.

Fraud – Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation

Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligent and intentional misrepresentation fail because Plaintiff cannot establish that Defendants made any misrepresentations regarding the condition of the unit when entering the lease.

“[A] cause of action for negligent misrepresentation typically is subject to a two-year limitations period. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 339; Ventura County Nat. Bank v. Macker (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1528, 1530–1531, 57 Cal.Rptr.2d 418. See generally, 1 Schwing, Cal. Affirmative Defenses, supra, § 25:21, pp. 1415–1416, fns. 8–9.) That same two-year period generally applies to actions ‘upon a contract, obligation or liability not founded upon an instrument of writing....’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 339, subd. 1.) A cause of action for fraud or mistake is governed by a three-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) And the limitations period for a written contract is four years.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Services (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1316.” “Where more than one statute might apply to a particular claim, ‘a specific limitations provision prevails over a more general provision.’” (Id.)

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

Defendants provide evidence that IHG did not know there was mold in Plaintiff’s unit nor that they represented that the unit was free of any dangerous conditions when it was leased to Plaintiff. (UMF#3, 4; Ex B ¶9-24; Plaintiff’s Deposition Testimony Ex G 50:4-12.) Defendants further argue that Plaintiff fails to identify the person or people who guaranteed these conditions and, as a result, cannot prove misrepresentation. Thus based on this, Defendants satisfy their burden under Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(p)(2).

In opposition, Plaintiff provides evidence that in June of 2019, Defendant’s agent, Ms. Kwok represented to Plaintiff that Defendant IHG was inspecting and cleaning the Unit. (De La Paz Decl. Exh 6.) Plaintiff believed that Defendant had the responsibility to make sure the Unit was safe before Defendant turned it over. (Id. at Exh 12.)

Plaintiff relies on a statement by Ms. Kwok in or around April of 2019, that the space would be “cleaned” or “inspected.” However, at the time the statement was made, the space was being used by a different tenant as a spa facility. (De La Paz Decl. Exh. 4.) Ms. Kwok was not a manager when Plaintiff signed the Lease and there is no allegation of any other conversation with an agent of Defendant prior to the Lease being signed. In addition, the Lease provides that the property will be delivered in “as is” condition. (UMF 3.)

Given the evidence presented, there is, at best, a passing remark by an employee of Defendant that the space would be “inspected” or “cleaned” before the Lease began. That remark was made months prior to a lease being signed, months prior to Plaintiff’s walk-through of the property, and by an employee who was no longer employed by Defendants when the lease was signed. Finally, Plaintiff concedes that she spent significant sums of money renovating the space after the prior tenant left, suggesting that she was fully aware of any deficiencies with the leased space and had taken the space in “as-is condition.” Taken together, Plaintiff has not shown any evidence that Ms. Kwok made a misrepresentation or that Plaintiff relied on any misrepresentation to her detriment.

Therefore, the Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the Second and Third causes of action for fraud are GRANTED.

Unfair Business Practices

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s cause of action for Unfair Business Practices fails because Plaintiff has failed to establish any evidence that Defendants engaged in unfair competition under Business & Professions Code. Section 17200.

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

Plaintiff’s cause of action for Unfair Business Practices is predicated on her other cause of action for fraud. As the court GRANTS summary adjudication as to those causes of action, Defendants are entitled to judgment as a matter of law as to this cause of action.

Punitive Damages

Defendants contend that there is no evidence to support a claim for exemplary damages because there is no evidence of any misrepresentation or fraud. As the court has determined that Plaintiff cannot prevail on her fraud causes of action, there is no basis for punitive damages.

CONCLUSION

In light of the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

Case Number: 23TRCV01702    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: P

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Freddy Alberto Rondon

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.,

The Court has considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

BACKGROUND

This is a lemon law action arising from an allegedly defective 2022 Honda Civic (the “Subject Vehicle”) that Plaintiff purchased on April 14, 2022. Over the next year, Plaintiff made three visits to Defendant’s authorized repair facility for issues with the vehicle slowing down on its own and alerting collision even though there were no obstacles in front of it, rattling noises coming from the front left side of the vehicle, and dashboard lights remaining illuminated. On Plaintiff’s last visit, a technician replaced the Subject Vehicle’s actuator.

Plaintiff filed the operative Complaint against Defendants alleging various causes of action under the Song-Beverly Act and for negligent repair.

Procedural Background

According to the declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel, Chris Grigoryan (“Grigoryan Decl.”): (1) on January 11, 2024, Plaintiff propounded Request for Production of Documents, Set Two (“RFP”) (Grigoryan Decl. at ¶ 14; Exh. 3); (2) on February 12, 2024, Defendant served its responses to Plaintiff’s RFPs (Id. at ¶ 15 and Exh. 4); (3) on February 15, 2024, Plaintiff initiated the meet and confer process by sending a letter to Defendant explaining the relevance of each request (Id. at ¶ 17 and Exh. 5); and (4) the parties continued to correspond via email and telephone, and agreed no narrow down the dispute to the RFPs at issue in this motion (Id. at ¶¶ 18-22).

MEET AND CONFER

The meet and confer requirement has been met.

DISCUSSION

Under California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 2017.010 “any person may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking

discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “The Court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611.) “For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.” (Id.) Discovery statutes are “construed liberally in favor of disclosure.” (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.) Where a party objects to a motion to compel, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) To support an objection to a request for production of documents, the objecting party has to establish the validity of its objections with supporting facts in order to meet its burden. (Southern Pac. Co. v. Superior Court (1969) 3 Cal.App.3d 195, 198.)

Plaintiff’s RFPs

Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiff’s RFP nos. 10, 14, 23, 24, 33, 37, 46, 47, 56, 60, 69, and 70. Plaintiff contends that these requests see Defendant’s internal investigations of defects in the Subject Vehicle. Specifically, nos. 10-32 concern electrical defects; nos. 33-55 concern structural defects, and nos. 56-78 concern suspension defects. Plaintiff does not request monetary sanctions.

Plaintiff asserts that: (1) the scope of discovery is broad; (2) the requests are relevant and necessary to establish Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly claims; and (3) the prevailing authorities support discovery of documents relating to similar defects experienced by other customers.

Defendant opposes Plaintiff’s motion on the grounds that: (1) Plaintiff’s motion seeks documents not requested in the RFP; (2) Plaintiff ignores Defendant’s substantive responses that it had no responsive documents; (3) nonconformities that the manufacturer has only been given one opportunity to fix are not actionable under the Song-Beverly Act; (4) Plaintiff’s RFPs are vague and overbroad; and (5) other consumers’ experiences are beyond the scope of discovery.

Plaintiff contends that the requested documents are relevant to show the existence of defects not only in the Subject Vehicle but also other 2022 Honda Civics, as well as Defendant’s knowledge of such defects. Plaintiff contends that these documents evince necessary elements of Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly claims, and that Plaintiff’s expert requires the technical information requested to give reliable opinion testimony as to the nature of the defects.

Defendant attacks the authorities Plaintiff cites. In his motion, Plaintiff relies heavily on Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138 and Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967 for the position that the scope of discovery is not limited to Plaintiff’s vehicle; rather, similar defects experienced by other customers are also discoverable. Defendant contends that there is no indication in these cases that the manufacturer ever raised an objection based on relevance during discovery and courts do not adjudicate matters that are not timely raise because they are considered waived. It is true that neither the Donlen court nor the Doppes court address the permissible scope of discovery. However, the cases’ procedural history show that trial courts routinely permit discovery of evidence pertaining to defects in vehicles other than the plaintiff’s vehicle.

Nevertheless, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s RFPs are overbroad. Requests must be reasonably particularized from the perspective of the party burdened with producing the materials. (See Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 222). Plaintiff has limited the requests to defects that appeared in cars of the same year, make and model as the Subject Vehicle. However, Plaintiff failed to specifically identify the types of defects in those vehicles and their relevance to Plaintiff’s claims. As Defendant pointed out, Plaintiff’s motion argues for the production of information relating to Powertrain Control Modules, Body Control Modules, written warranties, warranty policies and procedures, how AHM handles customer complaints, and refund policies and procedures. (Motion, p. 8-10.) But none of that information is sought in the discovery requests at issue or the parties’ meet and confers.

Plaintiff failed to define the term “field technical report”, which appears in every single RFP at issue in this motion.

Further, as defined, the three types of defects for which Plaintiff seeks related discovery each combine seemingly distinct symptoms and include overbroad phrases, such as “shall be understood to mean such defects which result in symptoms including . . .”; “and/or similar concerns”, etc.

Lastly, Plaintiff ignores that aside from objections, Defendant did provide substantive responses to the RFPs. (See, e.g., Defendant’s response to RFP no. 10, Grigoryan Decl., Exh. 3 [“Subject to and without waiving these objections, AHM is unable to comply with this request. AHM has made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry in an effort to comply with this request and has no field technical reports directed to warranty parts replacement trends and no responsive documents have ever existed.”].)

CONCLUSION

Pursuant to the discussion above, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s second set of requests for production is DENIED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV02355    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Defendant Woodforest National Bank

Responding Party: Plaintiff PVR Group Inc.

DEFENDANT WOODFOREST NATIONAL BANK’S DEMURRER

RULING

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. Defendant Woodforest National Bank’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety as to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

Procedural Background

On July 20, 2023, PVR Group, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Woodforest National Bank (Defendant) alleging claims in relation to breach of contract surrounding the terms of a loan. After the Court sustained Defendant’s initial demurrer to the Complaint, a First Amended Complaint (FAC) was filed on March 4, 2024. The FAC alleges six causes of action:

1. Breach of Verbal Contract;

2. Breach of Implied In Fact Contract;

3. Breach of Covenant of Good Faith & Fair Dealing;

4. Fraud;

5. Declaratory Relief;

6. Violation of Business and Professions Code §17000 et seq.

The motion now before the Court is Defendant Woodforest National Bank’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint (Demurrer). Defendant demurs to each of the six causes of action. Plaintiff opposes the Demurrer, and Defendant files a reply.

Factual Background

In 2017 Plaintiff requested and was given a loan (the Loan) by Defendant. (FAC, ¶ 10. Also see Declaration of Thomas Aderhold, Exh. A.) At the time of the initial lending, Defendant did not require any collateral for the loan due to the long-term positive relationship with Plaintiff. (Id., ¶11.) Throughout the ensuing term of the loan, Plaintiff regularly paid the loan to Defendant’s satisfaction. (Id., ¶13.) With the arrival of the pandemic in 2020, Defendant asked Plaintiff to collateralize the loan’s remaining balance. (Id., ¶14.) Plaintiff complied with the

Defendant’s request, and additionally made larger than contractually required principal payments to mitigate Defendant’s concerns, with the understanding that in the short term the loan would convert into a long-term amortized loan, inclusive of principal and interest payments. (Id.)

Around the same time, Plaintiff alleges Defendant granted multiple forbearances to Plaintiff, and after said forbearances, Plaintiff resumed making large principal payments for short-term loan servicing purposes. (Id., ¶15.) The FAC then alleges that after the summer of 2022, Plaintiff approached Defendant to implement the long-term understanding/servicing of the loan, as Plaintiff was neither willing nor required to continue making larger than expected monthly payments and desired to implement the understanding negotiated with Defendant. (Id., ¶16.)

The terms and understanding was negotiated with Defendant’s representative David McDonald, Defendant’s Executive Vice President. Id., ¶17. During this time, Plaintiff and Defendant started reviewing the outstanding balance of the loan. Id., ¶18. Eventually, Plaintiff learned for the first time that Defendant had certain issues in relation to alleged deficiencies relating to the loan. (Id., ¶19.) In January 2023, Defendant took the position that plaintiff was “two payments behind,” which was apparently due to the fact Defendant, contrary to the aforementioned understanding and forbearances, had somehow not applied or extended the second forbearance that Plaintiff had been relying and acting upon. (Id., ¶24.)

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant’s retraction of the forbearance triggered a myriad of fees, charges, and penalties. (Id.) The FAC further alleges that in spite of Defendant’s extensive delay in addressing significant and material alleged deficiencies and payment-related matters, Defendant

took the unreasonable position that Plaintiff was now liable not only for past due amounts but also for “attorneys expenses” and various penalties and charges. (Id., ¶25.)

In May 2023, Defendant and Plaintiff agreed to amend certain terms of the loan reflecting the following terms: (1) Defendant would receive additional collateral of its choosing and provide certain reassurances and information as further guarantee to Defendant; (2) in return, the loan would be turned into a short-term interest-only loan, and (3) subsequently turn into a small-payment paydown and full-interest, followed by a balloon payment; and an interest rate higher than that applicable to the loan. (Id., ¶29.)

As part of the amendment, and as an inducement by Defendant for Plaintiff to purportedly enter into the amendment, Plaintiff was asked and agreed to provide additional unrelated properties to pledge for the benefit of Defendant, for purposes of securing the loan and amendment. (Id., ¶30.) Plaintiff was asked and agreed to provide additional financial and personal information. (Id., ¶31.) In June 2023, during the final phase relating to the amendment, Defendant once again switched gears and informed Plaintiff that it did not intend to honor the amendment and deemed Plaintiff in default, which triggered immediate repayment of the loan in full. (Id., ¶32.) Plaintiff’s last timely payment due under the loan was not accepted by Defendant, who returned it without explanation. (Id., ¶33.)

Request for Judicial Notice

Concurrently filed with their Demurrer, Defendant files a request for judicial notice, requesting that the Court judicially notice the Court’s own Tentative Ruling on the previous Demurrer. As the Court’s review of the filings in relation to the instant Demurrer typically necessitate a review of prior related documents, like the tentative ruling, judicial request is unnecessary and therefore denied.

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff files an objection to the exhibits filed in the Declaration of Thomas Aderhold (Aderhold Decl.), arguing that the statement in paragraph three of the Aderhold Decl. is improper, lack foundation, and that the declarant lacks personal knowledge. The objection is overruled as the declarant is the custodian of the records attached.

DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a).) “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.”

Defendant submits the Declaration of Christopher O. Rivas (Rivas Decl.) which states that the parties conferred on April 5, 2024, however, the parties were unable to reach an agreement. Nonetheless, the requirements of Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a) have been satisfied. The Court now turns its attention to the Demurrer.

Legal Standard for Demurrer

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Analysis for Demurrer

a. 1st Cause of Action for Breach of Contract and 2nd Cause of Action for Breach of Implied In Fact Contract

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

A “contract implied in fact” consists of obligations arising from mutual agreement and intent to promise where agreement and promise have not been expressed in words. (Rose v. County of San Benito (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 688, 714.)

Defendant demurs to both these causes of action on two main grounds: (1) they are barred by the statute of frauds and (2) they are barred by the parole evidence rule. The Court agrees, and additionally notes that (3) the FAC fails to demonstrate the existence of a verbal contract.

First, the Court notes that the FAC does not appear to reference any written agreement with regard to this first cause of action. The FAC appears to target the unwritten agreements Plaintiff alleges were made, specifically alleging that Defendant failed to “(i) honor the promised forbearance, (ii) re-negotiate the terms of the Loan, (iii) prepare and/or provide and/or finalize documents as reasonably requested by and expected from PLAINTIFF pursuant to the promises and representations made by the Defendants and/or their agents/representatives to PLAINTIFF…” (FAC, ¶38.)

1. Statute of Frauds

As relevant here, Under Civil Code §1624(a), the following contracts are invalid, unless they, or some note or memorandum thereof, are in writing and subscribed by the party to be charged or by the party’s agent: “(1) An agreement that by its terms is not to be performed within a year from the making thereof. . . . (7) A contract, promise, undertaking, or commitment to loan money or to grant or extend credit, in an amount greater than $100,000 . . . made by a person engaged in the business of lending or arranging for the lending of money or extending credit. . . .”

The three promises explicitly mentioned under the first cause of action could all presumably be completed within one year, however, the agreements surround the extension of credit well beyond $100,000.00. Plaintiff has not alleged that any promise to honor the forbearance, promise to renegotiate the terms of the Loan, nor any promise to prepare and/or provide and/or finalize documents was made in writing. Without an allegation that these were in writing, the claim is barred by the statute of frauds.

2. Parole Evidence Rule

The parol evidence rule provides that “[t]erms set forth in a writing intended by the parties as a final expression of their agreement with respect to such terms as are included therein may not be contradicted by evidence of any prior agreement or of a contemporaneous oral agreement.” (Alling v. Universal Manufacturing Corp. (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1435.) As it pertains to the terms of the initial Loan agreement, no other extrinsic evidence may be permitted that contradicts the terms of the Loan, therefore any writing, statement, or other evidence that does not fall within

an exception is barred. This applies to the alleged verbal promises that Plaintiff alleges were made. Plaintiff, upon opposition, does note that fraudulent representations are admissible, and Plaintiff alleges fraud as their fourth cause of action regarding these same verbal promises. However, the FAC encounters one final fatal barrier; it fails to properly allege the existence of a verbal contract.

3. FAC Fails to Allege a Proper Verbal Contract

As aforementioned, a plaintiff can plead the legal effect of a contract rather than its precise language, however, the FAC fails to do so. It is unclear within the FAC what consideration was given for the (i) forbearance, (ii) the promise to renegotiate the terms of the Loan, or (iii) the promise to prepare and/or provide and/or finalize documents. The FAC asserts these promises were made, but it is unclear what was offered, accepted, or given as consideration. Without these, the first cause of action fails on the first element, and the second contract cannot stand if there is no contract implied in fact in the first place. The Demurrer is sustained as to the first and second causes of action.

b. 3rd Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

“A breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing involves something beyond breach of the contractual duty itself and it has been held that bad faith implies unfair dealing rather than mistaken judgment.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1394.) “If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated … [T]he only justification for asserting a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant is to obtain a tort recovery.” (Id. at pp. 1394-1395.) To recover in tort for breach of the implied covenant, the defendant must “have acted unreasonably or without proper cause.” (Id. at p. 1395, citations and italics omitted.)

Plaintiff clarifies upon opposition that the third cause of action targets the alleged implied-in-facts agreements that were made verbally. However, as the implied-in-fact agreements fall to demurrer, the third cause of action must fail. Without the properly plead existence of these verbal contracts, no cause of action based on them can stand. The Demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained.

c. 4th Cause of Action for Fraud

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

The Court stated in its prior ruling that the allegations of fraud in the fourth cause of action were conclusory, that deficiency remains. The FAC alleges that “Defendants intentionally and maliciously made misrepresentations and promises to material matters, and concealed material facts that they had a duty to disclose…” (FAC, ¶ 54.) According to the FAC this was part of a scheme to induce Plaintiff to trust Defendant and provide collateral for an uncollateralized loan. These statements are conclusory. Because Defendant is a corporation, Plaintiff is required to state who said what, when. The FAC fails to do so. It is unclear what misrepresentation was made to Plaintiff and when. It is also unclear as to how this misrepresentation was to induce Plaintiff to rely on these statements and what damage was suffered by Plaintiff collateralizing a loan that was initially uncollateralized. Moreover, the FAC alleges without supporting facts that Defendant never intended to enter into any amendment and intended only to defraud Plaintiff. Without proper supporting facts, this cause of action falls to demurrer. Therefore, the Demurrer is sustained as to the fourth cause of action.

d. 5th Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

Defendant argues that the fifth cause of action is derivative of the first two causes of action, and because the first two were not properly plead, the sixth fails as well. The Court agrees. Therefore, the Demurrer is sustained as to the fifth cause of action.

e. 6th Cause of Action for Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq.

To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

Defendant argues that this claim fails because plaintiff fails to allege any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent conduct by the bank. Defendant contends it was not obligated to modify the agreement and any modification must have been in writing and signed by both parties. The Court agrees and sustains the Demurrer to the final cause of action.

Legal Standard and Analysis for Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. As there may be reasonable possibility of successful amendment, the Court grants 20 days leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

Defendant Woodforest National Bank’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in its entirety as to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.
