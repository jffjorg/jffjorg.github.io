Case Number: 21AVCV00419    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a premises liability action. Plaintiff  Jason Bailey (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on or about June 02, 2019, he was lawfully on the premises of Defendant OMG Sports II Inc., dba Rockin’ Jump Trampoline Park, Palmdale, erroneously sued as Rockin’ Jump, LLC (“Defendant”) at or near 1301 W Rancho Vista Blvd., Unit B, Palmdale, CA 935513 (the “Premises”) and was walking towards the cashier area when he stepped on water and/or a similar substance on the floor causing him to slip and fall. Plaintiff further alleges that the slip and fall caused him to endure severe injury and pain.

 

On May 24, 2021, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging two causes of action for Negligence and Premises Liability.

 

On February 02, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer. The Court notes that the Answer states that Defendant is “OMG SPORTS II INC., dba Rockin' Jump Trampoline Park, Palmdale (erroneously sued as ROCKIN' JUMP, LLC, a California Corporation) SBK EXPERIENCES, LLC (hereinafter "Rockin' Jump" or "Defendant"). The Court inquires at the hearing as to the addition of SBK Experiences, LLC in the Answer.

 

On July 07, 2023, the Court held a hearing on Defendant’s Ex Parte Application for an Order (1) Specially Setting Defendant's Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication or (2) Alternatively Continuing Trial and all Related Dates. The Court ultimately continued (1) the jury trial from October 13, 2023 to April 26, 2024, and (2) the Final Status Conference from October 04, 2023 to April 17, 2024. The new trial date governs all law, motion, and discovery.

 

On July 03, 2023, Defendant filed this Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition.

 

On February 01, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Summary Judgment – The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc.¿§437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67 (“Juge”), citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Cal.¿Code Civ.¿Proc.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. Discussion

 

Issues Framed by the Pleadings – As an initial matter, the Court’s review of the issues framed by the pleadings indicates that the entirety of the complaint is predicated upon whether Defendant was negligent in maintaining the Premises, ultimately causing a dangerous, defective, and/or unsafe condition in the form of the wet floor leading to Plaintiff’s fall, causing him to suffer an injury. (See Complaint.) While Plaintiff has alleged two causes of action sounding in General Negligence and Premises Liability, the gravamen of the complaint is one sounding entirely in premises liability, as the standard of review of negligence and premises liability are identical.[1]  Accordingly, the Court will assess the instant motion for summary judgment or adjudication on that basis.[2]

 

Application – Defendant presents that, as a condition of accessing the Premises, Plaintiff mandated that all customers sign a release agreement and Plaintiff filled and signed release agreements for himself and his two sons. (See Decl. Daisy Diaz ¶¶ 11-16; Exh. A; Decl. Paul Cooley ¶ 6; Exh. E and F at RFA No. 2.) Defendant argues that, as Plaintiff signed the release agreements, including an assumption of the risks clause, Defendant owed no duty to Plaintiff. Defendant also describes the risks as “open and obvious.” Defendant provides that: (1) assumption of the risk removes any duties owed to Plaintiff; (2) a release will be upheld so long as the exculpatory language clearly, explicitly, and unambiguously expresses that contracting parties’ intent; (3) the release agreement is valid, citing various cases; (4) courts have upheld releases of negligent conduct, citing Westlye v. Look Sports, Inc. (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 1715 (“Westlye”), Saenz v. Whitewater Voyages, Inc. (1990) Cal.App.3d 760 (“Saenz”), Allan v. Snow Summit (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 1358 (“Allan”), and Paralift, Inc. v. Superior Court (1993) 23 Cal.App.4th 748 (“Paralift”); (5) knowledge of the risk is not required to enforce a release; (6) a person who signs a release is demed to have read and understood the release.

 

The elements of¿a premises liability and¿negligence¿cause of action are the same:¿duty, breach, causation and damages. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp¿(2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)¿ “The owner of premises is under a duty to exercise ordinary care in the management of such premises¿in order to¿avoid exposing persons to an unreasonable risk of harm.¿ A failure to fulfill this duty is negligence.”¿(Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp.¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619;¿Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)¿¿While an¿owner of premises is not an insurer of the safety of its patrons, the owner still owes them a duty to exercise reasonable care in keeping the premises reasonably safe.¿(Ortega v. Kmart Corp.¿(2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.) “A store owner exercises ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the portions of the premises open to customers, and the care required is commensurate with the risks involved.”¿(Ibid.)¿¿

It is signed by Plaintiff and lists two participants, Plaintiff’s minor children. It is unclear from the document whether Plaintiff is signing on behalf of himself or solely on behalf of his children.

 

Defendant’s argument hinges on the release agreement as it believes the release agreement relieves it of any duty owed to Plaintiff. Notably, the release agreement does not explicitly describe the premises (i.e., whether it applies solely to the trampoline area or the whole premises), attempts to include being on the “premises” as an activity, and seeks to release Defendant from liability as to all negligent conduct, including Defendant’s own negligence.

Defendant’s argument related to when a release agreement will be upheld and the validity of the agreement does not incorporate the release agreement in this action. Rather, as to the argument describing when a release agreement will be upheld, the argument states only: “A release will generally be upheld so long as the exculpatory language clearly, explicitly, and unambiguously expresses the contracting parties’ intent. ‘It is the outward expression of the agreement, rather than a parties' expressed intention, which the court will enforce.’ Paralift, supra, 23 Cal. App. 4th at 755.” (MSJ 9:16-19.) Notably, there is no discussion as to why or how the release agreement in this action relates to the cited standard. Next, as to the argument that the release agreement in this action is valid, Defendant states only that “[m]any cases have demonstrated that release agreements are valid as a matter of law[]” and provides a string cite. (MSJ 9:20-28.) Again, there is no discussion of the release agreement at issue in this action.

 

As to the cases cited by Defendant for upholding releases due to negligent conduct, several are distinguished:

 

·         Westlye, supra, 17 Cal.App.4th 1715: This case stems from an injury incurred by the plaintiff when he fell while skiing at Sugar Bowl ski resort, and the left safety binding failed to release the ski from the boot. (Westlye, supra, 17 Cal.App.4th at 1723.) The plaintiff had rented the ski equipment from Klein's, which had purchased the equipment from the distributors. (Id. at 1723-24.) The interplay of express assumption of the risk and the release agreement in this case was reviewed and the Westlye court held that “as a matter of public policy, product suppliers cannot insulate themselves from strict liability in tort for injuries caused by defects in products they place on the market by obtaining a consumer's signature on an express assumption of risk.” (Id. at 1747.) The Westlye court reviewed various factors through the lens of contract law, products lability, and misrepresentation. This case does not discuss assumption of the risk in the context of premises liability.

·         Saenz, supra, Cal.App.3d 760: Saenz is a wrongful death action that stemmed from decedent’s death while whitewater rafting. (Saenz, supra, Cal.App.3d at 759-61.) A release agreement was signed prior to the whitewater rafting expedition. (Id. at 760.) The Saenz court held that “[w]ithout question the risk of death by drowning is a risk inherent in white water rafting and apparent to anyone about to embark upon a three-day recreational rafting trip[]” and  “[t]he release was valid.” (Id. at 766.) This is patently different from Plaintiff signing a release agreement for an indoor trampoline park and slipping and falling by a register.

·         Allan, supra, 51 Cal.App.4th 1358: This case concerned Plaintiff’s injuries which resulted from falling while skiing. (Allan, supra, 51 Cal.App.4th at 1363-64.) The release agreement at issue was binding as the injuries Allan sustained were encompassed by the release agreement which provided that plaintiff specifically agreed to release defendant and its employees from any liability for injuries caused by participating in the ski lesson. (Id. at 1366-1375.) As with Saenz, the facts in Allan are patently different from Plaintiff signing a release agreement for an indoor trampoline park and slipping and falling by a register.

·         Paralift, supra, 23 Cal.App.4th 748: This case concerns a wrongful death action brought by heirs of decedent, a highly qualified and licensed skydiver, who died due to the impact of his body on the surface of water during a landing. (Paralift, supra, 23 Cal.App.4th at 751.) The Paralift court went through a variety of elements and determined that the risks of the particular jump was in the reasonable expectations of both parties; the risks of parachuting activity stemmed from what defendant Paralift was to do according to the understanding of both parties; any negligence on the part of Paralift was reasonably related to the object or purpose for which the release was given;  the decedent's express release of any negligence liability on the part of Paralift binds his heirs in this action and provides Paralift with a complete defense; and the release was evidently the intention of the parties that it have a broad and ongoing scope, unlimited to a certain time period, a certain geographical vicinity, or certain weather conditions. The agreement, its scope, and the nature of the injury in Paralift are different from the action at hand.

 

First, as mentioned ante, there exists an issue as to whether Plaintiff signed for himself or solely his two children. This is shown by the signer and participant designations. (See Exh. A.) There appears to be another page which designates “signing party” and “included” minors which states: “The Signing Party listed below digitally agreed to the above agreements.) (Ibid.) This, too, provides no clarification as the only signing party is Plaintiff, who has two included minors, and the designation of individuals is distinct from the document signed (e.g. participants to included minors). There exists a question of consent as the contract is unclear as to who it applies to. (See Cal. Civ. Code § 1580.)

                                                                                                    

Unlike the cases cited above, slipping on a wet floor on the way to a cash register is not an inherent risk of going to a trampoline park which focuses on providing fun in the form of trampolines, activities that involve trampolines, and other fitness-related and/or recreational classes. However, the release agreement seemly tries to include use of the “premises,” an undefined term in the release agreement, as part of the definition of activities and attempts to release Defendant from all liability due to injury. Defendant has provided the following legal standard: “A release will generally be upheld so long as the exculpatory language clearly, explicitly, and unambiguously expresses the contracting parties’ intent.” (MSJ 9:16-17.) Defendant has not analyzed its own release agreement. While the instant release agreement attempts to release Defendant from liability as to all claims, it fails to (1) clearly identify the contracting parties as it is unclear whether Plaintiff signed on behalf of himself or his minor children (see ante); and (2) it is ambiguous as to where the on the premises the release agreement applies (see ante). For example, an individual who has not signed the agreement can access the lobby that Plaintiff was in and, if hurt, may sue. Further, Defendant has not provided case law showing that its release agreement should be upheld as all the cases cited are substantially different from the action at hand. The Court also notes that such a release agreement also leads to a question of whether the full release for any and all claims, including any of Defendant’s negligence, follows public policy. (See Hass v. RhodyCo Productions (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 11, 29 [citing Tunkl v. Regents of University of California (1963) 60 Cal.2d 92 (“Tunkl”)]; see also Celli v. Sports Car Club, Inc. (1972) 29 Cal.App.3d 511 [discussion of distinction between passive negligence and negligent omission and citation to Tunkl regarding whether “public policy in this state would permit judicial enforcement of a provision. . .designed to permit a tortfeasor to shift the risk of the injury to the victim even where the release is sufficient to encompass defendants' own negligence”].)

 

As to the open and obvious argument that is contained in Defendant’s moving papers, it is not clear that a reasonable person could be expected to observe the dangerous condition in this situation. (See Jacobs v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (2017) 14 Cal,App.5th 438, 447.) First, the surveillance video shows (1) yellow placards next to the door, identified in the video by a mat, and (2) two stanchions supporting a yellow chain barrier. There is no warning placard that indicates that the wet floor would extend from the doorway to the cashier nor is there a placard by the stanchions to indicate that they were placed due to the wet floor. In all likelihood, some reasonable people could be expected to observe the stanchions with the yellow chain barrier and conclude that it has been placed there as a divider rope for a queue or other purpose. Defendant has conceded that the floors were mopped prior to Plaintiff’s fall. (See Decl. Diaz ¶ 4). Whether a verbal warning was given is immaterial to open and obvious argument brought by Defendant. Therefore, there are issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff was actually aware of the wet condition prior to the incident, which Defendant argues was open and obvious, and whether Defendant sufficiently warned Plaintiff of the dangerous condition.

 

Accordingly, Defendant does not meet the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. Rather, Defendant’s arguments and presentations raise more questions that are unaddressed by its moving papers.

 

The Court need not address the Opposition as Defendant has not met its initial burden.

 

For the same reason, the Court need not address the Reply. The Court need not rule on the objections provided with the Reply as they are not material to the disposition of the motion. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(q)[“In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court need rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion. Objections to evidence that are not ruled on for purposes of the motion shall be preserved for appellate review.”].)

 

For the knowledge of the parties, the Court notes that it has reviewed both the Opposition and Reply in their entirety.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Judgment on the Pleadings – A plaintiff may move for judgment on the pleadings only when the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 438(c)(1)(A).)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings challenges the legal sufficiency of a pleading’s allegations, not their veracity. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) Thus, a motion for judgment on the pleadings is the functional equivalent of a general demurrer. (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198; see also Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999 [“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.”].)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

The standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer, that is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-322 [citing Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216].)¿Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice. (Id. at 321-22.) The parties’ ability to prove their respective claims is of no concern. (Cloud, supra, 67 Cal.App.4th 995 at 999.) ¿¿

 

A motion by a plaintiff for judgment on the pleadings is in the nature of a general demurrer to the answer, and the motion must be denied if the answer raises a material issue or sets up affirmative matter constituting a defense. Such a motion does not operate as a special demurrer. Uncertainty and ambiguities must be specifically raised by proper procedure. (MacIsaac v. Pozzo (1945) 26 Cal.2d 809, 812-813; Fabbro v. Dardi & Co. (1949) 93 Cal.App.2d 247, 252; Bergerow v. Parker (1906) 4 Cal.App. 169, 172.) Where the answer, fairly construed, suggests that the defendant may have a good defense, a motion for judgment on the pleadings should not be granted. (Patterson v. Pacific Indem. Co. (1931) 119 Cal.App. 203, 206-207.) The moving party admits the untruth of his own allegations insofar as they have been controverted, and all such averments must be disregarded whether there is a direct and specific denial or an indirect denial by virtue of affirmative allegations of a contrary state of facts. (MacIsaac, supra, 26 Cal.2d 809, 813; Osborne v. Abels (1939) 30 Cal.App.2d 729, 730.) Every allegation affirmatively pleaded in the answer must be deemed true. (Cuneo v. Lawson (1924) 203 Cal. 190, 193.)

Leave to Amend

 

Plaintiff argues that leave to amend should not be granted as there is no reasonable probability that Bacelis, Jr. can amend his Answer due to the admissions that have been deemed admitted.

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿¿ 

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.300(b) allows a court to permit the withdrawal or amendment of admissions only if the court “determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.” Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437(b) allows a court to, “upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”

 

However, Bacelis, Jr. has not requested any form of relief.

 

With that in mind, the Court believes that, at this time and based on the facts in this case, there is no reasonable possibility of a successful amendment.

 

Accordingly, the Court declines to allow leave to amend.

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) Negligence, (2) IIED, (3) Fraudulent Concealment, (4) Public Nuisance, and (5) Breach of Contract.

.

On September 11, 2023, Defendants filed their Demurrer to the SAC, subsequently overruled in part as to the Third Cause of Action (Fraudulent Concealment) and sustained with leave to amend in part as to the Second Cause of Action (IIED), Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance), and Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract).

 

On November 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleging five causes of action for: (1) Negligence, (2) IIED, (3) Fraudulent Concealment, (4) Public Nuisance, and (5) Breach of Contract.

 

On December 26, 2023, Defendants filed their Demurrer to the TAC.

 

No Opposition has been filed. “All papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days. . .before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Ibid.) The hearing on this matter is set for February 06, 2024. Accordingly, an Opposition was due by January 24, 2024.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (CCP § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Id.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿Stated otherwise, a general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified (Dec. 23, 2003).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

-----

 

Meet and Confer Requirement – Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.)  The Court notes that counsel for Defendants, Tiffanie Q. Spivey (“Spivey”) has declared that she attempted to meet and confer via email, but received no response. (See Decl. Spivey ¶ 3.) “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.31(a)(4).) The Court will address the Demurrer on its merits.

Discussion

 

a.      Demurrer

 

Defendants demur to the Second Cause of Action (IIED), Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance), and Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract).

 

 i.  Second Cause of Action (IIED)

 

As an initial matter, the difference between this cause of action in the SAC and the TAC is:

 

The above extreme and outrageous conduct by Defendants’, and DOES 2 through 20, was done with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, Plaintiffs’ severe emotional distress.

 

Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, had knowledge and were on notice of the existing bedbug infestation at the Subject Hotel as evidenced by the extensive online reviews by other prior guests which indicates presence of said infestation and conditions present at the Subject Hotel. Given this existing knowledge of the unsanitary conditions and infestation, Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, intentionally failed to inform Plaintiffs of said conditions existing in the room to which they were assigned. Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, intentionally did not make any representations to the Plaintiffs regarding this infestation present at the Subject Hotel. In fact, Plaintiffs were unaware of the said conditions until after being after being assigned to the room at the Subject Hotel.

 

(TAC ¶¶ 68-69.)

 

Defendants present that in order for conduct to be outrageous, there must be a specific intent to injure, or a reckless disregard of the substantial certainty of a severe emotional injury under Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868. Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s allegations do not reflect that Defendants had a substantial certainty of a severe harm or severe emotional reaction, that each individual has a different reaction to bed bug bites, and the alleged wrongful conduct does not rise to conduct so extreme and outrageous so as to exceed the bounds of decency and is not regarded as atrocious and utterly intolerable in a civilized community. Defendants emphasize that the Complaint alleges only that Defendants had notice of a bed bug infestation based on some unknown time in the past and are conclusory as to when and if Defendants knew of a current infestation. Defendants also highlight that Plaintiff concedes in the TAC that she received a refund, thus showing that Defendants attempted to remedy the situation and showing that the behavior was not extreme, outrageous, went beyond all possible bounds of decency, was atrocious, was utterly intolerable in a civilized community, and/or would arouse the resentment of an average member of the community against the Defendant, and lead to an exclamation of "Outrageous!”

 

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035,1050-1051.)

 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)

 

Taken alone, the statement, “[t]he above extreme and outrageous conduct by Defendants’, and DOES 2 through 20, was done with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, Plaintiffs’ severe emotional distress[,]” would be conclusory; however, Plaintiff has included the following statement – “Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, had knowledge and were on notice of the existing bedbug infestation at the Subject Hotel as evidenced by the extensive online reviews by other prior guests which indicates presence of said infestation and conditions present at the Subject Hotel. Given this existing knowledge of the unsanitary conditions and infestation, Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, intentionally failed to inform Plaintiffs of said conditions existing in the room to which they were assigned. Defendants, and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive, intentionally did not make any representations to the Plaintiffs regarding this infestation present at the Subject Hotel. In fact, Plaintiffs were unaware of the said conditions until after being after being assigned to the room at the Subject Hotel.” While Defendants are correct that no time is specified for when the reviews are posted, “[w]hen considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.”¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)¿The Court believes that a liberal reading of the TAC at this stage of the pleadings would allow for a reasonable inference that the online reviews put Defendants on notice of the bed bug infestation. Thus, the first element, extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress is satisfied.

 

Plaintiff satisfied the second element by alleging damages as to emotional injuries which include, but are not limited to, severe embarrassment, annoyance, discomfort, pain, apprehension, tension, anxiety, and emotional distress. (See TAC ¶ 45.) The Court notes that Plaintiff alleges physical damages in the IIED cause of action as well; however, the physical damages are not an element of IIED.

 

As to the third element, Plaintiff has alleged that such action was legal and proximate cause of her injuries and a liberal reading can also support such an inference. (See Complaint ¶¶ 77-78.)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action (IIED) is OVERRULED.

 

 

ii.                  Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance)

 

There has been no change between this cause of action from the FAC to the SAC or from the SAC to the TAC.

 

Defendants present the same argument as in the two previously filed demurrers – Plaintiff does not plead any instances where the alleged bedbug issue affected a considerable amount of people.

 

The Court incorporates its previous analysis from the SAC as applied to this cause of action in the TAC:

 

"A public nuisance is one which affects at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons, although the extent of the annoyance or damage inflicted upon individuals may be unequal." (Civ. Code, § 3480.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff does not plead any instances where the alleged bedbug issue affected a considerable amount of people, thus, affecting the community at large. Therefore, as alleged, Defendants believe that the FAC fails to state a cause of action for Public Nuisance. (Demurrer, Page 10, lines 23-25.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that the unsanitary and uninhabitable conditions of bed bugs infestation caused injuries both to her health and safety, and interfered substantially with Plaintiff’s comfortable enjoyment of the room she rented. (FAC ¶¶101-102.) Plaintiff states that bed bug infestation can spread from the Hotel room to the public and into their residence which can affect the community at large (FAC ¶ 103). However, the FAC does not allege that anyone other than Plaintiff was injured, and so fails to meet the statutory requirements (Civ. Code, § 3480) for a public nuisance claim.

 

However, Plaintiff’s FAC does not prove how the bed bugs affected the community at large since it does not state any facts on how the bed bugs affected other individuals besides Plaintiff.

 

(10/25/2023 Statement of Decision at pp. 8-9.)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance) is SUSTAINED.

 

iii.                Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract)

 

The Fifth Cause of Action has not been changed from the SAC.

 

Defendants’ argument remains the same as in the previous demurrer – Plaintiff has neither attached a copy of the contract, incorporated by reference, or recite it verbatim in the Complaint and that Plaintiff has failed to allege facts demonstrating that she was damaged from any breach of the contract alleged because she received a refund for her night’s stay.

 

The Court incorporates its previous analysis from the SAC as applied to this cause of action in the TAC:

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)

 

The addition in the SAC appears to be an attempt to plead facts demonstrating that Plaintiff was damaged from a breach of contract. However, the deficiency with regards to the allegations related to the contract remains. That is, (a) “[t]o prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff[]” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186); and (b) Plaintiff did not attach the contract or recite the terms of the contract and “[o]rdinarily a written contract is sufficiently pleaded if it is set out in full or its terms alleged according to their legal effect . . . . But if the instrument is ambiguous, the pleader must allege the meaning he ascribes to it[]” (Beck v. American Health Group Internat., Inc. (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1555, 1561; see also Otworth v. S. Oac. Transp. Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 459 [“[I]f the action is based on an alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written instrument must be attached and incorporated by reference.”]). Here, no contract is attached nor are terms provided. Plaintiff alleges only that a written contract was entered into “for the rent of Plaintiff’s room in compliance with California Health & Safety Code.” (SAC ¶ 112.) Plaintiff has not sufficiently pled each element of a breach of contract claim.

 

(10/25/2023 Statement of Decision at p. 9.)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract) is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿ 

 

Defendants emphasize that this is their fourth demurrer to the same causes of action and it is patent that Plaintiff cannot cure her defects.

 

The defects in the Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance) and Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract) have been present since Plaintiff’s original Complaint. The Court has opined in all its Statement of Decisions that (1) Plaintiff has failed to present that the bed bug infestation affected the community at large (03/28/2023 Statement of Decision at pp. 9-10, 07/11/2023 Statement of Decision at p. 6, 10/25/2023 Statement of Decision at pp. 8-9), and (2) Plaintiff has not properly pled the alleged contract at issue (03/28/2023 Statement of Decision at pp. 10-11, 07/11/2023 Statement of Decision at pp. 6-7, 10/25/2023 Statement of Decision at p. 9). Further, Plaintiff has made no changes to the Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance) since the FAC and, regarding the Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract), Plaintiff has (1) added only one paragraph from the FAC to the SAC, (2) made no changes from the SAC to the TAC, and (3) has never attached the contract or recited the terms of the alleged contract. Finally, Plaintiff has not opposed a filed demurrer since the Demurrer to the Complaint. Thus, it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.

 

The Court declines to allow leave to amend.

Conclusion

 

Defendants Best Western International, Inc. and Bains Hospitality, LLC (erroneously sued and served as Best Western Plus Desert Poppy Inn) Demurrer is OVERRULED in part as to the Second Cause of Action (IIED) and SUSTAINED in part as to the Fourth Cause of Action (Public Nuisance) and Fifth Cause of Action (Breach of Contract) without leave to amend.

Background

 

This is a mortgage foreclosure action. Plaintiff Javier Ortiz (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he acquired real property legally known as Lot 39, of Tract No. 46757, in the City of Palmdale, state of California, recorded in Book 1301, pages 64-73, in the official records of the County Recorder, County of Los Angeles, State of California, APN 3024-036-039 (the “Subject Property”) pursuant to a Deed of Trust in favor of Defendants Qualityloan Service Corporation (“Qualityloan”); Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (“Select Portfolio”); Wilmington Savings Fund Society, FSB, as owner trustee of CMSC 2021-RPL11 (“Wilmington Savings”); and all persons claiming legal, equitable, lien, and estate in the property  located at 38140 Archwood Way, Palmdale, California 93552, APN 3024-036-039. Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants failed to comply with provisions of federal and California law as they have refused and continue to consider Plaintiff for a loan modification or his request for approval of a short sale as they state he does not qualify. Plaintiff presents that Defendants recorded a Notice of Default and election to sell under Deed of Trust with a recorded sale date of October 24, 2023 at 10:00 am. Plaintiff disputes the validity of Defendants’ declaration and any claims of exemption from the time frame for giving notice of a sale under Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.52.

 

On October 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Complaint alleging three causes of action for: (1) Injunction Enjoining Defendants from Foreclosing on Plaintiff’s Property on October 24, 2023 at 10:00 am, Pending Exhaustion of Alternative Means of Retention Including Loan Modification or a Short Sale; (2) Unfair Business Practice; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

 

On October 26, 2023, Defendants Select Portfolio and Wilmington Savings filed a Demurrer to the Complaint, subsequently sustained on January 02, 2024. Leave to amend was given as to the Second Cause of Action (Unfair Business Practice).

 

On December 28, 2023, Select Portfolio filed this Motion Deem the Truth of Matters and Genuineness of Documents Admitted and Sanctions in the Amount of $1,587.00 (“Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted).

 

No Opposition has been filed. “All papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days. . .before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Ibid.) The hearing on this matter is set for February 06, 2024. Accordingly, an Opposition was due by January 24, 2024.

 

Should an Opposition be filed, it is now untimely.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Deeming Admissions Admitted – Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c) states, in relevant part: “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)¿

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – Select Portfolio moves to deem Requests for Admission (“RFAs”) admitted against Plaintiff.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250 provides: 

 

“Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared, unless on motion of the requesting party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.” 

 

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).) 

 

Select Portfolio presents that they served the Requests for Admission (“RFAs”) to Plaintiff on November 15, 2023. (Decl. James J. Ramos ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) Thus, responses were due December 15, 2023. Counsel for Select Portfolio, James J. Ramos (“Ramos”), declares that Plaintiff neither served responses nor reached out to request an extension. (Decl. James J. Ramos ¶¶ 5-6.) To date, Plaintiff has failed to serve responses.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted is GRANTED.

 

-----

 

Sanctions

 

Select Portfolio requests sanctions in the amount of $1,587.00 (1.6 hrs @ $345.00/hr) in connection with this motion.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c) provides, in relevant part:

 

It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.

 

Accordingly, the Court imposes a sanction of $500.00 on Plaintiff.

 

-----

 

 

Conclusion

 

Defendant Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc.’s Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters and Genuineness of Documents Admitted and Sanctions in the Amount of $1,587.00 is GRANTED.

 

A sanction of $500.00 is imposed on Plaintiff Javier Ortiz.
