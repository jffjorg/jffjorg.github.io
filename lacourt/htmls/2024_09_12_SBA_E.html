DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02815    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

JULIA HEREDIA,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV02815

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

BODEGA LATINA CORPORATION, et al.,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             September 12, 2024   

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff, Julia Heredia

Responding Party:      Defendant, Chedraui USA, Inc., erroneously named as Bodega Latina Corporation

HEARING: MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES (x4)

            The Court considered the moving papers.

 

RULING

Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Further Responses to SPROGS, FROGS, RPDs, and RFAs are GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs are awarded sanctions in the amount of $2046.60.

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 28, 2023, Plaintiff Julia Heredia filed a complaint against Defendants Bodega Latina Corporation, Chedraui USA, Inc., and Does 1 through 100, alleging (1) premises liability; and (2) general negligence. On June 29, 2022, Plaintiff alleges that she was shopping at the property 15202 Hawthorne Blvd, Lawndale, CA 90260 (“Premises”), owned and managed by Defendants, where Plaintiff slipped and fell due to a liquid substance on the floor. (Compl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff alleges that the defendants negligently failed to maintain and inspect the premises, causing her injuries, pain, suffering, and loss of income. (Compl. ¶¶ 10-14.)

 

            On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, set for hearing on September 12, 2024.

 

            On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, set for hearing on September 19, 2024. Advanced to September 12, 2024, by the Court.

 

On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, set for hearing on September 12, 2024.

 

            On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, set for hearing on September 12, 2024.

 

            On July 30, 2024, an IDC was held. The Court noted that the motions to compel further discovery scheduled for September 12, 2024 and September 19, 2024 would remain on calendar.

 

            On September 6, 2024, Defendant untimely filed oppositions to Plaintiff’s discovery motions. Given the untimely filing and prejudice to Plaintiff, the oppositions are not considered.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to interrogatories or requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

If a party fails to serve timely responses to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and seek a monetary sanction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subds. (b), (c).) Failure to respond timely waives all objections, including those based on privilege and work product, unless the party has (1) subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance and (2) the failure to timely respond was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff served discovery requests, including Special Interrogatories, Set One; Form Interrogatories, Set One; and Request for Production, Set One on Defendants. (Graves Decl. ¶ 3, Exhib. A.) Defendants did not respond to these requests. (Id. ¶ 4.) On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel reached out to the Defense, who claimed not to have received the discovery requests. (Id.) Plaintiff granted Defendants’ request for extensions. (Id. ¶ 5.) Defendants served responses on February 28, 2024. (Id. ¶ 6, Exh. E.) On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email stating that objections were already waived due to missing the original deadline. (Id. ¶ 7, Exhib. G.) Plaintiff stated that email served as her attempt to meet and confer. (Id.)

 

SPROGS

 

Special Interrogatories No. 1-92 consist of requests regarding the circumstances surrounding the slip and fall incident involving Plaintiff on June 29, 2022. These requests seek to uncover specific information about various aspects of the incident and Defendant Bodega Latina Corporation’s knowledge, actions, and procedures. The requests seek information regarding presence of any safety measures, such as warning signs, cones, or notices in the area where the incident occurred (SPROG No. 1), and whether Defendant was aware of the hazardous condition, such as a liquid substance on the floor, prior to the incident (SPROGS Nos. 2-4). Plaintiff also asks about Defendant’s position on the reasonableness and necessity of the medical treatments Plaintiff received following the incident. (SPROGS Nos. 5-6.) Other requests focus on the details of the incident itself, including how it occurred (SPROG No. 7), who investigated it (SPROGS Nos. 9-10), and the identification of all persons with knowledge of the incident. (SPROG No. 11.) Plaintiff requests identification of any documents or evidence that Defendant intends to introduce at trial. (SPROG No. 8.) Additionally, the interrogatories explore Defendant’s contentions regarding the incident, including any claims that it did not occur (SPROGS Nos. 20-23), that Plaintiff was comparatively negligent (SPROGS Nos. 24-27), or that Defendant did not create or allow a dangerous condition to exist on the premises. (SPROGS Nos. 28-35.) Plaintiff also seeks detailed information about Defendant’s maintenance, repair, and inspection practices for the premises over the past ten years and specifically on the day of the incident (SPROGS Nos. 36-64), as well as identification of any insurance policies that might provide coverage for the claims arising from the incident. (SPROGS Nos. 65-68.) The interrogatories include questions about the flooring where the incident occurred, covering topics such as installation (SPROGS Nos. 69-72), maintenance (SPROGS Nos. 73-78), cleaning practices (SPROGS Nos. 79-88), and the specific products used (SPROGS Nos. 83-90). Furthermore, Plaintiff asks for details regarding the opening date of the premises. (SPROG No. 92.)

 

In response, Defendant objects to all of these interrogatories on various grounds, including claims that they are vague, ambiguous, overbroad, irrelevant, and burdensome.

 

The Court finds the requests to be relevant to the issues in this case as they seek to uncover the circumstances surrounding Plaintiff’s fall. Defendant’s response in the form of one objection is improper. Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.210(c) requires each interrogatory to be responded to separately — which Defendant failed to do. Defendant has failed to provide any arguments in opposition. The Court finds that further responses are warranted.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to SPROGS set one.

 

FROGS

 

The FROGS seek information regarding various aspects of the slip and fall incident involving Plaintiff such as the identities of individuals who prepared Defendant’s responses (FROG No. 1.1), the corporate structure of Defendant (FROG No. 3.1), and any insurance policies that might provide coverage for damages related to the incident (FROG No. 4.1). The requests also seek information about potential witnesses, statements made at the scene, and any individuals with knowledge of the incident. (FROG No. 12.1.). Further interrogatories seek details about Defendant’s business structure, insurance coverage, and contentions regarding liability, damages, and other defenses, including whether Defendant operated as a partnership or joint venture, the existence of any insurance policies, and whether Defendant obtained any statements or conducted interviews related to the incident. (FROGS Nos. 3.2-3.7, 4.2, 12.2-12.7, 13.1-14.2, 15.1, 16.1-16.10, 17.1.)

 

In response, Defendant objected to all these interrogatories on numerous grounds, arguing that the requests were vague, ambiguous, overbroad, irrelevant, unduly burdensome, and intended to harass. Defendant also claimed that certain requests improperly sought confidential or privileged information, required expert opinions or legal conclusions, and invaded the attorney-client privilege or attorney work product doctrines. Defendant did not provide substantive responses, instead repeating identical objections to each interrogatory.

 

Plaintiff contends that these objections are without merit and have been waived due to Defendant’s failure to provide timely responses as required under California law. Consequently, Plaintiff has filed a motion to compel further responses, arguing that Defendant’s objections are invalid and that complete answers are essential for properly evaluating and preparing the case.

 

            The Court finds that the requests appear to be relevant. Defendant’s responses are deficient as they fail to provide substantive answers to the other interrogatories. The objections raised by Defendant—claiming the requests were vague, overbroad, irrelevant, or unduly burdensome—are not adequately justified, and the failure to respond timely under California law results in a waiver of these objections.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to FROGS set one.

 

RPDs

 

The Requests for Production seek various documents such as insurance policies that may cover damages related to the incident, as well as any incident or investigation reports, including statements from any witnesses and the Plaintiff. (RPDs Nos. 1-4.) Plaintiff also requests visual and surveillance evidence, such as photographs, diagrams, and video footage from the premises on the day of the incident. (RPDs Nos. 5-6.) Additionally, Plaintiff seeks documents related to Defendant’s investigation of the incident, any admissions, and any documents supporting Defendant’s claim that Plaintiff was comparatively negligent. (RPDs Nos. 7-10.) Further, the requests include records concerning the maintenance, repair, and inspection of the floors on the premises over the past 10 years, and any safety procedures or policies regarding slip and fall or personal injury accidents that were in effect at the time of the incident. (RPDs Nos. 11-18, 24-29, 42-44.) Plaintiff also seeks documents related to any prior slip and fall incidents at the premises within the past 10 years and any citations by government agencies concerning conditions at the premises over the last 15 years. (RPDs Nos. 19-21.) In addition, the requests cover management agreements for the premises for the last 10 years and the current deed for the ownership of the premises. (RPDs Nos. 22-23.) Plaintiff further requests documents regarding the installation, purchase, maintenance, and cleaning of the flooring where the incident occurred, including records of glossing, waxing, buffing, polishing, and the brands of cleaning solutions used before and at the time of the incident. (RPDs Nos. 30-41.) Finally, Plaintiff seeks documents detailing procedures and policies for inspecting the floors in effect at the time of the incident and any supporting documents referenced in Defendant’s responses to the Form and Special Interrogatories. (RPDs Nos. 43-46.) Plaintiff states that these requests are aimed at establishing the circumstances of the incident, identifying potential negligence, and determining any contributory factors related to Defendant’s liability.

 

Defendant provides one response to all of Plaintiff’s RPDs Nos. 1-46. Defendant states that the requests are speculative, compound, vague, ambiguous, uncertain, confusing, unintelligible, and overbroad. It further contends that the requests are overly broad in scope and time, irrelevant to the subject matter, and not likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Defendant also claims that complying with the requests would be unduly burdensome, cause unwarranted annoyance or embarrassment, and be oppressive. Additionally, Defendant objects to the requests as being argumentative, assuming facts that may not be true, and improperly requiring the adoption of those assumptions. It argues that the requests seek confidential, proprietary information that is irrelevant and invades privacy rights, including those of third parties. Defendant also contends that the requests violate attorney-client privilege and the attorney work product doctrine, call for expert opinions and legal conclusions, and seek premature disclosure of expert witness information. It further states that the information sought is equally or more readily available to Plaintiff. Defendant reserves the right to supplement and amend its responses as discovery is ongoing.

 

The Court finds the requests to be relevant to the issues in this case as they seek to uncover the circumstances surrounding Plaintiff’s fall. Defendant’s response in the form of one objection is improper. Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.210(c) to be responded to separately — which Defendant failed to do. Defendant has failed to provide any arguments in opposition. The Court finds that further responses are warranted.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RPDs set one.

 

RFAs

 

            In the Requests for Admissions, Plaintiff requests that Defendant admit several key points related to the slip and fall incident that occurred on June 29, 2022. These include the ownership and management of the premises by Bodega Latina Corporation, the occurrence of the incident, and the resulting injuries and medical expenses sustained by Plaintiff. Plaintiff also seeks admissions that Bodega Latina Corporation’s negligence caused or contributed to the incident and that the company had a duty to maintain, inspect, and repair the premises to ensure safety. Furthermore, Plaintiff requests Defendant to admit that a liquid substance was present on the floor at the time of the incident, that Defendant knew of this condition but failed to respond appropriately, and that no proper procedures or policies for inspection, maintenance, or handling of slip and fall accidents were in place or followed on the day of the incident. Additionally, Plaintiff requests that Defendant admit that it did not provide any warning signs or other warnings for slip and fall hazards and failed to call emergency personnel after the incident. Finally, Plaintiff seeks admissions regarding the existence of video surveillance footage that captured the incident, which Defendant allegedly destroyed or allowed to be erased.

 

In response, Defendant objects to requests for admission numbers 1-40 in one response. It asserts that the requests are speculative, vague, ambiguous, uncertain, confusing, unintelligible, overbroad, and compound. It further contends that the requests are overly broad and irrelevant to the subject matter of the lawsuit, not likely to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, and unduly burdensome and oppressive, imposing an undue burden and expense. It also asserts that the requests appear to be intended to annoy and harass them and rely on assumptions that may not be true, thereby improperly requiring the adoption of these assumptions. Additionally, it argues that the requests seek confidential and proprietary information that is irrelevant and invade their right to privacy as well as the privacy rights of third parties. It further objects on the grounds that the requests violate attorney-client privilege and the attorney work product doctrine and seek expert opinions, legal conclusions, and the legal reasoning behind their case strategy. It maintains that the information sought is equally or more readily available to the Propounding Party and that the requests demand premature disclosure of expert witness information and opinions. Finally, it notes that discovery is ongoing and reserves the right to amend or supplement their response in the future.

 

The Court finds these requests to be relevant and aimed at establishing Defendant’s liability for Plaintiff’s injuries by confirming Defendant’s ownership and management responsibilities, failure to maintain a safe environment, and inadequate response to the incident. The Court also finds Defendant’s response in the form of one objection to be improper under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.210(a), which states “each response shall answer the substance of the requested admission, or set forth an objection to the particular request.” (emphasis added.) Thus, further responses are warranted.

 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RFAs, set one.

 

Sanctions

 

            As the prevailing party, Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions. For each of the SPROGS, FROGS, RPD, and RFA motions, Plaintiff seeks sanctions in the amount of $811.65, consisting of $300 hourly fee at 1 hour for the motion, 1 hour for reply, and 0.5 hours to attend the hearing, along with the $61.65 filing fee. The Court finds the $300 hourly fee to be reasonable. However, given the lack of opposition, the Court reduces the hours to 1.5 for each motion, at $511.65 for each motion.

 

            Thus, the Court awards sanctions to Plaintiff in the total amount of $2,046.60.
