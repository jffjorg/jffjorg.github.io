DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 23LBCP00476    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On December 26, 2023, Petitioners Long Beach Reform Coalition and Corliss Lee (collectively, “Petitioners”) filed a verified petition for peremptory writ of mandate (the “Petition”) against Respondents Dean C. Logan, Registrar-Recorder/City Clerk for Los Angeles County; Monique De La Garza, City Clerk of Long Beach (“City Clerk”); and Dawn A. McIntosh, as City Attorney of Long Beach, Real Party in Interest (“City Attorney”) alleging causes of action for: (1) peremptory writ of mandate to amend title and summary of Measure RW pursuant to Cal. Election Code sections 3204 and 9295; and (2) peremptory writ of mandate to amend title and summary of Measure RW pursuant to Cal. Election Code section 13314. Petitioners sought amendment of the Title & Summary of Measure RW, which is a minimum wage related ordinance for hotel workers in the City of Long Beach.

On December 28, 2023, Petitioners filed an ex parte application to shorten time for the hearing on the Petition, which was opposed by Dean C. Logan in his official capacity as the County of Los Angeles Registrar-Record/County Clerk.

 

On January 3, 2024, the Court granted the ex parte application filed by Petitioners. (01/03/24 Minute Order.) The Court’s order provides that “[p]ursuant to Government Code sections 68086, 70044, California Rules of Court, rule 2.956, and the stipulation of the appearing parties,” a certified shorthand reporter was “appointed as an official Court reporter pro tempore” in the proceedings. (01/03/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court held a hearing on the Petition and heard testimony. (01/03/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court denied the Petition. (01/03/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court ordered Respondents to submit a proposed judgment for the Court’s signature. (01/03/24 Minute Order at p. 2.)

 

On January 10, 2024, an order was entered denying the Petition and entering judgment in favor of Respondents.   

 

On January 23, 2024, Respondents City Attorney and City Clerk (collectively, “Respondents”) filed and served a notice of entry of judgement.

 

Also, on January 23, 2024, Respondents filed and served a memorandum of costs in which Respondents request $3,276.15 in costs for Court-ordered transcripts. The memorandum of costs was served on Petitioners by mail.

           

            On February 13, 2024, the Court issued an order awarding costs to Respondents and awarded Respondents costs in the amount of $3,276.15.[1] (02/13/24 Order at p. 2:10-13.) The Court’s order noted that Petitioners did not file any objection to Respondents’ memorandum of costs within 15 days pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1), or otherwise file any objection. (02/13/24 Order at p. 2:8-9.)

 

On February 13, 2024, at 8:05 PM, which was after entry of the Court’s order concerning costs, Petitioners filed an objection to the memorandum of costs. Such objection, however, does not set forth a hearing date or time for a hearing. The objection provides that “Petitioners . . . hereby oppose and move to strike the Memorandum of Costs filed by Respondents.” (Objection at p. 1: 19-20.)

 

On May 23, 2024, Petitioners filed and served the instant motion for hearing on objection to memorandum of costs, which sets forth a hearing date and hearing time. (See Not. of Mot. at p. 1:13-14.) Pursuant to the motion, Petitioners “move . . . for an order setting a hearing date for consideration of Petitioners’ Objection to Memorandum of Costs filed on April 13, 2024 (sic).” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:19-21.)

 

Also, on May 23, 2024, Petitioners filed a notice of lack of ruling on objection pursuant to CCP § 472(d), which indicated that the objection had not been ruled upon by the Court as of such date.

 

            On October 29, 2024, Respondents filed an opposition to the motion.

 

            As of November 5, 2024, no reply brief has been filed. Any reply brief was required to have been filed and served at least five court days prior to hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)    

 

2.     Motion to Tax Costs

a.         Parties’ Positions

Petitioners assert that the charges for court-ordered transcripts set forth in the memorandum of costs are improper and are baseless.

 

Respondents contend that: (1) the costs incurred are recoverable pursuant to CCP § 1033.5(a)(9); (2) Petitioners’ motion is without legal basis and is untimely; (3) a motion for reconsideration should be denied as untimely; and (4) a motion for reconsideration should be denied for lack of justiciable basis.

b.         Legal Standard

“Except as otherwise provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) “The right to recover . . . costs is determined entirely by statute.” (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 71.) “In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court’s first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face.” (Ibid.) “If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show the costs to be unnecessary or unreasonable.” (Ibid.) “Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary.”  (Ibid.) “Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.) California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 1033.5(c)(4) gives a court discretion to allow or deny a claimed cost where it is not explicitly allowed or prohibited by Section 1033.5.

 

“[T]he items on a verified cost bill are prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred.” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum.” (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1267.) “Only if the costs have been put at issue must supporting documentation be submitted.” (Ibid.) “If the items appearing on a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.) “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ibid.) “[T]he mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a proper objection to an item, the necessity of which appears doubtful, or which does not appear to be proper on its face.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131, internal quotations omitted.)

 

c.         Timeliness  

“Any notice of motion to strike or tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1).)  Where service is effectuated by mail “any period of notice and any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended five calendar days . . . if the place or address and the place of mailing is within the State of California . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013, subd. (a).) “If the last day for the performance of an act provided or required by law to be performed within a specified period of time is a holiday, then that period is hereby extended to and including the next day that is not a holiday.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 12a, subd. (a).)

 

The Court finds that the objection filed by Petitioners on February 13, 2024 is timely under Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(1). The memorandum of costs was served on Petitioners by mail on January 23, 2024. Thus, any objection or motion to tax costs was required to have been filed and served by February 12, 2024. The Court, however, takes judicial notice on its own motion that Monday, February 12, 2024 was a court holiday.[2] Thus, the objection to the memorandum of costs was timely filed.

 

Although the objection was timely filed, the Court opines as to why Petitioners did not provide a noticed hearing date and time on such objection. “Notices must be in writing, and the notice of motion . . . must state when, and the grounds upon which it will be made, and the papers, if any, upon which it is to be based.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.) A notice of motion must, on the first page, set forth “[t]he date, time, and location, if ascertainable, of any scheduled hearing and the name of the hearing judge if ascertainable.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(b)(1).)

 

Here, the Court finds that the objection to the memorandum of costs cannot be construed as a motion because it did not articulate a hearing date or hearing time. Thus, the Court finds that the purported objection is too procedurally deficient to function as a noticed motion. It follows that the noticed motion for hearing on objection to memorandum of costs—which was filed on May 23, 2024—is untimely under Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1). Petitioners should have filed and served a noticed motion to tax costs no later than February 13, 2024. Here, Petitioners failed to file such motion within the timeline articulated in Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1).

 

Thus, the Court denies Petitioners’ request to strike and/or tax Respondents’ costs.

 

d.        Respondents’ Costs are Proper   

Even if Petitioners’ objection or the instant motion had been timely filed, the Court would have found that Respondents’ claimed costs are proper. Transcripts of court proceedings ordered by the court are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(9).) The Court appointed the court reporter at the hearing on the Petition. (01/03/24 Minute Order.) Further, a memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence that the costs were reasonably and necessarily incurred. Petitioners present no declarations or evidence to support their contention that the costs for court-ordered transcripts were unnecessary or unreasonable. Petitioners have presented only mere argument to support their contention that the costs should be stricken.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Respondents’ costs are proper, and Petitioners have not met their burden challenging such costs. The Court will not strike or tax Respondents’ memorandum of costs.

 

3.     Conclusion

The Court therefore DENIES the objection to the memorandum of costs. The Court also DENIES the instant motion to set a hearing on the objection to the memorandum of costs.

2.     Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

a.         Legal Standard

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.)  

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a)), MC-052 (Declaration) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(c)), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e)). The proposed order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

 

Further, the requisite forms must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).) The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).) A motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client.  (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

Counsel declares that it has been unable to confirm Client’s current address or locate a more current address for Client after: (1) mailing the motion papers to Client’s last known address, return receipt requested; (2) calling Client’s last known telephone number or numbers; and (3) conducting a skip trace report, which was unable to locate any other possible address. (Form MC-052 at Section 3b(2).) Counsel declares that it has been unable to personally serve any document at Client’s last known address. (Form MC-052 at Section 3b(2).) 

 

Counsel has not followed this Court’s instructions set forth in its September 5, 2024 order. (09/05/24 Minute Order.) None of the proofs of service, declarations of non-service, or unsigned return receipts attached to either the Form MC-051, Form MC-052, or Form MC-053 shows that service of the instant motion was attempted on Plaintiff Francine Detriech Pearce. Further, Counsel has not provided a copy of a signed return receipt with the signature of Plaintiff Francine Detriech Pearce.

 

The Court finds that the issue of service has not been remedied. Counsel should have undertook efforts to serve the instant motion on their client. The fact that Counsel attempted to serve Client with past motions to be relieved as counsel does not warrant the granting of the instant motion.

Case Number: 23LBCV01458    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On August 2, 2024, Sandra Luz Sarmiento (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Niko Llamas (Defendant) alleging two causes of action: (1) negligence and (2) premises liability. The Complaint stems from allegations that on September 9, 2021, Plaintiff sustained various injuries after “her foot broke against raised concrete at the walkway” of 540 West 22nd Street, San Pedro, California (hereinafter, the Premises). (Compl., ¶12.) The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition (the Motion). The Motion also requests sanctions in the amount of $5,125.00. Plaintiff opposes the Motion; no reply was filed.     

 

2.     Motion to Compel Deposition

a.     Legal Standard

A motion lies to compel deposition attendance and document production, after service of a deposition notice, where a deponent fails to appear at, or proceed with, a deposition, without having served a valid objection. (Code Civ. Proc. §2025.450(a).) No meet and confer is required to compel initial deposition attendance, but instead there must be a declaration showing that moving party inquired about the nonappearance. (Code Civ. Proc. §2025.450(b)(2).)  

“Implicit in the requirement that counsel contact the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance is a requirement that counsel listen to the reasons offered and make a good faith attempt to resolve the issue,” including by rescheduling. (Leko v. Cornerstone Bldg. Inspection Serv. (2001) 86 Cal. App. 4th 1109, 1124. See also L.A.S.C.L.R. 3.26, Appendix 3.A(e) (reasonable consideration should be given to accommodating schedules in setting depositions).)   

 

b.     Timeline

In their moving papers, Defendant contends Plaintiff failed to appear at three separate depositions. Prior to the first deposition, Defense counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel requesting dates of availability for Plaintiff’s deposition, however Defense counsel received no response. (Declaration of Kevin R. Jolly, hereinafter, Jolly Decl., ¶2.)

On February 16, 2024 Defendant served a Notice of Deposition set for April 11, 2024. The same Notice of Deposition was re-noticed after an error was found. It was re-served on March 28, 2024 for the same April 11, 2024 date. Plaintiff served no objections to either Notice of Deposition. (Id. at ¶3.) Plaintiff’s counsel requested a continuance on April 9, 2024, however the objection to the April 11 date was received after the deadline specified in Code Civ. Proc. §2025.410(b). (Id. at ¶4.) Plaintiff failed to appear at the April 11, 2024 deposition and a Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken. Additionally, although Plaintiff’s counsel twice promised to provide future deposition availability, no dates were ever provided. (Id. at ¶6.)

On June 7, 2024, a 2nd Amended Notice of Deposition was served for a June 28, 2024 deposition. Again, no objections were received, and another Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken. (Id. at ¶7.) Defense counsel sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel on June 28, 2024 suggesting future dates for a deposition, but no response was received. (Id. at ¶8.)

On June 28, 2024, a 3rd Amended Notice of Deposition of Plaintiff was served, and again no objections were received. The deposition was set for July 19, 2024. On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s Counsel contacted Defense counsel regarding the difficulties Plaintiff’s counsel was experiencing with contacting the Plaintiff. (Id. at ¶10.) On July 17, 2024, two days before the scheduled deposition, Plaintiff’s counsel claimed Plaintiff could not appear. (Id. at ¶11.) On July 19, 2024, Plaintiff failed to appear, and a third Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken. (Id. at ¶13.)                                    

c.     Analysis

Upon opposition, Plaintiff makes four separate contentions: (1) that Defendant failed to meet and confer appropriately and unilaterally noticed depositions, (2) Plaintiff’s counsel had a calendar conflict for the third deposition scheduled for July 19, 2024, (3) Plaintiff’s counsel encountered communication issues with their client and plans to file a motion to be relieved, and (4) the request for sanctions is unwarranted given the circumstances. The Court disagrees with all contentions, takes each in turn, and grants the Motion.

First, no meet and confer is required to compel initial deposition attendance, simply a declaration showing the moving party inquired about the nonappearance, pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. 2025.450. Defendant makes this showing. Defense counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel prior to serving the first Notice of Deposition. After the first Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed promising to provide future dates, Plaintiff’s counsel never provided such dates. After Plaintiff failed to appear at the second deposition date, Defense counsel again emailed Plaintiff’s counsel suggesting dates. No response was received. After the 3rd Amended Notice of Deposition was served, Plaintiff’s counsel waited until four days before the deposition to suggest a continuance, and waited until 10:45p.m. on July 17, two days before the deposition to state Plaintiff would not attend. The contention that Defense counsel failed to meet and confer and unilaterally noticed depositions has no merit.

Second, Plaintiff’s counsel’s contention of a scheduling conflict for the 3rd Amended Notice of Deposition scheduled for July 19, 2024 is unpersuasive. Plaintiff’s counsel received the 3rd Amended Notice of Deposition on June 28, 2024 and had more than sufficient time to communicate such a conflict and provide alternative dates. Plaintiff’s counsel failed to do so.

Third, the communications issues that Plaintiff’s counsel alleges arose, were only communicated four days prior to the third scheduled deposition. Plaintiff’s counsel provides no explanation for why this issue was not brought up earlier with Defense counsel.

Fourth and finally, Plaintiff’s counsel argues sanctions are unwarranted and cites to Code of Civ. Proc. §2025.450(g)(1) which provides that the court shall impose monetary sanctions unless the court finds that the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust. To summarize the above, the Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel acted without substantial justification and the circumstances here warrant the imposition of sanctions. Plaintiff’s counsel’s arguments to the contrary are not convincing.    

d.     Sanctions

Defense counsel provides the following calculations regarding costs expended in an effort to schedule Plaintiff’s deposition:

·         Counsel’s hourly rate is $450.00;

·         Counsel spent 6 hours on the instant matter including: reasonable attorney fees in preparation of the Motion, and meet and confer efforts;

·         Counsel anticipates spending 1 hour appearing for this Motion;

·         Counsel spent 1.5 hours in appearing for the depositions;

·         Counsel expended $1,200.00 in Certificate of Non-Appearance charges;

·         Counsel states the filing fee for the Motion was $100.00;

·         Counsel requests a total of $5,125.00 in monetary sanctions.

The Court notes that the current Statewide Civil Fee Schedule for discovery motions filed under the Discovery Act (Code Civ. Proc. §2016.010 et seq.) lists the fee for the instant Motion as $60.00, not $100.00. Moreover, the attached receipt for the instant Motion from the Court Portal shows that no fee was paid for the Motion. Therefore, the Court will subtract $100.00 from the total sanctions amount. Thus, the Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and awards them to Defendant in the amount of $5,025.00. 

 

3.     Conclusion

Accordingly, Defendant Niko Llamas’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff Sandra Sarmiento’s Deposition is GRANTED. Additionally, the sanctions request is GRANTED. The Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and awards them to Defendant in the amount of $5,025.00. Plaintiff is ordered to pay the sanctions to Defendant within 30 days of this order. Additionally, the parties are ordered to schedule the deposition of Plaintiff Sandra Sarmiento within 30 days of this order.

Case Number: 23LBCV02005    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

This action arises from an automobile accident that occurred on December 8, 2021, on the 91 Freeway in Long Beach, CA. On October 18, 2023, Plaintiff Rolando Diaz, Jr. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Omran M.H. Awad (“Awad”), 3 Brothers Trucking, Inc. (“3BT”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and DOES 1 to 20, alleging causes of action for: (1) negligence; and (2) motor vehicle.

 

On January 31, 2024, Defendant 3BT filed a notice of related action indicating that the instant action was related to The Kroger Company v. Omran Awad, et al., LASC Case No. 23STLC07801 (the “Kroger Action”). Also, on such date, Defendant 3BT filed its answer to the complaint.

 

On February 1, 2024, the Court issued an order finding that the instant action and the Kroger Action were not related within the meaning of Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). (02/01/24 Minute Order.)   

 

On February 2, 2024, Defendants filed a joint amended answer to the complaint. Also, on such date, Defendants filed an amended notice of related action.

 

On August 2, 2024, Defendants filed and served the instant motion to consolidate related actions. Defendants seek to consolidate the instant action with the Kroger Action.

 

            As of November 5, 2024, no opposition brief has been filed. Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)    

 

2.     Motion to Consolidate

a.         Legal Standard

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).) “Coordination of civil actions sharing a common question of fact or law is appropriate if one judge hearing all of the actions . . . will promote the ends of justice taking into account whether the common question of fact or law is predominating and significant to the litigation; the convenience of the parties, witnesses, and counsel; the relative development of the actions and the work product of counsel; the efficient utilization; the calendar of the courts; the disadvantages of duplicative and inconsistent rulings, orders, or judgments; and, the likelihood of settlement of the actions without further litigation should coordination be denied.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 404.1.)   

“The purpose of consolidation is merely to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.” (Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485 [citation omitted].) “A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.” (Ibid.) Consolidation does not require identical causes of action in each case, absolute identity of the parties, or identical allegations. (Ibid.) Where common issues are present in two cases then consolidation is appropriate. (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-79.)   

 

Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1) provides that “[c]ases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related in a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”  

 

b.         Judicial Notice

“The court may take judicial notice of its own file.” (Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.) “It is settled that a court may take judicial notice of the contents of its own records.” (Dwan v. Dixon (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 260, 265.) A court may take judicial notice on its own motion. (Estate of Sanchez (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 331, 338, fn. 9.)

 

On its own motion, the Court takes judicial notice of the file in the Kroger Action.

 

c.        The Cases Have Not Been Deemed Related  

In support of the motion, counsel for Defendants, Douglas M. DeGrave (“DeGrave”), provides a declaration. Mr. DeGrave states that the instant action is the lead action and was assigned to Department 27 and given LASC Case No. 23LBCV02005. (DeGrave Decl., ¶ 5.) The Kroger Action was filed on December 7, 2023, and was assigned to Department 25 and given LASC Case No. 23STLC07801. (DeGrave Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendants request consolidation of the instant action and the Kroger Action. (DeGrave Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

Initially, the Court notes that the instant action and the Kroger Action have not been deemed related.  Further, the instant action is pending in Department S27 while the Kroger Action is currently pending in Department 25 of the Spring Street Courthouse. The cases have not been related in a single department. Thus, consolidation is inappropriate under Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1).

 

Additionally, even if the cases had been related, the instant motion would fail. Defendants’ counsel has failed to articulate any of the factors to warrant consolidation in his declaration. The Court reminds Defendants that “[i]n law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

d.        The Instant Motion is Procedurally Deficient  

A notice of motion to consolidate must: “(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; (B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest numbered case shown first; and (C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.350(a)(1)(A)-(C).)

 

Here, the Court finds that Defendants have not complied with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(1)(A) and (C). Defendants did not name the respective attorney(s) of record for Plaintiff The Kroger Company in the notice of motion. Moreover, Defendants did not file the instant motion for consolidation in the Kroger Action. 

Moreover, the instant motion for consolidation is procedurally defective on the additional ground that Defendants did not serve the instant motion on Plaintiff The Kroger Company, whom is the sole plaintiff in the Kroger Action. (See Motion at p. 9.) The proof of service indicates that the motion was only served on Plaintiff Rolando Diaz Jr. Defendants therefore have failed to comply with Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(2)(B). (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.350(a)(2)(B) (a motion to consolidate “[m]ust be served on all attorneys of record and all nonrepresented parties in all of the cases sought to be consolidated.”).) Thus, Defendants’ contention that they complied with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.350 is inaccurate. (Motion at pp. 4-5.)

 

3.     Conclusion

The Motion to Consolidate Related Actions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

Case Number: 23LBCV02177    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff Mediterranean Shipping Company (USA) Inc. (“Plaintiff”) sued defendant Green Valley Trucking, Inc. (“Defendant”) on November 15, 2023 for breach of contract and related claims.

On August 22 and August 23, 2024, respectively, Defendant moved for an order to compel Plaintiff to respond to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. (On October 15, 2024, the Court granted an equivalent motion to deem matters admitted for failure to timely respond to requests for admission.)

On October 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition. On November 1, 2024, Defendant replied.

 

2.     Motion to Compel

a.     Legal Standard

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, “[i]f a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [t]he party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010.  . . .   [and] The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)-(b).)  

Similarly, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, “[i]f a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it . . . [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010.  . . .   [and] The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.  (Id., § 2031.300(a)-(b).)  

 

b.     Discussion

Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration and the exhibits to Plaintiff’s memoranda of authorities establish (1) Plaintiff propounded the disputed discovery on May 10, 2024 (MPA, Exh. A), and (2) at the time of filing, Plaintiff had received no response (Garber Decl., ¶ 3). Plaintiff did not file a declaration of counsel supporting his form interrogatories motion (he filed two Proposed Orders instead), but Defendant did not deny Plaintiff’s contention in its opposition.

Each of Defendant’s oppositions contends the motions should be denied because it “filed the attached responses to Defendant’s [discovery]”. The oppositions have no attached exhibits, nor are they signed, by Defendant or its attorney. Defendant’s oppositions are legally inoperative.

Plaintiff is entitled to the order it seeks.

 

 

c.     Sanctions

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under [section 2023.010 et seq.] against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel [initial responses] ... unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c) [interrogatories], 2031.300(c) [requests for production].)

Defendant does not contend it was substantially justified in its failure to serve responses or that sanctions would otherwise be unjust. Sanctions are mandatory.

Plaintiff seeks an eminently reasonable $960.00 in fees and costs in connection with each of its motions. The Court grants both requests and imposes a total of $1,920.00 in sanctions for both motions.

 

3.     Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide Code-compliant responses to the disputed discovery, without objections, and to pay $1,920.00 in sanctions to Plaintiff and/or its counsel of record within thirty (30) days of this order.

Moving Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24LBCV00161    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

This is a landlord-tenant action. Plaintiff Cecila L. Wong (“Plaintiff”) sued defendant Leroy Stone (“Defendant”) on January 29, 2024, asserting twenty causes of action arisign from her residential tenancy at a property on Montair Avenue in Long Beach.

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff moved for an order compelling Defendant to provide further responses to her Form Interrogatories, Set One – specifically, to her Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, which requests that the responding party state all facts, identify all witnesses, and identify all documents in support of each of the party’s denials or affirmative defenses.

On October 28, 2024, Defendant filed his opposition. Plaintiff filed no reply.

 

/ / /

/ / /

 

2.     Motion to Compel Further Responses

a.     Legal Standard

An interrogatory is an authorized method of discovery to which recipient parties must respond within 30 days, unless the propounding and responding parties agree to an extension. (§§ 2030.260(a), 2030.270(a).) Each response must be either an answer, a reference to external writing from which the answer may be found, or an objection to a specific interrogatory. (§ 2030.210(a).)

If the response is an answer, it must be “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (§ 2030.220 (a).) “If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible,” and “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” (§ 2030.220(b)-(c).)

If the propounding party receives an incomplete response, or a meritless or overly general objection, the propounding party may file a motion to compel further responses. (§ 2030.300(a).) The motion must include a meet and confer declaration and a separate statement. (§ 2030.300(b).)

 

b.     Meeting and Conference

The Civil Discovery Act requires a moving party to attest to a good-faith meet and confer effort as described in Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040 before a motion to compel further discovery responses may be granted. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b).)

Section 2016.040 “requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1439.) “ ‘[T]he parties must present to each other the merits of their respective positions with the same candor, specificity, and support during informal negotiations as during the briefing of discovery motions. Only after all the cards have been laid on the table, and a party has meaningfully assessed the relative strengths and weaknesses of its position in light of all available information, can there be a “sincere effort” to resolve the matter.’ ” (Id., at p. 1435 [federal standard “applies with equal force” under California law].) A “pro forma” attempt is insufficient. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration does not demonstrate a good-faith meet and confer effort that complies with section 2016.040. Plaintiff’s counsel sent a single, one-page email to Defendant’s counsel that merely repeated the terms of Plaintiff’s discovery requests and identified the responses Plaintiff deemed insufficient. (Mot., Exh. C, pp. 1-2.) Defendant’s counsel responded that she disagreed, and Plaintiff’s counsel made no further effort to explain Plaintiff’s position. (Id., at p. 1; Raedeker Decl., ¶¶ 3-5 [Defendant’s counsel’s declaration in opposition].) Plaintiff filed no reply papers to demonstrate further efforts were made.

Plaintiff’s motion is denied for failure to meet and confer prior to filing as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040.

 

c.     Sanctions

A party unsuccessfully moving to compel further discovery responses is ordinarily subject to mandatory monetary sanctions. Defendant requested none, so none are imposed.

 

3.     Conclusion

The motion is denied. Moving Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24LBCV00478    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On March 4, 2024 Jayar Vat – a minor – filed a Complaint by and through his Guardian Ad Litem Marliny Sok (Plaintiff) against the Long Beach Unified School District (the District) for two causes of action: (1) premises liability and (2) negligence. The Complaint stems from an incident where Plaintiff burnt his face on hot exposed metal pipes at Smith Elementary School located at 565 E. Hill St., Long Beach, CA 90806 (hereinafter, the Property). (Compl., ¶Prem. L-1.)

The motion now before the Court is the District’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint. Plaintiff opposes the demurrer; the District filed a reply.

2.     Demurrer

a.     Meet and Confer

“Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a).) According to the Declaration of Matthew J. Lippold (Lippold Decl.), Defense counsel initiated the meet and confer process with a letter on July 17, 2024. (Lippold Decl., ¶ 2.) The parties then briefly conferred on the same day but were unable to come to an agreement. Further meet and confer efforts initiated by Defense counsel were not responded to. (Id. at ¶¶5-7.) Nonetheless, the requirements of Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a) have been met.

b.     Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

c.     Analysis

In their moving papers the District argues that Plaintiff fails to specify an underlying statutory basis for liability against the District, which is a public entity. As specific to the second cause of action for negligence, the District contends the cause of action is barred as a matter of law. Upon opposition, Plaintiff contends that Gov. Code §835 is the statute that allows for liability against a public entity. Upon reply, the District points out the Complaint fails to mention Gov. Code §835 and that this failure is fatal. The Court agrees and sustains the demurrer, with leave to amend.

a.     Premises Liability and Negligence

“The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.)

            The District contends failure to name an underlying statutory basis for liability is fatal. Plaintiff through their opposition points to Gov. Code §835, arguing the statue allows for liability to be placed upon the District.

            Gov. Code §835 provides the following:

“Except as provided by statute, a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and that either:

 

(a) A negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or

 

(b) The public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.”

Plaintiff then contends they have pled sufficient factual allegations including the date of the incident, the location of the incident, and how Defendant’s negligent actions or omissions were the cause of Plaintiff’s injuries. The Court disagrees.

Although Gov. Code §835 is an underlying statute that allows liability to be placed on a public entity, a plaintiff must still state the basis of statutory liability in the Complaint and provide sufficient factual allegations. The Complaint fails to do the former. As to the latter, to state a cause of action against a public entity, each fact essential to the existence of statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity. (Searcy v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 802 (“Searcy”). Also see Susman v. City of Los Angeles (1969) 269 Cal.App.2d 803, 809.) The Complaint here falls short. The Complaint merely states “Plaintiff, an elementary school student, burnt his face on hot, exposed metal pipes” on the Property. (Compl., ¶Prem.L-1.) It is unclear how exactly this occurred. It is unclear where on the Property the pipes were, how the student accessed them, and how contact with the face was made. Plaintiffs allege the District had actual and constructive notice of the existence of the alleged dangerous condition in sufficient time prior to the injury to have corrected it. (Compl. ¶Prem.L-4). This is a conclusory statement because the Complaint omits any detail as to how the District had notice.  

The duty here is alleged in the Complaint and well understood, the District including the school Plaintiff attended as a student had a duty to keep Plaintiff safe. School districts and their employees have never been considered insurers of the physical safety of students. (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 869 (“C.A.”). However, the California Supreme Court also stated that “a school district and its employees have a special relationship with the district's pupils, a relationship arising from the mandatory character of school attendance and the comprehensive control over students exercised by school personnel, “analogous in many ways to the relationship between parents and their children.” (Id.) To be clear, by virtue of Plaintiff being a student at a school within the District, a duty was owed by the District to Plaintiff. (C.A., supra at 870. Also see Virginia G. v. ABC Unified School Dist. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854; Dailey v. Los Angeles Unified School District (1970) 2 Cal.3d 741, 747; and Bartell v. Palos Verdes Peninsula School District (1978) 83 Cal.App.3d 492, 498.) What is not clear, is how the District breached that duty. Without sufficient facts plead with the particularity Searcy demands, the Complaint must fall to demurrer.      

d.     Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. As there is reasonable possibility of successful amendment, Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

 

3.     Conclusion

Accordingly, Defendant Long Beach Unified School District’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.
