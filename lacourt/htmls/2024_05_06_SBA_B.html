DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00469    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

RST INVESTMENTS GROUP, LLC, 

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

21TRCV00469

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

JOCELYN YOUNG,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                         May 6, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Jocelyn Young

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff RST Investments Group, LLC

Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On June 28, 2021, plaintiff RST Investments Group, LLC filed a complaint against Jocelyn Young for (1) specific performance, (2) breach of contract, and (3) declaratory relief.  Plaintiff alleges that on May 5, 2021, the parties entered into a Residential Purchase Agreement whereby plaintiff agreed to purchase and defendant agreed to sell the property located at 2302 W. 115th Street, Hawthorne, for $550,000.  The pertinent terms were “all-cash” and “as-is” and a 14-day escrow.  On May 11, 2021, escrow opened.  Pursuant to the terms of the agreement, plaintiff was to initially deposit $5,000 into escrow after escrow opened; however, defendant failed and refused and continues to fail and refuse to sign escrow instructions and advised the broker that she had no intention of continuing on with the sale, thereby breaching the executed Residential Purchase Agreement.

            On September 13, 2021, a default was entered against defendant.

            On October 13, 2022, a default prove up hearing was held.  The court ordered judgment entered for plaintiff and against defendant for damages of $95,000, attorney fees of $10,000, interest of $12,987.67, costs of $517, and other of $7,150, for a total of $125,654.67.  The court also ordered the Court Clerk to execute all documents necessary to complete the sale, including the grant deed.  Defendant was ordered to turn over the premises upon completion of escrow.

            On October 13, 2022, a default judgment was entered.

            On February 24, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s ex parte for order allowing court clerk to execute deed in lieu of foreclosure after defendant’s default on the underlying mortgage.

            On March 23, 2023, an amended judgment was entered.

            On July 11, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s ex parte to amend order to add legal description for property.

            On July 27, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s ex parte to amend the July 11, 2023 order to specify the name of defendant and the documents to be executed by the court clerk.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“[U]nder subdivision (d) of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, the court may set aside orders and judgments that are ‘void,’ including orders and judgments void for want of fundamental jurisdiction or personal jurisdiction.”  Bae v. T.D. Service Co. of Arizona (2016) 245 Cal. App. 4th 89, 97 (citations omitted). 

            “Where a party moves under section 473(d) to set aside ‘a judgment that, though valid on its face, is void for lack of proper service, the courts have adopted by analogy the statutory period for relief from a default judgment’ provided by section 473.5, that is, the two-year outer limit.”  Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal. App. 4th 175, 180 (citations omitted).   

            “[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

CCP § 473.5(a) states, in relevant part:  “When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of:  (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.”

CCP §473.5(b) requires that “a notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party’s lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.  The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action.”

CCP §473.5(c) allows “upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.”

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Jocelyn Young requests that the court set aside the default and default judgment, pursuant to CCP §§415.20 and 473.5, and 473(d), and under extrinsic fraud and/or mistake.

            The proof of service filed on July 29, 2021 indicates that defendant was substitute served on July 26, 2021 at 1:15 p.m. on “Joseph Young Co-Occupant/Brother” and that the summons and complaint were thereafter mailed.  

            A default was entered against defendant on September 13, 2021, and a default judgment was entered against defendant after a prove-up on October 13, 2022.

            Defendant states in her declaration that the proof of service is “inaccurate.”  She acknowledges that her brother was visiting when she was purportedly served but that her brother was not a co-occupant at that time and did not live with her at the time of the alleged service, and that her brother had never given her any court documents and did not tell her about “any mail he received from someone coming to my home.”  She states that “[t]he issue with Joseph was that due to his severe condition and alcoholism, he was often disoriented and suffered mental confusion because he was consistently intoxicated, in pain, taking muscle relaxers, on pain medications, and other related heart medication.”  She asserts that when Joseph visited her for a few days the week of July 26, 2021, she believes that he took her mail and disposed of it, and that he did not know or understand he was receiving court documents.  She also states that her brother passed away on November 11, 2021, and that she “grieved for years” and “I was not in a good position.”  She further states that she “never had a copy of the Complaint and after everything that was going on, I was so confused and didn’t understand what was happening and believed nothing could be done.  [James] Vega even bought out the note for the Subject Property without my knowledge and even when I had never been given notice of any default.  The whole time I thought he was trying to kick me out of my home because he purchased the loan on my home which did not make any sense.  I eventually found some courage and started calling law firms to see if anyone could help me understand what was going on.  I then learned about how this lawsuit was filed and the false claims Plaintiff was making against me.”  

            Defendant also refers to an unlawful detainer action against her in case no. 23IWUD01792, which she answered on August 24, 2023.  In that case, on March 4, 2024, she entered into a stipulation and order to vacate the premises by April 4, 2024.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant was properly sub-served at her residence after numerous attempts and after she ignored numerous attempts to mediate and arbitrate her default of the Residential Sales Agreement, which she “executed because she was in default on her Note and Deed of Trust and was facing foreclosure.”  Plaintiff also contends that the motion is procedurally defective because she failed to file and serve a proposed responsive pleading, as required under CCP §473.5.  Plaintiff asserts that defendant had actual knowledge of the lawsuit.  Plaintiff also points to a “companion case” in San Bernardino Superior Court, where defendant defaulted, and her wages are being garnished.

            In reply, defendant contends that the failure to provide a responsive pleading is “clearly an oversight.”  Defendant further asserts that she did not receive notice of the pending action or any emails regarding mediation or arbitration because the emails were sent to her government work email “which directs unfamiliar emails to a spam folder.” 

            The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that the motion is untimely filed as she failed to file it “within a reasonable time.”  Further, the default was entered more than two years prior to her filing the herein motion.  Defendant was served with at least the following documents by mail:  notice of pending action on July 1, 2021, notice of the request for entry of default on September 13, 2021, and order on plaintiff’s ex parte application for order to allow court clerk to execute deed in lieu of foreclosure after defendant’s default on underlying mortgage.  Further, defendant acknowledges her wages have been garnished (from a companion case) and she was served with an unlawful detainer in August 2023.  Her factual assertions of lack of knowledge of the suit long ago are rejected by the Court as not credible.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

            

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            This action was initiated in May 2023.  Defendant Barnes appeared in late September 2023.  What should the Court do when two counsel, purporting to represent the same individual defendant both file cross-complaints on behalf of that one defendant?  What should the Court do when one of those defendant’s attorneys (after attempting in January 2024 to obtain the consent of opposing counsel) then files a motion seeking leave to amend the cross-complaint and asserts that the proposed amended cross-complaint essentially combines both cross-complaints into one proposed cross-complaint.  It is suggested that the proposed cross-complaint adds new claims and parties.  And what should the Court do when the trial is set for October 15, 2024.

            The Court should grant the motion, clean up the status of the pleadings, and move this case forward.  The motion for leave to amend is GRANTED.  Cross-complainant Bryan Barnes is ordered to file his SACC within five days.  Defense counsel are ordered to resolve the issue as to which defense counsel is representing Barnes, one or both.  Only one cross complaint for Barnes shall be operative, and it shall include all claims asserted by Barnes.

BACKGROUND

            On May 19, 2023, plaintiff Maliya Saani filed a complaint against Bryan D. Barnes for motor vehicle negligence and negligence based on an incident that occurred on April 6, 2023, on the 405 freeway, Hawthorne.  Plaintiff alleges that she was “rear ended with great force” by defendant, which caused plaintiff to rear end another vehicle and consequently a fourth vehicle was also struck.

            On September 25, 2023, defendant Barnes (represented by defense counsel Ryan Naim at Gould & Jefferson) filed a cross-complaint against plaintiff for apportionment of fault, declaratory relief, motor vehicle negligence, and negligence.

            On September 26, 2023, defendant Barnes (represented by defense counsel Mary Fersch at Daniels, Fine, Israel, Schonbuch & Lebovits) filed a second cross-complaint against plaintiff for equitable indemnity, implied indemnity, comparative fault, and declaratory relief. 

            On February 29, 2024, defendant and cross-complainant Barnes filed amendments (and amended amendments) to cross-complaint designating The Discovery Recovery Community LLC as Doe 1 and John Z. Petti as Doe 2.

            On April 8, 2024, the court granted and deemed the herein case related to case number 24TRCV00291. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant and cross-complainant Bryan Barnes (filed by counsel Mary Fersch at Daniels, Fine [name changed to Schonbuch Hallissy LLP]) requests leave to file an amended cross-complaint to combine the two currently-filed cross-complaints, one filed by Barnes’ personal injury attorney at Gold & Jefferson LLP and one filed by Barnes’ defense attorneys at Daniels, Fine, into a “clearer singular Cross-Complaint.”  

            Cross-complainant Barnes asserts that no new causes of action or damages claims have been added, and that the owners of plaintiff’s vehicle, The District Recovery Community, LLC and John Z. Petti, have been added as parties and served.  Cross-complainant argues that the proposed amendment is necessary to avoid any argument that the later-filed cross complaint for equitable indemnity is improper due to alleged “claim splitting.”  Barnes asserts that defense counsel engaged in good faith efforts to combine the cross-complaints and to add the Doe parties but to no avail.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the motion for leave to amend was not timely made and that cross-complainant has been dilatory, noting that defense counsel had circulated a copy of the proposed amendment in January 2024, but waited to make the motion until after a trial date was set.  Plaintiff also argues that the proposed amendment adding negligence will unduly prejudice plaintiff.  Plaintiff notes that the cross-complaint filed on September 25, 2023 along with the Doe amendments were never served on plaintiff.   (The Court would note that moving party claims to have asked that Plaintiff stipulate in January 2024 to allow the amendment, which would have been about 10 months prior to the trial date, thereby eliminated the claimed delay that Plaintiff now points to.  Plaintiff’s suggestion of undue delay is the product of its actions in part.)

            In reply, cross-complainant argues that the motion was timely filed and that there is no prejudice to plaintiff/cross-defendant because the statute of limitations does not run until April 2025, and that plaintiff has been aware of cross-complainant’s affirmative negligence and equitable indemnity claims since he appeared in September 2023.

            The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that cross-complainant complied with CRC Rule 3.1324.  The Court notes that there is no authority that allows for two cross-complaints to be operative at the same time.  While a party may have as many attorneys representing the party as they desire, they still must file one operative pleading for the one party.  The filing of the cross-complaint on September 26, 2023 “superseded” the one filed on September 25, 2023, and is the operative pleading.  There is no prejudice to Plaintiff caused by the fact that Plaintiff’s refusal to stipulated to the filing of the amended cross-complaint in mid-January has resulted in this matter not being resolved by the Court until early May.

In any event, in light of the liberal policy allowing for amendment, the motion is GRANTED to combine the cross-complaints, including adding causes of action based on negligence to the operative pleading.

The Court notes that it is unclear which counsel is representing defendant and cross-complainant, since both purported to file a cross complain tin the name of the singular cross-complaint.  The Court orders defense counsel/ cross-complainant counsel to address this issue.  Again, the party may have multiple counsel, but they must not create confusion regarding the operative pleadings of that singular party. 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            Cross-complainant Barnes is ordered to file its SACC within five days.

            Defense counsel are ordered to take appropriate action to clarify between themselves and to the court, if needed, which counsel is acting in which capacity.

Cross-complainant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
