DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30828    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 73

10/02/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/opposing party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/moving party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO FORM INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE) (filed on 06/17/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant DM Cosmetics, Inc.’s request to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory, Set One, is granted and denied in part. Further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, and 14.2 is DENIED. Further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 15.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, and 50.6 is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). On August 10, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”) asserting 8 causes of action against Cross-Defendants: (1) Breach of Oral Contract I; (2) Breach of Oral Contract II; (3) Breach of Oral Contract III; (4) Breach of Oral Contract IV; (5) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (6) Trademark Infringement; (7) Unfair Competition in Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq. and California Trademark Law; and (8) Declaratory Relief.

 

The TACC alleges the following. Prior to 2017, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Choi owned Yedam. (TACC, ¶ 11.) Yedam was experiencing serious financial problems. (Ibid.) In February of 2017, Choi approached Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lee asking for help. (Id., ¶ 12.) Choi proposed Lee serve as Yedam’s general manager, whereby Lee would oversee the Korean market and Choi would oversee the Chinese market. (Ibid.) In exchange, Lee would be paid a $10,000 per month salary. (Ibid.) In addition, Choi would give Lee 49% ownership of Yedam if Lee could help Yedam “overcome [its] financial hardship and become profitable." (Ibid.) Lee agreed and moved from the US to Korea to serve as Yedam’s general manager. (Id., ¶ 13.) To help Yedam solve its financial issues, Lee loaned Yedam $70,000.00 in operating capital. (Id., ¶ 14.) Choi promised that the $70,000.00 would be paid back when Yedam become profitable, along with 7% interest per annum from April 1, 2017. (Id., ¶ 12.) Lee successfully managed Yedam thereafter, and Yedam went from net losses in 2016 and 2017 to net profits of over a million dollars per year by the end of 2019. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, in July of 2019, Yedam faced supply chain issues when the “Milk House Candle" company in the United States refused to renew its exclusive distribution contract with Yedam. (Id., ¶ 16.) In response, the Lees created a new brand of merchandise named “Dani Mackenzie" under DM Cosmetics, Inc. (Id., ¶ 17.) The Lees trademarked Dani Mackenzie in the United States in May of 2020. (Id., ¶ 18.) Around that same time, Choi proposed that Yedam purchase Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics in exchange for Yedam having the right to use the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 19.) Choi told the Lees that there was no need to have a written licensing and distribution agreement because Choi would transfer 49% of Yedam’s ownership interest to Lee by the end of December 2020. (Id., ¶ 20.)

On December 20, 2020, Lee demanded that Choi transfer 49% of Yedam to him. (Id., ¶ 23.) Lee also demanded that Choi and Yedam repay the $70,000 operating capital loan with 7% interest from April 1, 2017. (Ibid.) However, Choi terminated Lee from Yedam shortly thereafter and refused to convey the 49% ownership share. (Id., ¶ 25.) Further, Choi and Yedam stopped purchasing Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics and started using other suppliers to manufacture products bearing the Dani Mackenzie trade name. (Id., ¶ 28.) On March 18, 2021 and April 14, 2021, Cross-Complainants demanded that Cross-Defendants cease and desist manufacturing, supplying and/or selling products under the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 32.)

 

On June 17, 2024, Cross-Complainants/Defendant DM Cosmetics filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Answers to Form Interrogatories (Set One) propounded on Cross-Defendant Eun Kyung Choi aka Grace Choi, arguing:

·       Defendants move to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, 14.2, 15.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6.

·       Plaintiff Choi provided inadequate, evasive and frivolous responses and objections

·       Defendants requests that the Court award monetary sanctions against Choi and her attorney of record in the sum of $2,700.00.

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       In the Court’s ruling on 5/17/24, the Court found that the term “incident” was unclear in the Form Interrogatories that were propounded by Defendant Daniel Lee on Cross-Defendant Choi

o   Similarly here, further response is not warranted to Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, 14.2 because the term “incident” is unclear

·       Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 concerns denial of “material” allegations. it is unclear as to what are the “material” allegations. Defendants also failed to provide a trade secret designation

·       Plaintiffs have further supplemented Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6

·       Sanctions should be awarded to Plaintiffs in the amount of $6,682.50

 

DM Cosmetics filed a reply arguing:

·       The term incident is not vague and ambiguous

·       Plaintiffs fail to provide a basis for failure to respond to Rog No. 15.1. Plaintiffs have identified a trade secret.

·       Plaintiffs have not further supplemented Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6. Plaintiff further supplemented the Form Interrogatories that were propounded by Defendant Daniel Lee on Cross-Defendant Choi, but did not supplement Form Interrogatories presently at issue that were propounded by DM Cosmetics.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”¿ CCP § 2030.010(a).¿ “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”¿ CCP § 2030.260(a).¿ 
¿ 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).¿ 

¿ 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

¿ 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(c).¿“[T]he time limitations set forth in section 2030 and section 2034, subdivision (a) [are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿(Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

a.       Interrogatory Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, 14.2

 

Form Interrogatory Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, and 14.2 seek information regarding the “Incident,” which is defined as “The circumstances and events surrounding the alleged accident, injury, or other occurrence or breach of contract giving rise to this action or proceeding.” (Park Decl., Exh. 1, p. 1.) 

 

Choi objects on the grounds that the interrogatories are vague and ambiguous as to the term “Incident.”

 

The Court agrees that the interrogatories are vague and ambiguous, as it is unclear what the “Incident” or occurrence giving rise to this action refers to. The TACC references multiple oral contracts and there appears to be multiple occurrences that gave rise to this action. Without more context as to the information Plaintiff seeks, the Court is unable to fashion a definition for “Incident.”

 

Thus, DM Cosmetic’s motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, and 14.2 is DENIED.

 

b.      Interrogatory Nos. 15.1

 

DM Cosmetics argues that further responses to Form Interrogatories No. 15.1, Set One, must be compelled. Request No. 15.1 requests the following:  

 

“Identify each denial of a material allegation and each special or affirmative defense in your pleadings and for each:

(a) state all facts upon which you base the denial or special or affirmative defense;

(b) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and

(c) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your denial or special or affirmative defense, and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT.”

 

Plaintiffs responded “Objection. Vague and ambiguous as to the term INCIDENT. Overbroad, oppressive and unduly burdensome.”

 

Here, the Court finds further response is warranted. The term incident is not included in this Interrogatory. Plaintiffs have not shown that this interrogatory is overbroad, oppressive or unduly burdensome. “If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.” CCP Section 2030.220(b).

 

Accordingly, DM Cosmetic’s motion to compel further response to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 is GRANTED.

 

c.       Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6

 

Form Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6 reference “agreements alleged in the pleadings.”

 

Choi responded to these Form Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, by stating “Objection. Overbroad, oppressive and unduly burdensome. Duplicative. Asked and answered.”

 

The Court finds further response is warranted. Choi asserts these interrogatories have been asked and answered. However, DM Cosmetics asserts that even if this interrogatory has been propounded by another Defendant, it has not been previously asked by the propounding party DM Cosmetics. Therefore, Choi fails to show that this interrogatory is duplicative. Choi also fails to show that these interrogatories are overbroad, oppressive or unduly burdensome. The information sought related to the alleged oral agreements is relevant to Defendants’ claims.

 

Based on the foregoing, DM Cosmetic’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatory nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6 is GRANTED.

 

B.      Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Defendants request that the Court award monetary sanctions against Choi and her attorney of record in the sum of $2,700.00. In opposition, Choi requests sanctions of $6,682.50 on grounds that Defendants brought this motion without substantial justification.¿ 

 

Here, Defendants’ motion is granted and denied in part. The Court does not exercise its discretion to impose sanctions.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant DM Cosmetics, Inc.’s request to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory, Set One, is granted and denied in part. Further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 14.1, and 14.2 is DENIED. Further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 15.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, and 50.6 is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied.

SUPERSTAR MONITORING LLC, et al. v. SHARP GROUP LLC, et al. (23STCV31024)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Suzanne Rand-Lewis (Gary Rand & Suzanne E. Rand-Lewis, Professional Law Corporations)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Corine Zygelman (Murchison & Cumming LLP)

 

demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE complaint (filed 6/24/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ fifth and sixth causes of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s second, third, and ninth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs Superstar Monitoring LLC; Annmarie Baranik (“Baranik”); Raymond Anthony Lerma; Samantha Brown; Adrian Napolitano and Roman Moreno filed this premises liability action against Defendants Sharp Group LLC and Rozita Natanzi.

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges the following causes of action against the Defendants: (1) Breach of Express and Implied Contract; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing/Breach of Express and Implied Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment, Trespass, Nuisance; (3) Nuisance; (4) Fraud, Negligent Misrepresentation, Concealment; (5) Negligence – Premises Liability; (6) Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Negligent Management; (7) Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200; (8) Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiffs were tenants/occupants of the subject premises, at Defendants’ Oakridge Plaza with the street address of 14336 Ventura Boulevard. Plaintiff Baranik was the lessee of the premises. Defendants entered into a written lease with Plaintiff Baranik, which provided for her to have the exclusive use of and to occupy 1,117 square feet of retail space, parking and amenities located at the premises for the use of her business Superstar Monitoring LLC, and her employees, occupants and clientele. Defendants breached the lease and their duties to Plaintiffs, including failing to remediate water intrusion on the premises which caused the premises to be unfit for use, unhealthy and unsafe.

 

On June 24, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs’ second cause of action fails for uncertainty and failure to state a claim. It is actually unclear what is being alleged in the second cause of action since the caption page shows one distinct cause of action and the body of the complaint shows another.

·       Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for nuisance is duplicative. The nuisance claim is a mere clone of the negligence cause of action.

·       Plaintiffs’ fifth and sixth causes of action for negligence are duplicative. Both causes of action are for negligence.

·       Plaintiffs’ ninth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress fails to state facts. Allegations supporting a showing that Defendants acted intentionally or outrageously have not been adequately pled. Further, the allegations regarding Plaintiffs' severe emotional distress are conclusory and sparse.

·       Defendants move to strike punitive damages because Plaintiffs fail to allege sufficient facts to support the claim for punitive damages.

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing that the Complaint sufficiently sets forth facts to constitute a cause of action.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff failed to plead facts to constitute each of its causes of action.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating she sent Plaintiffs’ counsel a letter outlining the alleged issues in the Complaint and that she attempted to call Plaintiff’s counsel but was told counsel was not available. (Mantovani Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Defendants’ counsel received an email from Plaintiffs’ counsel stating they were unwilling to amend the Complaint. (Ibid.) While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.      Second Cause of Action

 

First, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ second cause of action is uncertain.

 

CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)

 

Here, the caption of the Complaint lists the second cause of action as “Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing/Breach of Express/Implied Warranty of Habitability.” (Complaint, p. 1.) However, the body of the Complaint indicates the second cause of action is for “Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing/ Breach of Express and Implied Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment, Trespass, Nuisance.” (Complaint, p. 7.) While the body of the Complaint lists Nuisance under the second cause of action, it also indicates that Nuisance is the third cause of action.

 

Here, the Court finds the second cause of action is uncertain. The second cause of action lists various theories, the body and caption of the Complaint are inconsistent, and the second cause of action alleges Nuisance despite the third cause of action also alleging Nuisance. Thus, the second cause of action is not sufficiently clear to apprise the Defendants of the issues which they are to meet.

 

Based on this ground, the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

D.     Third Cause of Action: Nuisance

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for nuisance fails because it is duplicative of its negligence causes of action.

 

To establish an action for private nuisance, (1) “the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of his property”; (2) “the invasion of the plaintiff’s interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, that is, that it causes the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, but it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.”  (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263, cleaned up.)

 

The Complaint alleges:

 

“The conditions that Defendants negligently and intentionally caused to exist constitute a nuisance within, but not limited to the meaning of Civil Code §3479, et seq., in that said defective conditions were and are injurious to the health and safety of Plaintiffs, unsafe, offensive to the senses of Plaintiffs and did interfere substantially with Plaintiffs’ use and enjoyment of the PREMISES. Pursuant to Civil Code §3501, Plaintiffs bring this civil action for private nuisance.

 

The dangerous and defective conditions of the PREMISES constituted a nuisance, and deprived Plaintiffs of the safe, healthy and comfortable use of the PREMISES. Defendants failed to adequately abate the nuisance as required by law. Plaintiffs allege that they were exposed to unsafe, unfit premises with long standing and ongoing safety, water, mold and smoke intrusion issues at the PREMISES as more fully set forth above. Plaintiffs continued to be exposed to these unsafe conditions until they vacated the PREMISES. Defendants retaliated against the Plaintiffs when they complained and forced them to move.”

 

(Complaint, ¶¶ 32-33.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ nuisance claim appears to be a negligence claim. Plaintiffs’ nuisance cause of action is premised on the “conditions that Defendants negligently and intentionally caused to exist constitute a nuisance.” (Complaint, ¶ 32.) Where negligence and nuisance causes of action rely on the same facts about lack of due care, the nuisance claim is a negligence claim. (El Escorial Owners' Assn. v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal. App. 4th 1337, 1349; Martinez v. Pacific Bell (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 1557, 1565.) Further, the requested relief in the nuisance cause of action is also identical to the negligence cause of action. Plaintiffs do not seek injunctive relief under their nuisance cause of action.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that court’s holding in El Escorial, does not apply here because that concerned a construction defect case, in which the Court of Appeal held that the plaintiff could not maintain a nuisance cause of action for construction defect as the claim simply restated a negligence cause of action under a different label, whereas this is a habitability case.

 

However, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ argument persuasive. In El Escorial, the court did not indicate that the court’s reasoning would not apply to a habitability case. Similar to the plaintiff in El Escorial, Plaintiff does not show how the nuisance cause of action differs from its negligence cause of action.  A cause of action alleging a continuing nuisance is usually accompanied by a request for an injunction. However, also like the plaintiff in El Escorial, Plaintiffs do not request an injunction, but seek the same monetary relief that is requested in its negligence cause of action. Thus, Plaintiffs fail to distinguish their nuisance cause of action from their negligence causes of action.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

E.      Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action: Premises Liability Negligence and Negligence

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for Negligence – Premises Liability and the sixth cause of action for Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Negligent Management on the grounds that they are duplicative and must be combined into one cause of action.

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.”  (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

Premises liability is a form of negligence where the entity that owns or controls the property fails to exercise ordinary care in the management of the property and thereby exposes others to an unreasonable risk of harm. (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619.)

 

Here, although Plaintiffs’ claim for premises liability is premised on the same elements of its negligence claim, California case law recognizes premises liability as a distinct cause of action. Claims for “¿premises liability and negligence rest on the same elements—namely, (1) a legal duty of care, (2) breach of that duty, and (3) proximate cause resulting in injury.” (Martinez v. City of Beverly Hills (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 508, 517.) “A premises liability claim is distinct from a negligence claim as it ‘ “ ‘is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.’ ” ’ ” ” (Green v. Healthcare Services, Inc. (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 407, 419.) Premises liability is not interchangeable with professional negligence; it occurs where the defendant breaches an obligation “by virtue of operating facilities open to the public, to maintain [its] premises in a manner that preserves the well-being and safety of all users” of the premises. (Johnson v. Open Door Community Health Care Centers (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 153, 160.) Thus, the Court does not find that Plaintiffs must consolidate their fifth and sixth causes of action into a single cause of action. Further, Plaintiffs allege a duty (Complaint ¶ 43), a breach (id. ¶¶ 45-46), causation (id. ¶ 47) and damages (Ibid.).

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the fifth and sixth causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

F.       Ninth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ ninth cause of action fails to state a cause of action.

 

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, cleaned up.)

 

The “mere allegation that the plaintiffs suffered severe emotional distress, without facts indicating the nature or extent of any mental suffering incurred as a result of the defendant's alleged outrageous conduct, fail[s] to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.” (Pitman v. City of Oakland (1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 1037, 1047.) “The allegation that plaintiff suffered shame, humiliation and embarrassment without further factual explanation does not meet the requirement of specificity….” (Id.)

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendants’ acted deliberately and intentionally to cause Plaintiffs to suffer humiliation, mental anguish and emotional distress. (Complaint ¶ 66.) The Complaint further alleges:

 

“Defendants failed to advise Plaintiffs of issues at the PREMISES; continually refused to properly inspect, repair, maintain and remediate the damages which Plaintiffs continued to complain of, lied about and hid dangerous conditions. Defendants, and each of them, were aware that failing to provide proper security and failing to assist Plaintiffs with the items listed above, defrauding, refusing and ignoring Plaintiffs’ ongoing requests for assistance and pleas for help, caused Plaintiffs to suffer extreme emotional distress, and other consequential damages….

 

As a proximate consequence of Defendants’ wrongful acts against Plaintiffs they have suffered medical expenses, moving expenses, storage expenses, attorneys’ fees, emotional distress and other general and special damages.”

 

(Complaint ¶¶ 66, 69.)

 

The Court does not find Plaintiffs allegations sufficient to plead a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiffs merely allege they suffered emotional distress without alleging facts indicating the nature or extent of any mental suffering incurred as a result of Defendants’ alleged outrageous conduct. Thus, Plaintiffs’ allegations do not meet the requirement of specificity.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the ninth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

G.     Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendants request the Court to strike portions of the Complaint as follows:

1.      Complaint, ¶ 26

2.      Complaint, ¶ 30

3.      Complaint, ¶ 35

4.      Complaint, ¶ 41

5.      Complaint, ¶ 48

6.      Complaint, ¶ 52

7.      Complaint, ¶ 64

8.      Complaint, ¶ 70

9.      Complaint, ¶ 5 of the Prayer

 

Defendants argue the punitive damages of the allegations of the complaint are unsupported by a clear and convincing factual showing of malice, oppression or fraud.

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

The Complaint alleges Defendants advised Plaintiffs that the PREMISES was safe and habitable. Defendants made the representations with the intent to defraud and induce Plaintiffs to become and remain tenants. The Complaint further alleges Defendants concealed and/or suppressed material facts, including, but not limited to the fact that the PREMISES was not safe and had long standing and ongoing safety, water, mold and smoke intrusion issues. The Complaint also alleges, in summary, that Plaintiffs informed Defendants of these issues with the premises and Defendants failed to correct it and permitted it with the knowledge of the consequences thereof.

 

Plaintiffs also allege that both Defendants owned and managed the Premises, and that “Defendants and each of them, were the agents, principals, servants and employees of each of the remaining Defendants, and were at all times acting within the purpose and scope of said agency, service and employment. Each Defendant has consented to, ratified, permitted, encouraged and approved the acts of their/its agent. Plaintiffs are informed and believe and thereon allege, that at all material times each of the Defendants were the agents, servants, and employees of the co-Defendants, and in doing the things herein alleged were acting in a managerial capacity within the scope of their authority, whose acts and conduct herein alleged were with the permission and consent of the co-Defendants. Each of the Defendants’ actions and conduct were known to, authorized and ratified by the co-Defendants.” (Complaint, ¶¶ 2, 13.)

 

Although sparse, this factual scenario is sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages at the pleading stage. Contrary to Defendants’ assertion, pleading specific facts is not mandated for punitive damages, which merely requires ultimate facts. (Id. at 1055-1056.) The court in Perkins v. Superior Court noted that “’the distinction between conclusions of law and ultimate facts is not at all clear and involves at most a matter of degree’ [Citation.] . . . . What is important is that the complaint as a whole contain sufficient facts to apprise the defendant of the basis upon which the plaintiff is seeking relief. [Citations.]” ((1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6–7.) Taken in context of the Complaint, Plaintiffs have adequately pled a claim for punitive damages. The final decision of “[w]hether to award punitive damages . . . and the amount of such an award are questions committed to the trier of fact. [Citation.]” (Uzyel v. Kadisha (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 866, 925.) 

 

Thus, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ fifth and sixth causes of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s second, third, and ninth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike.
