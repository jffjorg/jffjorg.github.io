DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV34900    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgement

Moving Party:         Defendant 4070 Venice LLC 
Responding Party: Plaintiff Deborah McCree
Tentative Ruling: DENIED

BACKGROUND 

This is a trip and fall case. Plaintiff alleges that on October 28, 2019, Plaintiff was walking on the sidewalk in front of 4070 Venice Blvd., when she tripped and fell causing her to sustain serious injuries. Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant for general negligence and premises liability.

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues the Court should grant summary judgement because Defendant did not own, maintain or control the public sidewalk where Plaintiff fell nor create the alleged dangerous condition.

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues there is a triable issue of material fact as to Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligence and premises liability, including whether Defendant exercised dominion or control over the subject over the area of the sidewalk where Plaintiff fell and whether Defendant created the subject dangerous condition.

REPLY

No reply has been filed.

ANALYSIS 

I. Evidentiary Objections 

Plaintiff submits 4 evidentiary objections concerning the Declaration of George Mekhaiel and Ronald Mayer. Plaintiff’s objections are overruled.

II. Summary Judgement

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a)).)  To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  

In this case, Defendant meets its burden and makes a prima facie showing that there is no triable issue with respect to Defendant’s duty.  Namely, Defendant’s evidence that it did not control the public sidewalk or create the alleged dangerous condition is sufficient to make a prima facie showing that Defendant did not have a duty to Plaintiff with respect to maintaining the sidewalk. Indeed, the case Williams v. Foster provides that “[o]rdinarily, in the absence of a special relationship or statute giving a rise to a duty to act, a person has no affirmative duty to protect another from perils he did not create.” (Williams v. Foster (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 510, 521.) Defendant, by providing evidence that it neither controlled nor created the peril Plaintiff alleges, makes a prima facie case that it owed no duty to Plaintiff. The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to show a triable issue exists that Defendant owed a duty to protect Plaintiff from the alleged dangerous condition.

“An abutting landowner may be held liable for the dangerous condition of portions of the public sidewalk which have been altered or constructed for the benefit of his property and which serve a use independent of and apart from the ordinary and accustomed use for which sidewalks are designed.” (Peters v. City and County of San Francisco (1953) 41 Cal.2d 419, 423 (citing Sexton v. Brooks (1952) 39 Cal.2d 153, 157.))  “[L]iability under this theory does not arise upon a mere finding that the abutting owner derives a benefit from the alteration. The additional factors of whether the alteration serves a use independent of and apart from the ordinary and accustomed use of the sidewalk and the degree of exclusivity of such use must be considered. (Contreras v. Anderson (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 188, 202 (citing Seaber v. Hotel Del Coronado (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 481, 490–491)).  

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact as to whether a duty was owed.  There are triable issues of fact as to whether the subject driveway has any other purpose other than to provide access to the parking on Defendant’s property.  It remains for a jury to decide whether the alteration of construction of the driveway was for Defendant’s benefit and whether the driveway was exclusively used for access to Defendant’s property.  

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for summary judgement is DENIED. 

Case Number: 21STCV15295    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Rafael Moreno

Responding Party:      N/A

Tentative Ruling:        GRANT

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a car collision case arising from an April 30, 2019 incident in which Plaintiff alleges Defendant Ryan Thomas failed to stop for traffic on the highway and caused a chain-reaction collision that resulted in injuries to Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff noticed Defendant Thomas’ deposition on July 28, 2023, but it taken off calendar by counsel for Defendants. On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff again served an Amended Notice of Mr. Thomas’ deposition, but it was taken off calendar by counsel Defendant. On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff noticed Defendant Thomas’ deposition yet again, but on April 12, 2024, counsel for Defendant informed Plaintiff’s counsel that they have tried multiple times to reach Defendant Thomas, but he has been unresponsive.

 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff again noticed the deposition of Defendant Thomas.  When Defendant and Plaintiff met and conferred on June 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel informed Defense counsel that the timely noticed deposition of Defendant Thomas was going to remain on calendar unless they provided firm availability on another date for his deposition. Defense counsel reiterated that they had no contact with Defendant Thomas. Plaintiff stated the deposition would proceed as noticed, and Defendant made no objections. Defendant Thomas did not appear at the deposition, prompting Plaintiff’s counsel to obtain a Certificate of Non-Appearance. 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Plaintiff argues the Court should compel Defendant Thomas’ deposition because Plaintiff has attempted unsuccessfully multiple times to obtain it, including in cooperation with Defendant’s counsel. Plaintiff argues Defendant will not be prejudiced by granting Plaintiff’s motion, whereas Plaintiff will be heavily prejudiced if Mr. Thomas’ deposition cannot be obtained, particularly given the current December 2, 2024 trial date.

 

Plaintiff also seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,424.50 against Mr. Thomas based on a $700.00 per hour rate and 1.50 hours of work preparing this Motion and proposed Order, in addition to an estimated 1.0 hour reviewing the Opposition and preparing a reply brief and 0.50 hours attending the hearing on this motion (Declaration of Elizabeth A. Hernandez ¶13.)

 

OPPOSITION


There is no opposition filed for this motion.

 

ANALYSIS


I.          Motion to Compel Deposition 


Where a would-be deponent fails to appear for properly noticed deposition, the noticing party may move for an order to compel said deponent’s attendance, testimony, and/or production for inspection of any document described in the deposition notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.230, 2025.450 (a).) 


Here, Plaintiff’s declaration and exhibits demonstrate Plaintiff repeatedly attempted to depose Defendant Thomas and to schedule a deposition — but to no avail. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition, Exhibits 1-5.) Given that Defendant Thomas was the driver who allegedly caused the chain reaction collision, there is clearly good cause to compel the deposition.  The Court accordingly finds an order compelling Defendant Thomas to attend his deposition to be appropriate.  


II.        Sanctions


If the court grants a motion to compel deposition and produce documents, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) 


Here, Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $2,100 against Defendant Thomas for 1.50 hours of work in addition to another 1.50 hours of anticipated work relating to this motion — based on a $700.00 per hour rate, plus $254.50 for the non-appearance fee and the $60 filing fee for this motion.  The requested hours appear reasonable, but given the routine nature of this motion, the Court finds that an hourly billing rate of $450 is appropriate.  


RULING


Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition is GRANTED.  Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions against Defendant Thomas is GRANTED in the amount of $1,664.50.

Case Number: 21STCV21363    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgement/Adjudication

 

Moving Party:  Defendants Jeremy and Teresa Maltz 

Responding Party:  Plaintiff Sharon Moultrie

Tentative Ruling:    Denied

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a trip and fall case. Plaintiff alleges that on June 30, 2020, Plaintiff tripped and fell while walking on a public sidewalk adjacent to residential property in which Defendants were tenants.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

 

Defendants argue there is no triable issue in this case because they are not responsible for maintenance and repair of the public sidewalk and have no legal duty to Plaintiff. 

 

OPPOSITION

 

Plaintiff argues the motion for summary judgment should be denied because Defendants failed to completely dispose the issue of duty, and submits that Defendants owed Plaintiff a duty to use due care in the maintenance of the sidewalk in front of the property.

 

REPLY

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff failed to produce any evidence that they exercised sufficient control over the parkway to create a legal duty to third parties.

 

ANALYSIS 


I.          Evidentiary Objections 


Defendants submit three objections to the Declaration of Armin Abazari. Cross Defendants’ objections are overruled.

 

Plaintiff submits ten objections to Defendants’ evidence, specifically with respect to the Declarations of Ruben M. Green and John Coffey. Plaintiff’s objections are overruled.


II.        Summary Judgement


Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  

 

To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  An owner is charged with constructive knowledge of the existence of a dangerous condition if the condition was present for a sufficient period of time. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a)).) (Id.)

 

In Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, our Supreme Court held that a landowner who exercises control over an adjoining strip of land has a duty to protect or warn others entering the adjacent land of a known hazard there. (Id. at 1170-1171.) This duty arises even if the person does not own or exercise control over the hazard and even if the person does not own the abutting property on which the hazard is located. (Id. at 1155-1156.).  

 

Defendants’ burden on summary judgment is to affirmatively establish that no duty was owed to Plaintiff.  Here, Defendants merely established that they were tenants and did not own the public sidewalk.  However, Defendants failed to meet their burden of establishing that they did not exercise control over the subject property, that they did not maintain the subject property to the extent that might create a duty, or that they did not create the subject hazard.  More specifically, Defendants’ motion is silent as to what, if any, maintenance and control they exercised over the tree which Defendants’ expert contends was the cause of the alleged defect in the sidewalk.  Further, while Defendants contend they were not responsible for the maintenance of the sidewalk, they failed to establish that they, in fact, did not maintain the sidewalk.  Because a duty could arise if the character of Defendants maintenance and control over the tree or sidewalk was more than “minimal, neighborly maintenance” (Alcaraz, supra, 14 Cal.4th at 1167), Defendants were obligated to negate that possibility.  They failed to do so.  Consequently, Defendants failed to meet their burden on summary judgment/adjudication that one or more elements “cannot be established.”

 

RULING 

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion for summary judgement/adjudication is DENIED.

 

Case Number: 21STCV27987    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgement

Moving Party: Defendant Costco Wholesale Corporation
Responding Party: Plaintiff Cynthia Hopes
Tentative Ruling: DENY

BACKGROUND

This is a trip and fall case arising from a November 19, 2020, incident wherein Plaintiff was shopping at the Costco Warehouse (“Costco”) and allegedly tripped over an empty pallet that had been left askew in the walkway. Plaintiff now brings claims against Costco for injuries attributed to the trip and fall, alleging that Costco was negligent in its maintenance and inspection of the warehouse. 

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues the Court should grant summary judgement on three grounds. First, that Plaintiff cannot establish that Defendant did not exercise reasonable care; second, that Plaintiff cannot meet her burden of proof that Defendant had actual or constructive notice of the condition with sufficient time to discover and correct it; and third, that Plaintiff cannot meet her burden of proof regarding causation. 

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues there are disputed issues of material fact as to whether Defendant exercised reasonable care, whether Defendant breached its own policies and procedures and duty to inspect the area where the subject pallet was left unattended, and whether Defendant had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition of the unattended pallet.

REPLY

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s Opposition fails to provide any admissible competent evidence which would create a genuine issue of material fact, as Plaintiff has not disputed any of Defendant’s facts but merely qualified some of them.

ANALYSIS 

I. Evidentiary Objections 

Defendant submits 9 evidentiary objections in total concerning the Declaration of Ariella E. Perry and Supporting Evidence. All 12 of Defendant’s objections are overruled.

II. Summary Judgement

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case.  

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Civil Code § 1714(a)).) In order to impose liability on a property owner for a dangerous condition, the owner must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able to discover the condition by the exercise of ordinary care. (See Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at 1206.) An owner is charged with constructive knowledge of the existence of a dangerous condition if the condition was present for a sufficient period of time. (See id.) “Whether a dangerous condition has existed long enough for a reasonably prudent person to have discovered it is a question of fact for the jury.” (Id. at 1207.)  To establish premises liability, plaintiff must prove that the defendant breached a duty of care owed to the plaintiff that proximately caused injury and damages. “Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.” (Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  

A. Reasonable Care  

Defendant meets its initial burden of making a prima facie showing that it exercised reasonable care and thus did not breach its duty of care to Plaintiff.  Defendant provides evidence of a routine standard of care in place at the time of the incident, including hourly floor-walk inspections by employees, who are required to note any hazards or problems and immediately act to remedy the hazard. (Motion for Summary Judgement p. 11-12.) The policies Defendant provides suffice to show a prima facie case that there is no triable issue.

Plaintiff, in turn, meets its burden to show a triable issue does exist with respect to whether Defendant exercised reasonable care. Namely, while Plaintiff here does not dispute the adequacy of Defendant’s policies at the time of the incident, Plaintiff raised triable issues of fact as to whether Defendant breached its duty to inspect and breached its own policies and procedures. (Plaintiff’s Undisputed Material Facts No. 4, 6 – 8, 15, 21.) 

B. Notice

Defendant similarly meets its initial burden of making a prima facie showing that it did not have actual or constructive notice of a dangerous condition by providing evidence of its inspection policies as well as evidence that the empty pallet was on the warehouse floor for an insufficient amount of time for Defendant to have constructive notice of it. (MSJ p. 13.)

However, Plaintiff here is also able to meet her burden in showing a triable issue exists as to whether Defendant had constructive notice of the dangerous condition.  Here, Plaintiff provides evidence that the pallet she fell over was unattended and left empty for “a good ten minutes, at least.” (Plaintiff’s Undisputed Material Facts No. 4.) Plaintiff also introduced evidence that two Costco employees were near the location of the pallet.  (Plaintiff’s Undisputed Material Facts No. 7.)  Further, Plaintiff’s presents evidence that Costco’s policy was to immediately remove empty pallets from customer walkways.  (Plaintiff’s Undisputed Material Facts No. 15.)

Cases such as Ortega v. Kmart Corp. are instructive here by establishing that a similar period of time may be sufficient to create a triable issue as to whether a defendant retail company had constructive notice of a dangerous condition. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.) Moreover, Ortega reiterates the notion that the “failure to inspect the premises within a reasonable time prior to the accident” may “give rise to an inference that the defective condition lasted long enough to have been discovered and remedied.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1211.)  

Here, there are triable issues of fact regarding whether the allegedly dangerous condition existed for a sufficient time to give rise to a duty to correct it and whether Defendant was on constructive notice of the condition.  

C. Causation  

Defendant here has not met its burden of proof to make a prima facie showing that there is no triable issue on whether Defendant’s alleged conduct caused Plaintiff’s injury.  Namely, Defendant fails to provide evidence that Plaintiff’s injury was not caused by tripping over the pallet, nor does Defendant provide evidence to make a prima facie showing that Defendant was not responsible for the placement of the pallet. Defendant’s analogy to Oldenburg v. Sears is unavailing, as the pallet in this case does not parallel the piece of chalk in Oldenburg, as the pallet in this case is not located outside the storefront, and is directly owned and controlled by Defendant inside Defendant’s warehouse. Thus, a triable issue exists as to whether Defendant caused Plaintiff’s injury by failing to notice and remedy the alleged dangerous condition. (Oldenburg v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 733, 743.)

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for summary judgement is DENIED.
