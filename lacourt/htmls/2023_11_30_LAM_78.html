DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV20465    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
ROSA STEPHENSON,
Plaintiff, 
vs. 
POMONA HEALTHCARE & WELLNESS CENER, LLC, et al., 
Defendants. Case No.: 22STCV20465
Hearing Date: November 30, 2023 
 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:  
PLAINITFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INERROGATORIES, SET ONE BY DEFENDANT POMONA HEALTHCARE AND WELLNESS
Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.
Defendant is ordered to serve verified responses without objections within 10 days after the date of this order. 
Sanctions in the amount of $2,310 are imposed against Defendant. Sanctions are payable within 10 days after the date of this order.
Moving party to provide notice and to file proof of service of such notice within five court days after the date of this order.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for elder abuse and negligent hiring. Plaintiffs allege that Decedent Rosa Stephenson was a patient at Park Avenue Healthcare & Wellness Center in Pomona, California in February 2022. Defendants failed to turn Decedent every two hours to avoid the development of pressure sores, causing Plaintiff to develop severe pressure sores. Defendants’ facility was understaffed and there were no employees who could assess and treat Plaintiffs’ skin conditions. After Plaintiff was discharged from the facility, the facility was investigated by the California Department of Public Health, which found that the facility had violated federal regulations.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On June 22, 2022, Rosa Stephenson filed her Complaint against Pomona Healthcare & Wellness, LLC (“Pomona Healthcare”), Pomona Nursing & Healthcare Centre, LLC, Brius Management Co., and Rockport Administrative Services, LLC.
On August 15, 2022, Defendants answered.
On December 20, 2022, the parties participated in IDC.
On December 23, 2022, Rosa Stephenson passed away and the action was temporarily abated.
On August 3, 2023, the parties again participated in IDC.
On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff Penny Lyles, individually and as successor in interest to Rosa Stephenson, filed this motion to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One.
DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff moves for orders compelling Defendant to produce further responses to Special Interrogatories (“SROGs”), set two. 
I. Legal Standard
Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 2030.300, the Court may order a responding party to serve a further response to interrogatories when the Court finds that any of the following apply: (1) the answer to a particular interrogatory is incomplete or evasive, (2) the exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is improper or inadequate, or (3) that an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. Such a motion “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040,” which “shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., sections 2016.040, 2030.300(b)(1).) 
Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subdivision (d) authorizes a mandatory issuance of sanctions against “any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section 2030.300, subd. (d).) 
II. Discussion
Plaintiff moves to compel Pomona Healthcare’s further responses to Special Interrogatories numbers 10, 22, 23, 28, 29.
Pomona Healthcare originally served responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (“SROGs”), Set One on December 9, 2022. (Motion, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that the parties participated in IDC in December 2022 and agreed Pomona Healthcare would provide supplemental responses to SROGs 10, 22, 23, 28, 29, and 30. (Partain Decl., ¶4.) However, the parties’ proposed order was never signed because Rosa Stephenson passed away shortly after the IDC. (Id.) III. SHP’s SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE
SHP filed a special motion to strike as to the entirety of Plaintiff’s Complaint. SHP alleges that this action arises from using SHP’s funds to fund legal proceedings and planning for litigation, specifically the filing of the interpleader complaint in the Interpleader Action, and as such is subject to this motion to strike.
In assessing a defendant’s section 425.16 special motion to strike, the court must engage in a twostep process. (Shekhter v. Financial Indem. Co. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.) First, the court must decide whether the defendant has met the threshold burden of showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s constitutional rights of free speech or petition for redress of grievances. (Id.) This burden may be met by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff's cause of action was an act that falls within one of the four categories of conduct set forth in 425.16(e). If the defendant meets his initial burden, then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to establish a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim—i.e., present facts which would, if proved at trial, support a judgment in the plaintiff’s favor.  (Id. at 15051.) 
In making its determination of the anti-SLAPP motion, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b)(2).) However, the court does not “weigh credibility [nor] compare the weight of the evidence. Rather, [the court] accepts as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff and evaluates the defendant’s evidence only to determine if it has defeated that submitted by the plaintiff as a matter of law.” (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 326.) 
A. Protected Activity
Moving parties have the initial burden to demonstrate that a cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike. (Martinez v. Metabolife Inter. Ins. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 186; Fox Searchlight Pictures Inc. v. Paladino (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 294, 304.) Specifically, courts decide whether moving parties have made a prima facie showing that the attacked claims arise from a protected activity, including defendants’ right of petition, or free speech, under the Constitution, in connection with issues of public interest. (Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1, 5; Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278; Paulus v. Bob Lynch Ford, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 659, 671; Equilon Ent. v. Consumer Cause (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67; Gov. Gray Davis Committee v. Amer. Taxpayers Alliance (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 449, 458-59; CCP §425.16(e).) Moving parties can satisfy their burden by showing that causes of action arise from (1) statements made before legislative, executive or judicial proceedings, or made in connection with matters being considered in such proceedings, or (2) statements made in a public forum, or other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional rights of petition or free speech, in connection with issues of public interest. (CCP §425.16(e); Equilon Ent., LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66.)
“A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1062.) “‘defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’”  (Id. at 1063 (quoting City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78) (emphasis in original).) “‘[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’” Id. (quoting Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89).) “Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’” (Id. (quoting Navellier, supra, 29 Cal.4th at 92) (alteration in original).) “‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy that [‘arising from’] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . . . .’”  (Id. (quoting Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66) (emphasis in original).)  “In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.”  (Id.)
“Allegations of a protected activity that merely provide context, without supporting a claim for recovery, cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 395.) The legislature indicated that particular alleged acts giving rise to a claim for relief may be the object of an anti-SLAPP motion. (Id.) In cases involving allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the plaintiff is required to establish a probability of prevailing on any claim for relief based on allegations of protected activity. (Id.) Neither the form of the complaint nor the primary right at stake is determinative. (Id.) 
“Analysis of an anti-SLAPP motion is not confined to evaluating whether an entire cause of action, as pleaded by the plaintiff, arises from protected activity or has merit. Instead, courts should analyze each claim for relief — each act or set of acts supplying a basis for relief, of which there may be several in a single pleaded cause of action — to determine whether the acts are protected and, if so, whether the claim they give rise to has the requisite degree of merit to survive the motion. (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010.) “If a cause of action contains multiple claims and a moving party fails to identify how the speech or conduct underlying some of those claims is protected activity, it will not carry its first-step burden as to those claims. (Id at p. 1012.)
Here, Defendant SHP directs its anti-SLAPP motion to the entire complaint. SHP did not identify in its motion individual claims or allegations that should be stricken even if the entire complaint was not stricken. 
“Where a defendant moves to strike the entire complaint and fails to identify, with reasoned argument, specific claims for relief that are asserted that arise from protected activity, the defendant does not carry his or her first-step burden so long as the complaint presents at least one claim that does not arise from protected activity.” (Park v. Nazari (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1099, 1108.)   
Here, Plaintiff’s complaint presents at least one claim that did not arise from anti-SLAPP protected activity. For example, the fifth cause of action for breach of contract.
To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint states that in February 2019, VSP and SHP entered into a written lease wherein SHP was the tenant and VASP was the landlord. (Compl., ¶59.) SHP breached the lease by failing to pay rent for August through October of 2021. (Compl., ¶60.) Plaintiff, a liquidating member of VSP, seeks damages against SHP in the amount of $112,500, interest, and attorney’s fees and costs. (Compl., ¶60.) 
This  claim arises from SHP’s failure to pay VSP rent. This is not protected activity because it is not conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or any other protected activity enumerated under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16.
This cause of action does not relate to the Petition for Court Supervision of Voluntary Windup filed on 9/9/2022 or actions taken in connection with that matter (22STCP03350) as the failure to pay rent arose before the filing of the petition. 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context (at best) and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) Moreover, the filing date of the Interpleader Action mentioned in the Complaint is July 14, 2022, after the conduct that is the subject of this cause of action. 
It appears that the motion could be denied on this basis.
However, since the court found the Park v. Nizari authority after the oral argument on the motion was held on October 12, 2023 and the case was not raised by the other side, the court declines to deny the motion in its entirety.  Therefore the additional causes of action will be addressed.

1. First Cause of Action – Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act (“UFTA”)
In California, a fraudulent conveyance under the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act (“UFTA”) arises where a transfer made by a debtor was (1) made with the actual intent to hinder, delay or defraud any creditor or (2) made without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer and the debtor either was engaged in or about to engage in a business or transaction for which the debtor’s assets were unreasonably small or  the debtor intended to incur, or believed or reasonably should have believed that the debtor would incur, debts beyond the debtor’s ability to pay as they came due. (Civ. Code, § 3439.04(a).)
Furthermore, a transfer made by a debtor is fraudulent as to a creditor whose claim arose prior to the transfer if the debtor made the transfer without receiving a reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer and the debtor was insolvent at that time or the debtor became insolvent as a result of the transfer. (Civ. Code, § 3439.05.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that Defendants were aware that SHP’s shareholders expected to receive a distribution of SHP’s assets and that SHP’s creditors expected to be paid in full from SHP’s assets. (Compl., ¶32.) SHP transferred $3,140,000 for the benefit of Defendants in July 2022. (Compl., ¶32.) The transfers were fraudulent and intended to hinder, delay, and defraud SHP’s creditors. (Compl., ¶33.) The transfers were made without SHP receiving reasonably equivalent value in exchange for the transfer because they were made for future services to be performed by Williams, Buchalter, Tomasulo, HFB, Knepper, Buxbaum, and BBK for the benefit of SHP and its officers. (Compl., ¶33.) 
SHP argues that the protected activity giving rise to every cause of action, including this cause of action, is SHP’s decision “in selecting and paying attorneys and the litigation conduct of its lawyers for petitioning the courts for a judicially-supervised dissolution through different options like interpleader.” (SHP Motion, p. 16.) 
Here, the conduct giving rise to liability is the alleged transfer of the funds when SHP had prior creditors who were owed money. 
In Manlin v. Miller (2022) 298 Cal.App.5th 1004, 1019-1020 the Court of Appeal held that in a case where it was alleged that company funds were diverted to fund legal expenses, the protected activity of funding the payment of legal expenses was not cause of the alleged harm, but rather the diversion of funds in the first instance. Or in other words in that instance “no element of [Plaintiff’s] claim depends on the purpose of that diversion, but only on the diversion itself and whether it constituted self-dealing. The diversion may have been to further some protected activity . . . . but that purpose does not convert [Plaintiff’s] suit to one arising from the protected activity.” (Id. at p. 1020.) A claim is not based on the purpose of a diversion of funds merely because the defendant did in fact use the funds to support a protected activity. (Gaynor v. Bulen (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 864, 881.) 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394..) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
2. Second Cause of Action – Breach of fiduciary duty
The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that Defendants owed fiduciary duties to SHP and its shareholders because they were SHP’s officers, directors, or managers. (Compl., ¶¶36-38.) Defendants Underwood, Artukovich, and Knepper, as officers of SHP, breached their fiduciary duties by, doing the following:
a. Making decisions without conducting proper due diligence 
b. taking and authorizing actions in bad faith and to the financial detriment of SHP and its shareholders
c. acting in their own interests to the detriment of SHP, SHF, and their respective shareholders and members
d. failing to maximize the assets and valuations of the respective entities for which they owed fiduciary duties
e. acting in disregard of their conflicts of interest and refusing to abstain from decisions when conflicts of interest were clearly present
f. authorizing the transfer of $3,140,000 of SHP’s assets to the detriment of SHP’s creditors and shareholders so that they could and would personally benefit from such transfers. 
g. withholding financial information from plaintiff and his agents and denying access to such financial information without justification 
h. causing payments to BUCHALTER, HFB, BUXBAUM, BBK and other advisors and entities without (sic)
i. failing and refusing to dissolve Horwath & Co. so as to benefit Timothy Horwath and his family instead of acting in the best interests of S&H and its shareholders
j. failing and refusing to have SHP pay the amounts it owed to SHF and VSP because they knew that any payments to SHF and/or VSP would be distributed to the members of SHF and VSP, including plaintiff
k. intentionally withholding financial information regarding SHP from director Nadine Oddo
l. failing and refusing to shut down the business operations of SHP despite demands to do so by SHP’s shareholders 
(Compl., ¶40.) 
Buchalter, HFB, Buxbaum, and BBK aided these breaches by facilitating and advising the other Defendants to breach their fiduciary duties to SHP. (Comp., ¶41.) As a result of this and other breaches, SHP, its creditors, and shareholders suffered damages. (Comp., ¶42.) 
The facts with respect to this cause of action are akin to those in Manlin, v. Milner (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 1004.) as discussed above. The allegations do not implicate protected activity for the same reasons. Moreover, numerous theories of breach of fiduciary duty are alleged that have nothing to do with the transfer of the funds or with litigation, including failure to maximize the value of assets and conflicts of interest.
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394..) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
3. Third Cause of Action – Intentional interference with prospective economic advantage
The elements of a claim for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage include “(1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional or negligent acts on the part of the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.” (Crown Imports, LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1404.) Further, “the alleged interference must have been wrongful by some measure beyond the fact of the interference itself. For an act to be sufficiently independently wrongful, it must be unlawful, that is, it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Id.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that each Defendant was aware that SHP was winding down and that its creditors were to be paid in full, with the remaining proceeds to be distributed to SHP’s shareholders, including Plaintiff. (Compl., ¶45.) As of April 2022, SHP had assets in excess of $4 million. (Compl., ¶46.) Defendants intentionally engaged in a scheme to fraudulently transfer $3,340,000 from SHP to the defendants to disrupt Plaintiff’s economic relationships with SHP, SHF, and VSP. (Compl., ¶¶47-48.) As a result of Defendants’ interference, SHP has only $300,000 to pay all of its creditors and shareholders. (Compl. ¶48.) 
This cause of action, like the others discussed above, is based on the premise that funds were diverted. For this reason, like the other causes of action discussed, the basis for liability is the diversion of the funds, not the purpose for which they were diverted.
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
4. Fifth Cause of Action – Breach of Contract
To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint states that in February 2019, VSP and SHP entered into a written lease wherein SHP was the tenant and VASP was the landlord. (Compl., ¶59.) SHP breached the lease by failing to pay rent for August through October of 2021. (Compl., ¶60.) Plaintiff, a liquidating member of VSP, seeks damages against SHP in the amount of $112,500, interest, and attorney’s fees and costs. (Compl., ¶60.) 
This  claim arises from SHP’s failure to pay VSP rent. This is not protected activity because it is not conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or any other protected activity enumerated under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16.
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394..) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
5. Sixth Cause of Action – Open Book Account
“A book account may furnish the basis for an action on a common count . . . when it contains a statement of the debits and credits of the transactions involved completely enough to supply evidence from which it can be reasonably determined what amount is due to the claimant.  A book account is described as ‘open’ when the debtor has made some payment on the account, leaving a balance due.”  (Interstate Group Administrators, Inc. v. Cravens, Dargan & Co. (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 700, 708.)
Here, the Complaint alleges that an open book account exists between SHP and VSP because SHP’s own financial records show it owes VSP $450,001.17. (Compl., ¶63.) This debt does not appear to be related to the transfers to the law firm defendants because this debt was the amount SHP owed VSP as of August 31, 2021. (Compl., ¶62.) Therefore, this cause of action arises from the preexisting debts SHP owed VSP and not the alleged protected activity. 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394..) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
6. Seventh Cause of Action – Conversion
To plead a cause of action for conversion, one must allege (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)  
Here, the Complaint alleges that the 2019 lease between VSP and SHP required SHP to perform accounting and bookkeeping services for VSP. (Comp., ¶65.) SHP took possession of VSP’s books, records, and bank accounts. (Compl., ¶66.) After Plaintiff became a liquidating member of VSP, Plaintiff demanded that SHP make immediate payment of the amounts owed to VSP and turn over VSP’s financial documents, checkbooks, and bank statements. (Compl., ¶66.) SHP and the Director Defendants refused. (Compl., ¶67.) On October 21, 2021 and November 22, 2021, SHP transferred funds to HFB from VSP’s bank account without VSP’s permission. (Compl., ¶¶68-69.)
This cause of action arises from the withholding of VSP’s financial records and the transfer of funds from VSP’s bank account to HFB. The transfers took place in October and November 2021 and are thus not the same transfers as the July 2022 transfers allegedly used to fund litigation. The transfers were justified as “inter-company transfers” from VSP to SHP made on behalf of SHP. (Compl., ¶74.) This cause of action does not arise from the protected activity alleged by SHP. 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394..) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
7. Ninth Cause of Action – Removal of Directors
Corp. Code, section 304 provides that “the superior court of the proper county may, at the suit of shareholders holding at least 10 percent of the number of outstanding shares of any class, remove from office any director in case of fraudulent or dishonest acts or gross abuse of authority or discretion with reference to the corporation and may bar from reelection any director so removed for a period prescribed by the court. The corporation shall be made a party to such action.”
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that the Director Defendants looted SHP’s assets, knowingly filed a false verified Complaint, and set up a fund for their own use to the detriment of SHP’s shareholders and creditors. (Compl., ¶80.) 
Here, there is both protected and unprotected activity alleged as the basis of liability. With respect to the alleged looting of the assets of SHP and the transfer of funds, for the reasons discussed earlier, no protected activity is at issue.
However, the filing of the Interpleader Action is included in the conduct alleged to form the basis for the alleged statutory violation.
SHP has met its burden with respect to that part of the cause of action that relies on the filing of the Interpleader Action.  Otherwise, it has not met its burden.
8. Tenth Cause of Action – Appointment of Provisional Directors
Corporations Code, section 308 provides that a court may appoint provisional directors in two situations:
“a) If a corporation has an even number of directors who are equally divided and cannot agree as to the management of its affairs, so that its business can no longer be conducted to advantage or so that there is danger that its property and business will be impaired or lost, the superior court of the proper county may, notwithstanding any provisions of the articles or bylaws and whether or not an action is pending for an involuntary winding up or dissolution of the corporation, appoint a provisional director pursuant to this section. Action for such appointment may be brought by any director or by the holders of not less than 33 ¿ percent of the voting power.
b) If the shareholders of a corporation are deadlocked so that they cannot elect the directors to be elected at an annual meeting of shareholders, the superior court of the proper county may, notwithstanding any provisions of the articles or bylaws, upon petition of a shareholder or shareholders holding 50 percent of the voting power, appoint a provisional director or directors pursuant to this section or order such other equitable relief as the court deems appropriate.”
(Corp. Code, section 308)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that SHP has three directors, Underwood, Artukovich, and Oddo. (Compl., ¶82.) Underwood and Artukovich voted to override Oddo to act in favor and Patrick and Timothy Horwath and against Plaintiff. (Compl., ¶83.) Unless provisional directors are appointed, SHP and Plaintiff will suffer irreparable harm. (Compl., ¶84.) 
This cause of action arises from a conflict between SHP’s three directors. It does not arise from any protected conduct. 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
9. Eleventh Cause of Action – Injunctive Relief
Plaintiff’s Complaint does not set forth a statute or other legal authority under which he seeks injunctive relief. The Complaint alleges that as a liquidating member of VSP, Plaintiff was required to prepare a statement of VSP’s assets and liabilities for distribution to the members of VSP, and to liquidate the assets and property of VSP. (Compl., ¶87.) SHP, the Director Defendants, and Buchalter refused to return VSP’s books and records and falsified the records to nullify the amounts SHP owed to VSP. (Compl., ¶88.) In August 2022, these Defendants presented a court-appointed referee with records listing the professional fees owed to VSP for 2021 was $99,456. (Compl., ¶89.) However, they later increased the professional fees incurred by VSP for 2021 by $500,000 without Plaintiff’s knowledge or approval. (Compl., ¶89.) When the referee informed the members of VSP about this change, the members objected. (Compl., ¶90.) The referee advised Buchalter that it was inappropriate to increase VSP’s professional fees on its income statement. (Compl., ¶92.) As a result of the change, VSP is unable to file its tax returns. (Compl., ¶93.) Plaintiff seeks injunctive relief preventing SHP, Knepper, Buxbaum, the Director Defendants, and Buchalter from altering, reviewing, or maintaining VSP’s financial and business records. (Compl., ¶94.)
This claim arises from a dispute over VSP’s Income Statement, which was changed to reflect higher professional fees for 2021. It is not related to SHP’s Interpleader Action or its allocation of funds to pay anticipated legal fees. Therefore, the claim does not arise from activity performed in furtherance of SHP’s protected right to petition. 
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Defendant SHP has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
B. Probability of Success at Trial
SHP met its burden for the first prong as to the causes of action for removal of directors (Ninth Cause of Action) with respect to the allegation concerning the filing of the Interpleader Action. 
If the defendant meets its initial burden to show its activity is protected by the ant-SLAPP statute, then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to prove he has a legally sufficient claim and to prove with admissible evidence a probability of prevailing on the claim. (De Havilland v. FX Networks, LLC¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 845, 855.) The trial court considers the pleadings and evidence of both parties. (Ibid.) The plaintiff’s proof must be made upon competent admissible evidence. (Sweetwater Union High School Dist. v. Gilbane Building Co. (2019) 6 Cal.5th 931, 940.) The court “does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.” (Ibid.) The court’s inquiry “is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment” accepting the plaintiff’s evidence as true. (Ibid.) “The court evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law. [Citation.] ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’” (Id.; see also Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89.)¿ 
Plaintiff argues that because Defendants failed to meet their burden to show this claim arose from protected activity, Plaintiff was not required to establish the merit of those claims. Thus, Plaintiff fails to meet his burden of showing he has a probability of prevailing on this claim at trial. 
SHP’s special motion to strike is granted with respect to the ninth cause of action with respect to the theory of liability based on the filing of the Interpleader Action.




IV. The Director Defendants’ Special Motion to Strike
The Director Defendants are named in the following  causes of action (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, (3) Conversion, (4) Removal of Directors, (5) Appointment of Provisional Directors, and (6) Injunctive Relief. 
The Court would have denied this motion as untimely but Plaintiff never filed proof of service with respect to these Defendants.
A. Protected Activity
The Director Defendants allege that the following activity is protected:
1. SHP’s interpleader action: This activity is protected because it is a written or oral statement or writing made before a judicial proceeding under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e)(1).
2. Planning, pursuing, and funding: This activity is protected because they are acts in connection with litigation under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e)(2).
3. Winding up SHP: This activity is protected because it is related to SHP’s Court Supervision Petition and SHP’s 2017 settlement agreement. The court presiding over the original lawsuit continues to have jurisdiction over the settlement agreement. Therefore, the winding up of SHP is connected to litigation under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e)(2).
4. The Director Defendants’ refusal to dissolve Horwath & Co.: This activity is also protected because it is related to SHP’s 2017 settlement agreement. It is connected to litigation under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e)(2).
A settlement agreement executed in the context of active litigation is made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a judicial body. (O&C Creditors Group, LLC v. Stephens & Stephens XII, LLC (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 546, 566.) Negotiation and the execution of a settlement agreement are also protected activities. (Id at p.568.) However, a claim for breach of a settlement agreement does not arise from protected activity because the settlement agreement is incidental to the breach. (Old Republic Construction Program Group v. The Boccardo Law Firm, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 859, 869.) Breach of a settlement agreement cannot arise from protected activity because it would follow that every suit to enforce a settlement would meet the first prong of an anti-slapp motion, greatly diminishing the utility of a settlement agreement and the incentive to settle disputes. (Id at p.870.)
Here, the Director Defendants provide a copy of a 2017 settlement agreement, which the Horwath family, their companies, and other parties reached to resolve four lawsuits. (Director Defendants’ request for judicial notice, vol. 3, Exh. A.) The agreement states that the Horwath brothers agreed that their entities and orchard would be marketed for sale and the proceeds of any sale shall be divided equally among the brothers and their wives. (Id at p.3.) These entities included SHP and Horwath & Co. (Id at p.1.)
1. Second Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty
The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)
Here, as discussed in SHP’s special motion to strike, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that the Director Defendants breached their fiduciary duties by authorizing the transfer of SHP’s assets to the detriment of its creditors and shareholders, failing and refusing to dissolve Horwath & Co., failing and refusing to have SHP pay its debts to VSP, and other conduct. (Compl., ¶40.) 
The Director Defendants allege that this claim arises from the second, third, and fourth categories of protected activity. Specifically, the allegation that the transfer of SHP’s funds to the law firm defendants for future litigation expenses is protected activity. The Court finds that this claim does not arise from the funding of litigation for the same reasons as in SHP’s motion. The facts of this case are more akin to Manlin, v. Milner (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 1004, where a court ruled that the diversion of funds itself gives rise to a claim for breach of fiduciary duty, not the purpose of the diversion. Here, Plaintiff’s claim for breach of fiduciary duty arises from the diversion of funds itself, not the purpose of the diversion.
The claim for breach of fiduciary does not arise from the formation or execution of the 2017 settlement agreement. Rather, the Complaint alleges that the Director Defendants breached their fiduciary duties by failing to shut down SHP’s business operations and failing to dissolve Horwath & Co. However, according to the Director Defendants, the 2017 settlement agreement required that SHP shut down its business and dissolve Horwath & Co. Thus, the claim here is that Defendants breached their obligations under the settlement agreement.
The contention that any action by Director Defendants relating to SHP arise from protected activity since the actions arise from SHP’s Petition for Court Supervision, which was granted, also fail. The present case was filed on November 23, 2022. The petition for court supervision was filed on September 9, 2022 and the petition was not granted until January 12, 2023, after the filing of the lawsuit here. Thus, the conduct at issue predates the granting of the petition and the start of court supervision. 

With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Director Defendants have not met their burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
2. Third Cause of Action – Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage
The analysis here is the same as that set forth above with respect to SHP and the ruling is the same.
3. Seventh Cause of Action – Conversion
The analysis here is the same as that set forth above with respect to SHP and the ruling is the same.
4. Ninth Cause of Action – Removal of Directors
The analysis here is the same that set forth above with respect to SHP and the ruling is the same.
5. Tenth Cause of Action – Appointment of Provisional Directors
Corporations Code, section 308 provides that a court may appoint provisional directors in two situations:
“a) If a corporation has an even number of directors who are equally divided and cannot agree as to the management of its affairs, so that its business can no longer be conducted to advantage or so that there is danger that its property and business will be impaired or lost, the superior court of the proper county may, notwithstanding any provisions of the articles or bylaws and whether or not an action is pending for an involuntary winding up or dissolution of the corporation, appoint a provisional director pursuant to this section. (Corp. Code, section 308)
The Complaint alleges that the dispute between SHP’s directors rendered SHP unable to conduct its business or dissolve and wind up in an orderly manner.
Here, the Director Defendants argue that this claim arises from protected activity because it arises from the 2017 settlement agreement. The court does not see how this particular claim arises from the settlement agreement. This claim arises from an alleged dispute between SHP’s three directors and does not arise from protected activity.
With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Director Defendants have not met their burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.

The analysis here is the same as that set forth above with respect to SHP and the ruling is the same.

V. Buchalter’s Special Motion to Strike
Buchalter is named under the causes of action for (1) Violation of the UFTA, (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (3) Unjust Enrichment, and (4) Injunctive Relief. 
Buchalter alleges that Plaintiff’s claims against Buchalter arise from petitioning activity arise from actions Buchalter took during litigation on behalf of its client, SHP. Specifically, Buchalter alleges that its actions presenting information to a court-appointed referee were undertaken during litigation on behalf of SHP, as well as the filing of Interpleader Action.
Petitioning activity, including communications that are intimately intertwined with, and preparatory to, the filing of judicial proceedings is protected activity. (Cabral v. Martins (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 471, 480.) Petitioning activity committed by attorneys on behalf of a client is also protected. (Id.)
A. Protected Activity

The same analysis and ruling applies as that set forth above for this cause of action with respect to SPH.
2. Second Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty
The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)
Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges that the Buchalter breached its fiduciary duty by  facilitating and advising the Director Defendants to breach their fiduciary duties by causing the transfer of SHP’s assets to the law firm defendants. (Compl., ¶41.) 
As discussed in SHP’s and the Director Defendants’ special motions to strike, the facts of this case are more akin to Manlin, v. Milner (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 1004, where a court ruled that the diversion of funds itself gives rise to a claim for breach of fiduciary duty, not the purpose of the diversion. Here, Plaintiff’s claim for breach of fiduciary duty arises from the diversion of funds itself, not the purpose of the diversion.

For these reasons, Defendant Buchalter has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
3. Fourth Cause of Action – Unjust Enrichment
“The elements of an unjust enrichment claim are the receipt of a benefit and the unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.” (Peterson v. Cellco P’ship (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593.) “Unjust enrichment is not a cause of action, however, or even a remedy, but rather a general principle, underlying various legal doctrines and remedies. It is synonymous with restitution.” (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 231.) 
This cause of action alleges that Buchalter and other recipients of the fund transfers were unjustly enriched at the expense of SHP’s creditors and shareholders. Since this essentially is a restitution request and is derivative of the causes of action for fraudulent transfer and breach of fiduciary duty, the analysis is the same as for those substantive causes of action.
 With respect to the allegation regarding the filing of the Interpleader Action which is alleged in the introductory allegations and incorporated by reference into this cause of action, this allegation merely provides context and cannot support a claim for recovery here. As such, it cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Baral v. Schnitt  (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394.) 
For these reasons, Defendant Buchalter has not met its burden with respect demonstrating that this cause of action arises from a protected activity.
4. Eleventh Cause of Action – Injunctive Relief
With one exception, this cause of action does not arise from protected activity for the same reasons as in SHP’s and the Director Defendants’ motions. This claim arises from a dispute over VSP’s Income Statement, which SHP changed to reflect higher professional fees for 2021. It is not related to SHP’s interpleader action or its allocation of funds to pay anticipated legal fees. Therefore, the claim does not arise from activity performed in furtherance of Buchalter’s protected right to petition. 
The exception is a portion of paragraph 88 which alleges that Buchalter presented false information to a court-appointed referee in a case in which Buchalter represented SPH. Buchalter has met its burden with respect to this allegation.
B. Probability of Success at Trial
Plaintiff did not brief the merits of the eleventh cause of action. There, Buchalter’s motion is partially granted as to this cause of action.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for breach of contract, fraud, negligent misrepresentation, intentional interference with contract, and unlawful competition arising over a dispute over the purchase of real property located at 31700 Railroad Canyon Road, Canyon Lake, Riverside County, California 92587. Plaintiff alleges that on January 26, 2023, she entered into an agreement to buy the subject property from Defendant Joseph & Patricia Catallo Family Trust (“Trust”). On January 31, 2023, made a counter-offer for the property which Trust accepted. DISCUSSION
Ronda Baldwin-Kennedy, Counsel for Plaintiff Jean Shir moves to be relieved as counsel.

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. (2)) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’  [Citation.]”  (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)  The court should also consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.”  (Ramirez v. Sturdivant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)
 
California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)).  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (d), (e).)
Here, Counsel filed a Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel (MC-051), Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel (MC-053), and a Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel (MC-052) on the appropriate forms, as outlined within California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subdivisions (a), (c), and (e). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (e).) The Court is satisfied that Counsel has a compelling reason to withdraw because of a breakdown of the attorney-client relationship. (MC-052, Item #2.)
Counsel served Plaintiff via mail at its last known address and confirmed the address was current through conversation at least 30 days before this motion was filed. (MC-052 Item #3b.) 
There are no other hearings set for this matter. There is a case management conference scheduled for the same date as the hearing on this motion. Trial has not yet been set. There is sufficient time for Plaintiff to secure new counsel. Plaintiff would not be prejudiced if this motion was granted.
