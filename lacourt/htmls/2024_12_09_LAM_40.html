DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV12319    Hearing Date: December 9, 2024    Dept: 40

23STCV12319 Daniel E. Kelly v. Jonathan S. Kelly, et al.

Monday, December 9, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING IN PART AND OVERRULING IN PART DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT BY DEFENDANTS, JONATHAN S. KELLY AND DAVHAN, INC.

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The first amended complaint (“FAC”) filed on June 26, 2024, alleges that Defendant, Jonathan S. Kelly, (“Jonathan”), breached a 2015 written contract that would guarantee Plaintiff £10,000 every month in exchange for Plaintiff ceding management and control of two parcels of real property located in London, England. The first two conversion claims arise from the alleged forgery and transfer of Plaintiff’s 15 percent interest in Kellytoy Worldwide, Inc. (“KT Stock”) by Defendant, Jonathan (father) to his children, Adam Kelly, Maya Kelly, and David Kelly (collectively, “Kelly Defendants”).

       The third count for conversion arises from the alleged wrongful transfer of Plaintiff’s 35 percent interest in Hamilton Towers, LP (“Hamilton interest”) to Jonathan’s mother. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants engaged in a coverup of these forged transfers with Defendant, Citrin Cooperman, alleged to be a licensed certified public accountant, who filed tax returns on Plaintiff’s behalf without Plaintiff’s knowledge.

       The fourth conversion count arises from Jonathan’s alleged wrongful distribution of funds from the sale of 28 Lancaster Gate in London without distributing Plaintiff’s share of the sale proceeds. Plaintiff alleges 15 causes of action related to these events, including derivative claims for dissolution on behalf of the Hamilton Partnership and 4811 S. Alameda Partnership (“Alameda Partnership”).  

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Defendants, Jonathan and Davhan, Inc., (collectively, “Davhan”), demur to the conversion claims (second, third, 14th, and 15th causes of action), and the sixth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty on grounds these claims are barred by the applicable statute of limitations. Plaintiff did not allege delayed discovery or other facts to avoid the limitations bar. The alter ego allegations are insufficient and render the claims uncertain. The claim for violation of Penal Code, section 496(e) (15th cause of action) does not allege facts to support all elements of the claim.

       In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the statute of limitations is not clearly barred based on the allegations of the complaint. Plaintiff does not have to allege delayed discovery unless it affirmatively appears that the claims are barred. Plaintiff alleges Defendant Jonathan concealed the transactions. The 15th cause of action is adequately alleged. The allegations are not fatally uncertain. Alternatively, Plaintiff asks for leave to amend.

       In reply, Davhan argues that “on or about” allegations do not avoid the limitations bar. Allegations regarding delayed discovery are equally insufficient. The remaining claims at issue are defective for the reasons previously asserted. Leave to amend should not be granted.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

       A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing the complaint’s sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations as well as facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded facts. The court may also consider matters properly subject to judicial notice. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

       The court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) Plaintiff is required to allege facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126.)

       Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

       A demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

       A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.) However, unlike federal courts, California state courts are not a notice pleading jurisdiction, and notice alone is not a sufficient basis for any pleading. California is a fact pleading jurisdiction. Merely putting an opposing party on notice is not sufficient. (Bach v. County of Butte (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 554, 561; see Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 244, 250.)

IV.    DISCUSSION

A.      Demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the second, third, sixth, 14th, 15th causes of action as the claims are clearly barred by the statute of limitations, and Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support delayed discovery to avoid the limitations bar.

       In order for the court to sustain demurrer to the complaint based on a statute of limitations defect, “the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint… . A demurrer will not lie where the action may be but is not necessarily barred.” (Citizens for a Responsible Caltrans Decision v. Department of Transportation (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 1103, 1117.)

       The statute of limitations for conversion is three years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338 subd. (c).) The statute of limitations for breach of fiduciary duty is the same as fraud (three years from the date of discovery). (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 828.)

       The causes of action for conversion are based on alleged forgeries that occurred on January 7, 2007; December 15, 2009;  and December 18, 2009. (Complaint, ¶¶ 25, 31, 41.) Plaintiff alleges he learned of the alleged conversions less than 12 months prior to filing the complaint and could not have discovered these events earlier because Defendants kept the events hidden from Plaintiff (Complaint, ¶¶ 27, 31, 45.) The claim for breach of fiduciary duty is based on the alleged forgeries and 2017 acts to cover up the alleged conduct. (Complaint, ¶ 72.)

       The 14th cause of action for conversion arises from Jonathan’s failure to distribute Plaintiff’s interest in the sale of London Choice Estates Limited, which was dissolved on May 7, 2011. (Complaint, ¶ 130-131.) Plaintiff did not discover this until December 2022, because of what Plaintiff was “led to believe.” (Complaint, ¶ 133.)

       The 15th cause of action for violation of Penal Code § 496(c) is based on the alleged forgeries (Complaint, ¶ 139.)

       If Plaintiff claims that belated discovery applies, Plaintiff must allege it in the complaint by stating when the discovery was made, the circumstances behind the discovery, and the facts showing that the failure to discover was reasonable, justifiable and not the result of a failure to investigate or act. (Bastian v. County of San Luis Obispo (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 520, 527.) Plaintiff has not adequately alleged delayed discovery for each of these claims.

 

B.      Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the alter ego allegations to support liability for the sixth, eighth, 10th, and 12th causes of action

       Liability based on an alter ego theory is not itself a claim for substantive relief, but rather a procedural claim “to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice." (Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359.)

       Liability based on an alter ego theory is not itself a claim for substantive relief, but rather a procedural claim “to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice." (Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359.) “[W]hile it is the better practice to allege the facts upon which a plaintiff seeks to hold a defendant on the alter ego theory, still it is the law of California that that issue may be raised by a simple allegation that the defendant sought to be charged had made the contract involved. (Pan Pacific Sash & Door Co. v. Greendale Park, Inc. (1958) 166 Cal.App.2d 652, 655-656.)  “Had petitioner elected to proceed by such a conclusionary but permissible allegation, its right thereafter to seek by discovery the facts by which that allegation might be proved would have been beyond question.” [emphasis added].) (Los Angeles Cemetery Ass'n v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 492, 494.) Here, Plaintiff alleges “Jonathan and DAVHAN acted without corporate separateness and Defendant Jonathan exercised complete dominance and control over DAVHAN, and its assets, such that the DAVHAN served as a mere shell and instrumentality for the conduct and activities of Defendant Jonathan. On information and belief, Plaintiff alleges that due to the commingling and diversion of funds and assets, treatment of corporate assets as his own, failure to maintain corporate requirements and disregard of legal formalities, and many other factors, there was a unity of interest and ownership between DAVHAN and Defendant Jonathan such that the separate personalities of DAVHAN and Defendant Jonathan do not in reality exist.” [FAC ¶8] Accordingly, the alter ego theory is adequately pleaded.

 

C.      Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 15th cause of action for violation of Penal Code § 496 .

       This cause of action is based on the alleged forgeries, and concealment of such as previously alleged. Defendants argue that Plaintiff must allege that Defendant Jonathan received or had possession of the KT Stock. Plaintiff alleged that Jonathan’s children received the interest.

       Section 496 of the Penal Code is commonly referred to as receiving stolen property "(1) that the particular property was stolen, (2) that the accused received, concealed or withheld it from the owner thereof, and (3) that the accused knew that the property was stolen." (Finton Construction, Inc. v. Bidna & Keys, APLC (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 200, 213.) The elements required for civil liability to attach “are simply that (i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property." (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)

        The express language of the statute permits statutory liability against every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen "or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained … .” (Pen. Code, § 496.) The allegations previously identified states that Both Jonathan and Citrin concealed or aided in concealing the transfer of Plaintiff’s property interests.

       Moreover, "the requisite possession of the stolen property may be either actual or constructive and need not be exclusive. In fact, physical possession is not required, as it is sufficient if the defendant acquires a measure of control or dominion over the stolen property.” (In re Anthony J. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 718, 728.)  As previously noted above, Plaintiff repeatedly alleges that both Jonathan and Citrin intentionally and wrongfully exercised dominion and control over the KT Stock and Hamilton partnership interests inconsistent with Plaintiff’s rights. (FAC ¶ 72, et seq.)

V.      CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is SUSTAINED in part as to the second, third, sixth, 14th, and 15th causes of action as the claims are clearly barred by the statute of limitations and Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support delayed discovery to avoid the limitations bar.

       Demurrer to the 15th cause of action on grounds Plaintiff does not allege Jonathan’s receipt of the allegedly stolen property interests is OVERRULED.

       Demurrer to the sixth, eighth, 10th, and 12th causes of action based on alter ego allegations is OVERRULED.

       The court grants 30 days leave to amend. (Colvig v. RKO General, Inc. (1965) 232 Cal.App.2d 56, 69–70. [Leave to amend is proper where Plaintiff has not had a fair opportunity to amend, and the defect is capable of being cured.].)

DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

I.        ARGUMENTS

       Defendant, CITRIN COOPERMAN & COMPANY, (“Citrin”), demurs to the sixth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, seventh cause of action for conversion, and the 15th cause of action for violation of Penal Code § 496(c) because Plaintiff cannot allege facts that Citrin cause Plaintiff’s injuries and because all claims are barred by the statute of limitations. Plaintiff did not allege that Citrin had knowledge that the property (KT Stock and Hamilton partnership interest) were stolen, or that Citrin received, withheld, or concealed the property at issue.

       In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the claims are not clearly barred by the statute of limitations. Plaintiff alleged he did not discover the misconduct until one year before Plaintiff filed the complaint. Plaintiff does not have to allege delayed discovery unless it affirmatively appears that the claims are barred. Plaintiff alleges that Citrin undertook to file Plaintiff’s tax returns without Plaintiff’s knowledge and concealed them from Plaintiff. That Plaintiff did not hire Citrin as his accountant does not negate that Citrin owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty at the moment Citrin undertook to prepare Plaintiff’s taxes. Plaintiff does not allege a claim for negligent preparation of tax returns that triggers an audit. The Penal Code violation is adequately alleged.

       In reply, Citrin reiterates that the FAC does not allege facts to toll the statute of limitations. A fiduciary duty does not exist under the alleged facts. There can be no legal injury unless Plaintiff has suffered a tax deficiency assessment, which is not alleged. Leave to amend should not be permitted.

II.      LEGAL STANDARDS

       The court incorporates the legal standards previously articulated with respect to the DAHAN demurrer.

III.                DISCUSSION

A.      Demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the sixth, seventh, and 15th causes of action as the claims are clearly barred by the statute of limitations and Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support delayed discovery to avoid the limitations bar.

       In order for the court to sustain demurrer to the complaint based on a statute of limitations defect, “the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint… . A demurrer will not lie where the action may be but is not necessarily barred.” (Citizens for a Responsible Caltrans Decision v. Department of Transportation (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 1103, 1117.)

       The statute of limitations on a claim for injury caused by the wrongful act of another is two years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 335.1.) The statute of limitations for breach of fiduciary duty three years from the date of discovery. (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 828.) This claim arises from the alleged forgeries and 2017 acts to cover up the alleged misconduct. (FAC, ¶ 72.)The 15th cause of action for receipt of stolen property is based on alleged theft of Plaintiff’s property arising from the 2007, 2009, and 2017 acts of alleged forgery. (FAC, ¶ 239.) Actions based upon a liability created by statute including actions for the specific recovery of personal property is three years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338.)

       Plaintiff alleges he learned of the alleged misconduct less than 12 months prior to filing the complaint and could not have discovered these events earlier because Defendants kept the events hidden from Plaintiff (FAC, ¶¶ 27, 31, 45.)

       Delayed discovery of Plaintiff’s claims must be alleged in the complaint by stating when the discovery was made, the circumstances behind the discovery, and the facts showing that the failure to discover was reasonable, justifiable and not the result of a failure to investigate or act. (Bastian v. County of San Luis Obispo (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 520, 527.) Plaintiff has not adequately alleged delayed discovery for each of the claims at issue.

B.      Citrin has not persuasively established that a fiduciary relationship did not arise between Plaintiff and Citrin.

       Citrin argues that tax accountants are not fiduciaries as a matter of law and such a duty was not created since Plaintiff alleges that Citrin operated without Plaintiff’s knowledge or consent. Citrin contends that the client must deliberately create a fiduciary relationship with his accountant. (Richard B. LeVine, Inc. v. Higashi (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 566, 586 ["The creation of a fiduciary obligation or duty must, at a minimum, arise from facts demonstrating the formation of a confidential relationship. None are present here.”].) Higashi is distinguishable. Higashi addressed the issue of whether an accountant’s fiduciary duty owed to a partnership that hired him extended to an individual partner. Higashi answered that question in the negative. (Higashi at 586.)

       Here, Plaintiff alleges that Citrin and Jonathan together created the alleged forgeries and concealed them from Plaintiff, in part when Citrin undertook to prepare Plaintiff’s tax returns without Plaintiff’s knowledge, as Plaintiff was living in the United Kingdom at the time. (FAC ¶42.)

       Plaintiff’s case authority supports the contention that a fiduciary relationship can arise where the alleged fiduciary "either knowingly undertake[s] to act on behalf and for the benefit of another or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’ [Citation."].(Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1338.) Plaintiff’s case authority does not preclude the creation of a fiduciary relationship under the alleged circumstances.

C.      Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the elements of causation and legal damage allegedly suffered by Plaintiff in support of each cause of action alleged against Citrin.

       Citrin argues that for all claims alleged against Citrin, Plaintiff failed to allege that any misconduct caused harm to Plaintiffs, since the claimed injury was already complete before Citrin’s alleged act or omission. Specifically, Plaintiff cannot allege facts that Citrin’s post-2002 tax preparation services caused him to lose the KT Stock or Hamilton shares between 2007 and 2017, or that Citrin erred in its tax accounting or subjected Plaintiff to any sort of tax penalty or tax overpayment flowing from Plaintiff’s loss of the KT stock or Hamilton shares.

       Citrin’s argument fundamentally misconstrues the Plaintiff’s allegations as arising from Citrin’s post-2020 tax preparation services. The alleged misconduct was both Jonathan’s and Citrin’s exercise of dominion and control over the KT stock and Hamilton shares and the transfer of ownership to other parties by hiring a licensed CPA employed by Citrin to forge and conceal the transactions by Citrin’s preparation of Plaintiff’s tax returns without Plaintiff’s knowledge (FAC, ¶ 72.a. – h.) Plaintiff alleges he lost income and property as a result. (Complaint, ¶ 73.)

       Citrin’s contention that Plaintiff failed to allege legal injury arising from Citrin’s preparation of negligent tax preparation is inaccurate since Plaintiff does not allege negligent tax preparation, but rather Jonathan’s and Citrin’s alleged concerted conduct in effecting the ownership and transfer of Plaintiff’s property rights and concealing the nature of those transactions by Citrin’s filing of Plaintiff’s tax returns.

       Citrin relies on International Engine Parts, Inc. v. Feddersen & Co. (1995) 9 Cal.4th 606, for the “bright line rule” that the legal injury arising from a negligent tax preparation claim does not occur until an IRS audit process is finalized and a tax deficiency is assessed. (Dem. 11:22-12:1.) Fedderesen has no application here.  Plaintiff is not alleging negligent tax preparation.

       Citrin contends that based on the allegations, any damage suffered by Plaintiff occurred when Jonathan allegedly transferred Plaintiff’s interests to third parties, such that anything Citrin could have done in preparing Plaintiff’s tax returns did not cause damage. This argument is not based on what Plaintiff has actually alleged, namely that Jonathan and Citrin intentionally exercised dominion and control over Plaintiff’s property interests by preparing forged documents and then concealing those transfers from Plaintiff by undertaking to “forge and conceal” Plaintiff’s tax returns that evidence Plaintiff’s ownership interests. (FAC ¶ 72.a-h.)

D.     Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 15th cause of action for violation of Penal Code § 496 (c).

       This cause of action is based on the alleged forgeries and concealment of such misconduct as previously alleged. Citrin argues that Plaintiff did not allege that Citrin received or had possession of the interests purportedly stolen, or that Citrin had knowledge that the property was being stolen.

       Section 496 of the Penal Code is commonly referred to as receiving stolen property: "(1) that the particular property was stolen, (2) that the accused received, concealed or withheld it from the owner thereof, and (3) that the accused knew that the property was stolen." (Finton Construction, Inc. v. Bidna & Keys, APLC (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 200, 213.) The elements required for civil liability to attach “are simply that (i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property." (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)

       As the express language of the statute provides and as Citrin readily admits, statutory liability is imposed against every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen "or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained … .” (Pen. Code, § 496.) The allegations previously identified states that Citrin concealed or aided in concealing the transfer of Plaintiff’s property interests.

       Moreover, "the requisite possession of the stolen property may be either actual or constructive and need not be exclusive. In fact, physical possession is not required, as it is sufficient if the defendant acquires a measure of control or dominion over the stolen property.” (In re Anthony J. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 718, 728.)  As previously noted above, Plaintiff repeatedly alleges that both Jonathan and Citrin intentionally and wrongfully exercised dominion and control over the KT Stock and Hamilton partnership interests inconsistent with Plaintiff’s rights. (FAC ¶ 72, et seq.)       

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is SUSTAINED in part as to sixth, seventh, and fifteenth, causes of action as the claims are clearly barred by the statute of limitations and Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support delayed discovery to avoid the limitations bar. In all other respects, demurrer is OVERRULED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING THE MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT IN PART AND DENYING IN PART FILED BY CITRIN COOPERMAN, LLP.

 

I.        ARGUMENTS

       Citrin moves to strike the demand for restitution, attorney’s fees, and exemplary and punitive damages. The claim for restitution against Citrin is not supported since Plaintiff does not allege that Citrin was unjustly enriched. The title interests were alleged transferred to third parties, not Citrin. Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support a claim for punitive damages to support corporate ratification of its employees alleged conduct (Bernard Golditch), who allegedly took part in the alleged forgeries, concealment, and preparation of Plaintiff’s tax returns. The prayer for attorney’s fees arising from the claim for breach of fiduciary duty is not supported by any facts to support such recovery based on contract or statute.

       In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the motion is procedurally defective because Citrin did not fully quote the phrases to be stricken. Plaintiff did not quote the attorney’s fees portion of the FAC verbatim as required by the California Rules of Court. The prayer should not be stricken since Plaintiff also seeks recovery of punitive damages against Jonathan. Punitive damages are appropriate in a claim for breach of fiduciary duty. Restitution is appropriate in a claim for breach of fiduciary duty.

       In reply, Citrin reiterates the points made in its motion and requests that the court not grant leave to amend.

 

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARDS

       The court may, upon motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of the pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the Court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436 subd (a)-(b).) Grounds for the motion to strike are limited to matters that appear on the face of the pleading or on any matter which the court shall or may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

III.    DISCUSSION

A.      The prayer for attorney’s fees is proper as alleged.

       It is not an abuse of discretion to refuse to strike a claim for attorney fees where Plaintiff has not had a full opportunity to determine the basis for such fees. (Camenisch v. Superior Court (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1689, 1699 ; Yassin v. Solis (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 524, 533, ["There is no requirement that a party plead that it is seeking attorney fees, and there is no requirement that the ground for a fee award be specified in the pleadings."].)

 

B.      Plaintiff has not alleged corporate ratification to support imposition of punitive damages against Citrin.

Punitive damages will not be imposed against a corporate employer absent proof of advance knowledge of an employee’s unfitness or ratification of that employee’s conduct by an officer, director or managing agent. (Civ. Code, § § 3294 subd. (b).) Plaintiff alleges that liability against Citrin is vicarious since Citrin employed Bernard Golditch, who allegedly engaged in the conduct of forging documents to support transfer of Plaintiff’s rights and prepared Plaintiff’s tax returns without Plaintiff’s knowledge in order to conceal those transfers. (FAC ¶ 4.)

       Plaintiff may recover on a claim for exemplary damages if Plaintiff establishes “by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code, § 3294 subd. (a).) The predicate acts to support a claim for punitive damages must be intended to cause injury or must constitute “malicious” or “oppressive” conduct as defined by statute. “Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294 subd. (c)(1); College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725 ["malice involves awareness of dangerous consequences and a willful and deliberate failure to avoid them"]. "Oppression" is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294 subd. (a) subd. (c)(2).)

       Absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, the conduct must be “despicable” which is defined as “base, vile, or contemptible.” (College Hospital Inc. at 725.) Plaintiffs must demonstrate that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others." (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 90.) Conduct constituting negligence, gross negligence or recklessness is insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. (Dawes at 87.)

       While the facts alleged against Golditch may suggest a finding that his conduct was “despicable," Plaintiff does not allege a claim against Golditch, individually. Liability against Citrin as employer for Golditch’s conduct must comply with the requirements for corporate ratification.

C.      The prayer for restitution is not supported by the FAC.

       Relief for restitution is proper where “the defendant obtained a benefit from the plaintiff by fraud, duress, conversion, or similar conduct. In such cases, the plaintiff may choose not to sue in tort, but instead to seek restitution on a quasi-contract theory (an election referred to at common law as “waiving the tort and suing in assumpsit”).” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 388.)

       While Plaintiff may elect to pursue this equitable relief instead of damages in tort, Plaintiff must still allege that Citrin unjustly obtained a benefit from the plaintiff to support the claim. Accordingly, the prayer for restitution is not supported without additional facts.

IV.    CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, the motion to strike is GRANTED in part as to the prayer for restitution and the claim for punitive damages against CITRIN. In all other respects, the motion to strike is DENIED. Plaintiff is GRANTED 30 days leave to amend to allege additional facts to support the claim.

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT BY DEFENDANTS, ADAM KELLY, MAYA KELLY, AND DAVID KELLY

 

 

I.        ARGUMENTS

       Defendants, Adam Kelly, Maya Kelly, and David Kelly (collectively the “Kellys”), who are alleged to be Jonathan’s children , demur to the claims asserting conversion, for unjust enrichment, and for violating Penal Code § 496(c) on grounds they are barred by the statute of limitations. (FAC ¶ 25.) The conversion claims (in two counts) fail for uncertainty. It is not clear what role the Kellys played in the conversion, since they were young children at the time. The claim for unjust enrichment is a remedy and not a cause of action.  The claim for stolen property fails because Plaintiff does not allege the Kellys’ knowledge that their ownership interests were stolen, which the Kellys deny.

       In opposition, Plaintiff alleges that the conversion claims are adequately stated since the Kellys refused to return Plaintiff’s interests upon Plaintiff’s demand. The claims are not fatally uncertain. While there is technically no claim for unjust enrichment, Plaintiff alleges an adequate basis for restitution. Plaintiff alleges that the Kellys had knowledge of their father’s forgery and theft of Plaintiff’s interests and that they acted in concert with him.

       In reply, the Kellys argue that Plaintiff has not alleged any acts by the Kellys from which the two-count claims for conversion arose. Plaintiff alleges only that the Kellys acted in concert with Jonathan; Plaintiff did not allege facts that the Kellys acted intentionally to deprive the Plaintiff of his rightful possession. A pre-lawsuit demand is not required to support the claims for conversion under the circumstances alleged here.

II.      DEMURRER

       The court incorporates the legal standards on demurrer as previously articulated.

III.    DISCUSSION

A.      Demurrer to the conversion claims is overruled.

       The second and third causes of action for conversion are alleged against the “Joint Defendants” who are Jonathan (father) and his children, Adam, Maya and David Kelly. The children are referred to here as “the Kellys.” (FAC ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges that on January 7, 2007, Jonathan and the Kellys acted in concert without Plaintiff’s consent to intentionally and wrongfully exercise dominion and control over Plaintiff’s 7.5 percent interest in KT Stock by forging a letter signed by Plaintiff purporting to gift the stock to the Kellys. (FAC ¶ 24).

       The second conversion claim arises from the alleged conduct on December 15, 2009, of Plaintiff’s remaining interest in KT stock (7.5 percent) by Jonathan and his children (FAC ¶ 39.) The Kellys argue that the conversion claims are fatally uncertain because they are conclusory.

       The Kellys rely on a case stating the general requirements of pleading requiring a “clearness and precision so that nothing is left to surmise.” (Ankeny v. Lockheed Missiles and Space Co. (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 531, 537.)

       “Conversion” is defined as “the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another.” (Duke v. Superior Court (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 490, 501.) The alleged facts must support the following elements: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant's conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages. “It is the uniform law that shares of stock in a company are subject to an action in conversion.” (Id.)

       Plaintiff allege he was the lawful owner of 15% in Kellytoy Worldwide, which Jonathan and the Kellys, acting in concert and without Plaintiff’s consent, intentionally and wrongfully exercised dominion and control in a manner inconsistent with Plaintiff’s property rights. (FAC ¶¶ 25, 39.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants accomplished this by forging letters purporting to gift the stock to the Kellys. (FAC ¶ 39.) Plaintiff attaches exhibits to the pleading which are the purportedly forged letters addressed to Jonathan and signed by Plaintiff releasing Plaintiff’s shares in KT Stock. (FAC, Exh. 3, 4.)

       The Kellys claim of uncertainty arises from their assertion that they were children at the time, and it is not clear how they could have acted in concert with Jonathan to forge the letters. The uncertainty arises from facts not alleged in the pleading. Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

       A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.) Demurrers for uncertainty are strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, “because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

       Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case alleged "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.) 

       Plaintiff has alleged facts to support the required elements. Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

D.     Demurrer to the claim for restitution is OVERRULED.

       Relief for restitution is proper where “the defendant obtained a benefit from the plaintiff by fraud, duress, conversion, or similar conduct. In such cases, the plaintiff may choose not to sue in tort, but instead to seek restitution on a quasi-contract theory (an election referred to at common law as “waiving the tort and suing in assumpsit”).” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 388.)

       The claim for restitution arises from the allegation that Plaintiff has been deprived of property interests arising from the alleged wrongful acts of Jonathan and the Kellys. A person who is unjustly enriched at the expense of another is required to make restitution and is recognized as a cause of action. (Professional Tax Appeal v. Kennedy-Wilson Holdings, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 230, 238 [“The elements of a cause of action for unjust enrichment are simply stated as ‘receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.’”].)  

       Regardless, the Kellys do not explain how the inclusion of this cause of action causes any prejudice based on the assumption that restitution is not a formal cause of action. The court must disregard errors in pleadings or proceedings, which do not affect the substantial rights of the parties. (Code Civ. Code, §. Proc., § 475.)

E.      Demurrer to the claim for violation of Penal Code § 496(c) is OVERRULED.

       This cause of action is based on the alleged forgeries and concealment of such as previously stated. Defendants argue that Plaintiff did not allege that the Kellys knew the property was stolen and will not be able to “prove” the claim because Defendants were minor children. (Dem. 4:14-18.)

       Section 496 of the Penal Code is commonly referred to as receiving stolen property "(1) that the particular property was stolen, (2) that the accused received, concealed or withheld it from the owner thereof, and (3) that the accused knew that the property was stolen." (Finton Construction, Inc. v. Bidna & Keys, APLC (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 200, 213.” The elements required for civil liability to attach “are simply that (i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property." (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)

       The matter of Defendants’ infancy at the time of the alleged conversion is an evidentiary matter affecting Plaintiff’s burden of proof, which the court does not consider at the demurrer stage. Furthermore, liability is imposed under the statute if the receiver knows the property to be stolen or obtained “or [the person] conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained," (Switzer at 126.)

       Accordingly, the claim is sufficient as alleged.

IV.    CONCLUSION.

       Based on the foregoing, the Kelly Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint is OVERRULED.

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AMENDMENT NO. 1 TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT TO ADD ALL PARTNERS AS DEFENDANTS IN THE DISSOLUTION CLAIMS

 

I.        RELEVANT BACKGROUND

       The first amended complaint (“FAC”) filed on June 26, 2024, alleges that Defendant, Jonathan S. Kelly, (“Jonathan”), breached a 2015 written contract that would guarantee Plaintiff £10,000 every month in exchange for Plaintiff ceding management and control of two parcels of real property located in London, England. The first two conversion claims arise from the alleged forgery and transfer of Plaintiff’s 15 percent interest in Kellytoy Worldwide, Inc. by Defendants, Jonathan Kelly, Adam Kelly, Maya Kelly, and David Kelly (collectively, the “Joint Defendants”).

       The third count for conversion arises from the alleged wrongful transfer of Plaintiff’s 35 percent interest in Hamilton Towers, LP (“Hamilton Partnership”) to Jonathan’s mother. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants engaged in a coverup of these forged transfers with Defendant, Citrin Cooperman, alleged to be a licensed certified public accountant, who filed tax returns on Plaintiff’s behalf without Plaintiff’s knowledge.

       The fourth conversion count arises from Jonathan’s alleged wrongful distribution of funds from the sale of 28 Lancaster Gate in London without distributing Plaintiff’s share of the sale proceeds. Plaintiff alleges 15 causes of action related to these alleged events, including derivative claims for dissolution on behalf of the Hamilton Partnership and 4811 S. Alameda Partnership (“Alameda Partnership”). 

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Plaintiff requests leave to add all partners as Defendants in the 12th and 13th causes of action for involuntary dissolution and for an appointment of a receiver. The partners are indispensable parties in the dissolution claims. There is no prejudice to defendants who asserted in their respective answers that Plaintiff failed to join necessary and indispensable parties as a second affirmative defense.

       In opposition, Defendants argue that they have spent considerable time and effort preparing their respective motions including a motion to compel arbitration and a motion to stay the dissolution claims set for hearing on December 12, 2024. Defendants contend they have a right to be heard. Defendants argue it makes no sense for Plaintiff to amend the complaint again if the litigation will be stayed pursuant to Defendants’ pending motions. The arbitrator can determine who are the proper parties in this case. Alternatively, Defendants ask that the court continue this hearing until after Defendants’ motions are heard on December 12, 2024.

       In reply, Plaintiff argues Defendants have not shown prejudice given their second affirmative defense asserting failure to join indispensable parties. Defendants’ motions to compel arbitration and stay the action set for December 12, 2024 will not render this motion moot. The arbitration motion has nothing to do with the dissolution claims. The court cannot order a buyout without all necessary partners included in the action.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

       Leave to amend is permitted at the court’s discretion upon any terms that may be just. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 subd. (a)(1).) The statute is liberally construed to permit amendment of the pleadings “unless an attempt is made to present an entirely different set of facts by way of the amendment.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 760.)

       If the motion is timely made, and the granting of the motion will not result in prejudice to the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend. (Morgan v. Superior Court of Cal. In and For Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) Where denial of the motion will result in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action, “it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Id.) Amendments are permitted up to the date of trial or during trial where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party. (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.)

IV.    DISCUSSION

       For purposes of clarity, the court construes Plaintiff’s motion as a motion for leave to file a second amended complaint.

        The joinder of a party is compulsory where complete relief cannot be accorded without that party or that party claims an interest relating to the subject matter such that disposition in his absence will impede that party’s ability to protect that interest. Under these circumstances, the court must order joinder of that party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 389.)

       In any suit for judicial dissolution of a limited partnership, “the other partners may avoid the dissolution of the limited partnership by purchasing for cash the partnership interests owned by the partners so initiating the proceeding … .” (Corp. Code, § 15908.02.) Accordingly, the partners Plaintiff seeks to join have an interest in this litigation and are indispensable parties. (Kraus v. Willow Park Public Golf Course (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 354, 369 ["Normally, in a suit to dissolve and wind up the affairs of a partnership, all of the partners are considered indispensable parties to the litigation.”]

       Defendants have not shown any substantial prejudice to their rights resulting from the amendment except for the time and resources they have spent preparing their respective motions. The policy permitting amendments is liberally construed at any stage of the proceeding so as to dispose of cases upon their substantial merits where the authorization does not prejudice the substantial rights of others.’" (Board of Trustees v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1163.) Permitting leave to amend will bring all necessary parties before the court and will promote judicial efficiency with respect to later motions that may potentially affect the new parties. This is especially true of the Alameda Partnership, who seeks a stay of the dissolution and proposes a buyout of Plaintiff’s interest. The proposed buyout affects all of the partners in the partnership and cannot be fairly considered without them.

V.      CONCLUSION

       Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to file a second amended complaint is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to file the proposed Second Amended Complaint within 30 days.
