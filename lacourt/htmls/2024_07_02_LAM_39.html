DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV23481    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

July 2, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV23481

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Deposition

MOVING PARTIES:

Defendants Virginia Toledo, Ronnie Toledo, Edward Toledo, and Guadalupe Toledo

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Estibi Fuentes Tamayo

 MOTIONS

            Defendants Virginia Toledo, Ronnie Toledo, Edward Toledo, and Guadalupe Toledo (“Defendants”) move to compel Plaintiff Estibi Fuentes Tamayo (“Plaintiff”) to appear for deposition.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If a party to the action fails to testify at deposition after service of a deposition notice, and the party has not served a valid objection to that deposition notice, the party who noticed the deposition may move for an order to compel the deponent to attend and testify at deposition.  (Code Civ. Proc., §2025.450, subd. (a).) 

Defendants served Plaintiff with a notice of the deposition.  (Declaration of Jonathan P. Cyr, Exhibit D.)  Plaintiff objected to the deposition notice on the basis Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel were unavailable on the noticed date.  The date of the deposition is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice, to which Plaintiff could object. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410.)  To date, Plaintiff has not appeared for deposition.  Accordingly, the motion is granted.

Plaintiff argued Defendants failed to adequately attempt to informally resolve this matter.  Plaintiff is incorrect.  Defendants were only required to “contact[] [Plaintiff] to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  Defendants did so. (Declaration of Jonathan P. Cyr, Exhibits E, F.)

The Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to appear for deposition as noticed is an abuse of the discovery process and imposes sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., §2025.450, subd. (g).)    Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Jacob O. Partiyeli, are to pay sanctions to Defendants in the amount of $800, which represents four hours of attorney time to prepare the motion and attend the hearing at $200 per hour.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion to compel Plaintiff to appear for deposition is granted.  Plaintiff is to appear for deposition within 30 days of the notice of this order, unless Defendants stipulate otherwise.  The scope of the deposition is as set forth in Exhibit D attached to the Cyr Declaration.

Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Jacob O. Partiyeli, jointly and severally, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $800 to Defendants, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV03300    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: 39

 TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 2, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV03300

MOTION

Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant Wilson Sporting Goods Co.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Marielita Palacios

 BACKGROUND

Defendant Wilson Sporting Goods Co. (“Defendant”) moves for an order quashing the service of summons in this action.  Plaintiff Marielita Palacios (“Plaintiff”) opposes the motion.  

ANALYSIS

Both parties rely extensively on federal case law.  The Court is not bound to follow the decisions of lower federal courts.  (People v. Superior Court (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 409, 431.)  Therefore, the court disregards those citations.  The court also disregards citations to trial court orders in such cases.  “Neither federal district court procedures nor decisions are binding on this court.” (Thomas G. Ferruzzo, Inc. v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 501, 503.)  Likewise, “a written trial court ruling has no precedential value. [Citation.]” (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshé (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831.)

Under the due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment to the United States Constitution, state courts may exercise personal jurisdiction over nonresidents who have “minimum contacts” with the forum state.  Minimum contacts exist when the relationship between the nonresident and the forum state is such that the exercise of jurisdiction does not offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.”  (International Shoe Co. v. Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316, internal quotations omitted.)

“Personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific.  A nonresident defendant may be subject to the general jurisdiction of the forum if his or her contacts in the forum state are substantial[,] continuous and systematic.  In such a case, it is not necessary that the specific cause of action alleged be connected with the defendant's business relationship to the forum.  Such a defendant's contacts with the forum are so wide-ranging that they take the place of physical presence in the forum as a basis for jurisdiction.”  (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 445-446, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

Even if a nonresident defendant does not have sufficient contacts with California such that the defendant is subject to suit in California generally, the defendant may nonetheless be subject to jurisdiction in California for claims based on the defendant’s activities in the state.  To assert “limited” or “specific” personal jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant, the plaintiff must demonstrate that: (a) the out-of-state defendant purposefully established contacts with the forum state; (b) the plaintiff's cause of action “arises out of” or is “related to” defendant's contacts with the forum state; and (c) the forum's exercise of personal jurisdiction in the particular case comports with “fair play and substantial justice.”  (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 477-478.)

“[W]hen jurisdiction is challenged by a nonresident defendant, the burden of proof is upon the plaintiff to demonstrate that ‘minimum contacts’ exist between defendant and the forum state to justify imposition of personal jurisdiction.”  (Elkman v. National States Ins. Co. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1313.)  If the plaintiff meets the initial burden, “it becomes the defendant’s burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.” (Buchanan v. Soto (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1353, 1362.)

Defendant provides evidence that it is a Delaware corporation, with its principal place of business in Illinois.  (Declaration of Daniel S. Hurwitz, Exhibit A.)  Defendant provides no information regarding Defendant’s operations in California.  As such, Defendant fails to show it lacks sufficient minimum contacts with California for California to exercise general jurisdiction over Defendant. 

Further, Plaintiff’s complaint in this case is based on accessing Defendant’s website from California.  A nonresident defendant who engages with California residents through the Internet may be subject to local personal jurisdiction.  (See Jewish Defense Organization, Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1060.)  Plaintiff presents evidence Defendant’s website includes a privacy policy, which specifically addresses the privacy rights of Californians who visit its website, as well as information on Defendant’s efforts to comply with the California Transparency in Supply Chains Act of 2010.  (Declaration of Scott J. Ferrell, ¶¶ 8-9.)  Further, Plaintiff’s evidence reveals Defendant operates retail stores in California and enables users to locate nearby retail stores in California through its website.  (Id., ¶ 11.)  As such, Defendant expected Californians to access and interact with its website.  Accordingly, the motion to quash is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTIONS

            Respondent Liberty Mutual Fire Insurance Company (“Respondent”) moves to compel responses from Claimant Bijan Haghnazarzadeh (“Claimant”) to Form Interrogatories ("FROG"), set one and Specially Prepared Interrogatories (“SROG”), set one.     

 ANALYSIS

In its notice of motion filed on April 10, 2024, Respondent notes it moves to compel Claimant to respond to the FROG.  Although it references a memorandum of points and authorities, a declaration from its counsel, and a proposed order, neither of those were filed with the notice concerning the FROG.  Separately, Respondent did file a memorandum of authorities.  But that memorandum only spoke to the SROG, and Respondent attached to it as exhibits the SROG propounded on Claimant and a series of meet and confer letters regarding the discovery that had been served but for which no responses had been received.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, Exhibit A.)   In the memorandum, there was no mention of, or reference to, the FROG, nor did Respondent provide a copy of the FROG that was propounded on Claimant.  The Court cannot compel Claimant to respond to the FROG, which is not before the Court.  Accordingly, the motion as to the FROG is denied without prejudice. 

As for the SROG, Respondent has established Claimant did not provide any responses to them, despite Respondent having served multiple letters requesting such responses since they were first served on Claimant on August 15, 2023.  Under the circumstances, Claimant has waived his right to object to the SROG.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)  Therefore, Claimant is ordered to provide responses to the SROG without objections within 20 days. 

Respondent asks for sanctions to be levied against Claimant in the amount of $418.65.  However, Respondent has not detailed by way of a declaration or otherwise how that amount was derived. On that basis alone, the court will not grant the request for sanctions in this instance. 

Respondent is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
