DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV43151    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: W

KEITH SMITH vs LIAT BEROUKHAI

 

motion FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT  

 

Date of Hearing:        September 5, 2042               Trial Date:       March 17, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        20STCV43151

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Liat Talasazan

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Keith Smith (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Liat Talasazan (erroneously sued as Liat Beroukhai) for damages arising from an automobile incident.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Liat Talasazan’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance is MOOT.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant Liat Talasazan requests this court take judicial notice of Plaintiff Keith Smith’s Complaint filed in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 20STCV43151, filed on November 12, 2020. (Exh. A). The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Defendant Liat Talasazan moves the court for an order granting summary judgment in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff on the grounds that Plaintiff’s alleged injuries and damages were not caused by Defendant and therefore, Plaintiff’s complaint for negligent against Defendant has no merit as a matter of law.

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Id.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

Defendant Talasazan moves for summary judgment on the grounds Plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence has no merit. Specifically, Defendant Talasazan argues Plaintiff cannot establish any breach of duty of care or that any action or inaction on the part of Defendant Talasazan was the proximate cause of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries or damages. The court agrees. Defendant Talasazan submits evidence Plaintiff testified that the car that struck him was a white Lexus SUV. (UMF 7.) On the date of the accident, Plaintiff observed a white Lexus SUV illegally parked and when Plaintiff waited for the driver of the white Lexus SUV to exit the parking lot, Plaintiff approached the driver and informed her she was parked illegally. (UMF 7.) While conversing with the woman, Smith was approximately 10 to 15 feet away and standing to the rear of the passenger side of her vehicle. (UMF 8.) Plaintiff Smith proceeded to move directly behind the woman’s SUV while trying to get all the way across the rear of the SUV and out of the path of the SUV. (UMF 9.) However, Plaintiff failed to get out of the path of the USV in time and the white Lexus struck the right side of Plaintiff. (UMF 10.) After colliding with Plaintiff Smith, the driver of the white Lexus SUV continued to back up while rolling down her window to ask if Plaintiff Smith was ok. (UMF 11.) Plaintiff Smith stated that he was not ok, but the driver of the SUV just “took off”. (UMF 11.) The white Lexus SUV that struck Plaintiff did not have a license plate. (UMF 12.) Plaintiff testified he does not know the identity of the driver or the owner of the white Lexus USV and did not speak with the driver after the date of the accident. (UMF 13.) Defendant testified that she was not involved in the incident and does not know who was involved. (UMF 16.) On the day of the accident, Defendant was driving a 2017 or 2018 charcoal Cadillac Escalade registered to her ex-husband. (UMF 18.) She further testified she never owned, drove or had access to a white Lexus SUV and did not recognize the vehicle. (UMF 20, 21.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant has met their burden demonstrating no triable issue of material fact exists. As noted by Defendant, “a non-suit is properly granted where the plaintiff offers no evidence to show who was the driver or owner of the vehicle alleged to have negligently caused the injury claimed by plaintiff.” (Calderon v. Dispatch Trucking Co. (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 217, 222–223.)

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff has failed to oppose the motion. Therefore, Defendant is entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Talasazan’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH THIRD PARTY SUBPOENA TO JONATHAN MARSAND BALTER AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff moves to compel the deposition and production of documents of non-party Jonathan Marsand Balter (“Balter”).  Plaintiff asserts he served Balter with a valid deposition subpoena that required Balter to appear for his deposition on October 18, 2023, but Balter failed to appear.  

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records.  (CCP § 2020.010.)  A deposition subpoena may command: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent, (2) only the production of business records for copying, or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things.  (CCP § 2020.020.)

 

A “written notice and all moving papers supporting a motion to compel an answer to a deposition question or to compel production of a document or tangible thing from a nonparty deponent must be personally served on the nonparty deponent unless the nonparty deponent agrees to accept service by mail¿or electronic service¿at an address¿or electronic service address¿specified on the deposition record.”  (CRC Rule 3.1346.)   

 

In this case, Plaintiff asserts Balter was present at the time of the incident, and had assisted Plaintiff in identifying Defendant. Plaintiff avers Balter drafted an incident report, and is in possession of video surveillance of the incident. Balter allowed Plaintiff to view the video, but did not send a copy. Defendant provides proof of service showing personal service upon Balter with the deposition subpoena. (Mot. Exh. 1.) There has been no objection filed to the subpoena for Balter’s deposition, nor has any opposition or motion to quash been filed.  

 

The court previously denied the motion on the grounds the proof of service indicated Balter was served by mail and there was no evidence that Balter agreed to accept service by mail. Therefore, Plaintiff must serve the moving papers by personal service. The proof of service now indicates the motion was served by mail and e-mail. At the hearing, Plaintiff must provide evidence the parties agreed to service by e-mail. If not, the motion is denied without prejudice.

 

Nonetheless, the court believes this motion is MOOT given the court’s intention to grant summary judgment to defendant. 


Case Number: 22VECV02549    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: W

14241 VENTURA LLC v. EMPIRE CONSTRUCTION SERVICES INC., et al.

 

DEMURRER to the third amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        September 5, 2024                           Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02549

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Forms and Surfaces, Inc.

Responding Party:     Plaintiff 14241 Ventura LLC

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Danishwar Decl. ¶¶2-4.)[1]

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 23, 2022, Plaintiff 14241 Ventura LLC filed a complaint against Defendants Empire Construction Services Inc. and Kean Christopher Evens for breach of contract, breach of implied good faith and fair dealing, intentional misrepresentation, and negligent misrepresentation. Plaintiff alleges on September 6, 2022, Plaintiff 14241 entered into a written contract with the Defendant Empire where Defendant would perform certain construction work on Plaintiff’s property in exchange for monetary consideration. However, Defendant Empire breached the contract by failing to perform the contracted work, abandoning the project in its entirety, and failing to refund the payments made by Plaintiff.

 

On June 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint, asserting causes of action for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of implied warranty, breach of express warranty, and negligence against Empire Construction Services Inc., All United Roofing Inc., Beyond Electric Inc. Calgrove Rentals, Door Max, ESD Euro Image LLC dba ESD European Style Doors, Forms and Surfaces, Inc., King Iron Works, Inc., Kone, Inc., Pico Glass Inc., Sam’s Custom Finishing Inc. dba Meza Painting Company, Santos Lopez Drywall, and Waterproofing Experts, Inc. Plaintiff alleges they entered into agreements with each of the named Defendants to provide work on their property; however, each Defendant failed to fulfill their obligations in performance of the agreements in that they failed to complete their scope of work, delayed construction of the Subject Property, failed to perform work that is free from defects and in compliance with the applicable standards of care in the industry. Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint on May 17, 2024.

 

Defendant Beyond Electric, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff on August 17, 2023. On August 24, 2023, All United Roofing Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Beyond Electric, Inc. Kone Inc. filed a cross-complaint on January 9, 2024. King Iron Works, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff on January 26, 2024. Empire Construction Services Inc. filed cross-complaint against MOES 1-25 on August 30, 2023. Empire dismissed their cross-complaint on May 6, 2024. Cal Grove Rentals, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Mikayel Israelyan and 7007 Riverside LLC. Cal Groves dismissed the cross-complaint on May 22, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Forms and Surfaces Inc.’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Forms and Surfaces, Inc. demurs to Plaintiff’s Third Amended Complaint on the grounds the complaint is vague, ambiguous and unintelligible as well as fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action against Defendant and is duplicative.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs to the breach of contract claim on the grounds Plaintiff fails to allege it complied with the contract and inspected the goods as required.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) 

 

Defendant argues the complaint fails to identify what breach occurred and what damages were sustained. Specifically, Plaintiff’s TAC fails to state that it complied with the “contract” and inspected the goods as the contract clearly requires that any damage claims are filed within five business days of delivery. Moreover, Plaintiff’s TAC fails to allege whether the goods were delivered damaged or whether the alleged conditions complained about were damaged during installation.

 

The court disagrees. The complaint has sufficiently alleged Plaintiff complied with the terms of the contract, Defendant’s breach and damages resulting from Defendant’s breach. Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged Defendant breached the contract by supplying architectural metal sheets that were wavy with distorted surfaces, when they were supposed to be flat like a mirror, without any waves and distortions, and to have holes in them. (SAC ¶38.) Taking Plaintiff’s allegations as true, Plaintiff alleges they fully performed all covenants, conditions, and obligations required of it to be performed in accordance with the terms and conditions of said agreements, which would include Plaintiff filing a damage claim within five business days. (SAC ¶21.) Moreover, Plaintiff has alleged damages as a result of receiving the distorted metal sheets from Defendant. (SAC ¶¶50-51.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Defendant demurs to the breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing cause of action on the grounds Plaintiff again fails to state that it complied with the “contract” and inspected the goods as well as allege whether the goods delivered damaged or whether the alleged conditions complained of were damaged during installation.

 

The covenant of good faith and fair dealing is implied by law in every contract, and it acts “as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract.” (Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031-1032.) 

 

Defendant makes the same arguments as their breach of contract claim. For the reasons stated above, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Breach of Implied Warranty

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for breach of implied warranty on the grounds the TAC fails to allege Plaintiff complied with the contract and inspected the goods as required or that the goods were delivered damaged.

 

An action for breach of warranty requires a showing that there was a sale of goods, that defendant expressly or impliedly warranted the goods sold, and that there was a breach of that warranty that caused plaintiff to suffer harm. (See Scott v. Metabolife International, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 404, 415-16.) A breach of warranty cause of action will not lie where only services have been furnished. (See Gautier v. General Telephone Co. (1965) 234 Cal.App.2d 302, 307.) The court looks to the essence of the agreement to determine whether the agreement was for the sale of goods or the provision of services. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1186.) “When service predominates, the incidental sale of items of personal property [] does not alter the basic transaction.” (Id. (alteration in original).)  

 

Defendant makes the same arguments as the breach of contract cause of action. For reasons stated above, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Negligence

 

Defendant demurs to the cause of action for negligence on the grounds the TAC is utterly devoid of material facts establishing the elements of negligence.

 

To plead a cause of action for negligence, one must allege (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.) “In order to state a cause of action for negligence, the complaint must allege facts sufficient to show a legal duty on the part of the defendant to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 614, 619.)  In California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) 

 

For reasons stated above, Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action is OVERRULED.


 


Case Number: 24VECV00215    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: W

WALTER BANEGGAS vs JAGUAR LAND ROVER NORTH AMERICA, LLC, et al.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

 

Date of Hearing:        September 5, 2024                                        Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        24VECV00215

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Walter Baneggas

Responding Party:     Defendant Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC   

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Thomas decl. ¶¶7-11.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On January 17, 2023, Plaintiff Walter Baneggas filed a complaint against Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC, Galpin Jaguar Lincoln-Mercury, Inc. dba Jaguar Van Nuys/Land Rover Van Nuys for Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty. Plaintiff purchased a 2018 Lan Rover Range Rover Velar. Plaintiff alleges they delivered the vehicle to an authorized repair facility for nonconformities, but Defendants were unable to conform the subject vehicle to the applicable express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Special Interrogatories and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED, IN PART.

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Production of Documents is GRANTED, IN PART.

III.                Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Request for Admissions is DENIED. 

 

discussion

 

I.                    PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ FIRST SET OF SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff Baneggas moves the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiffs’ first set of Special Interrogatories and monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,660.00 against Defendant Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC and its attorney of record, Turner Henningsen Wolf and Vandenburg LLP.

 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ (CCP § 2030.300(a).)¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ (CCP § 2030.300(b).)¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ (California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).)

 

Plaintiff moves for further responses to Plaintiff’s special interrogatories, set one. Specifically, in response to Special Interrogatories 1-3, 8-9, 17 and 42 Defendant referenced by name certain documents it claimed were “produced concurrently herewith.” However, Defendant produced no documents. In Response to Special Interrogatories 10-16 and 19-20, Defendant referred Plaintiff to its response to Form Interrogatory 15.1. Lastly, in response to Special Interrogatories 18, 21-27, 29-41 and 43-48, Defendant asserted only objections.

 

In opposition, JLRNA argues it properly responded to the subject interrogatories. Specifically, JLRNA has identified the specific documents and as for the rest, the objections are warranted as the interrogatories are vague and ambiguous. The court disagrees, in part. Further responses are warranted as to SROG Nos. 8, 9, 11-17, 19-20. JLRNA cannot just refer to another response or case. As for the remaining requests, the court finds they are vague or overbroad as to time and therefore, the objections are not without merit. The court also notes JLRNA’s response “Discovery is continuing, and JLRNA reserves the right to supplement its response based upon further investigation” is not necessarily improper. However, if some information is present, it should be provided now. If new information does come up during JLRNA’s ongoing investigation, JLRNA’s should supplement its answers. If after reasonable inquiry, JLRNA cannot answer the request, JRLNA must state so.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Special Interrogatories is GRANTED, in part, as to SROG Nos. 8, 9, 11-17, 19-20.

 

The request for sanctions in the amount of $3,660.00 is DENIED.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST SET OF REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff Baneggas moves the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiff’ first set of request for production of documents and monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,660.00 against Defendant JLRNA and its attorney of record, Turner Henningsen Wolf & Vandenburg, LPP. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds JLRNA has failed and refused to provide code-compliant responses to discovery as to request for production of documents nos. 1-41 and 44-46.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).)

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant has provided unverified responses on April 9, 2024 and still has not provided verifications for these responses. In response to RFPs 1-10, 12-13, 15 and 44 Defendant stated it would produce certain documents. In response to RFPs 16, 17 and 33-36, Defendant provided a link to a website where it contended responsive documents could be accessed “for a nominal fee.” Lastly, in response to RFPs 11, 14, 18, 19-32, 37-41 and 45-46, Defendant asserted only objections. Plaintiff notes as to those JLRNA agreed to produce, JLRNA must be held to its own agreement to produce documents (RFPs 11, 14, and 19-32.)

 

In opposition, JLRNA argues the motion was not served with sufficient notice because Plaintiff forgot to take into consideration the additional two court days required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6. JLRNA contends the motion should be denied on this basis alone and if not, requests this court reschedule the hearing to a later time with sufficient amount of notice. The court finds JLRNA was not prejudiced by the late filing. The filing was late by two days and JLRNA was able to oppose the motion on the merits despite forgetting to properly calendar their opposition.

 

JLRNA next argues the motion is procedurally defective because what Plaintiff really seeks is a production of documents, not further responses. The court finds no grounds to deny the motion for basis of mislabeling. Plaintiff provided all the necessary information for a motion to compel compliance and JLRNA was able to oppose the motion on that basis.

 

JLRNA lastly argues Plaintiff has failed to show good cause for the Franchise Agreement between the selling dealership and JLRNA, Plaintiff has failed to show good cause for documents identifying all OBDII Codes, and the requests related to vehicles of same make, model and year are overbroad, vague, and seek third party private information.

 

The court finds further responses warranted as to RFP Nos. 18 (only as to sections that relate to handling of warranties, repairs and customer complaints), 37 (only as to ODBII codes for the subject complaints presented by Plaintiff), and 45 (only as to California, same make, model and defect). No further responses are required as to RFP Nos. 1 – 17, 19 – 36, 38 – 41, 44 and 46.  To the extent that defendant has stated that it would produce certain documents, those must be produced without further delay. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Production of Documents is GRANTED, IN PART.

 

The request for sanctions in the amount of $3,660.00 is DENIED.

 

III.                PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST SET OF REQUEST FOR ADMISSIONS AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Plaintiff Baneggas moves the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiffs’ first set of request for admissions and monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,210.00 against Defendant JLRNA and its attorney of record, Turner Henningsen Wolf & Vandenburg, LPP. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds Defendant has failed and refused to provide code-compliant discovery responses.

A motion to compel further responses be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (CCP § 2033.290(a).)  However, a motion to compel further responses cannot compel the admission of matters already denied.  (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 821.) 

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s responses to RFAs 12, 13, 15, 20-23 and 33-34 are clearly deficient and further responses must be ordered. Defendant objects to the RFAs on the grounds attorney client privilege/work product doctrine, trade secrets, as well as vague, ambiguous overbroad, calls for a legal conclusion and calls for information equally available to Plaintiff or in the possession of a third party.

In opposition, Defendant argues the RFAS are vague, ambiguous and overbroad. It is unclear what Plaintiff is seeking. The court agrees. The RFAs are vague and ambiguous as to time. These RFAs could be interpreted differently and are not clear whether to admit or deny.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Request for Admissions is DENIED.  

 

The request for sanctions in the amount of $3,210.00 is DENIED.
