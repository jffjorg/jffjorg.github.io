ORDER GRANTING IN PART DEFENDANT MILAD KESHAVARZ’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

 

 

I.               INTRODUCTION

This action was brought by Plaintiffs DRW Real Estate, LLC and Roger K. Rosen (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) for an alleged breach of a commercial leasing agreement with a tenant whose primary use was intended to be a chiropractic clinic located at 10801 Foothill Blvd., San Bernardino, CA 91730 (the “Premises”). Defendants allegedly failed to pay rent in April 2020, Plaintiffs served a Notice of Termination on December 3, 2020, and the Defendants vacated the Premises on January 4, 2021.

Plaintiffs’ operative complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Breach of contract against Defendants Cora Lanyon, Jeffrey F. Lanyon, and C Lanyon Chiropractic Professional Corporation dba Core Integrated Health; (2) Common Counts by Plaintiffs against Defendants Cora Lanyon, Jeffery F. Lanyon, and Milad Keshavarz (“Keshavarz”); (3) Fraud against Defendants Cora Lanyon and Jeffrey F. Lanyon; and (4) Negligence against Keshavarz.

On June 17, 2024, this Court found in favor of Keshavarz after holding a non-jury trial and ordered Plaintiffs to incorporate the Court’s ruling into the Final Judgment at the appropriate time.

Keshavarz now seeks his attorney’s fees and costs as the prevailing party.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 22, 2021, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint.

On April 20, 2021, Plaintiffs amended their Complaint by adding Defendant Corlan, LLC, an Arizona Limited Liability Company, a party previously identified as Doe 1. Given the way Plaintiffs pled their Complaint, Corlan was added as a defendant to every cause of action by way of Paragraph 4.b.(1).

On April 18, 2023, Plaintiffs requested and obtained entry of default as to Defendants Cora Lanyon and Jeffrey F. Lanyon.

On May 25, 2023, the previously assigned bench officer sustained Keshavarz’s Demurrer without leave to amend as the first cause of action, overruled it as to the second cause of action, and sustained it with leave to amend as to the fourth cause of action. Plaintiffs never filed an amended complaint.

Sometime between May 25 and June 22, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint. The date is uncertain because it never appeared on the docket until the Court pointed it out at the time of trial against Keshavarz. Apparently, however, Plaintiffs served it on Keshavarz at the least and since Keshavarz filed his Answer timely, there was no prejudice to the Court receiving it at the time of trial against him.

On June 22, 2023, Keshavarz filed his Answer to First Amended Complaint.

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendants Core Health and C. Lanyon Chiropractic Professional Corporation with prejudice. Plaintiffs intend on proceeding with a default prove-up as to the remaining defendants, Cora Lanyon and Jeffrey F. Lanyon, at a later time.

On June 17, 2024, this Court found in favor of Keshavarz after holding a non-jury trial and ordered Plaintiffs to incorporate the Court’s ruling into the Final Judgment at the appropriate time.

On September 9, 2024, Keshavarz filed the instant Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs (“Motion”).

On November 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition.

Keshavarz did not file a reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

“A party is entitled to an award of¿attorney¿fees¿if there is a specific authorization therefor by statute or private agreement.” (Beverly Hills Properties v. Marcolino (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d Supp. 7, 10.)  

 “With respect to attorney fees incurred to litigate a contract claim, the recovery of such fees are governed by¿Section 1717.” (Intelligent Investments Corp. v. Gonzales (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th Supp. 1, 4.) Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) provides in, pertinent part, “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that¿attorney's¿fees¿and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the¿prevailing¿party, then the party who is determined to be the¿party¿prevailing¿on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable¿attorney's¿fees¿in addition to other costs.” “California courts liberally construe the term ‘on a contract’ as used within¿section 1717. As long as the action involves a contract it is ‘on the contract’ within the meaning of¿Section 1717.” (Dell Merk Inc. v. Franzia (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 443, 455, (internal citations omitted).) 

“The court…shall determine who is the party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section, whether or not the suit proceeds to final judgment…the party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract. The court may also determine that there is no party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1717(b).)

Code of Civil Procedure § 1032(b) provides that, “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled … to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” Attorneys’ fees awarded pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1717 are allowable as costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1032 as they are attorneys’ fees authorized by contract. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B); Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(a)(10)(A).) Attorneys’ fees are therefore recoverable as an element of costs.

IV.          ANALYSIS 

A.       Keshavarz’s Fees Are Granted in Part.

1.               Request for Judicial Notice

As a preliminary matter, Plaintiffs request this Court take judicial notice of “the article downloaded by Plaintiffs’ counsel Michael Worthington from the Los Angeles Times web page archives dated 09/05/2014 titled ‘A cure for diabetes?  Don’t look to chiropractors, state says’.” (Plaintiffs’ Request for Judicial Notice.) The Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request, but only as to the existence of the article, not as to its content. Courts may not take judicial notice of the contents of Internet sites and blogs but may as to their existence. (See Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 194.)

2.               Keshavarz is Entitled to an Award of Reasonable Attorney’s Fees and Costs Under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a).

Keshavarz requests a total of $34,582.50 in attorney’s fees and $2,661.31 in costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a). (See Motion, pg. 11.) Keshavarz argues he is entitled to attorney’s fees because the contract on which Plaintiffs based their First Amended Complaint, the commercial lease, contains a prevailing party attorneys’ fees clause and Keshavarz is the prevailing party. (See Motion, pg. 5-7.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs seek to relitigate their claims against Keshavarz. (See Opposition, pg. 14 (“Plaintiffs respectfully[sic] request that the court determine Plaintiffs are the prevailing party on the First Cause of Action for breach of contract, the Second Cause of Action on Common Count for monies due on an open book account, on the Third Cause of Action for Fraud and the Fifth Cause of Action for Exemplary Damages and that Keshavarz has not prevailed on any contract related claim.” (emphasis added).) The Court will not consider Plaintiffs’ attempts to revive their claims against Keshavarz as such arguments are inapplicable to the instant Motion and inappropriate at this stage. Plaintiffs’ attempts to address the merits of the instant Motion are similarly unpersuasive. For example, Plaintiffs argue this Court should determine they are the prevailing parties on the contract for purposes of attorney’s fees and costs. In support of this argument, Plaintiffs quote Scott Co. v. Blount, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1103, 1109, which states “[i]f neither party achieves a complete victory on all the contract claims, it is within the discretion of the trial court to determine which party prevailed on the contract or whether, on balance, neither party prevailed sufficiently to justify an award of attorney fees.” But Plaintiffs’ citation to Scott is unhelpful to their argument. Here, Keshavarz achieved “complete victory” on all Plaintiffs’ claims against him.

The Court thus finds Keshavarz is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs. Though not party to the contract, Keshavarz is entitled to recover his attorney’s fees and costs under the contract because Plaintiffs would have been entitled to recover their fees and costs under the contract had they prevailed at trial. (See Real Property Services Corporation v. The City of Pasedena (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 375, 382.) The Court also finds Keshavarz is the prevailing party because this Court found in favor of him after a non-jury trial.

Accordingly, Keshavarz is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) as the prevailing party in this action.

2.       Mr. Grace and Mr. Denny’s Rates are Reasonable

Keshavarz’s counsel, Mr. Scott Grace (“Mr. Grace”) provides a declaration detailing his requested hours and providing support for the billing rate of he and his paralegal, Mr. Dave Denny (“Mr. Denny”). (See Declaration of Scott Grace filed September 11, 2024 (“Grace Decl.”).) Mr. Grace also details Mr. Denny’s hours. (See Grace Decl., ¶ 12, Ex. B.)

The Court finds Mr. Grace’s billing rate of $525.00/hour reasonable given the prevailing rates in the Greater Los Angeles Area for attorneys with the same level of experience as Mr. Grace, who has been licensed to practice law in California for approximately 17 years, as well as the level of complexity of the case and skill demanded by it. (See Grace Decl., ¶¶ 7-10, 12-14.) The Court also finds Mr. Denny’s billing rate of $125.00/hour reasonable given the prevailing rates in the Greater Los Angeles Area for paralegals with the same level of experience as Mr. Denny, who has worked in the civil defense field for over 14 years. (See Grace Decl., ¶¶ 11-13, 15.)

3.       Not All of Mr. Grace and Mr. Denny’s Hours Are Reasonable.

Mr. Grace’s requested $31,395.00 in fees account for a total of 59.80 hours of legal work. The Court finds most of Mr. Grace’s request appropriate. However, the Court finds some of Mr. Grace’s requested hours to be excessive given his experience. The Court also finds some of Mr. Grace’s hours were more appropriate for his paralegal Mr. Denny to perform and has reduced the applicable billing rate to that of Mr. Denny’s for those tasks. Mr. Grace’s reduced hours and billed time are below.

Mr. Grace, on behalf of Mr. Denny, requested $3,187.50 in fees, accounting for Mr. Denny’s total 25.50 hours of paralegal work. The Court again finds most of Mr. Denny’s request appropriate, however, the Court finds some of Mr. Denny’s billed time lacks the detail necessary to describe what task was performed such that the Court can properly evaluate the reasonableness of said task. Mr. Denny’s reduced hours and billed time are also below.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds it appropriate to reduce Mr. Grace’s requested fees by $1,567.00 for a total of $29,828.00 in fees. The Court also finds it appropriate to reduce Mr. Denny’s hours by 4.8 hours, resulting in 20.7 hours of paralegal work and $2,587.50 in fees.

5.       Keshavarz is Not Entitled to Costs

Keshavarz requests $2,661.31 in costs. Keshavarz is not entitled to costs as he did not file a Memorandum of Costs as required by Rules of Court, rule 3.1700.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART Defendant Milad Keshavarz’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs and awards him $32,415.50 in attorney’s fees. Defendant Milad Keshavarz is not entitled to costs.
