DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV20591    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: T

Motion for Summary Judgment

MOVING PARTY:             Defendant State of California, acting by and through the California Highway Patrol

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       None

The court considered the moving papers. The motion is unopposed.

BACKGROUND

This is an action for negligence arising from a vehicle collision which took place in August 2020. Plaintiff Margarita Beltran (“Plaintiff”) filed her Complaint against Defendants the California Department of Transportation, California Highway Patrol (“CHP”), the Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, the City of Long Beach, the County of Los Angeles, Kenny’s Auto Service, II, and John Doe on June 2, 2021.

On July 11, 2023, CHP filed the instant motion for summary judgment. The motion is unopposed.

LEGAL STANDARD

            A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  A defendant moving for summary judgment carries the initial “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit” by proving that “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p).)  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Ibid.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿

JUDICIAL NOTICE

CHP Request for Judicial of the Government Claim filed by Plaintiff and received by City on February 8, 2021, is GRANTED. See Gong v. City of Rosemead, 226 Cal. App. 4th 363, 368 n.1 (2014) (“The court may take judicial notice of the filing and contents of a government claim, but not the truth of the claim.”)¿

DISCUSSION

            CHP moves for summary judgment on the basis that Plaintiff’s case rests entirely on proving that CHP employs the tow truck driver involved in the subject vehicle collision, and she cannot as a matter of fact or by operation of law under California’s Freeway Service Patrol program (“FSP”).

            As to general employment, CHP argues that CHP has no contract under the FSP for the services of Kenny’s Auto or its individual tow truck driver. (UMF Nos. 25-27.) CHP’s dispatch and Computer Aided Dispatch (“CAD”) logs do not mention the tow truck’s collision with another vehicle, and in fact CHP only learned of the accident after it occurred. (UMF No. 32.) Thus, there is no evidence or basis to conclude that CHP was the tow truck driver’s general employer.

            CHP also relies on State ex rel. Dept. of California Highway Patrol v. Superior Court (2015) 60 Cal.4th 1002, 1007 (“Alvarado”) The court in Alvarado noted that the legislature contemplated generally that local agencies would contract with tow truck employers to provide FSP services. (State ex rel. Dept. of California Highway Patrol v. Superior Court (2015) 60 Cal.4th 1002, 1013.) The court there concluded that the language of the FSP statutory scheme alone does not support a finding that CHP was a special employer of FSP tow truck drivers. (Id. at 1015.) However, the holding did “not eliminate the possibility that CHP might act as a special employer if it takes on responsibilities beyond those outlined in the FSP statutes” because CHP is permitted to “make its own agreements with tow service providers in addition to the primary contracts between providers and local transportation agencies.” (Ibid.)

            CHP points out that Plaintiff does not even allege that there was a special employment relationship between CHP and the tow truck driver. Further, there are no facts or evidence showing that CHP overstepped its role as established by the FSP and thereby created a special employment relationship with Kenny’s Auto and/or its driver.

            As Plaintiff has not alleged a special employment relationship and unless CHP takes on responsibilities beyond those outlined in the FSP statutes, there is no special employment relationship as a matter of law under Alvarado, the Court finds that CHP has met its initial burden.

            The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to create a triable issue of material fact. As Plaintiff has failed to oppose, the motion is GRANTED.  Moving Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.


Case Number: 21STCV37863    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: T

DEFENDANT LYFT, INC.’s MOTION TO COMPEL AN INDEPENDENT NEUROSURGERY EXAMINATION OF PLAINTIFF

MOVING PARTY:            Lyft, Inc.

RESPONDING PARTY:  None.

TENTATIVE RULING:  Granted
The court considered the moving papers. No opposition was filed.
BACKGROUND

On October 14, 2021, Plaintiff Nikeia Toi Mccaa (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Lyft, Inc. (“Defendant”), asserting causes of action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence, arising out of a collision that occurred on October 20, 2019 in Long Beach, California.

On March 28, 2024, Defendant filed this motion for an order compelling a mental independent medical examination (“IME”) of Plaintiff. No opposition has been filed.

 
LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 2032.220(a) provides: 

In any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff, if both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1)¿The examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive.

(2)¿The examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee. 

CCP section 2031.310 states: 

(a)¿If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with¿Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court. 

(b)¿A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under¿Section 2016.040. 

(c)¿Notice of the motion shall be served on the person to be examined and on all parties who have appeared in the action. 

CCP section 2032.320(a) provides: “The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under¿Section 2032.310¿only for good cause shown.” 

¿       “Section 2036 defines a showing of ‘good cause’ as requiring that the party produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Vinson v. Superior. Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)

  The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action.  (CCP §2032.020(a).)  This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation.  (Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337.)  Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.”  (See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837 [wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer].)  Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged.   The good cause requirement checks any potential harassment of the plaintiff.  (See Shapira v. Superior Court
(1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.) 

DISCUSSION 

Defendant moves to compel a neurosurgery examination of Plaintiff within 60 days of this order, or no later than 30 days before trial, whichever is sooner. Defendant argues that good cause exists for conducting the examination because Plaintiff is making a claim for injuries to her bilateral knees, back, neck, head, hips, and emotional stress. Plaintiff has also suffered a stroke after the accident. During her deposition, Plaintiff testified that she still has constant neck and back pain which she described as burning and radiating. Sometimes her neck pain keeps her up at night. Plaintiff claims that her pain is getting worse.  

Based on the foregoing and in absence of an opposition contesting any of the facts set forth in motion, the Court finds good cause to grant the motion. 

The Court does note that Defendant’s motion does not contain the time of the proposed examination as required by Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310(b).  However, given that the requested relief is only that the examination be ordered during a specific time frame, the Court still finds good cause to grant the motion. 

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.  The examination is to take place within the next 30 days at the office of Dr. Cary Alberstone at 1700 N. Rose Avenue, Suite 250, Oxnard CA 93030.  The parties are ordered to meet and confer with respect to the specific date and time.  The parties may stipulate in writing to the examination being conducted at a different location.  Defendant is encouraged but not required to make accommodations for any limitations Plaintiff may have regarding travel.  

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 21STCV45071    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: T

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant Anthony Pesce

RESPONDING PARTY:       None.

TENTATIVE RULING:        Greant

The court considered the moving papers. No opposition papers have been received.

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiff Eddie Hernandez, a minor by and through his guardian ad litem Alyssa Lyn Welch, (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action on December 10, 2021 against Defendants Anthony Pesce (“Pesce”), Livia Tuberosi, Victor Tuberosi (jointly, the “Tuberosis”), the Tuberosi Family Child Care (“TFCC”) (collectively, “Defendants”), and Does 1 to 100, alleging the following causes of action: (1) general negligence against Defendants; (2) premise liability against Defendants; and (3) battery against Does 51-74 only.  As alleged in the complaint, Plaintiff was injured on January 14, 2021 by another child while at child care when he was struck in the eye with a beaded necklace. It is further alleged that the Defendants failed to use reasonable care to prevent the harm that occurred and did not comply with Health and Safety Code §§ 1597.30-1597.622. Additionally, Defendants are alleged to have negligently owned and operated the subject premises by failing to properly survived the children that attended the day care.

            On August 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed Amendments to Complaint and added Frankie Vuoso, Jimmy Vuoso, and Nubia Dilorio as named defendants.

            On February 22, 2024, Pesce filed the instant motion for summary judgment. No opposition has been received by the court.

LEGAL STANDARD

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

A defendant moving for summary judgment carries the initial “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit” by proving that “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p).)  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Ibid.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ 

DISCUSSION

            Pesce moves for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s complaint on the ground Plaintiff is unable to present evidence to support the elements of duty, breach and causation in order to maintain his claims for negligence and premises liability against Pesce. (See Notice of Motion at pg. 2.)

A.    Undisputed Material Facts

On January 14, 2021, Plaintiff was three years old and attending the daycare, Tuberosi Family Care Center. (UMF No. 6; Meininger Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. C, Plaintiff’s Response to Form Interrogatories No. 2.2; Meininger Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. D, Plaintiff’s Response to Special Interrogatories, No. 4.) At the time of the incident, Defendant Frankie Vuoso (“Frankie”) had struck Plaintiff in the eye with a beaded necklace. (UMF No. 7, Meininger Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. D, Plaintiff’s Response to Special Interrogatories Nos. 8, 12; Meininger Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. E, Defendant TFCC’s Response to Form Interrogatories, Nos. 12.1, 12.6.)

No one had witnessed the incident, and Defendant Livia Tuberosi only became aware of Plaintiff’s injury when he approached her in discomfort. (UMF Nos. 8-9; Meininger Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. D, Plaintiff’s Response to Special Interrogatories Nos. 8, 12; Meininger Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. E TFCC’s Response to Form Interrogatories, Nos. 12.1, 12.6; Meininger Decl., ¶ 7, Exhibit F, the Tuberosis’ response to Form Interrogatories, Nos. 12.1, 12.6 Meininger Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. G, the Tuberosis’ response to Special Interrogatories, Nos. 4-7.)

Frankie’s parents, Defendants James Vuoso and Nubia Dilorio, had dressed Frankie with the subject necklace, but Defendant Livia Tuberosi did not see the subject necklace on Frankie when he arrived at the daycare. (UMF No. 10-12; Meininger Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. D, Plaintiff’s Response to Special Interrogatories, No. 8; Meininger Decl., ¶ 6, Exhibit E, TFCC’s response to Form Interrogatories, No.16.1; Meininger Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. G, the Tuberosis’ Response to Special Interrogatories, Nos. 1-2.)

The subject daycare is located at 836 11th Street in San Pedro, California, and it shares the same lot as a residence located at 838 11th Street. (UMF No. 13; Meininger Decl. ¶ 9, Exh. H, Pesce Depo, at pp. 5:22-7:11, 42:13-14.) Both residences are detached from one another and separated by a gate. (UMF No. 14, Meininger Decl. ¶ 9, Exh. H, Pesce Depo, at pp. 5:22-7:11.) Pesce has owned these properties since 1990. (UMF No. 15; Pesce Decl. ¶2; Meininger Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. G, Pesce Depo at pp. 6:1-7:11.) Beginning in 2019, Pesce entered into an oral agreement with his nephew, Defendant Victor Tuberosi wherein his nephew was permitted to use the 836 11th Street property without rent in exchange for taking care of Pesce in his old age. (UMF Nos. 16-17; Pesce Decl. ¶ 3; Meininger Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. G, Pesce Depo at pp. 7:12-25, 8:9-11, 12:23-13:3.)

TFCC is owned and operated by Defendant Livia Tuberosi, and she is responsible for maintaining the subject daycare. (UMF Nos. 18-19; Meininger Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. E, TFCC’s Response to Form Interrogatories No. 3.6; Meininger Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. G, the Tuberosis’ Response to Special Interrogatories Nos. 4-7; Pesce Decl. ¶¶ 3-8.) Pesce is currently disabled and spends most of his time in his home. (UMF No. 20; Meininger Decl., ¶ 9, Exhibit H, Pesce Depo., at pp.  10:10- 11:21; 13:6-14:12.)

Pesce is not involved with the operations of the subject daycare and lacks knowledge of its affairs or who attends. (UMF Nos. 21-24; Pesce Depo at pp. 12:10-14, 24:16-25:1, 24:16- 25:4; 26:12-28:1, 28:21-29:20, 29:25-30:5, 35:16-24, 36:2-4; Pesce Decl. ¶¶ 3-10.) Pesce knew of Defendants Jimmy Vuoso and Nubia Diloria, but had not spoken to then in 10 years. (UMF No. 27; Meininger Decl., ¶ 9, Exhibit H, Pesce Depo., at pp. 24:2-14, 30:1-23, 33:2-34:10.) Prior to the incident, Pesce did not know of Frankie or that he had any alleged behavior issues, assaultive propensities, or prior altercations. (UMF Nos. 27, 29; Pesce Decl. ¶¶8-12.) Additionally, he had never received complaints regarding Frankie or observe Frankie engage in any altercations. (UMF Nos. 30-31; Pesce Decl. ¶¶ 8-12.) Pesce also did not supply Frankie with the necklace, and he only learned of the incident from his nephew. (UMF Nos. 32-33; Pesce Decl. ¶¶ 13-14; Meininger Decl., ¶ 9, Exhibit H, Pesce Depo., 25:9-25.)

B.     Lack of Duty

Pesce moves for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s claims for negligence and premises liability on the ground that there are no triable issues of material fact establishing he had duty to protect Plaintiff from the harm that occurred.

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.  (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal. App. 4th 994, 998.)  “Whether a duty exists is a question of law to be resolved by the court.” (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal. 5th 204, 213; see Regents v. University of California v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 608, 620 [“The determination whether a particular relationship supports a duty of care rests on policy and is a question of law.”].)  “If there is no duty, there can be no liability, no matter how easily one may have been able to prevent injury to another.” (Margaret W. v. Kelley R. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.)

“Under some circumstances, a defendant may have an affirmative duty to protect the plaintiff from harm at the hands of a third party, even though the risk of harm is not of the defendant's own making.” (Brown, supra, 11 Cal. 5th at 215.) “In particular, a person may have an affirmative duty to protect the victim of another's harm if that person is in what the law calls a ‘special relationship’ with either the victim or the person who created the harm.” (Id. at 215.)

“In Brown the Supreme Court established a two-step inquiry to determine whether a defendant has a legal duty to take action to protect a plaintiff from injuries caused by a third party: ‘First, the court must determine whether there exists a special relationship between the parties or some other set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty to protect. Second, if so, the court must consult the factors described in [Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 70 Cal.Rptr. 97, 443 P.2d 561 (Rowland)] to determine whether relevant policy considerations counsel limiting that duty’.” (Doe v. Roman Cath. Archbishop of Los Angeles, 70 Cal. App. 5th 657, 670 [citing Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 209 and Regents, supra, 4 Cal.5th at p. 627 (special relationship doctrine is an exception to the general rule that there is no duty to protect others from the conduct of third parties)].)

Here, Pesce first argues that there is no special relationship between him and the plaintiff because he had no custody or control over the subject daycare where the incident occurred.  (Motion at pg. 13; UMF Nos. 14-19, 21-23.) Pesce concedes that he was the owner of the property where the subject daycare is located. (UMF No. 15.) Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property in order to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.  (Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, 1156.) Nevertheless, based on the evidence presented by Pesce, the inference has been created that Pesce has relinquished control of the residence where the subject daycare operated from, which precludes the finding that he owed a duty to a childcare attendee. (Nally v. Grace Community Church (1988) 47 Cal.3d 278, 293 [determining that there is no duty “in the absence of a special relationship of custody or control.”].)  For instance, Pesce is disabled, spending most of his time in his private residence. (UMF No. 20.) Also, he has permitted his nephew to use the residence located at 836 11th Street since 2019. (UMF No. 16.) Lastly, Pesce is not involved with the affairs of the subject daycare, which is controlled by Defendant Livia Tuberosi. (UMF No. 18-19, 21-24.) Based on this evidence, the court finds that there are no trial issues of material fact to suggest there is a special relationship between Pesce and Plaintiff.

Second, Pesce argues that, even if Plaintiff could establish a special relationship, he would still not owe a duty to Plaintiff because Pesce lacked actual knowledge of Frankie’s alleged dangerous or violent propensities to show that the incident was foreseeable. (Motion at pg. 14.) In this regard, Pesce has presented evidence to show that he does not know Plaintiff or Frankie, and he had never observed Frankie act aggressively towards others. (UMF Nos. 24, 26-27.) Furthermore, he asserts that he had never heard a complaint regarding Frankie’s behavior. (UMF No. 30.)

Upon review of the evidence, the court finds that Pesce has met his burden on this basis as well to undermine the element of duty. In addition to establishing that a special relationship exists, facts must be presented to show the defendant had actual knowledge of the third-party’s propensity to cause harm. (Doe, supra, 37 Cal.App.5th at 682-683.) “Foreseeability, when analyzed to determine the existence or scope of a duty, is a question of law to be decided by the court.” (Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal.4th 666, 678.) In this instance, the evidence presented by Pesce creates the inference that he lacked actual knowledge of Frankie’s assaultive propensities.

Because Pesce has met his burden in establishing that there are no triable issues of material facts regarding whether there is a special relationship between the parties or whether Pesce had actual knowledge of Frankie’s propensity to cause harm, the burden now shifts to Plaintiff. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) However, because Plaintiff has failed to file an opposition, he is unable to meet this burden.

Accordingly, because Plaintiff is unable to establish the element of duty, Pesce’s motion for summary judgment is granted on this ground. In the interest of judicial economy, the court declines to address the remaining arguments presented in the instant motion.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendant Anthony Pesce’s motion for summary judgment.

Defendant Anthony Pesce is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
