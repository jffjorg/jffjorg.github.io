DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

REQUESTING ORAL ARGUMENT PER CRC 3.1308

The Court will attempt to post all Tentative Rulings at least the day prior to the hearing by 3:00 p.m.; however, the Court does not post Tentative Rulings for all matters.  

The Court will indicate in the Tentative Ruling whether the Court is requesting oral argument.  For cases where the Court is not requesting argument, then the Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) where the Court requests notice of intent to appear.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, a party seeking argument should notify "all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue."  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is requested.   

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Notice of the ruling must be served as indicated in the tentative.  Remote appearances are permitted for all law and motion unless otherwise indicated by the Court.  

 

Case Number: 22BBCV00813    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: A

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH CENTRAL DISTRICT - BURBANK

DEPARTMENT A

 

TENTATIVE RULING

AUGUST 2, 2024

MOTION TO BIFURCATE TRIAL

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22BBCV00813

 

MP:  

Kelly Walsh Sweeney (Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant)

RP:  

Richard M. Foster dba Law Offices of Richard M. Foster (Defendant and Cross-Complainant)

NOTICE:

The Court is not requesting oral argument on this matter.  The Court is guided by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1308(a)(1) whereby notice of intent to appear is requested.  Unless the Court directs argument in the Tentative Ruling, no argument is requested and any party seeking argument should notify all other parties and the court by 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing of the party’s intention to appear and argue.  The tentative ruling will become the ruling of the court if no argument is received.  

Notice may be given either by email at BurDeptA@LACourt.org or by telephone at (818) 260-8412.

Background

Plaintiff brings a motion to bifurcate trial and Defense filed an opposition.

Plaintiff filed suit against Defendant asserting that in 1999, Robert Walsh retained Defendant for attorney services in the matter of Robert Walsh v. Galen Walker. (Compl. ¶2).  On April 12, 2022 Robert Walsh obtained a judgment against Walker for $1,950,000 plus costs. (Compl. ¶3).  Defendant made no effort to recover the judgment. (Compl. ¶5).  Two years after the judgment, Kelly and Robert Walsh entered into a dissolution of marriage.  The settlement resulted in Robert Walsh assigning one-half of the judgment to Kelly Walsh.

In 2012, Kelly Walsh was advised by Defendant that the judgment had been renewed on September 23, 2011. (Compl. ¶12).  However, Ms. Walsh subsequently learned that the judgment renewal had not been perfected.  Ms. Walsh then sought to perfect the judgment on April 8, 2021. (Compl. ¶15).  Plaintiff received $450,000 to satisfy the judgment of which 50% was given to the heirs of Robert Walsh.

The heirs of Robert Walsh filed a petition in the Probate Division of the Los Angeles Superior Court (19STPB00299), in which the probate judge determined that Richard Foster’s lien was barred under CCP §366.2 (action to enforce liability of a decedent must be brought within one-year). (Compl. ¶24)

Plaintiff filed suit to determine the respective rights of the parties and whether the lien of Richard Foster remains enforceable against Kelly Walsh’s settlement proceeds or in the alternative to make a determination of the reasonable value of Defendant’s services.

I.                    NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS

Plaintiff asserts that the matter should be bifurcated such that the Court determines the validity of a lien asserted by Defendant upon a judgment. 

II.                 LEGAL STANDARD

The Court may bifurcate a case, “when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby.” Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 598.  Similarly, “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues.” Cal. Code Civ. Proc.§ 1048(b).  Nonetheless, the Court has the inherent authority to regulate the order of trial and can entertain a motion to bifurcate at any time, even during trial.  (McLellan v. McLellan (1972) 23 Cal. App 3d 343, 353).

 

III.               MERITS

Defense argues that Plaintiff’s request is time barred because it was not brought 30 days before trial citing CCP §598.  The Court finds this argument without merit.  Initially, the matter was to be discussed at the August 29, 2024 court hearing which defense counsel failed to appear.  As of July 30th, defense has filed no declaration to explain the missed court date.  The trial is scheduled for September 3, 2024, which is more than 30 days from today’s hearing.

In this instance, the threshold issue for the case is whether the lien exists.  Without a determination of this issue, the second inquiry as to the amount due for legal services is irrelevant.  Any trial on the second issue prior to the initial determination would not efficiently handle the litigation, particularly with a jury.

 

IV.               CONCLUSION

The Court exercises its inherent discretion over matters of court resources and judicial efficiency, GRANTS Sweeney’s motion to bifurcate.

In addition, the Defendant is liable for interest at the contract default rate of 18% per annum thereafter and reasonable attorney’s fees.

In preparation for selling the Truck, Plaintiff incurred reconditioning fees in the amount of $4,696.54. At the time of the sale, the Truck's wholesale value was $115,500.00.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    LEGAL STANDARDS

 

As to each claim framed by his complaint, a plaintiff moving for summary adjudication satisfies his burden by submitting proof showing a prima facie case for each element of a cause of action. (C.C.P. § 437c(p)(1).) Once a moving plaintiff has satisfied this burden, a defendant opposing the motion has the responsive burden of showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Id.)

 

Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary [adjudication] and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) However, if all the evidence submitted and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, then the moving party is entitled to summary adjudication as a matter of law. (C.C.P. § 437c(c); Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

Failure to file an opposition to the motion indicates the other parties' acquiescence that the motion is meritorious. CRC Rule 8.54(c).

 

II.                 MERITS

 

Pursuant to CACI 303, to recover damages for breach of contract, Plaintiff must prove the following:

 

1.      That Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a contract;

 

2.      That Defendant failed to do something that the contract required it to do;

 

3.      That Plaintiffs was harmed; and

 

4.      That Defendant’s breach of contract was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm.

 

Since the motion was unopposed, the Court need not extensively set forth an analysis of the motion.  The evidence presented to the Court was the existence of an agreement between the parties. (Elli Decl. ¶4 and Exh 1).  Plaintiff met its obligations pursuant to the Agreement by providing financing for the Truck. (Elli Decl. ¶ 6).  Defendant breached the Agreement and Plaintiff, through its counsel sent a default letter to Defendant.  (Khatchadourian Decl. ¶2 and Exh. A).  Defendant failed to make payments or cure the default which resulted in damages to Plaintiff. (Elli Decl. ¶¶7-9, 17 and Exh. 7).

 

The defense has filed no response refuting the Motion for Summary Adjudication.   As noted above, the failure to file an opposition to the motion indicates the other parties' acquiescence that the motion is meritorious. CRC Rule 8.54(c).

 

III.              CONCLUSION

 

The Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the first cause of action is GRANTED.
