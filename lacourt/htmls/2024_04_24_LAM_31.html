DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling by emailing Dept. 31 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. The email address is smcdept31@lacourt.org. Please do not call the court to submit on the tentative. Please do not submit to the tentative ruling on behalf of the opposing party. Please do not e-mail the Court if you plan to appear and argue.

In deciding whether to submit on the tentative ruling or attend the hearing and present oral argument, please keep the following in mind:

The tentative rulings authored by this court reflect that the court has read and considered all pleadings and evidence timely submitted to the court in connection with the motion, opposition, and reply (if any). Because the pleadings were filed, they are part of the public record.

Oral argument is not an opportunity to simply regurgitate that which a party set forth in its pleadings. Nor, is oral argument an opportunity to "make a record" when there is no court reporter present and the statements and arguments of counsel are already part of the record because they were set forth in the pleadings. Finally, simply because a party or attorney disagrees with the court's analysis and ruling or is not satisfied with it does not necessarily warrant oral argument when no new arguments will be articulated.

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify all other parties email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. If all parties to the motion submit, this tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date and it will be memorialized in a minute order. This tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such document will be considered by the Court.

**Tentative rulings on Motions for Summary Judgment will only be available for review in the courtroom on the day of the hearing.


Case Number: 20STCV44367    Hearing Date: April 24, 2024    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:     April 24, 2024                                    TRIAL DATE:  Vacated

                                                          

CASE:                                State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company v. Ahmad Martin, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV44367

 

 

MOTION TO VACATE THE DISMISSAL AND MOTION TO ENFORCE A SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT AND ENTER JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     No opposition

 

 

            On November 19, 2020, Plaintiff, State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company, filed a Complaint against Defendants, Ahmad Martin (“Martin”) and John Ruffin (“Ruffin”) for subrogation recovery in the sum of $26,155.80.

 

            On April 5, 2021, Ruffin was dismissed from this action.

 

            On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed the parties’ Stipulation for Settlement and Entry of Judgment (“Stipulation”).  On the same day, the court dismissed the case without prejudice and retained jurisdiction to enforce the terms of the settlement and enter judgment in the event of default.

 

            On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Vacate the Dismissal and Motion to Enforce a Settlement Agreement and Enter Judgment against Martin.  Plaintiff sought entry of judgment in the amount of $16,220.01, consisting of $26,155.80 in damages, $2,720,43 in prejudgment interest, $1,174.67 in attorney fees, $593.22 in costs, and less $14,424.02 paid by Martin’s insurance carrier.

 

            The motion was heard on April 10, 2024.  The court issued an order finding that Plaintiff substantiated the damages sought, properly calculated the interest, and supported the costs requested with a memorandum of costs.  However, the court found that the Stipulation required each party to bear their own attorney fees.  The court was therefore inclined to enter judgment in the sum of $15,045.34, which was the judgment requested less attorney fees of $1,174.67.  The court continued the motion to allow Plaintiff to file an amended proposed judgment.

 

            On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended proposed order and an amended proposed judgment requesting a total judgment of $15,045.34 against Martin.

 

The court finds that the amended proposed judgment is consistent with the court’s previous order.  Accordingly, the unopposed Motion to Vacate the Dismissal and Motion to Enforce a Settlement Agreement and Enter Judgment is GRANTED.  Judgment is entered in favor of Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company and against Defendant Ahmad Martin in the total sum of $15,045.34.

CASE:                         Christine Paulsen v. Pixel Labs, LLC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV22371

 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION AND TO STAY PROCEEDINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Pixel Labs, LLC and Assembly Parent Corp.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Christine Paulsen

 

 

I.          BACKGROUND

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff Christine Paulsen (“Paulsen” or “Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants, Pixel Labs, LLC (“Pixel”) and Assembly Parent Corp. (“APC”).  On February 16, 2024, Paulsen filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC), alleging causes of action for:

1.      Sexual Harassment under FEHA

2.      Sex Discrimination under FEHA

3.      Retaliation under FEHA

4.      Retaliation under Labor Code section 1102.5

5.      Failure to Prevent Harassment, Discrimination, or Retaliation under FEHA

6.      Age Discrimination under FEHA

7.      Unpaid Wages

8.      Waiting Time Penalties under Labor Code section 203

9.      Intersectional Discrimination under FEHA.

Paulsen makes the following allegations:  In 2020, Paulsen was hired by Defendants as the Vice President of Marketing.  Adam Shahbaz (“Shahbaz”) was hired around the same time as Paulsen as the Vice President of Strategy.  Shabaz and the events teams were responsible for planning a company event called the Sell + Scale Summit (the “S3” event).  As stated in her opposition to the motion to compel, Plaintiff alleges that “[m]ost of the harassing conduct alleged in the FAC took place during [the S3] multi-day work event in Las Vegas, Nevada. Plaintiff was required to attend this event by virtue of her position. Plaintiff alleges that misogyny and sex/gender discrimination were rampant at this event, which created a hostile work environment for herself and other women in attendance.”  (Opp. at p. 2.)   The event was permeated with a hypermasculine atmosphere.  Male leadership drank alcohol excessively throughout the event.  Paulsen was the only woman present who was in a leadership position.  Shortly after the event concluded, Paulsen met with Chief Operating Officer Rahul Choraria (“Choraria”), Paulsen’s boss, to complain about the hostile work environment that she experienced and witnessed against other women at S3.  Paulsen requested a sabbatical.  Choraria apologized and approved Paulsen’s request.  Paulsen took her sabbatical.  Shortly after returning from her sabbatical, Choraria told Paulsen that her employment was being terminated as part of a group layoff.  Choraria confirmed that Paulsen’s termination was not due to her personal job performance.  Paulsen and one other female executive were selected for termination.  Both were over the age of 40.

            On February 22, 2024, Defendants filed this Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings.

            Paulsen filed an Opposition.  Defendants replied.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72.) Under both the FAA and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, the Court can compel parties to an arbitration agreement to arbitrate their dispute.

 

The party petitioning to compel arbitration under written arbitration agreement bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and party opposing petition must meet the same evidentiary burden to prove any facts necessary to its defense.  The trial court acts as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc. (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

           

            There is no dispute Plaintiff signed a valid arbitration agreement with Pixel and that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) applies.  It is further undisputed that APC may enforce the agreement as a third-party beneficiary.  The issues to be decided are two-fold: whether the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual harassment Act (the “Act”) prohibits arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims and whether Defendants waived their right to compel arbitration.   The court addresses each below.

1.      The Act

“In March 2022, President Joseph R. Biden signed the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 (the Act) (9 U.S.C. §§ 401, 402), representing the first major amendment of the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) (9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.) since its inception nearly 100 years ago.”  (Murrey v. Superior Court (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 1223, 1230.)  “Section 402, subdivision (a), of the [EFAA] describes its applicability, stating that effective immediately, predispute arbitration agreements and joint-action waivers in the context of sexual assault or harassment were no longer valid or enforceable.”  (Id. at p. 1234.)  “[A]t the election of the person alleging conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute or sexual assault dispute, or the named representative of a class or in a collective action alleging such conduct, no predispute arbitration agreement or predispute joint-action waiver shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.  (9 U.S.C. § 402, subd. (a).)”  (Id. at pp. 1234-35.)

2.      Sexual Harassment Allegations

Leadership Retreat

Plaintiff claims that at a leadership retreat, a peer made a comment to Plaintiff that she was “‘hot’ for her age.”  (FAC, ¶ 14.)

S3 Event – Four-day conference in Las Vegas

Paulsen was the only woman in leadership at the S3 event.  She alleges the S3 event “was permeated with a hypermasculine atmosphere” and that “[m]ale leadership drank alcohol excessively”.  (FAC, ¶ 21.)

Paulsen claims that “men” at the S3 event “wrapped their arms around Plaintiff and hung on Plaintiff’s body while drunk in a manner that made Plaintiff uncomfortable and was unwelcome.”  (FAC, ¶ 22.)  Other men were not touched in the same way.  (Id.)

At a dinner, Shahbaz asked Paulsen, the only woman present, to evaluate a co-worker’s biceps; no other men were asked to do the same.  (FAC, ¶ 23.) 

During the event Choraria repeatedly asked Paulsen where her male counterparts were and showed no interest in having conversations with Paulsen about anything else. (FAC, ¶ 24.)

Choraria asked Paulsen multiple times on the Monday of the S3 event to contact a male colleagues to come drink around 4-5 pm.  (FAC, ¶ 25.)  

Paulsen went out drinking with her supervisor at the second night of S3, and Plaintiff claims Choraria laughed at her for ordering a “girly” alcoholic beverage in front of her male counterparts.  (FAC, ¶ 26.)

Shahbaz got very drunk and engaged in inappropriate and sexually suggestive conduct with Anne Ferris, an S3event speaker, in front of Paulsen and junior employees, many of whom were women.  (FAC, ¶ 27.)  Shahbaz later commented on how “hot” and “sexy” the event speaker was.  (Id.)

Shahbaz did not attend a session at the event because he was hungover, with Choraria quipping to Paulsen that he did not know if Shabaz would ever wake up because Shahbaz had been so drunk the prior night.  (FAC, ¶ 28.)  

Several men inquired whether Shahbaz went “home with the Walmart girl” referring to a Walmart employee present at the event, and an unnamed host at one of the events said she was told by an unidentified person that Shahbaz was “grinding on the Walmart girl.”  (FAC, ¶ 29.)

A male executive bragged that Shahbaz was “hitting on” his girlfriend, and the Shahbaz congratulated that executive for having a young girlfriend because Shahbaz’s fiancée was “old AF.”  (FAC, ¶ 30.)

The overall atmosphere of the SS3 event was of a misogynistic boys’ club where women were treated as second-class citizens.  (FAC, ¶ 31.)

Chief Executive Officer Even Sandeep Kella required Paulsen to perform secretarial work for him during the event which should have been entirely under Shahbaz’s purview.  (FAC, ¶ 31.)  

After the S3 event, Paulsen complained of the hostile work environment to Choraria and requested sabbatical leave.  (FAC, ¶¶ 33, 34.)

3.      Application to Paulsen’s Claims

Paulsen’s sexual harassment dispute arose after the passage of the Act.  The central issues to be decided are (1) whether Paulsen alleges “severe or pervasive” sexual harassment within the meaning of FEHA and (2) if the Act applies, whether all of Plaintiff’s causes of action are subject to the Act.

A.  Severe or Pervasive Conduct

Defendants argue the Act does not apply because Paulsen fails to establish severe or pervasive conduct sufficient to constitute a viable sexual harassment cause of action.

Paulsen argues otherwise.  Citing Government Code section 12923, Paulsen argues that a single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment if the harassing conduct has unreasonably interfered with her work performance or created an intimidating, hostile or offensive work environment.

            To state a hostile work environment sexual harassment claim, a plaintiff “must demonstrate that the conduct complained of was severe enough or sufficiently pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive to employees because of their sex.”  (McCoy v. Pac. Mar. Assn. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 283, 293.)  “The words ‘severe’ and ‘pervasive’ have no peculiar meanings under the law.  The adjective “severe” is defined as “strongly critical and condemnatory” or “inflicting pain or distress.”  The verb ‘pervade” is defined as “to become diffused throughout every part of.’” (Caldera v. Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation¿(2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 31, 38 [internal citations omitted].)  The totality of the circumstances are considered when determining whether conduct is severe or pervasive such as “[¶] (a) The nature of the conduct; [¶] (b) How often, and over what period of time, the conduct occurred; [¶] (c) The circumstances under which the conduct occurred; [¶] (d) Whether the conduct was physically threatening or humiliating; [¶] (e) The extent to which the conduct unreasonably interfered with an employee’s work performance.’ (CACI No. 2524.)”  (Caldera, 25 Cal.App.5th at pp. 38-39.)  “A single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment if the harassing conduct has unreasonably interfered with the plaintiff's work performance or created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment.”  (Beltran v. Hard Rock Hotel Licensing, Inc. (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 865, 878, citing Gov. Code, § 12923, subd. (b); CACI No. 2524.)

            “The FEHA imposes two standards of employer liability for sexual harassment, depending on whether the person engaging in the harassment is the victim’s supervisor or a nonsupervisory coemployee. The employer is liable for harassment by a nonsupervisory employee only if the employer (a) knew or should have known of the harassing conduct and (b) failed to take immediate and appropriate corrective action. This is a negligence standard. Because the FEHA imposes this negligence standard only for harassment ‘by an employee other than an agent or supervisor’ by implication the FEHA makes the employer strictly liable for harassment by a supervisor.”  (State Dept. of Health Services v. Superior Court (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1026, 1040-1041.) 

            Here, Paulsen sufficiently pleads severe or pervasive conduct. Considering the totality of the circumstances, including the conduct of the supervisory and non-supervisory employees, Paulsen alleges work environment harassment that was directed at her, in part, because of her sex.  Paulsen was required to attend the leadership retreat and S3 events.  These are workplace environments.  Choraria (Chief Operating Officer) or Kella (Chief Executive Officer) contributed to the hostile work environment by, in part, tasking Paulsen to contact her male peers to set up times to drink, laughing at Paulsen’s “girly” alcoholic beverages, and directing Paulsen to carry out secretarial work which was Shahbaz’s responsibility.  Plaintiff alleges she was tasked with these assignments because of her gender.  In addition, Defendants knew or should have known about the harassing conduct of the other employees.   For example, men at the S3 event “wrapped their arms around Plaintiff and hung on Plaintiff’s body while drunk in a manner that made Plaintiff uncomfortable and was unwelcome.” (FAC, ¶ 22.) [1]  Paulsen alleged a plethora of sexually harassing conduct by Shahbaz occurring over the course of a multi-day event.  Even as Plaintiff’s peer, Defendants may be held liable for Shahbaz’s alleged conduct, or the conduct of any of its non-supervisory employees, if they knew or should have known of the conduct.  And, as alleged, they did or should have known.

As a result of the alleged conduct at the S3 event, Paulsen suffered a psoriasis flare up and a panic attack shortly thereafter.  Paulsen requested sabbatical leave because of anxiety and emotional distress stemming from the hostile work environment.  The alleged conduct altered the conditions of Paulsen’s employment.  Paulsen ended up having to take a sabbatical because of the hostile work environment. 

Under Beltran, supra, Paulsen alleged conduct sufficient to state a claim for work environment sexual harassment.[2]  Having found the Act applies to bar arbitration of Paulsen’s sexual harassment claims, the court does not address Paulsen’s argument regarding waiver.

            B.  Whether Paulsen’s Non-Sexual Harassment Claims Must Be Compelled to Arbitration

            The last issue the court must decide is whether Paulsen’s non-sexual harassment claims may be compelled to arbitration notwithstanding the applicability of the Act.  The parties fail to cite to any California authority on point.  In the absence of California authority, the parties advance two competing ideas based on nonbinding, federal authorities.

            Defendants cite Mera v. SA Hospitality Group, LLC (S.D.N.Y. 2023) 675 F.Supp.3d 442 and Dixon v. Dollar Tree Stores, Inc. (W.D.N.Y. Mar. 7, 2023) No. 22-CV-131S, 2023 WL 2388504 in support of their argument that claims which do not relate in any way to the sexual harassment dispute must be arbitrated. 

            Plaintiff, on the other hand, cites Turner v. Tesla, Inc. (N.D. Cal. Aug. 11, 2023) No. 23-CV-024510 WHO, 2023 WL 6150805 and Johnson v. Everyrealm, Inc. (S.D.N.Y. 2023) 657 F.Supp.3d 535 for the proposition that when a plaintiff alleges sexual harassment alongside other claims, the entire case is exempt from arbitration. 

            Plaintiff has the better argument.  Turner is particularly instructive.  Turner brought sexual harassment and non-sexual harassment claims.  Tesla argued Turner’s non-sexual harassment claims should be severed and compelled to arbitration.  The court rejected Tesla’s argument, concluding that the “EFAA permits [the court] to find that the arbitration is unenforceable with respect to Turner’s entire case because the core of her case alleges “conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute” as defined by the EFAA.”  (Turner, 2023 WL 6150805, at *5.)  

The Turner Court also rejected Tesla’s reliance on Mera.  In Mera, the district court for the Southern District of New York compelled Mera’s wage and hour claims to arbitration because they were brought on behalf of all non-exempt employees.  Unlike Mera, Turner brought individual wage and hour claims only.  Thus, the Turner court found that Turner’s workplace injury and wage claims “relate[d] only to her own experience and employment at Tesla—and are intertwined with her sexual harassment claims.” (Turner, at *5.)

Like Turner, Paulsen brought a sexual harassment claim and other, non-harassment employment claims on her behalf alone.  Indeed, at least four of Paulsen’s causes of action (1st, 2nd, 5th, and 9th) are predicated on sexual harassment.  Further, all of Paulsen’s claims are related to her own experience and employment with Defendants, and are likewise intertwined with her sexual harassment claims. 

Defendants’ reliance on Dixon, supra, is misplaced.  In Dixon, the court found that the Act did not apply to alleged conduct which occurred between 2017 to 2020.  (Dixon, 2023 WL 2388504, at *7.)  The court so concluded because the Act, which was passed in 2022, applied (and applies) prospectively; not retroactively.  Dixon is readily distinguishable.

Having concluded that the Act applies and that Paulsen’s sexual harassment claims are intertwined with her non-sexual harassment claims, the court further finds that Paulsen’s non-sexual harassment claims cannot be compelled to arbitration.

V.        CONCLUSION

 

            Accordingly, the motion to compel arbitration is DENIED.
