ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On April 20, 2023, Plaintiff Ofelia Capristo (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendants Gerardo Ayala Lopez (“Lopez”) and Cecilia Resendez (“Resendez”) (“Defendants”) and Does 1-20, alleging a Motor Vehicle cause of action. (Compl., pp. 1, 4.) The underlying facts of this dispute involve Plaintiff sustaining injuries and incurring damages from a motor vehicle collision between the parties at or near West 11th Street and South Grand View Street. (Compl., p. 4, ¶ MV-1.)

 

On September 14, 2023, default was entered against Defendant Lopez. On January 24, 2024, the parties stipulated to set aside the default.

 

On January 29, 2024, Defendants filed an Answer.

 

On August 27, 2024, the Court granted the parties’ stipulation to continue trial from October 17, 2024 to February 19, 2025. (8/27/24 Order.)

 

On October 17, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition with Document Production (the “Motion”) and Request for Sanctions.

 

No Opposition was filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, section (a) provides:  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  

            A motion under Section 2025.450, subdivision (a), must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production of the requested documents in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).) Good cause is construed liberally and has been found where documents are necessary for trial preparation. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 587.) The motion must also “be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce documents…by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)

III.              Discussion

 

Defendants seek to compel the deposition of Plaintiff to determine the nature, extent, and causation of Plaintiff’s claimed damages. (Motion, p. 5.)  Defendants seek to compel document production to prepare for and conduct the deposition, verify whether Plaintiff had a valid driver’s license to operate a motor vehicle, and verify whether Plaintiff has needed and/or received additional medical treatment since Plaintiff last provided her medical records to Defendants. (Motion, p. 7.)

 

On January 22, 2024, Defendants’ counsel served Plaintiff’s counsel with a Notice Taking the Virtual Deposition and Demand for Production of Documents for May 30, 2024 (the “Notice of Deposition”) via email. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.) Defendants’ counsel served Plaintiff’s counsel with an Amended Notice of Deposition. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel advised he was not available on that date, suggested June 19, 2024 as an alternative, and stated a Spanish interpreter would be necessary. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. C.) On May 17, 2024, Defendants’ counsel served Plaintiff’s counsel with an Amended Notice of Deposition for June 19, 2024. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 8; Exh., D.)

 

On the day before the scheduled deposition, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendants’ counsel to inform that Plaintiff was no longer available for the deposition due to a health issue. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. E.) Due to the late cancellation, Defendants incurred a late cancellation fee. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 10.) Defendants’ counsel requested that Plaintiff’s counsel provide Plaintiff’s July availability by June 24, 2024. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. G.) Defendants’ counsel received no response from Plaintiff. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 13.) On July 11, 2024, Defendants’ counsel sent another Amended Notice of Deposition for July 25, 2024. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 13, Exh. H.) On July 24, 2024, Defendants’ counsel emailed Plaintiff’s Counsel to confirm the deposition set for the next day, received no response, and sent the deposition link. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 14, Exh. I.)

 

On July 25, 2024, neither Plaintiff nor Plaintiff’s counsel appeared at the deposition. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 15; Exh. J.) Defendants’ counsel emailed Plaintiff’s Counsel to inquire about the nonappearance but received no response. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶¶ 15-16, Exhs. J, K.) Defendants’ counsel received no response, and a Certificate of Non-Appearance was taken. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 16, Exh., K.) Defendants incurred additional costs. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 17.)

 

Each of the Notices of Depositions contained three Requests to Produce Documents: (1) a copy of Plaintiff’s driver’s license or photo identification; (2) “[a]ny and all documents not previously produced regarding the injuries or claims sustained in the accident which is the subject of this lawsuit”; and (3) “[a]ny and all documents [Plaintiff] reviewed in preparation for this deposition.” (Motion, p. 6, Exhs. A, B, D, H.)

 

Here, Defendants demonstrated they served multiple notices of deposition upon Plaintiff, but Plaintiff repeatedly failed to appear for deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) Further, Defendants’ counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel to inquire about Plaintiff’s nonappearance at the July 25, 2024 deposition, and Defendants alleged specific facts to demonstrate good cause exists for the document production requests. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2); Motion, p. 7.) Notably, Plaintiff did not file an opposition demonstrating any reasons why this Motion should be denied. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants are entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff to appear at the previously noticed July 25, 2024 remote deposition, and good cause exists to require the production of the requested documents identified in the most recent Amended Notice.

 

Thus, Defendants’ Motion is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

The Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450 provides that the court must impose monetary sanctions if a motion to compel is granted under this section unless the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or other circumstances exist that would make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

 

            Here, Defendants’ Motion is granted. Accordingly, the Court must impose mandatory sanctions. (Code. Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

 

Defendants seek sanctions of $2,890.00, reflecting 4 hours incurred on bringing the Motion at an hourly rate of $150.00, plus 4 hours to review Plaintiff’s Opposition and prepare a Reply, 1 hour to attend the motion hearing, and $1,690 on Spanish Interpreter fees. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 18.) Plaintiff has not filed an Opposition demonstrating that imposition of sanctions would be unjust or that she was justified in failing to comply with discovery obligations.

 

However, the amount sought is excessive given the simplicity of this motion and the lack of Opposition and Reply briefs. The Court finds $375.00 in attorney fees, based on 2 hours to bring the Motion and .5 hours to attend the hearing at an hourly rate of $150.00, to be reasonable.

 

Defendants also request Spanish Interpreter fees of $1,690 be included in the sanctions award. (Yen-Yu Liu Decl., ¶ 18.) However, proof of cancellation fees have not been produced to the Court as Exhibit “L” was omitted from the Motion.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $375.00 in attorney fees. The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for sanctions for Spanish Interpreter fees not to exceed $1,690 once Moving Party has produced proof of invoice of cancellation to Defense counsel. 

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

Defendants Cecilia Resendez and Gerardo Ayala Lopez’s unopposed Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff and the Production of Documents at Deposition is GRANTED.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $2,065.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to appear for her deposition within 30 days of this order, to produce all documents contained in the notice of deposition no later than 3 court days prior to the deposition.

 

Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel are ordered to pay sanctions in the reduced amount of $2065 to Defense counsel within 30 days of this order.

ANALYSIS:

(Pet. ¶¶ 6-8, pp. 20-22.)

 

On April 22, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Petition”) as to Respondents.

 

On August 26, 2024, the Court noted the Petition was filed timely, Petitioner had complied with nearly all requirements, and Respondents were properly served with the Petition. However, the Court noted that Respondents were not properly served with the Notice of Hearing and thus continued the hearing on the Petition. The Court ordered Petitioner to serve Respondents with the Notice of Hearing at least 16 court days prior to the next scheduled hearing and to file proof of service with the Court. (8/26/24 Order.)

 

On September 5, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of service of the Notice of Hearing on Respondents. No Opposition was filed.

 

On September 26, 2024, the Court found Petitioner failed to provide proper notice of the hearing date and continued the matter to allow Petitioner “one final opportunity to properly provide Respondents with a Notice of Hearing that lists the correct time and location of the hearing.” (9/26/24 Order.)

 

On October 4, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of service of the Notice of Hearing.

 

No Opposition was filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

“Regardless of the particular relief granted, any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants¿(2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.)  “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: it may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc.¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.) “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.”  (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.) 

 

“Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.”  (EHM Productions, supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at pp. 1063-64.) 

 

III.              Discussion

 

A. Filing Requirements (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4 states: “A petition under this chapter shall: 

 

(a)   Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement. 

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators. 

(c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.” 

 

            Here, Petitioner sets forth in the Petition, and files a copy of, the Future Receipts Sale Agreement containing an arbitration provision governing disputes between the parties arising out of the agreement (“Arbitration Agreement”). (Pet. ¶ 4, Attach. 4(b).)  Petitioner also sets forth the name of the Arbitrator, Shawn K. Aiken, from the AAA and submits a copy of the Arbitration Award, titled “Final Award of Arbitrator,” which was written and signed by the Arbitrator Shawn K. Aiken and awarded in favor of Petitioner and against Respondents in the sum of $22,757.41. (Pet., ¶¶ 5-8, Attach. Exh. 8(c).)

 

            Thus, Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4.

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 states, in pertinent part:  

 

(a)   “A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice. 

(b)   If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” 

 

Here, the Arbitration Agreement does not provide the manner in which service shall be made, and Respondents have not previously appeared in the proceeding. Thus, they must be served in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action within this state. On May 8, 2024, Petitioner filed proof that on May 3, 2024, Respondents were properly served with the Petition by substitute service. However, Petitioner twice failed to properly serve Respondents with the Notice of Hearing. (8/26/24 Order; 9/26/24 Order.)

Based on the foregoing, the service requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 are satisfied.

 

C. Service of the Arbitration Award & Timeliness of Petition (CCP §§ 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”¿

 

“A petition to confirm an award shall be served and filed not later than four years after the date of service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner. A petition to vacate an award or to correct an award shall be served and filed not later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.)

 

“No petition [to confirm arbitration award] may be served and filed … until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.4.)

 

“[B]efore a court can enter an arbitrator’s award as its judgment, the parties must have the notice of the award. … ‘[T]he superior court is without jurisdiction to act further in the matter until the parties are served or otherwise obtain actual notice of the award.’ ”(Domingo v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transp. Auth. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 550, 554.)

 

Here, the arbitration award includes proof of service that the AAA served a copy of the arbitration award on Petitioner and Respondents via first class mail and email on August 4, 2023. (Petn., Attach. Exh. 8(c).)

 

In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by serving and filing a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award was served.  (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.) Here, the arbitration award was served on August 4, 2023, and the original Petition was filed more than 10 days and less than four years later, on April 22, 2024.

 

Thus, the Court finds the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4 are satisfied.

Petitioner Forward Financing, LLC’s Amended Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award is GRANTED.

A petition to vacate an award or to correct an award shall be served and filed not later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.)

 

“No petition [to confirm arbitration award] may be served and filed … until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.4.)

 

“[B]efore a court can enter an arbitrator’s award as its judgment, the parties must have the notice of the award. … ‘[T]he superior court is without jurisdiction to act further in the matter until the parties are served or otherwise obtain actual notice of the award.’ ”(Domingo v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transp. Auth. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 550, 554.)

 

Here, the arbitration award includes proof of service that the AAA served a copy of the arbitration award on Petitioner and Respondents via first class mail and email on August 22, 2023. (Petn., Attach. Exh. 8(c).)

 

In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by serving and filing a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award was served.  (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.) Here, the arbitration award was served on August 22, 2023, and the original Petition was filed more than 10 days and less than four years later, on April 22, 2024.

 

Thus, the Court finds the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4 are satisfied.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

Petitioner Forward Financing, LLC’s Amended Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On May 21, 2024, Arbitrator Anita Rae Shapiro (the “Arbitrator”) with Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (“FINRA”) Dispute Resolution Services, issued an Arbitration Award in favor of Petitioner Zhen Liu (“Petitioner”) and against Respondent Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith (“Respondent”). (Petn., ¶¶ 3-5, Exh. 2.) The Award requires expungement of all references to Occurrence Number 2191837 from the registration records maintained by the CRD for Petitioner. (Petn., Exh. 2.) The CRD is a database maintained for FINRA for firms and individuals that participate in the U.S. Securities industry.

 

On June 14, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Verified Petition to Confirm FINRA Dispute Resolution Arbitration Award (the “Petition”) against Respondent and FINRA. FINRA was named as a respondent as required by FINRA Rule 2080 (Petn., ¶ 4, Exh. 1), and Petitioner filed proof of personal service on FINRA of the Verified Petition, Notice of Hearing, and associated papers. (6/24/24 Proof of Service.) However, Petitioner did not name Respondent as a party to the action.

 

On July 30, 2024, FINRA filed a response stating it does not oppose confirmation of the Arbitration Award or the expungement relief therein. However, FINRA noted that the “other party to the underlying action (Respondent Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith Incorporated (‘Merrill Lynch’) is not named in this action and the time for it to seek vacatur has not lapsed.” (Response, p. 2.)

 

On September 18, 2024, Petitioner served Respondent with Proof of Service of the Verified Petition, Notice of Hearing, and associated papers via mail. (9/18/24 Proof of Service.)

 

On October 1, 2024, the Court continued the Petition hearing to November 14, 2024 due to the Court’s unavailability and calendar congestion.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter and as noted by FINRA in its Response to the Petition, Petitioner has failed to name Respondent as a party to this action.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

“Regardless of the particular relief granted, any arbitrator’s award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.”  (O’Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants¿(2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.)  “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: it may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.”  (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc.¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.) “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.”  (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.) 

 

“Neither the trial court, nor the appellate court, may ‘review the merits of the dispute, the sufficiency of the evidence, or the arbitrator’s reasoning, nor may we correct or review an award because of an arbitrator’s legal or factual error, even if it appears on the award’s face.”  (EHM Productions, supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at pp. 1063-64.) 

 

 

IV.              Discussion

 

A. Filing Requirements (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4 states: “A petition under this chapter shall: 

 

(a)   Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement. 

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators. 

(c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.” 

 

            Here, Petitioner sets forth in the Petition, and files a copy of, the agreement between the parties to arbitrate their dispute with FINRA (“Arbitration Agreement”) as well as a copy of the Arbitration Award. (Pet. ¶¶ 1-5, Exh. 2.)  Petitioner also sets forth the name of the Arbitrator, Anita Rae Shapiro, with FINRA Dispute Resolution Services and submits a copy of the Arbitration Award, titled “Award,” which was written and signed by the Arbitrator Anita Rae Shapiro and awarded in favor of Petitioner and against Respondent. (Pet., ¶¶ 1, 4-5, Exh. 2).

 

            Thus, Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4.

 

B. Service of the Petition, and Notice of Hearing (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4)

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 states, in pertinent part:  

 

(a)   “A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice. 

(b)   If the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision: ¶ (1) Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” 

 

Here, the Arbitration Agreement does not provide the manner in which service shall be made, and Respondent has not previously appeared in the proceeding. Thus, Respondent must be served in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action within this state. On June 24, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of service that on June 21, 2024, FINRA was properly served with the Petition, Notice of Hearing, and accompanying papers by personal service. On September 18, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of service that on June 24, 2024, Respondent was served with the Petition, Notice of Hearing, and accompanying papers by electronic service. However, electronic service is not a valid manner of service of summons in California. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 415.10-415.95.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the service requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 1290.4 are not satisfied.

 

C. Service of the Arbitration Award & Timeliness of Petition (CCP §§ 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”¿

 

“A petition to confirm an award shall be served and filed not later than four years after the date of service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner. A petition to vacate an award or to correct an award shall be served and filed not later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.)

 

“No petition [to confirm arbitration award] may be served and filed … until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.4.)

 

“[B]efore a court can enter an arbitrator’s award as its judgment, the parties must have the notice of the award. … ‘[T]he superior court is without jurisdiction to act further in the matter until the parties are served or otherwise obtain actual notice of the award.’ ” (Domingo v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transp. Auth. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 550, 554.)

 

Here, the Arbitration Award states that it was served on the parties on May 21, 2024 but does not include a proof of service. (Pet., Exh. 2.) Thus, Section 1283.6 is not satisfied.

 

In addition, a party may seek a court judgment confirming an arbitration award by serving and filing a petition no more than four years, but not less than 10 days, after the award was served.  (Code of Civ. Proc., §§ 1288, 1288.4.) Here, the Arbitration Award was served on May 21, 2024, and the Petition was filed more than 10 days and less than four years later, on June 14, 2024. Thus, the Court finds the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure sections 1288 and 1288.4 are satisfied.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

Based on the foregoing, Petitioner Zhen Liu’s Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award is DENIED without prejudice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff David Castillo (“Plaintiff”), proceeding pro se, initiated this action against Defendants Miguel Aguirre Reyes and Corona Millworks (“Defendants”).

 

On June 21, 2024, Defendants each filed a demurrer. No opposition was filed.

 

On August 5, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrers with leave to amend; the Court ordered Plaintiff to file a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) within 30 days of the order. (8/5/24 Order.) In sustaining the demurrer, the Court found Plaintiff’s form complaint lacked the cause of action form for negligence (PLD-PI-001(2)). While the Court noted the traffic collision report attached to the Complaint may provide some requisite facts, the Court found “none of the elements have been pled for a cause of action for negligence, such as what acts or omissions Plaintiff alleges Defendants performed negligently, because the cause of action form is missing from the complaint.”  (8/5/24 Order.)

 

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a FAC.

 

On September 12, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrers to Plaintiff’s FAC (“Demurrers”). Plaintiff did not file an Opposition.

 

On October 1, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Demurrers to November 7 due to the Court’s unavailability and calendar congestion. (10/1/24 Order.) On November 4, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the Demurrers to November 14 due to the Court’s unavailability and calendar congestion. (11/4/24 Order.)

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

“As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

 

 

III.              Discussion

 

Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

The Demurrers are accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). (Demurrers, Spelder Decl.) However, there is a discrepancy in the meet and confer statement – Robert de Spelder, attorney for Defendants, declares that he spoke to Plaintiff “[o]n October 11, 2024, at 4:20 p.m.” regarding the FAC and Demurrers. (Demurrers, Spelder Decl. ¶ 5.) But the declaration is dated one month earlier on September 12, 2024, which is also the same day the Demurrers were served and filed. (Demurrers, Spelder Decl.; Demurrers, Proof of Service.)

 

Demurrers

 

Defendants demur to the FAC on the basis it does not “state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Demurrers, p. 3.)

 

The elements for negligence are: (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)  “Ordinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms, without specific facts showing how the injury occurred, but there are ‘limits to the generality with which a plaintiff is permitted to state his cause of action, and ... the plaintiff must indicate the acts or omissions which are said to have been negligently performed. He may not recover upon the bare statement that the defendant’s negligence has caused him injury.’ [Citation].” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527.)  

 

A review of the FAC reveals that, despite this Court’s prior order, Plaintiff has still failed to attach the cause of action form for negligence (PLD-PI-001(2)). While Plaintiff attaches additional documents from the traffic crash report that provide potentially more of the requisite facts, the Court finds the same deficiencies exist as were present with the original Complaint – namely, “none of the elements have been pled for a cause of action for negligence, such as what acts or omissions Plaintiff alleges Defendants performed negligently, because the cause of action form is missing from the [FAC].”  (8/5/24 Order.)

 

Accordingly, the unopposed Demurrers are SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants Miguel Aguirre Reyes and Corona Millworks’ Demurrers to the FAC is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS’ LEAVE TO AMEND. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to file 2nd Amended Complaint within 20 days of this Court’s order.
