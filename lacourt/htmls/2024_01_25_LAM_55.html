DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV20102    Hearing Date: January 25, 2024    Dept: 55

 

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS:  Motion to Stay Proceedings Pending Determination of Administrative Proceeding Before the Los Angeles County Civil Service Commission [C.C.P. §128].

 

The motion is denied.

 

Background

In this FEHA case, RIAD ROY ITANI (“Plaintiff”) alleges that the COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES (“Defendant”) engaged in discrimination and retaliation, based on his age and national origin/race, including by not promoting him.

Defendant has filed a motion seeking a stay of this action pending the outcome of an administrative proceeding Plaintiff filed that it pending before the Los Angeles County Civil Service Commission (the “Administrative Proceeding”). Plaintiff opposes the motion.

Legal Standard

An employee may, but need not, exhaust a city’s internal grievance procedures in addition to FEHA's administrative remedy, before pursuing a FEHA action in superior court.  Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal. 4th 1074, 1092.  If an employee proceeds with internal grievance procedures, it is theoretically possible, although not automatic, that an adverse determination by a civil service commission can become collateral estoppel or res judicata, depending on factors not now before the Court in this case.  See, e.g., McDonald v. Antelope Valley Cmty. Coll. Dist. (2008) 45 Cal. 4th 88, 113 (“plaintiff elected to pursue her discrimination claim before a civil service commission, which held numerous hearings and ultimately entered an adverse ruling. Rather than challenge the ruling through available judicial avenues, the plaintiff filed a complaint with the DFEH. The Court of Appeal held that the plaintiff had failed to exhaust her judicial remedies by not seeking mandamus, the civil service commission's findings were binding until set aside, and those findings barred her FEHA complaint from going forward.”).

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.”   Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.  CCP sections 128(a)(4) and 187 are “generic statements of the court's powers to formulate suitable procedures and command obedience to its orders….”  Clark v. Optical Coating Lab. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 150, 164-65.

Analysis

The Court notes that, per the Minute Order entered 11/9/22, the Defendant informed the Court that the Administrative Proceeding was still pending. The Court thus lifted the stay it had initially issued in this case on 1/25/22 and set a trial date for 2/20/24.  Because the Court lifted the stay while the Administrative Proceeding was then still pending, it appears impliedly that the Court exercised its discretion to lift the stay because of inordinate delay with the Administrative Proceeding. 

Such delay remains apparent to date because there is no clarity as to when the Administrative Proceeding will conclude.  While the potential of collateral estoppel or res judicata theoretically could make a stay more efficient than allowing both administrative and civil cases to proceed simultaneously, this Court likewise seeks to avoid excessive delay in reaching some resolution of Plaintiff’s matters.  Further, Defendant has cited no authority mandating a stay, and the Court’s own independent research has revealed no such governing case law.

Therefore, the motion for a stay is denied. The Court expects to discuss the discovery and trial schedule with the parties at the hearing.

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS:  Motion of Counsel of Record to be Relieved as Counsel of Record as to Defendant Junior's Apparel Inc.


In this wage and hour employment case, counsel for Defendant JUNIOR'S APPAREL INC. has filed a motion to be relieved as counsel. No opposition has been filed.  The form notice, declaration, proposed order, and proof of service comply with the requirements for a motion to be relieved as counsel. See CRC Rule 3.1362. Case Number: 22STCV38810    Hearing Date: January 25, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS:  Plaintiff’s Motion To Compel Further Responses And Production Of Documents From Defendant NAI HIFFMAN Pursuant To Plaintiff’s Requests For Production Of Documents, Set One; Request For Sanctions; Plaintiff’s Motion To Compel Further Responses And Production Of Documents From Defendant HIFFMAN NATIONAL OF CALIFORNIA, INC. Pursuant To Plaintiff’s Requests For Production Of Documents, Set One; Request For Sanctions

 

Both motions are granted.

On or before 2/26/24, Defendant NAI HIFFMAN and Defendant HIFFMAN NATIONAL OF CALIFORNIA, INC. shall serve further responses, and produce documents, without objections, and in full compliance with the California Discovery Act, CCP §2016.010 et seq., as to the requests for documents served by Plaintiff KELLEY CLOUD.

On or before that same date, Defendant NAI HIFFMAN and Defendant HIFFMAN NATIONAL OF CALIFORNIA, INC. shall each pay discovery sanctions in the sum of $735 to Plaintiff KELLEY CLOUD, jointly and severally with counsel Gordon Rees Scully Mansukahani, LLP, the Court finding the absence of substantial justification.    E.g., CCP   § 2023.030.

The opposing requests for monetary sanctions are denied, the motions having been granted.

Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration is advanced from 2/28/24 and continued to 3/28/24, to allow Plaintiff the opportunity to include the ordered discovery responses in opposition to the arbitration motion.

Background

In this case, Plaintiff KELLEY CLOUD (“Plaintiff”) seeks damages based on allegations that defendants, including Defendants NAI HIFFMAN and HIFFMAN NATIONAL OF CALIFORNIA, INC. (“Defendants”) discriminated and harassed Plaintiff and wrongfully terminated Plaintiff’s employment, because of Plaintiff’s cancer-related disability and/or medical condition.

Plaintiff has filed two motions to compel further responses and production of documents against Defendants. The motions seek further responses and production of documents for the twelve document requests in Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One (“Requests for Production”), and monetary sanctions. Defendants oppose the motions and seek sanctions against Plaintiff.

Analysis

Plaintiff’s Requests for Production seek documents related to the motion to compel arbitration filed by Defendants, including agreements between Plaintiff and Defendants, Defendants’ acquisition of Plaintiff’s former employer NAI Capital Management, Inc., and any arbitration agreements between Plaintiff and NAI Capital Management. Plaintiff served the Requests for Production on 6/22/23 by email, and Defendants served objections only on 8/4/23. Plaintiff contends Defendants’ responses are untimely and therefore they have waived their objections. See CCP § 2031.300(a). While Defendants deny receiving the Requests for Production, the emails attached to the declarations filed by Plaintiff’s counsel support her argument that Defendants received them on 6/22/23, which would make the 8/4/23 responses untimely. See Rogal Decl., Ex. 1; Suppl Rogal Decl., Ex. A.

Even assuming that Defendants’ responses were timely, however, they are deficient. Defendants objected to each of the Requests for Production on the grounds that the case is subject to private mediation pursuant to a written arbitration agreement, and that Defendants have filed a motion to compel arbitration. Defendants also cite to Adolf v Coastal Auto Sales, Inc. (2010) 184 Cal. App. 4th 1443, 1451-52 and Augusta v. Keehn & Assoc. (2011) 193, Cal. App. 4th 331, 339-40. Defendants did not include code-compliant responses to the Requests for Production pursuant to CCP §§ 2031.210(a), 2031.220, 2031.280.

A party opposing a motion to compel arbitration under CCP § 1281.2 has “discovery rights under the Civil Discovery Act, subject to the relevancy requirement and other provisions limiting the scope and timing of that discovery.”  Bouton v. USAA Casualty Ins. Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 412, 427. Thus, the fact that Defendants have filed a motion to compel arbitration does not constitute a valid objection and refusal to respond to the Requests for Production with code-complaint responses. Neither of the cases cited by Defendants in their objections holds otherwise.

The burden to show good cause for production “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” Tbg Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 96 Cal. App. 4th 443, 448. “[E]ven when no . . . supporting affidavits are filed . . .good cause may be found in the pleadings theretofore filed in the action."  Greyhound Corp.  v.  Sup.  Ct.  (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 389, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in  Dowden v. Sup. Ct. (1999) 73 Cal. App. 4th 126, 132.

Here, the documents sought in the Requests for Production are plainly relevant to the arbitration motion. Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration, filed 6/28/23, seeks to enforce an arbitration agreement with Plaintiff and it places in issue the enforceability of that agreement, and the relationships between the defendants (e.g., Mot., 3:14-15  (“ ‘The Agreement defines “Client Company” as Hiffman, and “Covered Persons” to include Hiffman, as well as its “officers, directors, employees, affiliates or agents.” ’ ”)). The arbitration agreement contains a provision stating that if Plaintiff has a separate agreement with “Client Company” for arbitration, that agreement controls. (Assoian Decl., ISO Mot., Ex. A, Para. 6.) And the motion references the fact that Plaintiff used to work for NAI Capital Management, Inc., which Defendant Hiffman acquired in 2021. (Mot. at 2.) Document requests regarding the acquisition of NAI Capital Management and any arbitration agreements NAI had with Plaintiff are relevant to Defendants’ motion.

Generally, monetary sanctions are mandatory as to parties losing discovery motions, unless courts find substantial justification or other injustice.  E.g.,  Foothill Properties v. Lyon/Copley Corona Assocs., L.P. (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1542, 1557-58.  “‘[S]ubstantial justification” has been understood to mean that a justification is clearly reasonable because it is well-grounded in both law and fact.”  Doe v. U.S. Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1434.  “If the party seeking a monetary sanction meets its burden of proof, the burden shifts to the opposing party attempting to avoid a monetary sanction to show that it acted with ‘substantial justification.’”  Doe v. U.S. Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1435. Defendants do not have substantial justification for refusing to provide code-complaint responses and insisting on serving objections only when they responded to the requests late.

The Court therefore grants the motion and requests sanctions against Defendants. Defendants’ request for sanctions is denied.
