DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV19154    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: B

RICHARD CABRERA-LARA V. ARBOL GRADING, INC., ET AL.

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:          July 8, 2024                             Trial Date:       August 16, 2024

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         21STCV19154

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Arbol Grading, Inc. dba Arbol Construction (“Arbol”)

Responding Party:       Unopposed   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from Plaintiff Richard Cabrera-Lara (“Plaintiff”) allegedly sustaining injuries while working on a construction project on May 12, 2020 at 2451 Summitridge Drive, Beverly Hills, California. Plaintiff alleges that he sustained injuries while assisting with the cleaning of a heavy-duty drill.

 

On May 20, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendants Arbol Grading, Inc. dba Arbol Construction (“Arbol”), Melt De La Paz, Inc. (“Melt”), Miguel Mitchell (“Mitchell”), and Does 1 to 100, inclusive (collectively, the “Defendants”), alleging a single cause of action for General Negligence.

 

On April 15, 2022, State Farm Fire and Casualty Company (“State Farm”) filed a Motion for Leave to File a Complaint-in-Intervention.

 

On June 24, 2022, Defendant Melt filed its Answer to the Complaint. Also, on such date, Defendant Melt filed a Cross-Complaint against Roes 1 through 10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Indemnity; (2) Contribution; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

 

On July 29, 2022, Defendant Arbol filed its Answer to the Complaint.

 

On September 7, 2022, the Honorable Audra Mori sitting in Department 31 at Spring Street Courthouse granted State Farm’s Motion for Leave to Intervene. (09/07/22 Minute Order.)  

 

On September 9, 2022, State Farm filed a Complaint-in-Intervention against Defendants Arbol, Melt, and Mitchell alleging causes of action for: (1) General Negligence; and (2) Products Liability.  

 

On September 22, 2022, Defendant Melt filed its Answer to the Complaint-in-Intervention.

 

On October 6, 2022, Defendant Arbol filed its Answer to the Complaint-in-Intervention.

 

On April 12, 2024, Defendant Arbol filed and served the instant Motion for Summary Judgment as to the Complaint (the “Motion”).[1] The Motion is made on the grounds that Defendant Arbol is not the proper entity defendant; Defendant Arbol did not owe Plaintiff a duty of care under the circumstances and did not breach any alleged duty; and Defendant Arbol’s conduct was not the actual or legal cause of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries.

 

On June 5, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Michelle C. Kim to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz sitting in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East effective June 24, 2024.

 

As of July 2, 2024, no opposition brief has been filed. Any opposition brief was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) Given that the Motion is unopposed, an inference is created that the motion is meritorious. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Motion for Summary Judgment (the “Motion”) filed by Defendant Arbol Grading, Inc. dba Arbol Construction is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67.)

A cause of action has no merit if “[o]ne or more of the elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(1).) A defendant has met its burden in showing that a cause of action has no merit if the defendant “has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) Once the defendant has met its burden, “the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

“As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the defendant has met its burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th 826, 849.) 

“The summary judgment statute [is] not intended nor can it be used as a substitute for existing methods in the trial of issues of fact.” (Travelers Indemn. Co. v. McIntosh (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 177, 182.) The summary judgment “procedure is drastic and should be used with caution.” (House v. Lala (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d 412, 415.) “Any doubt in granting summary judgment should be exercised against the moving party.” (Ibid.)

Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant Arbol’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)  

Issue No.1: Negligence Cause of Action  

“Actionable negligence is traditionally regarded as involving the following: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of that duty; and (3) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Suarez v. Pacific Northstar Mechanical, Inc. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 430, 437.) “The threshold element of a cause of action for negligence is the existence of a duty to use due care toward an interest of another that enjoys legal protection against unintentional invasion.” (Ibid., citations omitted.) “Whether this essential prerequisite to a negligence cause of action has been satisfied in a particular case is a question of law to be resolved by the court.” (Ibid., citations omitted.)

“A tort, whether intentional or negligent, involves a violation of a legal duty, imposed by statute, contract or otherwise, owed by the defendant to the person injured.” (Romero v. Superior Court (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078, emphasis in original.) “Absent a legal duty, any injury is an injury without actionable wrong.” (Ibid.) “While it is the province of the jury, as trier of fact, to determine whether an unreasonable risk of harm was foreseeable under the particular facts of a given case, the trial court must still decide as a matter of law whether there was a duty in the first place, even if that determination includes a consideration of foreseeability.” (Ibid., citations omitted.) “Duty, being a question of law, is particularly amenable to resolution by summary judgment.” (Ibid., citation omitted.)

In determining the existence of a duty, the trial court evaluates “the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, the closeness of the connection between the defendant’s conduct and the injury suffered, the moral blame attached to the defendant’s conduct, the policy of preventing future harm, the extent of the burden to the defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach, and the availability, cost, and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.” (Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 112-13.)

Defendant Arbol’s Evidence

Given that the Motion is unopposed, all the facts set forth in Defendant Arbol’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Facts are undisputed.

Defendant Arbol presents the following undisputed material facts: Defendant Arbol was founded by President Alexander Shvartsman in 2017 and was a licensed general contractor that specialized in grading construction sites. (UMF Nos. 1-2.) On or about October 7, 2022, Defendant Arbol was completely and formally wound up and dissolved, in part, because the company was no longer being used and no longer had any contracts. (UMF No. 3.) Defendant Melt is an excavation contractor and, at all times relevant in the Complaint, Defendant Mitchell was an employee and drill operator for Defendant Melt and not Defendant Arbol. (UMF Nos. 4-5.)

At all times relevant, Plaintiff was an ironworker for Cruz Concrete Stone, Inc. (“Cruz”), a masonry and rebar contractor on the Project. (UMF No. 6.) Plaintiff generally alleges that, on May 12, 2020, he was working on a home construction site located at or near 2451 Summitridge Drive, Beverly Hills, CA (the “Property”). (UMF No. 7.) Plaintiff alleges that, on the date of the subject accident, Defendant Mitchell, who was allegedly drilling large holes in the ground for Defendant Melt, asked Plaintiff to assist with measurements and to help him clean an auger drill that Defendant Mitchell was operating. (UMF No. 8.) While Plaintiff was helping to clean the drill, the auger drill allegedly turned on and crushed two of Plaintiff’s fingers. (UMF No. 9.) Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges one cause of action for general negligence against Defendants. (UMF No. 10.)

Defendant Arbol presents the undisputed material fact that it did not have any involvement in the subject construction project. (UMF No. 11.) Defendant Arbol did not own, lease, occupy, or control the Property or the project site in any manner, including at the time of the alleged accident. (UMF No. 12.) Defendant Arbol did not have any employees at the time of the subject accident and did not employ, train, provide, supervise, direct, control, or undertake for the safety of any of the workers at the project site, including Defendant Mitchell or Plaintiff. (UMF Nos. 13-14.) Defendant Arbol did not own, supply, lease, maintain, service, control, or operate any equipment or machinery at the project site, including the drill/auger machine that Plaintiff claims caused or contributed to his alleged injuries in connection with the subject accident. (UMF No. 15.) At the time of the accident, the drill/auger machine that Plaintiff claims caused or contributed to his alleged injuries was owned by Commercial Credit Group, LLC and leased by Defendant Melt. (UMF No. 16.) Defendant Arbol was not hired to and did not perform any work in connection with the project and did not have any active contracts at the time of the project and subject accident. (UMF Nos. 17-18.)

Analysis

The Court finds that Defendant Arbol has met its burden in showing that it did not owe Plaintiff a duty of care. Defendant Arbol has presented evidence that it was not involved at all in the subject project; did not have any affiliation or association with the equipment that allegedly injured Plaintiff; and did not own, lease, operate, or control the Property.

 

Given that Plaintiff has failed to oppose the Motion, Plaintiff has not met his burden in showing a triable issue of material fact as to the issue of duty of care. Thus, as a matter of law, the Court finds that Defendant Arbol did not owe Plaintiff a duty of care.

 

The Court also finds that Defendant Arbol has shown that it did not breach a duty of care or cause Plaintiff’s injuries. “[I]f a defendant’s negligence was a substantial factor in causing the plaintiff’s harm, then the defendant is responsible for the harm.” (Yanez v. Plummer (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 180, 187.) “[C]onduct is not a substantial factor in causing harm if the same harm would have occurred without that conduct.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendant Arbol has presented evidence that it did not breach a duty of care and that it was not a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm. (UMF Nos. 1-18.) Given that Plaintiff has failed to oppose the Motion, the Court finds, as a matter of law, that Defendant Arbol did not breach a duty of care or cause Plaintiff’s injuries. (See Thompson v. Sacramento City Unified School Dist. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1352, 1366; see also Whiteley v. Philip Morris Inc. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 635, 694.)  

 

The Court also finds that Defendant Arbol has presented evidence that it cannot be held vicariously liable for Plaintiff’s injuries. “Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, an employer is liable for the torts of his employees committed within the scope of their employment.” (Halliburton Energy Services, Inc. v. Department of Transportation (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 87, 93-94, citations omitted.) Defendant Arbol has presented evidence that it did not have any employees at the time of the subject accident and had no association whatsoever with the subject construction project. Defendant Arbol has also presented evidence that it did not employ, train, provide, supervise, direct, control, or undertake for the safety of any of the workers at the project site. As such, there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant Arbol can be held vicariously liable. Defendant Arbol has presented evidence showing that Defendant Mitchell was not its employee or agent. Plaintiff, as stated above, has failed to oppose the Motion. Thus, Plaintiff has presented no evidence to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to the issue of vicarious liability.

 

Given Plaintiff’s lack of opposition to the Motion, Plaintiff has conceded all of the arguments raised therein as “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

 

Accordingly, the Motion is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motion is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

Dept. B

8:30 A.M.

Background

Plaintiff filed a complaint on September 7, 2021.  Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege any causes of action.  Plaintiff attaches a police report for battery as an attachment to the complaint. 

 

Discussion

1.     No POS

On April 22, 2024, self-represented plaintiff Richard Harris (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant “Motion for Judgment on Pleadings”, setting the hearing date for May 29, 2024.  The hearing was continued from May 29, 2024 to June 3, 2024.  The hearing was continued from June 3, 2024 to July 8, 2024 pursuant to an oral stipulation of the parties. 

 

Plaintiff has still not filed a proper POS of this motion, nor have Defendants filed an opposition.  Plaintiff has been given ample opportunity to file POS of this motion on Defendants.  Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is denied for failure to properly serve Defendants.

 

2.      Plaintiff fails to establish entitlement to judgment on the pleadings

“Judgment on the pleadings is akin to a demurrer and is properly granted only if the complaint does not state facts sufficient to state a cause of action against that defendant.  The grounds for the motion must appear on the face of the complaint, and in any matters subject to judicial notice. The court accepts as true all material factual allegations, giving them a liberal construction, but it does not consider conclusions of fact or law, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or judicially noticed facts.  Appellate courts review the record de novo to determine whether the complaint states a cause of action as a matter of law.”  (Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)

 

A CCP §438 Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings “may only be made on one of the following grounds:  (A) If the moving party is a plaintiff, that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.”  CCP §438(c)(1)(A).  However, a common law motion for judgment on the pleadings without such restrictions is still recognized.  (Cordova v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 89, 109.)

 

“A plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is analogous to a plaintiff’s demurrer to an answer and is evaluated by the same standards.  The motion should be denied if the defendant’s pleadings raise a material issue or set up affirmative matter constituting a defense; for purposes of ruling on the motion, the trial court must treat all of the defendant’s allegations as being true.”  (People ex rel. Becerra v. Superior Court (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 486, 499.)  Demurrer to an answer must be overruled if any defense to the complaint is properly alleged.  (South Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 733–734 (demurrer to answer properly overruled based on sufficiently pled general denial) (“if one of the defenses or counterclaims is free from the objections urged by demurrer, then a demurrer to the entire answer must be overruled”). 

 

An answer fails to raise any material issue or state a defense where it expressly or substantially admits or does not sufficiently deny all the material allegations of the complaint and sets up no new matter that is sufficient to bar or defeat the action.  (Adjustment Corp. v. Hollywood Hardware & Paint Co. (1939) 35 Cal.App.2d 566, 569-570.)  Thus, a general denial contained in the answer requires that the entire demurrer be overruled.  (People ex rel. Becerra, supra, 29 Cal.App.5th at 499 (discussing MJP based on failure to state facts to constitute a defense); Engine Manufacturers Assn. v. State Air Resources Bd. (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 1022, 1034 (MJP must be denied if “defendant's pleadings raise a material issue or set up affirmative matter constituting a defense”); Allstate Ins. Co., supra, 160 Cal.App.3d at 331 (MJP should be denied if answer either contains general denial or states facts in support of affirmative defenses); Barasch v. Epstein (1957) 147 Cal.App.2d 439, 443 (“[w]here the answer, fairly construed, suggests that the defendant may have a good defense, a motion for judgment on the pleadings should not be granted”).

 

A plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings tests (1) whether the plaintiff’s complaint states a cause of action and (2) whether the defendant’s answer states sufficient facts to constitute a defense against the complaint.  Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings fails to address either of these issues.  Plaintiff merely cuts and pastes blocks of law regarding the elements of fraud, assault, battery and agency liability.  Plaintiff does not address whether his complaint states a cause of action, nor does he address whether Defendants’ answer states sufficient facts to constitute a defense against the complaint.  On its face, Plaintiff’s complaint does not allege any cause of action and it is uncertain what he is alleging against Defendants.  As such, Plaintiff fails to establish entitlement to judgment on the pleadings. 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings iS DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

1. Background

            On February 3, 2022, Plaintiff Lizza Schlumbrecht (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Karen Solomon and DOES 1 to 50 (“Defendants”), alleging causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle and (2) General Negligence.   

Defendant Solomon, at this time, moves for an order continuing the trial to March 4, 2025 and continuing all trial-related dates. The motion has been fully briefed by the parties.

2. Preliminary Issues

            Plaintiff argues the motion should be denied on procedural grounds because the deadline to serve the motion was June 10, 2024 but she was served on June 12, 2024. Although the motion was not served on Plaintiff 18 days prior to the scheduled hearing date in accordance with Code of Civil procedure Section 1005(d), Plaintiff was served only two days late and has opposed the motion on the merits.

            Therefore, the Court will exercise its discretion and rule on the merits below.

3. Motion to Continue Trial

a. Legal Standard

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332(c): the court may grant a continuance for “good cause,” which includes: (1) unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (2) unavailability of a party due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (3) unavailability of trial counsel due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (4) substitution of trial counsel required in the interests of justice; (5) addition of a new party or other parties in regard to a new party’s involvement hasn’t had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial; (6) party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or (7) significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which indicates the case is not ready for trial. (Cal. Rules Ct., Rule 3.1332(c).)¿¿ 

¿¿           Other relevant factors to be considered may include: “(1) the proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; (6) if the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay; (7) the court’s calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials; (8) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial; (9) whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; (10) whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and (11) any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application” (Cal. Rules Ct., Rule 3.1332(d).)¿¿ 

¿¿           “A trial court has great discretion in the disposition of an application for a continuance. Absent a clear abuse of discretion, the court’s determination will not be disturbed.” (Estate of Smith v. Atkinson (1973) 9 Cal.3d 74, 81.) “Such discretion is abused, however, where the lack of a continuance results in the denial of a fair hearing.” (Rankin v. Curtis (1986) 183 Cal.App.3d 939, 947.)¿

b. Discussion

 Here, the trial is currently set for September 18, 2024, which is two months away. Defendant Solomon requests a seven-month continuance to allow the motions to quash subpoenas filed by Plaintiff to be heard, to get depositions completed, and to conduct/compel medical examination of Plaintiff regarding new alleged injuries. The Court notes that the parties have previously stipulated to three continuances since the commencement of this instant lawsuit. However, the interests of justice supports a continuance of the trial date as currently set. First, the discovery cut-off date is August 19, 2024 and two of Plaintiff’s motion to quash subpoenas are not set to be heard until August 2, 2024. The outcome of these motions will determine whether Plaintiff needs to produce the requested records or Defendant Solomon needs to reissue subpoenas. Furthermore, there are depositions of witnesses they have not been completed due to scheduling issues. Moreover, Plaintiff has placed her medical condition at issue in this case, thus complete records for her surgery and other treatment related to the alleged injuries are relevant to determine the veracity of her claims. As such, Defendant Solomon has shown good cause for the proposed continuance in that it will allow for a trial on the merits.

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Karen Solomon’s Motion to Continue Trial is GRANTED. The trial is continued to March 4, 2025.
