DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV05651    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: 73

6/04/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

BRIAN WHITAKER v. PABLITO’S TACOS DF, INC. (23STCV05651) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Prathima Reddy Price (The Reddy Law Firm) 

Counsel for Defendant:  None

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 4/08/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 14, 2023, Plaintiff Brian Whitaker (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Pablito’s Tacos DF, Inc. (“Defendant”) for violations of the Unruh Civil Rights Act (“Unruh”) and violations of the California Disabled Persons Act (“CDPA”).

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff suffers from a spinal cord injury and is a quadriplegic who uses a wheelchair for mobility. (Complaint, ¶ 1.) The action stems from Plaintiff’s visit to Defendant’s restaurant on October 22, 2022 where Plaintiff found that the facility failed to comply with ADA standards. (Complaint, ¶¶ 8-11.) Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that there was not enough toe clearance under the outdoor dining surfaces. (Complaint, ¶ 11.)

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default judgment. Plaintiff is seeking a judgment in the amount of $12,504.22, consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $79,544.24, $10,177.30 in interest, $574.00 in costs, and $2,480.88 in attorney’s fees. 

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.     Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.     Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                      Yes 

2.     Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                    Yes 

3.     Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                     Yes 

4.     Summary of the case                                                                          Yes 

5.     585(d) declarations/admissible evidence in support                        Yes

Here, Plaintiff has complied with all requirements for a default judgment. 

 

Plaintiff seeks a judgment in the amount of $4,000.00 in statutory damages. Generally, a plaintiff may obtain statutory damages for Unruh violations “if the plaintiff personally encountered the violation on a particular occasion, or the plaintiff was deterred from accessing a place of public accommodation on a particular occasion.” (Civ. Code, § 55.56, subd. (b).) To establish that a plaintiff was deterred, plaintiff must establish that plaintiff had actual knowledge of the violations and that the violations would have denied plaintiff “full and equal access” on that occasion. (Civ. Code, § 55.56, subd. (d).) Here, Plaintiff submitted a declaration stating he visited Defendant’s restaurant on October 22, 2022 and personally encountered the ADA violation, specifically the lack of toe clearance under the outdoor dining surfaces. (Whitaker Decl., ¶¶ 2-4.) Plaintiff also seeks $6,355.00 in attorney fees and $2,149.22 in costs as set forth in the declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel. (Price Decl., Ex. 2.)

 

Since Plaintiff has complied with the requirements, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for default judgment in the amount of $12,504.22.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for default judgment.


Case Number: 23STCV15571    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: 73

6/04/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

JANET DIXON v. THE PEOPLE CONCERN (23STCV15571) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Janet Dixon (Pro Per) 

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Norman Ayotte (Jones & Ayotte) 

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO RECLASSIFY ACTION (filed 10/26/2023) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant’s Motion to Reclassify Action is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 5, 2023, Plaintiff Janet Dixon filed the instant action against Defendant The People Concern.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is currently a resident of Defendant The People Concern, which is a temporary housing shelter. (Compl., p. 1.) Plaintiff informed Defendant’s staff that she does not eat meat, such as beef, chicken or turkey. (Ibid.) On April 27, 2023, a member of Defendant’s staff served Plaintiff dinner that contained chicken. (Compl., pp. 1-2.) The staff told Plaintiff the chicken was vegan. (Compl., p. 2.) However, Plaintiff alleges the chicken was not vegan and that after eating the food, she became very ill. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges she was ill 3 days following the incident and it took her a day and a half to recover. (Ibid.) She alleges that she reported the issue to Defendant’s staff, but they still continue to serve her meat. (Ibid.)

 

On April 3, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File a Proposed Complaint. The Court ordered Plaintiff to file a stand-alone amended complaint by April 12, 2024.

 

On October 26, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion for Reclassify Action, arguing:

·       Defendant claims to have good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier. Defendant asserts that initial discovery needed to be conducted to assess Plaintiff’s claimed damages.

·       Plaintiff’s discovery answers show that her claimed damages fall below the $25,000 jurisdictional minimum.

 

On November 20, 2024, Defendant filed a reply stating that Plaintiff failed to file an opposition and that failure to file an opposition may be deemed a consent to the granting of a motion.

 

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff failed a late opposition, arguing:

·       Defendant violated sections of the Sherman Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Law which has statutory penalties. Thus, Plaintiff asserts Defendant is liable for statutory damages in the total amount of $55,000, not including Plaintiff’s other damages, including pain and suffering.

 

On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed another opposition labeled as a “Reply” to Defendant’s motion, arguing that if Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file First Amended Complaint is granted, Defendant’s instant motion would be void.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

“If a party files a motion for reclassification after the time for that party . . . to respond to a complaint, cross-complaint, or other initial pleading, the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both the following conditions are satisfied: [¶] (1) The case is incorrectly classified [, and] [¶] (2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §¿403.040, subd. (b).)¿

 

“A party seeking to reclassify a case from unlimited to limited faces a “high threshold.” (Ytuarte v. Superior Court¿(2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 266, 278.) The trial court must conclude “that the verdict will ‘necessarily’ fall short of the superior court jurisdictional requirement of a claim exceeding $25,000.” (Walker v. Superior Court¿(1991) 53 Cal.3d 257, 270 (Walker); Code Civ. Proc., §¿85, subd. (a) [amending statute to provide that a limited civil case is one in which the amount of controversy does not exceed $35,000].) The trial court may believe it highly unlikely that plaintiff will recover the amount demanded, but this is not enough to defeat jurisdiction, unless it appears to a legal certainty that plaintiff cannot recover the amount of the demand.  (Id. at 270.) 

 

“The¿unlikeliness¿of a judgment in excess of $25,000 is¿not the test.¿The trial court reviews the record to determine whether the result is obtainable. Simply stated, the trial court looks to the possibility of a jurisdictionally appropriate verdict, not to its probability.” (Maldonado v. Superior Court¿(1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 397, 402 (Maldonado); Hiona v. Superior Ct. of City & Cnty. of San Francisco (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 866, 872.)  Thus, the court evaluates the amount fairly in controversy; it does not adjudicate the merits of the claim. (Chahal v. Superior Court (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 399, 402 (Chahal).) 

 

B.      Timeliness of Plaintiff’s Opposition

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to timely file her opposition.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). 

 

Here, the hearing in this matter was originally set for November 29, 2023. Plaintiff’s opposition was due by November 15, 2024. However, Plaintiff did not file and serve her opposition until November 20, 2024. Defendant did not file a Reply responding to the arguments raised in Plaintiff’s late filed opposition. Thus, the Court, in its discretion, declines to consider arguments raised in Plaintiff’s late filed opposition.

 

C.      Discussion

Defendant moves to reclassify this action as a limited civil case on the ground that the amount in controversy cannot possibly exceed $25,000. Because Defendant has already answered the Complaint, Defendant must make two showings: (1) a showing of good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier and (2) a showing that the case is incorrectly classified.

a.       Good Cause

 

Defendant claims to have good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier. Defendant asserts that initial discovery needed to be conducted to assess Plaintiff’s claimed damages. Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s discovery answers demonstrate her claimed damages fall below the $25,000 requirement. (Motion, Ex. 2.) It appears that Defendant received Plaintiff’s discovery answers on September 21, 2023. Defendant filed this motion on October 26, 2023. Since Defendant filed this action promptly after receiving Plaintiff’s discovery answers, the Court finds Defendant has shown good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.

 

b.      Propriety of Classification

 

Next, the Court evaluates Defendant’s showing that this action is incorrectly classified.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s damages fall below the $25,000 jurisdictional minimum because her discovery answers revealed that she did not receive healthcare in connection with the incident and she did not identify any health care expenses. Defendant asserts Plaintiff also did not identify any loss of earnings as a result of the incident or any special damages incurred.

 

However, Defendant has not sufficiently shown that Plaintiff’s general damages would not meet the jurisdictional minimum. Plaintiff’s Complaint states the incident caused her to experience “terrible aches and pains throughout [her] body, diarrhea, intense migraines and nausea.” (Complaint, p. 2.) In addition, Plaintiff’s discovery answers state she has suffered emotional distress. (Motion, Ex. 2.) For example in response to Form Interrogatory 6.2, Plaintiff states that from the incident, she attributes “emotional distress, including but not limited to an increase in anxiety and paranoia.” (Ibid.) Also, in response to Form Interrogatory 6.1, Plaintiff states she had a “heightened sense of anxiety when receiving meals that the plaintiff heavily relied on at the time; causing the plaintiff to have emotional outbursts towards staff members that involved yelling, crying, and having panic attacks when alone when the plaintiff would receive plates that contained meat but were described to be vegan.” (Ibid.) These are factors that must be considered by the fact-finder in considering damages for pain, suffering, and emotional distress. Defendant has not presented any evidence or support that to a legal certainty, a fact-finder would find that Plaintiff’s pain, suffering and emotional distress damages would not exceed $25,000. At this stage, the Court is not evaluating the likeliness of a judgment in excess of $25,000, but whether the result is possible. (Maldonado, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at 402.)

 

Thus, Defendant’s motion to reclassify is denied without prejudice.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant’s motion to reclassify is DENIED without prejudice.
