DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV00814    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Ricardo Menjivar v. True Bullion LLC, et al; Martin Pike v. True Bullion LLC, et al; Ethan Michael v. True Bullion LLC, et al; Norman Menjivar v. True Bullion LLC, et al; and Cynthia Abrams v. True Bullion LLC, et al.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

21VECV00814;21VECV00816;

21VECV00817;21VECV00818;

21VECV01020                           107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Motion to Consolidate

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Grant

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

This motion relates to five cases: (1) Case No. 21VECV00814, Ricardo Menjivar v. True Bullion LLC, et al.; (2) Case No. 21VECV00816, Martin Pike v. True Bullion LLC, et al.; (3) Case No. 21VECV00817, Ethan Michael v. True Bullion LLC, et al.; (4) Case No. 21VECV00818, Norman Menjivar v. True Bullion LLC, et al.; and (5) Case No. 21VECV01020, Cynthia Abrams v. True Bullion LLC, et al. Plaintiffs Ricardo Menjivar, Martin Pike, Ethan Michael, Norman Menjivar, and Cynthia Abrams (“Plaintiffs”) were employed by Defendants GSI Exchange, Subculture Servies, and Anthony Anderson and filed complaints alleging various causes of action relating to their employment. (Ricardo Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9; Pike Complaint ¶ 9; Michael Complaint ¶ 9; Norman Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9; Abrams Complaint ¶ 9.)

 

On September 19, 2024, Defendants True Bullion LLC, Gold Silver International Exchange dba GSI Exchange, Subculture Services, LLC, Anthony Allen Anderson, and Agnes Viacrucis (“Defendants”) filed a motion in the Ricardo Menjivar case to consolidate the five cases (the “Motion”). On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff Ricardo Menjivar filed an opposition. On January 3, 2025, Defendants filed a reply.

 

On October 25, 2024, Defendants filed the motion to consolidate in the Martin Pike, Ethan Michael, Norman Menjivar, and Cynthia Abrams cases. On December 27, 2024, Plaintiffs Martin Pike, Ethan Michael, Norman Menjivar, and Cynthia Abrams filed oppositions. On January 3, 2025, Defendants filed replies.

 

The oppositions filed in the Martin Pike, Ethan Michael, Norman Menjivar, and Cynthia Abrams cases appear to be identical. The opposition filed in the Ricardo Menjivar case contains additional arguments about the procedural defects in the Motion. As to the arguments raised in the Martin Pike, Ethan Michael, Norman Menjivar, and Cynthia Abrams oppositions, this court will refer only to the Martin Pike opposition.  Defendants’ motions and replies similarly appear to be identical.

 

II.             DEFENDANTS’ REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants request this court take judicial notice of: (1) Plaintiff Ricardo Menjivar’s Complaint; (2) Plaintiff Martin Pike’s Complaint; (3) Plaintiff Ethan Michael’s Complaint; (4) Plaintiff Norman Menjivar’s Complaint; and (5) Plaintiff Cynthia Abrams’ Complaint. (Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

The court may take judicial notice of “Records of . . . any court of this state.” (See Evid. Code § 452(d).) Thus, this court grants Defendants’ requests.

 

III.           PLAINTIFF’S EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiffs submit three evidentiary objections to statements in Defendants’ counsel’s declaration.

(Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections pp. 2-3.) This court sustains Plaintiffs’ first and second

objections because the statements lack relevance. This court overrules Plaintiffs’ third objection.

 

IV.           PROCEDURAL ISSUES

(Id. at p. 979.)  The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he trial court correctly anticipated that a large portion of the trial would be devoted to issues common to the three cases” and “the parties were able to use the same witnesses . . . [and h]ad there been three separate trials the same deposition testimony would have been introduced, many of the same expert witnesses called and much of the same evidence presented.” (Id.) Also, consolidation will further judicial economy. If the cases are consolidated, they will require a single trial and jury, rather than five separate trials and juries. Further, the common witnesses and Defendants would attend only one trial.

Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 978.) Defendants cite Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust and note that “[t]he Todd-Sternberg Court rejected the argument the jury was confused by having three different plaintiffs in the consolidated cases and that jurors were unable to distinguish between them.” (Reply pp. 6-7.)

V.              LEGAL STANDARD

 

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048(a).)

 

There are two types of consolidation. (Hamilton v. Asbestos Corps., Ltd. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1127, 1147.)  First, the court may consolidate actions “for purposes of trial only, where the two actions remain otherwise separate.” (Id.) Alternatively, the court may consolidate cases for all purposes, “where the two actions are merged into a single proceeding under one case number and result in only one verdict or set of findings and one judgment.” (Id.)

 

The decision to consolidate rests within the sound discretion of the trial judge. (Fisher v. Nash Bldg. Co.¿(1952) 113 Cal.App.2d 397, 402.)¿ “[I]t is possible that actions may be thoroughly ‘related’ in the sense of having common questions of law or fact, and still not be ‘consolidated,’ if the trial court, in the sound exercise of its discretion, chooses not to do so.” (Askew v. Askew¿(1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 942, 964.)¿ It is not abuse of discretion to deny a motion for consolidation of actions where parties in each action are different or issues are different.¿ (See¿Muller v. Robinson¿(1959) 174 Cal App 2d 511, 515.)¿ On the other hand, actions may be consolidated in the discretion of the trial court “whenever it can be done without prejudice to a substantial right.”¿ (Carpenson¿v.¿Najarian¿(1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 856, 862.) 

 

In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should consider whether the cases have “common issues and whether undue confusion or prejudice [is] likely to result from the consolidation.”¿ (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust¿(1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 979.)

VII.         DISCUSSION

 

First, this court considers whether there are common issues. (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 978.)¿ In Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, the trial court consolidated six actions, but by the trial, only three actions remained. (Id. at p. 979.)  The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he trial court correctly anticipated that a large portion of the trial would be devoted to issues common to the three cases” and “the parties were able to use the same witnesses . . . [and h]ad there been three separate trials the same deposition testimony would have been introduced, many of the same expert witnesses called and much of the same evidence presented.” (Id.) The Court of Appeal concluded that “consolidation was appropriate.” (Id.)

 

Here, although each of the cases involves a different plaintiff, the cases share the same Defendants. Further, the cases allege causes of action related to labor-law violations against Defendants. This court anticipates that the parties will present much of the same evidence and the trials will require calling many of the same witnesses.

 

Plaintiffs note that the Ricardo Menjivar and Norman Menjivar cases allege causes of action for defamation, while the remaining three cases do not.  Moreover, each of the Plaintiffs were hired by Defendants at different times. The Ricardo Menjivar complaint alleges he was hired on August 22, 2018. (Ricardo Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9.) The Abrams complaint alleges Plaintiff Cynthia Abrams was hired starting May 2019. (Abrams Complaint ¶ 9.) The Pike complaint alleges Plaintiff Martin Pike was hired starting May 27, 2019. (Pike Complaint ¶ 9.) The Michael complaint alleges Plaintiff Ethan Michael was hired December 1, 2018. (Michael Complaint ¶ 9.) Finally, the Norman Menjivar complaint alleges Plaintiff Norman Menjivar was hired December 3, 2018. (Norman Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9.) Further, Plaintiff submits evidence showing Plaintiff Martin Pike received payments of hourly minimum wages, while Plaintiff Ricardo Menjivar did not receive hourly minimum wages. (Ex. 1; Ex. 2; Vardapour Declaration ¶¶ 2-3.) Although Plaintiffs correctly point out that these cases do not involve identical fact patterns, and there are some issues that are not common to all of the cases, it does not alter this court’s conclusion that the vast majority of the issues in the case involve similar issues.

 

Also, consolidation will further judicial economy. If the cases are consolidated, they will require a single trial and jury, rather than five separate trials and juries. Further, the common witnesses and Defendants would attend only one trial.

 

Finally, this court also considers whether “undue confusion or prejudice [is] likely to result from the consolidation.”¿ (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 978.) Defendants cite Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust and note that “[t]he Todd-Sternberg Court rejected the argument the jury was confused by having three different plaintiffs in the consolidated cases and that jurors were unable to distinguish between them.” (Reply pp. 6-7.)

 

These cases involve different plaintiffs, employment dates, and different facts, including how each plaintiff was, or was not, paid wages. And the tort causes of action in the Menjivar cases will involve different facts and law.  However, in Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 980, the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]here were only three plaintiffs[, t]he jury was given a separate binder of evidence and separate chronologies prepared on each plaintiff” and “[i]t is highly unlikely that the jury would have confused the cases.” Likewise, here, the parties can organize trial such that the jury will not be confused about the different Plaintiffs, employment dates, and facts in each case.

VIII.      CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants the Motion.

IV.           PROCEDURAL ISSUES

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.350 (“Rule 3.350”) sets forth the procedural requirements for a motion to consolidate. In pertinent part, the rule requires a notice of motion to consolidate to:

 

(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record;

 

(B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest numbered case shown first; and

 

(C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated.

(Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.350(a)(1).)

 

The motion to consolidate “[i]s deemed a single motion for the purpose of determining the

appropriate filing fee, but memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed

only in the lowest numbered case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(2)(A).) The motion to

consolidate must also be “served on all attorneys of record and all nonrepresented parties in all of

the cases sought to be consolidated” and “have a proof of service filed as part of the motion.” (Cal.

Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(2)(B)-(C).)

 

Defendants served the motion on all attorneys of record, and in all of the cases sought to be consolidated and Defendants filed proofs of service. The notices of motion have also been filed in each case sought to be consolidated and include the appropriate case numbers.

 

In the opposition filed in the Martin Pike case, Plaintiff Martin Pike argues “the Court lacks discretion and must deny Defendants’ motion in the Ricardo Menjivar case (21VECV00814) on procedural grounds” because “Defendants failed to comply with all statutory requirements when they filed their motion. After briefing closed, this court postponed the hearing. However, that did not remedy Defendant’s procedural shortcomings as of the filing date.” (Pike Opposition p. 2.)

 

Plaintiffs cite no authority for the proposition Defendants were barred from curing the procedural defects with their motion after this court continued the hearing on this Motion.

 

V.              LEGAL STANDARD

 

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048(a).)

 

There are two types of consolidation. (Hamilton v. Asbestos Corps., Ltd. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1127, 1147.)  First, the court may consolidate actions “for purposes of trial only, where the two actions remain otherwise separate.” (Id.) Alternatively, the court may consolidate cases for all purposes, “where the two actions are merged into a single proceeding under one case number and result in only one verdict or set of findings and one judgment.” (Id.)

 

The decision to consolidate rests within the sound discretion of the trial judge. (Fisher v. Nash Bldg. Co.¿(1952) 113 Cal.App.2d 397, 402.)¿ “[I]t is possible that actions may be thoroughly ‘related’ in the sense of having common questions of law or fact, and still not be ‘consolidated,’ if the trial court, in the sound exercise of its discretion, chooses not to do so.” (Askew v. Askew¿(1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 942, 964.)¿ It is not abuse of discretion to deny a motion for consolidation of actions where parties in each action are different or issues are different.¿ (See¿Muller v. Robinson¿(1959) 174 Cal App 2d 511, 515.)¿ On the other hand, actions may be consolidated in the discretion of the trial court “whenever it can be done without prejudice to a substantial right.”¿ (Carpenson¿v.¿Najarian¿(1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 856, 862.) 

 

In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should consider whether the cases have “common issues and whether undue confusion or prejudice [is] likely to result from the consolidation.”¿ (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust¿(1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 979.)

VII.         DISCUSSION

 

First, this court considers whether there are common issues. (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 978.)¿ In Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, the trial court consolidated six actions, but by the trial, only three actions remained. (Id. at p. 979.)  The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he trial court correctly anticipated that a large portion of the trial would be devoted to issues common to the three cases” and “the parties were able to use the same witnesses . . . [and h]ad there been three separate trials the same deposition testimony would have been introduced, many of the same expert witnesses called and much of the same evidence presented.” (Id.) The Court of Appeal concluded that “consolidation was appropriate.” (Id.)

 

Here, although each of the cases involves a different plaintiff, the cases share the same Defendants. Further, the cases allege causes of action related to labor-law violations against Defendants. This court anticipates that the parties will present much of the same evidence and the trials will require calling many of the same witnesses.

 

Plaintiffs note that the Ricardo Menjivar and Norman Menjivar cases allege causes of action for defamation, while the remaining three cases do not.  Moreover, each of the Plaintiffs were hired by Defendants at different times. The Ricardo Menjivar complaint alleges he was hired on August 22, 2018. (Ricardo Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9.) The Abrams complaint alleges Plaintiff Cynthia Abrams was hired starting May 2019. (Abrams Complaint ¶ 9.) The Pike complaint alleges Plaintiff Martin Pike was hired starting May 27, 2019. (Pike Complaint ¶ 9.) The Michael complaint alleges Plaintiff Ethan Michael was hired December 1, 2018. (Michael Complaint ¶ 9.) Finally, the Norman Menjivar complaint alleges Plaintiff Norman Menjivar was hired December 3, 2018. (Norman Menjivar Complaint ¶ 9.) Further, Plaintiff submits evidence showing Plaintiff Martin Pike received payments of hourly minimum wages, while Plaintiff Ricardo Menjivar did not receive hourly minimum wages. (Ex. 1; Ex. 2; Vardapour Declaration ¶¶ 2-3.) Although Plaintiffs correctly point out that these cases do not involve identical fact patterns, and there are some issues that are not common to all of the cases, it does not alter this court’s conclusion that the vast majority of the issues in the case involve similar issues.

 

Also, consolidation will further judicial economy. If the cases are consolidated, they will require a single trial and jury, rather than five separate trials and juries. Further, the common witnesses and Defendants would attend only one trial.

 

Finally, this court also considers whether “undue confusion or prejudice [is] likely to result from the consolidation.”¿ (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 978.) Defendants cite Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust and note that “[t]he Todd-Sternberg Court rejected the argument the jury was confused by having three different plaintiffs in the consolidated cases and that jurors were unable to distinguish between them.” (Reply pp. 6-7.)

 

These cases involve different plaintiffs, employment dates, and different facts, including how each plaintiff was, or was not, paid wages. And the tort causes of action in the Menjivar cases will involve different facts and law.  However, in Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 980, the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]here were only three plaintiffs[, t]he jury was given a separate binder of evidence and separate chronologies prepared on each plaintiff” and “[i]t is highly unlikely that the jury would have confused the cases.” Likewise, here, the parties can organize trial such that the jury will not be confused about the different Plaintiffs, employment dates, and facts in each case.

VIII.      CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants the Motion.

Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)¿¿¿

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

IV.           DEMURRER

 

  1. Legal Standard

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

A party may demur to a cross-complaint because the cross-complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)¿ A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  

¿ 

Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)¿ 

 

  1. Discussion

 

  1. Assault

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for assault and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.)

 

The elements of an assault cause of action are: “(1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant's conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff's harm.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Fredrick Petrosian “intentionally acted with the intent to cause harmful contact approached Plaintiff and violently assaulted hi[m] at the [S]ubject [P]remises.” (FAC ¶ 13.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendant Petrosian “aggressively intentionally approached the Plaintiff, leaning forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner and intentionally threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner[.]” (FAC ¶ 15.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant Petrosian acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact or threatened to touch Plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner.

 

Plaintiff also alleges that “it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat.” (FAC ¶ 15.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff reasonably believed Defendant was about to carry out the threat.

 

Plaintiff alleges “Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct as Plaintiff reasonably believed that he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner by Defendant [Petrosian].” (FAC ¶ 16.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant’s conduct.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct the Plaintiff was directly physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action.” (FAC ¶ 17.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct the Plaintiff was directly physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action” and Defendant Petrosian’s “threatening and aggressive behavior was a substantial factor in Plaintiff’s fear for Plaintiff’s reasonable physical person and his sense of personal dignity.” (FAC ¶¶ 17-18.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed and Defendant Petrosian’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm.

 

Defendants argue that the allegations referenced above are insufficient because they are conclusory. (Demurrer p. 8.) As a general matter, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.)¿The allegations discussed above are sufficient to meet this standard.

 

Defendants also argue the assault cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 12-19.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s assault cause of action is not uncertain. 

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for assault.

 

  1. Battery

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for battery and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.)

 

The elements of a battery cause of action are: “(1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching.” (So v. Shin, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 669.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “On or about August 29, 2022 . . . Defendant [Petrosian] intentionally and with the intent to harm, forcefully and physically attacked Plaintiff, stabbing him with a sharp object; Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages.” (FAC ¶ 21.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant Petrosian touched Plaintiff, or caused Plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff also alleges that “Plaintiff did not consent to Defendants’ conduct of being stabbed by a sharp object.” (FAC ¶ 22.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff did not consent to the touching.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages” and “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct, the Plaintiff was physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action.” (FAC ¶¶ 21, 23.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed by Defendant Petrosian’s conduct.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “Reasonable persons in Plaintiff’s situation would have been offended by the manner in which Defendant [Petrosian] touched Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 24.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.

 

Defendants argue the allegations referenced above are insufficient because they are conclusory, “generalized and devoid of statements or context”, and “Plaintiff neglects to specify what ‘object’ he is referring to.” (Demurrer pp. 10-11.) Defendant cites no authority for the argument Plaintiff must allege statements, context, or what “object” Plaintiff is referring to. Moreover, as a general matter, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.)¿The allegations discussed above are sufficient to meet this standard.

 

Defendants also argue the battery cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 20-25.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s battery cause of action is not uncertain. 

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for battery.

 

  1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

¿“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress.” (So v. Shin, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 671.)  To be outrageous, the conduct must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant [Petrosian] acted with the intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress when Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages” and “Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct as set forth above was so outrageous as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated by a civilized community.” (FAC ¶¶ 27-28.) At the demurrer stage, this court cannot conclude that, as a matter of law, alleging a defendant stabbed a plaintiff is insufficient to allege the defendant’s behavior was extreme and outrageous. Accordingly, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges Defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, extreme emotional distress.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff has suffered severe and extreme emotional distress including, but not limited to, highly unpleasant mental suffering and anguish that entails such intense, enduring and nontrivial emotional distress that no reasonable person in a civilized society would be expected to endure.” (FAC ¶ 29.)  Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff suffered extreme or severe emotional distress.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] socially unacceptable conduct set forth above the Plaintiff has suffered severe and extreme emotional distress” and “Defendant [Petrosian’s] outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s emotional distress.” (FAC ¶¶ 29-30.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s conduct was the actual and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s extreme or severe emotional distress.

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory, Plaintiff “fails to state the context for the incident, as recited in the statement of facts” and “Plaintiff does not indicate statements made by himself or Defendant Petrosian or identify specific actions taken but alleges generalities to identify broad corners of conduct.” (Demurrer p. 12.) The allegations referenced above are sufficiently specific to allege a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

 

Defendants also argue the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 26-31.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action is not uncertain.

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

  1. Negligence

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for negligence and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

The elements of a negligence cause of action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.)

Plaintiff alleges “Defendants, and each of them, had an affirmative legal duty to use due care for the protection of Plaintiff against unreasonable risk of harm” and “Defendants, and each of them, formed a special relationship between the employees/owners and subject premises resulting in the affirmative duty on the employees/owners to take all reasonable steps to protect the occupants of the subject premises.” (FAC ¶¶ 33-34.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center, SBC Investment, and Does 1 to 100 “had a duty to control Defendant [Petrosian] and protect Plaintiff from physical and mental harm.”  (FAC ¶ 35.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendants had a legal duty to use care.

Plaintiff also alleges “Defendants, and each of them, breached their duty of care for the protection of Plaintiff when Defendant [Petrosian] in the presence of others, aggressively approached Plaintiff on the [S]ubject [P]remises, leaned forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner such that it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat; Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages due to the stabbing by the sharp object.” (FAC ¶ 36.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached their legal duty of care.

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a direct result of the breach of their affirmative duty to protect Plaintiff from physical or psychological injury while at the subject premises, the malfeasance and/or nonfeasance of Defendants, and each of them, was the proximate or legal cause of Plaintiff’s injuries”, “The conduct of Defendants, and each of them, was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm” and that Plaintiff suffered harm because of Defendant’s conduct. (FAC ¶¶ 37-39.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.

Defendants argue the allegations referenced above are insufficient because Plaintiff fails to allege facts, “Plaintiff does not sufficiently state the nature of damages with specificity”, “fails to state the context for the incident”, “does not indicate statements made by himself or Defendant Petrosian or identify specific actions taken” “does not specify how the alleged assault or battery occurred with any descriptive elements, nor make any indication of how intent was derived from the alleged act” but “merely supplies conclusory assertions to fulfil[l] elements of the cause of action, failing to meet the standard of pleading[.]” (Demurrer p. 13.) A “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.) The allegations referenced above are sufficient to meet this standard.

Defendants also argue the negligence cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 32-39.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action is not uncertain.

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for negligence.

 

V.              MOTION TO STRIKE

 

  1. Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿¿¿¿ 

 

  1. Discussion

 

  1. Punitive Damages

Defendants move to strike the request for punitive damages. (Motion to Strike p. 2.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.)¿¿ 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) To justify a punitive damages award, the defendant must be “guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”¿(Civ. Code., § 3294.)

 

Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c)(2).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).)¿ 

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And “’conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿

 

Plaintiff seeks punitive damages for the assault, battery, and intentional infliction of emotional distress causes of action. (FAC pp. 1, 7-8.) These causes of action are based on the allegations Defendant Petrosian intentionally “stabb[ed] Plaintiff’s arm with a sharp object” and “aggressively intentionally approached the Plaintiff, leaning forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner and intentionally threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner such that it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat.” (FAC ¶¶ 8, 15.) These allegations indicate Defendant Petrosian acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety” of Plaintiff. (See Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Thus, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges Defendant Petrosian acted with malice.

 

  1. Special and General Damages

 

Defendants move to strike the special and general damages to all causes of action. (Motion to Strike p. 2.) Defendants argue that the court should strike the portions of the first amended complaint seeking special damages because “the evidence presented fails to establish a clear and direct link between the Defendants’ alleged conduct and the alleged financial losses.” (Motion to Strike p. 6.) Defendants also argue that the court should strike the portions of the first amended complaint seeking general damages because “Plaintiff does not articulate any justification for general damages, and Plaintiff’s prayer is uncertain.” (Motion to Strike p. 7.)

 

“[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transport Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1192-1193.) Plaintiff’s first amended complaint sufficiently alleges causes of action for battery, assault, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. Plaintiff also alleges a causal connection between the alleged conduct and damages. (See FAC ¶¶ 17, 23, 30-31, 36-37.)  At this stage, this court assumes the truth of these allegations. Thus, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges a causal connection between the conduct and damages.

 

  1. Defendant Claude’s Repair Center

 

Defendants move to strike the fourth cause of action as to Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center because “Defendant Petrosian is the sole proprietor of the business” and “Defendant Petrosian’s business has no supervisory role over him or duty to control him as is alleged in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.” (Motion to Strike pp. 2-3, 8.)

 

Plaintiff does not appear to allege that Defendant Petrosian is the sole proprietor of Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center or that Claude’s Auto Service Center had no supervisory role or duty to control Defendant Petrosian. The only reference to a sole proprietor is found in the first amended complaint is in paragraph 3, that states: “Plaintiff is informed and believes, and based upon such information and belief alleges that at all times relevant hereto Defendants FREDRICK PETROSIAN, an individual; and DOES 1-100, inclusive, are, and at all times herein mentioned were individuals, corporations, sole proprietors, shareholders, associations, partners and partnerships, joint ventures, and/or business entities unknown, primarily residing and doing business in the County of Los Angeles, State of California.” Defendant has not requested the court take judicial notice of anything showing Defendant Claude’s Auto Repair Service is a sole proprietor.

 

In contrast, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center is a “corporation[] organized and existing under and by virtue of the laws of the State of California with its principal place of business in the County of Los Angeles, California.” (FAC ¶ 2.) Plaintiff also specifically alleges that Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center “had a duty to control Defendant [Petrosian] and protect Plaintiff from physical and mental harm.” (FAC ¶ 35.)

 

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿ “[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transport Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1192-1193.) ¿¿Plaintiff has alleged Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center had a duty to control Defendant Petrosian. (FAC ¶ 35.) This court assumes that factual allegation is true.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court overrules the demurrer and denies the motion to strike.

(First Amended Complaint “FAC” ¶¶ 7-8.) “Plaintiff had previously had work done on his vehicle and returned to the [S]ubject [P]remises with questions and/or concerns with the previously completed work.” (FAC ¶ 8.) Proc., §¿430.40(a).)¿Per California Code of Civil Procedure section¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)¿¿¿

IV.           DEMURRER

 

  1. Legal Standard

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

A party may demur to a cross-complaint because the cross-complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)¿ A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  

¿ 

Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)¿ 

 

  1. Discussion

 

  1. Assault

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for assault and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.)

 

The elements of an assault cause of action are: “(1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant's conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff's harm.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Fredrick Petrosian “intentionally acted with the intent to cause harmful contact approached Plaintiff and violently assaulted hi[m] at the [S]ubject [P]remises.” (FAC ¶ 13.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendant Petrosian “aggressively intentionally approached the Plaintiff, leaning forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner and intentionally threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner[.]” (FAC ¶ 15.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant Petrosian acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact or threatened to touch Plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner.

 

Plaintiff also alleges that “it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat.” (FAC ¶ 15.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff reasonably believed Defendant was about to carry out the threat.

 

Plaintiff alleges “Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct as Plaintiff reasonably believed that he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner by Defendant [Petrosian].” (FAC ¶ 16.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff did not consent to Defendant’s conduct.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct the Plaintiff was directly physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action.” (FAC ¶ 17.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct the Plaintiff was directly physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action” and Defendant Petrosian’s “threatening and aggressive behavior was a substantial factor in Plaintiff’s fear for Plaintiff’s reasonable physical person and his sense of personal dignity.” (FAC ¶¶ 17-18.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed and Defendant Petrosian’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm.

 

Defendants argue that the allegations referenced above are insufficient because they are conclusory. (Demurrer p. 8.) As a general matter, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.)¿The allegations discussed above are sufficient to meet this standard.

 

Defendants also argue the assault cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 12-19.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s assault cause of action is not uncertain. 

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for assault.

 

  1. Battery

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for battery and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.)

 

The elements of a battery cause of action are: “(1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching.” (So v. Shin, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 669.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “On or about August 29, 2022 . . . Defendant [Petrosian] intentionally and with the intent to harm, forcefully and physically attacked Plaintiff, stabbing him with a sharp object; Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages.” (FAC ¶ 21.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant Petrosian touched Plaintiff, or caused Plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff also alleges that “Plaintiff did not consent to Defendants’ conduct of being stabbed by a sharp object.” (FAC ¶ 22.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff did not consent to the touching.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages” and “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct, the Plaintiff was physically and psychologically damaged, incurred medical bills, sustained disability and had to retain an attorney and has incurred legal costs in connection therewith in order to prosecute this action.” (FAC ¶¶ 21, 23.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff was harmed by Defendant Petrosian’s conduct.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “Reasonable persons in Plaintiff’s situation would have been offended by the manner in which Defendant [Petrosian] touched Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 24.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.

 

Defendants argue the allegations referenced above are insufficient because they are conclusory, “generalized and devoid of statements or context”, and “Plaintiff neglects to specify what ‘object’ he is referring to.” (Demurrer pp. 10-11.) Defendant cites no authority for the argument Plaintiff must allege statements, context, or what “object” Plaintiff is referring to. Moreover, as a general matter, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.)¿The allegations discussed above are sufficient to meet this standard.

 

Defendants also argue the battery cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 2.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 20-25.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s battery cause of action is not uncertain. 

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for battery.

 

  1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

¿“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress.” (So v. Shin, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 671.)  To be outrageous, the conduct must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.” (Huntingdon Life Sciences, Inc. v. Stop Huntingdon Animal Cruelty USA, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1259.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant [Petrosian] acted with the intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress when Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages” and “Defendant [Petrosian’s] conduct as set forth above was so outrageous as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated by a civilized community.” (FAC ¶¶ 27-28.) At the demurrer stage, this court cannot conclude that, as a matter of law, alleging a defendant stabbed a plaintiff is insufficient to allege the defendant’s behavior was extreme and outrageous. Accordingly, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges Defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, extreme emotional distress.

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff has suffered severe and extreme emotional distress including, but not limited to, highly unpleasant mental suffering and anguish that entails such intense, enduring and nontrivial emotional distress that no reasonable person in a civilized society would be expected to endure.” (FAC ¶ 29.)  Thus, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff suffered extreme or severe emotional distress.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a result of Defendant [Petrosian’s] socially unacceptable conduct set forth above the Plaintiff has suffered severe and extreme emotional distress” and “Defendant [Petrosian’s] outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s emotional distress.” (FAC ¶¶ 29-30.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s conduct was the actual and proximate cause of Plaintiff’s extreme or severe emotional distress.

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory, Plaintiff “fails to state the context for the incident, as recited in the statement of facts” and “Plaintiff does not indicate statements made by himself or Defendant Petrosian or identify specific actions taken but alleges generalities to identify broad corners of conduct.” (Demurrer p. 12.) The allegations referenced above are sufficiently specific to allege a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

 

Defendants also argue the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 26-31.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action is not uncertain.

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

  1. Negligence

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for negligence and that the pleading is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.)

 

The elements of a negligence cause of action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.)

Plaintiff alleges “Defendants, and each of them, had an affirmative legal duty to use due care for the protection of Plaintiff against unreasonable risk of harm” and “Defendants, and each of them, formed a special relationship between the employees/owners and subject premises resulting in the affirmative duty on the employees/owners to take all reasonable steps to protect the occupants of the subject premises.” (FAC ¶¶ 33-34.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center, SBC Investment, and Does 1 to 100 “had a duty to control Defendant [Petrosian] and protect Plaintiff from physical and mental harm.”  (FAC ¶ 35.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendants had a legal duty to use care.

Plaintiff also alleges “Defendants, and each of them, breached their duty of care for the protection of Plaintiff when Defendant [Petrosian] in the presence of others, aggressively approached Plaintiff on the [S]ubject [P]remises, leaned forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner such that it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat; Defendant [Petrosian] physically attacked and assaulted Plaintiff with such force that Plaintiff suffered injuries and damages due to the stabbing by the sharp object.” (FAC ¶ 36.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached their legal duty of care.

Finally, Plaintiff alleges that “As a direct result of the breach of their affirmative duty to protect Plaintiff from physical or psychological injury while at the subject premises, the malfeasance and/or nonfeasance of Defendants, and each of them, was the proximate or legal cause of Plaintiff’s injuries”, “The conduct of Defendants, and each of them, was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm” and that Plaintiff suffered harm because of Defendant’s conduct. (FAC ¶¶ 37-39.) Thus, Plaintiff alleges the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.

Defendants argue the allegations referenced above are insufficient because Plaintiff fails to allege facts, “Plaintiff does not sufficiently state the nature of damages with specificity”, “fails to state the context for the incident”, “does not indicate statements made by himself or Defendant Petrosian or identify specific actions taken” “does not specify how the alleged assault or battery occurred with any descriptive elements, nor make any indication of how intent was derived from the alleged act” but “merely supplies conclusory assertions to fulfil[l] elements of the cause of action, failing to meet the standard of pleading[.]” (Demurrer p. 13.) A “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 550.) The allegations referenced above are sufficient to meet this standard.

Defendants also argue the negligence cause of action is uncertain. (Demurrer p. 3.) The first amended complaint is sufficiently detailed to acquaint Defendants with the nature, source, and extent of Plaintiff’s cause of action. (See FAC ¶¶ 32-39.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action is not uncertain.

 

Thus, this court overrules the demurrer on the ground the first amended complaint fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for negligence.

 

V.              MOTION TO STRIKE

 

  1. Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿¿¿¿ 

 

  1. Discussion

 

  1. Punitive Damages

Defendants move to strike the request for punitive damages. (Motion to Strike p. 2.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.)¿¿ 

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) To justify a punitive damages award, the defendant must be “guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”¿(Civ. Code., § 3294.)

 

Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(c)(2).) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).)¿ 

 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And “’conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,’ within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿

 

Plaintiff seeks punitive damages for the assault, battery, and intentional infliction of emotional distress causes of action. (FAC pp. 1, 7-8.) These causes of action are based on the allegations Defendant Petrosian intentionally “stabb[ed] Plaintiff’s arm with a sharp object” and “aggressively intentionally approached the Plaintiff, leaning forward and getting within inches of his person, and in a loud bellicose manner and intentionally threatened to touch and/or grab Plaintiff in a harmful manner such that it reasonably appeared that Defendant [Petrosian] was about to carry out the threat.” (FAC ¶¶ 8, 15.) These allegations indicate Defendant Petrosian acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety” of Plaintiff. (See Civ. Code § 3294(c)(1).) Thus, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges Defendant Petrosian acted with malice.

 

  1. Special and General Damages

 

Defendants move to strike the special and general damages to all causes of action. (Motion to Strike p. 2.) Defendants argue that the court should strike the portions of the first amended complaint seeking special damages because “the evidence presented fails to establish a clear and direct link between the Defendants’ alleged conduct and the alleged financial losses.” (Motion to Strike p. 6.) Defendants also argue that the court should strike the portions of the first amended complaint seeking general damages because “Plaintiff does not articulate any justification for general damages, and Plaintiff’s prayer is uncertain.” (Motion to Strike p. 7.)

 

“[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transport Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1192-1193.) Plaintiff’s first amended complaint sufficiently alleges causes of action for battery, assault, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. Plaintiff also alleges a causal connection between the alleged conduct and damages. (See FAC ¶¶ 17, 23, 30-31, 36-37.)  At this stage, this court assumes the truth of these allegations. Thus, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges a causal connection between the conduct and damages.

 

  1. Defendant Claude’s Repair Center

 

Defendants move to strike the fourth cause of action as to Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center because “Defendant Petrosian is the sole proprietor of the business” and “Defendant Petrosian’s business has no supervisory role over him or duty to control him as is alleged in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.” (Motion to Strike pp. 2-3, 8.)

 

Plaintiff does not appear to allege that Defendant Petrosian is the sole proprietor of Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center or that Claude’s Auto Service Center had no supervisory role or duty to control Defendant Petrosian. The only reference to a sole proprietor is found in the first amended complaint is in paragraph 3, that states: “Plaintiff is informed and believes, and based upon such information and belief alleges that at all times relevant hereto Defendants FREDRICK PETROSIAN, an individual; and DOES 1-100, inclusive, are, and at all times herein mentioned were individuals, corporations, sole proprietors, shareholders, associations, partners and partnerships, joint ventures, and/or business entities unknown, primarily residing and doing business in the County of Los Angeles, State of California.” Defendant has not requested the court take judicial notice of anything showing Defendant Claude’s Auto Repair Service is a sole proprietor.

 

In contrast, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center is a “corporation[] organized and existing under and by virtue of the laws of the State of California with its principal place of business in the County of Los Angeles, California.” (FAC ¶ 2.) Plaintiff also specifically alleges that Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center “had a duty to control Defendant [Petrosian] and protect Plaintiff from physical and mental harm.” (FAC ¶ 35.)

 

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437(a).)¿ “[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transport Authority, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1192-1193.) ¿¿Plaintiff has alleged Defendant Claude’s Auto Service Center had a duty to control Defendant Petrosian. (FAC ¶ 35.) This court assumes that factual allegation is true.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court overrules the demurrer and denies the motion to strike.
