DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV04020    Hearing Date: December 2, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint and Motion to Strike

 

Moving Parties: Defendants UHG LAX PROP, LLC and TWENTY4SEVEN HOTELS   CORPORATION    

Responding Party: Plaintiff Martin Smith

 

RULING

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the causes of action for Breach of Contract and IIED, and the motion to strike is GRANTED as to attorney’s fees and DENIED as to punitive damages.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On December 4, 2023, Plaintiff Martin Smith (“Smith”) filed a complaint against Defendant UHG LAX PROP, LLC (“UHG”) alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence – Premises Liability/Failure to Warn/Negligence Per Se; (2) Nuisance; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”); (4) Breach of Contract; and (5) Fraudulent Concealment.

 

On January 5, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer to the complaint, which was sustained in part, and a motion to strike, which was granted.

 

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the complaint, naming Doe 1 as Twenty4Seven Hotels Corporation (“Twenty4Seven”).

 

On September 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence – Premises Liability/Failure to Warn/Negligence Per Se; (2) IIED; (3) Breach of Contract; and (4) Fraudulent Concealment. UHG and Twenty4Seven (“Defendants”) filed the instant motions on October 14, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 15, 2024. Defendants filed a reply on November 21, 2024.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff filed this FAC against Defendants for their failure to properly maintain Plaintiff’s rented hotel room, leading to a bed bug infestation. (FAC, ¶ 2.)

 

On January 8, 2023, Plaintiff entered into a hotel rental agreement with Defendants to stay as a guest at the Subject Property. (FAC, ¶ 27.) Plaintiff noticed the bed bug infestation while staying as a guest in Room 234 and notified management, but no action was taken. (FAC, ¶ 28.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants had prior notice of the bed bug infestation as many guests have left negative reviews about the bed bugs and various review platforms. (FAC, ¶ 72.) Further, Defendants were aware of the ongoing infestation because Ecolab Pest Control Records show that they provided services at the Subject Property, including Room 234, at various times from 2021-2023. (FAC, ¶ 73.)

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to provide sufficient factual allegations to support each cause of action, and Plaintiff has not made any substantive changes between the FAC and the original complaint. Defendant further demurs on the basis that the complaint is fatally uncertain.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

Plaintiff argues that he has adequately alleged each cause of action.

 

Reply Argument

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff has still failed to allege sufficient facts. Further, Plaintiff fails to plead fraud with particularity.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) A motion to strike is subject to a similar meet and confer requirement.

 

Defense counsel declares that he sent several requests to meet and confer, but Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. The court finds that the parties have not satisfied the meet and confer requirements. However, the court will still consider the merits of the motion.

 

B.  Second Cause of Action for IIED

 

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.)

 

In addition, “it is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1002, citing Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.)

 

Plaintiff does not allege that any of Defendants’ conduct was directed at Plaintiff nor done in his presence. Plaintiff also cites to Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903 in support, but the court finds that this case is distinguishable. In Stoiber, there was a landlord-tenant relationship between the parties. (Id. at p. 911.) The tenants notified the landlord many times over the years of their tenancy that the property was in poor condition, but the landlord did not attempt to make repairs. (Id. at p. 911-912.) The Stoiber court found that the repeated failure to repair could sustain an IIED cause of action. (Id. at p. 922.)

 

At most, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants were negligent in the maintenance of the Subject Property, but Plaintiff does not provide factual allegations that Defendants directed their conduct at Plaintiff in particular. In fact, Plaintiff alleges that Ecolab Pest Control visited the Subject Property multiple times, which would indicate that Defendants took steps to eradicate or prevent an infestation.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

C.  Third Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) 

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges the parties “entered into a valid hotel rental agreement, setting forth terms surrounding Plaintiff’s stay at the Premises.” (FAC, ¶ 90.) Plaintiff does not actually allege any terms that the parties agreed to. He merely attaches an invoice and alleges that Defendants breached several implied terms by failing to keep the Premises free of bed bugs.

 

As pleaded, these allegations are insufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract. Further, Plaintiff has made no substantive changes to the allegations supporting this cause of action between the original complaint and the FAC.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

D.  Fourth Cause of Action for Fraudulent Concealment

 

“The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 189 Cal.Rptr.3d 31, at 36, citing Graham v. Bank of America, N.A., supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 606, 172 Cal.Rptr.3d 218.) “A duty to speak may arise in four ways: it may be directly imposed by statute or other prescriptive law; it may be voluntarily assumed by contractual undertaking; it may arise as an incident of a relationship between the defendant and the plaintiff; and it may arise as a result of other conduct by the defendant that makes it wrongful for him to remain silent.” SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Central Pacific Bank (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 859, 860. 

 

A concealment cause of action has a more relaxed specificity requirement as compared to affirmative misrepresentations because a concealment claim does not assert that Defendant actively or affirmatively defrauded the public in general of Plaintiff in particular. The rule of specifically pleading how, when, where, to whom and by what means, misrepresentations were communicated is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations and not to concealment. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356 at 1384.) 

 

As the court noted in its prior ruling, the mere existence of online reviews does not provide a sufficient basis for imputing knowledge. In the FAC, Plaintiff also provides evidence of Ecolab reports, which show that Ecolab visited the property “for bedbug complaints” at various time over two years prior to Plaintiff’s stay, including in rooms nearby Room 234 a few weeks prior to Plaintiff’s visit. Defendants argue in opposition that these reports do not show that there was an active infestation, as Plaintiff does not allege the purpose, findings, or substance of these alleged visits. Plaintiff merely concludes that any visit from Ecolab is an admission that there was an active infestation.

 

The court finds that Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to show that Defendants had prior knowledge of an infestation.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

E.  Uncertainty

 

A demurrer to a pleading lies where the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616, disapproved on other grounds in Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guar. Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 46 [holding claims for unfair business practices need not be pled specifically, impliedly disapproving Khoury].) As a result, a special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed only at uncertainty existing in the allegations already made. (People v. Taliaferro (1957) 149 Cal.App.2d 822, 825, disapproved on other grounds in Jefferson v. J.E. French Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 717, 719-720 [statute of limitations question].) 

 

Where complaint is sufficient to state a cause of action and to apprise defendant of issues he is to meet, it is not properly subject to a special demurrer for uncertainty. (See ibid.; see also Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643 [“A special demurrer [for uncertainty] should be overruled where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet”].) 

 

Considering Defendants raised substantive responses to Plaintiff’s complaint, the court finds that the FAC is not “so bad” that Defendants were not apprised of the issues they were to meet.

 

Accordingly, the special demurrer for uncertainty is OVERRULED.

 

F.  Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 

436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

 

Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees and punitive damages, and portions of the FAC which make conclusory allegations that Defendants acted willfully, wantonly, or maliciously. Defendants contend that there is no contractual or statutory basis to award attorney’s fees, and Plaintiff has failed to properly plead oppressive, malicious, or fraudulent conduct.

 

Plaintiff has not adequately alleged the existence of a contract, and thus, there is no basis to request attorney’s fees. Plaintiff also does not cite to any statute which would support her request. Thus, the court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees.

 

However, as discussed above, Plaintiff has provided sufficient facts showing that Defendants had notice of the infestation. Accordingly, Plaintiff has provided sufficient facts showing that Defendants’ conduct was “fraudulent.” Thus, the court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages.

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

 

G.  Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff has not made substantive changes to the breach of contract cause of action. Therefore, the court assumes that there are no other facts relevant to a cause of action for contract which can be pled.  Nor has plaintiff provided any additional facts to suggest that the Defendants targeted Plaintiff sufficient to show a valid claim for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. Therefore, the court DENIES leave to amend as to the causes of action for breach of contract and IIED.

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the court rules as follows: Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED in its part and OVERRULED in part. The motion to strike is GRANTED as to attorney’s fees and DENIED as to punitive damages. Leave to amend is DENIED as to breach of contract and IIED claims.

 

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
