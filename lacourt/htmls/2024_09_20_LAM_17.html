DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV20504    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CLAUDIA MARIA NUNEZ

 

         vs.

 

FCA US LLC     

 

 Case No.:  23STCV20504

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 20, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motion is MOOT. Defendant is sanctioned, jointly and severally with counsel, $350.00 payable within 30 days.

 

            On 8/25/2024, Plaintiff Claudia Maria Nunez (Plaintiff) filed suit against FCA US LLC (Defendant), alleging: (1) breach of express warranty; (2) breach of implied warranty; (3) violation of Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2; (4) violation of Tanner Consumer Protection Act.

 

            On 6/21/2024, Plaintiff moved to have its Requests for Admission (RFAs) (Set One) deemed admitted as to Defendant.

 

Discussion

 

            On 11/17/2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with RFAs (Set One). Despite responses being due on or before 12/22/2023, no such responses were received.

 

            In opposition, Defendant indicated that responses were served on 9/4/2024. Accordingly, the substance off Plaintiff’s motion is moot.

 

Defendant also contended that its failure to timely respond was due to excusable mistake: “Amidst the exponential increase in Song-Beverly matters, a substantial increase in emails contributed to the oversight. While there are protocols in place to try to avoid such errors, this is the type of excusable human error can occasionally occur, especially in light of the overwhelming increase in emails related to the unlimited growth of volume in these matters. As a result, the error in failing to provide timely responses went unnoticed and the deadline lapsed.” (Opp., 5: 12-16.)

 

However, if this were sufficient to meet the substantial justification standard for sanctions, sanctions would only be appropriate for bad faith failures to respond. Here, Defendant failed to timely respond by nearly nine months, and only provided responses after this motion was filed. Accordingly, sanctions are appropriate.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is moot. Defendant is sanctioned, jointly and severally with counsel, $350.00. ($350/hr x 1 hr.)

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  September    , 2024

                                                                                                                                                          

   Hon. Jon R. Takasugi
   Judge of the Superior Court

Case Number: 23STCV25626    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MURAL MEDIA, LLC 401 K PLAN

 

         vs.

 

LINDA J. MAULTSBY, et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV25626  

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 20, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motions to compel discovery are GRANTED. Defendant is sanctioned $350 per motion. Defendant’s motion for a protective order is DENIED.

 

On 10/20/2023, Plaintiff Mural Media, LLC 401 K. Plan (Plaintiff) filed an unlawful detainer action against Linda J. Maultsby (Defendant).

 

Now, Plaintiff moves to compel responses to six sets of discovery. Plaintiff seeks a protective order.

 

For ease, the Court has consolidated its analysis into a single ruling.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moved to compel responses from Defendant Linda Maultsby to the following sets of discovery: (1) Special Interrogatories (Set One); (2) Requests for Production (Set One); (3) Form Interrogatories—Unlawful detainer (Set One); (4) Requests for Admission (RFAs) (Set One); (5) Form Interrogatories—General (Set One); and (6) Demand for Inspection (Set One).

 

            Plaintiff served all of the discovery in question on Defendant on 7/26/2024. To date, no responses have been provided, and did Defendant oppose any of Plaintiff’s motions.

 

            In a separate motion, Defendant moved for a protective order from Plaintiff’s discovery, arguing that Plaintiff lacks standing to sue. However, as noted in the Court’s ruling on Defendant’s demurrer, this contention is without merit. Accordingly, the Court finds no good cause to grant Defendant’s request for a protective order.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motions to compel discovery are granted. Defendant is sanctioned $350 per motion. ($350/hr x 1 hr.) Defendant’s motion for a protective order is denied.

Superior Court of California

County of Los

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 









VIG PRIVATE LENDING


 


        
vs.


 


ILAN KENIG



 

 Case
No.:  24STCV05062  


 


 


 


 Hearing Date:  September 23, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s
motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

 

On 2/29/2024,
Plaintiff VIG Private Lending, Inc (Plaintiff) filed an unlawful detainer
action against Defendant Avivit Kenig (Defendant). On 4/24/2024, Plaintiff
filed a first amended unlawful detainer complaint. (FAC).

 

            On
8/30/2024, Plaintiff moves for summary judgment, or, in the alternative,
summary adjudication of its Complaint.

 

            The
motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff
moves for summary judgment, or alternatively, summary adjudication, of the
following issues:

 

1.     
Plaintiff is the owner of the property
at 1355 Berea Place, Pacific Palisades, CA 90272, County of Los Angeles (the
“Property”);

 

2.     
Plaintiff properly served Defendants
with a valid notice to quit on March 12, 2024; and

 

3.     
The daily fair market value of the
property at the Property is at least $1,000.00.

 

Plaintiff
argues that it is entitled to judgment because there are no materials facts in
dispute. More specifically, Plaintiff argues that neither title, service of
Notice, market value, or affirmative defenses are in dispute here.

 

As for title,
where, as here, the unlawful detainer action is brought under CCP section
1161a, a plaintiff must prove his acquisitions of title by purchase at the
sale. Under CCP § 1161a, unlawful detainer lies “(3) Where the property has
been sold in accordance with Section 2924 of the Civil Code, under a power of
sale contained in a deed of trust executed by such person, or a person under
whom such person claims, and the title under the sale has been duly perfected.”
(Code Civ. Proc., § 1161a.) Thus, the only issue before this court is
compliance with Section 2924. No other section is relevant for the purposes of
determining immediate right to possession in this action.

 

In a
nonjudicial foreclosure sale, a trustee exercises a power of sale contained in
a deed of trust. The purchaser at a nonjudicial foreclosure sale takes title by
a trustee’s deed. (Nguyen v. Calhoun (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 428, 441.)
When the deed that the trustee delivers to the buyer at a foreclosure sale
recites that all procedural requirements for the default and sale notices have
been met, a rebuttable presumption that these notice requirements have been
satisfied applies. If the buyer is a bona fide purchaser, the presumption
becomes conclusive. (Civ. Code § 2924.)

 

Here,
Plaintiff submitted evidence that a foreclosure sale was held on January 17,
2024, at which Yarden Consulting LLC (Yarden) purchased the Property and
subsequently received title pursuant to a Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale. (UMF ¶ 1.)
Plaintiff submitted evidence that it is the authorized agent of Yarden
regarding the Property, with authority to, inter alia, bring the instant
action on Yarden’s behalf. (UMF ¶2.)

 

Plaintiff
submitted evidence that the Trustee’s Deed upon Sale states, “All requirements
of law regarding the mailing of copies of notices or the publication of a copy
of the Notice of Default or the personal delivery of the copy of the Notice of
Default and the posting and publication of copies of the notice of a Sale have
been complied with.” (RJN #1). Therefore, unless Plaintiff had actual knowledge
or constructive notice of some irregularity in the sale proceedings at the time
of the sale, this statement is conclusively binding on Defendant as against
Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly,
the burden shifts to Defendant to show any purported irregularity in the sale
proceedings at the time of the sale.

 

Defendant did
not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this
issue. 

 

As for
Service of Notice, service carried out by a registered process server, as here,
“establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the
facts stated in the return.” (Evid. Code § 647.) “The effect of a presumption
affecting the burden of producing evidence is to require the trier of fact to
assume the existence of the presumed fact unless and until evidence is
introduced which would support a finding of its nonexistence, in which case the
trier of fact shall determine the existence or nonexistence of the presumed
fact from the evidence and without regard to the presumption.” (Evid. Code §
604.)

 

Here,
Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-        
On March 12, 2024, the notice to quit
in this action was served upon Defendants by a registered process server. (UMF
¶ 3.)

 

Plaintiff’s
evidence supports a reasonable inference that Defendant was properly served in
this case. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue
as to service. Notably, the simple denial of service is insufficient to
overcome the statutory presumption of Evidence Code section 647. (Palm
Property Investments, LLC v. Yadegar (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th
1219, 1428.) Defendant must produce affirmative evidence sufficient to both
rebut and overcome the presumption of service. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant did
not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this
issue. 

 

As for market
value, pursuant to Evidence Code section 813(a), “The value of property may be
shown only by the opinions of any of the following: (1) witnesses qualified to
express such opinions; (2) the owner or the spouse of the owner of the property
or property interest being valued; or (3) an officer, regular employee, or
partner designated by a corporation, partnership, or unincorporated association
that is the owner of the property or property interest being valued, if the
designee is knowledgeable as to the value of the property or property
interest.”

 

Here,
Plaintiff submitted a declaration by a qualified expert witness regarding the
market value of the Property. Vladimir Valsky is a real estate professional
with over 10 years of experience managing similar rental properties. He is
familiar with residential property valuation in Pacific Palisades, California.
According to Mr. Valsky, the Property’s fair market monthly rental value is
$39,500.00, and its daily rental value of at least $1,000.00.

 

Accordingly,
the burden shifts to Defendant to disclose a triable issue as to market value.

 

Defendant did
not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this
issue. 

 

 

Finally, as
for affirmative defenses, Plaintiff submitted evidence that prior to this
action, Defendants repeatedly stated, including in sworn declarations under
oath, that they are not occupants and would not occupy the Property. (UMF, ¶¶  12-18, see ¶¶  14 and 16 for sworn declarations.) As such,
Plaintiff contends that:

           

Therefore, it
is undisputed that defendants are unlawful holdover occupants following a
foreclosure sale, not tenants. Consequently, the purported affirmative defenses
asserted by Defendants are inapplicable because those affirmative defenses
apply to tenants, including but not limited to the not breach of the warranty
of habitability, repair and deduct, California Tenant Protection Act of 2019
(AB 1482), Los Angeles Rent Stabilization Ordinance (“LARSO”), and City of Los
Angeles Just Cause for Eviction Ordinance (LAMC §§ 165.00, et seq.)

 

            (MSJ,
9: 10-15.)

 

            Moreover,
Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s tenancy claims are contradictory and thus do
not create a triable issue. (See, e.g., California Medical Assn. v. Aetna
Health of California Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1075, 1095 ([“a party opposing
summary judgment cannot create an issue of fact by a declaration that
contradicts the party's prior discovery responses”]; Minish v. Hanuman
Fellowship (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 437, 449 [a party may not defeat summary
judgment by means of declarations or affidavits which contradict that party's
deposition testimony or sworn discovery responses]

 

Here,
Defendant claims in discovery that they are tenants of the Property’s former
owner, non-party 1355 Berea LLC pursuant to a written lease. Moreover,
Defendant claims their purported tenancy pursuant to said lease began March 21,
2023. However, Plaintiff submitted evidence that three weeks after this
purported lease start date, Defendants signed a loan application under oath on
April 11, 2023 that contradicts this claim.

 

Accordingly,
the burden shifts to Defendant to disclose a triable issue as to market value.

 

Defendant did
not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this
issue. 

 

Based on the
foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

On 5/9/2024, Plaintiff Grand Promenade (Plaintiff) filed an unlawful detainer action against Benardo Moretti (Defendant).

 

            On 8/26/2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant, who is representing himself in propria persona argues that Plaintiff has failed to state a claim because he claims that he paid Plaintiff rent.

 

The Court accepts well-pled allegations as true at the pleading stage. While it may be that Defendant did, in fact, pay all rent due to Plaintiff, this is a factual determination not properly made at this stage.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.
