DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV24782    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: 78

DANIEL CHU,

Plaintiff(s),

vs.

GRCA2 DEVELOPMENT, LLC, et al.,

Defendant(s).

Case No.: 

22STCV24782

Hearing Date: 

September 11, 2024

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

I.         BACKGROUND

On August 1, 2022, plaintiff Daniel Chu (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants GRCA2 Development, LLC (“GRCA2”), Gerry Wiener (“Wiener”), and CAA General Contractor, Inc. (“CAA”), and Cesar Andrino (Doe 1) for breach of contract action arising from renovation defects. Plaintiff alleges the following.

On October 15, 2015, he entered into a Residential Purchase Agreement (“RPA”) with GRCA2 to purchase real property and improvements located at 2260 Maravilla Drive, Los Angeles, California (“the Property”). (Compl. ¶ 9.) In sum, Plaintiff discovered material defects in the renovations in 2016, and the parties settled the dispute pursuant to a Settlement Agreement in which GRCA2 represented to Plaintiff that GRCA2 had repaired or addressed all matters and defects set forth in the Addenda. (Id. at ¶¶ 18-20.) In August 2021, Plaintiff attempted to settle the property to a third party when Plaintiff discovered that no certificate of occupancy was ever obtained, and discovered evidence that GRCA2 and CAA intentionally concealed the defective renovations. (Id. at ¶¶ 21-23.) The complaint sets forth five causes of action for (1) Negligence (CAA Contractor), (2) Breach of Settlement Agreement (GRCA2), (3) Fraud (GRCA2 and Wiener), (4) Declaratory Relief (GRCA2 and Wiener), and (5) Breach of Addendum to Purchase Agreement (GRCA2).

On March 21, 2024, GRCA2 and Wiener (collectively, “Defendants”) filed the instant motion for summary judgment against each cause of action against it, namely the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action.

On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition to the motion.

On August 2, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

On August 7, 2024, the Court continued the motion for additional time to consider the issues.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment. (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if the party does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c).)

 

III.      EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff submits three evidentiary objections to the declaration of Robert Rosen. Objection 1 is sustained, objection 2 is overruled, and objection 3 is sustained.

 

iv.       DISCUSSION

The following facts are undisputed. On October 15, 2014, Plaintiff and GRCA2 entered into the RPA in which Plaintiff agreed to purchase the Property. (UMF 1.) The sale of the Property closed in December 2014. (UMF 2.) On May 5, 2016, Plaintiff transmitted a demand letter setting forth defects in the Property that was allegedly discovered after Plaintiff had purchased the Property. (UMF 3.) On November 22, 2016, the parties engaged in mediation, which resulted in a Short Form Settlement Agreement with a mutual release and waiver. (UMFs 5-7.) Following execution of the Short Form Agreement, Plaintiff and GRCA2 entered into a long form Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release (i.e. the “2016 Settlement Agreement”) on December 21, 2016. (UMF 9.)

GRCA2 argues it is entitled to summary adjudication because (1) Plaintiff released his claims when he executed the 2016 Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release (“2016 Settlement Agreement”) on December 20, 2016, (2) Plaintiff’s action is time-barred by Civil Code § 337, and (3) GRCA2 did not breach the 2016 Settlement Agreement.

 

A.    Second and Third Causes of Action – Breach of 2016 Settlement Agreement & Fraud

 

 

1.     2016 Settlement Agreement & Release

Here, it is undisputed that GRCA2 and Plaintiff executed the 2016 Settlement Agreement. The recitals provide as follows: (1) seller GRCA2 sold the Property to buyer Plaintiff pursuant to the 2004 RPA, in which Plaintiff alleges there were defects in the Property which GRCA2 was obligated to repair, (3) GRCA2 denies all of Plaintiff’s allegations, and “believes that all such defects were repaired and/or addressed in Addenda to the RPA executed by Buyer”, (3) the parties agree the Agreement reflects a compromise and settlement of disputed and contested matters, and (4) the parties desire fully and finally compromise and resolve any and all claims they may have against one another, previously asserted or  unasserted. The settlement terms provide that GRCA2 would cause payment to be made to Plaintiff in the amount of $60,000 in exchange for the parties releases each other any and all claims.

The releases provisions are under paragraph 3 and 8 of the 2016 Settlement Agreement. Under “3. General Release”, it provides for a release of any and all past, present or future claims, causes of action of any kind, demands, “and compensation of any nature whatsoever...in any way related to the Property, the RPA, and/or the Allegations” and that the parties ““expressly waive and assume the risk of any and all claims for damages or any other relief, including injunctive relief, which exist as of this date but of which they do not know or suspect to exist, whether through ignorance, oversight, error, negligence or otherwise, and which, if known, would materially affect their decision to enter into this Agreement.” (Decl. Rosen; Exh. K.) Under “8. Section 1542 Release,” the agreement further provides that: “With regard to the matters expressly released under the provisions of this Agreement, the parties waive the rights, benefits and remedies conferred by Section 1542 of the California Civil Code…”

Defendants argue that the 2016 Settlement Agreement’s release clauses extinguished all liability Plaintiff may have against Defendants arising out of the Property in exchange for Defendants’ payment of $60,000. However, the issue, as framed by the second cause of action for breach of the 2016 Settlement Agreement, is whether Defendants have met their burden to show that the 2016 Settlement Agreement was not breached. As to whether there was a breach of the 2016 Settlement Agreement, the defendants point to the plain language of the 2016 Settlement, and the particular language used (“believes” and “denies”) in order to show there were no representations made, and therefore no breach. The Defendants meet their burden in this regard. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether the 2016 Settlement Agreement was breached.

Plaintiff’s first argument is that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Defendants breached the 2016 Settlement Agreement itself, because GRCA2 assured Plaintiff it addressed and/or repaired defects and missing permits that plagued the Property. Here, Plaintiff also points to the plain language of the 2016 Settlement Agreement, pointing the Court to language which the Plaintiff argues incorporates the representations in Addenda 3 into the material terms of the 2016 Settlement Agreement with the language “as if fully set forth herein and made a part hereof.” (AF 18)

The Court notes that this case was reassigned, effective July 5, 2024. (Notice of Case Reassignment, June 25, 2024.) Plaintiff points to another judge’s ruling regarding Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleading and demurrer; Defendants’ prior unsuccessful attack on the pleadings is not the same as a dispositive motion, nor does it act as a bar to granting the instant motion. Nonetheless, viewing the evidence in the light most favorable to the opposing party, the Court finds that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact as to whether the 2016 Settlement Agreement has been breached due to the factual question of whether the recitals were incorporated into the agreement as material terms.

As to the third cause of action, a party alleging fraud must generally prove a factual misrepresentation or concealment made with the intent to induce signing of the release and justifiable reliance thereon. (See CCP §1572; Rosencrans v. Dover Images, Ltd. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1080-1081. Although the existence of fraud is ordinarily a question of fact (Cal. Civ. Code § 1574), it may be decided as a matter of law “if reasonable minds can come to only one conclusion based on the facts.” (Guido v. Koopman (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 837, 843; see Rosencrans v. Dover Images, Ltd., supra, 192 Cal.App.4th at 1080-1081.)

Defendants rely on Salehi v. Surfside III Condo. Owners' Assn. (2011) 200 Cal. App. 4th 1146 and Winet v. Price (1992) 4 Cal. App. 4th 1159. The Court finds Salehi and Winet to be inapplicable here, where the current issue is whether the settlement agreement itself was breached.

Defendants address the third cause of action for Fraud by pointing the Court to the plain language of the 2016 Settlement Agreement, and the Declaration of Rosen. The Court considers the plain language of the 2016 Settlement Agreement to satisfy the Defendant’s burden of proof. Nonetheless, Plaintiff establishes a triable issue of fact as to whether there was fraud based on the deposition testimony of Weiner and Rosen, as the deposition testimony tends to show that Defendants were actually unaware of the status of the permits such that the representation of their “belief” that defects were addressed may be reasonably construed as false statements. (AF 2)

2.     Statute of Limitations

Generally, a statute of limitations begins to run when a cause of action accrues, meaning when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements.¿(Pineda v. Bank of America, N.A.¿(2010) 50 Cal. 4th 1389, 1397.) “An important exception to the general rule of accrual is the ‘discovery¿rule,’ which postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.¿[Citations.]” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc.¿(2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 807.) “[T]o rely on the discovery rule for¿delayed¿accrual¿of a cause of action, ‘[a] plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery¿and¿(2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.’ [Citation.] In assessing the sufficiency of the allegations of delayed discovery, the court places the burden on the plaintiff to ‘show diligence’; ‘conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer.’ [Citation.]” (Id. at 808.)

Here, the issue is when the causes of action accrued. Defendants argue that the statute of limitations has run because Chu knew of all the issues starting in 2014. (UMF 21-31) However, Plaintiff’s theory of the case is that new representations were made and/or reincorporated into the 2016 Settlement Agreement, and the discovery of any misrepresentations were delayed due to active concealment. Here, there is a triable issue of fact as to delayed discovery, given the apparent discrepancy between the language of the settlement agreement (“believes” and “denies”) and the deposition testimony of Rosen and Weiner which arguably shows those assertions in the 2016 Settlement Agreement are false. Thus, there is a question of fact to be determined as to when the causes of action accrued due to when plaintiff should have reasonably discovered the breach.

Summary adjudication to the second cause of action for breach of the 2016 Settlement Agreement and third cause of action for fraud are DENIED.

B.    Fourth Cause of Action – Declaratory Relief

Plaintiff pleads the fourth cause of action as an alternative to the second cause of action, seeking to invalidate the global release and waiver in the 2016 Settlement Agreement based on allegations that GRCA2 obtained the release through fraud and deceit. (Compl. ¶ 53.) GRCA2 argues it is entitled to summary adjudication because this cause of action is time-barred. The Court DENIES summary adjudication for the same reasons as above.

C.    Fifth Cause of Action – Breach of Addendum to RPA

Plaintiff pled the fifth cause of action in the alternative to the second cause of action, in which the cause of action assumes that the release and waiver contained in the 2016 Settlement Agreement was set aside, in that case alleging breach of the Addenda to the RPA. (Compl. ¶ 57.) Plaintiff alleges that Addendum 1 and Addendum 3 stated that GRCA2 would provide all required permits for the renovations, and obtain any required unapproved permits in post-closing, but concealed its non-compliance such that Plaintiff could not have reasonably discovered it sooner than the 2021 Sale Agreement. (Id ¶ 59.) GRCA2 argues it is entitled to summary adjudication based on the general release of the 2016 Settlement Agreement, and that the cause of action is time-barred. For the same reasons as above, summary adjudication is DENIED as to those arguments.

 

V.        CONCLUSION

Defendants GRCA2 Development, LLC’s and Gerry Wiener’s motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication, against Plaintiff’s complaint is DENIED in its entirety.

Case Number: 23STCV02134    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: 78

CHARLES BERNAL, et al.,

Plaintiff(s),

vs.

CHRISTOPHER MAGGIORE, et al.,

Defendant(s).

Case No.: 

23STCV02134

Hearing Date: 

September 11, 2024

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT FORCE PROTECTION AGENCY, INC.’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

 

I.         BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Charles Bernal (“Bernal”) and Artist Worldwide Agency, LLC (“AWA”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendants Christopher Maggiore (“Maggiore”), Artists Worldwide (“AW”), Jamison Properties, Inc. (“Jamison Properties”) [dismissed on October 12, 2023], Force Protection Agency, Inc. (“FPA”), Doe 1, and Does 2-25 premised upon various allegations, such as interference with AWA’s business, breach of contract, assault, and battery.

On March 3, 2023, Maggiore and AW (collectively, “Defendants”) filed their Answer to Plaintiffs’ complaint.

On July 3, 2024, Defendant FPA filed the instant Motion for Protective Order.   

Plaintiffs oppose the motion. A reply has not been filed.

A.    Moving Argument

            Defendant FPA requests that the court enter a Protective Order stating that the depositions of FPA or any of its employees shall not proceed until FPA has received the entire client file from Attorney Cole Sheridan, and until receipt of Plaintiff Charles Bernal’s discovery responses on or after July 31, 2024. FPA argues good cause exists for issuing the requested protective order because FPA’s due process rights would be violated without it. Specifically, Defense counsel has only recently come into the case, FPA does not have prior counsel’s entire client file, prior counsel failed to adequately represent FPA, it is reasonably believed there may be additional necessary information and documents of which FPA is unaware within the client file, and FPA has taken steps to promptly remedy the problem.

B.    Opposing Argument

            FPA’s Motion for Protective Order is not supported by any legal authority because there is no claim of unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, and no claim of undue burden and expense. Additionally, if a protective order were granted, Plaintiffs would be prejudiced because Plaintiff Bernal has been attempting to take depositions of FPA employees Guy Mack, Russell Stuart, and FPA’s person most knowledgeable. (Lauber Decl. ¶3.) Trial in this matter is only four months away, yet FPA has not produced a single witness for deposition.

C.    Reply Argument

None filed.

 

II.        MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

A.    Legal Standard

            A party may move for a protective order before, during, or even after a deposition. (Civ. Proc. Code § 2025.420.) With good cause shown, the Court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party or deponent from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. A protective order under this section may include direction that “the deposition not be taken at all,” or “be taken only on certain specified terms and conditions,” or “that certain matters not be inquired into,” or “that the examination be limited to certain matters.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2015.420(b)(1), (5), (9), and (10); see also In re Lifschutz (1970) 2 Cal.3d 415, 437.) Trial courts are “granted great discretion in making orders under [this provision] . . . and . . . the power to prevent abuse is the power to exercise discretion based upon the factual showing made.” (Durst v. Superior Court (1963) 218 Cal.App.2d 460, 467.)

B.    Discussion

            Defendant FPA has failed to provide sufficient evidence showing good cause as Defendant failed to show any unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, or undue burden and expense. Here, Defendant’s Counsel states that he substituted in as FPA’s counsel on May 30, 2024. (Stewart Decl. ¶ 3.) On the same day, he requested the complete client file from former counsel, however, to date has not received the complete file. (Id. at ¶¶ 4-12.) Counsel further states “Mr. Sheridan was a percipient witness to the occurrence on January 27, 2023, that forms the basis of the allegations of liability against FPA. Upon information and belief, this fact was never disclosed to the Plaintiffs by Mr. Sheridan, based on review of the portions of the client file has been produced to date by Mr. Sheridan. . . In addition, an actual conflict exists for any attorney who would be asked to concurrently represent both the interests of FPA and the interests of Maggiore and AW in this matter.” (Id. at ¶¶ 13-14.) Counsel further declares, “this conflict appears to have manifested in the apparent failure of Mr. Sheridan to pursue any meaningful discovery probative to the defenses of FPA in the litigation. . . Thus, I currently have no information from Mr. Bernal regarding liability or damages, beyond what is stated in the complaint.” (Id. at ¶¶ 15-16.) Defendant FPA’s President, Russell Stuart also provided his declaration stating “[w]ithin the past two months I have first become aware that Cole Sheridan did not pursue any meaningful discovery probative to the issues and defenses of FPA during the pendency of litigation.” (Stuart Decl. ¶ 12.) Mr. Stuart further stated that there were multiple issues with former counsel’s representation. (Stuart Decl. ¶¶ 8-12.)

            The Court notes that on June 27, 2024 “FPA served a request for prior pleadings and prior discovery upon Bernal. Bernal provide[d] the request[ed] documents on July 31, 2024.” (Lauber Decl. ¶ 4.) Moreover, “Bernal did not receive any written discovery requests or notices of subpoenas to third parties from Defendant Force Protection Agency’s (“FPA”) prior to the substitution of attorney filed on May 30, 2024.” (Id. at ¶ 2.) Additionally, Defendants’ former Counsel failed to attend mandatory court hearings, failed to propound any discovery, failed to take depositions, failed to file any motions, and failed to assert any affirmative defenses on behalf of FPA. (Stuart Decl. ¶¶ 9,12.) Moreover, FPA’s current counsel joined the case prior to the beginning of expert discovery. Therefore, this suggests that Defendant’s former Counsel does not have any additional information.

            Thus, Defendant failed to present evidence of any unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, or undue burden and expense.

 

IV. CONCLUSION

The Motion for a Protective Order is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On June 20, 2023, Plaintiff Zaheer Goodman-Bhyat (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Evolver Group, LLC (“Evolver”), Cheni Yerushalmi (“Yerushalmi”), and Adam Gerstein (“Gerstein”) (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

On September 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants alleging causes of action for: (1) Fraud in the Inducement; (2) Negligent Misrepresentation; (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Conversion.

 

On February 28, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court overruled Defendants’ demurrer to the first, second, and fifth causes of action, and sustained the demurer of Defendants to the third and fourth causes of action without leave to amend. (02/28/24 Minute Order.) The Court also denied Defendants’ motion to strike. (02/28/24 Minute Order.)

 

On May 23, 2024, Defendants filed a joint Answer to the FAC.

 

On June 25, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Jill Feeney to the Honorable Michelle C. Kim sitting in Department 78 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse effective July 5, 2024.  

 

On July 1, 2024, Patrick C. McGarrigle, Esq. and Michael J. Kenney, Esq. of McGarrigle, Kenney, & Zampiello, APC filed and served the instant unopposed Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Defendants (“Counsel”).

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.         Relieved as Counsel       

A.              Legal Standard

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 284(2).) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.  

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a)), MC-052 (Declaration) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(c)), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e)). The proposed order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

Further, the requisite forms must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).) The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).) A motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client. Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915. 

B.        Discussion

According to the declaration in support of the  motion, “Counsel requested that Defendants sign and return a Substitution of Attorney Form to effectuate the change by consent pursuant to CCP 284(a), but same was not completed and returned, nor have the circumstances necessitating the substitution and relief/withdrawal been rectified by the clients.” (See Form MC-052.) According to Attachment 2 of the Form MC-052, Defendants have not made a payment pursuant to the terms of the written retainer agreement since approximately January 2024. (See Form MC-052 at Attachment 2.) Counsel also indicates that further representation of Defendants has been made impossible due to Defendants’ unwillingness to communicate consistently regarding the subject matter of the instant action. (See Form MC-052 at Attachment 2.)

The Court finds that Counsel has set forth a valid reason to warrant withdrawal from representing Defendants. Further, the forms have been served on all clients.


II.        Conclusion                  
     Based on the foregoing, the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel filed by Patrick C. McGarrigle, Esq. and Michael J. Kenney, Esq. of McGarrigle, Kenney, & Zampiello, APC is GRANTED. The order will be final  upon proof of service of the Final Order. The Court sets a Status Conference re: Proof of Service of the Final Order on September __, 2024, 8:30AM.
