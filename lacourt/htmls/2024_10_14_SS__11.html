DEPARTMENT 11 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV36328    Hearing Date: October 14, 2024    Dept: 11


19STCV36328 (Martinez)

 

Tentative Ruling Re: Motion for Leave to Amend

 

Date:                           10/14/24

Time:                          2:30 pm

Moving Party:           Bibiyan Law Group, P.C. (“Plaintiff’s Counsel”)

Opposing Party:        ACS Staffing, Inc. (“Defendant”)

Department:              11

Judge:                         David S. Cunningham III

________________________________________________________________________

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The hearing on Plaintiff’s Counsel’s motion for leave to amend is continued.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wage-and-hour case.  Plaintiff alleges class and representative claims under the Labor Code, Unfair Competition Law, and Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”).[1]

 

Here, Plaintiff’s Counsel move for leave to file a third amended complaint (“TAC”).

 

LAW

 

“The court may grant leave to amend the pleadings at any stage of the action.  A party may discover the need to amend after all pleadings are completed and new information requires a change in the nature of the claims or defenses previously pleaded.”  (Edmon & Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group June 2024 Update) ¶ 6:636, emphasis in original.)  “Such changes usually cannot be made on ex parte procedure [citation].  Rather, a formal motion to amend must be served and filed.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“Motions for leave to amend are directed to the sound discretion of the judge.”  (Id. at ¶ 6:637.)  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  “However, the court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of pleadings.”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) 

 

“The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”  (Ibid.)  “Leave to amend should be denied only where the facts are not in dispute, and the nature of the plaintiff’s claim is clear, but under substantive law, no liability exists and no amendment would change the result.”  (Edwards v. Superior Court (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 172, 180.)  In fact, “[i]f the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.”  (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

The purpose of this motion is twofold – to add a new representative named Sara Sobenes in Plaintiff’s place and to dismiss Plaintiff without prejudice.

 

Plaintiff’s Counsel

 

Plaintiff’s Counsel contend leave to amend should be granted because Plaintiff has disappeared.  In April 2024, the attorneys attended a mediation where defense counsel made a settlement offer.  Plaintiff’s Counsel attempted to contact Plaintiff multiple times to get his approval.  He did not respond, so Plaintiff’s Counsel hired a private investigator to find him.  The private investigator searched for several weeks without success.  Now, Plaintiff’s Counsel claim they need to name Sobenes as a new representative to protect the putative class members’ interests and to prosecute the representative PAGA claim.  (See Motion, pp. 1-3.)

 

In particular, Plaintiff’s Counsel claim:

 

* the policy in favor of granting leave to amend is liberal (see id. at p. 3-4);

 

* California law requires courts to give litigants opportunities to name new representatives (see id. at pp. 4-6);

 

* the proposed TAC is necessary (see id. at pp. 6-7);

 

* the proposed TAC “relate[s] back to the filing of the original complaint” (id. at p. 7);

 

* Plaintiff’s Counsel did not delay in requesting leave to amend (see id. at pp. 7-8); and

 

* Defendant cannot show prejudice.  (See id. at pp. 8-9.)

 

Defendant

 

Defendant disagrees.  Defendant contends the motion should be denied because:

 

* Plaintiff’s Counsel’s notice of motion does not state the grounds for the motion (see Opposition, p. 9);

 

* the declaration of attorney Rafael Yedoyan fails to comply with rule 3.1324(b) (see id. at p. 10);

 

* the proposed TAC is unnecessary (see id. at pp. 10-11);

 

* Plaintiff’s Counsel fail to identify the date when they discovered Plaintiff’s disappearance (see id. at p. 11);

 

* Sobenes’s class and PAGA claims do not relate back (see id. at pp. 12-15);

 

* Sobenes signed an arbitration agreement (see id. at p. 15); and

 

* Defendant will suffer prejudice.  (See id. at pp. 15-18.)

 

Analysis

 

Defendant’s challenge to the notice is unavailing.  A “notice of motion must state in the first paragraph exactly what relief is sought and why (what grounds).”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 9:38, emphasis in original.)  Yet “this is not an absolute requirement.”  (Id. at ¶ 9:41.)  “Relief may be granted on grounds appearing anywhere in the accompanying declarations and points and authorities provided the notice states the motion will be made on grounds disclosed in the accompanying papers.”  (Ibid., emphasis in original.)  Plaintiff’s Counsel’s notice complies with these rules:

 

PLEASE TAKE NOTICE that on October 14, 2024 at 2:30p.m. in Department 11 of the above-entitled Court, located at 312 North Spring Street, Los Angeles, California, counsel for SILVESTRE MARTINEZ (“Plaintiff”) will and hereby does move this Court for an order granting Plaintiff leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) (a copy of the proposed TAC is hereto attached) in order to: (1) add Sara Sobenes as a plaintiff in all of the Class Action claims; (2) add Sara Sobenes as a named Plaintiff in the PAGA claims; (3) clarify that the alleged Causes of Action are on behalf of Sara Sobenes; and (4) dismiss Plaintiff Silvestre Martinez as a Plaintiff in this action without prejudice, given his sudden disappearance.

 

As such, Plaintiff respectfully requests that the Court grant Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a TAC. This motion will be based on this notice, the attached Memorandum of Points of Authorities, the Declaration of Rafael Yedoyan, the record on file in this action, as well as such other and further matters as are or may be presented before or at the hearing of this matter.  

 

(Notice of Motion, p. 2, emphasis added.)

 

Defendant’s challenge to the Yedoyan declaration is also unavailing.  Rule 3.1324(b) requires a declaration to be filed in support of a motion for leave to amend.  The declaration must state “the purpose and effect of the proposed amendments[,]” “why the proposed amendments are necessary and proper[,]” “when the facts giving rise to the proposed amendment were discovered[,]” and “the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 6:677, emphasis in original.)  Although the Yedoyan declaration lacks clear, organized statements addressing these factors point by point, it appears to contain the requisite information.  The purpose is to name a new class representative (see, e.g., Yedoyan Decl., ¶ 12); it is necessary to name a new class representative because Plaintiff’s Counsel could not find Plaintiff (see id. at ¶¶ 7-12); the facts of Plaintiff’s disappearance became known when Plaintiff’s Counsel received the private investigator’s report (see id. at ¶¶ 9-10); and Plaintiff’s Counsel did not move earlier because they were trying to resolve the matter informally first.  (See id. at ¶¶ 11-19.)  The Court finds rule 3.1324(b) satisfied.

 

The Court turns to Defendant’s prejudice argument. 

 

First, Defendant claims Plaintiff’s Counsel misled Defendant and the Court by attending the mediation without authority from Plaintiff and by failing to disclose that Plaintiff is missing.  (See Opposition, pp. 16-17.) 

 

This is conjecture.  The Yedoyan declaration, which is signed under penalty of perjury, states that, prior to the mediation and disappearance, Plaintiff’s Counsel had been “able to contact [Plaintiff] without issue.”  (Id. at ¶ 10.)  Defendant may doubt the veracity of the statement, but doubt is not evidence that Plaintiff’s Counsel lied.  The Yedoyan declaration suggests that Plaintiff’s Counsel did not learn about the potential of a problem until April 11th or 12th.  (See id. at ¶¶ 5-7.) 

 

Second, Defendant contends granting the motion would cause the case to “restart . . . on the eve of the five-year rule.”  (Opposition, p. 17, capitalizing and underlining deleted.) 

 

The Court disagrees.  Defendant’s own estimate is that the five-year deadline is more than a year from now.  (See ibid.)  The current record does not support the conclusion that the litigation cannot be completed within a year. 

 

Third, Defendant claims Plaintiff’s Counsel’s real goal is to open discovery to find another new representative to replace Sobenes since Sobenes signed an arbitration agreement.  (See id. at pp. 17-18.) 

 

The Court disagrees.  The arbitration agreement is attached to the declaration of Mirna Sevilla, Defendant’s operations director, at exhibit 1.  (See Sevilla Decl., Ex. 1.)  Defendant did not produce it to Plaintiff’s Counsel before Plaintiff’s Counsel filed this motion.  (See Yedoyan Decl., ¶¶ 17-19.)  Regardless, the agreement’s enforceability is a factual question for another day; and, even assuming it is enforceable, California policy probably would still favor discovery given the Court’s obligation to look out for the interests of the putative class (it is not their fault that Plaintiff went missing or that Defendant declined to stipulate to Plaintiff’s Counsel’s informal request to discover a new representative).  (See, e.g., CashCall, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 273, 284-296; see also, e.g., Safeco Ins. Co. of America v. Superior Court (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 814, 828-835.) 

 

Defendant would be free to demur to the TAC, file a motion to compel arbitration, request a Belaire-West notice procedure, and oppose class certification, so the prejudice to Defendant, if any, would be minimized.   

 

Nevertheless, the Court agrees that the proposed TAC is defective.  Plaintiff’s Counsel fail to show that Sobenes’s claims relate back and that she has standing under PAGA.  The relation-back discussion is conclusory (see Motion, p. 7), and Plaintiff’s Counsel fail to highlight any relation-back allegations in the proposed TAC.  The PAGA-standing paragraph is repeated, verbatim, from the second amended complaint (“SAC”).  It concerns Plaintiff, not Sobenes:

 

31. Plaintiff has complied with the procedures for bringing suit specified in Labor Code section 2699.3. On or around October 10, 2019, Plaintiff gave written notice to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (“LWDA”) and to the Staffing Defendants of the specified provisions of the Labor Code alleged to have been violated by it. The LWDA did not provide notice of its intention to investigate the Staffing Defendants’ alleged violations within sixty-five (65) calendar days of the October 10, 2019 postmarked date of the herein-described notice sent by Plaintiff to the LWDA and Defendant.

 

(SAC, ¶ 31.)

 

31. Plaintiff has complied with the procedures for bringing suit specified in Labor Code section 2699.3. On or around October 10, 2019, Plaintiff gave written notice to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (“LWDA”) and to the Staffing Defendants of the specified provisions of the Labor Code alleged to have been violated by it. The LWDA did not provide notice of its intention to investigate the Staffing Defendants’ alleged violations within sixty-five (65) calendar days of the October 10, 2019 postmarked date of the herein-described notice sent by Plaintiff to the LWDA and Defendant.

 

(Yedoyan Decl., Ex. H, ¶ 31 [attaching the proposed TAC].)  These deficiencies must be fixed before leave to file the proposed TAC can be granted.

 

Ultimately, the Court finds that the hearing should be continued to give Plaintiff’s Counsel a chance to decide whether Sobenes is the right person to serve as the new representative.  If they want to continue with Sobenes, they should redraft the proposed TAC to allege facts demonstrating relation back and PAGA standing, and they should prepare themselves to oppose an arbitration motion in case Defendant decides to file one.  If they want to conduct discovery to find a different new representative, they should set up a discovery conference.  The Court grants Plaintiff’s Counsel leave to pursue either option.

22STCV01988 (Quijano)

Tentative Ruling Re: Motion to Intervene

 

Date:                           10/14/24

Time:                          1:45 pm

Moving Party:           Diana Cardenas and Luis Ramirez (collectively “Proposed Intervenors”)

Opposing Party:        Gloria Lopez Quijano and Anthony Carlos (collectively “Plaintiffs”)

Department:              11

Judge:                         David S. Cunningham III

________________________________________________________________________

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Proposed Intervenors’ motion to intervene is denied.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wage-and-hour action.  Plaintiff alleges class and representative claims under the Labor Code, Unfair Competition Law, and Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”).

 

Plaintiffs and Defendant entered into a settlement in July 2023.[1]

 

Plaintiffs’ motion for preliminary approval is set for hearing on October 14, 2024.

 

Here, Proposed Intervenors move to intervene.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Proposed Intervenors contend the motion to intervene should be granted because, if approved, the settlement would improperly release:

 

* Proposed Intervenors’ class and representative quota-law claims (see Motion, pp. 1-5); and

 

* Defendant’s successors, predecessors, subsidiaries, affiliates, and related companies.  (See id. at pp. 1-3, 5-8.) 

 

Proposed Intervenors also contend the motion should be granted to prevent a reverse auction.  (See id. at pp. 11-12.)[2]

 

Timeliness

 

The first issue is whether the motion is timely.  The answer is no.  The Court set a briefing schedule for this motion, requiring the moving papers to be filed by August 30, 2024 (see 7/3/24 Minute Order, p. 2), but the eCourt docket indicates that Proposed Intervenors did not e-file the moving papers until September 3rd.  This is one reason for denying the motion.

 

The Court sees a second reason.  “Whether intervention is of right, or only permissive, the party seeking to intervene must make ‘timely’ application to the court.”  (Edmon & Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group June 2024 Update) ¶ 2:438.)  “Timeliness . . . is determined by the totality of the circumstances, with a focus on three primary factors: [1] the stage of the proceedings; [2] the prejudice to other parties from the delay in seeking to intervene; and [3] the reason for the delay.”  (Ibid.)  Importantly, timeliness “is measured . . . from the date the intervenors ‘knew or should have known their interests in the litigation were not being adequately represented.’”  (Id. at ¶ 2:439.)  Plaintiffs and Defendant reached the settlement at the mediation on July 12, 2023 (see Moon Decl., ¶¶ 14-15), and the Moon declaration suggests that Proposed Intervenors’ counsel knew about the settlement, the purported collusion, and the alleged deficiencies by August 9, 2023.  (See id. at ¶¶ 18-25; see also Defendant’s Opposition, p. 7.)  However, Proposed Intervenors did not file their ex parte application until July 2, 2024 and did not e-file the moving papers until September 3, 2024, more than a year later.[3]  These facts show that the motion is late.

 

Reply Briefs

 

The next issue concerns the reply briefs.  On the night of October 2, 2024, Proposed Intervenors e-filed two replies, one at 10:29 p.m., and one at 11:12 p.m.  The Court declines to strike them – and did read them – despite the fact that they were filed after close of business.

 

Mandatory Intervention

 

“[A] nonparty has the right to intervene . . . where the nonparty claims an interest in the property or transaction involved in the litigation, and is so situated that any judgment rendered in the nonparty's absence ‘may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest.’”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 2:407, emphasis in original.) 

 

“There is no right to intervene if the court finds that the nonparty’s interests are being adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties to the litigation.”  (Id. at ¶ 2:409.1, emphasis in original.)

 

Proposed Intervenors claim they must be allowed to intervene to preserve their quota-law claims and to narrow the settlement’s definition of released parties.  (See, e.g., Motion, pp. 10-11 [arguing that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring the quota-law claims and are atypical representatives].)

 

The Court disagrees.  Yes, Proposed Intervenors “have an interest in preserving their claims and rights[,]” but, as Plaintiffs and Defendant point out, their “ability to protect their interest would not be practically impaired or impeded by the settlement . . . because they could opt out of or object to the settlement.”  (Edwards v. Heartland Payment Systems, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 725, 733; see also Plaintiffs’ Opposition, p. 6; Defendant’s Opposition, pp. 8-9.)  Given these options, “formal intervention is unnecessary” to achieve Proposed Intervenors’ goals.  (Edwards, supra, 29 Cal.App.5th at 733.)

 

Indeed, when deciding whether to approve a settlement, the Court is obligated to “prevent fraud, collusion or unfairness to the class.”  (Ibid.)  It is standard for the Court to analyze the scope of the release as part of the approval process.  Any objection to the scope here would be scrutinized by the Court and could result in the release being narrowed.  This could be done without intervention.

 

The same goes for the reverse-auction argument.  Proposed Intervenors do not need mandatory intervention to raise a reverse-auction objection.  If raised, the Court could decide the objection on a developed record prior to granting final approval.[4]

 

As a matter of guidance, the Court is leaning toward removing the quota-law language from the release because Plaintiffs concede that they do not allege quota-law claims in their operative complaint (see, e.g., Plaintiffs’ Opposition, p. 12), and the quota-law claims arose after the alleged class period commenced.  (See First Amended Complaint, ¶ 2 [alleging that the class period is “from October 30, 2020 . . . into the present and ongoing”]; see also Plaintiffs’ Opposition, p. 7 [conceding that the quota-law statutes “were not in effect until 2022”].)  On balance, the Court tends to believe that carving out the quota-law claims while approving the settlement as to the other wage-and-hour claims would provide the best benefit to the class. 

 

Such a carveout would also probably take care of the parties’ dispute concerning the Labor and Workforce Development Agency’s interest in the quota-law claims.

 

Nevertheless, a quick discussion of Turrieta v. Lyft, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 664 is warranted.  Three Lyft drivers each filed a representative PAGA action against “the same employer for the same alleged Labor Code violations.”  (Turrieta, supra, 16 Cal.5th at 676.)  One of the drivers settled.  The other two drivers sought to intervene and to file objections to the settlement.  The California Supreme Court held that PAGA’s legislative scheme does not permit intervention and a right to object.  (See ibid.)

 

Turrieta might be distinguishable.  Turrieta governs when the representative PAGA claims overlap and involve the same alleged violations of the Labor Code.  (See ibid.)  Again, Plaintiffs’ operative complaint does not allege the quota-law claims, and they arose after the start of the alleged class period.

 

Ultimately, though, Turrieta does not change the result.  Proposed Intervenors have the option to either opt out or object, so the Court finds that mandatory intervention should be denied.

 

As for the released-parties definition, Plaintiffs and Defendant claim the settlement uses the Los Angeles Superior Court’s model definition.  The Court will address this issue with the attorneys at the hearing. 

 

Permissive Intervention

 

Permissive intervention is denied.  The Court “has discretion to permit a nonparty to intervene” where:

 

* “[t]he nonparty has a direct and immediate interest in the litigation” (Truck Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 342, 346, emphasis in original);

 

* “[t]he intervention will not enlarge the issues in the case” (ibid., emphasis in original); and

 

* “[t]he reasons for intervention outweigh any opposition by the existing parties.”  (Ibid.)

 

At minimum, intervention would enlarge the issues by adding the quota-law claims, which would necessitate a reopening of discovery, creating delay and a potential unraveling of the settlement of the non-quota-law claims.  Proposed Intervenors’ threat to file a motion to stay Plaintiffs’ case following intervention would add to the delay.  (See Motion, p. 13; see also Defendant’s Opposition, pp. 7-8.)  Moreover, the Court reiterates that “formal intervention is unnecessary” since Proposed Intervenors could either opt out of the settlement or object to it.  (Edwards, supra, 29 Cal.App.5th at 733.)
