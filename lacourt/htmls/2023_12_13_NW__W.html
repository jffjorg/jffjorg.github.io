DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV26696    Hearing Date: December 13, 2023    Dept: W

LANCE LOBDELL, ET AL. V. REGAL MEDICAL GROUP, INC., ET AL.

 

MOTIONS FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        December 13, 2023                          Trial Date:       June 23, 2024

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        19STCV26696

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 31, 2019, Plaintiffs Lance Lobdell (“Lance”) and Debra Lobdell (“Debra”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed this action for damages for medical negligence and loss of consortium. Plaintiffs filed their complaint against Regal Medical Group, Inc.; Lakeside Community Healthcare; Northridge Hospital Medical Center; Michael R. Shapiro, M.D.; Michael R. Shapiro, M.D., Inc.; Ryan McBirney, PA; Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D.; Kalpesh Patel, M.D.; Hrair Darakjian, M.D.; Mission Community Hospital; N’deya Kama, M.D.; Ali Ozhand, M.D.; Emily Alexiadis, NP; and Aaron M. Shelub.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

I.                    Defendant Mission Community Hospital’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication of Issue is GRANTED

II.                  Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan Mcbirney, P.A.’S Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEFENDANT MISSION COMMUNITY HOSPITAL’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION OF ISSUE

 

Defendant Mission Community Hospital moves, under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, for an order granting summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each cause of action in its favor and against Plaintiffs Lance Lobdell and Debra Lobdell on the grounds that there is no merit to Plaintiffs’ causes of action for medical malpractice and loss of consortium asserted in the complaint, there exists no issue of material fact remaining for trial, and that Defendant Mission is entitled to judgment against Plaintiffs as a matter of law.

 

The elements of medical malpractice are:¿“(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”¿¿(Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 696, 701-02 (citations omitted).)¿ “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”¿¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Defendant Mission Community Hospital (“MCH”) submits the declaration of Shahryar Ahmadi, M.D., a board-certified fellowship trained orthopedic surgeon. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Ahmadi obtained his medical degree in 1994 from Tehran University of Medical Sciences and completed his residency in orthopedic surgery at University of Toronto in Toronto, Canada in 2010. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Ahamdi also completed a fellowship in shoulder and elbow surgery at the Mayo Clinic in Rochester in 2011. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶1.) Currently, Dr. Ahmadi performs orthopedics, shoulder, elbow, and trauma surgeries at the Memorial Orthopedic Surgical Group in Long Beach, California. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Ahmadi attests based upon his education, training, and experience, it is his expert opinion to a reasonable degree of medical probability that MCH and its nursing and ancillary staff complied with the applicable standard of care at all times in the care and treatment of Mr. Lobdell. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶5.) Moreover, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, it is my opinion that there was no act or omission to act on the part of MCH or its staff which caused, contributed to, or was a substantial factor in bringing about the injuries and damages alleged by Mr. Lobdell. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶5.) Specifically, during both of Mr. Lobdell’s hospitalizations at MCH, the nursing staff appropriately and timely monitored and assessed him, reported changes in his condition to his physicians, provided care and treatments pursuant to physician orders, and ultimately discharged Mr. Lobdell pursuant to physician orders. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶5i.) The selection of diagnostic tests to be performed, the evaluation of test results, the formation and assessment of medical diagnoses and plan of treatment, the decision to include specialists and/or consultants in the medical care and treatment of the patient are also all within the scope of the medical staff, not nursing or ancillary staff. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶5j.) Accordingly, there was not a breach of the standard of care by the nursing staff of MCH in their care and treatment of Mr. Lobdell, nothing they did or failed to do caused or contributed to his injuries, and MCH’s nursing and ancillary staff followed and implemented physician orders in an appropriate and timely manner. (Ahmadi Decl. ¶¶5i-5k.)

 

MCH also submits the declaration of Mildred Mejia, the Pre-operative Logistic Coordinator at MCH, who attests MCH had the resources (operating room and staff) available had Mr. Lobdell’s physician/surgeon felt he needed to be taken to surgery sooner. (Mejia Decl. ¶¶3-6.)

 

Defendant MCH has met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury.  As Defendant MCH is entitled to judgment on the first cause of action for negligence, Defendant MCH is also entitled to judgment on the second cause of action for loss of consortium. (See Vanhooser v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 921, 927.)

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff instead filed a notice of non-opposition exchange for a waiver of costs and waiving any potential claim for malicious prosecution.  Therefore, Defendant MCH is entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendant MCH’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

II.                  DEFENDANTS MARY ANN H. TREPHAN, M.D. AND RYAN MCBIRNEY, P.A.’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. move the Court for an order that summary adjudication be granted in their favor and against Plaintiffs on the grounds there is no triable issue of material fact as to the summary judgment sought and therefore Defendants are entitled to summary judgment as a matter of law pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §437c. Specifically, Defendants met the standard of care with respect to the care and treatment he provided to Plaintiff Lance Lobdell and there was no negligence on the part of Defendants that was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff Lance Lobdell’s injuries.

 

As noted above, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. present the declarations of E.R. Physician David Barcay, M.D. and orthopedic surgeon William Brien, M.D. Dr. Barcay is a physician and board certified in Internal Medicine and Critical Care Medicine by the American Board of Internal Medicine. (Barcay Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Barcay testifies to a reasonable degree of medical probability, that based on his education, training, and experience, once it was determined that an orthopedic evaluation needed to be done as a result of the open fracture, then it meets the standard of care to transfer the care to the orthopedic surgeon for a more complete evaluation. (Barcay Decl. ¶29.) PA McBirney further met the standard of care in consulting with orthopedic surgeon Dr. Darakjian, however, Dr. Darakjian advised he could not consult on the patient as he was not in-house for his insurance but the patient was stable for transfer. (Barcay Decl. ¶30.) Dr. Barcay attests it was appropriate and met the standard of care for PA McBirney to rely on Dr. Darakjian’s assessment that the patient was stable for transfer from an orthopedic surgery standpoint as this is out of his scope of practice as a Physician Assistant in the Emergency Department. (Barcay Decl. ¶31.) Moreover, Dr. Barcay testifies Mr. Lobdell was hemodynamically stable for transfer and his stability did not decline while waiting for the ambulance to transfer him to Mission Community Hospital. (Barcay Decl. ¶33.) Thus, there was no need, from an Emergency Medicine standpoint to make any changes to the transfer plans. (Barcay Decl. ¶33.)   

 

Dr. Brien, a board certified in Orthopedic Surgery, similarly testifies to a reasonable degree of medical probability that there was no negligence on the part of PA McBirney or Dr. Trephan with regard to not performing any testing to look for a potential rotator cuff injury in this action. (Brien Decl. ¶19.) Dr. Brien contends even if diagnosed in the Emergency Department, rotator cuff surgery is an elective surgery that would be done months after the patient healed from the fracture and Dr. Brien believes to a reasonable degree of medical probability that once the x-rays confirmed the comminuted intra-articular fractures, it met the standard of care to defer to an orthopedic surgeon for any further complete evaluation of the patient’s injuries. (Brien Decl. ¶19.) Dr. Brien contends the standard of care did not require that Mr. Lobdell’s injury be surgically repaired emergently and therefore given Mr. Lobdell’s acceptance at Mission Community Hospital with information that an orthopedic surgeon agreed to consult was appropriate and met the standard of care. (Brien Decl. ¶24.) Accordingly, it is Dr. Brien’s opinion to a reasonable degree of medical probability that there was no negligence by PA McBirney or Dr. Trephan nor did PA McBirney or Dr. Trephan cause or contribute to Plaintiffs’ alleged injuries and damages. (Brien Decl. ¶¶26-30.)  

 

Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. have met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury.  As Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. are entitled to judgment on the first cause of action for negligence, Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. are also entitled to judgment on the second cause of action for loss of consortium. (See Vanhooser v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 921, 927.)

 

The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff instead filed a notice of non-opposition exchange for a waiver of costs and waiving any potential claim for malicious prosecution.  Therefore, Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A. are entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendants Mary Ann H. Trephan, M.D. and Ryan McBirney, P.A.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 21VECV01274    Hearing Date: December 13, 2023    Dept: W

BERNARD MICHAEL v. AAA TAX CONSULTANTS, ET AL.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

 

Date of Hearing:        December 13, 2023                          Trial Date:       July 31, 2023

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21VECV01274

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Bernard Michael

Responding Party:     No Opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

This wage and hour dispute went to trial on July 31, 2023 and resulted in a verdict in favor of plaintiff on all causes of action.  Judgment was entered in plaintiff’s favor on October 10, 2023 in the amount of $131,896.  Plaintiff now seeks attorney fees and costs as the prevailing party pursuant to Labor Code Section 218.5, in the amount of $48,600 and $1327 respectively.    

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for an Award of Attorney Fees and Costs is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff moves the court for reasonable attorney fees against defendants AAA Tax Consultants, Inc., aka Nationwide Tax, Classing Consulting, Inc. and Joseph Boodaie, jointly and severally, under Labor Code Section 218.5, which mandates an attorney fee award “[i]n any action brought for the nonpayment of wages,” if any party requests them at the initiation of the action. (§ 218.5, subd. (a).)  In the instant case, plaintiff requested attorneys’ fees and costs in the initial Complaint. 

 

The Court judgment awarded damages for 1) Unpaid Wages and Overtime, 2) Missed Meal

and Rest Breaks, 3) Improper Visa Reimbursement, 4) Waiting Time, and 5) Inaccurate Wage Statement Penalties.  Having prevailed on these claims, each of which are based on non-payment of wages, plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney fees under Section 218.5. 

 

Attorneys are entitled to compensation at hourly rates that reflect the market

value of their services in the community. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal. 4th 1084, 1095 (“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”)  Here, plaintiff’s trial counsel, Michael Short, charges $500 per hour, as does his partner, Mark Fang.  The court finds that based on their experience, as described in the declaration of Mr. Short, as well as the excellence of their written submissions, and Mr. Short’s extremely organized, cogent and efficient presentation at trial, that their hourly rate is fully justified and commensurate with attorneys of similar experience and skill in the local legal community.  

 

The court has reviewed the billing records submitted by Mr. Short and finds that all the work reported was reasonably performed and necessary to the litigation of this case.  The court finds no evidence of excessive, double billing or block billing.  Plaintiff’s counsel has fully established the reasonableness of the hours worked to litigate this case through trial, especially given the various delays engineered by defendant. 

 

Finally, the court has reviewed the costs submitted and agrees that all of the costs are properly recoverable under Section 218.5.

 

Accordingly, the court grants plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees in the total amount of $48,600 and costs in the amount of $1327.


Case Number: 22VECV02485    Hearing Date: December 13, 2023    Dept: W

ALLIANT CAPITAL LTD., ET AL. V. SANTORO HOLDINGS LLC, ET AL.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO (1) COMPEL DEFENDANT CAROLINE SMITH’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (2) COMPEL PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AND (3) IMPOSE MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST CAROLINE SMITH AND HER COUNSEL; PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH COURT ORDERED SANCTIONS AGAINST MARC H. RICHARDS AND MARC H. RICHARDS, APC AND FOR FURTHER SANCTIONS AND A CONTEMPT FINDING

 

Date of Hearing:        December 13, 2023                          Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02485

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Alliant Capital, LTD. and ADC Communities II, LLC

Opposing Party:        No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 20, 2022, Plaintiffs Alliant Capital LTD and ADC Communities II, LLC filed a complaint against Defendants Santoro Holdings LLC, Black Swan Properties, LLC, Craig Thomas individually and as trustee of the Carpe Vitam Revocable Trust dated July 13, 2017, Caroline Smith individually and as trustee of the CS Administrative Trust under the Santoro Family Trust dated September 29, 2010 for declaratory relief. Plaintiffs allege they have been forced to bring this action for declaratory relief because, following the death of Christopher Santoro (“Santoro”), with whom Plaintiffs developed several substantial multi-family rental projects, the trustees of the trusts that Plaintiffs understand hold Santoro’s interests have failed and refused, despite multiple requests over an eight-month period, to provide Plaintiffs with information demonstrating which of them is entitled to receive distributions in connection with each of the subject properties.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

I.                    Plaintiffs’ Motion to: (1) Compel Defendant Caroline Smith’s Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Production of Documents; (2) Compel Production of Documents; and (3) Impose Monetary Sanctions Against Caroline Smith and Her Counsel is GRANTED.

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Court Ordered Sanctions Against Marc H. Richards and Marc H. Richards, APC and for Further Sanctions and a Contempt Finding is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO: (1) COMPEL DEFENDANT CAROLINE SMITH’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFFS’ REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS; (2) COMPEL PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS; AND (3) IMPOSE MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST CAROLINE SMITH AND HER COUNSEL

 

Plaintiffs move the court for an order: (1) compelling Defendant Caroline Smith, individually and as trustee of the CS Administrative Trust under the Santoro Family Trust dated September 29, 2010 to serve further, verified written responses to Plaintiffs’ First and Second Sets of Requests for Production of Documents, (2) compelling Smith’s full production of documents; and (3) imposing monetary sanctions.

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2030.300(c); CCP § 2031.310(c).) The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2030.300(b); CCP § 2031.310(b).)¿¿¿¿ 

 

Plaintiffs argue Smith’s responses are evasive and incomplete. Plaintiffs’ contend Smith’s responses to Plaintiff's Requests for production are all identical and state:

 

Responding Party will produce those documents currently in her possession, custody or control that evidence, memorialize, reflect, reference, concern, and/or relate to Responding Party’s interests… (Biz Decl., Exh. D.)

 

However, Smith failed to produce any of the documents requested and did not state whether such documents even existed. The court finds there is good cause warranting the discovery sought by the demand. Plaintiffs contend the documents relate directly to key issues in this matter, and simply seek the production of documents and information bearing on the trusts’ or other entities’ — which set up by Santoro before his death — ownership interests in any of the multi-family projects in which Santoro had invested jointly with Plaintiffs. Defendant has failed to provide the requested documents.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Production of Documents, Set One and Two is GRANTED. Defendants are ordered to produce further discovery responses and documents in fourteen days.

 

Sanctions

 

Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust.  (CCP §§ 2031.310(h), 2030.300(d).)  

 

Plaintiffs seek $4,182.75 in sanctions against Smith and her attorney, jointly and severally, on the grounds Smith and her counsel provided evasive responses. Plaintiffs’ counsel contends Smith made no effort in response to Plaintiffs’ meet and confer attempts to justify her defective responses. (Biz Decl. ¶13.) As a result, Plaintiffs seek $4,182.75 for six hours preparing the motion at counsel’s hourly rate at $472.50 and .5 hours for the motion to be reviewed by Partner Michael Cypers as his hourly rate of $805.50. (Biz Decl. ¶14.) Counsel anticipates another two hours to draft a reply and two hours attending the hearing on the matter. (Biz Decl. ¶15.)

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $2,835.00. No opposition has been filed. The court finds only one hour necessary for attending the hearing on both motions.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH COURT ORDERED SANCTIONS AGAINST MARC H. RICHARDS AND MARC H. RICHARDS, APC AND FOR FURTHER SANCTIONS AND A CONTEMPT FINDING

 

Plaintiff moves the Court for: (a) an order compelling compliance with the Court Order dated September 12, 2023 requiring Marc H. Richards and Marc H. Richards, APC (together, “Richards”) to pay monetary sanctions; (b) a finding of violation of a court order compelling discovery pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010 et seq.; (c) the Court on its own motion to find Richards in contempt of court order; and (d) an order directing Richards to pay additional monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,347.75, for requiring Plaintiffs to seek this relief.

 

If a party fails to obey an order compelling responses to production requests, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, evidence sanction, or terminating sanction, and/or monetary sanctions, under Chapter 7 of the Discovery Act, i.e., sections 2023.010 to 2023.050 of the Code of Civil Procedure. (CCP § 2031.300(c).) However, numerous cases hold that severe sanctions (i.e., terminating or evidentiary sanctions) for failure to comply with a court order are allowed only where the failure was willful. (See R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495; Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545; Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) The “[d]isobedience of any lawful judgment, order, or process of the court” constitutes contempt. (CCP § 1209, subd. (a)(5).) When contempt is committed outside the presence of the Court, a proceeding is commenced by the filing of an affidavit charging the facts constituting contempt. (CCP § 1211.) The factual predicates to a finding of contempt are: (1) the rendition of a valid order, (2) actual knowledge of the order, (3) ability to comply, and (4) willful disobedience. (Conn v. Sup. Ct. (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 774, 784.) If the judicial officer finds that the affidavit charges facts sufficient to constitute contempt, an order to show cause may be issued. (CCP § 1212; In re Koehler (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1153, 1169.) 

 

Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds that on September 12, 2023, the Court issued an order for monetary sanctions for abuse of the discovery process against Defendant Caroline Smith, individually and as trustee of the CS Administrative Trust under the Santoro Family Trust dated September 29, 2010 (“Smith”) and her attorney Richards, in the sum of $4,725.00. Richards agreed to pay the sanctions on behalf of Smith. (Biz Decl. ¶5.) As of this date, Plaintiffs have not received the payment, and Richards has refused to return all attempts at communications on this or any other matter related to this case. (Biz Decl. ¶13.)

 

The court agrees Marc H. Richards and Marc H. Richards, APC has not complied with the court order and orders compliance with the September 12, 2023 Order. Moreover, the court finds additional monetary sanctions warranted. Plaintiff has followed up several times with Richards with no response.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel compliance with the September 12, 2023 Order is GRANTED. Richards is also ordered to pay Plaintiffs additional monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,347.75.

 

The court declines to set an OSC re Contempt as it does not believe threat of incarceration is an appropriate sanction for failure to pay a debt, even if it is in the form of a court order to pay sanctions.
