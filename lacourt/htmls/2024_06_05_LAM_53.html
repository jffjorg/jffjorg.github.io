Case Number: 22STCV32743    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: 53

[tentative] Order RE:

 

(1)   defendant’s demurrer to second amended complaint

(2)   defendant’s motion to strike portions of second amended complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Department of Motor Vehicles

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Gladys Hampton

(1)   Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike Portions of Second Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with the demurrer and motion to strike.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Gladys Hampton (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative Second Amended Complaint in this action on November 13, 2023, against defendant Department of Motor Vehicles (“Defendant”).  Plaintiff alleges 10 causes of action for (1) disability discrimination; (2) failure to accommodate; (3) failure to engage in the interactive process; (4) discrimination on the basis of gender; (5) discrimination on the basis of age; (6) harassment on the basis of disability, gender, and age; (7) retaliation; (8) failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation; (9) negligence; and (10) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Defendant now moves the court for an order (1) sustaining its demurrer to each cause of action alleged by Plaintiff, and (2) striking from the Second Amended Complaint the ninth and 10th causes of action and various allegations that it contends are inconsistent or sham allegations.

DEMURRER

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the first through third causes of action because they state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action on the ground that Plaintiff has adequately alleged that she suffers from a mental disability by alleging that she (1) developed severe anxiety, stress, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder after being assaulted by a coworker (SAC ¶¶ 13, 30, 38), which (2) limited her ability to perform major life activities, including interference with her ability to perform her job (SAC ¶¶ 45, 74-75), and therefore has alleged that she has a mental or psychological disorder or condition or emotional or mental illness that limits a major life activity.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Higgins-Williams v. Sutter Medical Foundation (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 78, 84 [elements of prima facie case of mental disability discrimination under FEHA]; Cuiellette v. City of Los Angeles (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 757, 766 [elements of failure to accommodate claim]; Gov. Code, §§ 12926, subd. (j) [defining mental disability], 12940, subd. (n).)  

The court acknowledges, as Defendant argues, that “[a]n employee’s inability to work under a particular supervisor because of anxiety and stress related to the supervisor’s standard oversight of the employee’s job performance does not constitute a disability under FEHA.”  (Higgins-Williams, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 84.)  The court further acknowledges that Plaintiff has alleged that she “suffered from several disabilities, including but not limited to psychological hardship, including high levels of stress and anxiety, caused by conditions at her workplace, as well as physical disabilities due to hostile work environment,” such that the resultant stress and anxiety may not qualify as a mental disability to the extent that the alleged emotional distress may be interpreted to constitute an inability to work for Defendant because of its employees’ standard oversight of Plaintiff’s job performance.  (SAC ¶ 65 [emphasis added].)  However, Plaintiff has also alleged—and thus the court must accept as true—that she suffers from anxiety, depression, and post-traumatic stress disorder as a result of the assault committed by a coworker, which is not a mere inability to work with a certain individual “because of anxiety and stress related to the supervisor’s standard oversight of the employee’s job performance . . . .”  (SAC ¶ 13; Higgins-Williams, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 84 [emphasis added].)

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action for disability discrimination because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff did not allege facts establishing that the adverse employment actions alleged (i.e., the denials of her requests for transfer) were taken because of her mental disability.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Higgins-Williams, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 84 [plaintiff alleging mental disability discrimination must allege that “she was subjected to an adverse employment action because of the disability”] [emphasis added].)  Instead, Plaintiff has expressly alleged that (1) her request for a lateral transfer “was denied by Ms. Bowie in retaliation for Plaintiff’s complaints about her supervisor, Ms. Bowie” (SAC ¶ 27 [emphasis added]) and to subject her “to further harassment” (SAC ¶ 28), and (2) her request to be transferred to the El Segundo office was denied (i) as a result of Defendant’s failure to engage in the interactive process, which does not constitute an adverse employment action, and (ii) to subject her to further harassment (SAC ¶ 42). 

To the extent that Plaintiff has attempted to allege that Defendant’s failure to accommodate her and to timely interact in a good faith interactive process constitutes adverse employment actions, the court disagrees.  (SAC ¶ 67; Doe v. Department of Corrections & Rehabilitation (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 721, 735-736 [“No court has ever held that a failure to reasonably accommodate an employee’s disability—which is a separate cause of action under FEHA [citation]—can qualify as the adverse action underlying a discrimination or retaliation claim”] [internal citation omitted]; Brown v. Los Angeles Unified School District (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1106 [“the commission of one specific prohibited employment practice does not, in and of itself, constitute commission of all other prohibited employment practices under the broad rubric of policies or practices affecting the ‘terms, conditions or privileges of employment’”].)   Moreover, although Plaintiff has also alleged that she “received . . . negative performance review[s]” in 2019 and 2020 (SAC ¶¶ 23-24), Plaintiff also alleged that she never “receive[d] a write-up or performance improvement plan” and that “[n]one of the procedures associated with a negative performance review were followed” (SAC ¶ 25), such that she has not alleged facts establishing that the negative performance reviews “‘materially affect[ed] the terms, conditions, or privileges of [Plaintiff’s] employment.’”  (Feathersone v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 1150, 1161 [internal citation omitted] [emphasis in original].)

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the second and third causes of action because they state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because (1) Plaintiff has alleged that she was not offered a reasonable accommodation, and (2) even if the First Amended Complaint alleged that she was offered telework to prevent her from working in the presence of the coworker that assaulted her, that allegation alone does not establish that such an offer constitutes a reasonable accommodation within the meaning of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (Gov. Code, § 12900 et seq.).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); SAC ¶¶ 44 [Defendant “never provided reasonable accommodations that accommodated Plaintiff’s mental health disabilities”], 50.)

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for discrimination on the basis of gender because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) in support of this cause of action, Plaintiff has alleged that she “was subjected to an adverse employment decision in that she was subjected to unjustified harassment due to her gender relating to her duties as an employee of Defendant” (SAC ¶ 94), which describes harassment, and not discrimination, and (2) Plaintiff has not alleged, in the factual background of the Second Amended Complaint, facts establishing that she suffered an adverse employment action under circumstances suggesting discriminatory motive.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 706 [“Because the FEHA treats harassment in a separate provision, there is no reason to construe the FEHA’s prohibition against discrimination broadly to include harassment”]; Khoiny v. Dignity Health (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 390, 397 [elements of prima facie case of discrimination].)

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action for discrimination on the basis of age because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has not alleged facts establishing that she suffered an adverse employment action under circumstances that give rise to an inference of unlawful discrimination on the basis of her age.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Sandell v. Taylor-Listug, Inc. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 297, 321 [elements of prima facie case of age discrimination].)

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the sixth cause of action for harassment because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has alleged facts establishing that Defendant harassed her (1) by forcing her “to oversee the ‘onboarding’” of the coworker who assaulted her (SAC ¶ 34), and (2) such harassment was committed on the basis of her disability, age, and gender (SAC ¶¶ 115-116).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Martin v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 149, 170 [elements of prima facie case of unlawful harassment].)

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the seventh cause of action for retaliation because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiff has alleged facts establishing that (1) she engaged in protected activity by complaining about harassment (SAC ¶¶ 18-21, 126), and (2) Defendant subjected her to an adverse employment action by denying her 2019 lateral transfer request (SAC ¶ 27).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Meeks v. Autozone, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 855, 878-879 [elements of prima facie case for retaliation under FEHA].)

The court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the eighth cause of action for failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since the court has overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the harassment and retaliation causes of action, and therefore those causes of action may support this derivative claim.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

            The court finds, for the reasons set forth in connection with the court’s ruling on the motion to strike, that the ninth and 10th causes of action should be stricken.  The court therefore does not rule on Defendant’s arguments that the ninth and 10th causes of action do not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff did not comply with the claim presentation requirements, and (2) Defendant is immune from liability.

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  The court finds that Plaintiff has not met her burden to show in what manner she can amend her first, fourth, and fifth causes of action to render them sufficient against Defendant.  The court therefore sustains Defendant’s demurrer to those causes of action without leave to amend.

MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendant moves the court for an order striking from Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint (1) the ninth and 10th causes of action in their entirety; (2) the portions of paragraphs 42, 43, 44, 49, and 50 that allege that Defendant did not engage in a meaningful interactive process with Plaintiff and failed to provide her reasonable accommodations; (3) the references to or allegations regarding the existence of Plaintiffs “post-traumatic stress disorder” set forth in portions of paragraphs 13, 30, 32, 38, 51, 137, and 143; and (4) paragraphs 31, 34, 34, and 36, in their entirety, which allege facts regarding the employment of Mr. Burns, the coworker that allegedly assaulted Plaintiff, and the effect thereof on Plaintiff.

First, the court grants Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s ninth and 10th causes of action because those causes of action were not filed in conformity with an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)

“Following an order sustaining a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings with leave to amend, the plaintiff may amend his or her complaint only as authorized by the court’s order.  [Citation.]  The plaintiff may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so, unless the new cause of action is within the scope of the order granting leave to amend.”  (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023 [internal citation omitted].)  On October 23, 2023, the court issued an order (1) sustaining Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first through eighth causes of action, and (2) granting Plaintiff 20 days leave to file an amended complaint “that cures the deficiencies set forth above” in that order.  (Oct. 23, 2023 Order, p. 5:8-10.)  The court did not authorize Plaintiff to add two new causes of action for negligence and intentional infliction of emotional distress against Defendant.  (Ibid.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff violated the scope of the court’s October 23, 2023 order granting leave to amend by adding the ninth and 10th causes of action and therefore exercises its discretion to strike them from the Second Amended Complaint in their entirety.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)  Plaintiff did not address this issue in her opposition papers.  Thus, the court strikes these causes of action without leave to amend.

Second, the court denies Defendant’s motion to strike the portions of paragraphs 42, 43, 44, 49, and 50 that allege that Defendant did not engage in a meaningful interactive process with Plaintiff and failed to provide her with accommodations, the references to Plaintiffs “post-traumatic stress disorder” in paragraphs 13, 30, 32, 38, 51, 137, and 143, and paragraphs 31, 34, 34, and 36, in their entirety, because the court finds that Defendant has not met its burden to show that those allegations are improper, irrelevant, or false sham allegations.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 426 [“Under the sham pleading doctrine, plaintiffs are precluded from amending complaints to omit harmful allegations, without explanation, from previous complaints to avoid attacks raised in demurrers or motions for summary judgment”].)

ORDER

            The court sustains defendant Department of Motor Vehicle’s demurrer to plaintiff Gladys Hampton’s first cause of action for disability discrimination, fourth cause of action for gender discrimination, and fifth cause of action for age discrimination, without leave to amend.  

            The court overrules defendant Department of Motor Vehicle’s demurrer to plaintiff Gladys Hampton’s second, third, and sixth through eighth causes of action.

            The court grants defendant Department of Motor Vehicle’s motion to strike plaintiff Gladys Hampton’s ninth cause of action for negligence and 10th cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress without leave to amend.

            The court denies all other relief requested in defendant Department of Motor Vehicle’s motion to strike.

The court orders defendant Department of Motor Vehicle to file an answer to plaintiff Gladys Hampton’s Second Amended Complaint within 10 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders defendant Department of Motor Vehicle to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

cross-defendants’ demurrer to second amended cross-complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., and Andre Morgan                    

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC

Demurrer to Second Amened Cross-Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this demurrer.

DISCUSSION

Cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc. and Andre Morgan (“Cross-Defendants”) move the court for an order sustaining their demurrer to the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action alleged by cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC (“Cross-Complainant”) in its Second Amended Cross-Complaint.

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action for negligence because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainant has alleged facts establishing that Cross-Defendants, who acted as the real estate broker and agent for plaintiff and cross-defendant Patricia Galea (“Galea”), owed Cross-Complainant a duty of care.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Romero v. Los Angeles Rams (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 562, 567 [elements of cause of action for negligence].)

“The extent of the broker’s duty of care is determined under common law by examining whether a reasonable person would have foreseen an unreasonable risk of harm to a third party and whether, in view of such risk, the broker exercised ordinary care under the circumstances.  [Citation.]  Whether a real estate broker has a duty of care to a third party is a question of law that is determined by weighing the following factors: (1) the extent that the transaction was intended to affect the third party; (2) the foreseeability of harm; (3) the degree of certainty that the third party suffered injury; (4) the closeness of the connection between the broker’s conduct and the injury suffered; (5) the moral blame attached to the broker’s conduct; and (6) the policy of preventing future harm . . . .”  (Greif v. Sanin (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 412, 428 [internal citations omitted].) 

Here, Cross-Complainant has alleged, for purposes of demurrer, facts establishing the existence of a duty by alleging that (1) Cross-Complainant entered into the subject purchase contract with Galea (such that the transaction was directly intended to affect Cross-Complainant) (SACC ¶ 3), (2) it was foreseeable to Cross-Defendants that failing to prepare the contingency removal with accuracy would harm Cross-Complainant since Cross-Complainant relied on the details of the contingency removal in order to proceed with the transaction (SACC ¶¶ 34-35),   (3) Cross-Complainant has been damaged by Cross-Defendants’ failure to accurately prepare the contingency removal, including because it has had to expend attorney’s fees and costs to pursue this action against Galea and has suffered lost profits because of the downturn in the market for similar properties (SACC ¶ 35), and (4) Cross-Defendants “failed to include receipt of the Certificate of Occupancy prior to the date scheduled for close of escrow as an additional excepted item despite the ongoing, openly discussed, and transparent discussion surrounding the City’s delay in issuance of the C of O” (SACC ¶ 30), such that Cross-Defendants knew that Galea required issuance of the certificate of occupancy but failed to include that in the contingency removal, thereby establishing the closeness of the connection between Cross-Defendants’ conduct and Cross-Complainant’s injury and the moral blame attached to Cross-Defendants’ conduct.  (Greif, supra, 74 Cal.App.5th at pp. 428, 434.)  The court, in considering the factors set forth above and the facts alleged in the Second Amended Cross-Complaint, finds that Cross-Complainant has alleged sufficient facts to establish the existence of a legal duty in support of this cause of action.

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action for negligence on the ground of misjoinder because Cross-Defendants have not met their burden to show that there is a misjoinder or defect of parties.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (d).)

The court sustains Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainant has not alleged the element of the existence of a fiduciary relationship between it and Cross-Defendants.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); O’Neal v. Stanislaus County Employees’ Retirement Assn. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1184, 1215 [elements of cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty].)

“While real estate brokers owe their own clients fiduciary duties, they owe third parties who are not their clients, including the adverse party in a real estate transaction, only those duties imposed by regulatory statutes.  [Citation.]  These duties include a general obligation of                 ‘ “honesty, fairness, and full disclosure toward all parties.” ’”  (Saffie v. Schmeling (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 564, 568 [internal citations omitted].)  Cross-Complainant contends that Cross-Defendants owed it fiduciary duties of honesty, fair dealing, and good faith pursuant to Civil Code section 2079.16.  (SACC ¶ 39.)

As set forth above, Cross-Complainant has alleged that Cross-Defendants “served as the real estate broker and agent for Galea” (SACC ¶ 38.)  As the buyer’s agent, Cross-Defendants owed (1) to Galea, a fiduciary duty of utmost care, integrity, honesty, and loyalty in dealings with her, and (2) to both Galea and Cross-Complainant, obligations to diligently exercise reasonable skill and care in performing their duties, duties of honest and fair dealing and good faith, and a duty to disclose all facts known to them materially affecting the value or desirability of the property that are not known to, or are not within the diligent attention and observation of, the parties.  (Civ. Code, § 2079.16.)  Thus, pursuant to this statutory disclosure form, Cross-Defendants did not owe Cross-Complainant a fiduciary duty, and instead owed it only general duties and obligations to exercise reasonable care and skill in the discharge of their duties and a duty of honest and fair dealing and good faith.  (Ibid.; Horiike v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1024, 1031 [“the disclosure form says that an agent representing only one of the two parties (i.e., an exclusive agent of the buyer or the seller) does not owe fiduciary duties to the other party to the transaction, though it does owe duties of ‘[d]iligent exercise of reasonable skill and care in performance of the agent’s duties’ and ‘honest and fair dealing and good faith’ to both parties”].)  Cross-Complainant has neither alleged nor argued that Cross-Defendants were acting as a dual agent for both itself and Galea so as to impose the existence of a fiduciary duty on that ground.  (Civ. Code, § 2079.16 [“In a dual agency situation, the agent has the following affirmative obligations to both the Seller and the Buyer; [¶] (1) A fiduciary duty of utmost care, integrity, honesty, and loyalty in the dealings with either the Seller or the Buyer”].)  Thus, the court finds that Cross-Complainant has not alleged facts establishing the existence of a fiduciary relationship between it and Cross-Defendants.

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action for indemnification because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainant has alleged that (1) Galea has filed suit against Cross-Complainant in this action, alleging that it is liable for her injuries, but that Cross-Defendants caused the injuries described by Galea in her Complaint due to their negligent or wrongful conduct (SACC ¶¶ 44, 46), and   (2) Cross-Complainant is entitled to recover from Cross-Defendants all amounts which may be paid by way of compromise, settlement or judgment in favor of Galea, as well as costs and expenses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); C.W. Howe Partners Inc. v. Mooradian (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 688, 700 [“The elements of a cause of action for [equitable] indemnity are (1) a showing of fault on the part of indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is . . . equitably responsible”] [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  The court finds that Cross-Complainant has not met its burden to show in what manner it can amend its fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty against Cross-Defendants.  The court therefore sustains the demurrer to that cause of action without leave to amend.

ORDER

            The court overrules cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., and Andre Morgan’s demurrer to cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC’s third and fifth causes of action.

            The court sustains cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., and Andre Morgan’s demurrer to cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC’s fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty without leave to amend.

            The court orders cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., and Andre Morgan to file an answer to cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC’s Second Amended Cross-Complaint within 10 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders cross-defendants Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., and Andre Morgan to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff / Judgment Creditor 101 Pine Street, LLC  

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion for Turnover Order for Artwork

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff and judgment creditor 101 Pine Street, LLC (“Judgment Creditor”) moves the court for a turnover order for assignment and possession of certain personal property and documentary evidence thereof belonging to defendants and judgment debtors Jeroen Bik and Miray Bik (“Judgment Debtors”).

“[A]t the conclusion of a proceeding pursuant to this article, the court may order the judgment debtor’s interest in the property in the possession or under the control of the judgment debtor or the third person or a debt owed by the third person to the judgment debtor to be applied toward the satisfaction of the money judgment if the property is not exempt from enforcement of a money judgment.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.205, subd. (a).)  The court may not order property or debt to be applied toward the satisfaction of a money judgment pursuant to this statute “[i]f a third person examined pursuant to Section 708.120 claims an interest in the property adverse to the judgment debtor or denies the debt and the court does not determine the matter as provided in subdivision (a) of Section 708.180 . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.205, subd. (b).)

Similarly, “[i]f a writ of execution is issued, the judgment creditor may apply to the court . . . for an order directing the judgment debtor to transfer to the levying officer either or both of the following: [¶] (1) Possession of the property sought to be levied upon if the property is sought to be levied upon by taking it into custody.  [¶]  (2) Possession of documentary evidence of title to property of or a debt owed to the judgment debtor that is sought to be levied upon.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 699.040, subd. (a).)  The court may issue an order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 699.040 “upon a showing of need for the order.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 699.040, subd. (b).)

On January 12, 2023, the court entered judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1710.25 in favor of Judgment Creditor and against Judgment Debtors in the total amount of $140,547.08, consisting of (1) $138,473.17 on the sister state judgment, (2) $1,638.91 in accrued interest on the sister state judgment, and (3) $435 in filing fees for entry of the sister state judgment.  (Jan. 12, 2023 Judgment, p. 1.)  Thereafter, on September 18, 2023, a writ of execution was issued. 

Judgment Creditor has presented evidence establishing (1) that it “has been unsuccessful in collecting any amounts under its Judgment” from Judgment Debtors, and (2) that, during the debtor examinations of Judgment Debtors, Judgment Debtors identified $85,000 in artwork that is located in their property on Cavalleri Road (in Malibu, California), and not pledged to any other creditor.  (Allyn Decl., ¶ 5; Allyn Decl., Ex. 3, Aug. 9, 2023 Judgment Debor Examination Transcript, pp. 61:18-21 [testifying that the art that is the subject of the appraisal belongs to Judgment Debtors], 62:2-10, 63:16-18 [testifying that Judgment Debtors have not pledged any pieces of the subject artwork and sculptures to other creditors]; Allyn Decl., Ex. 4 [Appraisal Report for Judgment Debtors’ artwork].)  Judgment Debtors have not filed an opposition to this motion, contending that Judgment Creditor does not have a need for a turnover order, that Judgment Debtors are not in possession of the subject property, or that the property is exempt from enforcement of a money judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 699.040, subd. (b) [court may issue turnover order “upon a showing of need for the order”], 708.205, subd. (a) [court may issue turnover order “if the property is not exempt from enforcement of a money judgment”].)  

Thus, the court finds that Judgment Creditor has met its burden to show that the court may issue a turnover order as requested because Judgment Creditor has shown that (1) a writ of execution has been issued, but that Judgment Creditor has not recovered the amounts due under the judgment, such that Judgment Creditor has shown “need for the [turnover] order” pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 699.040, and (2) a judgment debtor examination as to both Judgment Debtors has taken place, such that Judgment Creditor has shown that the court may issue a turnover order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 708.205.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 699.040, subds. (a), (b), 708.205, subd. (a).)  The court therefore grants Judgment Creditor’s motion.  (Ibid.)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff and judgment creditor 101 Pine Street, LLC’s motion for turnover order for artwork.

            The court will sign and file the proposed “Turnover Order for Artwork Against Defendants / Judgment Debtors Jeroen Bik and Miray Bik,” lodged by judgment creditor 101 Pine Street, LLC on October 5, 2023, as modified by the court.

            The court orders plaintiff and judgment creditor 101 Pine Street, LLC to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff and cross-defendant Madeline Moore

(2)   Motion for Reconsideration  

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with plaintiff Madeline Moore’s motion for reconsideration.  

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion for reconsideration.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

Plaintiff Madeline Moore (“Plaintiff”) requests that the court reconsider its order, dated May 22, 2024 and filed May 23, 2024, granting the motion in limine number 16 filed by defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP (“Defendants”).

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration because Marshall Oldman does not have special knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education sufficient to qualify him as an expert on the subject to which his testimony relates (Evid. Code, § 720).

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

Defendants request that the court reconsider its order, dated May 22, 2024 and filed May 23, 2024, denying their motions in limine numbers 2 and 13 to, respectively, preclude Plaintiff from presenting (1) evidence relating to the matter of Madeline Moore v. Crystal Ann Smith (Case No. LC101816), and (2) the expert opinion of Marc Sallus regarding the filing of the Madeline Moore v. Crystal Ann Smith (Case No. LC101816) matter.

The court finds that it should reconsider and modify its order denying Defendants’ motions in limine numbers 2 and 13 and therefore grants Defendants’ motion.[1]  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); State of California v. Superior Court (Flynn) (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 94, 100 [“Even without a change of law, a trial court has the inherent power to reconsider its prior rulings on its own motion at any time before entry of judgment”].)

First, although Plaintiff argued, in her opposition papers filed in connection with these motions in limine, that Defendants’ “conduct in connection with the case of Moore v. Smith has always been part of the action and MRK’s cross-action for fees[,]” the court disagrees.  (Pl. Opp. to MIL No. 2, filed Dec. 20, 2023, p. 1:26-27 [italics added]; Pl. Opp. to MIL No. 13, filed Feb. 15, 2024, p. 1:27-28 [italics added].)  In support of that assertion, Plaintiff cited (1) paragraph 29 of her First Amended Complaint, and (2) her response to form interrogatory number 15.1.  However, that allegation in the First Amended Complaint does not allege that Defendants breached their fiduciary duties to Plaintiff in connection with the Moore v. Smith action.  (FAC ¶ 29.)  Similarly, although the court recognizes that Plaintiff’s response to form interrogatory number 15.1 alleges that Mr. Riley insisted that she file Moore v. Smith, she did not allege that that conduct was negligent or breached a duty of care owed to her.  (Pl. Opps., Ex. A.)  Instead, she alleges that “it appears from propounding party’s billing invoices that responding party overpaid on the Moore v. Smith matter by approximately at least $13,500, an amount that must be credited or returned to her.”  (Ibid.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff did not support her contention that Defendants’ conduct in connection with the Moore v. Smith action “has always been part of” this action.

Second, the court finds that the evidence regarding Defendants’ conduct in connection with the Moore v. Smith action is not relevant to Plaintiff’s affirmative defense of offset because Plaintiff’s affirmative defenses of offset do not allege that they are based on Defendants’ conduct in connection with that action.  (Pl. Answer to Cross-Complaint filed May 31, 2018, pp. 5:10-23, 6:23-27.)  The court further finds that Plaintiff’s affirmative defense of offset is limited to her allegation that she overpaid approximately $13,500 to Defendants in connection with the Moore v. Smith action because, as set forth above, Plaintiff (1) did not allege that Defendants’ conduct in the Moore v. Smith action forms the basis of her affirmative defense of offset, and (2) alleged that she overpaid this amount in her response to form interrogatory number 15.1.

Third, the court finds that, because the affirmative defense of offset “is equitable in nature” and “‘occurs at the end of litigation[,]’” the court, not the jury, will decide whether the defense applies, at the end of trial.  (Los Angeles Unified School District v. Torres Construction Corp. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 480, 500.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff is not entitled to present evidence to prove offset as an affirmative defense at trial before the jury.  (Ibid. [noting that the plaintiff “would not have been required to disprove the affirmative defense of setoff at a jury trial, and [the defendant] would not have been entitled to prove it as an affirmative defense at such trial”].)

The court therefore orders that its order dated May 22, 2024 and filed May 23, 2024 is modified as follows.

The portion of the court’s order denying defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 2 is vacated.  The court issues a new order granting defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 2 because (1) evidence relating to the matter of Moore v. Smith (Case No. LC101816) is not relevant to the issues to be determined by the jury (Evid. Code, § 350), and (2) the court exercises its discretion to exclude such evidence because its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will (a) necessitate undue consumption of time and (b) create substantial danger of undue prejudice, of confusing the issues, or of misleading the jury (Evid. Code, § 352). 

The court grants defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 2, and orders that plaintiff Madeline Moore shall not introduce evidence relating to the matter of Moore v. Smith (Case No. LC101816), without prejudice to plaintiff Madeline Moore’s presenting evidence to the court (out of the presence of the jury) at the end of the trial to support her affirmative defense of offset for her alleged overpayment of approximately $13,500 in fees in connection with the Moore v. Smith (Case No. LC101816) action.

The portion of the court’s order denying defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 13 is vacated, and the court issues a new order granting defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 13 because (1) the expert opinion of Plaintiff’s standard of care expert, Marc Sallus, regarding the filing of the Moore v. Smith (Case No. LC101816) action is not relevant to the issues to be determined by the jury (Evid. Code, § 350), and (2) the court exercises its discretion to exclude such evidence because its probative value is substantially outweighed by the probability that its admission will (a) necessitate undue consumption of time and (b) create substantial danger of undue prejudice, of confusing the issues, or of misleading the jury (Evid. Code, § 352).  

The court grants defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP’s motion in limine number 13, and orders that plaintiff Madeline Moore shall not introduce the expert opinion of Plaintiff’s standard of care expert, Marc Sallus, regarding the filing of the Moore v. Smith (Case No. LC101816) action.

The court orders defendants Dennis P. Riley and Mesisca, Riley & Kreitenberg LLP to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
