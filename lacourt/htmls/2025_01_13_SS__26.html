If you desire to submit on the tentative ruling, you may do so by e-mailing Dept. 26 at the Spring Street Courthouse until the morning of the motion hearing.

The e-mail address is SSCdept26@lacourt.org

The heading on your e-mail should contain the case name, number, hearing date, and that you submit. The message should indicate your name, contact information, and the party you represent. Please note, the above e-mail address is to inform the court of your submission on the tentative ruling. All other inquiries will not receive a response.

If there are no appearances by either side and no submission on the Court's tentative ruling, the matter will be placed OFF CALENDAR. 

Due to overcrowding concerns of COVID-19, all parties shall make every effort to schedule a remote appearance via LACourtConnect (https://my.lacourt.org/laccwelcome) for their next hearing. The parties shall register with LACourtConnect at least 2 hours prior to their scheduled hearing time.

Case Number: 21TRCV00820    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 26

Shalyer v. Prairie Hospitality Group, Inc., et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiff James Shayler’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,636.14, AS DETAILED HEREIN. 

ANALYSIS:

On November 5, 2021, Plaintiff James Shayler (“Plaintiff”) filed the Complaint in this action against Defendants Prairie Hospitality Group Inc. (“Defendant PHG”), William B. Serns and Eydie Serns (“the Serns Defendants”). The action was assigned to an unlimited civil courtroom, Department 8 in the Torrance Courthouse. Default was entered against Defendant PHG on February 22, 2022, and against the Serns Defendants on April 25, 2022.

On May 4, 2022, Plaintiff and Defendant PHG stipulated to vacate the entry of default and Defendant PHG filed its answer on May 16, 2022. Plaintiff stipulated to the same with the Serns Defendants on August 2, 2022, who filed their answer on October 3, 2022. Plaintiff then dismissed Defendant PHG on October 18, 2022. Defendant PHG filed a memorandum of costs on October 28, 2022. On June 29, 2023, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike, or in the alternative, to Tax Costs sought in Defendant PHG’s memorandum of costs. (Minute Order, 06/29/23.)

The case was then reclassified to the limited jurisdiction court by stipulation on May 3, 2024. (Stip and Order, 05/03/24.) On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the Serns Defendants from the action. The Serns Defendants filed a memorandum of costs on the same date.

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Tax Costs on November 7, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date. 

Discussion

Plaintiff moves to tax the costs from the Serns Defendants’ Memorandum of Costs, filed on October 23, 2024. The Motion is brought pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b) and Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5. Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 states in relevant part: 

(c) An award of costs shall be subject to the following:

(1) Costs are allowable if incurred, whether or not paid.

(2) Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.

(3) Allowable costs shall be reasonable in amount.

(4) Items not mentioned in this section and items assessed upon application may be allowed or denied in the court's discretion. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c).)

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1).) The Memorandum of Costs was electronically served on Plaintiff on October 23, 2024. The instant Motion to Tax Costs was timely filed and served 15 days later.

Plaintiff objects to specific costs on the grounds that they were (1) not incurred by the Serns Defendants; and (2) already paid to defense counsel in July 2023 to compensate costs incurred by Defendant PHG. These costs include Item 4, deposition costs in the amount of $1,841.00, Item 9, court ordered transcripts in the amount of $50.00, Item 14, fees for electronic filing or service in the amount of $737.67, Item 15, fees for hosting electronic documents in the amount of $20.00, and Item 16, LASC Case Documents Access in the amount of $61.65. 

For Item 4, deposition costs in the amount of $1,841.00, Plaintiff demonstrates that the deposition was noticed by Defendant PHG, not by the Serns Defendants. (Motion, Janowski Decl., ¶5 and Exh. D.) Item 9, court ordered transcripts in the amount of $50.00, correspond to a motion to compel further responses brought by Defendant PHG with respect to discovery propounded only by Defendant PHG. (Id. at ¶6 and Exh. E.) For Item 14, fees for electronic filing or service in the amount of $663.49, Plaintiff shows that these fees were actually incurred by Defendant PHG for its filings and were claimed by Defendant PHG in its memorandum of costs. (Id. at ¶2 and Exhs. A-B.) The filings included Defendant PHG’s stipulation to vacate default, answer, case management statement, motion to compel, memorandum of costs, opposition to motion to tax costs, and ex parte application to continue hearing date on motion to tax costs. (Ibid.) Plaintiff challenges Item 15, fees for hosting electronic documents in the amount of $20.00 for failing to show that it was incurred by any party. Finally, Item 16, LASC Case Documents Access in the amount of $61.65 was incurred with respect to Defendant PHG’s motion to compel. (Id. at ¶¶4, 6 and Exhs. C, E.)

This evidence raises a successful, and uncontested challenge to the costs claimed by the Serns Defendants on the grounds that they were not actually incurred, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (c). The Serns Defendants have filed no opposition to the instant Motion to demonstrate that the costs are proper. Therefore, the memorandum of costs filed on October 23, 2024 is taxed in the amount of $2,636.14.

Conclusion 

Plaintiff James Shayler’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,636.14, AS DETAILED HEREIN.

Case Number: 23STLC02758    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 26

LAC Verdugo Operations, LLC v. Yelsik, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Motion of Defendant Evelyn Faith Corder, individually, and as Successor Trustee of the 2014 Dorothy Louise Yelsik Revocable Trust for Relief from Default and/or Default Judgment is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON APRIL 29, 2024 AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT ENTERED ON JULY 29, 2024 ARE HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANT IS TO FILE THE PROPOSED ANSWER WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. THE PARTIES ARE ALSO ORDERED TO FILE A JOINT REPORT REGARDING STATUS OF SETTLEMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.                                                                                                       

ANALYSIS: 

On April 25, 2023, Plaintiff LAC Verdugo Operations, LLC, dba Glendale Post Acute Center (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Dorothy Yelsik aka Dorothy Louise Yelsik (“Yelsik”) and Evelyn Faith Corder, individually, and as Successor Trustee of the 2014 Dorothy Louise Yelsik Revocable Trust (“Defendant”), and Does 1-20, alleging causes of action for Breach of Written Contract, Common Counts, and Quantum Meruit. Yelsik was subsequently dismissed on July 10, 2023. 

On June 9, 2023, the Court issued a “Notice of Rejection of Pleadings Re: Defendant’s proposed answer.” Defendant was directed to submit the answer in proper format and to use the correct forms. (6/9/23 Notice of Rejection.) Following Defendant’s failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered their default on April 29, 2024 and default judgment on July 29, 2024.

On October 18, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Notice of Motion and Motion for Relief from Default and/or Default Judgment (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 14, 2024, and Defendant replied on November 22, 2024.

Discussion

Defendant moves to vacate the entry of default and default judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). An application for discretionary relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) must be made within a reasonable time, no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought, and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the party’s excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) The motion must also be accompanied by a copy of the moving defendant’s proposed pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) This can be corrected if the defendant submits a proposed responsive pleading by the hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403.)The Motion is supported by Defendant’s declaration, which explains she made multiple attempts to file a responsive pleading in May 2023 but was hindered by her lack of knowledge of California court procedure. (Motion, Corder Decl., ¶¶4-5 and Exhs. 4-7.) Defendant’s attempt to respond upon receipt of Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment was also unsuccessful. (Id. at ¶¶6-7 and Exhs. 12-14.) Until September 2024, Defendant an elderly person living in New Mexico, could not find an attorney to take the case. (Id. at ¶2.)


In opposition, Plaintiff explains that its attorney has been in communication with Defendant since May 2023 and sought to settle the action. (Opp., Salamoff Decl., ¶¶3-9.) Plaintiff contends that Defendant agreed to a settlement, and that a check Defendant sent to Plaintiff’s counsel in the summer of 2023 was rejected by the bank. (Id. at ¶10.) Plaintiff also offers evidence that more than a year later, the parties may have reached another settlement and Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel a cashier’s check in September 2023. (Id. at ¶¶21-23.) Plaintiff’s counsel was then contacted by defense counsel in October 2023 with a retraction of the settlement agreement and request to return the cashier’s check. (Id. at ¶¶24-26.) 

The court notes that no party has submitted a copy of a signed, or enforceable, settlement agreement.

As an initial matter, the Court finds the Motion is timely. Where the statute requires that “ ‘ “the application must be made within a ‘reasonable time’ … what is a reasonable time in any case depends upon the circumstances of that particular case.” While in “the determination of that question, a large discretion is necessarily confided to [the trial] court” ... there must be some showing—some evidence—as the basis for the exercise of such discretion.’ ” (Caldwell v. Methodist Hospital (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1521, 1524 [citing Carrasco v. Craft (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 796, 805].) The Motion contends that moving for relief almost six months after entry of default was reasonable based on Defendant’s inability to file a pleading without the assistance of counsel, whom she only was able to retain in September 2024.

The same facts demonstrate that Defendant’s failure to timely file a responsive pleading was the result of excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. As Defendant points out, “[u]nless inexcusable neglect is clear, the policy favoring trial on the merits prevails. Doubts are resolved in favor of the application for relief from default, and reversal of an order denying relief results. Reversal is particularly appropriate where relieving the default will not seriously prejudice the opposing party.” (Elston v. Turlock (1985) 38 Cal. 3d 227, 235.) Moreover, the evidence attached to the papers indicates that the parties were in the process of settling the claims, but had an unresolved dispute over the accuracy of outstanding balance reflected in the health insurance billing information, and what Defendant owed Plaintiff for care rendered to Defendant Yelsik, Defendant’s sister. (Motion, Corder Decl., ¶¶10-11 and Exh. 16; Opp., Salamoff Decl., ¶15.) None of the evidence clarifies whether there was an eventual meeting of the minds regarding the outstanding balance, the amount owed, or the sum and terms under which the matter would settle.   

It is excusable that Defendant did not file a responsive pleading based on all these circumstances: several unsuccessful attempts to file a response, ongoing settlement discussions with Plaintiff’s counsel, unresolved disputes regarding the accuracy of the billing and what was owed for Yelsik’s care,  and the difficulty in finding an attorney. Therefore, relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is granted.   

The same facts demonstrate that Defendant’s failure to timely file a responsive pleading was the result of excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. As Defendant points out, “[u]nless inexcusable neglect is clear, the policy favoring trial on the merits prevails. Doubts are resolved in favor of the application for relief from default, and reversal of an order denying relief results. Reversal is particularly appropriate where relieving the default will not seriously prejudice the opposing party.” (Elston v. Turlock (1985) 38 Cal. 3d 227, 235.) Moreover, the evidence attached to the papers indicates that the parties were in the process of settling the claims, but appeare to have an unresolved dispute over the accuracy of outstanding balance reflected in the health insurance billing information, and what Defendant owed Plaintiff for care rendered to Defendant Yelsik, Defendant’s sister. (Motion, Corder Decl., ¶¶10-11 and Exh. 16; Opp., Salamoff Decl., ¶15.) It is excusable that Defendant did not file a responsive pleading based on all these circumstances: several unsuccessful attempts to file a response, ongoing settlement discussions with Plaintiff’s counsel, unresolved disputes regarding the accuracy of the billing and what was owed for Yelsik’s care, and the difficulty in finding an attorney. Therefore, relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is granted.   

Finally, Plaintiff asks the Court to impose a penalty in the amount of $1,000.00 against Defendant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (c)(1), which states:

Whenever the court grants relief from a default, default judgment, or dismissal based on any of the provisions of this section, the court may do any of the following:

 

(A) Impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party.

(B) Direct that an offending attorney pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund.

(C) Grant other relief as is appropriate. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(1).) To the extent the request for a penalty and/or sanction is brought “for requiring Plaintiff to unnecessarily and unjustifiably incurring [sic] the costs of preparing, filing and appearing on this motion,” the Court finds such a sanction is not warranted based on its ruling granting the Motion. 

Conclusion 

Motion of Defendant Evelyn Faith Corder, individually, and as Successor Trustee of the 2014 Dorothy Louise Yelsik Revocable Trust for Relief from Default and/or Default Judgment is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON APRIL 29, 2024 AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT ENTERED ON JULY 29, 2024 ARE HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANT IS TO FILE THE PROPOSED ANSWER WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER. THE PARTIES ARE ALSO ORDERED TO FILE A JOINT REPORT REGARDING STATUS OF SETTLEMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: 24STCP02846    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 26

Enenwali v. Framework Constr. & Remodeling, Inc., et al.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Petitioner Esomchi Enenwali’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE TO BRINGING AN AMENDED PETITION.

ANALYSIS: 

Petitioner Esomchi Enenwali (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition for Release of Mechanic’s Lien against Respondent Framework Construction & Remodeling, Inc. (“Respondent”) on September 3, 2024. Petitioner filed a proof of service by mail on October 1, 2024. No response to the Petition has been filed to date. 

Discussion

Petitioner moves to have the mechanic’s lien recorded against their property by Respondent released. Under Civil Code section 8484 the petition for release order must be verified by the petitioner and allege the following:

(a) The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.
(b) The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.
(c) The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.
(d) The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.
(e) Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.
(f) That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.
(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.
(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien.

(Civil Code, § 8484.) Petitioner filed a proof of service of the Petition and notice of the new hearing date on October 1, 2024, showing service by first-class mail on September 10, 2024. (Proof of Service, filed 10/01/24.) Service by first-class mail, however, does not meet the statutory requirements. Civil Code section 8486, subdivision (b) provides that “[t]he petitioner shall serve a copy of the petition and a notice of hearing on the claimant at least 15 days before the hearing. Service shall be made in the same manner as service of summons, or by certified or registered mail, postage prepaid, return receipt requested, addressed to the claimant as provided in Section 8108.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 8486, subd. (b).)

The Petition is also incomplete in that it does not indicate that Petitioner has not filed for relief under any law and fails to include a legal description of the subject property. The Petition only includes the subject property’s Assessor’s Parcel Number. (Pet., ¶3.) (MTC Financial Inc. v. California Dept. of Tax & Fee Administration (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 742, 749 [“a parcel number, by itself, does not necessarily demonstrate the actual, physical location of a property.”]

Therefore, the Petition does not meet the procedural and substantive statutory requirements to release the mechanic’s lien. 

Conclusion

Petitioner Esomchi Enenwali’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE TO BRINGING AN AMENDED PETITION.

Su v. Ni, et al.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Petitioner John Chung-An Su’s Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action on behalf of minor Plaintiff Kyle Su is GRANTED.  

ANALYSIS:

Minor Plaintiff Kyle Su (“Minor Plaintiff”), by and through their Guardian Ad Litem John Chung-An Su (“Petitioner”), filed the instant action for personal injuries against Defendant Byran Yueh Ni (“Defendant”) on August 15, 2024. Petitioner’s Application for Appointment of Guardian Ad Litem was granted on September 11, 2024. On October 14, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action (“the Petition”) on behalf of minor Plaintiff. 

Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) “‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid. [Citation.]  [¶] Nor is the settlement binding [on the minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’” (Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.) A minor, like Claimant, “shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that they have a right to compromise the minor’s claim under Cal. Probate Code section 3500. 

Having reviewed the instant Petition for Compromise of Pending Action, the Court finds it is complete and provides for a reasonable settlement based on the injuries sustained and future prognosis. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) Proposed orders with respect to the Petition on behalf of minor Plaintiff have also been filed.

Conclusion

 

Petitioner John Chung-An Su’s Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action on behalf of minor Plaintiff Kyle Su is GRANTED.

Case Number: 24STLC07003    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 26

McKnight v. Progressive Casualty Ins. Co., et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant Progressive Casualty Insurance Company’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. 

ANALYSIS:

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff Maliyah McKnight (“Plaintiff”), in propria persona, filed the Complaint in this action against Defendant Progressive Casualty Insurance Company (“Defendant”) and others. On December 12, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint. No opposition has been filed to date. 

Discussion

The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. (Demurrer, Lowell Decl., ¶2 and Exh. A.) Defendant demurs on the grounds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action. (Citing Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) The Complaint indicates that it asserts a cause of action for motor vehicle negligence but attaches no facts in support of such a claim, as required. (Compl., ¶10(a).) Without any attachments setting forth facts to support the purported claim, the Complaint fails to state a cause of action. (Id. at p. 2.) Nor has Plaintiff filed any opposition to the instant demurrer demonstrating that facts can be alleged to articulate a cause of action against Defendant. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 [the burden is on complainant to show the Court a reasonable possibility of successful amendment].) Therefore, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

Conclusion

Defendant Progressive Casualty Insurance Company’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.
