DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV36257    Hearing Date: December 30, 2024    Dept: 78

 

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles 

Department 78 

¿ 

JACKIE IZARRARAZ, 

Plaintiff(s), 

vs. 

CALIFORNIA DEPARTMENT OF TRANSPORTATION, et al., 

Defendant(s). 

Case No.: 

22STCV36257 

Hearing Date: 

December 30, 2024 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Jackie Izarraraz (“Plaintiff”) initiated this FEHA action against defendants California Department of Transportation, Dawn Kerr (“Kerr”), and Does 1 through 50 alleging that she was employed by California Department of Transportation and that supervisor Kerr subjected Plaintiff to harassment based on her sex and gender in October 2020. (Compl. ¶¶ 10-11.) The Complaint sets forth twelve causes of action against all defendants for (1) Breach of Employment Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith And Fair Dealing, (3) Unlawful Discrimination Based On Disparate Treatment, (4) Retaliation In Violation of FEHA, (5) Discrimination In Violation of FEHA - Cal. Gov Code §12940(A), et seq., (6) Harassment In Violation of FEHA - Cal. Gov Code §12940(J) ), et seq., (7) Retaliation In Violation of FEHA - Cal. Gov Code §12940(H), et seq., (8) Failure To Prevent Discrimination, Harassment and/or Retaliation In Violation FEHA - Cal. Gov Code §12940(K), et seq., (9) Violation Of Whistleblower Statute, Labor Code §1102.5, (10) Negligent Retention, (11) Wrongful Harassment in Violation of Public Policy, and (12) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”). 

On July 3, 2024, defendant The People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation and erroneously sued as “California Department of Transportation” (“State”) filed a demurrer against all twelve causes of action of the Complaint. Additionally, the State moves to strike punitive damages. 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions to the motions. 

On July 25, 2024, the State filed its replies. 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

Before filing a demurrer and/or motion to strike, the moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (CCP §§ 430.41(a); 435.5(a).)¿The moving party “shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference.” (Ibid.) 

The State’s counsel declares that on June 5, 2024, he sent a letter by email to Plaintiff’s counsel in an attempt to meet and confer, and that the letter requested that Plaintiff’s counsel set up a time to discuss the motion by telephone as required by code. (DiPietro ¶ 2.) Counsel sent another email to Plaintiff’s counsel requesting a time to discuss, but Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Id. at ¶¶ 3-4.) 

The Court finds the State fails to establish that it has complied with the meet and confer requirements under CCP §§ 430.41 and 435.5. The State’s counsel does not declare that he ever attempted to reach Plaintiff’s counsel telephonically to discuss the issues raised in the motions, and whether Plaintiff’s counsel refused to meet and confer despite such attempts. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the Court CONTINUES the hearing for the State’s demurrer with motion to strike to January 30, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. in Department 78 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse to provide the State an opportunity to comply with the meet and confer requirements of CCP §§ 430.41 and 435.5.  

The Court orders the State to file a meet and confer declaration that complies with the requirements of CCP §§ 430.41 and 435.5 no later than seven (7) court days prior to the continued hearing date. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING UNOPPOSED MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED ANSWER 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiffs Charles Bernal and Artist Worldwide Agency, LLC (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against defendants Christopher Maggiore, Artists Worldwide, Jamison Properties, Inc., Force Protection Agency, Inc. (“FPA”), Doe 1, and Does 2-25 premised upon various allegations, such as interference with business, breach of contract, assault, and battery.  

On March 24, 2023, FPA filed its Answer to Plaintiffs’ complaint. 

On August 14, 2024, FPA filed the instant motion for leave to file a First Amended Answer (“FAA”) to Plaintiffs’ complaint. 

As of December 16, 2024, no opposition has been filed. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD 

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:¿ “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.¿ The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.) 

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. 

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. 

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial. In most cases, the factors for timeliness are: (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party. If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.) Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery. (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.) 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

FPA provides that it was previously defended by Cole Sheridan and that Ian Stewart of Wilson Elser substituted as FPA’s counsel of record on May 30, 2024. FPA argues its current counsel evaluated the evidence and Plaintiffs’ allegations and discovered that prior counsel failed to assert any affirmative defenses. FPA seeks to amend the Answer to assert affirmative defenses. 

The Court notes that FPA’s motion and declaration do not fully conform with the requirements set forth in CRC Rule 3.1324. The motion fails to state where the proposed additions are to be added. Additionally, the separate declaration fails to state when FPA’s current counsel discovered the issue of lack of affirmative defenses nor provides reasons as to why the request for amendment was not made earlier when FPA’s current counsel has been present since May 30, 2024.  

Trial is currently set for January 13, 2025. The motion was initially set to be heard on December 5, 2024, but continued to December 30, 2024 due to the Court’s unavailability. (Min. Order, Nov. 22, 2024.) Notwithstanding the above-noted defects, no parties filed any opposition to the motion. A failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion. (CRC, Rule 8.54(c).) Since no parties identified any prejudice by allowing the filing of the FAA, the motion is granted. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION 

Accordingly, FPA’s motion for leave to file a FAA is GRANTED. FPA is ordered to file a separate copy of the proposed FAA within five (5) days. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER TAKING DISCHARGE AND DISMISSAL OF SURETY OFF-CALENDAR 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Anthony J. Sparano (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Casey's Cars, Inc., Woodside Credit, LLC, Hudson Insurance Company (“Hudson”), Denali State Bank, and Does 1 through 75 related to the purchase of a 2007 Porsche 911 Carrera. 

Hudson filed a Cross-Complaint against cross-defendants Anthony J. Sparano, Luis R. Ocampo, Casey’s Cars, Inc., Shohab Khawaja, Lorraine Flores (as Roe 1), and Roes 2 through 100 for interpleader, breach of contract of indemnity, and reimbursement. 

Woodside Credit, LLC filed a Cross-Complaint against cross-defendants Casey’s Cars, Inc. and Roes 1 through 20. 

On October 2, 2024, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Hudson filed the instant motion for discharge and dismissal of surety under license bond. Hudson argues that it has deposited $50,000 of its Motor Vehicle Dealer License Bond No. 10034548 (“the Bond”) with the Court, and that it is now requesting to be released from its obligation as a surety and dismissed from the underlying suit because the bond payment fulfills Hudson's liability under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §996.490(a). Hudson argues Luis R. Ocampo (LASC Case No. 23TRCV03037) and Lorraine Flores (LASC Case No. 24STCV13371) both also filed complaints against Hudson related to the Bond, and requests that these three bond claimants be restrained from further prosecuting their separate lawsuits. Lastly, Hudson requests costs and attorney fees disbursed from the Bond funds. 

On December 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition arguing that the matter was stayed pursuant to the Court’s November 8, 2024 Order. Plaintiff argues that he will file a substantive opposition to the motion once the stay is lifted and a new hearing date is set. Plaintiff requests the motion be taken off-calendar until completion of arbitration. 

On December 20, 2024, Hudson filed a reply arguing that it is unclear whether the Hon. Michelle Kim intended to apply the interpleader cause of action in Hudson’s Cross-Complaint as opposed to applying the stay only to the indemnity and reimbursement causes of action. Hudson requests that should the Court find the motion falls under the stay order, that the hearing on the motion be continued to the next Status Conference date of May 9, 2025. 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

On November 8, 2024, Judge Kim granted Casey’s Cars, Inc.’s motion to compel arbitration and stay the action. As for the Cross-Complaints filed in this matter, the Court stated: “The causes of action in both cross-complaints are not severable because they are about indemnification and fault. Thus, the entire action is stayed pending arbitration.” (Min. Order, Nov. 8, 2024.) The Order did not expressly exempt any portion of Hudson’s Cross-Complaint from the stay. As such, this Court finds that the most reasonable interpretation of the stay order was for it to apply broadly. As such, the Court will not address the merits of Hudson’s motion at this time pursuant to the stay. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

On the Court’s own motion, Hudson Insurance Company’s Motion for Discharge and Dismissal of Surety Under License Bond is taken off-calendar.  

Hudson Insurance Company may re-file the motion and reserve a new hearing date once the stay is lifted after completion of arbitration. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.
