DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21GDCV00974    Hearing Date: November 1, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT GRANDTAG’S MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND FOURTH AMENDED COMPLAINT


I.        INTRODUCTION

           Plaintiffs Futang Ji, Yi-Ching Wu, and Yong Zong (“Plaintiffs”) initiated this action against Defendants Punta Gorda Acquisitions, LLC, and various affiliated entities on July 27, 2021. Plaintiffs filed their operative Fourth Amended Complaint (FrAC) on July 24, 2024, naming as Defendants Punta Gorda Acquisitions, LLC; Punta Gorda Senior Living, LP dba The Palms of Punta Gorda Assisted Living; Madison Realty Equities, LLC; GT Madison Realty, LLC; Gary Langendoen; Grandtag Business Consultants, Ltd.; Jameson Leung; Rene Leung; and Does 1 through 50, inclusive.

The FrAC alleges ten causes of action: (1) Breach of Third-Party Beneficiary Contract; (2) Intentional Misrepresentation; (3) Negligent Misrepresentation; (4) Conspiracy to Convert Property; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (6) Violation of California Business and Professions Code section 17200; (7) Unjust Enrichment; (8) Conspiracy to Commit Fraud; (9) Fraudulent Transfer; and (10) Aiding and Abetting Fraud.

On October 3, 2024, Defendant Grandtag Business Consultants, Ltd. (“Grandtag”) filed a motion to quash service of summons and the Fourth Amended Complaint. Plaintiffs filed an opposition to this motion on October 21, 2024. On October 25, Grandtag filed a reply to Plaintiff’s opposition.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code Civ. Proc. §418.10(a)(1) provides that a defendant, on or before the last day to plead, may file a motion to quash service of the summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction.

          California’s “long-arm” statute provides for jurisdiction “on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.” Code Civ. Proc. §410.10.

          The possible bases for jurisdiction in this state are the following:

          - Physical presence in the forum when served, aka “gotcha” jurisdiction; Burnham v. Superior Court (1990) 495 U.S. 604, 612;

          - Domicile in the forum state; Milliken v. Meyer (1940) 311 U.S. 457, 462;

          - General appearance in the action; Code Civ. Proc. §410.50(a);

          - Contractual consent/forum selection clause; National Equipment Rental, Ltd. v. Szukhent (1964) 375 U.S. 311, 315-316;

          - Minimum contacts; International Shoe Co. v. State of Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316; Burger King v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475.

See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023), ¶3:131.

Only a limited set of affiliations with a forum state will render a defendant amenable to all-purpose jurisdiction there. (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 US 117, 137.) “[F]or a¿corporation, it is an equivalent place, one in which the corporation is¿fairly regarded as at home.” (Ibid.) The paradigm forums in which a corporation will be considered “at home” and thus subject to general jurisdiction are (1) its place of incorporation; and (2) its principal place of business. (Ibid.) The inquiry is not whether the corporation's contacts with the forum are continuous and systematic, “it is whether that corporation's affiliations with the State are so continuous and systematic as to render it essentially at home in the forum State.” (Id. at 138-139.) The fact that a foreign corporation has an in-state subsidiary or affiliate does not subject the corporate parent to general jurisdiction in that state. (Id. at 136;¿see also Young v. Daimler AG¿(2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 855, 866-867 [no general jurisdiction over parent corporation even if plaintiffs reside in state, accident occurred there, the vehicle was purchased there, and vehicle was manufactured by defendant's U.S. subsidiary].) 

          Even if a nonresident defendant's “contacts” with California are not sufficient for general jurisdiction, the defendant may still be subject to jurisdiction on claims related to its activities there under a specific jurisdiction analysis. (Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 283.)  Specific jurisdiction requires a showing of three elements: 1) the defendant purposefully established contacts with the forum state (purposeful availment); 2) the cause arises out of or is related to defendant’s contacts with the forum; and 3) the forum's exercise of personal jurisdiction in the particular case comports with “fair play and substantial justice.” (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 US 462, 477-478 [courts follow a “highly realistic” approach in determining whether a nonresident contracting party “purposefully established minimum contacts with the forum”]; see Vons Cos., Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446.) 

          “[I]t is essential in each case that there be some act by which the defendant purposefully avails itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum State, thus invoking the benefits and protections of its laws.” (Hanson v. Denckla (1958) 357 U.S. 235, 253.) Courts have held that the “purposeful availment” requirement will not be satisfied unless the defendant has taken deliberate action within the forum state or if it has created continuing obligations to forum residents. (See, e.g., Vons Cos. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446; Dow Chemical Canada ULC v. Superior Court (2011) 202 Cal. App. 4th 170, 179.) Moreover, the plaintiff’s own activities or those of a third party in the forum state are irrelevant; only the defendant’s activity can form the basis for jurisdiction. (See Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 291 [“it is the defendant, not the plaintiff or third parties, who must create contacts with the forum State.”].)

           

III.     ANALYSIS

General Jurisdiction

Defendant moves to quash for lack of general jurisdiction, arguing that Grandtag is not subject to general jurisdiction in California because it does not meet the “essentially at home” standard required by the U.S. Supreme Court. Grandtag asserts that it is a Hong Kong company with its principal place of business in Hong Kong, lacking any substantial, continuous, or systematic presence in California. Grandtag further contends that it neither maintains an office nor conducts business in California, and that mere associations or investments with entities operating within California are insufficient to establish general jurisdiction (Motion, pg. 4-5). Grandtag brings this motion as an out-of-state corporation with an out-of-state principal place of business, arguing that the motion is properly brought, and that the burden rests on Plaintiff to establish jurisdiction.

Plaintiff opposes Defendant’s motion and argues that general jurisdiction over Grandtag is warranted due to its substantial, continuous, and systematic activities in California. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that Rene Leung, the manager, and representative of Grandtag has actively participated in California-based operations by managing GT Madison Leary, LLC which is headquartered in Pasadena, CA. Plaintiffs contend that Grandtag maintains a physical presence in California through GT Madison’s Pasadena office and that Rene Leung, acting on behalf of Grandtag, manages the day-to-day operations of GT Madison within California. (Opposition, pg. 3) Plaintiffs also claim that Renee resides in California. (Id.) Plaintiffs also assert that Grandtag provided a business address on official documents and actively used California-based processes, such as an assignment for the benefit of creditors to transfer assets for GT Madison, which Plaintiffs allege was done to prevent them from accessing assets they claim is theirs. (Opposition, pg.4-5)

 

Specific Jurisdiction

Plaintiffs contend that Grandtag has purposefully directed its activities toward California and should have reasonably anticipated being subject to California’s jurisdiction. (Opposition, pg.6) They argue that Grandtag’s use of California’s assignment of creditors process demonstrates purposeful availment, asserting that Grandtag used this process to conceal GT Madison’s assets within California to prevent Plaintiffs from accessing funds they claim. Plaintiffs further assert that Grandtag has substantial ties to California through Rene Leung, Grandtag’s authorized representative, who resides in California and manages business activities on Grandtag’s behalf. Plaintiffs allege that Leung “likely” maintains Grandtag’s bank accounts in California. (Id.) Consequently, Plaintiffs argue that Grandtag’s ties to California support the court’s exercise of specific jurisdiction in this matter (Opposition, pg.4-6).

In Reply, Grandtag argues that Plaintiffs have not demonstrated that Grandtag purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits. (Reply, pg.5) Plaintiffs assert that Grandtag’s California business address and “personnel” (a single individual, Rene Leung) establish that Grandtag purposefully availed itself of California’s benefits (Opposition, pg.6). However, Grandtag contends that these facts are insufficient to establish specific personal jurisdiction, as the mere use of a mailing address in California and the residence of one Grandtag officer do not constitute purposeful availment of the state’s benefits (Reply, pg.5).

The Court finds that Plaintiffs conflate Grandtag’s role as manager of GT Madison and Grandtag’s own business activities. None of the evidence submitted by Plaintiffs regarding the activities of GT Madison is relevant without a foundational connection to Grandtag’s independent operations within California. Simply put, GT Madison’s presence in California does not render Grandtag “essentially at home” in the state. Plaintiffs fail to demonstrate a link between GT Madison’s California-based business activities and Grandtag specifically, such that any act by GT Madison could be attributed as a direct action by Grandtag. The fact that Grandtag holds a managerial position in GT Madison does not imply that Grandtag, a Hong Kong-based company, has itself established systematic contacts within California (Opposition, pg.4-5).

Additionally, the Court finds that merely asserting Grandtag’s use of California’s creditor process and speculating that Rene Leung may maintain a bank account in California for Grandtag falls short of establishing that Grandtag purposefully availed itself of the forum’s benefits. This assertion also fails to demonstrate that the present controversy arises out of any conduct directly attributable to Defendant Grandtag.

Based on the foregoing, Grandtag’s motion to quash for lack of jurisdiction is GRANTED.

IV.     CONCLUSION

           Accordingly, Grandtag’s motion to quash for lack of jurisdiction is GRANTED.

Grandtag is to give notice.

Case Number: 22AHCV01340    Hearing Date: November 1, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO MODIFY STIPULATION


 

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a lemon law case. Plaintiff Andy Meraz (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action against Defendant American Honda Motor Co. (“Defendant”) following Plaintiff’s purchase of a new Honda Accord in 2022. The complaint alleges three causes of action: (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Act for breach of express warranty, (2) violation of the Song-Beverly Act for breach of implied warranty, and (3) fraudulent inducement by concealment.

On December 4, 2023, the Court granted a Stipulation and Protective Order (“SPO”) in this matter, setting confidentiality parameters for documents produced during discovery. On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to modify the SPO, seeking permission to use confidential documents from this case in other cases involving similar allegations. Defendant opposed the modification on October 21, 2024, arguing against extending the SPO’s scope.

 

Meet and Confer

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email memorializing a meet-and-confer telephone call that had taken place with Defendant’s counsel regarding the proposed modification to the Stipulation and Protective Order. (Motion pg. 4) Accordingly, Plaintiff has complied with the meet-and-confer requirements.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

“The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b).) 

CCP section 128(a)(8) provides the court with power “[t]o amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128(a)(8).)

III.      ANALYSIS

Plaintiff moves to modify the SPO entered on December 4, 2023, under Paragraph 9 of the SPO, which allows any party to the proceeding to request a modification or relief from any provision of the SPO upon giving proper notice to all parties. (SPO pg. 8) Plaintiff’s request seeks to modify Paragraph 8 of the SPO to permit the use of confidential materials produced by Defendant in this case in other cases handled by Plaintiff’s counsel. (Motion pg.3) These cases involve similar allegations of fraudulent concealment related to the Honda Sensing and Collision Mitigation Braking Systems in Defendant’s vehicles. (Id.)

Plaintiff argues that allowing shared access to the discovery materials across related cases aligns with California public policy favoring efficient discovery and consumer protection. (Motion pg. 6) Plaintiff relies on Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 590 .and other California case law, asserting that such modifications are permissible when confidentiality protections are maintained, and the cases involve similar claims. (Motion pg.4-6)

Defendant opposes the motion to modify the SPO, arguing that Plaintiff’s request is overly broad and ambiguous, as it seeks to extend the use of confidential documents to an undefined number of current and future cases, some of which may fall outside the Court’s jurisdiction. (Opposition pg.2) Defendant contends that allowing this access could lead to inconsistent rulings, as other courts might interpret confidentiality protections differently. (Id.)

Additionally, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s motion constitutes an improper attempt to circumvent procedural requirements, effectively serving as a motion for reconsideration of the existing SPO. (Opposition pg.6)

In rebutting Plaintiff’s cited authority, Defendant specifically argues that Raymond Handling Concepts Corp is distinguishable from the present case. Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 590. (Opposition pg. 7) Defendant notes that Raymond included specific safeguards, such as requiring counsel in other cases to sign stipulations and notifying defense counsel of any disclosure—protections absent in Plaintiff’s current proposal. (Id.)

Finally, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s request risks disclosure of information irrelevant to specific cases which may unfairly expose Defendant to disclosure of sensitive proprietary data. (Opposition pg.3) 

The Court agrees with Defendant that Raymond is distinguishable from the present request by Plaintiff. In Raymond, the modification of the protective order included specific safeguards, such as requiring counsel to notify defense counsel upon disclosure and limiting disclosure to attorneys in similar cases who had signed a stipulation agreeing to be bound by the same protective order. (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 590.) These measures provided defense counsel an opportunity to contest the similarity of other cases and helped ensure that confidential materials were not misused.

Here, Plaintiff’s proposed modification lacks comparable safeguards. Granting this modification would leave the Court without mechanisms to prevent potential misuse of protected materials, risking potential harm to Defendant. Additionally, such an order may extend beyond this Court’s jurisdiction, as it seeks to apply the protective order’s terms to undefined future cases without procedural constraints.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to modify the stipulation and protective order.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to modify the stipulation and protective order entered on 12/04/23.

            Defendant is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV01347    Hearing Date: November 1, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING CROSS-DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINT IN PART

I.         INTRODUCTION

On February 8, 2024, A&B Plumbing Solutions, Inc. (“A&B”) and William Dodds (“Dodds”) (collectively “Cross-Complainants”) filed a cross-complaint against Landwin DMV LLC ("Landwin"), AYS Hospitality ("AYS"), and individuals Sonny Chen, Jason Chen, and Johnny Chen (collectively “Cross-Defendants”). The cross-complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) breach of contract as to the First Subcontract, (2) breach of contract as to the Second Subcontract, (3) unjust enrichment, (4) declaratory relief, (5) assault, (6) implied indemnity, and (7) equitable contribution and apportionment.

On June 3, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed a demurrer to the entire cross-complaint. Subsequently, on October 21, 2024, A&B and Dodds filed an opposition, followed by Cross-Defendants’ reply on October 25, 2024.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this case, counsel for Cross-Defendants sent meet and confer letters on April 19, 2024, and again on May 20, 2024, via email to Plaintiff’s counsel, Stephen Cho. However, Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to either communication. (Kromke Decl., ¶ 3, Exhibits A and B.) II.       LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Cross-Defendants demur to each of the seven causes of action in the Cross-Complaint, which include: (1) Breach of Contract as to the First Subcontract, (2) Breach of Contract as to the Second Subcontract, (3) Unjust Enrichment, (4) Declaratory Relief, (5) Assault, (6) Implied Indemnity, and (7) Equitable Contribution and Apportionment. Cross-Defendants argue that the allegations are insufficiently pled to state a claim and contend that the alter ego allegations lack sufficient detail to justify piercing the corporate veil.

 

First and Second Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

In the first and second causes of action, A&B and Dodds allege that Cross-Defendants breached two subcontract agreements. Under the First Subcontract, A&B claims it is owed $43,000. For the Second Subcontract, Cross-Complainants assert that Cross-Defendants failed to make necessary payments, including $61,113.88 owed to Hajoca for supplies and $65,181.59 owed to A&B, plus retention payments for completed work. A&B and Dodds contend that these failures constitute a breach of both subcontracts. (Cross-Complaint pg. 6-7)

 

            Cross-Defendants argue that these breach of contract claims are improperly asserted against all defendants, including Landwin DMV, LLC, and individuals Sonny, Jason, and Johnny Chen. They argue that the Cross-Complaint, specifically paragraphs 26 and 31, acknowledges that A&B entered into contracts solely with AYS Hospitality, Inc., not with Landwin or the Chens. Cross-Defendants contend that contractual privity is essential to assert breach claims, and since neither Landwin nor the Chens were parties to the agreements, they cannot be held liable for breach. They emphasize that defects on the face of the complaint and accompanying exhibits warrant dismissal of all defendants except AYS from the breach of contract claims (Demurrer pg. 12)

In opposition, A&B and Dodds argue that the alter ego doctrine justifies holding all Cross-Defendants liable. They allege that Johnny, Sonny, and Jason Chen, along with Landwin, are alter egos of AYS, referring to paragraph 11 of the Cross-Complaint. Cross-Complainants claim that recognizing the separateness of these entities and individuals would promote injustice, as they are all effectively one and the same for the purposes of these contracts (Opposition pg. 4)¿ In their reply, Cross-Defendants challenge the sufficiency of the alter ego allegations, arguing that they lack necessary factual detail and are conclusory (Reply pg. 3)¿

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Cross-Complainants have sufficiently pled breach of contract against all Cross-Defendants in their first and second causes of action. By naming all defendants, listing the agreements that were allegedly breached, and alleging alter ego as a basis to extend liability to all Cross-Defendants, A&B and Dodds have met the pleading requirements at this stage of litigation.

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first two causes of action.

Third cause of action: Unjust Enrichment

In the third cause of action, A&B and Dodds allege unjust enrichment, claiming that Cross-Defendants retained money that rightfully belonged to A&B and Dodds under the First and Second Subcontractor Agreements. Specifically, A&B and Dodds assert that they performed the work required by these agreements and would not have entered into these contracts had they known Cross-Defendants would withhold payment or instigate related litigation by Hajoca. They allege that Cross-Defendants have been unjustly enriched in an amount to be determined at trial (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 35-39)¿

Cross-Defendants argue in their demurrer that California does not recognize unjust enrichment as an independent cause of action. They rely on McBride v. Boughton, which characterizes unjust enrichment as a principle of restitution rather than a standalone claim (123 Cal.App.4th 379, 387). Additionally, Cross-Defendants contend that unjust enrichment cannot be applied where a valid, express contract exists. They argue that the existence of two written agreements governing the subject matter precludes any quasi-contract claim, as unjust enrichment generally applies only in the absence of a valid contract.

In opposition, A&B and Dodds argue that unjust enrichment is a valid claim under California law when a party receives and retains a benefit unjustly at another’s expense. They assert that Lecirodryer v. SeoulBank and similar cases have recognized unjust enrichment, defining it as the receipt and unjust retention of a benefit (77 Cal.App.4th 723, 726). Cross-Complainants contend that their unjust enrichment claim is valid despite the existence of a contract because they are asserting that all Cross-Defendants, including the alleged alter egos of AYS, were unjustly enriched. Thus, they argue that the demurrer to this cause of action should be overruled (Opposition pg. 5)¿

In their reply, Cross-Defendants maintain that unjust enrichment does not lie where enforceable agreements govern the disputed amounts, reiterating that unjust enrichment is a quasi-contract theory applicable only in the absence of a valid contract. (Reply pg. 4-5)

The parties disagree about whether unjust enrichment is an independent cause of action. While there is conflicting authority regarding whether there is a cause of action for unjust enrichment in California, the weight of authority in California is that there is no cause of action for unjust enrichment.  (See e.g., Hill v. Roll Int'l Corp. (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1295, 1307; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1138; McKell v. Washington Mut., Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1490; Melchior v. New Line Prods., Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 779, 793.)

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the third cause of action without leave to amend.

Fourth cause of action: Declaratory Relief

In the fourth cause of action, A&B and Dodds seek declaratory relief to establish the respective rights and obligations of all Cross-Defendants under the First and Second Subcontractor agreements. (Cross-Complaint ¶41) They allege an actual controversy regarding payment obligations, unpaid retention amounts and for attorney’s fees. (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 42-43)

CCP section 1060 provides that a person may bring an action for declaratory relief if he or she “desires a declaration of his or her rights or duties with respect to another, or in respect to, in, over or upon property . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.) A request for declaratory relief may be brought alone or with other relief. (See Id.) 

To state a declaratory relief claim, the plaintiff must allege a proper subject of declaratory relief and an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations. (See Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909.) The validity of a contract is a proper subject of declaratory relief. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.)

Cross-Defendants demur to this cause of action arguing that declaratory relief claim is fundamentally flawed because the two subcontracts were solely between A&B and AYS Hospitality, Inc., and do not include Landwin or the Chens as parties. They contend that declaratory relief can only extend to parties to the contract, and A&B’s alter ego allegations are insufficiently detailed to support extending liability to the non-signatories.

The Court finds that A&B and Dodds have adequately pled an actual controversy concerning their rights under the First and Second Subcontractor Agreements. Although Cross-Defendants challenge the privity of contract between A&B and the non-signatories, the alter ego allegations, if proven, could establish a unity of interest that binds all Cross-Defendants under the same obligations as AYS. At this stage, the Court is not required to assess the ultimate merits of the alter ego claim but only to determine whether an actionable controversy has been pled.

Accordingly, the Court finds that A&B’s declaratory relief claim is sufficiently supported to survive demur. Therefore, the Court OVERRULES demur to the fourth cause of action.

Fifth Cause of Action: Assault

A&B and Dodds allege that Cross-Defendant Jason Chen committed assault during a meeting in late February or early March 2023. According to Dodds, after leaving a meeting with Sonny Chen about outstanding payments, he observed Jason Chen swinging a pipe and banging it on a trashcan. Dodds alleges that Jason then walked toward him in an aggressive manner, which he interpreted as an imminent threat of harmful contact. Dodds claims he was harmed as a result, in an amount to be proven at trial, and that Jason’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing this harm (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 45-49).

The elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) the defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch the plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) the plaintiff reasonably believed he was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to the plaintiff that the defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) the plaintiff did not consent to the defendant's conduct; (4) the plaintiff was harmed; and (5) the defendant's conduct was a substantial factor in causing the plaintiff's harm.  (Carlsen v. Koivumaki (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 879, 890.)

The allegations fail to meet the fourth and fifth elements required for assault. Dodds does not allege any specific harm that he suffered because of Jason’s actions, nor is there any indication of how Jason’s alleged conduct was a substantial factor in causing harm. The complaint lacks specific facts indicating that Jason’s conduct had a substantial effect or caused measurable harm. As such, without allegations sufficient to satisfy the elements of harm and causation, the assault claim is insufficient to survive a demur.

As reflected in the use notes to CACI 1301, “[t]he tort of assault is complete when the anticipation of harm occurs.”  Kisesky v. Carpenters’ Trust for Southern California (1983) 144 Cal. App.3d 222,232.  Also, “[a] civil action for assault is based upon an invasion of the right of a person to live without being put in fear of personal harm.” Lowry v. Standard Oil Co. of California (1944) 63 Cal. App.2d 1, 6-7.  At present, Cross-Complainant’s cause of action for assault lacks the requisite detail about the nature of the harm/fear allegedly inflicted.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Cross-Defendants Demurrer to the fifth cause of action with leave to amend.

Sixth cause of action: Implied Indemnity

A&B and Dodds seek implied indemnity from Cross-Defendants, alleging they are entitled to indemnification for any recovery that Hajoca may obtain against them. Specifically, they assert that if they are held liable, Cross-Defendants should indemnify them for any potential settlement amounts, judgments, attorney’s fees, and costs (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 52-53).

Under California law, implied indemnity applies where one party is obligated to reimburse another for a loss, often arising from a legal obligation or equitable principles of fairness. In this case, the dispute centers on the interpretation of the indemnity provisions within the subcontract agreements, but since there is a disagreement over the terms, the Court is limited to examining the allegations within the cross-complaint itself rather than any extrinsic evidence.

Here, A&B and Dodds allege that they are entitled to indemnification from Cross-Defendants for any liabilities stemming from the Hajoca claim. (Id.)  This includes indemnification for any settlement, judgment, or associated litigation costs. (Id.) The Court finds that these allegations sufficiently plead an implied indemnity claim, as they establish a potential obligation for Cross-Defendants to cover losses incurred by A&B and Dodds.

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Cross-Defendants Demurrer to the sixth cause of action.

Seventh cause of action: Equitable Contribution

 A&B and Dodds seek equitable contribution from Cross-Defendants, asserting that they are entitled to reimbursements for any financial obligations incurred due to the action of Cross-Defendants.

Cross-Defendants argue that equitable contribution is applicable in cases involving joint or shared liability. They contend that A&B and Dodds have not demonstrated joint or shared liability with Cross-Defendants under the contracts in question.

In reviewing the sufficiency of the allegations, the Court finds that A&B and Dodds have not sufficiently established shared or joint liability with Cross-Defendants under the subcontract agreements. Equitable contribution requires a clear factual basis showing overlapping obligations or a shared liability. Although A&B and Dodds reference paragraph 3.2 of the Second Subcontractor Agreement in their opposition, it remains unclear from the complaint how this provision creates a shared liability with non-signatory parties like Landwin and the Chens. Without allegations indicating mutual or joint financial responsibility, the equitable contribution claim lacks the necessary factual support to proceed.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to seventh cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

  Alter Ego Allegations

“A claim against a defendant, based on the alter ego theory, is not itself a claim for substantive relief, . . . but rather, procedural, i.e., to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice.” (Hennessey’s Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359.)

            To invoke the alter ego doctrine, the plaintiff must plead unity of interest and ownership and that an inequity will result if the corporate entity is treated as the sole actor. (See Vasey v. California Dance Co. (1977) 70 Cal.App.3d 742, 749.) Factors to consider in applying the doctrine include the commingling of funds and other assets, the holding out by one entity that it is liable for the debts of the other, identical equitable ownership in the two entities, inadequate capitalization, and disregard of corporate formalities. (Sonora Diamond Corp. v Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538-39.)

The Court finds that A&B and Dodds have sufficiently alleged alter ego to withstand demurrer at this stage. To invoke the alter ego doctrine, a plaintiff must demonstrate unity of interest and ownership and show that inequity would result if the corporate form were maintained. Here, A&B and Dodds allege that Landwin and AYS are owned and operated by Sonny, Jason, and Johnny Chen, who commingle assets, operate these entities as undercapitalized shells, and use corporate assets for personal benefit. (Cross-Complaint ¶12) They further assert that Landwin and AYS lack separateness and function merely as instrumentalities for the individual defendants, and that failing to disregard this structure would result in injustice by allowing Cross-Defendants to evade liability. (Id.)

Cross-Defendants argue that A&B and Dodds fail to specify how maintaining the corporate form would lead to injustice. However, A&B and Dodds correctly contend that, at the pleading stage, they need not provide detailed evidentiary facts but rather only need to allege a factual basis sufficient to notify Cross-Defendants of their claims. Given that Cross-Defendants have more access to facts concerning their corporate operations, requiring further detail at this juncture would be premature.

Accordingly, the Court finds the alter ego allegations sufficient.

 

IV.       ORDER

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to the first, second, fourth and sixth causes of action.   

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the third cause of action for Unjust Enrichment without leave to amend.  The Court sustains the demurrer as to the fifth cause of action for Assault, with 20 days leave to amend. The Court sustains the demurrer as to the seventh cause of action for equitable contribution, with 20 days leave to amend.

Cross-Defendants are to give notice.
