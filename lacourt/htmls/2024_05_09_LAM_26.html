1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 23STCV21950    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 26

Procedural Background

On September 12, 2023, Plaintiff Dioncio Cruz (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendant AMPAM Parks Mechanical Inc. (“Defendant”). The Complaint asserts the following seven causes of action: (1) Discrimination in Violation of Government Code §§ 12940, et seq.; (2) Retaliation in Violation of Government Code §§ 12940, et seq.; (3) Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, and Retaliation in Violation of Government Code §§ 12940, et seq.; (4) Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodations in Violation of Government Code §§ 12940, et seq.; (5) Failure to Engage in a Good Faith Interactive Process in Violation of Government Code §§ 12940, et seq.; (6) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; and (7) Failure to Permit Inspection of Personnel and Payroll Records (Labor Code § 1198.5).

            On December 26, 2023, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel arbitration. On April 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition. Thereafter, on May 2, 2024, Defendant filed its reply.

           

Evidentiary Objections

            Plaintiff objects to various portions of the Declarations of Uzma Khan and Oscar Alvarado. As to the objections directed at Uzma Khan’s declaration, the Court overrules these in their entirety. With regard to the objections raised against portions of Oscar Alvarado’s declaration, the Court sustains Objection Nos. 6 and 7 on the ground that Mr. Alvarado lacks personal knowledge that Plaintiff definitively executed the arbitration agreement. The remaining objections are overruled.

            Defendant also object to various portions of Plaintiff’s declaration. The Court overrules these objections in their entirety.

 

Legal Standard

In a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party must prove by a preponderance of evidence the existence of the arbitration agreement and that the dispute is covered by the agreement. The burden then shifts to the resisting party to prove by a preponderance of evidence a ground for denial (e.g., fraud, unconscionability, etc.).  (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413-414; Hotels Nevada v. L.A. Pacific Center, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 754, 758.)

Generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting rulings on common issues.  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218-219.)

“California has a strong public policy in favor of arbitration and any doubts regarding the arbitrability of a dispute are resolved in favor of arbitration.” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.)  “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.” (Id. [internal quotations omitted].) 

 

Discussion

A.    Existence of a Valid Arbitration Agreement

The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

Once petitioners allege that an arbitration agreement exists, the burden shifts to respondents to prove the falsity of the purported agreement, and no evidence or authentication is required to find the arbitration agreement exists. (See Condee v. Longwood Mgt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219.) However, if the existence of the agreement is challenged, "petitioner bears the burden of proving [the arbitration agreement's] existence by a preponderance of the evidence." (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413. See also Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1058-1060.)

Here, Defendant has provided a copy of the arbitration agreement, suggesting that a signed arbitration agreement exists between the parties. (Alvarado Decl., Exh. A; see Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218 [“With respect to the moving party's burden to provide evidence of the existence of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court.”].) In pertinent part, the arbitration agreement states:

 

“I understand that this agreement requires me to pursue all claims I bring against the Company (and any third-party beneficiaries) through binding arbitration and requires the Company submit any claims it has against me to binding arbitration (except for those claims specifically excluded by this agreement). Our agreement to arbitrate includes any and all claims which arise out of the employment context or any other interaction/relationship we had, have or may have in the future. Those claims include, but are not limited to, any claim, dispute, and/or controversy that either party brings against the other (including, but not limited to, any claims of discrimination and harassment, whether they be based on the California Fair Employment and Housing Act, the Americans With Disabilities Act, Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, as amended, as well as all other applicable state or federal laws or regulations) which would otherwise require or allow resort to any court or other governmental dispute resolution forum between myself and the Company, as well as any third-party beneficiaries of the Company. Third party beneficiaries include the Company’s owners, directors, officers, managers, employees, agents, partners, attorneys, sister-companies, subsidiaries, parent companies, joint-venturers, affiliated persons/entities, successors, and assigns, and independent contractors, and parties affiliated with its employee benefit and health plans. These claims also include any claims arising from, related to, or having any relationship or connection whatsoever with my seeking employment with, employment by, or other association with the Company or third-party beneficiaries, whether based on tort, contract, statute, equity or otherwise.

 

(Ibid.)

Now that Defendant has presented the existence of the arbitration agreement, “the burden shifts to [Plaintiff], who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.) Once the validity of the agreement is in question, Defendant is “required to establish by a preponderance of the evidence that the signature was authentic.” (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App. 1047, 1057.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the arbitration agreement has not been properly authenticated because Defendant has failed to establish that the electronic signature found on the agreement genuinely belonged to Plaintiff. (Opposition at pp. 5-7.) Plaintiff reasons that Defendant’s representative, Oscar Alvarado, lacks personal knowledge that Plaintiff definitively accessed the electronic onboarding documents and has failed to include evidence of the employee who witnessed Plaintiff purportedly signing the document. (Id. at pg. 6) Furthermore, Plaintiff denies that the agreement bears his signature or having been told to use one of Defendant’s computers to complete the onboarding paperwork. (Ibid.; Cruz Decl. ¶¶ 5-7.) Instead, Plaintiff claims that he went to Defendant’s office in Carson to complete drug testing. (Cruz Decl. ¶ 7.) Based on a review of his only email account, Plaintiff attests that he did not receive any confirmation that he electronically completed the onboarding paperwork. (Id. at ¶ 8.) Thus. Plaintiff asserts that there is a critical gap in the evidence supporting the motion to prove that he actually signed the agreement. (Opposition at pg. 8, relying on Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842-843.)

In reply, Defendant argues that the motion should be granted because the arbitration agreement submitted within the moving papers is valid and Plaintiff has failed to show that someone else had signed the arbitration agreement on his behalf. (Reply at pp. 2-4.) However, the Court is not persuaded by this reasoning. Plaintiff has denied signing the arbitration agreement and attested that his personal email lacks proof of completing the onboarding paperwork. (Cruz Decl. ¶¶ 6-8.) Moreover, Defendant’s reliance on Mr. Alvarado’s declaration is insufficient to authenticate the signature found on the arbitration agreement because it does not establish that the purported signature was the act of the Plaintiff under Code of Civil Procedure § 1633.9. Rather, it would have been more persuasive if Defendant submitted the declaration of employee who was present during Plaintiff’s onboarding process.

Even if Defendant met its burden in establishing the authenticity of the document, Plaintiff has produced evidence to show that the signature was procured by fraud. (See Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413-414 [determining that the burden then shifts to the resisting party to prove by a preponderance of evidence a ground for denial (e.g., fraud, unconscionability, etc.)].) To this extent, Plaintiff has submitted his own declaration, attesting that he was asked to complete drug testing paperwork and was not directed to use Defendant’s computer to fill out any onboarding paperwork. (Cruz Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.) Thus, based on this evidence, there is an inference that Plaintiff was potentially misled in what documents he had signed. It is noted that Defendant have failed to submit any evidence in its reply to rebut this inference of fraud.

Accordingly, the Court denies Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration because the arbitration agreement has not been properly authenticated and there is an inference the signature was procured through fraudulent means.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Based on the foregoing, Defendant AMPAM Parks Mechanical Inc.’s motion to compel arbitration is denied.

Moving Party is ordered give notice to all parties.


Case Number: 23STLC01807    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: 26

Background

            On March 4, 2024, Plaintiff Barry Rosen (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) for declaratory and injunctive relief for the involuntary reorganization, receivership, or in lieu thereof, involuntary dissolution of Clark Drive Homeowners Association (“Defendant”).

            On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings. On April 26, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On May 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 Allegations of the Operative FAC

            The FAC alleges that:

            Plaintiff is the owner of a condominium unit located at 136 South Clark Drive, Unit 5, Los Angeles, CA and by virtue of said ownership is a member/shareholder of the Clark Drive Homeowners Association. FAC ¶ 1.

            Defendant is a common interest homeowner association that oversee real property development consisting of 8 individual condominium units, where the property owners share a common set of financial obligations with each unit having an interest in some common area. FAC ¶ 2.

            Defendant has continued to breach all of its fiduciary obligations under the terms of Bylaws and Covenants, Conditions & Restrictions, provisions of California Civil Code (“Davis-Stirling Act”), and California Corporations Code, in the operation of the homeowner association and does not have a board of directors neither properly nor duly elected, resulting in abandonment of its activity for more than one year. FAC ¶¶ 4, 6. Alternatively, those purportedly in control of the homeowner association have been guilty of or have knowingly countenanced persistent and pervasive fraud, mismanagement or abuse of authority or persistent unfairness toward any member, including but not limited to Plaintiff, and Defendant’s property is being misapplied and wasted by its purported directors or officers. Id.

            Specifically, Defendant failed to hold the required 12 meetings per year under Bylaws section 7.1. FAC ¶ 6a. Defendant only held about two improper telephonic meetings of the Board during all of 2022. FAC ¶ 6b. The Board willfully and negligently failed to take any corrective action and decided to retaliate against Plaintiff for initiating litigation. FAC ¶ 6c. Defendant failed to hold the required annual general meeting of all shareholders. FAC ¶ 6d. the current Board has fully and willfully failed to familiarize themselves with the Governing Documents. FAC ¶ 6e. No election policy was ever submitted to all shareholders. FAC ¶ 6f. Plaintiff suggested and attempted to force a special mandatory reorganization meeting of the shareholders by sending various petitions in 2021, 2022, and 2023 to Defendant. FAC ¶ 6g. The Board failed to maintain the common interest development building and security resulting in damage to all shareholders and directly Plaintiff. FAC ¶ 6h.

            The pattern of continual mismanagement and breach of fiduciary obligations by the Board goes back over a decade. FAC ¶ 7.

 

Legal Standard

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer but is made after the time for demurrer has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).)  Except as provided by statute, the rules governing demurrers apply.  (Civic Partners Stockton, LLC v. Youssefi (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1012.)  “Like a demurrer, the grounds for the motion [for judgment on the pleadings] must appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.”  (Id. at 1013.)  In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “[a]ll allegations in the complaint and matters upon which judicial notice may be taken are assumed to be true.”  (Rippon v. Bowen (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1313.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 438 subdivision (c) subsection (1) provides a motion for judgment on the pleading may only made on one of the following grounds:

              (A)     “[i]f the moving party is a plaintiff, that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint;

(B)       [i]f the moving party is a defendant, that either of the following conditions exist:

i.          The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint;

ii.         The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1).)

Code of Civil Procedure section 438 subdivision (f) provides a motion for judgment on the pleading may only made after one of the following conditions has occurred:

 

(1)   “[i]f the moving party is a plaintiff, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the plaintiff to demur to the answer has expired; [or]

(2)   [i]f the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).)

Meet and Confer

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 439 subdivision (a), before filing a judgment on the pleadings pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings...” (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a).) The moving party shall file and serve with the motion for judgment on the pleadings a declaration concerning the parties meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a)(3).)

Here, Plaintiff has not fulfilled the meet and confer requirement.  (Rosen Decl. ¶¶ 6-8.)

 

Discussion

            Plaintiff moves for a non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings on the grounds that Defendant failed to respond to the FAC by answer or demurrer, constituting a waiver per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.80. Plaintiff argues given this failure, the Court is authorized to enter judgment in his favor in the instant case. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends default was entered about a year ago and Defendant essentially recycled its prior demurrer to the wrong operative complaint, so there is no responsive pleading timely filed in response to the FAC. Moreover, Plaintiff asserts a demurrer is inappropriate because his complaint is for declaratory relief and he has standing under Corporation Code section 1800, subdivision (a)(2) to bring this action. Plaintiff also argues the purported legal counsel for Defendant, Swedelsongottleib, has demonstrated an ongoing pattern of inexcusable neglect and malpractice. Finally, Plaintiff contends currently no evidence exists that Swedelsongottleib was ever lawfully or otherwise retained to present Defendant.

            In opposition, Defendant argues the instant motion is incomprehensible in that it does not allege Defendant filed an insufficient Answer but seems to argue the Court should enter judgment in Plaintiff’s favor on the grounds that no responsive pleading was timely filed with respect to the FAC. In fact, Defendant contends the motion premature and procedurally improper because it filed a demurrer to the FAC on April 4, 2024. Moreover, Defendant asserts the FAC is substantively identical to the original Complaint except it adds language related to the Certificate of Compliance required pursuant to Civil Code section 5950. Additionally, Defendant asserts whether its demurrer is valid is a decision for the Court not Plaintiff. Lastly, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s evidence in support of the present motion, i.e., the unsigned declaration and other documentation, should be stricken because they are prohibited extraneous evidence before the Court.

            In reply, Plaintiff argues the Court should reject Defendants untimely served opposition because it needed to be served no later than April 20, 2024 and was not served on Plaintiff until April 26, 2024. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends Defendant’s demurrer is not responsive to the current FAC. Plaintiff also argues Defendant has pattern of unwillingness to meet and confer with him. Last, Plaintiff contends the question remains whether Swedelsongottleib was ever lawfully or otherwise retained to present Defendant.

            The Court finds that the motion for judgment on the pleadings is premature and substantive improper. A plaintiff may move for judgment on the pleadings after a defendant has filed their Answer to the Complaint and the time for a plaintiff to demur to the Answer has expired. Here, Defendant has not filed an Answer to the FAC and currently has a demurrer pending before this Court. Furthermore, a plaintiff may only move for judgment on the pleadings on the grounds that the complaint state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against defendant and defendant’s Answer fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint. As such, Plaintiff’s is substantively improper because it seeks judgment on the pleadings on the grounds that Defendant’s demurrer is not responsive because it does not demur as to the FAC but the original Complaint. Also, Plaintiff motion for judgment on the pleadings appears to be a request for entry of default judgment, when the entry of default has already been set aside and Defendant has a demurrer pending before the Court. Additionally, the opposition was timely filed and served on April 26, 2024 because that was at least nine court days before the noticed hearing date of May 9, 2024. Therefore, Plaintiff has not shown that the motion for judgment on pleadings is warranted at this time.

 

Conclusion and ORDER

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff Barry Rosen’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.
