[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO EXTEND DISCOVERY CUT-OFF DATE

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

January 10, 2025

 

On October 26, 2022, plaintiff William Henry Meurer (“Plaintiff”) filed this partition action against defendant Qinpei Li (“Defendant”). On October 11, 2024, the Court continued the non-jury trial to April 14, 2025, but kept discovery closed. On October 21, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to reopen discovery. Plaintiff filed an opposition brief on November 25, 2024. At the hearing on December 16, 2024, the Court continued the hearing to January 10, 2025, so that Defendant would have an opportunity to file a reply brief. Defendant filed a reply brief on December 24, 2024.

Except as otherwise provided, any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for trial of the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating a good faith effort at informal resolution. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (a).) The court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to: (1) the necessity and the reasons for the discovery, (2) the diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier, (3) any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party, and (4) the length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (b).)

Here, Defendant argues that discovery should be reopened “to excavate the information intentionally hidden by the alter ego of Plaintiff” (Motion, p. 7.) Defendant declares that it was recently discovered that “Plaintiff frauded [sic] the court by producing illegal evidence to evade paying alimony to Defendant, and also by not disclosing his income to evade child support with an alter ego business account.” (Li Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant also accuses Plaintiff of engaging in harassing conduct which interfered with her attempts to conduct discovery. (Li Decl., ¶ 4.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that discovery should not be reopened because Defendant has made no effort over the past two years to conduct any discovery. (Opp., p. 1.) Plaintiff also argues that the discovery sought relates to alimony and child support issues and not the partition action. (Opp., p. 2.)

In her reply brief filed on December 24, 2024, Defendant argues that she has, in fact, served interrogatories but that Plaintiff has been uncooperative. Plaintiff’s claim that she has participated in discovery is not persuasive because Defendant never moved to compel Plaintiff to serve responses to her interrogatories. Therefore, the Court is not inclined to reopen discovery based on Defendant’s evidence submitted in her latest filing. However, the Court notes that Defendant filed a motion for leave to file a cross-complaint and recognizes that discovery will necessarily be reopened if her request to file a cross-complaint is granted.

Accordingly, in the interest of judicial economy, the Court defers its decision to reopen discovery and CONTINUES this hearing to January 28, 2025, the same date that Defendant’s motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is scheduled to be heard.

The Court advises the parties that, given the allegations of the proposed cross-complaint, and Plaintiff’s argument with respect to this motion that the discovery sought relates to alimony and child support issues and not the partition action, the parties should be prepared, at the January 28 hearing, to address whether this matter belongs in family court and if any such court continues to have jurisdiction over the parties. The matter here is a real property partition action and any agreement with respect to ownership of or title to the property not reduced to writing may be non-cognizable. Moreover, Defendant apparently seeks to assert claims over real property that ultimately arise from modifications to court-ordered child and spousal support obligations without first obtaining the family court’s approval. Whether any such modifications, not having been reduced to writing or reflected in the property ownership records, would prevent a partition remains to be seen. But in any event, the valuation of Defendant’s claims to any partition proceeds would appear to require the application of family law child and spousal support principles.

Moving party to give notice.

I.            INTRODUCTION

This action was initiated on September 11, 2023, by plaintiff Michelle Wu (“Wu”) against defendants Eileen Wang, Yaoning Sun (“Sun”), Yuntao Chen aka Andy Chen (“Chen”), Jennifer Cheung (“Cheung”), and American Southwest Chamber of Commerce USA (“ASCC”). Wu asserts causes of action for defamation per se, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, breach of fiduciary duty, and accounting arising from her brief tenure as President and CEO of ASCC.

On August 27, 2024, Sun filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) against Wu for declaratory relief and asserting causes of action for misappropriation of funds, breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, accounting, damages, and “unlawful change of business entity.”

On October 24, 2024, Wu filed a motion to strike pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436 (which is misidentified on the Court’s docket summary as a “Motion to Strike with anti slapping motion”), as well as a special motion to strike pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16.

Sun filed opposition briefs on November 27, 2024.

Wu filed reply briefs on December 23, 2024.

II.          MOTION TO STRIKE PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 436

As an initial matter, the Court notes that although Wu’s motion is styled as a motion to strike “portions” of the FACC, Wu’s motion targets each cause of action in their entirety. Therefore, the Court will treat the so-called motion to “strike” as one for judgment on the pleadings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438.

A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought by a cross-defendant on the grounds that the cross-complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii).) “A trial court's determination of a motion for judgment on the pleadings accepts as true the factual allegations that the plaintiff makes. [Citations.] In addition, it gives them a liberal construction.” (Gerawan Farming, Inc. v. Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515.) As with a demurrer, “[a]ll properly pleaded, material facts are deemed true, but not contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law; judicially noticeable matters may be considered. (Kapsimallis v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 667, 672 [citing Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318].)

A.   First Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief/Defamation Per Se

For his First Cause of Action, Sun requests a judicial declaration that Wu is a limited public figure for purposes of the defamation per se cause of action asserted in Wu’s complaint. Declaratory relief may be obtained by any person claiming rights under a contract (oral or written) or under a written instrument other than a will or trust, or with respect to property. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.) Determining whether Wu is a “limited public figure” is not a proper subject for declaratory relief. Sun identifies no written instrument which sets forth his respective rights or duties, but only claims that whether she is a public figure affects the applicable standard of proof. (Opp., p. 5.) Indeed, the issue of public figure status merely delineates the constitutional limitations on the ability for certain plaintiffs to recover for defamation without proof of actual malice. (Reader’s Digest Assn. v. Superior Court (1984) 37 Cal.3d 244, 252-253 [citing New York Times Co. v. Sullivan (1964) 376 U.S. 254 and Gertz v. Robert Welch, Inc. (1974) 418 U.S. 323, 344-345.) This is insufficient to show that a proper subject for declaratory relief exists.

Insofar as Sun attempts to save his cause of action by claiming that it is rooted in Wu’s defamatory statements about him, this argument lacks merit because there are, in fact, no allegations within the FACC that Wu “falsely accused Sun of embezzlement and financial mismanagement within [] public communications.” (Opp., pp. 5-6.)

Accordingly, Wu’s motion to strike the First Cause of Action is GRANTED. Leave to amend is only granted insofar as Sun intends to allege a claim for defamation against Wu, which is not currently pleaded anywhere in the FACC.

B.   Sun’s Remaining Claims

Wu moves to strike Sun’s remaining Second, Third, Fourth, Fifth, Sixth, and Seventh Causes of Action on the grounds that Sun lacks standing to bring them. A plaintiff's lack of standing is a jurisdictional defect to an action that requires its dismissal. (Cummings v. Stanley (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 493, 501.)

For all these causes of action, Sun generally alleges that Wu owed fiduciary duties to ASCC and its members but breached them by depositing funds that were intended for ASCC into a personal bank account and issuing checks to herself from ASCC’s bank accounts without approval from the board of directors. (FACC, ¶¶ 25-27, 29, 35-37, 39, 46-47.) Sun also alleges that Wu failed to abide by ASCC’s bylaws and applicable laws in altering ASCC’s articles of incorporation without the board of director’s authorization. (FAC, ¶ 84.) Wu’s argument has merit because nowhere in the FACC does Sun allege that he sustained any personal injury from Wu’s alleged misconduct. All of the alleged damage is to ASCC’s operations, reputation, and relationships. (See, e.g., FACC, ¶¶ 30-32, 35-41, 49-52, 61, 93-94.) Furthermore, Sun does not address the issue of standing anywhere in his opposition brief, nor does he include any of the required allegations for a member’s derivative action in the FACC. (See Corp. Code, §§ 5710; 7710.) The Court additionally notes that “Damages” is not a cause of action, but a remedy, and the allegations comprising Sun’s Sixth Cause of Action simply repeat his allegations of misappropriation, breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, accounting.

Accordingly, Wu’s motion to strike the Second, Third, Fourth, and Fifth Causes of Action is GRANTED. Sun is granted leave to amend the FACC to add allegations of direct injury or properly allege a derivative action on behalf of ASCC.

III.        SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 425.16

Wu contends that the FACC arises from her constitutionally protected right to petition. However, since the Court GRANTS her motion to strike each cause of action from the FACC, the special motion to strike is MOOT.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Wu’s motion to strike is GRANTED with 20 days’ leave to amend.

Wu’s special motion to strike is MOOT.
