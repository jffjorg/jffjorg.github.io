DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV26319A    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV26319A

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Answers to Deposition Questions

MOVING PARTIES:

Plaintiff Veronica Mendez

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendants Guilford Glazer Properties, Inc. and The Diane and Guilford Glazer Foundation

           

MOTION

Plaintiff Veronica Mendez (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel Deponent William Neiman (“Deponent”) to answer deposition questions.  Defendants Guilford Glazer Properties, Inc. and the Diane and Guilford Glazer Foundation (“Defendants”) oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480, subdivision (a) provides, if a deponent fails to answer any question at deposition, the party that noticed the deposition may move to compel the deponent to answer.

In the instant case, counsel for Defendants instructed Deponent, who is the Chief Executive Officer of Defendant Guilford Glazer Properties, Inc., not to answer questions during Deponent’s deposition.  Counsel should not instruct a deponent not to answer any question unless the question implicates a privilege or deposing counsel’s behavior warrants suspension of the deposition.  (See Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015 (Stewart) [discovery rules “clearly contemplate that deponents not be prevented by counsel from answering a question unless it pertains to privileged matters or deposing counsel’s conduct has reached a stage where suspension is warranted”].)  

In their opposition brief, Defendants make no showing that these questions implicate any privilege or that suspension of the deposition was necessary for any reason.  Instead, Defendants argue the questions implicate the privacy of Defendants’ employees.  Privacy is not a privilege.  If Defendants believed the questions were so intrusive on the privacy rights of others to the extent they constituted harassment, Defendants’ remedy was to suspend the deposition and seek a protective order.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.470 [party may move for a protective order on the ground the examination is being conducted in bad faith or in a manner that unreasonably annoys, embarrasses, or oppresses that party]; Stewart, supra, 87 Cal.App.4th at p. 1015.)  Defendants failed to do so and have not filed a motion for protective order. 

For the same reason, Defendants’ arguments regarding questions about Defendants’ financial condition do not concern a privilege.  Accordingly, Deponent should have answered those questions. 

In opposition, Defendants argue Deponent answered the questions Plaintiff posed.  But Plaintiff seeks to compel Deponent to answer deposition questions that Defendants’ counsel instructed Deponent not to answer.  Deponent did not answer the questions on the advice of Defendants’ counsel.  As such, Defendants’ argument is nonsensical.

The motion to compel is granted.  Deponent is to appear for a second session of the deposition within 30 days of notice of this order, unless Plaintiff stipulates otherwise, and answer the questions set forth in Plaintiff’s Amended Separate Statement without objections.

Plaintiff requests sanctions in connection with this motion.  The Court finds Defendants opposed this motion without substantial justification and awards sanctions.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (j).)  The Court imposes sanctions against Defendants and their counsel of record, Cozen O’Connor, in the amount of $2,750, which represents five hours of attorney time to prepare the motion and attend the hearing at $550 per hour.  Defendants and their counsel of record, Cozen O’Connor, jointly and severally, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $2,750 to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days of notice of this order.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Plaintiff Veronica Mendez (“Plaintiff”) has filed two separate motions in which she moves to compel further responses from Defendant Guilford Glazer Properties (“Defendant”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set two (“RPD”) and Special Interrogatories, set two (“SROG”).  Defendant has not filed oppositions to those motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories or requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, 2031.310.)

Defendant’s responses to RPD Nos. 47 and 48 and SROG No. 35 consist only of objections.  As Plaintiff timely filed these motions to compel further responses, Defendant has the burden to justify its objections.  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  Defendant has not opposed the motions and, therefore, cannot meet that burden.  Accordingly, Defendant must further respond to RPD Nos. 47 and 48 and SROG No. 35.

In response to RPD Nos. 44, 45, 46, and 49 and SROG No. 36, Defendant provided responses with ambiguous limitations.  Defendant’s responses to RPD Nos. 44, 45, 46, and 49 are limited to documents “relating to Plaintiff’s claims and Defendant’s defenses in this case . . . ,” with no explanation of how Defendant defines relevance.  (See, e.g., Separate Statement in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, p. 4.)  Defendant’s response to SROG No. 36 is limited “to the time period,” with no explanation as to the specific period.  (Separate Statement in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, p. 7.)  These ambiguous limitations prevent Plaintiff from understanding the scope of the responses, rendering the responses effectively useless.  Defendant must further respond.

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 53 and 54 because Plaintiff does not believe Defendant produced all responsive documents.  Plaintiff cannot move to compel Defendant to further respond on the basis Plaintiff believes Defendant’s responses are untrue. (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 820.)  Plaintiff’s remedy is to offer Defendant’s allegedly incomplete responses as admissions or as prior inconsistent statements to impeach Defendant at trial, if Defendant seeks to introduce evidence it did not disclose in discovery.  (Evid. Code, §§ 1220; 1235.)  While Plaintiff complains Defendant redacted names of candidates for temporary employment with Defendant, the redaction is appropriate to protect the privacy rights of these candidates.  (Belaire-West Landscape, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 554,  .)  Defendant need not further respond to RPD Nos. 53 and 54.

The Court finds Defendant’s failure to properly respond to the written discovery an abuse of the discovery process, and awards sanctions.  The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record, Cozen O’Connor, in the amount of $1,650, which represents three hours of attorney time to prepare the motions at $550 per hour.  Defendant and its counsel of record, Cozen O’Connor, jointly and severally, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,650 to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days of notice of this order.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

CASE NUMBER

MOTION

Motion to Quash Subpoena

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Jane Doe

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.

 

MOTION

              Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves for protective orders to quash the subpoena Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) served on Deponent Dr. Christine Djapri (“Deponent”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires deposition testimony or the production of documents, the Court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the Court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

In the instant case, Plaintiff contends she was sexually harassed and assaulted by her co-worker Eric Pagel (“Pagel”) while she worked for Defendant.  Defendant served a subpoena on Deponent, Plaintiff’s treating gynecologist, in which Defendant indicated it seeks to depose Deponent and asks Deponent to produce documents.  Plaintiff testified she had a gynecology appointment with Deponent shortly after Pagel’s alleged assault, and Plaintiff informed Deponent of the assault.  As such, Deponent is a percipient witness.  Indeed, Plaintiff has also subpoenaed documents from Deponent in this action.  Further, Defendant has already deposed Deponent. 

Plaintiff did not serve this motion on Defendant until three minutes prior to the commencement of Deponent’s deposition.  Plaintiff’s counsel did not appear at the deposition, and Defendant proceeded with it, unaware of the motion to quash.  Under these circumstances,  the motion is moot.

In reply, Plaintiff argues Defendant wrongfully proceeded with the deposition because Plaintiff had objected to the deposition subpoena.  Plaintiff is incorrect.  Plaintiff was required to seek a protective order to preclude Defendant from taking Deponent’s deposition or to limit the scope thereof.  The scope of Deponent’s deposition is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice, to which Plaintiff could object.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).) 

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order, and file proof of service of such.

 


Case Number: 23STCV02273B    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 39

HEARING DATE

September 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV02273B

MOTION

Motion to Quash Subpoena

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Jane Doe

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.

 

MOTION

              Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves for protective orders to quash the subpoena Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) served on Deponent Christopher Lee (“Deponent”).  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires deposition testimony or the production of documents, the Court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the Court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

In the instant case, Plaintiff contends she was sexually harassed and assaulted by her co-worker Eric Pagel (“Pagel”) while she worked for Defendant.  Defendant served a subpoena on Deponent, in which Defendant also seeks the production of documents.  Deponent is Plaintiff’s brother-in-law as well as Plaintiff’s current and former co-worker.  Plaintiff has a right to privacy in “various private or sensitive information regarding [her] personal life . . . .”  (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1003-1004.)  This includes Plaintiff’s relationship with Deponent.  Accordingly, the Court cannot order disclosure of information regarding Plaintiff’s relationship with Deponent unless such information is directly relevant to this litigation.  (See Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  

However, Deponent is a percipient witness in this action.  As reflected in documents produced during discovery, after Pagel’s alleged assault of Plaintiff, Plaintiff discussed with Deponent, who was working for Defendant’s competitor at the time, the possibility of leaving with Pagel to work with Deponent for Defendant’s competitor.  Plaintiff also testified she told Deponent about Pagel’s assault.  (See Opposition, pp. 7-8.)  Therefore, Defendant is entitled to conduct discovery concerning what Plaintiff told Deponent.  Accordingly, the motion to quash is denied.

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV06987    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 39

DEPARTMENT

HEARING DATE

September 24, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV06987

MOTION

Motion for Sanctions

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Windsor Healthcare Management, Inc., SNF Management Company, LLC, Amanda Moore, Antelope Holdings I LLC, Newgen Administrative services, LLC, 9560 Pico LLC, and Pico AR LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Cynthia Batac

 

MOTION

             Defendants Windsor Healthcare Management, Inc., SNF Management Company, LLC, Amanda Moore, Antelope Holdings I LLC, Newgen Administrative services, LLC, 9560 Pico LLC, and Pico AR LLC (“Defendants”) move for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.  In its order of March 26, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ request for leave to file this motion notwithstanding the stay of this action pending arbitration.[1]  Plaintiff Cynthia Batac (“Plaintiff”) opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

In its order of March 26, 2024, the Court stated, “Defendants request monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,810 under Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030. The Court finds that Plaintiff’s Counsel willfully violated this Court’s order by propounding discovery, which constitutes an abuse of the discovery process. The Court finds that there is no good cause for Plaintiff’s conduct in this case. The Court finds that the amount of the requested sanctions is fair and reasonable under the circumstances to compensate Defendants from having to litigate this issue. The Court finds that equitable considerations favor Defendants on this issue. Nevertheless, the Court must deny the request for sanctions because the Civil Discovery Act’s sections defining conduct subject to discovery sanctions do not independently authorize trial courts to impose monetary sanctions for misuse of discovery; they authorize sanctions only to the extent authorized by another provision of the act.”  (March 26, 2024 Minute Order.)  However, the Court expressly and explicitly granted Defendants’ request to file a motion for sanctions to address Plaintiff counsel’s blatant violation of the Court’s order. 

Defendants move for sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 based on “Plaintiff’s direct violation of this Court’s August 17, 2023 Order mandating a stay of this entire action pending arbitration by filing several ‘Doe’ Amendments in superior court and also improperly attempting to engage in discovery, including unilaterally noticing eight depositions with requests for production of documents.”  (Notice of Motion for Sanctions, p. 2.)  Section 128.5 permits the court to award attorney fees a party incurred “as a result of bad-faith actions or tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).)

 As set forth above, this Court has already found Plaintiff engaged in bad faith tactics by violating the Court’s stay order and propounding discovery in this matter despite the stay.  Among other things, Plaintiff contends Defendants did not give her an opportunity to withdraw the discovery or dismiss the Doe amendments before filing this motion, as required.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subds. (f)(1)(B), (D).)  However, it is clear, under the circumstances of this matter, Defendants could not satisfy the section 128.5’s “safe harbor” provision because the Court had already determined the Doe Defendants would be a part of any arbitration proceedings, and it had also determined Plaintiff had improperly initiated discovery concerning those Defendants.  Thus, there was nothing for Plaintiff to withdraw or correct.  It was in this context at the hearing on March 26, 2024 on Defendant’s motion to enforce the stay of this action pending arbitration the Court granted Defendant’s oral request for relief from stay to file the instant motion for sanctions concerning Plaintiff’s violation of the Court’s stay order. 

Therefore, the Court does not find Defendants’ motion to be procedurally defective and grants the motion. Accordingly, the Court imposes monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel in the amount of $8,482.40 for Plaintiff’s violations of the Court’s order.  Plaintiff is to pay this amount within 30 days of the service of this order.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]Defendants filed a second and separate motion for sanctions related to what they describe as Plaintiff’s repeated claim Defendants had not paid the arbitration filing fee and, therefore, Defendants had waived any entitlement to arbitration, and the matter should be returned to this Court.  The Court’s March 26, 2024 order did authorize Defendants to bring the instant sanctions motion, but there was no mention of the stay being lifted for the filing of a separate sanctions motion involving Plaintiff’s assertions concerning Defendants’ failure to pay arbitration filing fees.  The Court declines to consider the second sanctions motion, as no provision was made for Defendants to file it before this Court while the stay is in place.
