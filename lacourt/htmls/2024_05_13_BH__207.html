DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV37567    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

May 13, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV37567

MOTION

Motion for Reconsideration

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Pericles Chamis

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Millennium Medical Associates, Inc.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On or about November 4, 2020, Plaintiff Pericles Chamis (“Plaintiff”) underwent an upper endoscopy procedure, during which “Plaintiff began vomiting and convulsing on the operating table.”  (Fifth Amended Complaint [“5AC”] ¶¶ 20, 26.)  A subsequent MRI revealed that Plaintiff suffered a stroke, causing central visual impairment, dysphagia, and cognitive and behavioral issues.  (5AC ¶ 32.) 

 

On October 12, 2021, Plaintiff brought suit against the surgeon, Defendant Dale Prokupek, M.D. and Defendant Dale Prokupek, M.D., Inc. (“Prokupek”); anesthesiologist Defendant Michelle Dinh, M.D. (“Dinh”); and Defendant Soma Surgery Center, Inc. (“Soma”), where the endoscopy was performed, alleging three causes of action: (1) professional negligence – medical malpractice; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; and (3) intentional misrepresentation.

 

Dr. Prokupek’s nurse practitioner, Defendant Jeanne Hershey-Weber (“Hershey-Weber”); the nurse who assisted during the procedure, Defendant Melanie Kerr (“Kerr”); Dr. Prokupek’s employee Defendant Andrew Gelis (“Gelis”); and Dr. Prokupek’s other company, Defendant Millennium Medical Associates, Inc. (“Millennium”) were added via Doe amendment on November 18, 2022, in place of Does 1-4, respectively.  Defendant Sean Ravaei (“Ravaei”), owner and operator of Soma, was added to the complaint via Doe amendment on December 1, 2022, in place of Doe 5.

 

On February 27, 2024, the Court granted Millennium’s motion for summary judgment.  Judgment was entered in Millennium’s favor on March 14, 2024.

 

Plaintiff now moves for reconsideration of the Court’s February 27, 2024 order granting summary judgment in favor of Millennium.  Millennium opposes and Plaintiff replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a), “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make an application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.  The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)  Where the statutory requirements are met, reconsideration should be granted; upon reconsideration, however, the court may simply reaffirm its original order.  (Corns v. Miller (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 195, 202.) 

 

The moving party on a motion for reconsideration “must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time[.]” (Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342, internal quotations & citations omitted; see New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 221 [on a motion for reconsideration, a party must present new or different facts, circumstances, or law, which the moving party “could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced” in connection with the original hearing].)

 

JURISDICTION

 

As Millennium points out, “after entry of judgment, a trial court has no further power to rule on a motion for reconsideration.”  (Ramon v. Aerospace Corp. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1233, 1236.)  “Once judgment has been entered […] the court may not reconsider it and loses its unrestricted power to change the judgment. It may correct judicial error only through certain limited procedures such as motions for new trial and motions to vacate the judgment.”  (Ibid.)

 

This is true even where, as here, the motion for reconsideration was filed before judgment, but judgment was entered prior to the scheduled hearing.  (See Safeco Insurance Company of Illinois v. Architectural Facades Unlimited, Inc. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1477.)

 

In Reply, Plaintiff argues that the Court has jurisdiction because Plaintiff filed the motion within 10 days after service of written notice of the entry of the Court’s summary judgment order, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.  Plaintiff further argues that if the Court were divested of jurisdiction to hear Plaintiff’s motion, “Section 1008 would be rendered meaningless.”  (Reply at p. 2.)

 

Although Plaintiff complied with the jurisdictional requirements of Section 1008, the law is clear that entry of the judgment divests the Court of jurisdiction to hear Plaintiff’s pending motion for reconsideration. 

 

Moreover, Section 1008 is not rendered meaningless by virtue of this rule.  Motions for reconsideration are still a valid way of challenging orders prior to entry of judgment.  Once judgment has been entered, however, the parties are limited to pursuing post-judgment remedies.[1]

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Therefore, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied because the Court no longer has jurisdiction to rule on it.

DEPARTMENT

CASE NUMBER

23SMCV00036

MOTION

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

MOVING PARTY

Jacob O. Partiyeli, Esq.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff SM 10000 Property LLC

 

MOTION

 

            Jacob O. Partiyeli, counsel for Defendant Tyrina Lee (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel, citing breakdown of the attorney-client relationship.  Plaintiff SM 10000 Property LLC (“Plaintiff”) opposes the motion.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 provides “[t]he attorney in an action or special proceeding may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination as follows: 1. Upon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered in the minutes; 2. Upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

 

Procedural Requirements

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, requires:

 

(1) the motion must be made on form MC-051; (subd. (a));

 

(2) it must be accompanied by a declaration on form MC-052 stating why the motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) instead of a consent brought under section 284(1); (subd. (c));

 

(3) a proposed order on form MC-053 must be lodged with the court, specifying all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known; (subd. (e)); and

 

(4) The documents must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. (subd. (d).)

 

If the notice is served by mail or electronic service, it must be accompanied by a declaration indicating that the address served is the current address, or in the case of service by mail, that it was served on the last known address and a more current address could not be located after reasonable efforts within 30 days before filing the motion.  (Ibid.)  The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Substantive Requirements

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(a) outlines the reasons a lawyer must withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client is bringing an action, conducting a defense, asserting a position in litigation, or taking an appeal, without probable cause and for the purpose of harassing or maliciously injuring any person;

 

(2)   the representation will result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the State Bar Act;

 

(3)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it unreasonably difficult to carry out the representation effectively; or

 

(4)   the client discharges the lawyer.

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(b) outlines the reasons a lawyer may withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client insists upon presenting a claim or defense in litigation, or asserting a position or making a demand in a non-litigation matter, that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law;

 

(2)   the client either seeks to pursue a criminal or fraudulent course of conduct or has used the lawyer’s services to advance a course of conduct that the lawyer reasonably believes was a crime or fraud;

 

(3)   the client insists that the lawyer pursue a course of conduct that is criminal or fraudulent;

 

(4)   the client by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(5)   the client breaches a material term of an agreement with, or obligation, to the lawyer relating to the representation, and the lawyer has given the client a reasonable warning after the breach that the lawyer will withdraw unless the client fulfills the agreement or performs the obligation;

 

(6)   the client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the representation;

 

(7)   the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal;

 

(8)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(9)   a continuation of the representation is likely to result in a violation of these rules or the State Bar Act; or

 

(10)         the lawyer believes in good faith in a proceeding pending before a tribunal that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Counsel has filed forms MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053.  The attorney declaration (MC-052) indicates that the motion was filed instead of filing a consent because:

 

There has been a breakdown in attorney client relationship. (1) THERE EXIST MULTIPLE CAUSES ALLOWING PERMISSIVE WITHDRAWAL AND WARRANTING MANDATORY WITHDRAWAL

 

(MC-052 at ¶ 2.)  As such, the Court finds that the motion complies with the Rules of Professional Conduct, rules 1.16(b)(4).) 

 

            The proof of service indicates each of these forms was served by mail to the client at 1000 Santa Monica Blvd., Unit 2507 and to counsel of record for Plaintiff.  The declaration further indicates that the client was served by mail at the client’s last known address and the attorney has confirmed within the past 30 days that the address is current by conversation.  (MC-052 at ¶ 3.)

 

            Plaintiff opposes the motion on the grounds that, despite Counsel’s declaration indicating that the address has been confirmed current by a conversation within the past 30 days, Counsel knows that address is not Defendant’s current address, because on December 28, 2023, the parties entered into a settlement agreement that indicated Defendant vacated the premises on May 15, 2023, nearly a year ago.  (Olsen Decl. ¶¶ 2-3 and Ex. 1.)  Counsel has not replied.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Considering Plaintiff’s uncontested evidence that Counsel knowingly served the client at an address vacated nearly a year ago, without declaring that Counsel has been unable to confirm that the address is current or locate a more current address and describing the efforts taken to locate the client, the Court denies without prejudice the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for improper service on the client.

 

Counsel shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service within 5 days of the ruling.

Case Number: 23SMCV02555    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

May 13, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23SMCV02555

MOTION

Demurrer to Amended Answer to Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Elisa Perlman

OPPOSING PARTY

Cross-Defendant Andrea L. Valero

 

MOTION

 

The case and consolidated case no. 23SMCV03059 arise from a neighbor dispute between Defendant and Cross-Complainant Elisa Perlman (“Perlman”), and her downstairs neighbors, Andrea and Gelly Valero.  Cross-Defendant Andrea Valero (“Valero”) was also the sole member of the board of directors for the Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Malibu Road Homeowners Association (“HOA”) that governs the property during most of the events giving rise to the litigation.  The Valeros and the HOA brought two separate lawsuits against Perlman stemming from the same factual dispute concerning water leaks in the Valeros’ unit, allegedly caused by Perlman.

 

Perlman filed a Cross-Complaint in this action against the HOA and Valero, alleging nineteen causes of action for breach of the CC&Rs; breach of other governing documents; violations of Civil Code sections 5210; 4950; 4910; 4920; 4765; 5600; 4923; 4930; 5605; 4925; 4935; 5610; 5615; and 4040; two causes of action for breach of fiduciary duties; and declaratory relief.

 

On February 8, 2024, Valero answered Perlman’s Cross-Complaint, and on March 1, Valero filed a first amended answer. 

 

Perlman now demurs to Valero’s first amended answer on the grounds that the affirmative defenses fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a defense and are uncertain, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.20.   

 

Valero opposes the motion and Perlman replies. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.     DEMURRER TO ANSWER – GENERAL STANDARDS

 

A plaintiff may demur to an answer if: “(a) The answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.  [¶]  (b) The answer is uncertain. As used in this subdivision, ‘uncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.  [¶]  (c) Where the answer pleads a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the answer whether the contract is written or oral.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.20.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 431.30, subdivision (b) provides that an answer to the complaint shall contain, in addition to a “general or specific denial” of the complaint's allegations, a statement of any new matter constituting a defense. It has long been held that if the onus of proof is thrown upon the defendant, the matter to be proved by him is new matter.”  (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 239 [cleaned up].) 

 

As “[t]o the allegations of new matter, [n]umerous defenses were purportedly raised by defendants’ allegations of affirmative defense.  Most of these allegations fail to state a defense even when liberally construed in defendants’ favor. Some are simply immaterial.  For example, defendants allege as a conclusion that plaintiff's claim is barred by laches, an equitable defense that has no application to the plaintiff's legal claim.  All of the allegations are proffered in the form of terse legal conclusions, rather than as facts averred as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and are alleged in the complaint. The only affirmative defenses that are mentioned in the summary judgment proceedings, fraud in the inducement and failure of consideration, are not well pled, consisting of legal conclusions, and would not have survived a demurrer.”  (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384 [cleaned up]; see also Quantification Settlement Agreement Cases (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 758, 813 [“affirmative defenses cannot be pled as mere legal conclusions but must instead be alleged with as much factual detail as the allegations of a complaint”]; Department of Finance v. City of Merced (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 286, 294, fn. 6 [boilerplate affirmative defenses without factual support are deficient].) 

 

2.     HEIGHTENED PLEADING STANDARDS FOR EQUITABLE DEFENSES

 

Perlman argues that Valero’s sixth affirmative defense (estoppel/waiver), seventh affirmative defense (laches), and seventeenth affirmative defense (unclean hands) fail to satisfy the heightened pleading standard for equitable defenses.  (See Sofranek v. County of Merced (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1238, 1250-1251 [“The elements of estoppel must be especially pleaded […] with sufficient accuracy to disclose facts relied upon”] (hereafter Sofranek); Meyer Koulish Co. v. Cannon (1963) 213 Cal.App.2d 419, 432 [“Waiver is an affirmative defense that must be pleaded with specificity and separately stated”].)

 

The elements of estoppel are “(1) the party to be estopped must be apprised of the facts; (2) he must intend that his conduct be acted on, or must so act that the party asserting estoppel had a right to believe it was so intended; (3) the party asserting estoppel must be ignorant of the true state of facts; and (4) he must rely upon the conduct to his injury.”  (Sofranek, supra, 146 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1250-1251.)  Here, the sixth affirmative defense alleges, “Cross-Complainant was appropriately apprised of all relevant facts and developments in the underlying proceeding, knew of the facts and circumstances that give rise to the Cross-Complaint, and engaged in conduct that is contrary to her efforts to now obtain relief in this action.” 

 

Because the sixth affirmative defense does not address Valero’s ignorance of “all relevant facts and developments in the underlying proceeding;” Perlman’s intent that her conduct be acted upon; or Valero’s reliance, and because the sixth affirmative defense combines waiver and estoppel, instead of separately stating the affirmative defense of waiver, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the sixth affirmative defense.

 

However, Perlman does not provide any case law that the affirmative defenses of laches or unclean hands require heightened pleading requirements.  Valero’s seventh cause of action for laches alleges, “Cross-Complainant knew or should have known of the facts that give rise to her claim well over a year prior to filing this action, and unduly delayed in seeking relief.” 

 

Similarly, Valero’s seventeenth affirmative defense for unclean hands alleges, “Cross-Complainant, and/or her agents, were appropriately apprised of all relevant facts and developments in the underlying proceeding, and remained actively involved in the decision-making process regarding the manner in which the underlying litigation proceed. This answering Cross-Defendant alleges that Cross-Complainant’s conduct in this regard contributed and/or caused all, or a proportion of, the damages that Cross-Complainant now claims, to the extent Cross-Complainant has any damages, which this answering Cross-Defendant denies.”

 

These allegations provide sufficient factual detail, absent a heightened pleading standard.

 

3.     MERE DENIALS

 

Perlman next argues that Valero’s first (failure to state a cause of action), eighteenth (breach of obligations), nineteenth (no breach of duty), and twenty-first (failure to use reasonable diligence) affirmative defenses are mere denials of Perlman’s operative causes of action, as opposed to affirmative defenses that introduce “new matter.”  In support, Perlman cites State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 721, 725 (hereafter State Farm).  In State Farm, the appellate court held that the trial court erred in excluding evidence at trial that the insurer followed the advice of counsel on the grounds that advice of counsel was not alleged in the answer.  Specifically, the Court held that following the advice of counsel addresses an essential element of the plaintiff’s causes of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, fraud, and bad faith, and therefore does not constitute a “new matter” not put in issue by the plaintiff which must be raised by way of the answer.

 

Thus, State Farm stands for the proposition that a defendant may introduce evidence of defenses at trial that were not raised in the answer, if they were raised by the operative cause of action, such that they do not constitute “new matter.”

 

However, the fact that Valero has included allegations in the answer affirmative defenses that are arguably not “new matter” does not necessarily mean they are demurrable.  Perlman has not cited any case law standing for the proposition that affirmative defenses that address allegations already made in the operative complaint (or cross-complaint) such that they are not “new matter” are somehow not “a defense” under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.20. 

 

Therefore, the fact that some of Valero’s affirmative defenses may or may not constitute “new matter” is not a basis for a demurrer.

 

4.     NEGLIGENCE DEFENSES

 

Perlman next argues that the second, fourth, and eighth affirmative defenses (contributory negligence, assumption of the risk, and comparative negligence, respectively) sound in negligence and are therefore inapplicable, as the Cross-Complaint does not allege any causes of action for negligence. 

 

“Contributory negligence is conduct on the part of the plaintiff which falls below the standard to which he could conform for his own protection, and which is a legally contributing cause co-operating with the negligence of the defendant in bringing about the plaintiff's harm.”  (Gyerman v. United States Lines Co. (1972) 7 Cal.3d 488, 500.)  As such, to allege comparative or contributory negligence as an affirmative defense, negligence must generally be alleged as to the defendant or cross-defendant.

 

Here, the Cross-Complaint alleges two causes of action for breach of governing documents; fourteen causes of action for violating various sections of the Civil Code; two causes of action for breach of fiduciary duties; and a cause of action for declaratory relief.  Because none of the causes of action in the Cross-Complaint allege Valero’s negligence, the affirmative defenses for contributory negligence and comparative negligence appear generally inapplicable and irrelevant here.

 

However, Civil Code section 5985 provides:

 

(a)   In an action maintained by an association pursuant to subdivision (b), (c), or (d) of Section 5980, the amount of damages recovered by the association shall be reduced by the amount of damages allocated to the association or its managing agents in direct proportion to their percentage of fault based upon principles of comparative fault. The comparative fault of the association or its managing agents may be raised by way of defense, but shall not be the basis for a cross-action or separate action against the association or its managing agents for contribution or implied indemnity, where the only damage was sustained by the association or its members. It is the intent of the Legislature in enacting this subdivision to require that comparative fault be pleaded as an affirmative defense, rather than a separate cause of action, where the only damage was sustained by the association or its members.

 

(b)   In an action involving damages described in subdivision (b), (c), or (d) of Section 5980, the defendant or cross-defendant may allege and prove the comparative fault of the association or its managing agents as a setoff to the liability of the defendant or cross-defendant even if the association is not a party to the litigation or is no longer a party whether by reason of settlement, dismissal, or otherwise.

 

Thus, a defendant or cross-defendant could allege as an affirmative defense the contributory or comparative fault of the homeowners association or its managing agent (like Valero, as the sole member of the HOA’s board of directors) to reduce their liability.  However, the second and eighth affirmative defenses allege the contributory/comparative negligence of Perlman, a homeowner, not of the HOA or of its managing agent, Valero.  Valero does not provide any legal basis for a sole member of the HOA’s board of directors to allege as an affirmative defense the comparative or contributory fault of a homeowner as exculpation for breaching the CC&Rs, breaching her fiduciary duty, or violating California’s Civil Code.

 

Moreover, the affirmative defense of assumption of the risk makes no sense in the context of the causes of action alleged.  Perlman could not assume the risk that Valero would breach the governing documents, violate the Civil Code, or breach her fiduciary duties. 

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the second, fourth, and eighth affirmative defenses.

 

5.     INAPPLICABLE DEFENSES

 

Perlman next contends that Valero’s sixteenth (violation of CC&Rs) and twenty-second (no punitive damages) affirmative defenses are inapplicable and irrelevant. 

 

With regard to the sixteenth affirmative defense, the Court agrees.  Whether Perlman also violated the CC&Rs or acted in bad faith has no bearing on Valero’s alleged liability for breaching the governing documents, violating the Civil Code, or breaching her fiduciary duty.  In other words, Valero is not absolved of her duty to abide by the CC&Rs and other governing documents, comply with statutory law, or act in accordance with her fiduciary duties just because Perlman may also have violated the CC&Rs or acted in bad faith.  A homeowner’s breach of the CC&Rs or bad faith does not absolve the HOA or its managing agent of their legal duties and obligations or otherwise give Valero carte blanche to violate statutes or abandon her fiduciary duties.  Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the sixteenth affirmative defense.

 

With regard to the twenty-second affirmative defense, on January 18, 2024, the Court granted Valero’s motion to strike and struck the punitive damages allegations from Perlman’s cross-complaint.  (Minute Order, Jan. 18, 2024.)  Valero’s answer and amended answer, which were subsequently filed, nonetheless assert the affirmative defense that Perlman is precluded for recovering punitive damages because Valero did not act with malice, oppression, or fraud, pursuant to California Civil Code section 3294. 

 

The Court agrees.  Because punitive damages are no longer at issue, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the twenty-second affirmative defense. 

 

6.     STANDING

 

Perlman also argues that Valero’s fifth affirmative defense for standing is improper because Valero alleges no facts in support of that affirmative defense.  Civil Code section 5975, subdivision (a) provides that CC&Rs may be enforced by a homeowner, like Perlman.  Similarly, Civil Code section 4955 provides that “A member of an association may bring a civil action for declaratory or equitable relief for a violation of this article” and Civil Code section 5235 provides “A member may bring an action to enforce that member’s right to inspect and copy the association records.” 

 

In opposition, Valero does not identify any reasons why Perlman would not have standing to raise the causes of action alleged.  Rather, Valero indicates she pleaded alternative grounds for relief “to ensure that no waiver occurs” and “As discovery is proceeding, Valero is still evaluating the cause of Perlman’s alleged damages, if any. There is no bar to pleading alternative theories in an answer[.]” 

 

Therefore, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the fifth affirmative defense.

 

7.     RESERVATION OF RIGHTS

 

Valero’s twenty-fourth affirmative defense indicates that Valero may, through the course of discovery, become aware of additional affirmative defenses, and reserves the right to assert them.  Perlman argues that should additional affirmative defenses come to light through the discovery process, Valero will be required to move for leave to amend the answer, and the purported reservation of rights does not circumvent this requirement.

 

Valero responds that she agrees that a noticed motion would still be required in such a scenario, but included the reservation of rights as an affirmative defense “to simply apprise Perlman, due to the lack of knowledge and information at the time of answering, that Valero, if discovery so indicates, would not be waiving its [sic] right to later seek leave to amend to add necessary affirmative defenses.”  (Opposition at p. 5.)

 

The Court agrees with the parties that in such a situation, a noticed motion would be required.  As such, while a statement reserving the right to amend the answer to assert additional affirmative defenses may be appropriate to include in the answer to put Perlman on notice that there is no waiver of such a right, it is not in itself an affirmative defense.

 

Therefore, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the twenty-fourth affirmative defense.

 

8.     INSUFFICIENT FACTUAL DETAIL

 

Perlman also argues that all twenty-three affirmative defenses fail to plead sufficient factual detail.  Specifically, Perlman asks:

 

(1)   How did the acts and omissions of third parties bar Ms. Perlman from seeking relief?  What third parties? (AA, ¶ 2.)

 

(2)   What conduct estops Ms. Perlman from seeking relief? (AA, ¶ 6.)

 

(3)   How did Ms. Perlman unfairly delay prosecuting this case?  Why does laches apply?  (AA, ¶ 7.)

 

(4)   What bad thing did Ms. Perlman do?  When?  Why are her hands unclean?  (AA, ¶ 17.)

 

(5)   How could Ms. Perlman have prevented or minimized her damages? (AA, ¶ 20.)

 

(Demurrer at p. 15.)

 

            “A demurrer shall distinctly specify the grounds upon which any of the objections to the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer are taken. Unless it does so, it may be disregarded.”  (Code. Civ. Proc., § 430.60.)  Because Perlman does not specify any other affirmative defenses that fail to plead sufficient factual detail, the Court disregards Perlman’s general demurrer for insufficient factual detail as to all other affirmative defenses not specifically identified.

 

            The Court has already sustained Perlman’s demurrer to the second and sixth affirmative defenses, and therefore, need not separately evaluate them here.

 

            Further, the Court has already analyzed the seventh and seventeenth causes of action, and determined that the allegations are sufficient to state an affirmative defense.

 

            With regard to the twentieth affirmative defense for failure to mitigate damages, the amended answer alleges “Cross-Defendant is informed and believes and upon such information and belief alleges that Cross-Complainant could have made reasonable efforts or expenditures, without undue risk or hardship, to avoid alleged damages. Cross-Complainant is not entitled to recover damages for harm she could have so avoided.” 

 

            The Court agrees with Perlman.  The Cross-Complaint alleges that Valero abused her discretion as the sole Board member “by asserting total control over Ms. Perlman’s deck repairs that only benefited her own interests.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 1, 21.)  The Cross-Complaint further alleges “As a direct and proximate cause of the HOA’s breaches, Ms. Perlman suffered damages, including incurring unnecessary and excessive repair costs, and lost rental income due to the intentional delays caused by the HOA” (Cross-Complaint ¶ 159) and that Perlman paid the invoice sent by Valero’s property manager for $3,470.52 for repairs and materials related to the purported water damage in Valero’s unit (Cross-Complaint ¶ 18.)  Within the context of the Cross-Complaint, it is unclear how Perlman could possibly have mitigated her damages.

 

            Moreover, Valero does not address mitigation at all in opposition to the demurrer, and has therefore abandoned the issue.  (Herzberg v. County of Plumas (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1, 20 [noting a plaintiff’s failure to oppose a demurrer to a cause of action or portion thereof constitutes an abandonment of the issue or claim].) 

 

Therefore, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the twentieth affirmative defense. 

 

9.     LEAVE TO AMEND

 

A plaintiff has the burden of showing in what manner the complaint could be amended and how the amendment would change the legal effect of the complaint, i.e., state a cause of action. (See The Inland Oversight Committee v. City of San Bernardino (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 771, 779; PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v. Hulven Int'l, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 156, 189.) A plaintiff must not only state the legal basis for the amendment, but also the factual allegations sufficient to state a cause of action or claim. (See PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v. Hulven Int'l, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.App.5th at p. 189.) Moreover, a plaintiff does not meet his or her burden by merely stating in the opposition to a demurrer or motion to strike that “if the Court finds the operative complaint deficient, plaintiff respectfully requests leave to amend.” (See Major Clients Agency v Diemer (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1133; Graham v. Bank of America (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 618 [asserting an abstract right to amend does not satisfy the burden].)

 

Here, Valero has failed to meet this burden, as she does not specify what additional facts could be added to correct the deficiencies identified. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court sustains Perlman’s demurrer to the second, fourth, fifth, sixth, eighth, sixteenth, twenty-second, and twenty-fourth affirmative defenses without leave to amend, but overrules Perlman’s demurrer to the first, seventeenth, eighteenth, nineteenth, and twenty-first affirmative defenses.  The Court also overrules Perlman’s demurrer to all other affirmative defenses, on the grounds that Perlman did not specify the specific grounds upon which her objections to the remaining affirmative defenses are taken. 

 

Perlman shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

CASE NUMBER

SC129270

MOTION

Leave to Extend the 5-Year Deadline

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Emaar LA Properties, LLC and EJL Homes Realty & Construction, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

(none)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 14, 2018, Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant James Lee doing business as JCL Contractors (“Plaintiff” or “Lee”) brought suit against Defendants and Cross-Complainants Emaar LA Properties, LLC (“Emaar”) and EJL Homes Realty & Construction, Inc. (“EJL”) (together, “Cross-Complainants”), stemming from a construction dispute.   

 

On September 4, 2018, Cross-Complainants filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff.  From May 14, 2018 when the original complaint was filed until April or May 2022, the parties did not conduct any discovery.  (Ralls Decl. ¶ 10; Lee Decl. ¶ 9.)  During that time, the parties attended four mediations on December 4, 2018; October 29, 2020; August 19, 2021; and November 11, 2021.  (Lee Decl. ¶ 6.) 

 

On May 4, 2023, the parties filed a stipulation to extend the 5-year deadline to May 14, 2024.  The parties settled Plaintiff’s claims and the original complaint was dismissed on August 9, 2023.  Pursuant to the parties’ stipulation, the First Amended Cross-Complaint was filed on August 8, 2023, naming Lee and JCL Contractors, Inc. (“JCL”).  Lee was dismissed from the cross-complaint on August 17, 2023.  Default was entered against JCL on September 26, 2023.

 

From September 26, 2023, when default was entered against JCL until early February 2024, Cross-Complainants were in discussions and document exchanges with AIG, JCL’s insurance provider, in expectation that AIG would defend JCL and/or intervene in the lawsuit.  (Pursiano Decl. ¶¶ 8-13.)  On February 2, 2024, AIG advised it would not defend JCL or intervene.  Cross-Complainants asked AIG to reconsider, and on February 9, 2024, AIG reiterated its position that it would not defend JCL or provide indemnity coverage to JCL.  (Pursiano Decl. ¶ 13.)

 

Cross-complainants now move to extend the 5-year deadline.  The motion unopposed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310, “[a]n action shall be brought to trial within five years after the action is commenced against the defendant.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.310.)  In computing the five-year deadline, the following time periods are excluded: (a) the jurisdiction of the court to try the action was suspended; (b) prosecution or trial of the action was stayed or enjoined; and (c) bringing the action to trial, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.340.)

 

“An action shall be dismissed by the court on its own motion or on motion of the defendant, after notice to the parties, if the action is not brought to trial within the time prescribed in this article.  The requirements of this article are mandatory and are not subject to extension, excuse, or exception except as expressly provided by statute.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.360.)  Dismissal is warranted where the plaintiff has failed to prosecute the action with “reasonable diligence.”  (Dunsmuir Masonic Temple v. Superior Court (1970) 12 Cal.App.3d 17, 22-23.)  However, Civil Code of Procedure section 583.330 provides:

 

The parties may extend the time within which an action must be brought to trial pursuant to this article by the following means:

 

(a) By written stipulation.  The stipulation need not be filed but, if it is not filed, the stipulation shall be brought to the attention of the court if relevant to a motion for dismissal. 

 

(b) By oral agreement made in open court, if entered in the minutes of the court or a transcript is made.

 

            Moreover, “If the time within which an action must be brought to trial pursuant to this article is tolled or otherwise extended pursuant to statute with the result that at the end of the period of tolling or extension less than six months remains within which the action must be brought to trial, the action shall not be dismissed pursuant to this article if the action is brought to trial within six months after the end of the period of tolling or extension.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.350.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Cross-Complainants argue that bringing the action to trial was impossible to bring the action to trial after default was entered against JCL on September 26, 2023.  In support, Cross-Complainants cite to Maguire v. Collier (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 309, which noted that the period between entry of default and default judgment must be excluded from the five-year rule calculation, and the Court has no discretion.  (Id. at p. 313.)  That rule was criticized and held to be dictum by Hughes v. Kimble (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th, which held “[t]he critical factor in determining whether the exception applies in a given factual situation is whether the plaintiff has exercised reasonable diligence in prosecuting his or her case.”  (Id. at p. 67.)

 

In Howard v. Thrifty Drug & Discount Stores (1995) 10 Cal.4th 424, the Supreme Court held that the five-year statute was tolled during the period in which the defendant attempted to vacate the default, until default was successfully vacated.  (Id. at p. 438.)

 

Here, default was entered against JCL on September 26, 2023 and Cross-Complainants requested default judgment on March 29, 2024.  However, default judgment cannot be entered on the existing Cross-Complaint, because it does not allege the specific amount of damages sought.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 580, subd. (a) [“The relief granted to the plaintiff, if there is no answer, cannot exceed that demanded in the complaint”]; Levine v. Smith (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 1131, 1136-1137 [“when recovering damages in a default judgment, the plaintiff is limited to the damages specified in the complaint”].)  Thus, Cross-Complainants have filed the concurrent motion for leave to amend the cross-complaint and this motion to extend the five-year deadline.

 

Because default was entered against JCL before the five-year deadline expired and because it is impossible to bring the action to trial while the only remaining cross-defendant is in default, the Court agrees that the five-year deadline was tolled as of September 26, 2023. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Court grants Cross-Complainants’ motion and deems the five-year deadline tolled as of September 26, 2023, when default was entered against JCL. 

 

Cross-Complainants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.

BACKGROUND

 

On May 14, 2018, Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant James Lee doing business as JCL Contractors (“Plaintiff” or “Lee”) brought suit against Defendants and Cross-Complainants Emaar LA Properties, LLC (“Emaar”) and EJL Homes Realty & Construction, Inc. (“EJL”) (together, “Cross-Complainants”), stemming from a construction dispute.  On September 4, 2018, Cross-Complainants filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff. 

 

On May 4, 2023, the parties filed a stipulation to extend the 5-year deadline to May 14, 2024.  The parties settled Plaintiff’s claims and the original complaint was dismissed on August 9, 2023.  Pursuant to the parties’ stipulation, the First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) was filed on August 8, 2023, naming Lee and JCL Contractors, Inc. (“JCL”).  Lee was dismissed from the cross-complaint on August 17, 2023.  Default was entered against JCL on September 26, 2023.

 

From September 26, 2023, when default was entered against JCL until early February 2024, Cross-Complainants were in discussions and document exchanges with AIG, JCL’s insurance provider, in hopes that AIG would defend JCL and/or intervene in the lawsuit.  On February 2, 2024, AIG advised it would not defend JCL or intervene.  (Ralls Decl. ¶ 12.)

 

            On March 29, 2024, Cross-Complainants moved for default judgment against JCL, which the Court is unable to grant because the specific dollar amounts are not alleged in the FACC. 

 

Cross-complainants now move for leave to amend the cross-complaint to specify the amount of damages sought and to clarify that Cross-Defendant Lee has since been dismissed from the cross-action.  The motion is unopposed. 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Amendments to Pleadings: General Provisions

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

            To wit, without notice to the other party the Court has wide discretion to allow either party (i) to add or strike the name of a party or (ii) to correct a mistake in the name of a party or a mistake in any other respect “in furtherance of justice” and “on any terms as may be proper.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see also Marriage of Liss (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1429.) Alternatively, after notice to the other party, the Court has wide discretion to allow either party to amend pleadings “upon any terms as may be just.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1). Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure section 576 states “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”

 

            Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, leave to amend is liberally granted. (Berman vs. Bromberg (1986) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945; Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489 [this has been an established policy in California since 1901] (citing Frost v. Whitter (1901) 132 Cal. 421, 424; Thomas v. Bruza (1957) 151 Cal.App.2d 150, 155).) The Court of Appeal in Morgan v. Superior Court held “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530, citations omitted.) Moreover, “it is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [opposing party did not establish harm by the delay in moving to amend the complaint].)

 

            “The court may grant leave to amend the pleadings at any stage of the action.” (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 6:636 (hereafter Weil & Brown).) Denial of a motion to amend is rarely justified if the motion is timely made and granting the motion will not prejudice the opposing party. (Id. at ¶ 6:639, citations omitted.) However, if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. (Id. at ¶ 6:655, citations omitted. Absent prejudice, any claimed delay alone is not grounds for denial. “If the delay in seeking the amendment has not misled or prejudiced the other side, the liberal policy of allowing amendments prevails. Indeed, it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave in such a case even if sought as late as the time of trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:653 (citing Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565).) “Prejudice exists where the amendment would result in a delay of trial, along with loss of critical evidence, added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc. . . . But the fact that the amendment involves a change in legal theory which would make admissible evidence damaging to the opposing party is not the kind of prejudice the court will consider.” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:656, citations omitted.)

 

            “Even if some prejudice is shown, the judge may still permit the amendment but impose conditions, as the Court is authorized to grant leave ‘on such terms as may be proper.’” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:663, citation omitted.) For example, the court may cause the party seeking the amendment to pay the costs and fees incurred in preparing for trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:664 (citing Fuller v. Vista Del Arroyo Hotel, 42 Cal.App.2d 400, 404).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324: Procedural Requirements

 

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

 

“(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleadings, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

(2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”

 

            In addition, under Rule 3.1324(b), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must be accompanied by a separate declaration that specifies the following:

 

“(1) the effect of the amendment;

(2) why the amendment is necessary and proper;

(3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

(4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”

 

DISCUSSION

 

            In support of the motion, Plaintiff has provided the Declaration of Cynthia D. Ralls, attached to which is a clean copy of the proposed Second Amended Cross-Complaint, as well as a red-lined version showing the changes and a separate list of all changes.  The Ralls Declaration further indicates that Cross-Complainants discovered JCL was a necessary party through discovery responses, and the parties stipulated to amend the original Cross-Complaint to add JCL on or about July 28, 2023.  (Ralls Decl. ¶¶ 9-10.)  The First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) was filed on August 8, 2023 and served on August 14.  (Ralls Decl. ¶¶ 10-11.)  Default was entered against JCL on September 26, 2023.  (Ralls Decl. ¶ 11.)  After it became clear on February 2, 2024 that JCL’s insurer, AIG would not be defending JCL or intervening in the action, Cross-Complainants filed a request for default judgment against JCL on March 29, 2024.  (Ralls Decl. ¶¶ 12-13.)  The Court could not enter default judgment against JCL because the specific amounts requested were not alleged in the FACC.  (Ralls Decl. ¶ 13; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 580, subd. (a) [“The relief granted to the plaintiff, if there is no answer, cannot exceed that demanded in the complaint”]; Levine v. Smith (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 1131, 1136-1137 [“when recovering damages in a default judgment, the plaintiff is limited to the damages specified in the complaint”].) 

 

            Therefore, the Motion satisfies the procedural requirements.  Substantively, the Court agrees that the Motion is necessary and proper so to apprise JCL of the damages being sought against it, as due process requires before default judgment can be entered.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Finding the motion both procedurally and substantively proper, the Court grants Cross-Complainants’ motion for leave to amend the Cross-Complaint.  Cross-Complainants shall file and serve the proposed Second Amended Cross-Complaint on or before June 10, 2024.

 

            Further, the Court continues the Order to Show Cause re: Entry of Default Judgment from May 15, 2024 to August 28, 2024 at 8:30 A.M. in Department 207.  Cross-Complainants shall file the request for entry of default and default judgment regarding the amended cross-complaint on or before July 31, 2024.  If default judgment is entered before August 28, 2024, no appearance will be necessary at the Order to Show Cause hearing.

 

Cross-Complainants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.
