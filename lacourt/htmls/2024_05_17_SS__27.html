DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV17922    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY

Hearing Date: 5/17/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV17922 LUCY NAZARIAN, et al. vs COSTCO WHOLESALE

Moving Party: Defendant Costco 

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARYJUDGMENT IS DENIED 

 

Background

 

On May 11, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present case against Defendant Costco for premises liability and general negligence. Plaintiff alleges that on July 16, 2018, at 5 p.m., Plaintiff tripped and fell on a sticky substance on the floor of Costco’s produce section. Costco now moves the Court for summary judgment, arguing that: (1) the alleged condition causing Plaintiff to fall was open and obvious, (2) Defendant did not have constructive notice of the alleged condition, and (3) Plaintiff cannot prove the existence of an alleged dangerous condition.

 

Legal Standard¿¿ 

¿¿ 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite its allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve its dispute.” Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843. “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A “defendant moving for summary judgment must show the plaintiff’s causes of action have¿no merit.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849, emphasis added.) In other words, summary judgment is not to be granted simply because a case is “weak” or because a “weak” showing was made in opposition. (Hagen v. Hickenbottom¿(1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 168, 187-188, superseded by statute on another point as recognized in¿Rice v. Clark¿(2002) 28 Cal.4th 89, 96;¿Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc.¿(2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 589, 722. [“to avoid summary judgment a showing need not be strong; it need only be sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact.”].)¿¿ 

¿ 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519. A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). The elements of negligence are (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.)¿ 

¿ 

Defendant has two methods to shift the burden of proof. In addition to the standard method of presenting evidence that negates an essential element, the defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. (Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 590.)¿“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).¿¿ 

 

Analysis

 

Open and Obvious Dangerous Condition

 

The elements of a premises liability and negligence cause of action are the same: duty, breach, causation and damages. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “The owner of premises is under a duty to exercise ordinary care in the management of such premises in order to avoid exposing persons to an unreasonable risk of harm. A failure to fulfill this duty is negligence.” (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619; Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)

 

“Generally, if a danger is so obvious that a person could reasonably be expected to see it, the condition itself serves as a warning, and the landowner is under no further duty to remedy or warn of the condition.” (Krongos v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 387, 393.) 

 

However, this is not always the case. (Ibid.) “[T]he obviousness of a condition does not necessarily excuse the potential duty of a landowner, not simply to warn of the condition but to rectify it.” (Martinez v. Chippewa Enterprises, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1179, 1184.) The obviousness of a danger “may obviate the duty to warn of its existence,” but if “if it is foreseeable that the danger may cause injury despite the fact that it is obvious (e.g, when necessity requires persons to encounter it), there may be a duty to remedy the danger, and the breach of that duty may in turn form the basis for liability . . . .” (Osborn v. Mission Ready Mix (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 104, 122.)  Thus, "it is foreseeable that even an obvious danger may cause injury, if the practical necessity of encountering the danger, when weighed against the apparent risk involved, is such that under the circumstances, a person might choose to encounter the danger." (Id. at 121.)

 

Defendant’s undisputed material fact relating to the open and obvious dangerous condition states that Plaintiff was not watching where she was going at the time of the incident. (UMF Nos. 14 and 19.) After Plaintiff fell, she saw a "mess on the ground" (UMF No. 15), which was very sticky and a dark color. (UMF No. 16.)  Defendant only analyzed whether or not the condition was open and obvious but failed to present further evidence to negate the foreseeability that the alleged condition might cause injury.

 

Defendant failed to analyze foreseeability in the context identified in Osborn. Although Defendant made a brief argument regarding foreseeability, it rehashes the same argument that Plaintiff made in relation to “open and obvious: Plaintiff was not looking where she was walking and did not observe the substance on the floor (Motion at pg. 7).  But that does not address the foreseeability issue that the court envisioned in Osborn. Specifically, it does not address the necessity of encountering the danger and the foreseeability of injury.  Thus, Defendant’s incomplete analysis has failed to meet its initial burden in showing that the alleged condition was open and obvious.  In addition, based on the photos provided, it simply is not clear to the Court that the alleged cause of the fall – some sort of sticky substance on the floor – was, in fact, open and obvious.

Causation

Defendant argues that the alleged condition that caused Plaintiff to fall did not exist, and therefore Defendant cannot prove causation or notice. Defendant introduces the declaration of David Martinez, a Costco employee who inspected the area where Plaintiff had fallen shortly after the fall. Martinez did not observe any water or other substances on the ground, nor did he observe any skid marks or other signs of a fall. (Declaration of David Martinez, ¶ 3.) On this basis, Defendant has met its initial burden.

Plaintiff produced the declaration of Jack Piandaryan, who declares that the area where Plaintiff fell was dirty and that Plaintiff tripped on a dirty substance on the floor, causing her to fall. Piandaryan does not, however, indicate that he saw the fall.  (Declaration of Jack Piandaryan, ¶¶ 4-6.) He also took photographs of the scene immediately after the incident; those photographs show skid marks in contradiction to Martinez's declaration and the presence of a possible dark substance. (Exhibit A to Declaration of Jack Piandaryan.) While Defendant challenges the validity of those photos, that is a credibility call for the trier of fact.  In any event, the photographs appear to conform with Plaintiff’s testimony that after she fell, she observed that the ground was sticky and dark. (Deposition of Lucy Nazarian 26:9-16.) Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact as to the existence of the alleged condition causing her to trip and fall.

Constructive Notice

As to constructive notice, the applicable rule as to constructive notice is whether "[t]he defective condition existed long enough so that it would have been discovered by an owner who exercised reasonable care." (Bridgman, supra, 53 Cal.2d at p. 447.)

Defendant, in fulfilling its initial burden, needs to show how long the condition existed and if, by exercising reasonable care, it would have discovered the condition.

Factually Devoid Discovery Reponses

Although Defendant can meet its initial burden by introducing Plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses, Defendant must demonstrate: (1) Plaintiff’s discovery responses fail to set forth facts and identify evidence supporting Plaintiff’s allegations; and (2) the court can reasonably infer from the proffered discovery and responses that Plaintiff is unable to produce any additional evidence. (Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 102-103.) Plaintiff must have been afforded adequate opportunity for discovery (Union Bank v. Sup.Ct. (Demetry) (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590) or have declared no further information following extensive discovery. (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1441-1442.) Defendant does not satisfy its burden of proof by producing discovery responses that do not exclude the possibility that Plaintiff may possess or may reasonably obtain evidence sufficient to establish its claim. (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1441-1442.)

Plaintiff responded "unknown at this time" when asked to specify any and all facts in support of Plaintiff’s contention that Defendant Costco knew of the “dangerous condition” or should have known of the existence of the dangerous condition.  However, these responses were made in September 2020, just months after the complaint was filed.  This response at that time does not exclude the possibility that Plaintiff may reasonably obtain evidence sufficient to establish its claim. Thus, Defendant failed to meet its initial burden through the route of Plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses.

Affirmative Evidence

Defendant produced affirmative evidence in the form of a declaration of Costco employee Juan Del Solar, whose duties included walking around all the areas of the store that are open to the public, observing all conditions that exist therein to clean and remove any potentially hazardous conditions. Upon completion of his floor walks, Del Solar would fill out the bottom part of the “Daily Floor Walk / Safety Inspection” sheet by putting the time of the beginning and end of his floor walk and printing his name. During his floor walks, Del Solar would walk the entirety of the areas of the store open to the public and ensure he did not observe any dangerous conditions. If Del Solar encountered a foreign substance, spill, debris, dangerous, or unsafe condition during one of his floor walks, he would either clean it himself or immediately and promptly call for another Costco Warehouse employee to clean it up. Further, he would mark it in the “Action Log” section. On the date of the incident, Del Solar performed a floor walk from 4:15 to 4:51 p.m. Had Mr. Del Solar seen any dangerous conditions such as that alleged by Plaintiff, he would have taken action. Further, he would have noted such condition and remedial action in the “Action Log” section of the Floor Walk Inspection Sheet. He did not note any such action in the “Action Log” section of the Floor Walk Inspection Sheet. (Del Solar Decl., ¶¶ 4-5 and Ex. A1 thereto (Ex. A1 filed separately under seal pursuant to Stipulated Protective Order).)

Del Solar walked around the store from 4:15 pm to 4:51 pm on the date of the incident and did not observe any dangerous condition. It is unclear from Del Solar’s declaration when he inspected the produce area where Plaintiff allegedly fell. Assuming Del Solar did a thorough inspection, the alleged condition could not have existed before 4:15 pm, but might have appeared after he inspected the produce area, putting the existence of the alleged condition anywhere from 1 to 45 minutes before Plaintiff’s fall at 5:00 pm.

"It is ordinarily a question of fact for the jury whether, under all the circumstances, the defective condition existed long enough so that it would have been discovered by an owner who exercised reasonable care." (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1209 [Sapp v. W.T. Grant Co.,172 Cal.App.2d 89, 91-92 [liability found when patron stepped on a spool of thread and there  had been no inspection for a period of 20 minutes]; Hale v. Safeway Stores, Inc., 129 Cal.App.2d 124, 128 [non-suit improper when customer slipped on banana and no inspection for a period of "12, 15, or 30 or more minutes"]; Louie v. Hagstrom's Food Stores, Inc., 81 Cal.App.2d 601, 607-609 [liability found when patron slipped in pool of syrup and no inspection for between 15 to 25 minutes]]). Moreover, “a person operating a fruit and vegetable section in a store should at least, as to that portion of the premises, in the exercise of ordinary care, maintain a more vigilant outlook than would be required in the operation of some other type of business where the danger of things falling on the floor upon which a person might easily slip and fall is not so obvious.” (Hale, 129 Cal.App.2d at 132.)

It is uncertain when Del Solar inspected the produce area where the alleged incident occurred and therefore the alleged dangerous condition could have been in existence anytime from 1 minute to 45 minutes before Plaintiff allegedly fell. In light of Sapp and other cases finding liability for a period of non-inspection between 10 to 30 minutes, plus the aggravating factor that the alleged incident occurred in the chilled room where the berries were located (Deposition of Lucy Nazarian, Vol. 2, at 75:18- 76:6), the Court finds whether Costco exercised reasonable care and should have constructive notice of the alleged condition is a question for the jury to decide.

Conclusion

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

MOTIONS TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/17/24

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV00320 JOSE MARISCAL vs SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA EDISON

Moving Party: Defendant Southern California Edison

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTIONS TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED.

 

Legal Standard

 

A defendant may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (a).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

 

Discussion

On January 4, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present Complaint against Defendant Southern California Edison. On August 25, 2023, Edison served its supplemental discovery requests on Plaintiff, including a Supplemental Request for Interrogatories and Supplemental Requests for Production of Documents. Plaintiff failed to provide any supplemental response by the statutory deadline and failed to respond to Defendant’s meet and confer attempts. Defendant now moves the Court to compel supplemental responses to interrogatories and requests for production.

Plaintiff did not provide the discovery responses at issue by the statutory deadline. Plaintiff did not file an opposition or any other document indicating that the responses were served prior to the hearing. Thus, Defendant’s motions to compel initial responses are granted. Plaintiff is ordered to provide complete responses to Defendant’s supplemental request for interrogatory and supplemental request for production without objection within 20 days of today.

Plaintiff’s non-response forced Defendant to file the present motions and incur attorneys’ fees. Defendant requests attorney fees in the amount of $540 per motion, which the Court finds reasonable. Consequently, the Court sanctions Plaintiff in the amount of $1,080. Plaintiff and his counsel are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1,080 to Defendant within 20 days of today’s date.

Case Number: 22STCV03418    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT¿AND SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 5/14/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03418 IGNACIO CHAVEZ, et al. vs JASON WAGNER

Moving Party: Defendants Samantha Wagner and Jason Wagner

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION OF THE CLAIMS AGAINST SAMANTHA WAGNER IS GRANTED; 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION OF THE CLAIMS AGAINST JASON WAGNER IS DENIED.

 

Background

 

On January 27, 2022, Plaintiff filed this automobile accident case against Defendants Jason Wagner (“Defendant”) and Samantha Wagner, alleging that Defendants rear-ended Plaintiff's car. Defendants now move for summary judgment or summary adjudication, arguing that Jason Wagner was suddenly stricken by an unforeseen illness and fell unconscious, thereby causing the accident. Additionally, Defendants assert that Samantha Wagner has no relation to this matter, as she is not the owner of the vehicle, was not in the vehicle at the time of the accident and was not involved in the accident in any way.

 

Evidentiary Ruling

 

The Parties did not make any objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).) The court GRANTS the Parties’ requests for judicial notice.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). "When the motion for summary judgment is supported by affidavits sufficient to sustain the motion,…the burden of showing that triable issues exist shifts to the party opposing the motion. [Citation.] A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)¿ 

 

MSJ For Claims Against Samantha Wagner

 

Defendant Samantha Wagner has met her initial burden by demonstrating that she is not the owner of the vehicle involved in the accident, her name is not on the vehicle’s certificate, she was not driving the vehicle at the time of the incident, nor was she involved with the accident in any capacity. (Declaration of Samantha Wagner ¶ 2; Declaration of Jason Wagner ¶¶ 2, 11-14, Def’s Exhibit A.) Plaintiff does not dispute this in his separate statement or opposition, and he has failed to introduce any evidence to raise a triable issue of fact. Therefore, summary judgment of Plaintiff's claims against Defendant Samantha Wagner is GRANTED.

 

MSJ For Claims against Jason Wagner

 

Cohen Rule/Sudden Illness

 

“‘California has approved the rule of Cohen v. Petty (D.C. Cir. 1993) 65 F.2d 820, 821: as between an innocent passenger and an innocent fainting driver, the former must suffer.’  [Citation.]”  (Bashi v. Wodarz (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1314, 1318.)  Generally, one who is suddenly stricken by an illness, which he had no reason to anticipate, while driving an automobile, which renders it impossible for him to control the car, is not chargeable with negligence.  (Id. at 1319; see, e.g., Hammontree v. Jenner (1971) 20 Cal.App.3d 528, 530-531 [loss of conscioiusness due to unexpected epileptic seizure]; Tannyhill v. Pacific Motor Transport Co. (1964) 227 Cal.App.2d 512, 520 [heart attack]; Zabunoff v. Walker (1961) 192 Cal.App.2d 8, 11 [jurors could have concluded that a sudden sneeze was an intervening cause similar to a fainting spell]; Ford v. Carew & English (1948) 89 cal.App.2d 199, 203 [fainting spell from strained heart muscle]; Waters v. Pacific Coast Dairy, Inc. (1942) 55 Cal.App.2d 789, 793 [driver rendered unconscious from sharp pain in left arm].)

 

The “basis behind the Cohen rule is related to the general concept of ‘unavoidable accident’ . . . [which] is predicated upon absence of fault.” (Bashi v. Wodarz (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1314, 1319.) “The conduct of an actor during a period of a sudden incapacitation or loss of consciousness resulting from physical illness is negligent only if the sudden incapacitation or loss of consciousness was reasonably foreseeable to the actor.” (Rest.3d Torts, § 11.)

 

In Waters, the defendant testified he became unconscious due to sharp pain in his arm.  However, the jury found in favor of the plaintiffs. The appellate court affirmed the determination of the jury because the defendant failed to present evidence showing how long he suffered from the cause of the loss of consciousness and whether he should have had reason to anticipate such an attack at said time and place. (Id. at p. 793.)

 

In Hammontree, the defendant suffered from epilepsy, which was controlled by medication.  The defendant’s doctor advised the DMV in writing, once per year, of the defendant’s condition and testified he believed it was safe for the defendant to drive. (Hammontree, supra, 20 Cal.App.3d at pp. 530-532.) The court found that Defendant had no reason to anticipate an attack while driving as there was no inkling or warning that he was about to have a seizure prior to the occurrence of the accident. (Ibid.)

 

Analysis

 

        On March 4, 2021, at 10:30 AM, Defendant drove to Cal State Northridge for his COVID-19 vaccine appointment. After receiving the vaccine, he waited 15 minutes as instructed before leaving and was told to return if he felt any symptoms. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 10.) During the 15-minute waiting period, Defendant did not experience any symptoms. He reported, “As I started to drive, I did not have any symptoms and generally felt fine.” (Wagner Decl. ¶¶ 11-12.)

Five minutes after leaving Cal State Northridge, Defendant began to feel lightheaded. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 13.) He decided to turn around and return to the vaccination site as advised. (Id.) After passing through the intersection of Nordhoff and Balboa, he turned around to head westbound on Nordhoff back to Cal State Northridge. (Id.) He stopped completely behind Plaintiff’s vehicle at the intersection of Nordhoff and Balboa due to a red light. (Id.) Shortly thereafter, Defendant suddenly and unexpectedly lost consciousness and collided with Plaintiff’s car. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 14.) Defendant also provided a declaration from his expert witness doctor who testified that the cause of Defendant’s loss of consciousness is unknown. (Declaration of Barry Ludwig.)

Summary judgment is a drastic remedy to be used sparingly, and any doubts about its propriety must be resolved in favor of the opposing party. (Castaneda v. Ensign Group, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1019.) Here, summary judgment as to Defendant is not appropriate because triable issues of fact exist.  For instance, Defendant failed to show how long he felt lightheaded before the accident and whether he had reason to anticipate fainting. Although Defendant produced evidence indicating he had no prior medical conditions and had no reaction within the 15-minute waiting period after taking the vaccine, and therefore he had no reason to anticipate feeling lightheaded or fainting in general, Defendant failed to produce evidence showing that fainting was not reasonably foreseeable at the "said time and place" once he started to feel lightheaded. This case is unlike Hammontree where there was no inkling or warning that he was about to have a seizure prior to the occurrence of the accident.

Additionally, it appears appropriate for a trier of fact to determine whether Defendant can be found free of fault or if the accident was truly unavoidable. In particular, Defendant failed to stop and pull over when he began feeling lightheaded, just minutes after leaving the vaccination site. Although the clinic advised him to return if he felt unwell, his lightheadedness or dizziness, directly impacted his ability to drive safely. Reasonable minds could differ on whether Defendant acted reasonably under the circumstances. Additionally, reasonable minds could also differ on whether his fainting was reasonably foreseeable from his earlier symptoms of lightheadedness, especially considering that he had received his first COVID-19 vaccine only 20 minutes prior and was informed to expect potential side effects. Thus, Defendant has failed to meet his initial burden and summary adjudication of the claims against Jason Wagner is denied.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT 

Hearing Date: 5/17/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV30442 MEGAN NICHOLE WIGGINS vs PENNY BRENNER 

Moving Party: Defendants Penny and Mark Brenner

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT IS GRANTED

 

As judicial policy generally supports the resolution of all disputed matters between parties, leave to amend or leave to cross-complain is typically granted liberally. (See Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 412.) The Court may, however, deny such leave if there is demonstrated prejudice to the opposing party, such as trial delays, loss of critical evidence, or increased preparation costs. (Id.) 

On December 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motor accident claim arising from a six-vehicle collision. Defendants Penny Brenner and Mark Brenner move the Court for leave to file a cross-complaint against Defendant Ryan Kessler, alleging he was the primary cause of the accident. No opposition was filed.

Defendants' request comes shortly after the complaint was filed, so there would be minimal trial delay, loss of evidence, or increased preparation costs. The cross-complaint arises from the same transaction or occurrence as the original complaint, and no opposition alleging prejudice was filed. Defendants also attached a copy of the cross-complaint to the motion. All statutory requirement has been met and therefore, Defendants' motion for leave is granted. Defendants are granted leave to file their cross-complaint within the next 10 days.
