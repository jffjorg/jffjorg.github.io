DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV42444    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 61

Plaintiff Gergis Ghobrial’s Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.

Defendants to give notice.

 

 

I.                   MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

 

In addition to any other sanctions permitted by law, the court may order a person, after written notice and an opportunity to be heard, to pay reasonable monetary sanctions to the court or an aggrieved person, or both, for failure without good cause to comply with the applicable rules. For the purposes of this rule, “person” means a party, a party's attorney, a witness, and an insurer or any other individual or entity whose consent is necessary for the disposition of the case. If a failure to comply with an applicable rule is the responsibility of counsel and not of the party, any penalty must be imposed on counsel and must not adversely affect the party's cause of action or defense thereto.

 

(CRC Rule 2.30, subd. (b).)

 

Plaintiff Gergis Ghobrial seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $17,450 against Defendants Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Memorial Health Services, and Desiree Thomas (Defendants) and their counsel Joshua Mino. Plaintiff identifies the basis for the motion as Defendants’ failure to include an affirmative factual showing justifying ex parte relief as required under CRC Rule 3.1202, subd. (c), in Defendants’ ex parte application to continue Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complaint, granted on January 17, 2024. (Motion at pp. 4–7.)

 

There is no cause for sanctions. The remedy for a failure to provide adequate factual support to an application for ex parte relief is to deny the application. Plaintiff had notice of the application at issue and opposed it on the very basis he now offers for sanctions. (1/16/24 Opposition at p. 3.) This court found the argument to be without merit and granted the application on January 17, 2024. Plaintiff’s argument is no more persuasive the second time.

 

Defendants argue that the court should set an OSC re: sanctions against Plaintiff on the position that the present motion is without basis and is a defective motion for reconsideration of the January 17, 2024 order on Defendants’ ex parte application. (Opposition at p. 4, citing Code Civ. Proc. § 1008, subd. (d); CRC Rule 2.30.) No such proceedings are necessary. The motion is simple in its factual and legal bases, and Defendant’s opposition memorandum is four pages. The expense and burden of further sanctions proceedings on this motion is likely to exceed the trouble it has already caused. 

 

The motion is DENIED.

 

 


Case Number: 22STCV19535    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 61

I.                   MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT

When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 473.5, subd. (a).)

 

Defendant Helio Marquez seeks relief from the default and default judgment entered respectively on October 20 and 21, 2022, on the grounds that service was not properly made against him, and he lacked notice of this litigation until September 2023. (Marquez Decl. ¶ 8.) Marquez also claims that substitute service was made on 5007 Eagle View Circle in Los Angeles, which is not his business or residence address. (Marquez Decl. ¶¶ 3–6.)

 

A defendant may serve and file a motion to quash service of summons on the grounds of a lack of jurisdiction over him or her. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10 subd. (a)(1).) A plaintiff opposing a motion to quash service for lack of personal jurisdiction “has the initial burden to demonstrate facts establishing a basis for personal jurisdiction.” (HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1167.) If satisfied, the burden then shifts to defendant to show that exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable. (Id.)

Mere notice of litigation does not confer personal jurisdiction absent substantial compliance with the statutory requirements for service of summons. (MJS Enterprises, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 555, 557.) While courts are not required to accept self-serving evidence — such as declarations that one was not served — submitted to support a motion to quash, facial defects of the proof of service will rebut its presumption of proper service. (American Exp. Centurion Bank, supra, 199 Cal.App.4th at p. 390.) The burden is on a plaintiff to prove facts showing that service was effective. (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.)

Here, Defendant has presented evidence that he lacked notice of the litigation, and that the substitute service alleged against him was not made upon his “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address,” as required by Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20, subd. (b). No opposition has been filed.

The motion to vacate default judgment and quash service of summons is therefore GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23STCV00238    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendant Stylenquaza, LLC’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs to provide notice.

 

I.                   OBJECTIONS

Defendant Stylenquaza, LLC’s objections to the declaration of Raphael Metzger are SUSTAINED, as the paragraphs objected to consist of legal argument, hearsay, or facts not within Metzger’s personal knowledge concerning artificial stone and whether Defendant operates the website at issue.

II.                MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE 

Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10, subd. (a)(1) states: “A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes . . . (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”

Mere notice of litigation does not confer personal jurisdiction absent substantial compliance with the statutory requirements for service of summons. (MJS Enterprises, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 555, 557.)

While courts are not required to accept self-serving evidence — such as declarations that one was not served — submitted to support a motion to quash, facial defects of the proof of service will rebut its presumption of proper service. (American Exp. Centurion Bank, supra, 199 Cal.App.4th at p. 390.) The burden is on a plaintiff to prove facts showing that service was effective. (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.)

Defendant Stylenquaza, LLC (Defendant) moves to quash service of summons upon it on the grounds that it lacks minimum contacts with California such that the state may exercise jurisdiction over it.

A defendant may serve and file a motion to quash service of summons on the grounds of a lack of jurisdiction over him or her. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10 subd. (a)(1).) A plaintiff opposing a motion to quash service for lack of personal jurisdiction “has the initial burden to demonstrate facts establishing a basis for personal jurisdiction.” (HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1167.) If satisfied, the burden then shifts to defendant to show that exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable. (Id.)

A court may exercise jurisdiction on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of California or of the United States. (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.) The exercise of personal jurisdiction is constitutionally permissible only “if the defendant has sufficient ‘minimum contacts’ with the forum state so that the exercise of jurisdiction ‘does not offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” [Citations.]’ ” (HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (“HealthMarkets, Inc.”) (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1166 [citing International Shoe Co. v. Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316].) A defendant’s conduct with the forum state must be such that the defendant has “fair warning” that its activities might subject it to personal jurisdiction. (HealthMarkets, Inc., supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at p. 1166–1167; Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 472.)

“Minimum contacts exist where the defendant's conduct in the forum state is such that he should reasonably anticipate being subject to suit there, and it is reasonable and fair to force him to do so. [Citations.] In contrast, contacts that are random, fortuitous, or attenuated do not rise to the minimum level, and general jurisdiction cannot be exercised under these circumstances.” (F. Hoffman-La Roche, Inc. v. Superior Court (“F. Hoffman-La Roche”) (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.)

A defendant that has substantial, continuous, and systematic contacts with the forum state is subject to general jurisdiction in the state, meaning jurisdiction on any cause of action. Absent such extensive contacts, a defendant may be subject to specific jurisdiction, meaning jurisdiction in an action arising out of or related to the defendant's contacts with the forum state. Specific jurisdiction depends on the quality and nature of the defendant's forum contacts in relation to the particular cause of action alleged.

 

(HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1167.)

 

In determining whether specific jurisdiction exists over a given defendant, “the appropriate inquiry is whether the plaintiff's cause of action “arises out of or has a substantial connection with a business relationship defendant has purposefully established with California.” (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 448.) “[A]s the relationship of the defendant with the state seeking to exercise jurisdiction over him grows more tenuous, the scope of jurisdiction also retracts, and fairness is assured by limiting the circumstances under which the plaintiff can compel him to appear and defend. The crucial inquiry concerns the character of defendant's activity in the forum, whether the cause of action arises out of or has a substantial connection with that activity, and upon the balancing of the convenience of the parties and the interests of the state in assuming jurisdiction.” (Ibid.)

 

It is the plaintiff’s initial burden to establish the factual bases justifying the exercise of jurisdiction. (ViaView v. Retzlaff (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 198, 209–10.) “Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, however, it becomes the defendant's burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.” (Vons Companies, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.4th at p. 449.)

 

Defendant presents the declaration of An Nguyen, its manager and co-owner, who testifies that Defendant is a Texas company with its principal place of business in Dallas. (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant is a distributor of quartz slabs for Vicostone JSC, a Vietnamese company, and distributes its products through Texas, Oklahoma, Kansas, Nebraska, South Dakota, North Dakota, Minnesota, Iowa, Missouri, Arkansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, Tennessee, Kentucky, Illinois, Indiana, Wisconsin, Michigan, West Virginia, North Carolina, South Carolina, and Georgia. (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendant’s distribution area does not include the west coast or California, which has another distributor — Pental Granite and Marble, LLC — employed by Vicostone JSC. (Nguyen Decl. ¶¶ 5–6.) Defendant’s only engagement with Pental’s west coast network is during emergency shortages, in which Pental purchases quartz slabs from Defendant through a third party truck company, over which Defendant lacks control. (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 7.) Between 2018 and 2022, Defendant supplied six shipments of stone slabs, amounting to a total of 22 slabs, to California, representing 0.001 percent of its product distribution. (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 8.) On rare occasions Defendant has also distributed “insubstantial amounts” of pre-fabricated tops to California, which are ready for installation and do not require further fabrication. (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 8.) Defendant does not otherwise do business or advertise in California. (Nguyen Decl. ¶¶ 10–11.) It is Vicostone JSC, not Defendant, that operates the website on us.vicostone.com that Plaintiffs Demetrio Luna Reyes and Guillemina Valdez (Plaintiffs) cite in their First Amended Complaint (FAC). (Nguyen Decl. ¶ 21; FAC ¶¶ 317–321.)

 

Plaintiffs in opposition note that Defendant does business in the United States under the name Vicostone USA. (Opposition at p. 8.) Plaintiffs also argue that the Vicostone website lists several “regional distributors for Vicostone product in California. (Opposition at p. 6.) Plaintiff notes that the website “contains pages headed ‘Vicostone USA.’” (Opposition at p. 8.) Plaintiff further cites distribution contracts between Defendant and Vicostone JSC, which state that Defendant is an entity operating “under the laws of the USA” and agrees “to distribute the Product in the USA.” (Opposition at p. 8.)

 

Plaintiffs have not shown that Defendant purposefully availed itself of the benefits of doing business in California. “Purposeful availment” requires contacts that are other than “random, fortuitous, or attenuated,” or derived from the “unilateral activity of another party or third person.” (Malone v. Equitas Reinsurance Ltd. (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1430, 1437.) None but attenuated contacts have been shown here. Plaintiff’s reliance on the website is rebutted by Defendant’s showing that the website is controlled by a different entity, and that the California regional distributors identified there are affiliated with a different entity. The sole evidence of Defendant’s contacts with California having anything to do with the allegations of the FAC consists of Nguyen’s acknowledgement that Defendant has distributed 22 stone slabs to California, a region it does not ordinarily serve, and which quantity represents a fraction of a fraction of its ordinary distribution. Purposeful availment has not been shown.

Plaintiffs have not met their burden to show that further jurisdictional discovery should be allowed. “In order to prevail on a motion for a continuance for jurisdictional discovery, the plaintiff should demonstrate that discovery is likely to lead to the production of evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction.” (In re Automobile Antitrust Cases I & II (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 100, 127.) Plaintiffs contend that they have served jurisdictional discovery, but have received no responses. (Opposition at pp. 13–14.) This appears to be false, however, as Plaintiffs served this discovery on December 13, 2023, and Defendant served responses on January 17, 2024, weeks before Plaintiffs filed their opposition to this motion. (Reply Hale Decl. ¶¶ 3–4, Exh. 3.) Plaintiff attempts to argue that more discovery is needed because of the purportedly “perjurious” testimony submitted by Defendant in successfully challenging jurisdiction in another toxic torts case in this county, in which Nguyen’s declaration contained a statement omitted from his declaration in the present case: that “neither Pental nor the [third party] truck company inform Stylenquaza of where the products are sent ]and that] Stylenquaza has no knowledge . . . where the products that Pental purchased from Stylenquaza are distributed.” (Opposition at p. 13; Metzger Decl. Exh. E.) The court does not find the omission of this statement indicative of perjury, or that further discovery would likely turn up proof establishing jurisdiction. As Defendant stated in discovery responses previously given to Plaintiffs, it has become aware that 11 stone slabs purchased by Pental have gone to California. (Reply Hale Decl. Exh. 3, No. 4.) Such contact, resulting from the actions of a third party distributor, does not meaningfully contribute to any showing of purposeful availment.

The motion to quash is therefore GRANTED.
