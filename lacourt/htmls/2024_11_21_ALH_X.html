DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 23AHCV01335    Hearing Date: November 21, 2024    Dept: X


DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE A CROSS-COMPLAINT

Defendants Barber and Total Access Elevator Corporation seek leave to file a cross-complaint for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief against Vibhay Prasad, M.D., The Regents of the University of California, and Los Robles Regional Medical Center.

Plaintiffs Yvonne Hatherill, Mikayla Hatherill, and Morgan Hatherill filed a motor vehicle negligence and wrongful death complaint, as well as a professional negligence complaint, alleging that due to a six-vehicle automobile collision in December 2021, their late husband and father, Mark Andrew Hatherill, underwent lumbar spine surgery and later suffered complications that led to his death.  The Hatherhill family sued various defendants including medical providers and other drivers, alleging professional negligence, wrongful death, and negligent infliction of emotional distress.  The defendants in the case have also filed cross-complaints against each other.

Tentative Ruling

Defendants’ motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is GRANTED.  Defendant is ordered to file the proposed cross-complaint within 10 days.

Legal Authority

Code of Civil Procedure section 428.10 provides that a party against whom a cause of action has been asserted may file a cross-complaint setting forth:  “(b) Any cause of action he has against a person alleged to be liable thereon, whether or not such person is already a party to the action, if the cause of action asserted in his cross-complaint (1) arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause brought against him or (2) asserts a claim, right, or interest in the property or controversy which is the subject of the cause brought against him.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10(b).)  A party shall obtain leave of court to file a cross-complaint if it is not concurrently filed with the answer or at any time before the court sets a trial date.  Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10(c).)
 
If a cross-complaint is compulsory, leave must be granted as long as the cross-complainant is acting in good faith, so as to avoid forfeiture of the causes of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., §426.50; See Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 101 [concluding that the late filing of the motion to file a compulsory cross-complaint absent some evidence of bad faith is insufficient evidence to support denial of the motion].)  To be considered a compulsory cross-complaint, the related cause of action must have existed at the time defendant served its answer to the complaint.  (Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide (2008), Civil Procedure Before Trial §6:516; see also Crocker Nat’l Bank v. Emerald (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 852, 864.)  If the cross-complaint is not compulsory, but rather is permissive, the Court has sole discretion whether to grant or deny leave.  (Id.)

Analysis

The Court notes Defendants’ motion for leave to amend does not strictly comply with the rules because the proposed cross-complaint was not attached to the motion papers, but rather was filed as an exhibit to defense counsel’s declaration in Defendants’ reply papers.  The Court exercises discretion in considering the proposed cross-complaint from the reply papers rather than the moving papers. 

In considering the motion on its merits, Defendants seek leave to file a cross-complaint for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief against Vibhay Prasad, M.D., The Regents of the University of California, and Los Robles Regional Medical Center.  The motion argues that the cross-complaint arises out of the same transaction or occurrence as Plaintiffs’ claims, and granting leave to file the cross-complaint would avoid a multiplicity of suits and save time and resources. 

There are three oppositions, filed by the three proposed cross-defendants.  Their argument in common is that the cross-complaint would be permissive and not compulsory.  The Court agrees that the proposed cross-complaint would be permissive as the cross-complaint is argued to be based on the motor vehicle accident underlying this case.  However, the oppositions have not shown that an opposing party would be prejudiced by the filing of a cross-complaint at this stage.  There is also no trial date currently set.  The Court finds judicial economy would be served by granting Defendants leave to assert their indemnity claims against Prasad, the Regents, and Los Robles.  (See P&D Consultants, Inc. v. City of Carlsbad (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 1332, 1345 [“Courts must apply a policy of liberality in permitting amendments at any stage of the proceeding, including during trial, when no prejudice to the opposing party is shown.”])  

Defendants’ motion for leave to file a cross-complaint is granted.  Defendants are ordered to file the proposed cross-complaint within 10 days.

Moving Party is to give notice.

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES

Plaintiff Shakeh Ataian moves for an order compelling Defendant to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set One), Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 9, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 39, 40, 41, 43, 44, and 45.  

This is a medical malpractice case arising from Plaintiff Shakeh Ataian’s upper-eyelid surgery with Defendant Richard Weise, M.D.  Plaintiff Shakeh sues for professional negligence and violation of Business and Professions Code section 654.2(a), and her husband Plaintiff Albert Ataian sues for loss of consortium.

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One) is granted in part, as to Interrogatory Nos. 9, 14, 15, 16, 17, and 18.  The motion is denied as to the remaining requests. 

Legal Authority and Analysis

Each answer in the response must be “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.  If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220, subd. (a), (b).)  “Where the question is specific and explicit, an answer which supplies only a portion of the information sought is wholly insufficient.  Likewise, a party may not provide deftly worded conclusionary answers designed to evade a series of explicit questions.”  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783.)  Discovery requests are generally afforded liberal construction. (See Code of Civ. Proc., § 2017.010; Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) 

When the responding party answers with objections, and a motion to compel is filed, the burden is on the objecting party to establish whatever facts are necessary to justify the objection.  (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221; Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

Interrogatory Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 4 ask for the percentage of surgeries that Defendant performed to treat a patient with (1) blepharoplasty, (2) conjunctivoplasty, (3) ptosis, and (4) ectropion conditions, where Defendant first performed a punctal plugs surgery.  Defendant objects that these requests seek information not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  Defendant also asserts that responding to the interrogatories is also unduly burdensome as it would require a manual review of thousands of patient treatment records.  Lastly, Defendant objects that the interrogatories seek information that is protected from disclosure under both state and federal privacy rights.  The Court determines that no further responses are required.

Interrogatory Nos. 9, 14, 15, 16, and 17, which begin with, “After YOU examined plaintiff Shakeh Ataian on March 16, 2022,” and then asks Defendant about the diagnoses, treatment plan, and surgical procedures.  Defendant’s responses response Plaintiff “to her medical records previously produced in this action, which contains information responsive to this request.”  The Court finds these responses insufficient pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230 and grants Plaintiff’s request for further responses.

Interrogatory No. 18 asks, “After YOU examined plaintiff Shakeh Ataian on March 16, 2022, if YOU did not discuss with her YOUR treatment plan, did YOU assign anyone to discuss YOU treatment plan with her?”  Defendant’s “Not applicable” response is non-responsive.  Defendant must provide a response in accord with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.220 and 2030.230.

Interrogatory Nos. 39, 40, and 41 ask about the $5,000 Plaintiff Shakeh tendered for her September 15, 2022 surgery and to where or whom the money was going.  Defendant responds, the “money is being paid to the practice for the services that are being rendered to her,” or states that the request is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, or seeks information protected by individual privacy rights.  No further responses are required.

Interrogatory Nos. 43, 44, and 45 ask if Plaintiff’s health insurance was billed, how much payment Defendant received from Plaintiff’s health insurance company, and what portion of Plaintiff’s surgery was paid by insurance and paid by her $5,000 cash payment.  Defendant “refers Plaintiff to the ledger details maintained by Glendale Eye Medical Group, Inc. and/or Foothill Surgical Center, Inc., which sets forth the information responsive to this request.”  These responses are sufficiently compliant, and no further responses are warranted. 

Moving Party is to give notice.

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT; EX PARTE APPLICATION RE: RECONSIDERATION

This lawsuit arises from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on December 17, 2021.  Plaintiff filed his Complaint on December 19, 2023.  Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s sole cause of action on the grounds that it is time-barred.

Tentative Ruling

Defendant’s Demurrer is sustained with 30 days leave to amend.  An Amended Complaint must be filed on or before December 23, 2024.

Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application is denied.

The Court sets an Order to Show Cause Hearing re: Amended Complaint for January 30, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. in Department X of the Alhambra Courthouse.  If the amended pleading is filed by that time, the matter will go off-calendar and no appearance will be necessary.

Plaintiff’s Claim Appears To Be Time-Barred; Leave To Amend Is Granted

In reviewing the legal sufficiency of a complaint against a demurrer, a court will treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions, or conclusions of law.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; C & H Foods Co. v. Hartford Ins. Co. (1984) 163 Cal.App.3d 1055, 1062.)  The Court may only consider the complaint’s allegations or matters which may be judicially noticed.  (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.) The Court may not consider any other extrinsic evidence or judge the credibility of the allegations pled or the difficulty a plaintiff may have in proving his allegations.  (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.) 

Turning to the issue of the statute of limitations, where the dates alleged in the complaint show the cause of action is time-barred, a general demurrer is appropriate.  (Vaca v. Wachovia Mortgage Corp. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 737, 746.)  The running of the statute of limitations must appear clearly and affirmatively on the face of the complaint.  (Committee for Green Foothills v. Santa Clara County Bd. of Supervisors (2010) 48 Cal.4th 32, 42.) 

Defendant demurs to the Complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff’s sole cause of action for motor vehicle negligence is time-barred under Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1 which provides a two-year statute of limitations for actions for injury due to negligence.  Defendant contends that the deadline for Plaintiff to file this action was December 17, 2023, based on the accident occurring on December 17, 2021.  Given that Plaintiff filed this action on December 19, 2023, Defendant contends Plaintiff filed past the two-year deadline.

No opposition has been filed.  Rather, Plaintiff filed an Ex Parte Application seeking more time to file an opposition to the demurrer and for reconsideration of the Court’s ruling on Defendant’s prior motion to vacate the default judgment entered against him.  

The request for additional time to prepare an opposition is denied.  Based on the Court’s review of the Complaint, Plaintiff’s cause of action is time-barred under section 335.1 of the Code of Civil Procedure.  However, the Court grants leave for Plaintiff to file an amended complaint to the extent that the issue can be corrected.

Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application is Denied; Any Request For Reconsideration Is Untimely

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008.)

The 10-day time limit for a motion for reconsideration runs from service of notice of entry of the order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a).)  This deadline is extended under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013 for service by mail, fax, electronic service, or overnight delivery, which applies “in the absence of a specific exception provided for by this section or other statute or rule of court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1013(a), (c), (e).  See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2022) ¶ 9:326.1, p. 9(1)-155 [“Because CCP § 1008 does not contain an express exception, the extensions provided by § 1013 apply to motions under § 1008.”].)

Plaintiff seeks to have the Court reconsider its ruling on the Motion to Set Aside/Vacate the Default Judgment against Defendant.  Notice of the ruling was mailed to Plaintiff on October 28, 2024.  Plaintiff had until November 12, 2024 to file a request for reconsideration.  The application was not filed until November 19, 2024, one-week too late.  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s ex parte application is denied.  (Marriage of Furie (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 816, 831 [a judge does not have jurisdiction to consider a motion that is untimely].)

Defendant is to provide notice of the rulings.
