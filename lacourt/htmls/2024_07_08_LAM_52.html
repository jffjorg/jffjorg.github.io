Case Number: 22STCV18057    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Danielle Schlichter’s Motion to Set Aside Judgment

            Plaintiff Danielle Schlichter moves to set aside the judgment and enter an amended judgment including prejudgment interest.

            Plaintiff shows good cause to correct the judgment.  Courts have inherent power to correct or amend judgments.  (Curci Investments, LLC v. Baldwin (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 214, 220; Ames v. Paley (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 668, 672.)  After the court trial, the court’s minute order dated January 10, 2024, states, “The Court awards damages in the amount of $61,440.00 plus costs as provided by law and prejudgment interest as provided by law.”  The judgment entered on February 23, 2024, does not include any prejudgment interest.  Plaintiff submitted a proposed judgment including $22,826.43 in prejudgment interest, along with a spreadsheet explaining how that amount was calculated.  (Stefan Decl., ¶ 9.)   

            Plaintiff did not, however, file proof of service of this motion on defendant The Skid Row Housing Trust.  Defendant is entitled to notice of the motion.  The court will continue the hearing on this motion to give defendant an opportunity to respond and be heard.          

            The court hereby continues the hearing on plaintiff’s motion to set aside judgment to August 9, 2024, at 9:00 a.m. 

Plaintiff Danielle Schlichter is ordered to serve the moving papers and notice of the continued hearing on defendant The Skid Row Housing Trust forthwith.  Plaintiff shall file proof of service of the papers and notice of hearing no later than July 22, 2024.


Case Number: 23STCV28538    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Magdalena Lopez’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Deposition Subpoena

Plaintiff Magdalena Lopez moves to compel non-party Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (DWP) to comply with a deposition subpoena for production of business records. 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480(a) provides, “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” 

Plaintiff properly served DWP with a deposition subpoena for production of business records.  “[I]f a deposition subpoena commands only the production of business records for copying, the custodian of the records or other qualified person shall, in person, by messenger, or by mail, deliver both of the following only to the deposition officer specified in the subpoena: (1) A true, legible, and durable copy of the records.  (2) An affidavit in compliance with Section 1561 of the Evidence Code.”  (CCP § 2020.430(a).)  Evidence Code section 1561(b) provides, “If the business has none of the records described, or only part thereof, the custodian or other qualified witness shall so state in the affidavit, and deliver the affidavit and those records that are available.”

Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena sought three categories of business records.  DWP did not produce any documents in the third category: “All DOCUMENTS RELATED to interconnection agreements for 20609 Cantlay St., Winnetka, CA 91306.”  DWP responded to the subpoena with an affidavit stating: “1. I am an employee of the DEPARTMENT OF WATER AND POWER, and one of the persons duly authorized to have custody of the original records hereinafter described.  2. I have authority to certify and do hereby certify that the attached copies of the original records are true and correct copies of the original business records kept in the regular course of business by the DEPARTMENT OF WATER AND POWER, ACCOUNT SERVICES UNIT and were prepared by the personnel of the DEPARTMENT OF WATER AND POWER ACCOUNTS SERVICES UNIT, in the ordinary course of business near or at the time of the facts as conditions or events recorded.  3. The attached copies are copies or summaries of the original records of the DEPARTMENT OF WATER AND POWER ACCOUNT SERVICES UNIT.  4. The attached certified copies of the records are submitted in response to the demand received by the CUSTODIAN OF RECORDS, DEPARTMENT OF WATER AND POWER.”  (Separate statement, p. 4.)

Because DWP did not produce documents in response to the third category, this affidavit does not comply with Evidence Code section 1561(b).  The custodian of records did not state that DWP has no records in response to the third category.  DWP must either produce responsive documents or present an affidavit stating it has no such documents.

Sanctions

            Plaintiff also moves for $2,775 in sanctions against DWP.  Plaintiff cites no authority permitting sanctions against the non-party deponent.  Assuming such authority exists, the court finds monetary sanctions are not just under the circumstances.         

Disposition

            Plaintiff Magdalena Lopez’s motion to compel compliance with deposition subpoena for production of business records is granted. 

Los Angeles Department of Water and Power is ordered to either (a) produce documents responsive to category three of plaintiff’s subpoena, along with an affidavit authenticating the documents under Evidence Code section 1561(a); or (b) provide an affidavit under Evidence Code section 1561(b) stating it has no such documents, within 20 days.

Case Number: BC635357    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: 52

Judgment Creditor FCP Brands, Inc.’s Motion for Attorney Fees

            Judgment creditor FCP Brands, Inc. (FCP) moves for $77,919 in attorney fees from judgment debtor Mimi Park aka Mi Jin Kim. 

Evidentiary Objections

            Park makes 7 objections to FCP’s evidence.  Objection No. 1 is overruled.  Objection Nos. 2-7 are sustained. 

Attorney Fees

FCP seeks fees incurred in opposing Park’s motion to compel acknowledgment of satisfaction of judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 724.050.  Code of Civil Procedure section 724.080 provides, “In an action or proceeding maintained pursuant to this chapter, the court shall award reasonable attorney’s fees to the prevailing party.”  FCP prevailed in such a proceeding.  It is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees.

In determining what constitutes a reasonable fee, the court ordinarily begins with the lodestar, that is, “the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (PLCM).)  The court may rely on its own experience and knowledge in determining the reasonable value of attorney fees.  (Id. at p. 1096; accord Taylor v. Nabors Drilling USA, LP (2014) 222 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1249.) 

Hourly Rates

            FCP’s hourly rates are reasonable.  “The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM, supra, 22 Cal.4th at p. 1095.)  The court should consider many factors, including the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the stakes involved, the skill required and employed by the attorney in handling the case, the attorney’s experience, the success or failure of the litigation, and other relevant circumstances.  (Id. at p. 1096; Contractors Labor Pool, Inc. v. Westway Contractors, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 152, 168.)

            Michael Adams charges an hourly rate of $875.  Adams is a managing partner of Rutan & Tucker, LLP.  (Adams Decl., ¶ 7.)  He graduated from Stanford Law School and has practiced law for over 25 years.  (Ibid.)  Heather Blackinton charges $650 hourly.  Blackinton graduated from the University of California Los Angeles, School of Law in 2001 and has practiced business litigation since then.  (Ibid.)  Seth Jessee charges $635 hourly.  Jessee has practiced business litigation since he graduated from the University of Virginia School of Law in 2009.  (Ibid.)  Marcel Budiono, who graduated from the University of California, Irvine School of Law in 2019, charges $460 hourly.  (Ibid.)  Considering their credentials and experience, each attorney’s hourly rate is reasonable.  

            Park’s opposition argues FCP has not introduced evidence showing plaintiff “in fact agreed to pay these rates.”  (Opp., p. 6.)  Courts may award hourly rates exceeding the amount billed or paid.  (Pasternack v. McCullough (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1050, 1058.)  “There is no requirement that the reasonable market rate mirror the actual rate billed.”  (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 701.)  The court finds FCP’s attorneys claim appropriate market rates for their work.    

Reasonable Hours

            FCP did not reasonably incur attorney fees for all 104.1 hours it claims.  In calculating the lodestar, the court must determine whether the tasks performed by an attorney were necessary and whether the amount of time billed for each task was reasonable.  (Baxter v. Bock (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 775, 793.)  The moving party has the burden of proof on these issues.  (Ibid.)

FCP billed an excessive amount for drafting the papers in support of its opposition to Park’s motion to compel acknowledgment of satisfaction of judgment.  Between December 11, 2023, and January 2, 2024, Blackinton billed 32 hours to draft the papers.  (Adams Decl., Ex. A, pp. 1-4.)  The opposing memorandum was only 10 pages.  Of those 32 hours, at least 4.2 were billed for drafting the declaration of Michael Chang in support of the opposition.  Chang’s declaration was 10 paragraphs long.  Four paragraphs merely describe and authenticate exhibits.  (Chang Decl. Dec. 20, 2023, ¶¶ 2-5.)  Other portions are identical or nearly identical to a prior declaration Chang submitted on June 12, 2023, with the reply brief in support of FCP’s motion for a charging order.  (Id., ¶¶ 1, 7-10.)  Blackinton’s work on these papers should have taken no more than 18 hours.  The court therefore will reduce FCP’s attorney fees by $9,100, representing 14 hours of fees at $650 hourly.

FCP also billed an excessive amount for preparing for the hearing on the motion.  Between February 7 and February 28, 2024, Adams billed 35.4 hours for various tasks related to preparing for the hearing.  (Adams Decl., Ex. A, pp. 6-8.)  Based on the tasks described, the court concludes this work should have taken no more than 26 hours.  The court will therefore reduce FCP’s attorney fees by $8,225, representing 9.4 hours at $875 hourly.    

  After reviewing the billing records, the court finds all other fees were reasonably incurred.  FCP therefore will recover $60,594.50 in attorney fees. 

Disposition

            Judgment creditor FCP Brands, Inc.’s motion for attorney fees is granted in part.  Judgment creditor FCP Brands, Inc. shall recover $60,594.50 in attorney fees from judgment debtor Mimi Park aka Mi Jin Kim.
