DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV23711    Hearing Date: July 22, 2024    Dept: 26

07/22/2024

Dept. 26

Rolf M. Treu, Assigned Judge presiding

 

D.R. v. Los Angeles Unified School District (22STCV23711)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Jamie M. Majerus/ Herman Law

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Jeffery P. Wade Jr.

 

motion for summary judgment and in the alternative summary adjudication (filed 12/14/2023)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Motion for Summary Judgment and in the alternative Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 22, 2022, Plaintiff D.R. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD” or “Defendant”) alleging that he was sexually abused and molested by an employee of the school district while he attended Middleton Street Elementary School (“Middleton”).

 

Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on April 25, 2023, alleging two causes of action against Defendant for 1) Negligence and 2) Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision.

 

On December 14, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment and in the alternative Summary Adjudication, arguing:

·                 Defendant argues that summary judgment should be granted as it is undisputed that LAUSD did not employ Mr. Rico while Plaintiff attended Middleton (Mot. p. 9.)

o   Plaintiff attended Middleton in 1986 and did not return to Middleton for the second grade, which would have been during the 1987-88 academic year.  (Def. Exh. A, 34:7-17.)

o   Plaintiff claims that he was molested by a “Mr. Rico” while attending Middleton. (Id. 46:20-22, 48:2-5.)

o   Deputy Director of Employee Relations in the Office of Employee Relations, Matthew Frohwein’s review of the records does not indicate that LUSD employed Mr. Rico during the 1986-87 school year. (Frohwein Decl. ¶ 4.)  LAUSD records indicate that it employed Mr. Rico during the 1982-83 school year. (Id. ¶ 5.) Mr. Rico did not work for LAUSD until he was reemployed in July 1987, after Plaintiff left Middleton. (Id.)

·                 There is no evidence that any administrator knew or should have known that Mr. Rico posed a reasonably foreseeable risk of harm to students. (Mot. p. 10.)

·                 Plaintiff’s description of the Perpetrator does not match the Mr. Rico hired by LAUSD. (Mot. p. 9:21-25.)

o   Plaintiff’s own testimony the Mr. Rico LAUSD employed in July 1987 was not the person who allegedly molested D.R. D.R. claimed that a “Mr. Rico” molested him. (Def. Exh. A, p. 72:2-14.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that:

·                 LAUSD's motion should be denied because the evidence suggests that the defendant, who has sole custody of the relevant files, might not be able to search for past employees with the first, middle, or nickname "Rico." (Opp’n p. 6.)

o   Just because LAUSD does not have records of an employee with the last name "Rico" does not mean they did not employ someone with the first, middle, or nickname "Rico." (Id.)

·                 Plaintiff’s testimony and corroborative evidence raises questions of fact regarding the employee-employer relationship between LAUSD and Perpetrator that cannot be resolved on summary judgment. (Id.)

o   Plaintiff specifically testified that he recalls the abuse happening at Middleton in first grade. (Plaintiff Depo. 46:13-22.)

o   Plaintiff believed that Defendant employed the perpetrator based on his observations, including that the Perpetrator brought Plaintiff into the Perpetrator’s office inside the School and that Plaintiff waited for the perpetrator to gather his belongings inside of the school before driving Plaintiff home. (Id. p. 51:24-52:24.)

o   Plaintiff’s testimony additionally indicates that he could testify in detail as to what the Perpetrator’s office looked like inside. (Id. p. 54:1-5.)

·                 Alternatively, the motion for summary judgment is premature and should be continued under CCP § 437c(h). (Opp’n pp. 7-8.)

o   Plaintiff contends that the deposition of LAUSD’s PMK could lead to evidence and additional information needed by Plaintiff to respond to LAUSD’s motion for summary judgment fully. (Id. p. 7: 22-28.)

III. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Motion for Summary Judgment

 

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c).) The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742- 743.) Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) When a defendant seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence that either one or more elements of a cause of action cannot be established or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).) The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions, or ‘ultimate’ facts” and the courts must construe the evidence in support of the opposing party, resolving any doubts in favor of the opposing party. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639; Scalf, 128 Cal.App.4th at 1519; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial movant’s burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.) Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p).) The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.) Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

B.     Discussion.

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision are: (1) the employer’s hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee; (2) the employee was incompetent or unfit; (3) the employer had reason to believe undue risk of harm would exist because of the employment; and (4) harm occurs. (Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 828, 836-837.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot prove that Defendant employed the Perpetrator when the alleged incidents occurred. (Mot. p. 9.)

 

Defendant provides the following evidence.  Plaintiff attended Middleton during his first-grade year in 1986 and did not return to Middleton for the second grade, which would have been during the 1987-88 academic year.  (Def. Exh. A, 34:7-17.) Plaintiff alleges that he was molested by “Mr. Rico” while attending Middleton. (Id. 46:20-22, 48:2-5.) Matthew Frohwein, the Deputy Director of Employee Relations in the Office of Employee Relations, reviewed the records and found no indication that LASD employed a Mr. Rico during the 1986-87 school year. (Frohwein Decl. ¶ 4.)  LAUSD records indicate that it employed a Mr. Rico during the 1982-83 school year. (Id. ¶ 5.) Mr. Rico did not work for LAUSD until he was reemployed in July 1987, after Plaintiff left Middleton. (Id.)

 

Defendant further cites Plaintiff’s deposition testimony in which he confirms that the Mr. Rico LAUSD employed in July 1987 was not the person who allegedly molested Plaintiff. (Def. Exh. A, p. 72:2-14.) Plaintiff described the Perpetrator as light-skinned, tall, with brown comb-over hair, oversized reading glasses, always wearing a polo shirt, thinly built, and with no facial hair. (Id. p. 48:9-14.) Plaintiff testified that he could recognize the Perpetrator if he saw him today. (Id. p. 73.:15-17.) Middleton’s Administrative Assistant, Juanita Ramos, states that she searched for photographs and records related to an employee named Mr. Rico. (Ramos Decl., ¶ 2.) Ms. Ramos searched through old employee files at Middleton but found no documents relating to Mr. Rico. (Id.) Ms. Ramos looked through a filing cabinet that contained binders of classroom photographs and photographs of staff. (Id. ¶ 3.) The only photograph found with Mr. Rico’s name was a classroom photo for Mr. Rico’s third-grade class for the 1987-1988 school year. (Id.) The classroom photograph shows the Middleton Street Elementary School Third Grade Class taught by Mr. Rico. (Id.; Def. Ex. “C”.) Ms. Ramos did not locate any other documents or photographs with the name Mr. Rico on them. (Id. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff was shown the picture of Mr. Rico’s 1987-88 third-grade class at his deposition and testified that no one in the photo looked like the perpetrator. (Def. Exh. A p. 72:2-14.)

 

Based on the evidence provided Defendant has satisfied its initial burden. Here, Defendant has shown evidence that it did not employ a person by the name of Mr. Rico during the time Plaintiff alleges to have been molested. While Defendant did hire a “Mr. Rico” at Middleton, it was before and after Plaintiff had attended the school. Plaintiff further is the only person who can identify the perpetrator as he did not tell anyone about the molestation until he told his wife in 2006. When shown a photograph of Mr. Rico’s third-grade class, Plaintiff could not identify any individual as the perpetrator. Thus, based on the foregoing, Defendant has carried its initial burden.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the absence of records of an employee with the last name "Rico" does not mean they did not employ someone with the first, middle, or nickname "Rico." (Opp’n p. 6.) However, Evidence Code § 1272 provides that the absence of a record of an act, condition, or event is a trustworthy indication that the act or event did not occur, or the condition did not exist. (Cal. Evi. Code § 1272 (b).)

 

Plaintiff additionally provides his deposition testimony in which he states that “[the Perpetrator] came out of the school. He took me into his office.” (Plaintiff’s Depo. p. 52:1-2.) Plaintiff further testified in detail, describing the size and layout of the Perpetrator's office. (Id. p. 54:1-12.) In response, Defendant notes that the statement is speculative because the plaintiff stated, “I’m guessing.” However, context is key here as Plaintiff testified that. “It was about the size of this room, I'm guessing. And his desk was up to a wall, just a regular sized desk, standard, I guess.” (Id. p. 54:1-4.) This would not be speculative because Plaintiff is describing the room as he could recall it and giving a comparison of the size of the room to the room in which the deposition was taken. Regardless, this shows a triable issue of fact because a jury could likely conclude that the Perpetrator was an employee of LAUSD because he had an office in Middleton. Thus, based on this deposition testimony, Plaintiff has shown a triable issue of fact still exists.

 

C.     Summary Adjudication

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication for the reasons discussed in Part B of the Court’s Order.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Defendant LAUSD’s Motion for Summary Judgment and in the alternative Summary Adjudication is DENIED.
