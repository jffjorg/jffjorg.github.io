DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 20STCV42430    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 74

Jayson Toquia Fama v. Astro Aluminum Treating Co., Inc.

Motion for Attorney Fees and Costs.

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from an employment dispute.

            On November 4, 2020, Plaintiff Jayson Toquia Fama (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendant Astro Aluminum Treating Co., Inc. (Defendant).

            On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel filed this motion for attorney fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98. Plaintiff’s counsel seeks $108,822.91 in attorney fees and costs.

EVIDENCE

            The court overrules Defendant’s evidentiary objections.

LEGAL STANDARD

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (c)(1), “If the employee or consumer withdraws the claim from arbitration and proceeds in a court of appropriate jurisdiction . . . [t]he employee or consumer may bring a motion . . . to recover all attorney’s fees and costs associated with the abandoned arbitration proceeding.”

DISCUSSION 

            On June 7, 2023, the court found that Defendant breached the arbitration agreement in place between the parties. Plaintiff has withdrawn his claim from arbitration and now pursues his action here. Consequently, Plaintiff may recover attorney fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (c)(1). Defendant in turn contends the fee claim is unreasonable.

            The attorney bears the burden of proof as to reasonableness of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(c)(5).) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.) A plaintiff’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred. (See Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.” (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488, quoting Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys., Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.)

            To begin, the court finds Plaintiff’s counsel’s proposed hourly rates reasonable. Defendant disagrees, but “[t]he experienced trial judge is the best judge of value of professional services rendered in his court.”” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) Next, Plaintiff’s counsel has furnished billing invoices. (Koulloukian Decl., ¶ 9; Ex. 1; Ex. 2.) But Defendant has directed the court’s attention to specific entries and argues in part that they are redundant. (Herman Decl., ¶¶ 9, 12; Ex. 2; Ex. 10.) The court notes that roughly 75 percent of managing partner Nazo Koulloukian billing entries duplicate those of associate Christine Harmandayan. Accordingly, the court will award Nazo Koulloukian 25 percent of his requested hours for a sum of $15,474.38 ((.25 x 117.9) x $525). The court will award Christine Harmandayan the total amount of her requested attorney fees: $44,440.

            Defendant also points out that the discovery work Plaintiff’s counsel billed during the abandoned arbitration will be used in this action. However, Defendant has not cited authority in support of the proposition that work billed during the abandoned arbitration is no longer associated with that proceeding because the work will be relied upon in subsequent litigation. Nor has Defendant persuaded the court that Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, subdivision (c)(1), may be interpreted in this manner. Accordingly, the court will not deduct discovery fees and costs from Plaintiff’s award. Thus, the court will award Plaintiff attorney fees and costs in the sum of $62,399.79 ($15,474.38 (Koulloukian’s attorney fees) + $44,440 (Harmandayan’s attorney fees) + $2,485.41 (costs).)

CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the court grants Plaintiff’s counsel’s request for attorney fees and costs. Defendant shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel $62,399.79 by January 7, 2023.

Plaintiff shall give notice.


Case Number: 21STCP00179    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 74

Soar Real Estate Investors, LLC v. Sea Breeze Group, LLC, et al.

Motion for Reconsideration

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a dispute between members of a Limited Liability Company. Plaintiff Soar Real Estate Investors, LLC (Plaintiff) filed its complaint on January 22, 2021, against Defendants Sea Breeze Group, Tradewinds Property Group, Charles L. McMillan, Team Investment Group, Marcus Wilson, and Maria Monjaraz (Defendants).

            In the third cause of action, Plaintiff seeks involuntary dissolution of Tradewinds Property Group, LLC.

            On March 3, 2023, Defendants filed a motion to stay the dissolution claim. On March 27, 2023, the court granted the motion. On June 9, 2023, the court signed and filed the order.

            On June 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion for reconsideration of the court’s order.

LEGAL STANDARD

            “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted […] any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

DISCUSSION 

            Plaintiff requests that the court reconsider its order granting Defendants’ motion to stay dissolution proceedings in light of new facts. In support of its request, Plaintiff notes that Defendants’ person most knowledgeable revealed at deposition that (1) Defendants’ members did not meet to vote about Plaintiff’s membership status, and (2) Defendants did not document their decision about Plaintiff’s status. Relying on Corporations Code sections 17701.13, subdivision (d) and 17704.10, subdivision (h) and Defendants’ operating agreement (Overby Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. 2.), Plaintiff contends a membership vote and proper documentation were required to stay the dissolution proceedings.

Plaintiff reads too much into the statutes and the operating agreement. First, the statutes Plaintiff cites do not require a meeting and a vote (see § 17701.13, subd. (d) [document requirements], § 17704.10, subd. (h) [waiver of rights]). Second, sections 3.6 and 3.7 of the operating agreement make annual meetings merely optional but do not require them. (Overby Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. 2.) Hence, even if the information Defendants’ person most knowledgeable constituted “new facts” sufficient to support a motion for reconsideration, those facts do not warrant vacating the stay of the dissolution proceedings.

CONCLUSION 

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration.

Plaintiff shall give notice.

Case Number: 21STCV04930    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 74

Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. v. Paragon Relocation Resources, Inc., et al.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Defendant Paragon Relocation Resources, Inc. to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One)

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND

            This action arises from a contractual dispute.

            On February 8, 2021, Plaintiff Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Paragon Relocation Resources (Defendant) and Paragon Global Resources, Inc. The complaint alleges breach of contract, conversion, and unjust enrichment.

            On September 2, 2021, Plaintiff propounded requests for production of documents (RFPs) on Defendant.

            On December 7, 2021, Defendant responded to the RFPs. Plaintiff was dissatisfied with the responses.

            On April 26, 2023, Defendant provided supplemental responses to the RFPs. Plaintiff was still dissatisfied with many of Defendant’s responses.

            On June 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel further response to RFP Nos. 1, 4, 5, 7-17, 20-22, 24-28, 30, 43, 50-62, 64-74, and 77-79.

LEGAL STANDARD

            A propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response to a demand for inspection if the propounding party deems that an answer or statement of compliance is evasive or incomplete, or that an objection is “without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) Such a motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery sought and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subd. (b), 2031.210, subd. (b).) ¿  

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff seeks further responses from Defendant to RFP Nos. 1, 4, 5, 7-17, 20-22, 24-28, 30, 43, 50-62, 64-68, 70-74, and 77-79.[1] As a preliminary matter, the court finds this motion timely despite Defendant’s contention to the contrary. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c) [“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response . . . the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.”].) Plaintiff received Defendant’s supplemental responses on April 26, 2023. (Toboco Decl., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff thereafter provided notice of this motion within 45 days on June 2, 2023. The court also notes that in its opposition, Defendant claims it provided further supplemental responses and a further supplemental production of documents to RFP Nos. 1, 13, 14, 17, 20, 28, 43, 44, 51, 52, 54-60, 64, 74, and 77 on October 26, 2023, making this motion to compel moot for those RFPs. Accordingly, the court will address Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the remaining RFP Nos. 4, 5, 7-12, 15, 16, 21, 22, 24-27, 30, 50, 53, 61-62, 65-68, 70-73, and 78-79, which are not moot.

            In its initial responses and initial supplemental responses to the remaining RFPs at issue, Defendant asserted numerous objections, but Defendant does not repeat those objections in its opposition. Defendant maintains, however, that it lacks the ability to comply with the remaining RFPs at issue because the responsive documents have been lost on a corrupted hard-drive. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230, a representation of an inability to comply must affirm that the responding party performed a diligent search and reasonable inquiry to comply with the demand. The statement must also set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed to have possession, custody, or control of the sought item. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.) The court notes Defendant did not assert its inability to comply due to the corrupted hard-drive in its initial responses and initial supplemental responses to the remaining RFPs at issue. Thus, further responses will be necessary. Defendant shall state in each response that it made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry. More importantly, Defendant shall state the name and address of anyone who may possess the sought items. In this context, that would be whoever has access to another hard-drive, not affected by the corruption, that holds the items Plaintiff seeks.

The court does not award sanctions because Plaintiff’s counsel has not documented the attorney’s fees she incurred for this motion. Plaintiff’s counsel instead requests a lump sum for the work she performed on multiple discovery motions, which is inadequate. (Toboco Decl., ¶ 21.)

CONCLUSION

                The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RFP Nos. 4, 5, 7-12, 15, 16, 21, 22, 24-27, 30, 50, 53, 61-62, 65-68, 70-73, and 78-79. Defendant shall provide further, Code-compliant responses without objection by December 17, 2023. The court denies Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

            Plaintiff shall give notice.


[1] The court notes Plaintiff’s separate statement does not include RPF Nos. 18 and 69. Accordingly, the court will not consider those requests.


Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. v. Paragon Relocation Resources, Inc., et al.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Defendant Paragon Global Resources, Inc. to Requests for Admission (Set One)

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND

            This action arises from a contractual dispute.

            On February 8, 2021, Plaintiff Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Paragon Relocation Resources and Paragon Global Resources, Inc. (Defendant). The complaint alleges breach of contract, conversion, and unjust enrichment.

            On September 2, 2021, Plaintiff propounded request for admission (RFAs) on Defendant.

            On December 7, 2021, Defendant responded to the RFAs. Plaintiff was dissatisfied with the responses.

            On April 19, 2023, Defendant provided supplemental responses to the RFAs. Plaintiff was still dissatisfied with Defendant’s supplemental response to RFA No. 10.

            On June 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel further response to RFA No. 10.

LEGAL STANDARD

            Under the Code of Civil Procedure, “On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems . . . [a]n answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete [or] [a]n objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (a)(1) & (2).)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff seeks a further response to RFA No. 10: “Admit that YOU have identified or listed amounts for debts owed by [Defendant] to [Plaintiff] on YOUR corporate DOCUMENTS, including, but not limited to, financial documents, profit and loss statements, earnings statements, balance statements, tax returns or other filings, between April 1, 2004 and the present.” Defendant initially responded with objections and nothing more. In its supplemental response, Defendant repeated its objections.

First, Defendant contends RFA No. 10 violates Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.060, subdivision (d): “Each request for admission shall be full and complete in and of itself. No preface of instruction shall be included with a set of admission requests unless it has been approved under Chapter 17 (commencing with Section 2033.710).” The court finds RFA No. 10 complete in and of itself, especially since Plaintiff defines the documents sought within the request. Moreover, Defendant has not demonstrated how the RFA’s preface or instruction violated Chapter 17 beginning at Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.710.

Defendant also contends RFA No. 10 violates Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.060, subdivision (f): “No request for admission shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive request . . .” Although RFA No. 10 asks Defendant to admit it identified or listed debts owed in corporate documents, the RFA is not truly disjunctive because identifying and listing debts in documents amount to the same act. In addition, the request is not truly conjunctive because the corporate documents listed are merely examples of places where Defendant may have identified debts owed. Thus, the court finds these objections lack merit, meaning a further response is necessary.

The court does not award sanctions because Plaintiff’s counsel has not documented the attorney’s fees she incurred for this motion. Plaintiff’s counsel instead requests a lump sum for the work she performed on multiple discovery motions, which is inadequate. (Toboco Decl., ¶ 21.)

CONCLUSION

                The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further response to RFA No. 10. Defendant shall provide a further code-complaint response to RFA No. 10 without objection by December 17, 2023. The court denies the request for sanctions.

                Plaintiff shall give notice.


Case Number: 21STCV23325    Hearing Date: December 7, 2023    Dept: 74

The LAAC Corp. v. Stewart, Romberger, & Kite, et al.

Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a contractual dispute.

            On June 23, 2021, Plaintiff The LAAC Corp. (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Mistry Engineering, Inc. (Defendant) and others. In the complaint, Plaintiff alleged the following causes of action: breach of contract, negligence, and “indemnification.”

            In April 2023, the parties settled.

            On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion for leave to file an amended complaint.

DISCUSSION 

            Plaintiff seeks to amend the operative complaint. The proposed amended complaint adds allegations about the settlement agreement, which the parties entered almost two years after plaintiff filed the operative complaint. An amended complaint is not the proper vehicle to allege new, post-complaint facts to state a new, post-complaint cause of action that accrued after the complaint was filed. (Foster v. Sexton (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 998, 1032; Flood v. Simpson (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 644, 647; Code Civ. Proc., § 464, subd. (a).)

CONCLUSION 

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion.

Plaintiff shall give notice.
