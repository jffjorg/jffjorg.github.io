DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20PSCV00849    Hearing Date: November 4, 2024    Dept: H

Liu v. Lanterprise, Inc., et al., Case No. 20PSCV00849

Defendants Lanterprise, Inc. dba Lanterprise Corporation dba Lanterprise Construction, Waiming Li, and Weiheng Zhen’s Motion for Damages to be Added to Cost Bill Under Code of Civil Procedure Section 907 is DENIED.

Background

Plaintiff Miller Liu (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

In or about September 2018, Plaintiff engaged in discussions with Waiming Li (“Li”) and Weiheng Zhen (“Zhen”), whereby Li’s construction company, Lanterprise, Inc. dba Lanterprise Corporation dba Lanterprise Constriction (“Lanterprise”), would build an ADU for Plaintiff in Plaintiff’s backyard. Plaintiff advised Li and Zhen that he intended to move into the ADU and wanted the ADU to have a separate and independent water, gas, and electric meters. The parties thereafter entered into a contract and Plaintiff paid $170,000.00 towards the $180,000.00 projected costs as construction progressed. On August 15, 2019, Li prepared a change order, which set forth additional costs of $21,965.00 for items related to gas and water connections and meters.

Li and Zhen refused to finish construction of the ADU when Plaintiff disputed paying the $21,965.00. Plaintiff has obtained a written quote of $41,705.44 to finish the construction. As Plaintiff was inspecting the ADU, he discovered that Li and/or Zhen sabotaged some of the construction before leaving the premises, including drilling a hole in one of the new metal water pipes and removing a previously installed electrical breaker that had passed inspection and replacing it with an inexpensive one which failed inspection by the building inspector in a subsequent inspection.

On December 7, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Lanterprise, Li, Zhen, and Does 1-20 for (1) breach of written agreement and (2) intentional destruction of property.

On April 11, 2023, the Court granted Lanterprise, Li, and Zhen’s motion for judgment on the pleadings. On May 4, 2023, judgment was entered; that day, “Notice of Entry of Judgment” was filed.

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a “Notice of Appeal.” On August 7, 2024, remittitur was issued (affirmed).

 

Legal Standard

“When it appears to the reviewing court that the appeal was frivolous or taken solely for delay, it may add to the costs on appeal such damages as may be just.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 907 [Emphasis added].)

“On motion of a party or its own motion, a Court of Appeal may impose sanctions, including the award or denial of costs under rule 8.278, on a party or an attorney for: (1) Taking a frivolous appeal or appealing solely to cause delay; (2) Including in the record any matter not reasonably material to the appeal’s determination; (3) Filing a frivolous motion; or (4) Committing any other unreasonable violation of these rules.” (Cal. Rules Ct., rule 8.276(a) [Emphasis added].)

 

Discussion

Lanterprise, Li, and Zhen (collectively, “Defendants”) move the Court, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 907, for an order awarding it damages in the amount of $42,559.67 on the grounds that the appeal was frivolous and taken solely for harassment and to attempt to exact revenge.

The motion is denied. The reviewing court here is the Second District, Division 7 Court of Appeal. Defendants have not provided the Court with any authority which reflects that Code of Civil Procedure section 907 provides a non-reviewing Court with the ability to impose such damages. Defendants’ citation to Bucur v. Ahmad (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 175 involved the Fourth District, Division One Court of Appeal’s (and not the trial court’s) imposition of section 907 sanctions.  

Case Number: 24PSCV01975    Hearing Date: November 4, 2024    Dept: H

Monreal v. Real Time Resolutions, Inc., et al., Case No. 24PSCV01975

 

ORDER ON MOTION FOR PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

Plaintiff Antonio Monreal’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction is DENIED.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Antonio Monreal (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

On December 13, 2006, David and Angela Lopez (together, the “Lopezes”) obtained (1) a first position loan from WMC Mortgage Corp. (“WMC”) for $332,00.00 and (2) a second position loan from WMC in the amount of $83,000.00, both of which were secured by Deeds of Trust (i.e., “Deed 1 and Deed 2,” respectively) recorded January 5, 2007 on the property located at 2442 Lennox Street in Pomona (the “subject property”). Deed 1 and Deed 2 were assigned multiples times but the loans continued to be serviced by Seneca Mortgage Servicing LLC (“Seneca”) under one loan number, i.e., Loan No. 0005230626.

On or about September 7, 2016, a Notice of Trustee’s Sale was recorded in connection with Deed 1; in lieu of foreclosure, the subject property was listed for sale as a short sale. In or about December 2016, Seneca approved the short sale in connection with Loan No. 0005230626; the Short Sale Approval stated the “Current Balance” of the Deed 1 was $242,195.32 with $242,195.32 to be applied and the “Current Balance” of the Deed 2 was $98,070.292 with $45,183.34 to be applied, with the total due listed as $287,378.66.

On November 16, 2016, Plaintiff purchased the subject property with a $305,000.00 purchase money loan from Guild Mortgage Company secured by a Deed of Trust recorded December 23, 2016. These funds were used to pay off the balance due under Deed 2. Despite the payoff of Deed 2, however, it was not released and a reconveyance was not recorded; instead, Deed 2 was purportedly assigned to Real Time Resolutions Inc. (“Real Time”) via an Assignment of Deed of Trust recorded on October 7, 2020. On September 28, 2023, a Substitution of Trustee was recorded whereby ZBS Law, LLP (“ZBS”)[FN1] was substituted as the foreclosure trustee under Deed 2. On October 20, 2023, ZBS recorded a Notice of Default relative to Deed 2. On February 13, 2024, Real Time and ZBS were advised via letter that they were required to release the Deed 2, execute a full reconveyance, and record that reconveyance. To date, no reconveyance has been recorded. On June 5, 2024, ZBS recorded a Notice of Sale relative to Deed 2. 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Real Time, ZBS, Seneca, All Other Persons Unknown, Claiming Any Right, Title, Estate, Lien, or Interest in the Real Property Described in the Complaint Adverse to Plaintiff’s Ownership Interest or Any Cloud on Plaintiff’s Right Thereto, and Does 1-50 for:

1.       Violation of Civil Code Section 2941(b),

2.       Quiet Title,

3.       Cancellation of Instruments,

4.       Unjust Enrichment,

5.       Declaratory Relief, and

6.       Injunctive Relief. 

On June 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a “Notice of Pendency of Action (Lis Pendens).” On July 8, 2024, an “Amendment to Complaint” was filed, wherein “ZBD Law, LLP’s” name was corrected to “ZBS Law, LLP.” 

On July 26, 2024, ZBS filed a “Declaration of Nonmonetary Status.” 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion for a preliminary injunction. On September 6, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application for a temporary restraining order so as to prevent the sale of the subject property while the motion for a preliminary injunction was pending. The Court ordered further briefing on the motion for preliminary injunction, as follows: Plaintiff was ordered to file a supplemental moving brief by October 14, 2024, RTR was ordered to file a response by October 21, 2024, and Plaintiff was permitted to file an optional reply by October 28, 2024. The Court received an opposition from RTR but never received a supplemental moving brief or reply brief from Plaintiff. For the reasons stated herein, the motion for preliminary injunction is denied.

II.      Legal Standard

“An injunction may be granted in the following cases: (1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually[;] (2) When it appears by the complaint or affidavits that the commission or continuance of some act during the litigation would produce waste, or great or irreparable injury, to a party to the action[;] (3) When it appears, during the litigation, that a party to the action is doing, or threatens, or is about to do, or is procuring or suffering to be done, some act in violation of the rights of another party to the action respecting the subject of the action, and tending to render the judgment ineffectual[;] (4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief[;] (5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief[;] (6) Where the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of judicial proceedings[;] (7) Where the obligation arises from a trust.” (CCP § 526(a).)

“In considering whether to issue a preliminary injunction, a court evaluates two interrelated factors: the likelihood plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial and the interim harm to plaintiff or defendant if the court denies or grants the preliminary injunction. Plaintiff carries the burden of proof and persuasion on these issues.” (Drakes Bay Oyster Co. v. California Coastal Com. (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 1165, 1171.)

“The trial court’s determination must be guided by a ‘mix’ of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.” (Butt v. State of California (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 678.) “[A]n injunction should not issue where there is no possibility of success even though its issuance might prevent irreparable harm. . . Where there is indeed no likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail, an injunction favoring the plaintiff serves no valid purpose and can only cause needless harm.” (American Academy of Pediatrics v. Van de Camp (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 831, 838.)

An application for a preliminary injunction may be based upon a verified complaint, or upon affidavits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 527, subd. (a).)

Finally, “[a] preliminary injunction ordinarily cannot take effect unless and until the party applying for it provides an undertaking” for damages which the enjoined defendant may sustain due to the injunction if the court finally decides that the plaintiff was not entitled to the injunction.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 529, subd. (a); City of S. San Francisco v. Cypress Lawn Cemetery Ass’n (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 916, 920.)


III.     Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court for a preliminary injunction to enjoin and restrain Real Time, its agents, attorneys, and representatives, and all persons acting in concert or participating with it, from recording a notice of trustee’s sale, selling, attempting to sell, or causing to be sold the subject real property at issue in this case, either under the power of sale in the subject deed of trust or by any other foreclosure during the pendency of this action.

 

Interim Harm

It is beyond dispute that real property is unique such that injury or loss cannot be compensated in damages.  (Civ. Code § 3387; Aspen Grove Condominium Assoc. v CNL Income Northstar LLC (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 53, 62.)  Plaintiff thus has sufficiently demonstrated interim harm, in that this dispute revolves around ownership of real property and the potential loss of title ownership.

 

Reasonable Likelihood of Prevailing on the Merits

The burden upon Plaintiff is to show that there is a reasonable likelihood that Plaintiff will prevail on the merits. On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a verified complaint, asserting causes of action for (1) Violation of Civil Code section 2941(b), (2) Quiet Title, (3) Cancellation of Instruments, (4) Unjust Enrichment, (5) Declaratory Relief, and (6) Injunctive Relief.

Plaintiff proffers the following facts in support of its request for a preliminary injunction:  

Plaintiff purchased the subject property in November 2016. (Complaint, ¶¶ 7 and 14, Exh. 7; see also Plaintiff’s Decl., ¶¶ 3 and 4, Exh. 3). On or about December 13, 2006, Plaintiff’s predecessors in interest, the Lopezes obtained first and second position loans from WMC Mortgage Corp. in the original principal amounts of $332,000.00 and $83,000.00, respectively. (Complaint, ¶¶ 8 and 9; Dunn Decl., ¶¶ 4 and 5, Exhs. 1 and 2). The first position loan was secured by Deed 1, which was recorded on January 5, 2007 as Document No. 20070025973; the second position loan was secured by Deed 2, which was recorded that same day as Document No. 20070025974. (Complaint, ¶¶ 8 and 9, Exhs. 1 and 2). Deed 1 and Deed 2 were thereafter assigned multiple times but the loans continued to be serviced by Seneca under one loan number (i.e., Loan No. 0005230626), as evidenced by statements dated August 16, 2016, November 16, 2016 and December 16, 2016 obtained from Seneca (each of which identify both the first and second mortgage loan). (Complaint, ¶ 10; Dunn Decl., ¶ 6, Exhs. 5-7). On or about September 7, 2016, a Notice of Trustee’s Sale recorded in connection with Deed 1; in lieu of foreclosure, the subject property was listed for sale as a short sale. (Complaint, ¶ 11).

In or about December 2016, Seneca approved the short sale in connection with Loan No. 0005230626. (Complaint, ¶ 12, Exh. 6; Dunn Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. 8.) The Short Sale Approval states the “Current Balance” of Deed 1 was $242,195.32 with $242,195.32 to be applied and the “Current Balance” of Deed 2 was $98,070.292 with $45,183.34 to be applied, with the total due listed as $287,378.66. (Complaint, ¶ 13, Exh. 6; Dunn Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. 8.) In purchasing the subject property, Plaintiff obtained a purchase money loan in the amount of $305,900 from Guild Mortgage Company, which was secured by a first position deed of trust recorded December 23, 2016; $287,378.66 of these funds were wired to Seneca to satisfy Deed 1 and Deed 2. (Complaint, ¶¶ 15-18, Exh. 8; Dunn Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. 9; Plaintiff’s Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 4.)

Plaintiff contends that Deed 2 was not released and a reconveyance was not recorded, despite it being paid off, that Real Time was purportedly assigned the satisfied and extinguished Deed 2 via an Assignment of Deed of Trust recorded on October 7, 2020, that ZBS substituted in as trustee under Deed 2 on or about September 28, 2023, and that ZBS recorded a Notice of Default and Notice of Sale relative to Deed 2 on October 20, 2023 and June 5, 2024, respectively. (Complaint, ¶¶ 21-25; Dunn Decl., ¶ 11). The foreclosure sale of the subject property was subsequently rescheduled for September 10, 2024. (Plaintiff’s Decl., ¶ 8.)

All of Plaintiff’s causes of action are based on the contention that Deed 2 was paid off through the short sale. (Complaint, ¶¶ 47, 51 and 56.) Real Time, however, asserts that Deed 2, which it services, was not satisfied through the short sale.

In support of its position, Real Time proffers the following evidence:

Real Time does not possess any information that Deed 2 was satisfied through the 2016 short sale or otherwise. (Trakhtenbroit Decl., ¶ 14.) There is no indication that Seneca ever serviced Deed 2; rather, an Assignment of Deed of Trust was recorded in 2013 which reflected that Deed 2 was assigned to HSBC Bank, USA, National Association (“HSBC”). (Trakhtenbroit Decl., ¶¶ 7 and 15, Exh. 2.) Deed 2 remained assigned to HSBC at the time of the 2016 short sale. (Id., ¶ 15.) Real Time’s records reflect that Bank of America was the prior servicer of the loan secured by Deed 2. (Id., ¶ 16.)

Real Time acquired ownership of the second position loan in March 2020 and began servicing same shortly thereafter. (Id., ¶ 9.) On October 7, 2020, an Assignment of Deed of Trust was recorded, through which Deed 2 was assigned to Real Time. (Id., ¶ 9, Exh. 3.) At the time Real Time began servicing Deed 2, Real Time obtained the Lopezes’ credit reports, which reflected only one tradeline pertaining to Seneca, with the loan number thereon matching the number on the documentation enclosed with Plaintiff’s counsel’s February 13, 2024 correspondence, No. 005230626. (Id., ¶¶ 12 and 17, Exh. 5.)

The tradeline reflects that the term of the mortgage is 47 years (id.), whereas the maturity date listed on the face of Deed 1 is January 1, 2037, which is 30 years after its origination. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. 1.) Real Time’s corporate counsel, Samuel Trakhtenbroit, opines, based on his experience, that this suggests that the loan secured by Deed 1 was modified and the maturity date was extended. (Id., ¶ 18.)

The mortgage statements proffered by Plaintiff do not support Plaintiff’s position. While the mortgage statements refer to a “1st Mortgage” and a “2nd Mortgage,” they are referred together as Loan No. 0005230636. (Dunn Decl., ¶ 6, Exhs. 5-7.) The August 16, 2016 statement refers to the “2nd mortgage” as having a principal balance of $98,070.29, which does not accord with the fact that Deed 2’s original principal balance was $83,000.00. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. 5.) In Trakhtenbroit’s view, this $98,070.29 figure reflects a deferred, lump sum balance that resulted from a modification agreement entered into with the Lopezes on the loan secured by Deed 1. (Trakhtenbroit Decl., ¶ 18). The statements also refer to the “2nd mortgage” as having a 0% interest rate and the “1st mortgage” as having a 4% interest rate (the latter of which contradicts the Adjustable Rate Rider to Deed 1, which states that “[t]he interest rate. . . will not be greater than 10.425% or less than 5.425%”), again suggesting to Trakhtenbroit that a modification occurred. (Dunn Decl., ¶¶ 4 and 6, Exhs. 1 and 5-7; Trakhtenbroit Decl., ¶ 21.)

According to Trakhtenbroit, while Plaintiff claims both loans were serviced together by Seneca as loan No. 005230626, separate loans are typically given separate loan numbers by mortgage brokers and that here, Bank of America had a separate loan number while servicing the loan secured by Deed 2. (Id., ¶ 21.)

Finally, records from Seneca demonstrate that Seneca only serviced Deed 1. Seneca, in response to a deposition subpoena issued by Plaintiff’s counsel, did not produce a copy of Deed 2 or any other document relating to Deed 2 or that referenced the recording information for Deed 2. (Id., ¶ 24.) Seneca produced a “Lien Release Initial Review Checklist,” which makes reference only to the recording information for Deed 1. (Id., ¶ 24, Exh. 6.) The short sale approval letter attached to attorney Kelly Vierra’s February 13, 2024 correspondence also only refers to the release of one lien in connection with the short sale (Complaint, ¶ 26, Exh. 10(B) [“Seneca . . . will release the lien on the subject property if the following conditions are met”].)

The aforementioned evidence suggests that contrary to Plaintiff’s claim, Deed 2 was not satisfied in conjunction with the 2016 short sale. Accordingly, Plaintiff has not demonstrated a reasonable likelihood of success on the merits. The motion is denied.    


[FN] ZBS was erroneously sued as “ZBD” in the complaint. On July 8, 2024, an “Amendment to Complaint” was filed, wherein ZBS’s name was corrected.
