DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03418    Hearing Date: May 23, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT¿AND SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 5/23/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03418 IGNACIO CHAVEZ, et al. vs JASON WAGNER

Moving Party: Defendants Samantha Wagner and Jason Wagner

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION OF THE CLAIMS AGAINST SAMANTHA WAGNER IS GRANTED; 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION OF THE CLAIMS AGAINST JASON WAGNER IS DENIED.

 

Background

 

On January 27, 2022, Plaintiff filed this automobile accident case against Defendants Jason Wagner (“Defendant”) and Samantha Wagner, alleging that Defendants rear-ended Plaintiff's car. Defendants now move for summary judgment or summary adjudication, arguing that Jason Wagner was suddenly stricken by an unforeseen illness and fell unconscious, thereby causing the accident. Additionally, Defendants assert that Samantha Wagner has no relation to this matter, as she is not the owner of the vehicle, was not in the vehicle at the time of the accident and was not involved in the accident in any way.

 

Evidentiary Ruling

 

The Parties did not make any objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).) The court GRANTS the Parties’ requests for judicial notice.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). "When the motion for summary judgment is supported by affidavits sufficient to sustain the motion,…the burden of showing that triable issues exist shifts to the party opposing the motion. [Citation.] A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)¿ 

 

MSJ For Claims Against Samantha Wagner

 

Defendant Samantha Wagner has met her initial burden by demonstrating that she is not the owner of the vehicle involved in the accident, her name is not on the vehicle’s certificate, she was not driving the vehicle at the time of the incident, nor was she involved with the accident in any capacity. (Declaration of Samantha Wagner ¶ 2; Declaration of Jason Wagner ¶¶ 2, 11-14, Def’s Exhibit A.) Plaintiff does not dispute this in his separate statement or opposition, and he has failed to introduce any evidence to raise a triable issue of fact. Therefore, summary judgment of Plaintiff's claims against Defendant Samantha Wagner is GRANTED.

 

MSJ For Claims against Jason Wagner

 

Cohen Rule/Sudden Illness

 

“‘California has approved the rule of Cohen v. Petty (D.C. Cir. 1993) 65 F.2d 820, 821: as between an innocent passenger and an innocent fainting driver, the former must suffer.’  [Citation.]”  (Bashi v. Wodarz (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1314, 1318.)  Generally, one who is suddenly stricken by an illness, which he had no reason to anticipate, while driving an automobile, which renders it impossible for him to control the car, is not chargeable with negligence.  (Id. at 1319; see, e.g., Hammontree v. Jenner (1971) 20 Cal.App.3d 528, 530-531 [loss of consciousness due to unexpected epileptic seizure]; Tannyhill v. Pacific Motor Transport Co. (1964) 227 Cal.App.2d 512, 520 [heart attack]; Zabunoff v. Walker (1961) 192 Cal.App.2d 8, 11 [jurors could have concluded that a sudden sneeze was an intervening cause similar to a fainting spell]; Ford v. Carew & English (1948) 89 cal.App.2d 199, 203 [fainting spell from strained heart muscle]; Waters v. Pacific Coast Dairy, Inc. (1942) 55 Cal.App.2d 789, 793 [driver rendered unconscious from sharp pain in left arm].)

 

The “basis behind the Cohen rule is related to the general concept of ‘unavoidable accident’ . . . [which] is predicated upon absence of fault.” (Bashi v. Wodarz (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1314, 1319.) “The conduct of an actor during a period of a sudden incapacitation or loss of consciousness resulting from physical illness is negligent only if the sudden incapacitation or loss of consciousness was reasonably foreseeable to the actor.” (Rest.3d Torts, § 11.)

 

In Waters, the defendant testified he became unconscious due to sharp pain in his arm.  However, the jury found in favor of the plaintiffs. The appellate court affirmed the determination of the jury because the defendant failed to present evidence showing how long he suffered from the cause of the loss of consciousness and whether he should have had reason to anticipate such an attack at said time and place. (Id. at p. 793.)

 

In Hammontree, the defendant suffered from epilepsy, which was controlled by medication.  The defendant’s doctor advised the DMV in writing, once per year, of the defendant’s condition and testified he believed it was safe for the defendant to drive. (Hammontree, supra, 20 Cal.App.3d at pp. 530-532.) The court found that Defendant had no reason to anticipate an attack while driving as there was no inkling or warning that he was about to have a seizure prior to the occurrence of the accident. (Ibid.)

 

Analysis

 

        On March 4, 2021, at 10:30 AM, Defendant drove to Cal State Northridge for his COVID-19 vaccine appointment. After receiving the vaccine, he waited 15 minutes as instructed before leaving and was told to return if he felt any symptoms. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 10.) During the 15-minute waiting period, Defendant did not experience any symptoms. He reported, “As I started to drive, I did not have any symptoms and generally felt fine.” (Wagner Decl. ¶¶ 11-12.)

Five minutes after leaving Cal State Northridge, Defendant began to feel lightheaded. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 13.) He decided to turn around and return to the vaccination site as advised. (Id.) After passing through the intersection of Nordhoff and Balboa, he turned around to head westbound on Nordhoff back to Cal State Northridge. (Id.) He stopped completely behind Plaintiff’s vehicle at the intersection of Nordhoff and Balboa due to a red light. (Id.) Shortly thereafter, Defendant suddenly and unexpectedly lost consciousness and collided with Plaintiff’s car. (Wagner Decl. ¶ 14.) Defendant also provided a declaration from his expert witness doctor who testified that the cause of Defendant’s loss of consciousness is unknown. (Declaration of Barry Ludwig.)

Summary judgment is a drastic remedy to be used sparingly, and any doubts about its propriety must be resolved in favor of the opposing party. (Castaneda v. Ensign Group, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1019.) Here, summary judgment as to Defendant is not appropriate because triable issues of fact exist.  For instance, Defendant failed to show how long he felt lightheaded before the accident and whether he had reason to anticipate fainting. Although Defendant produced evidence indicating he had no prior medical conditions and had no reaction within the 15-minute waiting period after taking the vaccine, and therefore he had no reason to anticipate feeling lightheaded or fainting in general, Defendant failed to produce evidence showing that fainting was not reasonably foreseeable at the "said time and place" once he started to feel lightheaded. This case is unlike Hammontree where there was no inkling or warning that he was about to have a seizure prior to the occurrence of the accident.

Additionally, it appears appropriate for a trier of fact to determine whether Defendant can be found free of fault or if the accident was truly unavoidable. In particular, Defendant failed to stop and pull over when he began feeling lightheaded, just minutes after leaving the vaccination site. Although the clinic advised him to return if he felt unwell, his lightheadedness or dizziness, arguably directly impacted his ability to drive safely. Reasonable minds could differ on whether Defendant acted reasonably under the circumstances. Additionally, reasonable minds could also differ on whether his fainting was reasonably foreseeable from his earlier symptoms of lightheadedness, especially considering that he had received his first COVID-19 vaccine only 20 minutes prior and was informed to expect potential side effects. Thus, Defendant has failed to meet his initial burden and summary adjudication of the claims against Jason Wagner is denied.

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/23/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV26523 JESSICA MARIE MOORE, et al. vs POMONA UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT

Moving Party: Pomona Unified School District 

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:  

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION IS GRANTED;

REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

 

Legal Standard 

¿¿¿¿ 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450¿provides in pertinent part the following:

 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

¿¿¿ 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040¿or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

 

Background

On August 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present case alleging that Plaintiff Jessica Moore, who was five at the time of the incident, was choked by a teacher’s aide within the Pomona Unified School District. The parties agreed that Defendant would take Plaintiff’s and Plaintiff’s guardian ad litem’s depositions on January 11, 2024. On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s guardian ad litem attended the deposition; however, Plaintiff did not attend. On January 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s deposition was noticed again for February 7, 2024. On February 6, 2024, Defendant emailed Plaintiff’s counsel to confirm Plaintiff’s appearance for the deposition. Plaintiff’s counsel responded that he would not “voluntarily subject [Plaintiff] to a deposition without a Court Order.” Defendant now moves the Court to compel Plaintiff’s deposition.

All prerequisites to compel Plaintiff's deposition have been met. Plaintiff's deposition was duly noticed for February 7, 2024, but Plaintiff failed to attend. Furthermore, no timely written objections to the notice of deposition were served. Additionally, Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement, as the parties have reached an impasse regarding whether Plaintiff should be required to attend a deposition.

As to the issue of whether Plaintiff should be compelled to attend a deposition given her status as a minor, California Evidence Code Section 700 provides that "every person, irrespective of age, is qualified to be a witness, and no person is disqualified to testify to any matter." Plaintiff’s counsel has not contended that Plaintiff is unfit to testify. Conversely, California Evidence Code Section 765 mandates that the Court exercise heightened care with witnesses under the age of 14 or dependent persons with substantial cognitive impairments, to protect them from undue harassment or embarrassment and to limit repetitive questioning. Therefore, while Plaintiff is eligible to testify, the Court must ensure that the deposition procedures are adjusted appropriately to accommodate her age and safeguard her rights during the testimony.

As analyzed above, Defendant has met all the requirements for a motion to compel. Consequently, the Court grants the present motion, and Plaintiff is ORDERED to sit for her deposition within 30 days from today. Additionally, the Court ORDERS both parties to meet and confer regarding the duration, location, and presence of a guardian during the deposition, as well as the scope of the questions to be asked, to ensure the child is protected from undue harassment.

The Court declines to impose sanctions at this time, as the deposition of children is not a well-developed area of law. There are conflicting needs to protect the interests of the child and to develop a defense. Plaintiff’s hesitation to produce the minor for a deposition without the Court’s guidance on this matter is understandable, though the Court questions why counsel appears to have agreed to such deposition on at least one occasion.  Nonetheless, the Court deems counsel to have acted with substantial justification in requiring court involvement in the process.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT 

Hearing Date: 5/23/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV15104 LESLIE VILLA MORALES vs TOWER OF SERRANO, LLC

Moving Party: Defendants TOWER OF SERRANO, LLC; WINSTAR PROPERTIES, LLC; ALAN STERN; and JONATHAN WINTNER

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT 

IS GRANTED. 

 

Legal Standard 

 

A cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the initial complaint or cross-complaint against the cross-complainant must be filed before or at the same time as the answer to the initial complaint or cross-complaint, which answer must be filed within 30 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint.¿(CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 428.50(a), 432.10.)¿Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a trial date.¿(CCP §428.50(b).) 

 

If a party fails to file a cross-complaint within the time limits described above, he or she must obtain permission from the court to file the cross-complaint. (CCP §§ 426.50, 428.50(c).) The proposed cross-complaint is mandatory when it arises out of the same transaction as plaintiff’s claim. The court must grant leave to file the mandatory cross-complaint absent bad faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50, Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 99.) Leave to file a permissive cross-complaint need only be granted in the interest of justice. (CCP §428.50(c).) Judicial policy generally supports the resolution of all disputed matters between parties, leave to amend or leave to cross-complain is typically granted liberally. (See Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 412.) The Court may, however, deny such leave if there is demonstrated prejudice to the opposing party, such as trial delays, loss of critical evidence, or increased preparation costs. (Id.)  “Cross complaints for comparative equitable indemnity would appear virtually always transactionally related to the main action.” (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Develop. Co. (1991) 230 CA3d 30, 38.) 

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 requires a copy of the proposed cross complaint to be attached to the motion. 

 

Discussion

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present trip and fall case against multiple defendants, including TOWER OF SERRANO, LLC; WINSTAR PROPERTIES, LLC; ALAN STERN; and JONATHAN WINTNER (“Defendants”). Defendants performed due diligence and discovered that the City of Los Angeles was responsible for the maintenance and repair of the sidewalk and driveway where the incident occurred. Defendants filed a government claim against the City but was rejected. Defendants now moves the Court to file a cross-complaint for indemnity and declaratory relief against the City.

“Cross complaints for comparative equitable indemnity would appear virtually always transactionally related to the main action.” (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Develop. Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 30, 38.) The cross-complaint is therefore mandatory. No opposition was filed, nor was there any other filing indicating bad faith. Further, a copy of the proposed cross-complaint is attached to the motion. Thus, Defendants’ motion for leave is granted.

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

Hearing Date: 5/23/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV23141 MEGAN WERNER, AN INDIVIDUAL vs STATE OF CALIFORNIA

Moving Party: Defendant State of California acting by and through the State of California Department of Fish and Wildlife and the Santa Monica Mountains Conservancy; Joined by City of Los Angeles, Department of Recreation and Parks, and Mountains Recreation and Conservation Authority

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS IS GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART

 

Background

On 9/25/23, Plaintiff filed the present case, alleging that she was attacked by a mountain lion approximately 1.2 miles from Griffith Park while walking her dog on a sidewalk in a residential area. Plaintiff asserts four causes of action: 1) Negligence per se for violations of California Fish & Game Code §§ 1801, 4801, and 4803; 2) Negligence; 3) Negligent infliction of emotional distress; and 4) Dangerous condition of public property against multiple public entities. Defendant State of California, acting by and through the State of California Department of Fish and Wildlife and the Santa Monica Mountains Conservancy, now moves the court for judgment on the pleadings, arguing that they did not violate any provisions of California Fish & Game Code as the statue does not mandate the state to "take" the mountain lion upon reports of depredation. Defendants further contend that Plaintiff’s negligence and negligent infliction of emotional distress claims fail because the state has no duty to control wild animals, and the dangerous condition claim also fails because a mountain lion does not constitute a physical condition of a public property. City of Los Angeles, Department of Recreation and Parks, and Mountains Recreation and Conservation Authority joined The State of California in its Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

Legal Standard 

 

A defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (CCP § 438(f).) A motion by a defendant may be made on the grounds that (1) the court “lacks jurisdiction of the subject of one or more of the causes of action alleged” or (2) the complaint or cross-complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (CCP § 438(c).) 

 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.) “A motion for judgment on the pleadings is akin to a general demurrer; it tests the sufficiency of the complaint to state a cause of action.” (Wise v. Pacific Gas and Elec. Co. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 725, 738.) 

 

Like a general demurrer, “ordinarily, a [motion for judgment on the pleadings] does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action, and if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the [motion] will be overruled.” (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether properly pled factual allegations—assumed to be true and liberally construed—are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Stone Street Capital, LLC v. Cal. State Lottery Com’n (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 109, 116.)

 

Discussion

1.  Negligence Per Se for Alleged Violations of California Fish & Game Code §§ 1801, 4801, And 4803

          Plaintiff’s claim of negligence per se for alleged violations of California Fish & Game Code §§ 1801, 4801, and 4803 fails as a matter of law. 
 
          California Fish & Game Code § 1801 articulates the state's policy to encourage the preservation, conservation, and maintenance of wildlife resources, outlining numerous objectives for this policy.
 
          California Fish & Game Code § 4801 provides “The department may remove or take any mountain lion, or authorize an appropriate local agency with public safety responsibility to remove or take any mountain lion, that is perceived to be an imminent threat to public health or safety or that is perceived by the department to be an imminent threat to the survival of any threatened, endangered, candidate, or fully protected sheep species.”
 

          California Fish & Game Code § 4802 provides “Any person, or the employee or agent of a person, whose livestock or other property is being or has been injured, damaged, or destroyed by a mountain lion may report that fact to the department and request a permit to take the mountain lion.”

 

          California Fish & Game Code § 4803 provides “Upon receipt of a report pursuant to Section 4802, the department, or any animal damage control officer specifically authorized by the department to carry out this responsibility, shall immediately take the action necessary to confirm that there has been depredation by a mountain lion as reported. The confirmation process shall be completed as quickly as possible, but in no event more than 48 hours after receiving the report. If satisfied that there has been depredation by a mountain lion as reported, the department shall promptly issue a permit to take the depredating mountain lion.”

 

          California Fish & Game Code § 4806 provides “Any person issued a permit pursuant to Section 4803 or 4805 shall report, by telephone within 24 hours, the capturing, injuring, or killing of any mountain lion to an office of the department or, if telephoning is not practicable, in writing within five days after the capturing, injuring, or killing of the mountain lion. At the time

of making the report of the capturing, injuring, or killing, the

holder of the permit shall make arrangements to turn over the

mountain lion or the entire carcass of the mountain lion which has been recovered to a representative of the department and shall do so in a timely manner.”

 

California Fish & Game Code §§ 1801 and 4801 do not establish grounds for a negligence per se claim as they fail to impose a specific statutory duty enforceable under such a claim. Section 1801 primarily sets forth broad objectives for the preservation, conservation, and maintenance of wildlife resources, serving as a policy guideline rather than a directive imposing legal obligations. Meanwhile, Section 4801 uses discretionary language ("may"), allowing but not requiring the department to act in the removal or taking of a mountain lion deemed an imminent threat to public health or safety or to endangered wildlife species.

In contrast, Section 4803 does delineate a specific duty: upon receiving a report under Section 4802, the department is required to verify reports of depredation by a mountain lion within a strict timeframe and, if confirmed, to issue a permit to take the mountain lion. However, this duty is directed towards ensuring a response to private parties' reports of depredation, not towards mandating state action in controlling or taking the mountain lion itself. The statute assigns the responsibility for dealing with the mountain lion to the permit holder, not to the state. As detailed in Section 4806, once a permit is issued, the holder must report any action taken against a mountain lion and arrange for the turnover of the carcass to the department, demonstrating that it is the role of the private party rather than the state in addressing the specific threat.

The statutory duties outlined in these sections do not support a claim of negligence per se against the state as alleged in the complaint, where the state allegedly failed to take the mountain lion despite a prior report of the same mountain lion attacking a pedestrian's dog. The statutory framework is intended to facilitate the management of wildlife through engagement with private parties and local agencies, not to impose direct action requirements on the State itself. Consequently, Defendant’s negligence per se claim for violations of California Fish & Game Code §§ 1801, 4801, and 4803 fails as a matter of law. Thus, Plaintiff’s first cause of action is dismissed without leave to amend.

2.  Negligence and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

Butler v. City of Palos Verdes Estates (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 174 is on point procedurally and substantively in relation to Plaintiff’s claims for negligence and negligent infliction of emotional distress. In Butler, plaintiffs brought several causes of action including negligence and intentional infliction of emotional distress due to the City's allowance of a feral peafowl population to proliferate on city-owned lots. The court in Butler affirmed the decision to sustain the demurrer for the negligence and emotional distress claims, emphasizing that the state does not own wild animals nor does it control them. This principle was similarly underscored in Moerman v. State of California (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th at 452, 455-458, in which despite the state’s active management of relocated tule elk, including capturing, releasing, monitoring movements via ear tags and radio collars, attempting to move them off private property upon complaints, and preparing management plans involving monitoring, habitat improvement, sport hunting, and necessary relocation, the court found that the tule elk "are not instrumentalities of the state nor controlled by the state." Thus, Plaintiff’s second and third causes of action for negligence and negligent infliction of emotional distress are dismissed without leave to amend.

3.  Dangerous Condition of Public Property

Plaintiff asserts two theories regarding how the mountain lion constitutes a dangerous condition of public property. First, Plaintiff argues that the mountain lion itself is public property, and due to its history of prior attacks on humans and dogs, it is dangerous. Second, Plaintiff contends that the sidewalk in the residential area where Defendant was attacked is the public property, and the presence of the mountain lion created a dangerous condition on that property.

A “dangerous condition” is defined as “a condition of property that creates a substantial (as distinguished from a minor, trivial, or insignificant) risk of injury when such property is used with due care in a manner in which it is reasonably foreseeable that it will be used.” (Gov. Code, § 830, subd. (a)). The Court has determined that a mountain lion does not qualify as public property under this legal framework, since it is not a property intended for public use. Although there have been varying judicial interpretations in cases such as Arroyo v. State of California (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 755, Butler, supra, and Moerman v. State of California, supra, regarding whether wild animals are property of the state, these discussions do not address the Gov. Code § 830 analysis. This statute is specifically concerned with tangible public property like sidewalks that are designated for public use. Consequently, a mountain lion, which is neither intended for public use nor being used by the public, cannot be considered a public property under this statute.

However, a dangerous condition can exist not only when public property is physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective in such a way as to foreseeably endanger those using the property, but also when the location or property’s relationship to its surroundings endangers users. (Hernandez v. City of Stockton (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1222, 1230). Garcia v. American Golf Corp. (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 532 underscores this point.  In that case, the court denied summary judgment in a scenario where a plaintiff was injured by an errant golf ball while walking on a public pathway adjacent to a golf course, illustrating how the relationship of the property to its surroundings can create a dangerous condition.

Moreover, immunity under Gov Code Section 831.2 is inapplicable to the present case. Section 831.2 states that neither a public entity nor a public employee is liable for an injury caused by a natural condition of any unimproved public property, including but not limited to any natural condition of any lake, stream, bay, river, or beach. Given that the incident occurred on a sidewalk in a residential area, and not in a park, beach, or unimproved public property, the immunity provided under Section 831.2 does not apply.  Thus, at this early stage in the proceedings, Plaintiff's fourth cause of action is not dismissed.

Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ motion to dismiss is granted without leave to amend as to the first, second and third causes of action and denied as to the fourth cause of action.

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

Hearing Date: 5/23/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV23614 JOHN MERRIETT vs UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC 

Moving Party: Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER IS DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE

 

Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants Vincent Mack, Jr and Uber Technologies, Inc. for damages arising out of an automobile accident. Vincent Mack, Jr was driving for Uber at the time of the accident.

 

Uber moves for a protective order. Uber contends that Plaintiff has propounded form interrogatories, special interrogatories, requests for documents, and requests for admissions that seek to discover confidential information. Uber requests that the Court issue a protective order concurrently with the present motion. Upon reviewing the exhibits, the Court notes the absence of a proposed draft order that Uber intends for the Court to adopt. Although Uber has provided communications with Plaintiff that include a protective order attached, there are two versions, and it is unclear which version Uber wishes the Court to adopt, or if there is another version that was not attached but is intended for the Court's consideration.

 

Uber primarily relies on CCP §§2031.060, et seq., in support of its motion. “When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (a).) “The Court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” A protective order may direct “[t]hat the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling be made only on specified terms and conditions [or] [t]hat a trade secret or other confidential research, development, or commercial information not be disclosed, or be disclosed only to specified persons or only in a specified way.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subds. (b)(4)-(5).) Although Uber cites § CCP 2025.420, the statutory provision governing protective orders for depositions, Uber's motion does not reference any deposition being noticed nor does it attach a copy of the notice of deposition.

 

Per Civil Code section 3426.1, “Trade secret means information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (Civ. Code, § 3426.1.)

 

The issue with Uber's motion is that it is overly broad. Uber seeks a protective order for "documents, testimony, or information" (Motion at pg. 2) even though the motion was not even made in response to Plaintiff's notice of deposition. Of note, Uber’s notice of motion fails to indicate any authority, statutory or otherwise, upon which the motion is based for such broad protective order sought.

 

Uber cites case law concerning filing of documents under seal (In re Providian Credit Card Cases (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 292, 298-299. It also cites case law concerning a stipulated protective order (GT, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 748, 751-52). Uber cites case law concerning whether or not private financial information is subject to discovery (Dompeling v. Superior Court (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 798, 808-809). It does not, however, cite any authority permitting the Court to essentially require the parties to sign the court’s suggested stipulated protective order governing confidential information.

 

The Court notes that Uber argues various trial courts have granted Uber’s requested motion for a protective order. The Court is also aware of various trial court orders denying these motions. Trial court orders are, of course, not binding authority. Absent applicable statutory or appellate authority, the motion is denied.

 

Uber cites specific discovery requests and argues that these requests might disclose trade secrets, yet fails to justify the blanket protective order it seeks in the present motion, which pertains to all information, documents, or testimonies. Furthermore, during the meet and confer emails with opposing counsel, no arguments were communicated regarding how specific discovery requests might disclose trade secrets. Instead, Uber requested that opposing counsel sign a blanket protective order to obtain document production. Uber needs to meet and confer on how a specific discovery request will disclose trade secrets or why a protective order is necessary for the request sought. For example, in Uber’s meet and confer letter, Uber states that it will be producing documents responsive to certain requests for production without a protective order. However, Uber did not meet and confer with opposing counsel on why the rest of the documents or categories of documents require a protective order. Furthermore, adequate meet and confer efforts usually require an in-person meeting or a phone call.

 

The present motion is DENIED without prejudice for failing to comply with the meet and confer requirement, as well as for the ambiguity noted above regarding what specific protective order Uber is seeking

.

Uber is ordered to give notice.
