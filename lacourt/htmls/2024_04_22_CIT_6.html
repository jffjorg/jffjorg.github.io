DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00179    Hearing Date: April 22, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Pedro Gonzalez v. General Motors, LLC 

Defendant General Motors LLC’s Motion to Tax Counsel’s Memorandum of Costs 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS Defendant General Motors LLC’s motion to tax counsel’s memorandum of costs in part, by taxing $1,927.87 in printing related costs. The Court otherwise DENIES the motion. Plaintiff is therefore awarded costs in the amount of$9,914.66. 

                  Defendant shall submit a proposed order and is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.

BACKGROUND 

This is a lemon law case. On February 9, 2024, plaintiff Pedro Gonzalez (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendant General Motors, LLC (Defendant) and Does 1 through 50, alleging causes of action for violation of subdivision (d) of Civil Code section 1793.2, violation of subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 1793.2, violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code section 1793.2, Civil Code section 1791.2, breach of express written warranty, and breach of the implied warranty of merchantability. 

On February 20, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to tax costs. On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion. On April 11, 2024, Defendant replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD

“(1) Striking and taxing costs

Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).

(2) Form of motion

Unless objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion to strike or tax costs must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.

(3) Extensions of time

The party claiming costs and the party contesting costs may agree to extend the time for serving and filing the cost memorandum and a motion to strike or tax costs. This agreement must be confirmed in writing, specify the extended date for service, and be filed with the clerk. In the absence of an agreement, the court may extend the times for serving and filing the cost memorandum or the notice of motion to strike or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days.

(4) Entry of costs

After the time has passed for a motion to strike or tax costs or for determination of that motion, the clerk must immediately enter the costs on the judgment.”

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700, subd. (b).)

DISCUSSION 

Summary of Arguments 

Defendant seeks to tax the three following sets of costs:  (1) $150.00 in jury fees; (2) $1,927.87 in printing related costs; and (3) $388.77 in filing and motion fees. Defendant contends Plaintiff bears the burden of showing that the costs were necessary and reasonable. Defendant argues it should not be forced to reimburse the jury fees because this case never went to trial. Defendant then contends the printing fees relating to courtesy copies are improper, unreasonable, excessive, and that a worksheet or verification of the alleged costs were not attached to the memorandum of costs. Defendant further contends it should not be required to reimburse Plaintiff in costs related to various motions and Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees before that latter motion was filed, that the motion to compel related costs were optional and the motion practice was one-sided, and that it should not be required to pay for filing fees related to a notice of change of address, rejection notice, and re-filing. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends the burden is on Defendant to show the charges are unreasonable if the items on their face appear to be proper. Plaintiff contends the law is clear that jury fees, costs related to courtesy copies, and costs attributable for reservations of hearings and motion filings are recoverable as costs. Plaintiff contends Defendant must provide more than a subjective opinion that the costs are unreasonable, that Defendant has not provided sufficient evidence to show Plaintiff’s claimed costs are unreasonable, and that Defendant’s actions led to the increased costs at issue. 

Analysis           

            “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. [Citations.] Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion. [Citation.] However, because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute [citation] a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774, italics in original.) 

            The Court finds that some of the items on the memorandum of costs do not appear to be proper charges, namely the printing related costs and the hearing transcripts. (See Memorandum of Costs, Attachment 16; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(3) and (a)(9) [“Postage, telephone, and photocopying charges, except for exhibits” excluded as costs; “Transcripts of court proceedings ordered by the court” allowable as costs.]) Thus, the burden is on Plaintiff to prove they are reasonable. (See Ladas, supra,  19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.) The Court finds Plaintiff has provided little to no evidence to justify these costs, citing only the general proposition that prevailing buyers in Song-Beverly Act claims are entitled to recover “costs” and “expenses.” (Jensen v. BMW of North America, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 137; Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).) This is insufficient considering the clear language of Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (b)(3). Moreover, there is no evidence provided showing that the Court ordered transcripts of law and motion hearings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(9).) Therefore, the Court GRANTS the motion to tax costs as to the $1,927.87 in costs. 

            As for the jury fees and motion related fees, the Court finds them to be proper on their face, and thus the burden is on Defendant to prove they are not reasonable. (See Ladas, supra,  19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774.) “Filing, motion, and jury fees” are explicitly listed as recoverable costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).) The Court finds Defendant’s arguments against them here to be unpersuasive. Defendant cites no legal authority for the proposition that the case must have gone to trial for the prevailing party to recover the $150.00 jury deposit. Defendant’s arguments regarding motion related fees are also unpersuasive because they are conclusory, conjectural, and not supported by any evidence or citations to legal authority. The Court therefore DENIES Defendant’s motion to tax costs as to jury fees and motion related fees. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant General Motors LLC’s motion to tax counsel’s memorandum of costs in part, by taxing $1,927.87 in printing related costs. The Court otherwise DENIES the motion. Plaintiff is therefore awarded costs in the amount of$9,914.66 

             Defendant shall submit a proposed order and is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.

Case Number: 22PSCV00257    Hearing Date: April 22, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  City of El Monte v. Lino James Ibarra, et al.

Defendant Tucker Lincoln’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint of Plaintiff City of El Monte 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Tucker Lincoln’s demurrer to First Amended Complaint of Plaintiff City of El Monte, as to Defendant Tucker Lincoln, without leave to amend. Defendant Lincoln may submit a proposed judgment for the Court’s review and signature. 

The Court DENIES Defendant Tucker Lincoln’s motion to strike as moot. 

Defendant Tucker Lincoln is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling and file proof of service of same within five days of the Court’s order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a nuisance action relating to illegal sales of cannabis. On March 16, 2022, plaintiff City of El Monte (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Lino James Ibarra (Ibarra), Michael Atencio, Manuel Aquino, Joseph Hernandez, Jung Hsiang Chen, Ya Fen Hsu, Tucker Lincoln (Lincoln), Philip Gonzales, Shane Sapp, H.C. Rue Royale Partner LLC (collectively, Defendants) and Does 1 through 100, alleging causes of action for narcotics abatement (Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq.), public nuisance (Civil Code section 3479 et seq.), violation of El Monte Municipal Code (EMMC Chapters 5.18 and Ch. 1.19), violation of unfair competition law (Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.), and violation of MAUCRSA (Business and Professions Code section 26000 et seq.) 

On January 18, 2024, after the Court granted Lincoln’s motion for judgment on the pleadings, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same Defendants and alleging causes of action for narcotics abatement (Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq.), public nuisance (Civil Code section 3479 et seq.), violation of El Monte Municipal Code (EMMC Chapters 5.18 and Ch. 1.19), violation of unfair competition law (Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.), and violation of MAUCRSA (Business and Professions Code section 26000 et seq.) 

On March 18, 2024, Lincoln demurred to and moved to strike portions of the FAC. On April 9, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the demurrer and motion to strike. On April 15, 2024, Lincoln replied to both oppositions. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Demurrer 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (Code Civ. Proc., § 422.10; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (Id.) 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862, disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287 [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) 

A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery. (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-972.) The “face of the complaint” includes material contained in attached exhibits that are incorporated by reference into the complaint, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94; see also Barnett v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 500, 505 [“[W]e rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader’s allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”]) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE – Demurrer           

The Court GRANTS Lincoln’s request for judicial notice. (See Evid. Code § 452, subd. (d).) The Court takes judicial notice of the foregoing documents only as to “the existence, content and authenticity of public records and other specified documents”; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400.) However, the Court does not find any contradictory statements in Exhibit 4 to be considered in ruling on the demurrer. While the responses to Special Interrogatory No. 1 do not specifically mention activity in the City of El Monte, the response states that Tucker Lincoln operated an unlawful THC dispensary…in a conspiracy to distribute the product beyond the business located at the property in Covina. 

MEET AND CONFER – Demurrer

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the parties were required to meet and confer telephonically or in person before bringing this motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The Court finds the parties’ meet-and-confer efforts to be sufficient. (Kashfian Decl., ¶¶ 11-12.)

DISCUSSION – Demurrer           

Standing – Location of Conduct           

A municipal corporation has generally no extraterritorial powers of regulation. It may not exercise its governmental functions beyond its corporate boundaries. (See Const., art. XI, sec. 11; Oakland v. Brock (1937) 8 Cal.2d 639, 641. Further, “an action to abate a nuisance may be taken by the city attorney or city prosecutor of the city within which the nuisance exists, is kept, or is maintained.” (Health & Saf. Code, § 11571.5 (emphasis added).) Plaintiff contends that the fact that the offending conduct originates outside of a city’s boundaries does not necessarily undermine that city’s ability to sue for nuisance. (People v. City of Los Angeles (1958) 160 Cal.App.2d 494, 507 [“Respondent attacks this cause of action upon the grounds that by it, the city of Manhattan Beach seeks to give extraterritorial effect to its ordinance and therefore does not state a cause of action. This contention cannot be sustained.”]; see also People v. Selby Smelting and Lead Co. (1912) 163. Cal. 84, 88 (Selby Smelting) [nuisance law governs the effect of one’s actions on the enjoyment of life or property another; it matters not where the nuisance originated].) Plaintiff argues that just because the allegedly offending conduct occurred in Covina and West Covina does not categorically mean it cannot have been a nuisance that interfered with Plaintiff’s rights within its boundaries. 

Defendant Lincoln contends the entire FAC fails because Plaintiff is not a real party in interest.[1] Lincoln contends Plaintiff’s causes of action in the FAC are all based on the use of specific properties in Covina and West Covina, neither of which are located in the City of El Monte, and therefore undermines Plaintiff’s claims here. Lincoln contends the exclusive power to pursue these causes of action is vested in another, and that Plaintiff has not suffered an injury entitling it to relief. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends it has standing for nuisance abatement actions that extend beyond city boundaries. Plaintiff contends that although the source of the illegal marijuana activities were based on properties outside of the City of El Monte, the nuisance existed in and affected Plaintiff and its community. Plaintiff cites an incident alleged in the FAC regarding Defendant Ibarra having been pulled over by the El Monte Police Department in which Ibarra was found in possession of certain marijuana products allegedly manufactured by the Defendants and financed by Lincoln. Plaintiff also contends its injury is alleged in the FAC, as Plaintiff alleges that Lincoln was involved in distributing, storing, and selling marijuana in the City of El Monte. 

The Court agrees with Defendant Lincoln. The FAC does not contain any factual allegations showing that Lincoln was selling unlicensed marijuana products in the City of El Monte. The only specific incident alleged to have occurred in the City of El Monte is the search of Defendant Ibarra’s vehicle, i.e., not Lincoln’s, after having been pulled over for having illegal tinted windows in his vehicle and a subsequent search of that vehicle discovered some of the allegedly illegal marijuana products manufactured by Lincoln. (FAC, ¶ 45.) This allegation does not demonstrate Lincoln sold illegal marijuana products within the City of El Monte. It does not even demonstrate that Ibarra sold them. At most, it demonstrates that the illegal products were found during a search of his vehicle that happened to occur within the City of El Monte. 

Moreover, the FAC does not allege any other specific instances of alleged sales of illegal marijuana products within the City of El Monte. Instead, the FAC contains speculative allegations of such sales activity. (See FAC, ¶¶ 8-9, 21, 29, 40-45, 44, 48; Serrano v. Priest (1970) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591 [court treats the demurrer as admitting all properly pleaded material facts, “but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].”]) The FAC otherwise alleges that the illegal manufacturing occurred at properties and locations outside of the City of El Monte. (FAC, ¶¶ 24-49.) 

As the Court previously found in its prior ruling on Lincoln’s motion for judgment on the pleadings, the FAC fails to allege any specific facts showing the specific effect of that conduct on Plaintiff. (Order re Tentative Ruling (12/13/23).) Plaintiff fails to allege facts, as opposed to conclusions, showing how the alleged unlawful conduct affected or created a nuisance in the City of El Monte. While governmental entities like Plaintiff have strong interests in regulating controlled substances within their boundaries, the FAC fails to allege any conduct or nuisance within its boundaries. Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiff lacks standing to assert these claims. 

The Court also notes that Plaintiff did not make an offer of proof as to how these defects can be corrected. (See Opp., 13:20-22.) Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the entire complaint as to Defendant Lincoln without leave to amend. 

First Cause of Action – Narcotics Abatement (Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq.) 

“Every building or place used for the purpose of unlawfully selling, serving, storing, keeping, manufacturing, or giving away any controlled substance, precursor, or analog specified in this division, and every building or place wherein or upon which those acts take place, is a nuisance which shall be enjoined, abated, and prevented, and for which damages may be recovered, whether it is a public or private nuisance.” (Health & Saf. Code, § 11570.)

Defendant Lincoln demurs to the First Cause of Action for narcotics abatement for failure to state a cause of action. Lincoln reiterates the argument from above that none of the allegedly offending conduct occurred within the boundaries of the City of El Monte and therefore lacks standing. In opposition, Plaintiff contends it has standing to bring this cause of action because the effects of the nuisance are what matters, not the source, citing the Selby Smelting case. 

For the same reasons set forth above, the Court agrees with Lincoln. A narcotics abatement action under Health and Safety Code section 11570 is limited to unlawful activities occurring in the specific buildings where those acts took place. (Health & Saf. Code, § 11570.) The FAC alleges that the buildings at issue are located in West Covina and Covina, i.e., not El Monte. (See FAC, ¶¶ 24-49.) 

As for the rule set forth in Selby Smelting regarding the effects of a nuisance versus its origin, this still does not solve the pleading problems of the FAC. Selby Smelting did not address Health and Safety Code section 11570. (See generally Selby Smelting, supra, 163. Cal. 84.) Either way, the FAC still fails to allege facts demonstrating the occurrence of any unlawful conduct, i.e., Lincoln illegally selling or distributing marijuana within the City of El Monte, for purposes of a narcotics abatement action within the City of El Monte. 

Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS Lincoln’s demurrer to the First Cause of Action without leave to amend. 

            Second Cause of Action – Public Nuisance (Civil Code section 3479 et seq.)           

“Anything which is injurious to health, including, but not limited to, the illegal sale of controlled substances, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, or unlawfully obstructs the free passage or use, in the customary manner, of any navigable lake, or river, bay, stream, canal, or basin, or any public park, square, street, or highway, is a nuisance.” (Civ. Code § 3479.) 

            Defendant Lincoln demurs to the Second Cause of Action for public nuisance for failure to state a cause of action. Lincoln contends to the extent that Plaintiff is bringing this action in its representative capacity rather than as a private party, it has failed to allege any special injury entitling it to relief. Lincoln reiterates the argument that Plaintiff lacks standing to bring this cause of action because the alleged nuisance is based on properties outside of El Monte, and that the FAC pleads a section of the Civil Code which applies only to properties. In opposition, Plaintiff reiterates its argument that what matters is the effect of the nuisance and not the source.[2] 

             For the same reasons set forth above with respect to the First Cause of Action, the Court agrees with Lincoln that the FAC fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for public nuisance. Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS Lincoln’s demurrer to the Second Cause of Action without leave to amend.

            Third Cause of Action – Violation of El Monte Municipal Code (EMMC Chapters 5.18 and Ch. 1.19)           

El Monte Municipal Code section 5.18.040 states that, “[n]o person may engage in any commercial cannabis activity within the city unless the person (1) has a valid commercial cannabis license issued by the city; (2) has a valid state license; (3) any other applicable approvals, including, but not limited to, a building permit and city business license, and (4) is currently in compliance with all applicable state and local laws and regulations pertaining to the commercial cannabis activity.” (El Monte Municipal Code § 5.18.040; FAC, ¶ 73.) 

            Defendant Lincoln demurs to the Third Cause of Action for violation of the El Monte Municipal Code for failure to state a cause of action. Lincoln contends the FAC fails to allege sufficient facts here because, as set forth above, the allegedly unlawful conduct at issue occurred outside of the City of El Monte, and the City of El Monte cannot regulate licensing for properties outside of its boundaries. In opposition, Plaintiff cites the allegations of distribution, storing, and selling illegal marijuana in the City. 

            The Court agrees with Lincoln. As set forth above, the FAC fails to allege any facts demonstrating the illegal sale, storage, or distribution of marijuana by Lincoln within the City of El Monte. (See FAC, ¶¶ 8, 9, 24-49; Serrano, supra, 5 Cal.3d at p. 591.) 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Third Cause of Action without leave to amend. 

            Fourth Cause of Action – Violation of Unfair Competition Law (Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.) 

            “The UCL prohibits any unfair, unlawful, or fraudulent business act or practice. [Citation.] Only the first two types of practices are at issue here. A practice is unlawful under the UCL where it violates a predicate law.” (CADC/RADC Venture 2011-1 LLC v. Bradley (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 775, 792-793.) 

            Defendant Lincoln demurs to the Fourth Cause of Action for unfair competition for failure to state a cause of action. Lincoln contends the FAC fails to state a cause of action because it does not state facts showing both an injury in fact and a loss of money or property entitling Plaintiff to restitution, and Plaintiff therefore lacks standing. Lincoln contends Plaintiff did not suffer an injury in fact and it did not lose money or property to unfair competition. Lincoln also contends this claim fails because Plaintiff cannot claim it is entitled to money obtained from illegal marijuana sales. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends it has standing to bring an unfair competition law claim against Lincoln because Business and Professions Code section 17204 applies to injunctions, not restitution, by persons who have suffered an injury in fact, and because a city attorney is allowed to pursue an action on behalf of Plaintiff, as Plaintiff is doing here. Plaintiff contends it was injured by alleged illegal marijuana sales, storage, and distribution within the City of El Monte, and by incurring law enforcement costs. 

            The Court finds the FAC fails to plead sufficient facts to state a cause of action for unfair competition, albeit not necessarily for the reasons addressed in the parties’ respective briefs. The FAC predicates the Fourth Cause of Action squarely on violations of Health and Safety Code section 11570 et seq. and El Monte Municipal Code Chapter 5.18. (FAC, ¶¶ 82-84.) The FAC also alleges a violation based on the federal Controlled Substances Act under 21 U.S.C. section 856, (FAC, ¶ 84), which involves criminal punishments and civil fines for maintaining a drug-involved premises, which does not confer a private right of action, (see 21 U.S.C. § 856). The Court therefore understands the FAC to be alleging a representative cause of action here rather than a private cause of action, contrary to Lincoln’s arguments in the demurrer. (See Mem. Points & Authorities, 19:26-20:9.) The Court finds the Fourth Cause of Action to be duplicative of the First and Third Causes of Action, both of which fail for the reasons set forth above. Since those causes of action fail, the Fourth Cause of Action necessarily fails too. The FAC fails to allege any facts, as opposed to conclusions, showing a violation of Business and Professions Codes sections 17200 by Defendant Tucker in the City of El Monte or causing an injury in fact to the City of El Monte. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action without leave to amend. 

            Fifth Cause of Action – Violation of MAUCRSA (Business and Professions Code section 26000 et seq.) 

“A person engaging in commercial cannabis activity without a license as required by this division shall be subject to civil penalties of up to three times the amount of the license fee for each violation. Each day of operation shall constitute a separate violation of this section.” (Bus. & Prof. Code § 26038, subd. (a)(1).)[3] “Actions for civil penalties pursuant to paragraph (1) of subdivision (a) may be brought by the Attorney General on behalf of the people, on behalf of the department, or on behalf of the participating agency, or by a city or county counsel or city prosecutor.” (Id., 26038, subd. (c)(1).) 

            Defendant Lincoln demurs to the Fifth Cause of Action for violation of MAUCRSA for failure to state a cause of action. Lincoln contends Plaintiff does not have standing to enforce the Medicinal and Adult Use Cannabis Regulation and Safety Act (MAUCRSA), because its enforcement is left primarily to the California Department of Cannabis Control, and because city attorneys in cities with less than 750,000 people are only allowed to seek civil penalties for unlicensed marijuana operations under Business and Professions Code section 26038, subdivisions (a)(1) and (a)(3). Lincoln further contends that the city attorney has authority to enforce commercial marijuana licensing violations within its own boundaries, but not outside its city limits. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends it has standing to bring this action under Business and Professions Code section 26038, subdivision (a)(1), and that subdivision (e)(4) does not apply. Plaintiff cites the search of Defendant Ibarra’s vehicle as demonstrating marijuana being illegally manufactured by Lincoln and the Defendants and financed by Lincoln as evidence of a violation of MAUCRSA within the City of El Monte. 

            The Court agrees with Lincoln. The FAC fails to allege facts demonstrating that Plaintiff has standing to bring this cause of action. The allegations regarding the search of Defendant Ibarra’s vehicle fail to demonstrate the occurrence of marijuana being illegally manufactured in the City of El Monte, for the reasons set forth above. (See FAC, ¶ 45.) The Court also notes that Plaintiff’s opposition did not respond to the argument that enforcement of MAUCRSA is left primarily to the California Department of Cannabis Control, which the Court construes as a tacit admission that the argument is meritorious. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410; C. Opposing the Motion—and Rebutting the Opposition, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 9(I)-C, ¶ 9:105.10. 

In addition, Plaintiff’s contention that it is bringing this action under subdivision (a)(1) of Business and Professions Code section 26038 is unavailing, since the FAC alleges the violation is based on subdivision (a)(2) for aiding and abetting, which means subdivision (e)(4) does apply. (FAC, ¶ 91; see Bus. & Prof. Code, § 26038, subd. (e)(4).) Subdivision (a)(2) provides that such claims “shall be brought exclusively by the Attorney General on behalf of the people, on behalf of the department, or on behalf of the participating agency, or by a city or county counsel or city prosecutor in a city or county having a population in excess of 750,000.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 26038, subd. (e)(4).) The FAC does not allege that Plaintiff is a city with a population in excess of 750,000. Thus, Plaintiff lacks standing to bring a claim for aiding and abetting. 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action without leave to amend. 

LEGAL STANDARD – Motion to Strike 

            “Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(2).) “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id., § 436.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE – Motion to Strike           

The Court GRANTS Lincoln’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1, 2, and 3, but DENIES as to Exhibit 4 for the motion to strike. (See Evid. Code § 452, subd. (d).) The Court takes judicial notice of the foregoing documents only as to “the existence, content and authenticity of public records and other specified documents”; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400.) 

MEET AND CONFER – Motion to Strike 

Per Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a), the parties were required to meet and confer telephonically or in person before bringing this motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).) The Court finds the parties’ meet-and-confer efforts to be sufficient. (Kashfian Decl., ¶¶ 11-12.) 

DISCUSSION – Motion to Strike 

            In light of the Court sustaining the demurrer without leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as moot. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Tucker Lincoln’s demurrer to First Amended Complaint of Plaintiff City of El Monte, as to Defendant Tucker Lincoln, without leave to amend. Defendant Lincoln may submit a proposed judgment for the Court’s review and signature. 

The Court DENIES Defendant Tucker Lincoln’s motion to strike as moot.  

Defendant Tucker Lincoln is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling and file proof of service of same within five days of the Court’s order.

This should have been specified in the demurrer. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1320, subd. (a).) The Court will still consider this argument, but admonishes Lincoln to comply with the requirements of the California Rules of Court going forward.

Case Number: 22PSCV00369    Hearing Date: April 22, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Ralf Weigel v. Terrence Sperry 

Motion to be Relieved as Defendant’s Counsel 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court GRANTS the motion to be relieved as counsel. 

             Counsel is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a breach of contract action. On April 13, 2022, plaintiff Ralf Weigel (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendant Terrence Sperry (Defendant) and Does 1 to 10, alleging causes of action for breach of contract, conversion, replevin, and declaratory relief. Defendant filed an answer on September 26, 2022. 

On December 21, 2023, Defendant filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff and Roes 1 through 20, alleging causes of action for failure to pay overtime, willful misclassification of employee as an independent contractor, failure to provide meal and rest breaks, waiting time penalties, failure to reimburse expenses, and conversion. 

            On April 2, 2024, Charles P. Boylston of the Law Offices of Charles P. Boylston, APC (Counsel), moved to be relieved as counsel for Defendant. The motion is unopposed. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted, provided that there is no prejudice to the client, and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.)

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

DISCUSSION           

            Counsel moves to be relieved as counsel for Defendant on the grounds that conflicts have arisen, along with a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship and a breach of an obligation to Counsel’s office. (Boylston Decl., Attachment 2, ¶¶ 3-5.) The Court finds these to be proper grounds for withdrawal. Grounds for permitting an attorney to withdraw from representation include the client’s conduct that “renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively[.]” (Cal. Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16, subd. (b)(4).) A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is also grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) 

            The Court finds that Counsel’s motion and accompanying documents address the issues set forth in the Court’s March 27, 2024 order denying the prior motion to be relieved as counsel without prejudice. (Order re Tentative Ruling (3/27/24).) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS the motion to be relieved as counsel. 

             Counsel is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.
