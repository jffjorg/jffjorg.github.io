DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call.

 Case No.:          20STCV27665 [Lead Case] and 23STCV19070 [Butte/Related Case]

 Hearing Date:   6/11/24

 Trial Date:        8/20/24 [Both Actions]

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendants’ Motion to Determine if the Trustee of Terminated Trusts Can Self-Represent, Re: Lead Case [Res ID # 1132]; and

Defendants’ Motion to Determine if the Trustee of Terminated Trusts Can Self-Represent, Re: Related Case [Res ID # 0514].

 

I. Background

Based on the parties’ share surname (Norlund), for ease of discussion, the Court refers to the parties by their first names. No disrespect is intended.

A. Relevant Procedural History

On May 2, 2024, in LASC action 20STCV27665 (the Lead Case), Richard and Sandra Norlund filed a pro se Order to Show Cause (OSC) brief regarding Richard’s ability to self-represent three of the trusts for which Richard is being sued as trustee: (1) Successor Trustee of “Trust A” of the “Norlund Family Trust dated March 16, 1987” (Trust A); (2) Successor Trustee of “Trust B” of the “Norlund Family Trust dated March 16, 1987” (Trust B); and (3) Trustee of “Richard L. Norlund Separate Property Trust dated December 11, 1997” (Richard’s SPT). A hearing was set for May 9, 2024.

That same day, in LASC action 23STCV19070 (the Butte Case), Richard Norlund filed a pro se Order to Show Cause (OSC) brief regarding his ability to self-represent Trust A, Trust B, and Richard’s SPT. A hearing was also set for May 9, 2024.

The two briefs are identical in substance, but Richard and Sharon Norlund filed the brief in the Lead Case—a case in which Richard and Sharon are Defendants—while Richard alone filed the brief in the Butte Case—a case in which Richard is a Defendant but Sharon is not.

On May 3, 2024, Sandra Norlund filed a “reply”—in effect an opposition—to the May 2nd brief filed by Richard and Sharon Norlund in the Lead Case.

On May 6, 2024, in the Lead Case, Richard and Sharon Norlund filed a reply to Sandra Norlund’s May 3rd “reply,” as filed by Sandra in the Lead Case.

That same day, in the Butte Case, Richard filed a reply to Sandra Norlund’s May 3rd “reply,” as filed by Sandra in the Lead Case. (No similar “reply” by Sandra appears in the Court’s record for the Butte Case.)

On May 9, 2024, the Court heard the ex parte applications and ruled: “… Richard Norlund and Sandra Norlund can represent their own interests as beneficiaries but cannot represent a trust to the extent there are additional beneficiaries of that trust. They may represent a Trust to the extent they are the only beneficiaries.”

B. Motions Before the Court

On May 10, 2024, in the Lead Case, Richard and Sandra Norlund filed a pro per ex parte application to advance hearings on a forthcoming motion to self-represent Trust A, Trust B, and Richard’s SPT, then scheduled for July 18, 2024.

That same day, Richard filed a pro per ex parte application in the Butte Case that was identical in substance to the ex parte application filed in the Lead Case.

Richard and Sharon’s motion in the Lead Case and Richard’s motion in the Butte Case are now before the Court.

 

II. Motions for Leave to Self-Represent, Lead Case and Butte Case: GRANTED in part and DEFERRED TO ORAL ARGUMENT in part.

A. Preliminary Note – Identical Briefing, Single Ruling

Given that the briefing for all the papers before the Court is identical as between the two cases—the Lead Case and the Butte Case—the Court provides a single analysis of the arguments presented by the Norlund Defendants and Sandra Norlund.

No briefing from Nancy Hymas is before the Court.

B. Relevant Law

“No person shall practice law in California unless the person is an active licensee of the State Bar.” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6125.)

“A non-attorney trustee who represents the trust in court is representing and affecting the interests of the beneficiary and is thus engaged in the unauthorized practice of law. (§ 6125.)” (Ziegler v. Nickel (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 545, 549, fn. omitted.)

“But if a sole trustee is also the trust’s sole settlor and beneficiary, the rationale of these cases ceases to apply: no interests are at stake except those of one person.” (Aulisio v. Bancroft (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 1516, 1519 (Aulisio); Boshernitsan v. Bach (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 883, 893-894 [same].)

C. Court’s Past Ruling

On May 9, 2024, the Court heard the May 2nd ex parte applications and ruled: “… Richard Norlund and Sandra Norlund can represent their own interests as beneficiaries but cannot represent a trust to the extent there are additional beneficiaries of that trust. They may represent a Trust to the extent they are the only beneficiaries.”

D. Court’s Determination

The question before the Court is whether Richard Norlund may represent Trust A, Trust B, and Richard’s SPT despite not being a licensed attorney in the State of California.

The Court finds that Richard and Sharon Norlund have partially made a sufficient showing supporting self-representation.

The moving papers contain few, if any, arguments in favor of Richard’s self-representation over Richard’s SPT. The papers instead focus on Trust A and Trust B. For this reason, the Court cannot determine, for example, whether entry into the 2019 settlement agreement and its subsequent execution (including assignment of promissory note) in fact fulfilled the purposes of Richard’s SPT. (Prob. Code, § 15407, subds. (a), (a)(2).) Moreover, the requests for judicial notice filed on May 6, 2024, by the Norlund Defendants include copies of Trust A and Trust B (with amendment) but not Richard’s SPT. It may be that the parties do not really disagree as to the SPT, but without further information and documents relating to Richard’s SPT, the Court’s cannot say that Richard’s SPT has a single settlor, trustee, and beneficiary as to satisfy Aulisio.  The Court will invite clarification at oral argument as to whether Sandra disagrees about the SPT.

As to Trust A and Trust B, the settlement agreement between the parties made Richard the sole trustee over Trust A and Trust B. (20STCV27665, 3/20/24 SAC, Ex. R [settlement agreement documents, including Exhibit A, which at Settlement Terms Relating to Trust A, § 2, and Settlement Terms Relating to Trust B, § 4, make Richard Norlund the sole trustee over Trusts A and B].) Sections 14 and 10 in the Settlement Terms Relating to Trust A and to Trust B make Richard sole beneficiary of the residual property acquired by Trusts A and B that was not otherwise distributed by the settlement agreement, in effect making Richard the sole beneficiary of Trust A and B following the execution of the settlement. (20STCV27665, 3/20/24 SAC, Ex. R, Ex. A.) The settlors of Trust A and B were the parents of Richard Norlund and Nancy Hymas, who are deceased. (5/3/24 RJNs, Exs. 14, Trust B, p. 1 [Trust made by Ernest and Gertrude Norlund] & Ex. 15, Trust A Restatement, p. 1 [same]; 20STCV27665, 5/30/24 Exhibits, Exs. 3-4 [Ernest’s and Getrude’s Certificates of Death].) Under these circumstances, the Court agrees that with the passing of the settlors, Richard is the sole trustee and beneficiary of Trust A and Trust B, and there is no interest at stake in representing Trust A and Trust B other than Richard’s own interests. This showing satisfies Aulisio, allowing the Court to GRANT Richard and Sharon’s motion in the Lead Case and Richard’s motion in the Butte Case as relates to self-representation over Trust A and Trust B. 

III. Conclusion

A. Motion for Leave to Self-Represent, Lead Case

Defendants’ Motion to Determine if the Trustee of Terminated Trusts Can Self-Represent, Re: Lead Case [Res ID # 1132] is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part as follows:

(1) GRANTED as to self-representation by Richard Norlund over Trust A and Trust B; and

(2 Deferred until oral argument as to self-representation by Richard Norlund over Richard’s SPT.

B. Motion for Leave to Self-Represent, Butte Case

Defendants’ Motion to Determine if the Trustee of Terminated Trusts Can Self-Represent, Re: Lead Case [Res ID # 0514] is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part as follows:

(1) GRANTED as to self-representation by Richard Norlund over Trust A and Trust B; and

(2) Deferred until oral argument as to self-representation by Richard Norlund over Richard’s SPT.

Case Number: 23STCV07389    Hearing Date: June 11, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

CALIMA ELECTRIC, INC., a California corporation,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

THE HATCH GROUP, INC., a California corporation; ROLLING GREENS NURSERY, INC., a California corporation; SL RETAIL OWNER LLC, a Delaware limited liability company; and DOES 1 THROUGH 100, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV07389

 Hearing Date:   6/11/24

 Trial Date:        11/12/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Calima Electric, Inc.’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant The Hatch Group, Inc.’s Employee Hector Vasques and the Production of Documents Described in the Notice of Deposition; and

Plaintiff Calima Electric, Inc.’s Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against The Hatch Group, Inc., Hector Vasques and Their Attorney of Record, Neda Cate, Esq., in the Sum of $3,750.

 

I. Background

A. Relevant Procedural History

On January 2, 2024, January 16, 2024, and January 19, 2024, Calima Electric requested, in writing, that THG provide dates for the scheduling of depositions of THG’s employees Shanon Johnson and Hector Vasques. Calima contends that Shanon Johnson has been THG’s PMQ, and that THG has identified Johnson and Vasques as witnesses in this action.

In response, THG failed to provide any deposition dates.

Accordingly, on January 30, 2024, Calima served deposition notices noticing the deposition of Shanon Johnson for February 22, 2024, and the deposition of Hector Vasques for February 28, 2024. Both deposition notices included 15 categories of document for production.

On February 15, 2024, THG’s counsel Neda Cate, Esq. served objections to Plaintiff’s deposition notices. The objections claimed that neither the witness nor THG’s attorney of record were available on the scheduled deposition dates. THG also generally and specifically objected to each of the 15 categories of documents that were requested in Plaintiff’s deposition notices.

On February 20, 2024, Calima wrote to THG and agreed to continue the depositions of Shanon Johnson and Hector Vasques and requested that THG provide alternative deposition dates by Friday, February 23, 2024. The letter also explained why THG’s objections to Calima’s production requests were defective.

THG failed to respond to Calima’s February 20, 2024, letter or to provide any dates for the depositions or agree to produce any documents responsive to Calima’s deposition notices.

On March 6, 2024, Calima once again wrote to THG and requested deposition dates.

That same day, Neda Cate responded indicating that she would consult with the clients and respond.

THG and Neda Cate failed to follow-up or provide any deposition dates.

On March 13, 2024, Calima again reached out to THG and requested deposition dates.

That same day, Neda Cate replied by stating that she had not gotten dates from the clients and would continue to reach out to them. Neda Cate added that if Calima felt it had to file a motion to compel, Cate would “understand.”

As of April 15, 2024, THG had still failed to produce Hector Vasques or produce any documents in response to the deposition notice for Hector Vasques.

The above summary is derived from the moving papers and is not disputed in substance in the oppositions before the Court.

B. Motion Before the Court

On April 15, 2024, based on the non-production of Hector Vasques and the requested documents, Plaintiff Calima Electric filed a motion to compel the deposition of and production from Hector Vasques, plus a request for monetary sanctions against THG, Hector Vasques, and counsel, Neda Cate, Esq. The motion contends that the deposition of Hector Vasques is sought because THG identified Vasques as a person with information surrounding the construction project from which this dispute arose.

On May 9, 2024, THG filed a combined opposition to Plaintiff’s motion and to a similar motion heard and granted in relation to Shanon Johnson on May 22, 2024. The opposition almost exclusively relied on arguments that, in essence, THG was attempting to settle the case through an April 18, 2024, mediation with Rolling Greens, which Plaintiff did not join, causing the mediation to fail, after which THG decided to “now … focus[] on litigating this case,” giving Calima dates for the depositions of Shanon Johnson and Hector Vasques (May or June 2024). THG argued that the dates provided to Calima mooted Calima’s motion and that sanctions would be unjust where THG was attempting to resolve this case rather than filing motions.

On May 29, 2024—one week after the court granted the separate motion related to Shanon Johnson on May 22, 2024—THG filed a second opposition arguing that the motion to compel the deposition of Hector Vasques should be denied because after good faith efforts, counsel has discovered that Hector Vasques no longer works for THG, making the deposition notice ineffective as to THG and necessitating a deposition subpoena of Hector Vasques. THG also argues that sanctions are not merited because counsel operated with the best information they had until counsel discovered that Hector Vasques no longer worked for THG.

On June 3, 2024, Calima filed a reply that argues that this motion should be granted as to compelling the deposition of Hector Vasques insofar as counsel for THG is not credible. Calima argues in the alternative that if the Court determines that Hector Vasques can no longer be deposed according to the deposition notice served on THG, then THG should be ordered to disclose Mr. Vasquez’s last known contact information to Calima. Calima last argues that sanctions are merited because Calima’s counsel requested that THG’s counsel confirm Hector Vasques’s employment as early as January 2024 and counsel never confirmed Hector Vasques’s non-employment until May 29, 2024, well after counsel had served objections to the deposition notice on February 15, 2024, stretching credibility.

Calima’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Compel Deposition and Request for Sanctions

A. Motion to Compel Deposition: DENIED in part and GRANTED.

1. Legal Standard

If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

2. Court’s Determination

THG has filed a declaration from counsel, in which counsel relates receiving information from THG on May 29, 2024, to the effect that Hector Vasques is no longer a THG employee. (Opp’n, Cate, Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Even though the reply challenges the veracity of this representation (see, e.g., Reply, pp. 2-3), there are no separately filed objections in the record based on foundation, hearsay, or other grounds. There is therefore no serious challenge to the Cate declaration and its position that Hector Vasques is no longer a THG employee, other than veracity as discussed above. The Court credits THG’s reply and its evidence in the form of counsel’s declaration. Relying on this representation, the Court determines that because THG has no control over Hector Vasques, there is, as of May 29, 2024, no statutory basis to compel the deposition of Hector Vasques pursuant to the January 30, 2024, deposition notice.

Calima’s motion is thus DENIED as relates to compelling the deposition of Hector Vasques based on the January 30, 2024, deposition notice.

The Court also DENIES the motion as relates to the production of documents.

The production requests are addressed to Hector Vasques for documents in his custody and control, not in THG’s. (Mot., Gharakhani Decl., Ex. 3, pp. 2 [defining You as Hector Vasques], 5-7 [production requests].) It is not clear how the Court can order THG to produce documents that were supposed to be under Hector Vasques’s control. The reply highlights the lack of clarity. (See, e.g., Reply, p. 7 [“It is unclear, how THG can either produce documents in Mr. Vasques’ custody or compel Mr. Vasques to produce responsive documents if Mr. Vasques is not under THG’s ‘control.’ THG’s Opposition and Cate’s declaration are also silent on this issue.”].)

On balance, the Court agrees with the opposition: To obtain documents from Hector Vasques that are under his control, Calima must serve a subpoena on Hector Vasques. (See Opp’n, p. 3 at § II.) However, the Court finds that sanctions are merited against THG and counsel as discussed below.

However, the Court does GRANT Calima’s request for an order compelling THG to disclose Hector Vasques’s last known contact information, since this information should have been provided to Calima’s counsel long ago once this fact was ascertained.

B. Request for Sanctions: GRANTED in part.

1. Legal Standard

If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

2. Court’s Determination

First, the Court determines that even though a deposition of Hector Vasques and production of documents from him cannot be granted based on the January 30, 2024, deposition notices, sanctions are merited against THG and counsel, Neda Cate, Esq., for failing to submit to an authorized method of discovery prior to Hector Vasques leaving THG and making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery on February 15, 2024. It is not clear on what basis Neda Cate represented that Hector Vasques was not available for deposition when his employment was seemingly not confirmed until May 29, 2024. (Opp’n, Cate, Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.)

Next, the Court notes that it does not sanction Hector Vasques for lack of sufficient evidence as to his involvement in the discovery objections and proceedings generally.

Last, the Court determines that $3,750 in sanctions for the moving papers is reasonable. The sanctions are comprised of (1) a fee rate of $750 per hour for an experienced practitioner whose declaration supports this fee rate by showing counsel’s experience and skill; and (2) ten total hours reasonably expended and to be expended in relation to this motion and an identical motion for the deposition of Shanon Johnson, with this request limiting itself to five hours for Hector Vasques.

Sanctions are thus GRANTED in part (no Vasques) in the amount of $3,750 against THG and counsel, Neda Cate, Esq. (SBN 274971).

 

III. Conclusion

A. Motion to Compel Deposition

Plaintiff Calima Electric, Inc.’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant The Hatch Group, Inc.’s Employee Hector Vasques and the Production of Documents Described in the Notice of Deposition is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part.

B. Request for Sanctions

Plaintiff Calima Electric, Inc.’s Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against The Hatch Group, Inc., Hector Vasques and Their Attorney of Record, Neda Cate, Esq., in the Sum of $3,750 is GRANTED in part in the amount of $3,750 against The Hatch Group, Inc. and counsel, Neda Cate, Esq. (SBN 274971) alone.

Defendant The Hatch Group, Inc. and counsel, Neda Cate, Esq. (SBN 274971), are ordered, jointly and severally, to remit sanctions of $3,750 to Plaintiff Calima Electric, Inc. within 30 days of this order.
