ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS CONEJO VALLEY CONSTRUCTION INC. AND FORREST GREGGE’S MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS CONEJO VALLEY CONSTRUCTION INC. AND FORREST GREGGE’S MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM WAIVER OF DISCOVERY OBJECTIONS

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

I.              BACKGROUND 

This action is brought by Plaintiffs Perrin Davidson (“Davidson”) and Sarah Nazari (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) against the property management company responsible for managing their community where they own a home, Defendant Lordon Enterprises Inc., and the manager and assistant manager of that company, Defendant Donalea Bauer and Defendant Shelby Wagner, respectively (collectively “Defendants”).

On May 9, 2024, Plaintiffs amended their Complaint to add Defendants Conejo Valley Construction Inc. and Forrest Gregge (collectively, “Moving Defendants”), adding both in place of “Doe 1.”

Moving Defendants now move for a judgment on the pleadings. Moving Defendants also move for relief from waiver of discovery objections.

II.           PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On May 4, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging (1) Fraud [Intentional Misrepresentation]; (2) Negligence; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (5) Violation of B&P §17200 Unfair Business Practices.

On July 21, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer.

On January 4, 2024, the Court held an informal discovery conference.

On January 17, 2024, the Court granted the parties’ Stipulated Protective Order.

On February 5, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses.

On May 9, 2024, Plaintiffs amended their Complaint to add Moving Defendants, adding both in place of “Doe 1.”[1]

On July 5, 2024, Moving Defendants filed their Answer.

On December 17, 2024, Moving Defendants filed the instant Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (“MJOP”).

On December 24, 2024, Moving Defendants filed the instant Motion for Relief from Waiver of Discovery Objections (“Motion”).

On January 2, 2025, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition to the MJOP.

On January 7, 2025, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition to the Motion.

On January 17, 2025, Moving Defendants filed their Reply in support of their MJOP but not as to their Motion.

III.        LEGAL STANDARDS  

A.            Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1064.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether the factual allegations, assumed true, are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452-53.) Thus, it may be granted if, from the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 438(d); Saltarelli & Steponovich v. Douglas (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1, 5.).[2]

A defendant or plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(f).)

B.            Relief from Waiver of Discovery Objections

A party who fails to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand, including based on privilege or work product, unless the court finds that the party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance and the party’s failure was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 (a)(1)-(2).) The trial court has broad discretion to grant relief from waiver of objections on a noticed motion upon determining that: (a) the moving party subsequently served discovery responses which are substantially code compliant; and (b) the moving party’s failure to timely serve responses “was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), and 2033.280(a).)

A court may not find that only some portions of a document containing responses are code-compliant but must instead determine whether the document as whole substantially complies. (See St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 779-780.)¿¿

The statutory language “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect” in the discovery statute should be interpreted using the same general principles developed in application of the identical language in section 473(b). (See Scottsdale Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 263, 275.) Although the party moving for relief under section 473 has the burden to show the mistake, inadvertence, or neglect was excusable, any doubts as to that showing must be resolved in favor of the moving party. (See New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1420.)¿

IV.         ANALYSIS

A.            Moving Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is Denied

1.              Meet and Confer

“Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 439(a) (emphasis added).)

Moving Defendants’ counsel provides a lengthy meet and confer declaration attesting to his efforts to communicate with Davidson (Plaintiff and counsel), including via a phone call on November 26, 2024. (See MJOP Declaration of Randall Pitre filed December 17, 2024.) The Court finds Moving Defendants have satisfied their meet and confer obligations.

2.             Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiffs request this Court take judicial notice of “the entire file in the Subject Litigation” and “[d]ocuments obtained from Defendant LORDON in the Subject Litigation.” Plaintiffs’ requests for judicial notice are DENIED because their request is not specific enough to enable the Court to determine, on an item by item basis as it must, whether judicial notice is appropriate.

3.             Analysis

Moving Defendants argue they are entitled to judgment on the pleadings because Plaintiffs’ Complaint is devoid of any factual allegations against Moving Defendants. (See MJOP, pg. 2.) Moving Defendants argue they “are only identified in the complaint as the same Doe defendant (Doe 1). The complaint makes contentions and/or conclusory allegations that the Doe defendants are in some unspecified manner liable to Plaintiffs, but there are no facts alleged against either of the Moving Defendants.” (MJOP, pp. 3-4.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they “seek to hold Defendants responsible for the same incident and injuries referred in the original complaint” and have incorporated their DOE allegations throughout the Complaint. (See Opposition, pp. 1-2.)

In reply, Moving Defendants argue Plaintiffs never served their Opposition on Moving Defendants, and that Plaintiffs’ proof of service for their Opposition is deficient. (See Reply, pp. 1-2.)

As an initial matter, Plaintiffs’ proof of service is procedurally defective as it does not state the name of the person making the service as required by Code of Civil Procedure 1013(a). (See Opposition Proof of Service.)[3] More concerningly, however, is Plaintiffs’ apparent failure to serve their Opposition on Moving Defendants. (See Reply Declaration of Randall Pitre filed January 17, 2025, ¶¶ 3-11.) The Court will consider Plaintiffs’ Opposition in its below ruling, but only because it does not rely on Plaintiffs’ Opposition in its below analysis.

Moving Defendants are not entitled to a judgment on the pleadings. Winding Creek v. McGlashan (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 933 (“Winding Creek”) is instructive here. In Winding Creek, the complaint included the following: “‘Defendant [sic ] Doe 1 through Doe 50, inclusive, are sued herein under fictitious names. Their true names and capacities are unknown to Plaintiffs. When their true names and capacities are ascertained, Plaintiffs will amend this Complaint by inserting their true names and capacities herein. Plaintiffs are informed and believe and thereon allege, that each of the fictitiously named Defendants is responsible in some manner for the occurances [sic ] herein alleged, and that Plaintiffs' damages as herein alleged were proximately caused by those Defendants.’ (Italics added.) In paragraph 15, plaintiffs alleged on information and belief that ‘... each Defendant acted as agent for their co-Defendants and were acting in that capacity at all times herein mentioned, or were in the employment of said other Defendants acting within the course and scope of their employment.’ These allegations were incorporated by reference into each cause of action.” (Winding Creek, 44 Cal.App.4th at 938.)
         The Winding Creek Court found the above sufficient to satisfy the requirements of Doe pleading. (See Winding Creek, 44 Cal.App.4th at 941.) “As noted above, every single cause of action stated in that pleading incorporated by reference plaintiffs’ allegation that ‘... each of the fictitiously named Defendants is responsible in some manner for the occurances [sic ] herein alleged, and that Plaintiffs’ damages as herein alleged were proximately caused by those Defendants.’ Also incorporated in each cause of action was the allegation that ‘... each Defendant acted as agent for their co-Defendants and were acting in that capacity at all times herein mentioned, or were in the employment of said other Defendants acting within the course and scope of their employment.’ The prayer for relief requests judgment ‘against Defendants and each of them....’ The complaint thus alleges that defendants, whose identities or culpable acts were unknown at the time of filing, are in some way responsible for the acts of which plaintiffs complain.” Winding Creek, 44 Cal.App.4th at 941-942.)

Plaintiffs’ approach here mimics that of Winding Creek’s plaintiff. They added Moving Defendants via amendment on May 9, 2024. Plaintiffs’ Complaint states “Plaintiffs are ignorant of the true names or capacities, whether individual, corporate, associate, or otherwise, of Defendants named herein as DOES 1 through 25, inclusive. Said DOES are unknown to Plaintiffs, who therefore sue said Defendants by such fictitious names, and Plaintiffs will ask leave to amend this pleading to show their true names and capacities when same have been ascertained. Plaintiffs allege that each of the fictitiously named Defendants is in some manner responsible for the damaged herein alleged.” (Complaint, ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs’ Complaint also states Defendants “are the agents, servants, and employees of their Co-Defendants”, that “each of the named Co-Defendants were, in some way responsible for the occurrences herein alleged, and the damages suffered by Plaintiffs, as alleged, were proximately caused by their conduct.” (Id. at 2.) Plaintiffs also incorporate the previous paragraphs into each cause of action, allege each cause of action “Against All Defendants[,]” and pray judgment be entered against “the Defendants, and each of them[.]” (Complaint.)

Plaintiffs have alleged Moving Defendants are in some way responsible for the acts of which the Plaintiffs complain, sufficient for DOE pleading.

Accordingly Moving Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.   

B.            Moving Defendants’ Motion for Relief from Waiver of Discovery Objections is Granted

1.              Judicial Notice

Moving Defendants request this Court take judicial notice of (1) the judgment entered in the “Liberty Canyon Action”, case number 23VECV05614; (2) the subject matter of the complaint in the “Liberty Canyon Action”; (3) the subject matter of the complaint filed by Plaintiffs in the “Sensitive Environmental Action”, case number 22STCV01858; and (4) the dismissal with prejudice entered in the Sensitive Environmental Action.

Moving Defendants’ requests for judicial notice are DENIED.

2.             Analysis

Moving Defendants’ counsel Randall Pitre (“Pitre”) declares Plaintiffs served six discovery responses on Moving Defendants on September 5, 2024, via an email to Pitre. (See Motion Declaration of Randall Pitre filed December 24, 2024 (“Pitre Decl.”), ¶¶ 3, 5.) Pitre declares the six discovery requests “were only sent to me and, as such, no one else in my office had a chance to see these discovery requests. I did not see this email or the attached discovery requests until October 14, 2024.” (Pitre Decl., ¶ 6.) It was also on October 14, 2024, that Pitre also saw Davidson’s October 10th and 11th follow-up emails regarding the late discovery. (See id.) Pitre further declares he informed Davison of his mistake on October 14, 2024, and served discovery responses by mail on November 10, 2024, and by e-mail on November 11, 2024. (See id. at 7-8.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they granted Moving Defendants additional time to respond to the discovery requests only on the understanding that Moving Defendants’ objections were waived. (See Motion Declaration of Perrin Davidson filed January 7, 2025 (“Davidson Decl.”), ¶¶ 4, 5, Exs. 3, 4.) Plaintiffs argue Moving Defendants should not have served their late responses with objections and, either way, they are evasive and non-responsive. (See Davidson Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 6.)

The Court finds Moving Defendants’ failure to provide timely discovery responses was due to Pitre’s mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. Pitre declares he did not see the email containing the discovery responses and was in communication with Davidson from the time he discovered his mistake to service of discovery responses. While Davidson’s October 25, 2024, email to Pitre does state “I expect the responses to be substantive, (and without objections as they are waived)[]”, it is not clear to the Court that Pitre agreed to these conditions—at any rate, Davidson’s email stating objections are waived does not preclude Moving Defendants from requesting relief from waiver of objections. (See Davidson Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 3.) The Court also finds Moving Defendants’ discovery responses are, while not strictly code-complaint, substantially compliant such that relief is appropriate. A response may be in substantial compliance and still the proper subject of a (later) motion to compel. A response is in substantial compliance if it is in the form expected for an answer to the given request and conveys some information.  

Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ Motion is GRANTED. 

V.            CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Moving Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

However, the Court GRANTS Moving Defendants’ Motion for Relief from Waiver of Discovery Objections.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER AND GRANTING WITH LEAVE TO AMEND DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE

 

 

 

 

I.       INTRODUCTION

This case arises out of an employment dispute between Plaintiff Jason Weeks (“Plaintiff”) and Defendants Pure Imagination Holdings, LLC, Pure Imagination Studios Inc., Pure Imagination Labs, LLC, The Bridge XRC, LLC, and Joshua Wexler (“Wexler”)(collectively “Defendants”). Plaintiff alleges he was hired by Defendants in October 2020 as a Senior Developer. (See First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶ 18.) In September 2021, Plaintiff alleges Wexler and Andrew Tucker (“Mr. Tucker”) [not a party to this litigation] “verbally and implied through their conduct, on behalf of Defendants, agreed with Plaintiff on increased pay and a stake in PURE HOLDINGS” “[i]n exchange for immediately assuming the role and responsibilities of Vice President of Technology.” (See id. at ¶ 20.) Plaintiff further alleges Defendants also agreed to reimburse Plaintiff for the approximately fifty thousand dollars in work-related expenses Plaintiff incurred during his three-year tenure. (See id. at ¶ 32.) Plaintiff alleges he quit his employment with Defendants in June 2023 after “it became apparent that the Defendants did not intend on honoring the agreement” [to increase Plaintiff’s pay and give Plaintiff a stake in Pure Imagination Holdings, LLC]. (See id. at ¶ 29.)

On August 16, 2024, the Court overruled in part and sustained in part Defendants’ Demurrer and granted Defendants’ Motion to Strike.

On September 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed his FAC.

Defendants now demurrer to Plaintiff’s first through fourth causes of action. Defendants also seek to strike Plaintiff’s requested attorney’s fees in his sixth cause of action and in his prayer for relief.  

II.     PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Actionable Deceit – Civil Code § 1709; (3) Fraud in Contract Formation – Civil Code § 1572; (4) Intentional Misrepresentation– Civil Code § 1710; (5) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (6) Negligence; and (7) Unjust Enrichment / Restitution.

On August 16, 2024, the Court overruled in part and sustained in part Defendants’ Demurrer and granted Defendants’ Motion to Strike.

On September 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed his FAC alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Actionable Deceit – Civil Code § 1709; (3) Fraud in Contract Formation – Civil Code § 1572; (4) Intentional Misrepresentation– Civil Code § 1710; (5) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and (6) Unjust Enrichment / Restitution.

On November 6, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike (“Motion”).

On January 9, 2025, Defendants filed Notice of Plaintiff’s Non-Opposition.

III.    LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589(a); see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Dept. of Pub. Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007, citation omitted.) “[T]he facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

“A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)  

IV.     MEET AND CONFER

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

         Defendants filed a declaration attesting to the parties’ telephonic meet and confer efforts. (See Declaration of Ronnie Arenas filed November 6, 2024, ¶ 3, Ex. 1.) The Court finds the parties’ meet and confer efforts sufficient.  

V.      ANALYSIS 

A.            Plaintiff Has Stated a Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 The essential elements of a contract are: “(1) Parties capable of contracting; (2) Their consent; (3) A lawful object; and (4) A sufficient cause or consideration.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1550.) “The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s FAC now contains facts wherein “Plaintiff admits that no agreement was in place, as Defendants’ lawyers needed to provide a document in order for such an agreement to be effective.” (Demurrer, pg. 12., citing FAC, ¶¶ 23, 24, 27, and 28.) “[T]he Parties’ express reservation of critical terms for a future time, after lawyers drafted essential documentation, demonstrates “there is no meeting of the minds of the parties while they are merely negotiating as to the terms of the agreement to be entered into.” (Demurrer, pp. 13-14, citation omitted.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

The Court is again unpersuaded by Defendants’ argument. The additional facts in Plaintiff’s FAC are not a departure from the allegations in his Complaint. In his Complaint, Plaintiff alleged “[w]henever Plaintiff asked JOSHUA and Andrew Tucker for his pay increase and documentation of Plaintiff’s stake in PURE HOLDINGS, Plaintiff would be assured that the documentation was in progress.” (Complaint, ¶ 22.) In Plaintiff’s FAC, Plaintiff merely reiterates Defendants’ assurances that the agreement was in place but not memorialized in writing. (See FAC, ¶¶ 24 (“…JOSHUA again assured Plaintiff that the documentation of Plaintiff’s stake in PURE HOLDINGS was still being worked on…”); 25 (“…Andrew explained to Plaintiff that Plaintiff’s stake in PURE HOLDINGS would derive from a 20% stake that JOSHUA and Andrew owned and were giving up in PURE HOLDINGS to share with Plaintiff.”); 27 (“…Plaintiff was assured on several different occasions…that Plaintiff was a stakeholder in PURE HOLDINGS and that the documentation was being worked on by the lawyers.”); and 28 (“The most recent occasion that Plaintiff was assured that Plaintiff was a stakeholder in PURE HOLDINGS was in the early summer or late spring of 2023…”).)

Plaintiff has not, contrary to Defendants’ argument, “admit[ed] that no agreement was in place, as Defendants’ lawyers needed to provide a document in order for such an agreement to be effective.” The Court’s ruling on Defendants’ previous Demurrer still stands. Plaintiff has pled sufficient facts to support the elements of a claim for breach of contract.

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for breach of contract.

B.            Plaintiff Has Stated Causes of Action for Actionable Deceit (Code Civ. Proc. § 1709), Fraud in Contract Formation (Code Civ. Proc. § 1572), and Intentional Misrepresentation (Code Civ. Proc. § 1710)

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another's reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Conroy v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1244, 1255.)

“[F]raud must be specifically pleaded. This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.” (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) In addition, a cause of action of fraud against a corporation requires a plaintiff to allege the names of persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when. (See Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157-58.)

Nonperformance alone does not prove a defendant’s intent not to perform. (See Tenzer v. Superscope, Inc. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 18, 31 (“[I]f plaintiff adduces no further evidence of fraudulent intent than proof of nonperformance of an oral promise, he will never reach a jury.”).) However, a plaintiff can infer fraudulent intent from circumstantial evidence, such as “as defendant's insolvency, his hasty repudiation of the promise, his failure even to attempt performance, or his continued assurances after it was clear he would not perform.” (Tenzer, 39 Cal.3d at 30.)

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s fraud causes of action fail because they have not been alleged with the requisite specificity. (See Demurrer, pg. 15.) Specifically, Defendants argue Plaintiff failed to allege a specific misrepresentation, failed to allege Defendants’ knowledge or intent not to perform, failed to allege justifiable reliance, and failed to allege more than speculative damages. (See Demurrer, pp. 15-19.)

The Court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently plead his causes of action based on Defendants’ alleged fraud. Plaintiff’s FAC specifically pleads Defendants’ misrepresentations by adding multiple dates, names, and other details about those misrepresentations, identifies Defendants’ repeated assurances without further action (from which fraudulent intent can be inferred), Plaintiff’s justifiable reliance on Defendants’ repeated representations, and damages ($50,000.00 in expenses, stock, and a pay raise). Taken together, these allegations are sufficient to support Plaintiff’s causes of action for fraud.      

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s second, third, and fourth causes of action for actionable deceit, fraud in contract formation, and intentional misrepresentation.

VI.         MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for relief “[f]or an award of reasonable attorney’s fees pursuant to California Labor Code § 218.5” and Plaintiff’s requested attorney’s fees, also based on Labor Code § 218.5, contained in his sixth cause of action. (See Demurrer, pg. 20.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

First, Defendants’ Motion is procedurally deficient. A motion to strike must be filed separately and “must quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count, or defense. Specifications in a notice must be numbered consecutively.” (Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(a).) Defendants placed their Motion at the end of their Demurrer and did not properly set forth the portions of Plaintiff’s FAC that they wish to be stricken.

The Court will nonetheless rule on Defendants’ Motion in the interest of judicial efficiency. “A party is entitled to an award of¿attorney¿fees¿if there is a specific authorization therefor by statute or private agreement.” (Beverly Hills Properties v. Marcolino (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d Supp. 7, 10.) Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees is based on Labor Code § 218.5. Labor Code § 218.5 allows reasonable attorney’s fees and costs “[i]n any action brought for the nonpayment of wages, fringe benefits, or health and welfare or pension fund contributions.” (Lab. Code § 218.5.) This action isn’t a wage and hour lawsuit nor has Plaintiff asserted any violations of the Labor Code. Thus, Plaintiff cannot recover attorney’s fees via Labor Code § 218.5.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Defendants’ Motion to Strike as to Plaintiff’s prayer for relief for attorney’s fees and Plaintiff’s requested attorney’s fees contained in his sixth cause of action.

VII.     CONCLUSION

          Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court OVERRULES Defendants’ Demurrer.

The Court also GRANTS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Defendants’ Motion to Strike as to Plaintiff’s prayer for relief for attorney’s fees and Plaintiff’s requested attorney’s fees contained in his sixth cause of action.

If Plaintiff wishes to file a second amended complaint that addresses his request for attorney’s fees, which is the only subject for which he has leave to amend, he shall do so on or before February 7, 2025.
