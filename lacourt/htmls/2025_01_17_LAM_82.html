DEPARTMENT 82 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCP00049    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 82

Paul W. Harrison,                                                    Case No. 23STCP00049

 

v.                                                                    

                                                                        Hearing: January 17, 2025

Benevolent and Protective Order of                       Location: Stanley Mosk Courthouse    

Elks of the United States of America—                  Department: 82        

Grand Lodge, et al.                                                   Judge: Stephen I. Goorvitch           

           

                       

[Tentative] Order Sustaining in Part and Overruling in Part Demurrer

 

[Tentative] Order Granting in Part and Denying in Part Motion to Strike

 

 

INTRODUCTION  

           

            Petitioner Paul W. Harrison (“Petitioner”) filed a second amended petition and complaint against Respondents Benevolent and Protective Order of Elks of the United States of America-Grand Lodge (the “BPOE”) and Pasadena Lodge Number 672 Benevolent and Protective Order of Elks of the United States of America (the “Lodge” or “Pasadena Lodge”).  Petitioner also named a series of individual respondents: Louis J. Grillo, Robert L. Duitsman, Hugh H. Scanlon, Ronald J. Celeste, Donna Leiby, Robert Hollis-Brau, Aron P. Andersen, John D. McEntire, Jr., Joanna C. Rentschler, Robyn Sembenini, Robert E. Scott, Denise J. Ryan, Teresa Skobba, Neil F. Yeager, Rene Flores, and Bruce Hidley (collectively, “the individual respondents”).  Petitioner raises four claims: (1) Petitioner seeks a petition for writ of mandate, arguing that his expulsion from the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge did not comply with principles of due process; (2) Petitioner seeks a declaratory judgment concerning the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge’s bylaws, arguing that certain provisions do not comply with principles of due process; (3) Petitioner alleges that the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge breached a settlement agreement; and (4) Petitioner alleges that the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge’s due process violations constitute unfair competition under Business and Professions Code section 17200.  Now, every respondent except for Bruce Hidley demur to the causes of action and move to strike certain prayers for relief.  The court overrules the demurrer to the first and second causes of action as they relate to the BPOE, the Pasadena Lodge, and Hidley but sustains the second cause of action as it relates to every other individual respondent.  The court stays the third and fourth causes of action pending resolution of the first and second causes of action.  The motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part.    

 

BACKGROUND

 

Until his recent expulsion, Petitioner was a member in good standing of the Pasadena Lodge for 35 years.  (SAP ¶ 1.)  The Pasadena Lodge is one of many local lodges of the BPOE, which “is a national fraternal benefit organization chartered in 1868.”  (Id. ¶ 2.)  “Petitioner is informed and believes and, based upon such information and belief, alleges Respondent Pasadena Lodge 672 is a California Nonprofit Mutual Benefit Corporation # C0405219 pursuant to California Corporations Code § 7110; with its principal place of business located at 400 West Colorado Blvd. Pasadena, CA 91105.”  (Id. ¶ 3.)

 

On or about November 5, 2020, Pasadena Lodge received Executive Order No. 20-065 from the BPOE appointing Special Representative Donna Leiby to oversee, manage, and control the day-to-day operations of Pasadena Lodge.  The executive order was based on the Pasadena Lodge’s failure “to maintain a sound financial footing in that during the last three (3) years the club operation lost the sum $262,847.00 in 2017-2018, the sum of $289,986 in 2018-2019, and the sum of $310,059 in 2019-2020.”  (Id. ¶ 32 & Exh. 2.)

 

According to the petition, Petitioner has conducted his own investigation into alleged financial improprieties at the Pasadena Lodge.  Pursuant to Corporations Code section 8333, Petitioner made a written request to Lodge to inspect its bylaws, accounting books, records, minutes, and financial statements.  (SAP ¶ 38 & Exh. 5.)  On March 30, 2022, Petitioner filed a petition for writ of mandate in Los Angeles Superior Court (Case No. 22STCP01148) seeking to enforce his inspection demand.  (Id. ¶ 38 & Exh. 5.)  On or about June 7, 2022, Petitioner and Pasadena Lodge executed a “Confidential Settlement Agreement” related to the writ petition and Petitioner’s inspection demand.   (Id. ¶¶ 38-40, 100; Exh. 5.)  Petitioner alleges that that “[i]t quickly became apparent that Lodge 672 had not negotiated in good faith and had no intention of providing access to accurate books and records of the corporation as agreed upon in the Settlement Agreement.”  (Id. ¶ 42.)  Petitioner alleges that “[d]uring [his] efforts to gain access to the books and records of Pasadena Lodge 672, Petitioner has been continuously harassed and given false information.”  (Id. ¶ 35; see also id. ¶¶ 51-53.)

 

Petitioner alleges that, on a date not specified in the SAP, he was duly elected as a trustee of the Pasadena Lodge.  (Id. ¶ 88.)[1]  Petitioner alleges that, after he “continued to speak out vociferously” about “unlawful behavior” at the Pasadena Lodge, he was served on January 24, 2023, with Emergency Executive Order No. 22-110E, which recommended Petitioner’s expulsion from BPOE.  (Id. ¶ 54, Exh. 9.)  On that same date, Pasadena Lodge received Executive Order No. 22-109E placing Pasadena Lodge on probation and confirming the appointment of a Probation Committee which would have full and complete authority over the operation and finances of Pasadena Lodge.  (Id. ¶ 55.)  On or about February 13, 2023, Petitioner received Executive Order No. 22- 110F and Pasadena Lodge 672 received Executive Order No. 22-109F confirming Petitioner’s expulsion from the BPOE and Pasadena Lodge’s indefinite probation.  (Id. ¶ 56, Exh. 11.) 

 

Petitioner alleges that his expulsion was based on “hearsay statements” and that “Respondents did not give Petitioner any notice of the adverse actions being contemplated and/or to be taken against him, nor did Respondents give Petitioner any opportunity for a hearing or to otherwise defend himself or contest the charges against him, prior to the imposition of expulsion.”  (Id. ¶ 57.)  Petitioner further alleges that the expulsion was “arbitrary” and “constitute[s] an invasion of, infringement upon, or interference with Petitioner’s fair procedure rights.”  (Id. ¶¶ 63, 66.)  Petitioner further alleges that his expulsion failed to comply with Corporations Code section 7341 and is therefore “void.”  (Id. ¶ 25.)

 

            On August 9, 2023, the court (Beckloff, J.) sustained Respondents’ demurrer to the first amended petition on the grounds that Petitioner had failed to allege exhaustion of administrative remedies with respect to his own expulsion or allege standing to challenge the indefinite probation imposed by BPOE on the Lodge or other actions taken against Lodge members generally.  The court ruled, however, that Petitioner had alleged standing to challenge his own expulsion should he exhaust administrative remedies.  (Court Order, filed August 9, 2023 at 9.)

 

            On August 30, 2023, Petitioner filed the operative SAP.  On September 29, 2023, Respondents filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike.  The hearing on the demurrer and motion to strike was continued several times pursuant to stipulations submitted by the parties and entered by the court.  One such stipulation states:

 

Whereas, the decision on Petitioner’s appeal of his expulsion order to the Benevolent and Protective Order of the Elks Grand Forum heard on June 1-2, 2024 has not yet issued but is expected shortly;

 

Whereas, the decision on Petitioner’s appeal is expected to narrow the issues in dispute by Petitioner’s Second Amended Petition for Writ of Mandate;

 

(Stipulation filed September 11, 2024.)

 

On October 9, 2024, the Elks Grand Forum issued a 14-page opinion after a two-day hearing affirming Petitioner’s expulsion.  Respondents acknowledge that “Petitioner’s internal appeal of his expulsion from the Elks has now concluded.”  (Reply to Demurrer 1 and Respondents’ Request for Judicial Notice, Exh. A.)

 

Petitioner asserts the following claims against the following Respondents:

 

First Cause of Action – Writ of mandate against the BPOE, the Pasadena Lodge, Grillo, Duitsman, and Hidley

 

Second Cause of Action – Declaratory relief against the BPOE, the Pasadena Lodge, “and their Constituent Leadership, Inclusive of All Other Respondents.”

 

Third Cause of Action – Breach of contract against the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge

 

Fourth Cause of Action – Unfair competition under Business and Professions Code section 17200 against the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge. 

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A.        Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (Code Civil Proc. § 430.30, subd. (a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “We assume the truth of the allegations in the complaint, but do not assume the truth of contentions, deductions, or conclusions of law.”  (California Logistics, Inc. v. State (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 242, 247.)  The allegations in the petition must be liberally construed in favor of Petitioner on demurrer.  (See Mobil Oil Corp. v Exxon Corp. (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 942, 947.)  “A demurrer must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained.”  (Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.”  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) 

 

B.        Motion to Strike

 

Upon motion, the court may “strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading” or “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (CCP § 436.)  As with a demurrer, “[t]he grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.”  (CCP § 437.) 

 

Courts take a “cautious” approach to motions to strike.  “We have no intention of creating a procedural ‘line item veto’ for the civil defendant.”  (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal. App. 4th 1680, 1683.)  “Judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

EVIDENTIARY ISSUES

 

            Respondents seek judicial notice of the 14-page Findings of Fact, Conclusions of Law, and Order for Judgment issued on October 9, 2024 by the Elks Grand Forum in the matter entitled In the Grand Forum of the Benevolent & Protective Order of Elks of the United States of America, Grand Forum Case No. 1561, Appeal of Executive Orders No. 22- 110F.  Essentially, Respondents seem to argue the merits of the petition, i.e., that Petitioner did receive due process.  That issue must await trial.  Therefore, the court declines to take judicial notice of this document because it is not relevant to the issues on demurrer. 

 

            Petitioner requests judicial notice of three exhibits.  Two are orders relating to the underlying proceedings, and the court denies the request for the same reason it denies Respondents’ request for judicial notice.  The third is an email, which is not suitable for judicial notice, so the request is denied. 

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.        Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies – Overruled

 

In the demurrer, Respondents argued that Petitioner “still has not exhausted his administrative remedies” because his appeal before the BPOE Grand Forum was not completed at the time the demurrer was filed in September 2023.  (Demurrer (“Dem.”) 7-8.)  Respondents challenge the first cause of action for writ of mandate and second cause of action for declaratory relief on this basis.  (Notice of Demurrer (“Notice”) 3-4.)  On October 9, 2024, the Elks Grand Forum issued a 14-page opinion after a two-day hearing affirming Petitioner’s expulsion (hereafter, “final expulsion decision”).  (RJN Exh. A.)  Respondents acknowledge that “Petitioner’s internal appeal of his expulsion from the Elks has now concluded” and they do not argue that Petitioner has any other administrative appeal to exhaust.  (Reply to Demurrer 1.) Since Petitioner has now exhausted his administrative remedies, the demurrer for failure to exhaust administrative remedies is overruled.    

 

B.        First Cause of Action

 

            1.         The BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge – Overruled

 

There are two essential requirements to the issuance of an ordinary writ of mandate under Code of Civil Procedure section 1085: (1) a clear, present, and ministerial duty on the part of the respondent, and (2) a clear, present, and beneficial right on the part of the petitioner to the performance of that duty. (California Ass’n for Health Services at Home v. Department of Health Services (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 696, 704.)  Respondents argue that Petitioner seeks a writ to compel a discretionary rather than a ministerial act: “[W]hether the Elks should grant Petitioner’s appeal and reinstate Petitioner into the Elks is an act requiring discretionary judgment by the Elks and is not a perfunctory task unmixed with judgment.”  (Dem. 9:6-8.) 

 

Respondents misconstrue Petitioner’s claim.  Petitioner raises due process issues, which are suitable for review in a petition for writ of mandate.  Petitioner alleges that the expulsion orders “are based upon hearsay and third-party information” without notice and opportunity be heard because “Petitioner was unaware that there was any controversy or dispute regarding the matters alleged in the Orders.”  (SAP ¶ 57.)  Petitioner alleges that:

 

Respondents . . . had a duty to provide Petitioner with due process and fair process, including without limitation, notice of any potential disciplinary charges they were considering and to provide a fair opportunity to be heard and defend himself prior to the imposition of discipline, including expulsion. . . .  To impose punishment against Petitioner without notice of even bothering to charge him with any specificity under the GLS or California law and/or find him guilty is a per se violation of these Respondents’ duties to provide due process and fair procedure to Petitioner.

 

(Id. ¶¶ 75-76.)  Petitioner further alleges that the expulsion was “arbitrary” and “constitute[s] an invasion of, infringement upon, or interference with Petitioner’s fair procedure rights.”  (Id. ¶¶ 63, 66.)  Petitioner further alleges that his expulsion failed to comply with Corporations Code section 7341 and is therefore “void.”  (Id. ¶ 25.)  Accordingly, Petitioner seeks a writ requiring Respondents to “rescind the Orders and to provide a fair hearing before an independent and impartial hearing offier prior to any final determination of expulsion of Petitioner from the BPOE and Pasadena Lodge 672.”  (SAP ¶ 90.)  These issues fall squarely within the court’s authority under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1085 and 1094.5.

 

            In reply, Respondents argue that “whether the Court should reverse the Elks considered judgment after a two-day hearing and testimony from 19 witnesses and reinstate Petitioner into the Elks is a substantive act requiring significant discretion, not a perfunctory task devoid of judgment.”   (Reply 4:2-5.)  While the court lacks authority to compel a specific discretionary action, the court has authority to review discretionary decisions to determine whether there was an abuse of discretion.

 

Normally, mandate will not lie to control a [respondent’s] discretion, that is to say, force the exercise of discretion in a particular manner.  However, it will lie to correct abuses of discretion.  In determining whether a [respondent] has abused its discretion, the court may not substitute its judgment for that of the agency, and if reasonable minds may disagree as to the wisdom of the agency’s action, its determination must be upheld.  A court must ask whether the [respondent’s] action was arbitrary, capricious, or entirely lacking in evidentiary support, or whether the [respondent] failed to follow the procedure and give notices the law requires.

 

(County of Los Angeles v. City of Los Angeles (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 643, 654.)  Petitioner’s claim falls squarely within these confines.  Therefore, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled with respect to the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge.     

 

            2.         Grillo and Duitsman – Sustained

 

It appears that Grillo and Duitsman were included in the first cause of action because of their purported involvement in recommending Executive Order No. 22-110E and Executive Order No. 22-110F, which were the expulsion orders that Petitioner appealed to the Grand Forum.  (See SAP ¶¶ 54, 71 and Exh. 10-11 and RJN Exh. A.)  On October 9, 2024, the Elks Grand Forum issued a 14-page opinion after a two-day hearing affirming Petitioner’s expulsion.  (RJN Exh. A.)  Respondents Grillo and Duitsman were not members of the Grand Forum and did not decide the appeal.  (Ibid.)  Under these circumstances, Petitioner has not alleged any basis in the SAP for Respondents Grillo and Duitsman to be included as respondents in the first cause of action.  Furthermore, in opposition, Petitioner does not address the argument that the SAP does not allege any liability of Respondents Grillo and Duitsman for the first cause of action.  (See Sehulster Tunnels/Pre-Con v. Traylor Brothers, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1328, 1345, fn. 16 [failure to address point is “equivalent to a concession”].)  Therefore, the demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained with respect to Grillo and Duitsman. 

 

           


 

3.         Hidley – No Action

 

Bruce Hidley did not join in the demurrer.  Therefore, he remains a Respondent with respect to the first cause of action.  The court orders the parties to meet-and-confer to determine whether Petitioner will stipulate to dismiss him from the first cause of action. 

 

            C.        Second Cause of Action

 

                        1.         The BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge – Overruled

 

In the second cause of action, Petitioner seeks a judicial declaration that the Grand Lodge Statutes (“GLS”) sections 9.005 and 9.013, “as enacted in their current form and as interpreted by these Respondents in this matter, violate and are contrary to settled California law requiring due process and fair procedure prior to the deprivation of rights, and are therefore illegal and unenforceable without modification.”  (SAP ¶ 94.)  This issue is suitable for a declaratory judgment (though the claim may be redundant if the court must interpret these provisions in resolving Petitioner’s first cause of action).

 

Respondents argue that “a declaration seeking to void or otherwise adjudicating the parties’ rights and obligations under those statutes is a complex task involving significant judicial discretion, and is not a clear and perfunctory task unmixed with judgment.”  (Dem. 10:14-16.)  Respondents’ arguments are not persuasive.  Respondents’ counsel cites cases relating to petitions for writs of mandate, not claims for declaratory relief.  (See Dem. 10:14-16, citing Unnamed Physician v. Board of Trustees of Saint Agnes Medical Center (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 607, 618; County of San Diego v. State of California (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 580, 602; Morris v. Harper (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 52, 62.)  Moreover, Respondents misconstrue these cases when they argue that a court cannot interpret the challenged statutes if to do so would require a “complex task involving significant judicial discretion.”  (Dem. 10:15-16.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 1060 gives the court authority to interpret written instruments.  Therefore, the demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled. 

           

                        2.         The Individual Respondents except for Hidley – Sustained

 

            As an initial matter, it is unclear whether the individual respondents (except for Hidley)  are named with respect to the second cause of action.  The second amended petition does not specifically identify any individual respondent (except for Hidley); the allegations are specific to the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge; and the nature of the claim—one for declaratory relief concerning the organization’s bylaws—does not lie against individuals.  Therefore, the court sustains the demurrer with respect to every individual respondent except Hidley (to the extent Petitioner intended to name them as respondents.

 

3.         Hidley – No Action

 

Bruce Hidley did not join in the demurrer.  Therefore, he remains a Respondent with respect to the first cause of action.  The court orders the parties to meet-and-confer to determine whether Petitioner will stipulate to dismiss him from the first cause of action. 

D.        Third Cause of Action – Stayed

 

Petitioner asserts a breach of contract claim against the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge.  Petitioner alleges that Respondents breached the June 7, 2022, Settlement Agreement “[b]y denying access to the books and records of Pasadena Lodge 672 in connection with its business operations.”  (SAP ¶ 102.)  Petitioner alleges that “as a result of this breach of the Settlement Agreement, Petitioner has sustained physical, mental and monetary damages in an amount to be determined at trial.”  (Id. ¶ 103.)  In addition to damages, Petitioner also seeks an injunction directing Respondents to comply with the Settlement Agreement.  (See Id. ¶¶ 99-103; Prayer ¶ d.; Civ. Code § 3422.)

 

Pursuant to the local rules which designate that Department 82 is a specialized writs and receivers department and not a general civil department, only a cause of action for writ of mandate and related special proceedings are properly assigned to this department.  (LASC Local Rules 2.8(d) and 2.9.)  Local Rules 2.8(d) and 2.9 do not include a claim for breach of contract (settlement) as a special proceeding assigned to the writs departments.  Moreover, this claim may require a jury or bench trial involving live testimony.  Therefore, the court stays the third cause of action pending resolution of the remaining causes of action. 

 

E.         Fourth Cause of Action

 

            Petitioner asserts an unfair competition claim against the BPOE and the Pasadena Lodge.  Petitioner alleges that the due process violations alleged in the first cause of action constitute unfair competition, necessitating an injunction.  In ruling on the prior demurrer, the court (Beckloff, J.) stayed all proceedings on the fourth cause of action pursuant to Local Rules 2.8(d) and 2.9 pending reassignment to an independent calendar court.  (Court’s Order filed August 9, 2023, at 9, 12.)  That stay has not been lifted.  Moreover, the resolution of the fourth cause of action may depend upon the court’s ruling on the first cause of action.  Therefore, the court stays the fourth cause of action pending resolution of the remaining causes of action. 

 

            F.         Motion to Strike

 

            Respondents move to strike prayers (b), (d), and (f) of the second amended petition.  The court rules as follows:

 

            (b) – The court denies the motion as this prayer relates to the second cause of action.  The court declines to rule on the  motion as it relates to the fourth cause of action, since that claim is stayed.

 

            (d) – The court declines to rule on the motion.  This prayer relates to the third cause of action which is stayed.

 

            (f) – The court grants the motion as it relates to the first and second causes of action, since monetary damages are not available.  The court grants the motion as it relates to the fourth cause of action, since “personal damages” are not available with respect to an unfair competition claim.  The court declines to rule on the motion to the extent this prayer relates to the third cause of action, which is stayed. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Based upon the foregoing, the court orders as follows:

 

1.         The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled with respect to the BPOE, the Pasadena Lodge, and Hidley. 

 

2.         The demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained with respect to Grillo and Duitsman.

 

3.         The demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled with respect to the BPOE, the Pasadena Lodge, and Hidley.

 

4.         The demurrer to the second cause of action is sustained with respect to the remaining individual respondents.

 

5.         The third and fourth causes of action are stayed pending the court’s resolution of the first and second causes of action.

 

6.         The motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part. 

 

7.         Petitioner articulates no basis upon which he could amend the first and second causes of action.  Therefore, the court denies Petitioner’s ex parte application for leave to amend.

Case Number: 24STCP01215    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 82

Michael D. Marcus v. California Department of Motor Vehicles

Case No. 24STCP01215

[Tentative] Order Denying Motion to Augment Record

[Tentative] Order Continuing Trial Date

 

Michael D. Marcus (“Petitioner”) filed this petition for writ of mandate challenging the suspension of his driver’s license by the Department of Motor Vehicles (“Respondent” or the “DMV”).   The suspension was based on a finding that Petitioner’s “medical information was not favorable” and was imposed after a driver safety investigation, pursuant to Vehicle Code section 13953, determined that Petitioner is unable to operate a motor vehicle safely because of a medical condition, viz., mild cognitive impairment and dementia.  (See Petition for Writ of Mandate (“Pet.”) pp. 54, 105-107 of 114.)  Now, Petitioner seeks to augment the record with four documents. 

           

“As a general rule, a hearing on a writ of administrative mandamus is conducted solely on the record of the proceeding before the administrative agency.”  (Richardson v. City and County of San Francisco (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 671, 702.)  However, “[w]here the court finds that there is relevant evidence that . . . was improperly excluded at the hearing before respondent, it may enter judgment as provided in subdivision (f) remanding the case to be reconsidered in the light of that evidence; or, in cases in which the court is authorized by law to exercise its independent judgment on the evidence, the court may admit the evidence at the hearing on the writ without remanding the case.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1094.5, subd. (e).)  The movant must establish “the relevancy of the evidence” and demonstrate that the evidence was improperly excluded at the administrative hearing.  (Ibid.)

 

The court rules as follows:

 

Exhibit A – Petitioner seeks to augment the record with an email from Dr. Grossman.  This email was not excluded at the hearing on December 19, 2023.  To the contrary, Petitioner read the email into the record verbatim, and the hearing officer considered its contents.  Because the contents of this email are part of the record, the motion is denied as moot with respect to Exhibit A.

 

Exhibit B – Petitioner seeks to augment the record with a handwritten note from Dr. Koretz stating: “I would have completed the DMV form exactly as Dr. Grossman” and “Because I share Dr. Grossman’s perspective, it would be incorrect for me to state otherwise.”  Petitioner referenced this note during the hearing—describing it as “the statement from Dr. Koretz, who says that he really would do the same things that Dr. Grossman did . . .”—but Petitioner did not read it verbatim into the record.  Nor did he seek to introduce the note during the hearing.  Because this note was not improperly excluded by the hearing officer, the motion is denied with respect to Exhibit B.   

 

Exhibit C – This exhibit is already part of the certified administrative record.  Therefore, the motion is denied as moot with respect to Exhibit C.

 

Exhibit D – Petitioner seeks to augment the administrative record with a letter he wrote Respondent’s counsel. This letter, dated September 9, 2024, is not appropriately part of the administrative record.  Therefore, the motion is denied with respect to Exhibit D.

 

Trial is set for February 5, 2025, but Petitioner did not file his opening brief.  Therefore, the court continues the trial date and sets a new briefing schedule.

Case Number: 24STCP02898    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 82

Daniel Williams,                                                       Case No. 24STCP02898

 

v.                                                                     Hearing: December 13, 2024

                                                                        Location: Stanley Mosk Courthouse

                                                                                    Department: 82                                      City of Los Angeles,                                                        Judge: Stephen I. Goorvitch

 

                       

[Tentative] Order Granting Petition for Relief from Government Claims Requirement

 

 

INTRODUCTION

 

             Petitioner Daniel Williams (“Petitioner”) petitions for an order relieving him from the claim presentation requirement of Government Code section 945.4 with respect to his claim for damages against Respondent City of Los Angeles (“Respondent” or the “City”).  The City initially opposed the petition but did not file a supplemental opposition in response to the declaration of Shawn Athari.  The petition is granted.     

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On December 4, 2023, Petitioner allegedly tripped and fell on “uneven broken asphalt” at 1119 Hoover Street in Los Angeles, California.  (Hayes Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.)  Petitioner allegedly sustained injuries to his left leg, back, neck, and shoulders and claims damages of $1,000,000.  (Ibid.)  On or about May 26, 2024—which was a Sunday—Petitioner’s attorney, Ernest J. Lingenfelter, directed an employee of the Law Offices of Gene J. Goldsman to file a government claim against the City of Los Angeles.  (Lingenfelter Decl. ¶ 3.)  According to Lingenfelter, the claim “was completed and submitted on that date” through the City’s “claims presentation portal.”  (Id. ¶¶ 3-4.)  This is corroborated by the declaration of Shawn Athari, dated December 24, 2024, which states:

 

On or about May 26, 2024, I filed a government claim against the City of Los Angeles, which was completed and submitted on that date, which fell within the applicable six (6) month statutory period to file a government claim.  Because the City of LA submission form is through an online portal, there was no documents provided to us showing the submission which was done.

 

(Athari Decl. ¶ 3.)   

 

The deadline to submit a claim was June 4, 2024, per Government Code section 911.2.    On June 17, 2024, Petitioner submitted an untimely claim for damages.  Lingenfelter explains that this “supplemental” claim was submitted after he had not received “confirmation of the submission and filing” that was allegedly made on May 26, 2024.  (Id. ¶ 4 and Exh. 1, 2.)  This is corroborated by the declaration of Shawn Athari.  (Athari Decl. ¶ 4.)  On June 26, 2024, the City returned Petitioner’s claim without taking any action “[b]ecause the claim was not presented within the time allowed by law.”  (Hayes Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. B.) 

            On July 5, 2024, the City received documentation from Petitioner, which the City treated as an application for leave to present a late claim. (Hayes Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. C.)  Then, on or about July 10, 2024, Petitioner’s counsel sent a letter to the City of Los Angeles, City Clerk titled “Late Government Claim Supplemental Letter,” which stated in part:

 

Our office filed a claim through your online portal on May 26, 2024 which fell within the allotted 6 month period to file such claims for damages. After receiving no response, we filed a subsequent claim again through your online portal on June 17, 2024. Our office filed the claimed timely through your portal initially, and has done subsequent late filings along with emails sent to: attclaimsubmit@lacity.org as well as multiple calls and voicemails left at (213)978-1133 / (213)978-7081 / (213)978-7050 all to which we have received no response.

 

This letter is to advise that the claim was initially filed within the allotted timeframe of 6 months. It was filed electronically through your online portal on Sunday, May 26, 2024.

 

(Lingenfelter Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. 2.)  On July 11, 2024, the City denied Petitioner’s application for leave to present a late claim.  (See Hayes Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. D.)  Petitioner filed the instant petition on September 9, 2024.    

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Government Code section 911.2(a) states that “a claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property . . . shall be presented . . . not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.”  If a claimant fails to make a claim within six months pursuant to Government Code section 911.2, the claimant may make a written application to the board of the public entity for permission to present a late claim within a reasonable time but not to exceed one year from the accrual of the cause of action.  (Gov. Code § 911.4(a)-(b).)  If, pursuant to the provisions of Government Code section 911.6, the board denies the application to present a late claim, the claimant may petition the Court for relief from the requirements of Government Code section 945.4.  (Gov. Code § 946.6(a).)

 

Government Code section 946.6(b) requires that the petition to the court must show each of the following: (1) that the late claim application made to the board was denied or deemed denied; (2) the reason for failure to present the claim within six months of the accrual of the cause of action; and (3) the contents of the claim as required by Government Code section 910.  The petition must be filed within six months after the application to present a late claim to the board was denied or deemed to be denied.  (Ibid.) 

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the late-claim application was made within a reasonable time and that one of the statutory requirements under Government Code section 946.6(c) was met.  (Drummond v. County of Fresno (1987) 193 Cal.App.3d 1406, 1410.)  Under section 946.4(e), the trial court must make its determination upon the petition, “relying upon any affidavits in support of, or in opposition to, the petition and any additional evidence received at hearing on the petition.”  (Ebersol v. Cowan (1983) 35 Cal.3d 427, 431.) 

“Section 946.6 is a remedial statue intended to provide relief from technical rules which otherwise provide a trap for the unwary claimant.”  (Id. at 435.)  “[D]oubts will be resolved in favor of the party attempting to get to trial to the end that wherever possible, cases may be heard on their merits.”  (Kaslavage v. West Kern County Water Dist. (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 529, 537.) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Petitioner’s claim for damages arose on December 4, 2023, when he alleged fell.  The deadline to file a claim was June 4, 2024.  Petitioner’s attorney asserts that a government claim was “submitted” on May 26, 2024, within the six-month filing period.  (Lingenfelter Decl. ¶ 3 and Exh. 2.)  If so, Petitioner does not need relief from the claims filing requirement of section 945.4.  Therefore, the court assumes without deciding that Petitioner first submitted a claim on June 17, 2024, after the deadline.  Petitioner made a written application to the City for leave to present a late claim on July 5, 2024, within one year of accrual of Petitioner’s claim.   (Hayes Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. C.)  The court also finds that the late-claim application was made within a “reasonable time.”  (See Gov. Code § 911.4(a)-(b).) 

 

Pursuant to Government Code section 946.6(c)(1), the court may grant relief from section 945.4 if “the failure to present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect unless the public entity establishes that it would be prejudiced in the defense of the claim if the court relieves the petitioner from the requirements of Section 945.4.”  The showing required of a petitioner seeking relief because of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect is the same as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).  (See Viles v. State (1967) 66 Cal.2d 24, 29.)  “[I]t is uniformly held that for relief on any or all of the stated grounds it must be shown that one’s misconception was reasonable, or that it might have been the conduct of a reasonably prudent person under similar circumstances.”  (Kaslavage v. West Kern County Water Dist. (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 529, 539 fn.1.)

 

The declarations establish that Plaintiff’s attorneys mistakenly believed they submitted a timely claim on May 26, 2024, and did not discover the error until after the deadline had passed.  The record is sufficient to establish the requisite “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  The court notes that the City did not file a supplemental opposition to Athari’s declaration, as authorized by the court’s minute order of December 13, 2024.  Therefore, the court grants the petition.
