DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00995    Hearing Date: March 27, 2024    Dept: W

JONATHAN RAIMAN VS. GENERAL MOTORs, LLC, et al.

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’s FEES  

 

Date of Hearing:          March 27, 2024                           Trial Date:       None/Settled

Department:                   W                                                         Case No.:         22VECV00995

 

Moving Party:                 Jonathan Raiman

Responding Party:       General Motors

  

BACKGROUND 

 

Plaintiff, Jonathan Raiman, brought this action on July 18, 2022 against General Motors for violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

 

Plaintiff now moves for an order awarding it fees and costs as the prevailing party and as provided for in the Section 998 offer accepted by plaintiff.  The parties do not dispute that plaintiff is the prevailing party and entitled to such fees under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civil Code Section 1794(d). They dispute the reasonableness of the fees sought.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING 

 

 Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED, in a reduced amount.   

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorney fees in addition to other costs.  (CCP § 1033.5(a)(10).)  “Prevailing party” includes the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant.  (CCP § 1032(a)(4).)  In all other circumstances, the “prevailing party” shall be as determined by the court.  (Id.) 

 

The fee setting inquiry ordinarily begins with the “lodestar,” i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.  (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal. 4th 1084, 1095.)  “The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of facts specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”  (Gorman v. Tassajara Dev. Corp. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 770, 774.) 

 

In determining whether to adjust the lodestar figure, the court may consider the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.  (EnPalm LLC v. Teitler (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 770, 774.) The prevailing party bears the burden of proof and the amount is left to the trial court’s sound discretion.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal. App. 4th 1315, 1320.)  A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635.) 

 

In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence.¿ (Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Ins. Guaranty Assoc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.)¿ General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.¿ (Ibid.)¿ 

 

DISCUSSION 

 

Attorney’s Fees

 

Plaintiff here seeks attorney fees of $26,071.40 (which includes a multiplier of .22), costs of $777.68, and an additional $6899.44 in fees and costs incurred in filing the instant motion.   In the instant case, defendant made an offer to compromise on October 23, 2023, which was accepted by plaintiff on November 15, 2023.   There were no motions filed in the case or other activity before this court, other than a short IDC held on May 10, 2023.   The only pleadings filed by the Plaintiff were the complaint, a case management statement, a proof of service and a notice of Posting Jury Fees.  The IDC statement filed by plaintiff was two pages long.  There was no opposition filed to the IDC statement and the only issue discussed at the brief IDC related to the scheduling of the PMK deposition, which was never taken.  The only discovery in the case was boilerplate written discovery propounded by plaintiff and defendant. The only other activity involved emails and other communications between counsel about obtaining extensions to respond to discovery and scheduling the single PMK deposition. No deposition was ever taken.  Plaintiff never responded to the discovery propounded on it by defendant.  Defendants issued an initial Section 998 offer on May 5, 2023.  Plaintiff did not respond to it or accept it.  Plaintiff accepted the same offer to compromise for $80,500 on November 15, 2023.  The only difference between the two offers was that the second one included prejudgment interest. 

 

Civil Code section 1794(d) provides that a prevailing plaintiff in a case brought under the Song Beverly Act shall recover “a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred . . . in connection with the commencement and prosecution” of this action. (Civ. Code §1794(d); see Graham v. Daimler Chrysler Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 553, 557.)   “Even after determining that a party is entitled to fees because it ‘prevailed,’ the trial court must still determine what amount of fees would be ‘reasonable’ in light of the relative extent or degree of the party's success in obtaining the results sought.” (Sokolow v. County of San Mateo (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 231, 247.) Serrano v. Priest (Serrano III) (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25 defined the lodestar-adjustment method for determining the amount of reasonable attorney fees in California, and the California Court of Appeal has held the lodestar method applies to determining attorney's fees under the Song-Beverly Act. (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th, 785, 817; Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154).  

 

Hourly Rate

 

In determining the reasonable hourly rate, the court looks to whether the requested rates are “within the range of reasonable rates charged by and judicially awarded comparable attorneys for comparable work.” (Children's Hosp. & Med. Ctr. v. Bontá (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 740, 783.) “Generally, the reasonable hourly rate used for the lodestar calculation is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 616.)

 

Attorney Kaloustian billed his time at $600 per hour; Daniel Imber at $600 per hour, Judith Chamussy at $350 per hour and paralegals at $150 per hour. The court finds all of these rates reasonable given the current market rates for similar counsel, and the education, experience and specialized qualification of these particular attorneys. 

 

Reasonableness of Time Spent

 

Despite this being a simple case that settled with little work being performed by either side, plaintiff had two law firms working on the case.   The court has reviewed the billing records submitted and finds that there was some duplication of effort by attorneys Kalustian and Imber (two highly experienced consumer attorneys with years of experience litigating lemon law cases), and some unnecessary work after the final Section 998 offer was received, rendering some of the time spent excessive and unreasonable.   The court finds the following hours to have been unreasonably incurred.

 

7/18/23 – 1 hour by Imber to review complaint which the Kalustian firm had already drafted and filed ($600).

 

8/29/23 – 1 hour by Imber to review answer, duplicating review by Kalustian firm ($600).

 

10/23/23 -- .1 hours by Kaloustian (billing at $600) per hour to make a reservation for the fee motion – this should have been done by a paralegal at a far lower billing rate ($60.)

 

12/7/23 – .4 hours by Imber to review discovery responses by Imber when Kalustian also reviewed the same records ($240).

 

Time spent between the receipt of the second Section 998 offer and its acceptance, as plaintiff’s counsel appears to have engaged in “make work” during this period rather than simply accepting the offer.  The court notes that counsel’s own records show the Imber office received the 998 offer on 10/23 but did not communicate with the client about it until 10/30.  Although they presumably had the plaintiff’s authorization as of 10/30 (since there were no subsequent communications with plaintiff after that date and prior to the acceptance), Imber’s office did not prepare the acceptance until November 15, 2023.  Nonetheless, the Kaloustian firm was still engaging in busy work with respect to discovery that they would have known would never be provided. Specifically, the court finds the following work by the Kaloustian office to be unreasonable:

 

11/1/23 – Preparation of discovery responses for 2.5 hours by Chamussy ($875).

 

11/1/23 Draft objections to plaintiff’s deposition for .3 hours by staff ($45)

 

11/21/23 – Review of Objections to Plaintiff’s NOD by Kalustian for .1 hours ($60)

 

 

With respect to the request for $6899.44 in fees for bringing this simple motion for attorneys fees, most of which is boilerplate language, the court finds the fee request to be excessive. The court finds the reasonable amount of time to prepare the motion and to appear at the hearing to be as follows: 2.5 hours of staff time by the Kaloustian firm ($375) to prepare the motion; 1 hour by Kaloustian ($600) for preparation of his declaration and preparation of the motion;  and 1 hour by Chamussy ($350) for her preparation of the motion and declaration. No reply brief was filed.  Attorney Imber also reasonably spent an hour at $600 to prepare his declaration and review the motion which was drafted by the Kaloustian firm ($600).  In addition, the court finds an appearance by lead counsel Kaloustian at the hearing ($600) also to be reasonable, for a total charge of $2525. 

 

Multiplier

 

The court agrees with defendant that a “multiplier” is unjustified in this case. Counsel for plaintiff claims a multiplier is warranted because of the “contingent risk,” the delay in payment and because defendant refused to resolve the issue of fees.  None of these factors are present in this case, nor present to such a degree as to warrant a multiplier.  This case did not require any extraordinary legal skill – it was an ordinary lemon law case which presented no novel issue, was handled by attorneys whose value was already reflected in their high hourly charges, resulted in no motion practice, never got past the earliest stages of discovery and was resolved within 13 months of the defendant answering.  Plaintiff extended the life of the case only over the issue of whether it would receive prejudgment interest, ironically incentivizing delay on the part of plaintiff to resolve the case.  Little was gained by the refusal to accept the first Section 998 demand, which would have cut short the case by more than six months.  Under the circumstances, the court finds plaintiff has not justified the need for a multiplier in this case.  (See Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 819).

 

Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees in this action in the amount of $18890 on the underlying litigation, plus $2525 on the fee motion, or a total of $21,415. 

 

 

 

Costs 

 

Plaintiff is also entitled to recover reasonable costs for this action.  There does not seem to be a serious dispute as to plaintiff’s entitlement to recover their entire requested costs, or $789.90.

 

CONCLUSION & ORDER

 

For the foregoing reasons, the court awards Plaintiff $21,415 in attorney’s fees and $789.90 in costs. Plaintiff is ordered to prepare and serve a notice of ruling and proposed final judgment in accordance with California Rule of Court 3.1312 within 10 days.   


Case Number: 23VECV01887    Hearing Date: March 27, 2024    Dept: W

AARON KOONCE V. KIA MOTORS AMERICA, INC.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:        March 27, 2024                                             Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        23VECV01887

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Aaron Koonce         

Responding Party:     Defendant Kia Motors America, Inc.  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Cohen Decl. ¶¶9-21.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On May 1, 2023, Plaintiff Aaron Koonce filed a complaint against Defendant Kia Motors America, Inc. for Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; and Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Aaron Koonce moves for an order to strike Defendant Kia Motors’ meritless objections and compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents to Defendant, Set One Nos. 1, 2, 3, 7, 17, 20, 22, 24, 30, 35, 38, 40, 41, 42, 43, 48, 54, 55, 56, 65, 73, 75, 76, 77, and 85 (collectively, the “Requests”) and production of all responsive documents corresponding to those Requests. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds Defendant has failed to provide adequate responses to Plaintiff’s Requests, which seek documents directly relevant to their Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act claims, and failed to meet and confer with Plaintiffs in good faith. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant.

 

Upon receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that the statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (CCP § 2031.310(a).)

 

Plaintiff argues each and every request in RFP seeks discovery regarding Transmission Defects in other 2019 Kia Niro vehicles; however, Kia has responded by either limiting the scope of discovery to include only the Subject Vehicle, or by claiming in some responses an inability to comply. Moreover, Kia has not produced all responsive documents. According to Plaintiff, the requested documents are relevant to the issues related to Kia’s good faith compliance with the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, including the breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, as well as Plaintiff’s negligent repair claim. Moreover, Plaintiff is entitled to internal investigation, analyses and correspondence (i.e., emails) in cases brought under the Song-Beverly Act. (See Santana v. FCA US, LLC (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 334, 347.)

 

Kia first opposes the motion on the grounds Plaintiff did not sufficiently meet and confer. The parties dispute whether the meet and confer was sufficient. The court has reviewed the submitted correspondence and has reservations about whether either party made a good faith effort to informally resolve the dispute.  Nevertheless, as the correspondence makes clear that the parties have reached an impasse as to this issue, the court will address the motion on its merits.  (See Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 332.) 

 

Kia next opposes the motion on the grounds it has already produced all documents that pertain to the Subject Vehicle and all responsive policies and procedures. In reply, Plaintiff maintains to date, Kia has not served Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One. The parties are ordered to meet and confer with what documents have been actually produced. If they have not been so produced, as discussed further below, Kia is to produce all documents pursuant to the Protective Order.

 

Kia further argues the broad discovery that plaintiff seeks is neither proportional to the amount in controversy nor important to resolving the issues in this case. Specifically, the discovery requests propounded by Plaintiff are vastly disproportionate to the claims at issue in this matter involving only one vehicle. Kia contends Plaintiff’s discovery must be tailored to the dispute in this lemon law case—Plaintiff’s vehicle. Moreover, Plaintiff’s template Motion and the Declaration of Rosario Stoliker are devoid of any accurate facts necessary to show good cause or other justification for the class action style discovery that Plaintiff seeks.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause for the documents sought. Good cause “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) Plaintiff has done so by demonstrating the documents sought relate to Kia’s s good faith compliance with the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Specifically, some of the requested documents may show that Kia had knowledge of Plaintiff’s alleged defects from complaints by other consumers. This is relevant to willfulness. However, the court finds the “Kia Vehicles” definition should not be limited just to the defect alleged but should be further limited to vehicles purchased or leased in California of the same year, make, and model of the subject vehicle, for the period of the date of purchase to the present.

 

Lastly, Kia has provided no evidence that the quantum of work required to perform these tasks will be so unduly burdensome that Kia should be entirely excused from producing the requested documents. If Kia is unable to produce any of the requested documents with the new limitations, Kia is required to make a statement of inability to comply and affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made, and state why Kia is unable to comply.  (See CCP §§ 2031.10(a)(2), 2031.230.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED, in part.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,160.00 due to Kia’s unjustifiable discovery obstruction, including its response to the majority of the Requests consisting of meritless, boilerplate objections along with severely deficient document production.

 

There being substantial justification, with merit to both sides, the court denies the request for sanctions.¿¿¿
