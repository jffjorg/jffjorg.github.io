Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                                                            Monday, June 10, 2024

Department M                                                                                                                             Calendar No. 9

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Karen Johnston as Successor Trustee of the Morrow Family Trust Dated July 9, 1998 v. Stacey Morrow, et al.

            23TRCV03182

1.      Stacey Morrow’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Stacey Morrow’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is granted.           

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on September 26, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff is the legal owner of property located at 5017 W. 142nd Street, Hawthorne CA 90250. Defendant was allowed to remain at the property pending the exercise of Defendant’s right to purchase the property within six months after the death of settlor. Defendant did not purchase the property within the six-month period. Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for Ejectment.

 

            Motion to Quash

 

            Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10 states:

            “A summons may be served by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete at the time of such delivery. The date upon which personal delivery is made shall be entered on or affixed to the face of the copy of the summons at the time of its delivery. However, service of a summons without such date shall be valid and effective.”

 

            Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20 states, in relevant part:

            “(a) In lieu of personal delivery of a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served as specified in Section 416.10, 416.20, 416.30, 416.40, or 416.50, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in his or her office or, if no physical address is known, at his or her usual mailing address, other than a United States Postal Service post office box, with the person who is apparently in charge thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. When service is effected by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at a mailing address, it shall be left with a person at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.”

 

            Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10 states, in relevant part: “(a) A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes: (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”

 

            Substantial compliance requires a showing of the following: there must be some degree of compliance, that the circumstances show that it was highly probable that attempted service imparted the same notice as full compliance, and that the attempted service provided sufficient notice to Defendant to put on a defense. Carol Gilbert, Inc. v. Haller (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 852, 865-66.

 

            Pursuant to CCP § 418.10(a)(1), Defendant moves to quash service of the summons on the grounds that she was not properly served with the summons and complaint under CCP § 415.10. Defendant contends that she was never personally served as reflected in the proof of service. But, in fact, Defendant states that the trustee Plaintiff broke into the residence with the process server and left the summons and complaint in the foyer.

 

            Plaintiff filed no written opposition to this motion. Unlike the typical motion where the moving party has the burden of proof, here the Plaintiff has the burden of proof. “When a defendant challenges the court's personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process the burden is on the plaintiff to prove … the facts requisite to an effective service.”  Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413 (internal quotes omitted).

 

            Therefore, Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons is granted.

 

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

John Q. Rodgers v. HB Construction, et al.

            YC072916

1.      HB Construction’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees on Appeal


TENTATIVE RULING

 

HB Construction’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees on Appeal is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed his Complaint on May 30, 2018. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff and Defendant HB Construction entered into an oral contract wherein Defendant agreed to perform construction and re-modification work on an office building owned by Plaintiff.  Unknown to Plaintiff, however, Defendant failed to have a valid contractor’s license. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Declaratory Relief under CCP § 1060 and Bus. & Prof. Code § 7031(a); 2. Disgorgement under Bus. & Prof. Code § 7031(b).  

 

Timothy Roth, the owner of HB Construction initiated an arbitration proceeding seeking compensation, in the form of capital credits, which he believed was owed to him by Plaintiff under the operating agreement. The subject property was apparently owned by an entity named 2601 PCH LLC which was formed by Plaintiff and Roth. The arbitration commenced pursuant to an arbitration clause in the operating agreement of 2601 PCH LLC.  The instant action was stayed pending completion of arbitration.

 

The arbitration was heard by Judge Margaret M. Grignon (Retired).  Ultimately on October 9, 2019, Judge Grignon issued her Arbitration Award. The parties disputed who actually prevailed in the arbitration.  On February 17, 2021, Plaintiff dismissed the instant action without prejudice. On October 4, 2021, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for attorneys’ fees.  On December 27, 2023, the Court of Appeals affirmed the trial Court’s order granting Defendant’s motion for attorneys’ fees.

 

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

 

Defendant moves for an award of attorney’s fees on appeal in the amount of $26,388.00 as the prevailing party on appeal pursuant to Civil Code § 1717.  Here, the moving party prevailed on appeal and was not awarded costs on appeal.  An award of appellate costs does not preclude a party from seeking attorney fees. The statutory language does not specifically address the situation where a prevailing party on appeal is not awarded costs. “Unless the court orders otherwise, an award of costs neither includes attorney's fees on appeal nor precludes a party from seeking them under rule 3.1702.” Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 8.278(d)(2). Thus, the language in the statute suggests that the appellate court’s decision on costs does not affect the right to seek attorneys’ fees in either scenario. The Court notes that the Appellate Court decision does not address attorneys’ fees on appeal, at all.

 

The Court will not repeat the underlying basis for the attorneys’ fees pursuant to the contractual provision as set forth in the Court’s decision in 2021 and the Court of Appeal’s decision to affirm the Court’s ruling in 2023.

 

“The trial court has “broad authority” to determine the amount of a reasonable attorneys’ fees.  PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.  “[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.”  Id. [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].  “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in his court, and while his judgment is of course subject to review, it will not be disturbed unless the appellate court is convinced that it is clearly wrong.”  Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132 (internal quotation omitted.) 

 

Thus, Defendant is entitled to attorneys’ fees under the lodestar method based on the reasonable amount of time the attorney spent multiplied by a reasonable rate.  Defendant’s counsel has provided a list of the numerous post-judgment tasks that he undertook on this case, the hours expended, his qualifications and experience, and his hourly rate of $390.00 per hour.  (Decl., Einar Wm. Johnson, ¶¶ 4-8.)  The Court finds that the hourly rate is reasonable considering the facts of this case and the attorney’s experience.  The Court finds that Defendant’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient to determine whether the amount of time expended was reasonable. Defendant’s counsel’s declaration set forth the total hours expended, and the nature of the services provided within the hours expended. “[A]n award of attorney fees may be based on counsel's declarations, without production of detailed time records.”  Raining Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.  

 

Therefore, the Court awards attorneys’ fees on appeal in favor of Defendant in the total amount of $26,388.00.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
