DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 18LBCV00039    Hearing Date: February 15, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Discover Bank filed this action against Defendant, Seng Men on 11/02/18 for failure to pay his credit card bill.  On 8/27/19, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Settlement.  On 10/02/19, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for entry of judgment and retention of jurisdiction.  On 10/03/19, Plaintiff filed a proposed order to dismiss the case with retention of jurisdiction pursuant to CCP §664.6; the Court signed the judgment of dismissal the same day. 

 

  1. Motion to Set Aside Notice of Settlement and Enter Judgment Pursuant to Stipulation

a.     Request for Relief

On 8/10/23, Plaintiff filed this motion to set aside notice of settlement and enter judgment pursuant to terms of stipulation.  Plaintiff provides evidence that Defendant made payments per the parties’ stipulation in the total amount of $8505, but ceased making payments on 5/10/23 and has made no payments since that date.  Plaintiff provides evidence that $17,318.25 remains due and owing pursuant to the parties’ settlement agreement. 

 

b.     Law Governing Enforcement of Settlement per CCP §664.6 After Dismissal

Usually, the court loses subject matter jurisdiction when an action is voluntarily dismissed (with or without prejudice). Thereafter, it has no power to enforce a settlement agreement.  Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 917-918.  Where a settlement agreement provides the court may retain jurisdiction to enforce the settlement, but the dismissal fails to provide for retained jurisdiction, the plaintiff's remedy is to move to vacate or modify the dismissal under CCP § 473(b) for “mistake, inadvertence or excusable neglect.”  Basinger v. Rogers & Wells (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 16, 21. 

 

c.     Analysis

In this case, the dismissal explicitly provides that the Court retains jurisdiction to enforce the settlement per CCP §664.6.  Therefore, no order setting aside the dismissal is necessary. 

 

The motion to vacate the notice of settlement and enter judgment pursuant to stipulation is therefore granted.  The Court will sign the proposed judgment, submitted with the moving papers on 8/10/23, upon entry of this order. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.   


Case Number: 21STCV35939    Hearing Date: February 15, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Martin Delgado filed this action against Defendant, Super Center Concepts, Inc. on 9/29/21 for damages arising out of a fall Plaintiff sustained while shopping at Defendant’s store.  Specifically, Plaintiff alleges he was using a mobility scooter and, upon standing to exit the scooter, the scooter seat base broke and/or detached from its base, causing Plaintiff to sustain injuries.

 

On 11/22/21, Defendant answered the complaint and filed a cross-complaint against Amigo Mobility International, Inc. for indemnification and related claims.  Amigo is the entity that manufactured the scooter on which Plaintiff fell. 

 

On 1/04/24, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file an amended complaint adding Amigo as a defendant in the action; the amended complaint did not make any material changes to the allegations against Super Center. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

a.     Law Governing Summary Judgment

 Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)

Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

Amigo filed a response to Super Center’s summary judgment motion, as well as various declarations and documents related to the response.  The essence of the response is that Amigo does not oppose the motion, and Amigo notes that Super Center is not seeking any relief vis-à-vis the cross-complaint running between it and Amigo.  Amigo argues that, similar to Super Center’s argument, there is also no evidence that it (Amigo) caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries. 

 

Plaintiff filed an objection to all of Amigo’s papers, arguing Amigo is improperly seeking consideration of the merits of the case against Amigo, and has improperly filed and served evidence concerning the merits of the case against it that the Court should not consider without proper notice. 

 

The Court is making no determination concerning Plaintiff’s case against Amigo or Super Center’s cross-complaint against Amigo.  The Court notes that Amigo’s “response” adds nothing to the analysis of the motion.  The Court has reviewed the papers filed by Amigo, and finds no reason to strike the papers as suggested by Plaintiff, but does state, for the record, that the papers did not play any role in the Court’s resolution of the merits or outcome of the summary judgment motion filed by Super Center.

 

c.     Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff filed objections to the Declarations of Velasquez and Sermeno with his opposition papers.  As will be discussed more fully below, the objections are sustained. 

 

d.     Moving Burden

Super Center has the moving burden to show it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law because there is no dispute that nothing it did caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries sustained while exiting the subject mobility cart on the date of the incident.  As discussed above, Super Center can meet this burden in two ways.  First, it can affirmatively provide evidence that the cart was not defective.  Alternatively, it can provide evidence that it propounded the full panoply of discovery on Plaintiff, and Plaintiff’s responses to discovery revealed a lack of knowledge concerning any defect in the cart. 

 

Super Center tries, but fails, to meet its moving burden in both respects.  First, it tries to meet its moving burden to show the subject cart was not defective by providing affirmative evidence.  Specifically, it provides the Declarations of Nancy Sermeno and Eric Velasquez.  Velasquez declares he is the store director at the store where Plaintiff was injured.  He states, at ¶¶9-10 of his declaration, that there were no complaints about the cart prior to the injury or after the injury.  He fails, however, to lay a foundation for this statement, and therefore Plaintiff’s objections are sustained.  He fails to indicate what system Super Center has for lodging complaints, whether he reviewed any such system, whether he would be the person to whom such complaints would inevitably be directed, etc.  Similarly, Nancy Sermeno declares she was the office clerk at the subject store until 2022 and is now the active sales manager.  At ¶¶6-7 of her declaration, she also declares there were no complaints about the cart before or after the incident.  She also fails, however, to provide any details concerning how she would have this information, and therefore Plaintiff’s objections are sustained.  Without adequate foundation for these statements, Defendant failed to meet its moving burden to show it has affirmative evidence that the carts were not defective at the time of the incident.

 

Defendant next attempts to shift the burden by arguing Plaintiff lacks evidence concerning how the cart broke when he stood up.  Defendant relies on Plaintiff’s deposition testimony, wherein he admitted he did not know why the seat popped out of place and does not know if there is a latch to make the chair pop or detach.  This testimony does not constitute a showing that Defendant propounded a “full panoply” of discovery on Plaintiff and his responses were factually devoid.  Notably, determinations concerning what causes a cart to pop out of place would normally be within the scope of expert testimony, and a person using a cart would not be expected to know the cause of the defect on his own.  Thus, Defendant failed to shift the burden in this manner also.

 

The motion is denied on the ground that Defendant failed to meet its moving burden to show nothing it did caused or contributed to Plaintiff’s injuries. 

 

e.     Shifted Burden

Because Defendant did not meet the moving burden, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact.  The Court does, however, note that fact 15 is disputed.  Fact 15 states that the carts, which the parties agree were delivered a week prior from Amigo, did not come with instructions or information that the seat or any part of the cart needed to be assembled, installed, inserted, or otherwise serviced prior to having store customers use the cart.  Fact 15 is supported with the Declaration of Velasquez, who simply states the foregoing. 

 

Plaintiff provides the actual owner’s manual, which Defendant produced in discovery.  At page 7 of the manual, the manual explains that the seat need not be removed unless it needs to be replaced, but then explains, “the ‘opening pedastal’ design allows access to the batteries, controller and charger without requiring tools.  With two hands, pull both release levers apart to open the cover (Figure 8).”  Figure 8 shows two levers immediately under the seat, which appear to indicate the seat can then be pulled up (but not removed) to access the batteries, controller, and charger. 

 

There is no evidence, with the moving papers, that Defendant, through its employees, did not open the seat to access the batteries, controller, or charger prior to the incident.  If Defendant’s employees did so, then the jury could conclude Defendant caused Plaintiff’s incident by failing to properly secure the seat after doing so.  Again, it is Defendant’s moving burden to negate this allegation, and Defendant did not do so, either through competent affirmative evidence or through defective discovery responses. 

 

The motion is therefore denied on the additional ground that there are triable issues of material fact concerning what caused the incident.

Case Number: 22STCV04747    Hearing Date: February 15, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jane Doe filed this action against Defendants, LBUSD and Thomas Maggio for damages arising out of childhood sexual assault perpetrated by Maggio while he was a teacher for LBUSD and Plaintiff was a student.  Plaintiff alleges the abuse occurred in 1996 while Plaintiff was in eighth grade and was 13-14 years old. 

 

Plaintiff filed her complaint on 2/07/22, and LBUSD answered on 4/29/22.  LBUSD filed a cross-complaint against Maggio.  Maggio answered the complaint and cross-complaint on 10/31/22 and 11/14/22, respectively. 

 

On 2/28/23, the parties were scheduled to participate in an informal discovery conference before the Honorable Serena R. Murillo in the personal injury hub court.  The IDC was not held because the Court referred the matter to Department 1 to determine whether it was complicated and should be transferred to an IC court for all further proceedings.  On 3/08/23, the Court transferred the matter to Department S27 for all further proceedings. 

 

3.         Discovery Issues

a.         Matters on Calendar

Plaintiff has motions to quash on calendar today, 2/20/24, 2/22/24, 2/27/24, and 2/29/24.  Defendant has a related motion to compel on calendar on 3/12/24.  Plaintiff has a motion to compel production of records on calendar on 3/28/24.  The case is scheduled for trial on 9/03/24. 

 

b.         Continuance

The Court is continuing the hearing on the 2/15/24, 2/20/24, 2/22/24, 2/27/24 and 2/29/24 motions to 3/12/24.  The Court will provide guidance to allow the parties to meet and confer further prior to the 3/12/24 hearing.  If the parties are able to resolve their issues, they must take the 3/12/24 motions off calendar.  If they are unable to resolve their issues, the Court will be inclined to appoint a discovery referee.  The parties must submit referee nominations and/or any argument in opposition to the reference at least five court days prior to 3/12/24. 

 

c.         Guidance

All the motions detailed above ask the Court to balance Plaintiff’s right of privacy against Defendant’s right to learn of alternative sources of the emotional and mental distress she alleges she suffered as a result of the assault that forms the basis of this action.  Notably, Plaintiff argues the discovery seeks to discover her sexual history, which is directly precluded by CCP §2017.220; Defendant’s 3/12/24 motion concerns Defendant’s ability to conduct such discovery in this case. The Court will, at this time, provide guidance concerning both Defendant’s right to conduct discovery into sexual history and also Defendant’s right to conduct the remainder of the subject discovery that is not related to sexual history.

 

The Court will first consider whether and to what extent Defendant can discover evidence relating to Plaintiff’s sexual history.  CCP §2017.220(a) provides:

In any civil action alleging conduct that constitutes sexual harassment, sexual assault, or sexual battery, any party seeking discovery concerning the plaintiff's sexual conduct with individuals other than the alleged perpetrator shall establish specific facts showing that there is good cause for that discovery, and that the matter sought to be discovered is relevant to the subject matter of the action and reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. This showing shall be made by a noticed motion, accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, and shall not be made or considered by the court at an ex parte hearing.

 

Evidence Code §1106 details whether and when evidence of sexual history is admissible in a civil action.  It provides:

(a) In any civil action alleging conduct which constitutes sexual harassment, sexual assault, or sexual battery, opinion evidence, reputation evidence, and evidence of specific instances of the plaintiff's sexual conduct, or any of that evidence, is not admissible by the defendant in order to prove consent by the plaintiff or the absence of injury to the plaintiff, unless the injury alleged by the plaintiff is in the nature of loss of consortium.

(b) Subdivision (a) does not apply to evidence of the plaintiff's sexual conduct with the alleged perpetrator.

(c) Notwithstanding subdivision (b), in any civil action brought pursuant to Section 1708.5 of the Civil Code involving a minor and adult as described in Section 1708.5.5 of the Civil Code, evidence of the plaintiff minor's sexual conduct with the defendant adult shall not be admissible to prove consent by the plaintiff or the absence of injury to the plaintiff. Such evidence of the plaintiff's sexual conduct may only be introduced to attack the credibility of the plaintiff in accordance with Section 783 or to prove something other than consent by the plaintiff if, upon a hearing of the court out of the presence of the jury, the defendant proves that the probative value of that evidence outweighs the prejudice to the plaintiff consistent with Section 352.

(d) If the plaintiff introduces evidence, including testimony of a witness, or the plaintiff as a witness gives testimony, and the evidence or testimony relates to the plaintiff's sexual conduct, the defendant may cross-examine the witness who gives the testimony and offer relevant evidence limited specifically to the rebuttal of the evidence introduced by the plaintiff or given by the plaintiff.

(e) This section shall not be construed to make inadmissible any evidence offered to attack the credibility of the plaintiff as provided in Section 783.

 

Defendant argues §1106 makes clear that (a) evidence relating to Plaintiff’s sexual history with Maggio is admissible per §1106(b), (b) to the extent the evidence shows Plaintiff’s ex-husband sexually abused her, this would be admissible to prove the nature and extent of the injuries sustained as a result of Maggio’s abuse, and (c) the purpose of §1106 is to preclude the defendant from arguing a victim of a sexual assault consented to the act because she was promiscuous, but that is not what Defendant is seeking to do here; Defendant is seeking to show a history of trauma that contributed to Plaintiff’s emotional and mental injuries, which includes any abuse by her ex-husband.

 

Defendant’s first argument is misplaced.  §1106(a) provides that evidence of specific instances of the plaintiff’s sexual conduct is not admissible to prove consent or absence of injury.  §1106(b) provides that (a) does not apply to evidence of the plaintiff’s sexual conduct with the alleged perpetrator.  §1106(c), however, then provides that, if the plaintiff was a minor at the time of the assault, evidence of the conduct with the defendant is not admissible to show consent or absence of injury.  Thus, the code does not, on its face, permit discovery into the details of the assault at issue in this case.  That said, nothing in the code precludes discovery of every conversation Plaintiff may have ever had relating to Maggio’s assault; if Plaintiff discussed the assault and her distress relating to the assault with the health care providers whose records have been subpoenaed, those records would clearly be relevant to this action.

 

The Court understands that the remainder of Defendant’s argument re: §1106 concerns Plaintiff’s claimed damages.  This is a delicate issue, but the Court is inclined to agree with Defendant.  To the extent there is evidence of sexual abuse by Plaintiff’s ex-husband in the records, that evidence would be admissible to show alternative or additional causation of Plaintiff’s ongoing emotional and mental distress.  It would not be admissible to show consent or absence of injury, but rather to show Plaintiff’s ongoing emotional injuries have an additional or alternative source.

 

Defendant discusses the holding in Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, wherein the California Supreme Court discussed the scope of a mental health examination of a plaintiff claiming emotional distress damages as a result of sexual harassment.  The Court made clear that the defendant could not conduct an examination into the plaintiff’s sexual history or practices, but could require a psychiatric examination into the source and scope of her claimed damages.  Thus, the examiner was precluded from discussing the plaintiff’s sexual history or practices, but was permitted to discuss the plaintiff’s mental and emotional health, including potential other sources of pain or stress that could be contributing to her claimed distress.

In this case, Plaintiff has admitted to severe abuse from her ex-husband.  A mental health examiner would be permitted, under Vinson, to discover the details of that abuse, because it would be an alternative source of mental and emotional distress.  Similarly, then, the records relating to that abuse should also be discoverable.  The Court is inclined to find that includes any records relating to sexual abuse, because the records would not relate to Plaintiff’s sexual history or practices, but rather to alternative sources of her distress.

 

In conclusion, the Court finds any medical records that discuss the source(s) of Plaintiff’s mental and emotional health problems are discoverable by Defendant in this case.  The Court recognizes Plaintiff’s right to privacy in the records, but finds the need for Defendant to defend itself outweighs that right to privacy.  The Court asks the parties to meet and confer in light of the above guidance, and to attempt to resolve the remaining issues without the need for the 3/12/24 hearing if possible.

2.     Demurrer (Defendant, Borba Investment Realty, Inc.)

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

Again, Plaintiff failed to participate in meet and confer communications.  Plaintiff must do so in the future, as discussed above. 

 

b.     Analysis

Roselli demurs to the private nuisance, breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, IIED, CLRA, FEHA, and tenant harassment ordinance causes of action.  The crux of Roselli’s demurrer is that all of the subject cause of action are alleged to arise out of the parties’ duties under the subject lease, but Plaintiff did not attach the lease.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on this ground; Plaintiff must attach the lease to an amended pleading.

 

Roselli also argues the IIED cause of action fails to allege extreme and outrageous conduct, the FEHA cause of action fails to allege facts showing discrimination, and the harassment cause of action fails to allege facts showing harassment.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on each of these additional grounds; Plaintiff must plead additional facts in an amended pleading if he wishes to pursue these claims. 

 

5.     Conclusion

Borba’s demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.  Its motion to strike is moot.  Roselli’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is ordered to file an amended complaint, in compliance with this order, within twenty days.  Roselli is ordered to file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.  If Roselli challenges the amended pleading, Plaintiff must fully participate in the meet and confer communications and must file opposition to the challenge if he wishes to pursue his claims. 

 

6.     Case Management Conference

The parties are reminded that there is a CMC on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the above demurrers and motion to strike.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on the demurrers, motion and the CMC.
