[Tentative] RULING RE: MOTION TO COMPEL MENTAL EXAMINATION OF PLAINTIFF

 

 

MANHATTAN BEACH UNIFIED SCHOOL DISRICT, ET AL.

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 11, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Manhattan Beach Unified School District

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Jane Doe

Motion to Compel Mental Examination of Plaintiff

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant’s Motion to Compel IME is GRANTED.  However, the IME shall be limited to 4 hours and Defendant is ordered to provide results from the testing within 48 hours.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for IME on March 14, 2024 at 9:00 am within 75 miles of San Marcos, CA.  BACKGROUND

            During the 2004-2005 school year, Plaintiff was sexually assaulted by Thomas Wallace while she was a student and a member of the track team at Mira Costa High School, which is within Defendant Manhattan Beach Unified School District.  On June 23, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint alleging (1) negligence of district employees (GC §815.2); (2) negligent supervision; (3) negligence per se (Penal Code §11164, et seq.). 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.”  (CCP §2032.310(a).)  “A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (CCP §2032.310(b).) 

            A defendant in a personal injury case has the right to one physical examination of the plaintiff, without leave of court, simply by serving a written demand on plaintiff.  (CCP §2032.220(a).)  An additional physical examination may be ordered upon a finding of good cause.  (CCP §§2032.310 and 2032.320.)  A request for IME under CCP §2032.310 may only be granted upon good cause show.  (CCP §2032.320(a).)  This generally requires a showing both of: (1) “Relevancy to the subject matter”; and (2) specific facts justifying discovery: i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere.  (Vinson v. Sup.Ct. (Peralta Comm. College Dist.) (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)

DISCUSSION

            Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s IME, because Plaintiff has placed her mental and emotional health at issue by claiming emotional distress damages.  Defendant argues Plaintiff’s mental and emotional health are therefore directly relevant to the subject matter and good cause exists to compel the examination. 

            Defendant also argues the terms of the examination are reasonable.  Defendant identifies the examiner as Praveen Kambam, a physician and MD and a board-certified child and adolescent psychiatrist.  Defendant argues its physician will examine Plaintiff within 75 miles of her city of residence, San Marcos, CA.  Defendant argues the exam will be no more than 8 hours, excluding breaks, and it will not involve any invasive physical procedures.

            Defendant maintains it attempted to meet and confer in good faith.  Defendant has been attempting to obtain the exam since January 2, 2024 but parties were unable to reach an agreement. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s request for an IME was unduly delayed and will prejudice Plaintiff.  Plaintiff argues the trial date is less than a month away and the expert cutoff is on March 19, 2024.  Plaintiff argues the examiner, Dr. Kambam, will be deposed only eight days before trial.  Plaintiff argues this will severely prejudice her given that she has abided by all cutoffs and deadlines.  Plaintiff argues she will be timely producing all of her experts before the March 19, 2024 deadline. 

            Plaintiff also argues the examination fails to comport with CCP §2032.310.  Plaintiff argues the CCP requires that Defendant identify those tests that will be performed, not those that may be performed.  Plaintiff argues Defendant only identifies tests that may be performed.  Plaintiff argues a number of the tests are also irrelevant to her mental and emotion distress damages. 

            Plaintiff also argues Defendant is improperly seeking to redepose her through this IME.  Plaintiff argues there is no reasonable basis for the expert to inquire as to her developmental history, education, employment, social history, legal history, general medical history, family history, prenatal history and the alleged conduct of Defendant, culminating in a review of current psychiatric and physical complaints.  Plaintiff argues these areas were already covered by her deposition. 

            Plaintiff asks that the Court limit testing to four hours if the Court grants this motion.  Plaintiff also asks that Defendant be ordered to produce the testing results within 48 hours of examination, so that she can properly prepare for Defendant’s expert deposition.  Plaintiff also asks that the testing be limited to the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale-IV; Minnesota Mulitphasic Personality Inventory-2 or 3; ACE Questionnaire for Adults; Trauma Symptom Inventory-2; Clinician Administered PTSD Scale for DSM-5 (CAPs-5); and Test of Memory Malingering; Personality Assessment Inventory.  Plaintiff asks that the topics of the clinical interview be limited to basic background information, reported instances of onset of the alleged emotional injury and how the symptoms unfolded over time to the present. 

            Defendant establishes good cause to compel the IME.  Plaintiff does not dispute that she has placed her emotional and mental health at issue and the IME is directly relevant to her claims.  An IME is necessary to obtain information regarding Plaintiff’s injuries and damages.

            The only objection Plaintiff raises is (1) delay and (2) the scope and length of the exam.  Defendant initiated meet and confer efforts to obtain an IME in January 2024, approximately 3 months before the discovery cutoff.  Defendant filed this motion before the discovery cutoff in February 2024 and before the discovery motion hearing cutoff.  Although Defendant could have acted sooner, Defendant was within the cutoffs and delay is not grounds to deny the request for an essential IME.  And the history shows efforts to arrange the IME by agreement, which unfortunately failed after an extended period of time.

If the delay in the IME results in any prejudice to Plaintiff, Plaintiff should be prepared at the hearing to address scheduling, including a trial continuance so that any claimed prejudice may be eliminated.  Such modifications may include the exchange of expert information, depositions, and trial.

            As to Plaintiff’s objections to Defendant’s IME protocol, the Court disagrees that the testing protocol identified improperly identifies possible tests that may be administered.  Defendant states that “the examiner may use the following test/exams” and then proceeds to list the specific tests and exams that may be used.  “An order granting a physical or mental examination shall specify the person or persons who may perform the examination, as well as the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.”  (CCP §2032.230(d).)  Defendant sufficiently specifies the tests that will be used by the examiner.  The examiner may perform fewer than all of those identified but the examiner may not go outside the enumerated tests.  This is sufficient.  The trial court order in Carpenter v. Supr. Ct. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249, 257 is distinguishable, because the court did not identify any tests and merely stated “examination shall test emotional and cognitive functioning” and referenced “standardized written psychological tests.”  (Carpenter, supra, 141 Cal.App.4th at 257.) 

            Plaintiff’s request that the test results be provided to her within 48 hours is reasonable in light of the delay and impending cutoffs.  Plaintiff’s request that the testing be limited to 4 hours is also granted. 

ORDER

Defendant’s Motion to Compel IME is GRANTED.  The IME shall be limited to 4 hours and Defendant is ordered to provide results from the testing within 48 hours.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for IME on March 14, 2024 at 9:00 am within 75 miles of San Marcos, CA.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion To Vacate and Enter Different Judgment

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition or reply papers were filed.

RULING

            The Motion to Vacate and Enter Different Judgment is denied. 

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiffs allege Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority is using Plaintiff’s private property for public usage without compensation.  Plaintiffs allege Defendants Evan Rosenberg and Alvin Kusumoto converted the property as employees of LACMTA.  Plaintiffs filed a complaint on February 18, 2022. 

            The operative complaint was the Third Amended Complaint filed on October 13, 2023.  The TAC alleges Due Process Violation Enforce Public Contract.  On January 4, 2024, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the TAC without leave to amend.  On January 11, 2024, a judgment of dismissal was entered in Defendants’ favor. 

           

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §663

A judgment or decree, when based upon a decision by the court, or the special verdict of a jury, may, upon motion of the party aggrieved, be set aside and vacated by the same court, and another and different judgment entered, for either of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of the party and entitling the party to a different judgment:

1. Incorrect or erroneous legal basis for the decision, not consistent with or not supported by the facts; and in such case when the judgment is set aside, the statement of decision shall be amended and corrected.

2. A judgment or decree not consistent with or not supported by the special verdict.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate per CCP §663 must be denied for several reasons:

·         The motion was not served on Defendants.  Plaintiff was required to serve Defendants with notice of this motion in conformity with CCP §1005.

·         CCP §663 does not apply to orders sustaining demurrers without leave to amend.  A motion to vacate under section 663 may only be brought when “the trial judge draws an incorrect legal conclusion or renders an erroneous judgment upon the facts found by it to exist.” (County of Alameda v. Carleson (1971) 5 Cal.3d 730, 738.) “A motion to vacate under [Code of Civil Procedure] section 663 is a remedy to be used when a trial court draws incorrect conclusions of law or renders an erroneous judgment on the basis of uncontroverted evidence.” (Plaza Hollister Ltd. Partnership v. County of San Benito (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1, 14.)  “Applying these principles, it is clear that a section 663 motion does not lie to vacate a judgment following an erroneous ruling on a demurrer.  First, a demurrer tests only the sufficiency of the pleadings. (§ 430.30.) There were no ‘conclusions of law’ to be corrected from ‘uncontroverted evidence.’  Indeed, a ruling on a demurrer does not involve either admission of evidence or findings of fact. Second, section 663 only ‘empowers a trial court, on motion of ‘[a] party ... entitl[ed] ... to a different judgment’ from that which has been entered, to vacate its judgment and enter ‘another and different judgment.’”  (Payne v. Rader (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1569, 1575 (disapproved of on other grounds in Ryan v. Rosenfeld (2017) 3 Cal.5th 124).) 

ORDER

            For the aforesaid reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Judgment pursuant to CCP §663 is DENIED.

Motion to Approve Consent Judgment

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition or reply papers were filed. 

RULING

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Approve Consent Judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            On April 7, 2023, Plaintiff Keep America Safe and Beautiful, Inc. filed this action alleging violation of the Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, codified at Health and Safety Code §25249.6, et seq. (“Proposition 65”).  Plaintiff alleges Defendant Viking Cue Manufacturing, LLC failed to warn citizens of California, in violation of Prop 65, about the presence of di(2-ethylhexyl) phthalate (DEHP, diisononyl phthalate (DINP) and di-n-butyl phthalate (DBP). 

            On October 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present motion to approve the proposed Consent Judgment.  Plaintiff moves this court, per California Health & Safety Code §25249.7(f), for an order approving and entering the terms of A Proposed Stipulated Consent Judgment submitted with the moving papers.  No opposition or reply papers have been filed. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            The Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, colloquially known as Proposition 65, was passed as a ballot initiative by the California voters, and was designed to prevent the contamination of drinking water with, and generally protect the public from unknowing exposure to, harmful chemicals. (12 Witkin, Summary of California Law (11th ed. 2017) Real Property, §967.)  Health & Safety Code (“H&SC”) § 25249.7 governs the enforcement of Proposition 65.  Proposition 65 has both public and private enforcement mechanisms.  (H&SC § 25249.7(c), (d).)  Violations are punishable by injunction (H&SC § 24259.7(a)) and civil penalty (H&SC § 24259.7(b)).  In the case of private enforcement actions, parties may also recover attorney’s fees, pursuant to the provisions in CCP § 1021.5, governing actions concerning important rights affecting the public interest. 

            Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(f) governs the role of the Court in approving settlements and consent judgments for private actions to enforce Proposition 65.  Section 25249.7(f)(4) provides, in relevant part, that:

“If there is a settlement of an action brought by a person in the public interest under subdivision (d), the plaintiff shall submit the settlement, other than a voluntary dismissal in which no consideration is received from the defendant, to the court for approval upon noticed motion, and the court may approve the settlement only if the court makes all of the following findings:

(A) The warning that is required by the settlement complies with this chapter.

(B) The award of attorney’s fees is reasonable under California law.

(C) The penalty amount is reasonable based on the criteria set forth in paragraph (2) of subdivision (b).”  (Consumer Advocacy Group, Inc. v. Kintetsu Enterprises of America (2006)141 Cal.App.4th 46, 61 (“To stamp a consent agreement with the judicial imprimatur, the court must determine the proposed settlement is just….In the context of Proposition 65 litigation, necessarily brought to vindicate the public interest, the trial court also must ensure that its judgment serves the public interest.”).)

DISCUSSION

            Compliancy of Warning

            Health & Safety Code section 25249.6 states, in relevant part: “No person in the course of doing business shall knowingly and intentionally expose any individual to a chemical known to the state to cause cancer or reproductive toxicity without first giving clear and reasonable warning to such individual, except as provided in Section 25249.10.”  (Health & Safety Code, § 25249.6.)  “Health and Safety Code section 25249.6 (the central warning provision) requires that there be no ‘knowing and intentional’ exposure ‘without first giving clear and reasonable warning.’”  (Consumer Defense Group v. Rental Housing Industry Members (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1185, 1214.)  In order for a warning to be clear and reasonable, the manner of transmission must be reasonable, the message employed must be sufficiently clear to communicate the warning.  (Environmental Law Foundation v. Wykle Research, Inc. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 60, 67 fn. 6.)

            The Consent Judgment provides that sixty days from the Effective Date, Defendant will be permanently enjoined from manufacturing for sale in the State of California, or directly selling in the State of California, any Covered Product containing non-complaint levels of DEHP, DINP and DBP, unless accompanied by warnings pursuant to Proposition 65.  A noncompliant level shall be level of DEHP, DINP or DBP in a maximum concentration which is equal to or greater than .01 when analyzed by a laboratory accredited by the State of California, a federal agency, or a nationally recognized accrediting organization. 

            For those Covered Products that were packaged, distributed, shipped or sold by Defendant before the Compliance date, all claims are released.  Any Covered Products that are not exemption under Section 3.1 of the Consent Judgment shall be accompanied by a warning as provided in Section 3.2 of the Consent Judgment.  The acceptable warnings set forth in Section 3.2 must be displayed in a reasonably conspicuous manner and they adequately inform the reader of the potential exposure to DEHP, DINP and DBP, which are known to the State of California to cause cancer and birth defects or other reproductive harm.  The warnings reference Prop 65 and provide a website to obtain more information.

            The Consent Judgment satisfies the “clear and reasonable warning” requirement under Section 25249.6.

            Reasonableness of Civil Penalty

            Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(b)(1) states, in relevant part: “A person who has violated Section 25249.5 or 25249.6 is liable for a civil penalty not to exceed two thousand five hundred dollars ($2,500) per day for each violation in addition to any other penalty established by law.”  Section 25249.7(b)(2) states: “In assessing the amount of a civil penalty for a violation of this chapter, the court shall consider all of the following: (A) The nature and extent of the violation. (B) The number of, and severity of, the violations. (C) The economic effect of the penalty on the violator. (D) Whether the violator took good faith measures to comply with this chapter and the time these measures were taken. (E) The willfulness of the violator’s misconduct. (F) The deterrent effect that the imposition of the penalty would have on both the violator and the regulated community as a whole. (G) Any other factor that justice may require.”

            The Consent Judgment imposes a civil penalty of $1800 on Defendant, with 75% earmarked for California’s Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment and the remaining 25% earmarked for Plaintiff in accordance with Health & Safety Code §25249.12(c)(1) & (d).  Parties entered into the Consent Judgment after arm’s length negotiations.  As indicated by Plaintiff, the amount is reasonable given that 91) the amount is sufficient to remind Defendant to comply with Prop 65; (2) the Consent Judgment already ensures that Defendant’s products will carry the Prop 65 warning; (3) Defendant claims it did not intentionally ignore its obligations under Prop 65 and it immediately took action to withdraw the non-conforming product and will ensure that it complies with Prop 65 in the future; (4) Defendant sold very few nonconforming products prior to receiving Plaintiff’s notice and sold no such products since receiving Plaintiff’s notice; (5) Plaintiff believes the sale of products in violation of Prop 65 is a severe violation; and (6) payment of the penalty provides further incentive for Defendant to comply with Prop 65. 

            Reasonableness of Attorney’s Fees

            Pursuant to 11 Cal. Code Regs. § 3201(b)(1), “a settlement that provides for the giving of a clear and reasonable warning, where there had been no warning provided prior to the sixty-day notice, for an exposure that appears to require a warning, is presumed to confer a significant benefit on the public.” 

            The fees setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.  A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award.  The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided.  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)  Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary.  (Id. at 48, n.23.)  After the trial court has performed the lodestar calculations, it shall consider whether the total award so calculated under all of the circumstances of the case is more than a reasonable amount and, if so, shall reduce the section 1717 award so that it is a reasonable figure.  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-96.)

            Defendant agrees to pay attorney’s fees in the amount of $16,200 as complete reimbursement for Plaintiffs’ attorney’s fees and costs.  The amount of attorney’s fees is reasonable given that Plaintiff’s counsel attests to spending 62.5 hours for a total of $39,268.25 in fees and costs. 

            Proposed Consent Judgment Proper under Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(f)(4)

            For the aforesaid reasons, the Proposed Consent Judgment attached as Exhibit B to the Manning Declaration is approved.  The court finds the Consent Judgment provides for warnings in compliance with Prop 65, the civil penalty provided for reasonable per Health & Safety Code §25249.7(b)(2) and the attorney’s fees included as part of the Consent Judgment are reasonable.  The Consent Judgment is just and serves the public interest.

ORDER

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Approve Consent Judgment is GRANTED.  The Consent Judgment is entered pursuant to CCP §664.6 and the Court retains jurisdiction pursuant to CCP §664.6 to enforce the judgment. 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.
