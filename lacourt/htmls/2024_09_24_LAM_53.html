DEPARTMENT 53 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 21STCV27393    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 53

Samples v. Northern California Mortgage Fund XIV, LLC  21STCV27393

Ex Parte Application  9/24/24

Plaintiff filed two ex parte applications on September 23, 2024, which are presented to the court on September 24, 2024.

First, the court grants in part, and denies in part, plaintiff’s ex parte application for order shortening notice and advancing hearing dates or, in the alternative, advance hearing dates as follows.

The court orders that the hearing on plaintiff’s motion to continue trial is advanced from June 25, 2025, to October 31, 2024, at 10:00 a.m.

The court denies all other relief requested by plaintiff in that ex parte application. 

The court finds that plaintiff has not satisfied the requirement of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1202, subdivision (c), that an applicant for an ex parte order must make an affirmative factual showing in a declaration containing competent testimony based on personal knowledge of irreparable harm, immediate danger, or other statutory basis for granting relief ex parte.

The court does not find good cause to grant plaintiff’s request for an order shortening time on the hearings on the other motions listed in plaintiff’s ex parte application or to prescribe shorter times for the filing and service of papers than the times specified in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b).  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (b).)

Second, the court denies plaintiff’s ex parte application for order to file longer memorandum.  The court finds that plaintiff has not shown good cause for the requested relief.  

The court orders plaintiff to give notice of this order.

PETITION FOR APPROVAL OF COMPROMISE OF CLAIM ON BEHALF OF MINOR CLAIMANT

Petitioner Louise Gill (“Petitioner”) seeks court approval of the settlement made on behalf of minor claimant Ethan Gill (“Minor Claimant”) in this action.

The court notes two defects with Petitioner’s petition.

First, Petitioner did not lodge with the court a complete unredacted version of the proposed “Order Approving Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or Person with a Disability” (Judicial Council form MC-351).  Specifically, the proposed order that was lodged with the court (1) does not complete all required sections, including by failing to set forth the terms of the settlement, and (2) attaches a redacted copy of Attachment 8b(2).

Second, the redacted version of the proposed “Order Approving Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or Person with a Disability” that Petitioner filed with the court is not identified as “Public-Redacts materials from conditionally sealed record.”

Motion to Seal Portions of Joint Reply and Supporting Documents

The court considered the moving and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

The court did not consider “Defendant Eliran Grushkowsky’s Limited Oppositions to Plaintiff Forex Express Corp.’s Notice of Motion and Motion to Seal Mottram Declaration,” filed on September 13, 2024, because it was not filed with the court and served on the parties at least nine court days before the hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Forex Express Corp., d/b/a WireCash (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order filing under seal (1) page 8, lines 20-23, page 9, lines 7 and 22, page 10, lines 1, 2-3, and 12-14, and page 11, lines 1-3 and 11-12 of “Defendants’ Joint Reply in Further Support of the Motion to Quash Non-Party Subpoenas,” filed by defendants Inter & Co. Payments, Inc., Inter & Co., Inc., and Eliran Grushkowsky on April 23, 2024, (2) page 6, lines 1-7 and page 6, line 27 through page 8, line 4 of the “Supplemental Objections and Responses to Defendant Inter & Co. Payments, Inc.’s First Set of Special Interrogatories,” attached as Exhibit A to the declaration of Rachel Feldman filed on April 23, 2024, and (3) page 244, line 1 through page 247, line 25, of the deposition transcript of Plaintiff’s person most qualified, attached as Exhibit D to the declaration of Rachel Feldman filed on April 23, 2024.  Plaintiff requests this relief on the ground that these documents quote from, paraphrase, or discuss information (1) set forth in Plaintiff’s trade secret disclosures, that (2) the court previously ordered sealed.  

Generally, court records are presumed to be open unless confidentiality is required by law.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (c).)¿ If the presumption of access applies, the court may order that a record be filed under seal “if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d).)¿¿Further, in an action brought under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act, “a court shall preserve the secrecy of an alleged trade secret by reasonable means, which may include . . . sealing the records of the action . . . .”  (Civ. Code, § 3426.5.)

First, the court finds, as to the information set forth on page 8, between lines 20-23, page 9, lines 7 and 22, page 10, lines 1, 2-3, and 12-14, and page 11, lines 1-3 and 11-12 of the “Joint Reply in Support of Defendants’ Motions to Quash Subpoenas for Production of Business Records to Amazon Web Services, Inc., and Github Inc.[,]” that Plaintiff has not shown that there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record since those portions of the reply papers do not include information regarding Plaintiff’s trade secrets.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.550, subd. (d)(1).)  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion to seal those portions of the reply papers.

Second, the court finds, as to page 6, lines 1-7 and page 6, line 26 through page 8, line 4 of the “Supplemental Objections and Responses to Defendant Inter & Co. Payments, Inc.’s First Set of Special Interrogatories,” that (1) there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record because it includes information regarding Plaintiff’s potential trade secrets, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record to ensure the confidentiality of Plaintiff’s potential trade secrets, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed, (4) the proposed sealing, which seeks to seal only a few portions of the objections, is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.550, subd. (d); Civ. Code, § 3426.5 [court shall preserve the secrecy of “an alleged trade secret”].)  The court therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion to seal as to that information.

Third, the court finds, as to page 244, line 1, through page 245, line 20, and page 245, line 23 (beginning with “And all of those were stolen, yes”), through page 247, line 16 of the deposition of Jonathan Cooper, that Plaintiff has not met its burden to show that there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record because these portions of the deposition discuss the number of trade secrets allegedly misappropriated and the availability for review of the trade secrets, but do not discuss the substance of any of Plaintiff’s alleged trade secrets.  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion to seal as to that information.

Fourth, the court finds, as to page 245, lines 20-23, and page 247, line 25 of the deposition of Jonathan Cooper, that (1) there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record because it includes information regarding Plaintiff’s potential trade secrets, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record to ensure the confidentiality of Plaintiff’s potential trade secrets, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed, (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.550, subd. (d); Civ. Code, § 3426.5 [court shall preserve the secrecy of “an alleged trade secret”].)  The court therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion to seal as to that information.

            The court grants in part and denies in part plaintiff Forex Express Corp., d/b/a WireCash’s motion to seal portions of joint reply and supporting documents as follows.

The court orders that the unredacted versions of the following documents, lodged with the court on or about April 23, 2024, shall be filed under seal: (1) the “Supplemental Objections and Responses to Defendant Inter & Co. plaintiffs’ motion for attorney’s fees and costs

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George             

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George (“Plaintiffs”) move the court for an order awarding attorney’s fees and costs in their favor, to be paid from the settlement fund created by the “Settlement Agreement and Mutual General Release” executed by Plaintiffs, on the one hand, and defendants Morton Forshpan and Forshpan Capital, LLC (“Defendants”), on the other hand (the “Settlement”), in the total amount of $90,184.84, consisting of $51,009.70 in attorney’s fees and $39,175.14 in costs, pursuant to the common fund doctrine.  (Mot. for Prelim. Approval of Settlement filed June 11, 2024, Brown Decl., Ex. 1, Settlement, p. 1.)

First, the court finds that Plaintiffs have shown that they may recover, from the common fund created by the Settlement, attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to the common fund doctrine.   

“The common fund doctrine is based on the principle that ‘where a common fund exists to which a number of persons are entitled and in their interest successful litigation is maintained for its preservation and protection, an allowance of counsel fees may properly be made from such fund.’  [Citation.]  The purpose of the doctrine is to allow a party, who has paid for counsel to prosecute a lawsuit that creates a fund from which others will benefit, to require those other beneficiaries to bear their fair share of the litigation costs.  [Citation.]  In other words, the common fund doctrine permits plaintiffs’ attorneys to recoup their fees from the fund.”  (Northwest Energetic Services, LLC v. California Franchise Tax Bd. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 841, 878 [internal citations omitted].)  “The common-fund doctrine has been held to apply in favor of a plaintiff who has successfully maintained a stockholder’s derivative action on behalf of a corporation in this state.”  (Fletcher v. A.J. Industries, Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 313, 320.)

Here, Plaintiffs have obtained a benefit for the members of nominal defendant Victorville-Oasis, LLC, by recovering, pursuant to the Settlement, a gross settlement amount of $193,750, of which (1) $25,000 is to be allocated to Plaintiffs for their efforts, and (2) the remainder, after accounting for attorney’s fees and costs, is to be paid, proportional to their interests in the company, to the members of Victorville-Oasis, LLC.  (Mot. for Prelim. Approval of Settlement filed June 11, 2024, Brown Decl., Ex. 1, Settlement, ¶ 3.)  Thus, Plaintiffs have secured a benefit on behalf of the other members of Victorville-Oasis, LLC.

The court notes that Plaintiffs also appear to have obtained an individual benefit, separate and apart from the common fund created for the members of Victorville-Oasis, LLC. Specifically, pursuant to the Settlement, $25,000 of the $193,750 settlement amount will be paid to Plaintiffs “for their efforts, labor, and time involved[.]”  (Mot. for Prelim. Approval of Settlement filed June 11, 2024, Brown Decl., Ex. 1, Settlement, ¶ 3.)  “[W]hen a shareholder brings a derivative suit for the true purpose of advancing his or her ‘personal adverse interests,’ neither equitable doctrine [i.e., neither the common fund nor substantial benefit doctrines] allows an award of attorney fees.”  (Cziraki v. Thunder Cats, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 552, 558.)  However, the award to Plaintiffs for bringing this action does not appear to show that they filed this suit for the “true purpose” of advancing their own personal adverse interests against Victorville-Oasis, LLC.  Moreover, Defendants have not filed an opposition to this motion contending that this exception to the common fund doctrine applies.

The court therefore finds that Plaintiffs have shown that they may recover, from the common fund created by the Settlement, attorney’s fees and costs.  (Northwest Energetic Services, LLC, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 878.)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiffs have supported their request for attorney’s fees in the amount of $51,009.70.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted); Reck v. FCA US LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 682, 691 [“To determine a reasonable attorney fee award, the trial court applies the lodestar method”].)  “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿¿¿ 

Plaintiffs have submitted the declaration of attorney Ethan Brown, in which he attests to the qualifications, skill, and experience of himself and attorney Barnes.  (Brown Decl., ¶¶ 7-9.)  The court finds that the hourly rates requested for attorneys Brown ($795) and Barnes ($495) are reasonable in light of counsel’s qualifications and experience.  (Ibid.)  The court further finds, upon reviewing the time entries attached to the declaration of Brown, that Plaintiffs have shown that their attorneys reasonably expended approximately 299 hours in prosecuting and litigating this action.[1]  (Brown Decl., Ex. A, Time Entries.)  Defendants did not file an opposition to this motion contending that any of the hours claimed were unreasonable or unnecessary, and the court has found that the hours expended were reasonable.  (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 101 [“The party opposing the fee award can be expected to identify the particular charges it considers objectionable”].)

The court therefore finds that Plaintiffs have established a lodestar amount of $151,453.50 ((11 hours x Brown’s $795 hourly rate) + (288.3 hours x Barnes’ $495 hourly rate)).  However, Plaintiffs have requested $51,009.70 on the ground that their fee agreement entitles counsel to 33 percent of their recovery.[2]  (Brown Decl., ¶ 3.)  Thus, the court grants Plaintiffs’ request for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $51,009.70.

Third, the court finds that Plaintiffs have supported their request for costs in the amount of $38,440.83, and therefore grants their request to award costs from the common fund in that amount.  (Northwest Energetic Services, LLC, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 878 [purpose of common fund doctrine is to allow a party to require beneficiaries to bear their fair share of litigation costs]; Brown Decl., Ex. B, Expense Report.) 

The court denies Plaintiffs’ request to recover copying and postage costs in the amount of $734.28 because “postage . . . and photocopying charges, except for exhibits[,]” are not allowable as costs unless “expressly authorized by law[,]” and Plaintiffs did not (1) identify a law expressly authorizing these costs, or (2) present evidence showing that the copying charges were related to exhibits.  (Brown Decl., Ex. B, Expense Report, PDF pp. 33 [$150.30 for printing and copying], 34 [$46.20 for printing and copying, $9.90 for printing and copying, $1.75 for postage], 35 [$1.80 for printing and copying, $12.45 for printing and copying, $64.80 for printing and copying, $0.89 for postage, $3.30 for printing and copying, $48 for printing and copying, $0.67 for postage, $29.40 for printing and copying, $1.05 for printing and copying, $64.85 for printing and copying], 36 [$1.34 for postage, $0.22 for postage, $13.04 for postage, $0.67 for postage, $2.03 for postage, $6.83 for postage, $65.85 for printing and copying, $144.75 for printing and copying, $5.42 for postage, $55.50 for printing and copying, $0.67 for postage, $0.46 for postage], 37 [$1.20 for printing and copying, $0.47 for postage, $0.47 for postage]; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(3).)

ORDER

            The court grants in part plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs as follows.

            The court orders that plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George shall recover from the common fund created by the “Settlement Agreement and Mutual General Release,” executed on April 19, 2024 by and between plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George, on the one hand, and defendants Morton Forshpan and Forshpan Capital, LLC, on the other hand, a total of $89,450.53, consisting of $51,009.70 in attorney’s fees and $38,440.83 in costs.

            The court orders plaintiffs Oscar George, Robert Margolis, Tiros George Jr., and Tiros George to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
