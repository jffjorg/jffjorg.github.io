Case Number: 21VECV01059    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: 107


SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA
COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES
NORTHWEST DISTRICT

Chelato Chacon,

   v.


 Zeyad Elalami, et al. 
         Defendants.    Case Number             Department
      21VECV01059       107
COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
1) Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One; Request for Sanctions
(2) Compel Responses to Requests for Production; Request for Sanctions
(3) Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories; Request for Sanctions
(4) Deem Requests for Admission Admitted; Request for Sanctions
(5) Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two; Request for Sanctions

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:
(1) Grant against Elalami
(2) Grant
(3) Grant
(4) Grant against Elalami
(5) Grant against Luxor

Plaintiff Chelato Chacon moves to compel responses to discovery requests from and to deem requests for admission admitted by Defendants. This court grants Plaintiff’s motions. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions, which this court grants in a reduced amount. In opposition, Defendants Zeyad Elalami and Luxor Funding Group, Inc. request sanctions against Plaintiff, which this court denies.

I. BACKGROUND
This is a landlord-tenant case. The following factual allegations are drawn from the Complaint (as defined below). 
 
A. Factual 
 
In November 2014, plaintiff Chelato Chacon (“Plaintiff”) entered a lease agreement (the “Lease”) regarding a certain residential real property (the “Property”) with its prior owner (the “Original Owner”). (Complaint p. 2.) Between then and 2021, Plaintiff and the Original Owner made verbal alterations to the Lease, including rent increases; Plaintiff made timely rent payments, performed under the Lease, and made repairs to the Property without any rent offsets; and many habitability issues arose at the Property, including pest infestations and problems with heating. (Id. at pp. 2–3.)  
 
On January 20, 2021, Plaintiff learned that defendant Zeyad Elalami (“Elalami”) and the corporation in which Elalami holds multiple officer positions, defendant Luxor Funding Group, Inc. (“Luxor” and, together with Elalami, “Defendants”), had acquired the Property from the Original Owner, making Defendants the new owners of the Property. (Complaint pp. 1, 3.) The same day, Elalami informed Plaintiff that he was immediately increasing Plaintiff’s monthly rent by approximately 47.14% although Elalami did not provide Plaintiff with proper notice of any rent increase. (Complaint p. 3.) Plaintiff protested that such a rent increase violated rent control and stabilization laws governing the Property and continued to timely pay rent at his previous rate. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami accepted such payment for the months of February 2021, March 2021, and April 2021, but thereafter returned Plaintiff’s attempted payments to him. (Ibid.) Elalami made verbal and written implied threats to damage Plaintiff’s FICO credit score. (Id. at p. 3.) On February 1, 2021, Elalami attempted to issue Plaintiff a three-day notice to pay or quit via text message, alleging that Plaintiff had failed to make timely rent payments. (Id. at pp. 3–4.) Elalami told Plaintiff that he has “no lease for the Property” and Plaintiff received notice that an unlawful detainer action had been filed. (Id. at p. 4.)  
 
B. Procedural 
 
On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the warranty of habitability, (3) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (5) retaliatory eviction in violation of Civil Code section 1942.5, (6) declaratory judgment, and (7) injunctive relief. On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff amended the Complaint, adding a cause of action for (8) violation of Los Angeles Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance.

On May 3, 2022, Luxor responded to a request for production of documents from Plaintiff, a set of requests for admission from Plaintiff, and form interrogatories from Plaintiff. (Aly Decl. Exh. A.)

On August 11, 2023, Plaintiff served Elalami with written discovery requests consisting of form interrogatories, special interrogatories, requests for admission, and requests for production. (Motion p. 3.) On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff served Luxor with supplemental written discovery requests consisting of interrogatories and requests for production. (Id. at p. 4.) On August 21, 2023, Plaintiff served Luxor with a second set of form interrogatories. (Ibid.)

On April 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel responses to form interrogatories, set one from each of Defendants (“Motion #1”), a motion to compel responses to requests for production from each of Defendants (“Motion #2”), a motion to compel responses to special interrogatories from each of Defendants (“Motion #3”), a motion to deem requests for admission admitted by each of Defendants (“Motion #4”), and a motion to compel responses to form interrogatories, set two from each of Defendants (“Motion #5” and, together with Motion #1, Motion #2, Motion #3, and Motion #4, the “Motions”). On June 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition to the Motions. On June 17, 2024, Defendants filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to the Motions, and the same day, Plaintiff filed replies (the “Replies”) to the Oppositions.

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

A. Meet and Confer Efforts

“Unlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) 

Because no response has been provided to the instant discovery requests and Plaintiff files motions to compel, not motions to compel further, there is no requirement for Parties to have met and conferred prior to the filing of the Motions.¿ 

B. Timeliness of Filings

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

The Motions and the Replies were timely, and the Oppositions were untimely. Although we do not decline to consider the Oppositions on these grounds, we remind Defendants of the rules for timeliness of filings.

III. LEGAL STANDARD

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response,…[t]he party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2030.290.) And, “a party's failure to object to interrogatories within 30 days, or such additional time as extended by the court, constitutes a waiver of any right to object.” (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Similarly, “[i]f a party to whom¿a¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]… [t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the¿demand.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.300.) In the absence of any response to the discovery request, all that a motion to compel need show is that a set of¿interrogatories¿was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court, supra, at 905–06.)¿ 

“Any party may obtain discovery… by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared.”¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2033.250, subd. (a).) If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Cal. C. Civ. Proc § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).)¿ 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the discovery request without substantial justification necessitated the motion to compel or motion to deem request for admissions as admitted, unless other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc §§ 2030.290, subd. (c), 2033.280, subd. (c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) As to the amount of sanctions, Section¿2023.030, subdivision (a) authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing “the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result” of a party's misuse of the discovery process. (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.) 

IV. DISCUSSION

A. The Motions

Plaintiff submits evidence that he served a set of form interrogatories on Elalami, a set of special interrogatories on Elalami, a set of requests for production of documents on Elalami, and a set of requests for admission on Elalami on August 11, 2023. (Brockmeier Decl. Exh. A.) Plaintiff submits evidence that he served a supplemental interrogatory request and a supplemental request for production of documents on Luxor on August 18, 2023. (Id. at Exh. B.) Plaintiff submits evidence that he served a second set of form interrogatories on Luxor on August 21, 2023. (Id. at Exh. C.) Plaintiff further submits evidence that no response to any of the discovery at issue had been received by the date that the Motions were filed. (Id. at ¶ 7.) 

Defendants do not dispute that they did not timely respond to these discovery requests. Although Defendants point out that Luxor responded to previous discovery requests propounded by Plaintiff, this is irrelevant to the Motions, which relate to subsequent discovery requests on both Defendants.

Defendants argue that “Plaintiff seems to be requesting that [Elalami] duplicate the discovery responses of [Luxor] from May 3, 2022.” (Opposition p. 6.) Defendants argue, “[i]t would be reasonable to construe the previous discovery responses as responses by both Defendants, given that both Defendants have Answered the Complaint through [Elalami], and given that Plaintiff argues an alter ego theory of liability in the Complaint. [Elalami] signed the verifications on behalf of [Luxor], so it would be unreasonable to assume that he would respond to discovery any differently. Moreover, [Elalami] with these filings adopts those responses.” (Id. at p. 7.) However, each of Defendants is a separate party, and each party on whom a discovery request is served must respond to that discovery request. Plaintiff is not required to assume that Defendants’ responses to discovery requests are the same because the Complaint alleges an alter ego theory of liability or because Elalami is a signatory of Luxor. Additionally, Elalami may not adopt through the Oppositions Luxor’s responses to the discovery requests propounded on it. As Plaintiff points out in the Replies (Reply pp. 4–5), the discovery requests propounded on each of Defendants are different. And, the Oppositions are simply not code-compliant discovery responses.

Thus, we find that Plaintiff has met his burden of showing that the discovery requests at issue were properly served on Defendants and that no reply of any kind was received.

Accordingly, this court GRANTS Motion #1 against Elalami, GRANTS Motion #2, GRANTS Motion #3, GRANTS Motion #4 against Elalami, and GRANTS Motion #5 against Luxor.

B. Sanctions

Because we grant the Motions, we find that bringing them was necessary, and sanctions in favor of Plaintiff are therefore mandatory.

Each of the Motions requests sanctions in the amount of $3,911.65 against Defendants and Defendants’ counsel for a total sanctions request of $19,558.25. (Motion p. 7.) This is based on a $61.65 filing fee for each of the Motions, three hours of attorney time spent preparing each of the Motions at a rate of $550.00 per hour, two hours of attorney time reviewing each of the Oppositions and preparing each of the Replies at the same rate, and two hours of attorney time spent travelling to and attending a hearing on each of the Motions at the same rate. (Brockmeier Decl. ¶ 9.)

These calculations reveal some duplication in time. The Motions are now being heard together, meaning that Plaintiff’s counsel need only travel to and appear at one hearing. We therefore find it reasonable for Plaintiff’s counsel to bill only two hours of his time travelling to and appearing at the hearing on the Motions. We also note that each of the Motions are largely duplicative of each other, and each contains large sections that are identical to sections in the other Motions. The same is true of the Oppositions and the Replies. We therefore find it reasonable for Plaintiff’s counsel to bill only five hours of his time drafting all of the Motions and only three hours of his time considering the Oppositions and drafting the Replies. Thus, we find it reasonable for Plaintiff’s counsel to bill a total of ten hours bringing and defending the Motions. 

We find Plaintiff’s counsel’s billing rate to be reasonable. Ten hours of attorney time at a rate of $550.00 per hour totals to $5,500.00. We find it reasonable for Plaintiff to seek to recover the five $61.65 filing fees for the Motions. Five motions at $61.65 each totals to $308.25, and $308.25 and $5,500.00 sum to $5,808.25.

Defendants’ counsel substituted in as counsel for Defendants on April 23, 2024, months after the discovery requests at issue were served on Defendants and only three days before the Motions were filed. We find that Defendants’ counsel cannot therefore be said to be responsible for the necessity of the Motions and that it would be unjust to impose sanctions against Defendants’ counsel.

Accordingly, this court GRANTS sanctions against only Defendants and in favor of Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $5,808.25.

Because Defendants unsuccessfully opposed the Motions, sanctions in favor of Defendants are not warranted.

Accordingly, this court DENIES the sanctions requested in the Oppositions.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

(2) Demurrer

 

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Defendants Sergio Miramontes and Rosa Miramontes move to quash service of summons and demur to the complaint filed by plaintiff Jubilio Escalera. This court grants the motion to quash service of summons and denies the demurrer as moot.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On January 1, 2021, plaintiff Jubilio Escalera agreed to do certain construction work on a property owned by defendants Sergio Miramontes (“Sergio”) and Rosa Miramontes (“Rosa” and, together with Sergio, “Defendants”). (Complaint pp. 2–3.) To do so, Plaintiff furnished certain construction materials, for which Defendants later refused to pay Plaintiff. (Id. at p. 4.) On May 11, 2022, after completing the agreed construction work, Plaintiff recorded a mechanic’s lien against Defendants’ property as Instrument No. 20220514463. (Ibid.)

 

On August 12, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this action by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging a single cause of action for foreclosure of mechanic’s lien.

 

Plaintiff filed proofs of service (the “Proofs of Service”) indicating that the summons and the Complaint were personally served on each of Defendants on December 21, 2022 at 21025 Costanso Street, Woodland Hills, CA 91364.

 

On May 2, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to quash service of summons (the “Motion”).

 

On May 16, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the Complaint.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

A motion to quash service of summons may be brought on the grounds that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over the defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿418.10, subd. (a)(1).) “[W]here a defendant properly moves to¿quash¿service¿of¿summons¿the burden is on the plaintiff to prove facts requisite to an effective¿service.” (Sheard v. Superior Court (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 207, 211.) 

 

Personal service may be accomplished by personally delivering a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.) If the complaint and summons were personally delivered to, i.e., handed to, defendant then he could be said to have been “personally served.” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 389.) 

 

Filing a proof of service by a registered process server creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper. (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, supra, 199 Cal.App.4th 383 at 390; Evid. Code, § 647 [“The return of a process server registered pursuant to… the Business and Professions Code upon process or notice establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return”].)¿However, the presumption only arises if the proof of service complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.¿ (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441–42.)

 

Although the filing of a proof of service by a registered process server creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper (Evid. Code § 647), that presumption may be impeached by contrary evidence submitted by a defendant. (See, e.g., Fernandes v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932, 941, fn. 6.)   

  

If service of summons is not properly and validly effectuated by one or more of the approved methods, California courts lack personal jurisdiction over the defendants, and any judicial actions taken without such service violates principles of fundamental due process and is therefore void. (County. of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1231.) 

 

Filing a demurrer does not constitute a general appearance and does not waive the issue of lack of personal jurisdiction if it is done after or simultaneous with the filing of a motion to quash service of summons. (Air Machine Com. SRL v. Superior Court (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 414, 428; Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (e).)

 

III. DISCUSSION 

 

Defendants argue that the Complaint offends due process and fails to authorize personal jurisdiction over them because the Complaint and summons were never effectively served on them. (Motion p. 4.) Defendants argue that personal service on them at 21025 Constanso was impossible because the unit is unoccupied, and their residence is actually 21027 Constanso Street, Woodland Hills, CA 91364. (Motion, p. 4; Sergio Decl. ¶¶ 3–5, Exh. A; Rosa Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. A.) Each of Defendants submit a declaration signed under penalty of perjury in support of this claim.

 

To further support their claim, Defendants submit evidence that Susanna Turner (“Turner”), a paralegal of their counsel, called the court on May 24, 2024, and at that time was informed by a Call Center Clerk that no summons had been issued in the instant matter, Case No.  22VECV01156. (Turner Decl. ¶¶ 3–4.) While the truth of the matter of any assertions the Call Center Clerk made to Turner regarding the issuance of a summons in this matter would amount to hearsay, a review of the case file demonstrates that no summons has been filed in this action.   

 

As a preliminary matter, the court notes that Plaintiff’s process server Richard Williams (“Williams”) is not a registered California process server. (Rosa Proof of Service; Sergio Proof of Service.) As such, the Proofs of Service themselves do not create a rebuttable presumption that service was proper. To the extent Plaintiff contends service was properly made, Plaintiff must submit evidence or a declaration of Williams in support of the Proofs of Service. However, Plaintiff has not opposed the Motion. In addition, as discussed above, Defendants submitted evidence that neither Sergio nor Rosa was served with the summons and the Complaint in the manner described in the Proof of Service given the incorrect address and given that neither Sergio nor Rosa received service of the documents on the alleged date. Finally, the case file for the instant matter does not appear to include an issued filed summons, although the court acknowledges that this does not preclude a summons from having being issued to Plaintiff for service on Defendants.  

 

Plaintiff has not met his burden of demonstrating facts justifying the existence of this court’s personal jurisdiction over Defendants given that Defendants submitted evidence showing they were not served with the summons and the Complaint and that the Proofs of Service were not completed by a registered process server.  

 

Because the Demurrer was filed after the Motion, its filing does not constitute a general appearance or waiver of the issue of lack of personal jurisdiction by Defendants. However, this court’s lack of personal jurisdiction over Defendants renders the Demurrer moot.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion and DENIES the Demurrer as moot.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

  Relieved as Counsel

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

Plaintiff’s attorney Gerald L. Marcus (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel.

 

Counsel’s motion is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On October 25, 2021, plaintiff Harris Davis (“Plaintiff”) tripped and fell over a door mat at a restaurant owned by defendants Norms and Norms Restaurants, LLC (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”). Plaintiff thereby incurred injuries. (Complaint pp. 3–4.)

 

On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging a single cause of action for premises liability — negligence.

 

Counsel filed the instant motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”) on May 15, 2024. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284, subd. (2)). California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.         Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2.         A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (1)  (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3.         Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4.         The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).) The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case. (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-051).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on form MC-052 that “[Counsel] and client have a conflict of interest that precludes further representation of the client. There has been a break down in the client relationship that precludes further representation. [Counsel] is unable to represent client as a result of this breakdown and conflict.”

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service or mail. If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.         The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

2.         The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

 

As used in this rule, “current” means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011, subdivision (b) applies.

 

When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement. Each filing of the Motion includes a proof of service indicating that it was served on Plaintiff by first-class mail and by e-mail. Counsel’s declaration on MC-052 states that the filings of the Motion were served on Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last-known address and that the address at which service was effected was confirmed to be Plaintiff’s current address within the past 30 days by telephone.

 

(D) Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Protective Order; Request for Sanctions

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Plaintiff Katy E. Weber moves for a protective order and requests monetary sanctions against Defendant and Defendant’s counsel. In opposition. Defendant Ford Motor Company requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel. This court denies the motion for a protective order and denies Plaintiff’s request for sanctions. This court grants Defendant’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,200.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On November 27, 2023, plaintiff Katy E. Weber (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint (the “Complaint”) against defendant Ford Motor Company (“Defendant”) alleging violations of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act arising out of Defendant’s failure to replace or repurchase a certain 2022 Ford Explorer (the “Vehicle”) that did not conform to warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts.

 

On March 28, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a deposition notice that included requests for production of documents. (Proudfoot Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. C.)

 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion (the “Motion”) for a protective order, which Defendant opposed on June 11, 2024. Plaintiff did not timely reply to Defendant’s opposition.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.420 provides that “[b]efore, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).) 

 

For good cause shown, courts may enter protective orders regarding unwarranted annoyances, embarrassment, oppression, undue burden and expense, and commercial information.  (See Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 1138, 1141 [court abused discretion in compelling responses that may contain sensitive commercial information, without first reviewing them in camera to ascertain relevance, and whether sensitive matter should be redacted].)

 

The fact that information sought is relevant to the subject matter does not preclude relief via a protective order. A court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party or other natural person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (b).) Unlike other discovery orders, a protective order may be granted simply on the court’s determination that justice so requires. The motion is directed to the court’s inherent power to control the proceedings before it. The granting or denial of relief, therefore, lies within the sound discretion of the judge and is reviewable only for abuse of discretion. (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 255, 379–381, overruled on other grounds.)  

 

The burden of proof is generally on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Good cause requires a showing of specific facts demonstrating undue burden, etc., and justifying the relief sought. (See Goodman v. Citizens Life & Casualty Ins. Co. (1967) 253 Cal.App.3d 807, 819.) The facts are normally established in declaration by counsel for the party seeking the protective order. (Ibid.) The declaration must contain competent evidence—i.e., first-hand knowledge of the facts. (Ibid.) Hearsay allegations on information and belief and conclusory statements that particular relief is necessary are not enough. (Ibid.)  

 

Sanctions are mandatory “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless [the court] finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420, subd. (h).) 

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants seek to depose her “to do nothing but cause harassment of Plaintiff” and that “[i]t is burdensome and harassing to demand that witnesses appear for depositions in person solely because [Plaintiff] noticed the deposition of its PMK/PMQ and would not accept a 998 that does not ensure payment.” (Motion p. 4.) Plaintiff further argues that the deposition notice “contains an extensive request for production of documents,” which evidences Defendant’s efforts “to circumvent the Court’s order and the notice requirement in the Code of Civil Procedure § 2031 requirement that a party has thirty days to respond to a written demand for production of documents by serving deposition notices with unduly burdensome document production requests.” (Id. at p. 5.)

 

Defendant attaches a certified copy of the deposition notice at issue to its opposition. (Proudfoot Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. C.) The deposition notice at issue states that the deposition of Plaintiff is to be held “remotely via videoconferencing,” not in person as Plaintiff alleges. (Id. at Exh. C.)

 

Although Plaintiff argues that Defendant is attempting to circumvent an order by this court regarding discovery, it is not clear to what order Plaintiff is referring because this court has not yet issued any discovery orders in this action.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.030, subdivision (c)(2) requires that a party serving a request for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling permit the other party at least 30 days to produce the requested documents or things. The deposition notice at issue was served on Plaintiff on March 28, 2024 and set the deposition and the time to produce the requested documents and things for 56 days later, on May 23, 2024. (Proudfood Decl. Exh. C.) Because this amount of time is greater than the minimum 30 days required by California Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.030, subdivision (c)(2), we disagree with Plaintiff’s argument that the deposition notice’s requests for production of documents and things circumvents the timing requirements of written discovery.

 

Plaintiff submits no evidence to support its allegation that Defendant seeks to depose Plaintiff to harass her. Although Plaintiff alleges that Defendant served its deposition notice on Plaintiff in retaliation for or response to her service of a deposition notice of Defendant’s person most knowledgeable, the deposition notices that Plaintiff certifies and attaches to the Motion were served on Defendant on April 30, 2024, thirty-three days after Defendant’s service of its deposition notice on Plaintiff on March 28, 2024. (Whitworth Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. A.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff fails to meet her burden of showing good cause for the protective order.

 

Because the Motion is not successfully brought, Plaintiff is not entitled to sanctions. However, because it successfully opposed the Motion, sanctions in favor of Defendant are mandatory.

 

Defendant seeks $1,400 in sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel. (Opposition p. 9; Proudfoot Decl. ¶ 8.) This is based on five hours of attorney time spent preparing the opposition at a rate of $200 per hour and an anticipated two hours of attorney time spent reviewing any reply to the opposition and attending the hearing on the Motion at the same rate. (Proudfoot Decl. ¶ 8.) We find the rate at which Defendant’s counsel bills per hour to be reasonable. We also find the five hours spent preparing the opposition to be reasonable, but we note that no reply to the opposition was ever filed and therefore find that only one hour spent attending the hearing on the Motion is reasonable.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion and DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions. This court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,200.
