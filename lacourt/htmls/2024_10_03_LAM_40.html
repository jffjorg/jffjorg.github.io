DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV12948    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

WAWANESA GENERAL INSURANCE COMPANY,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

CHERYL LYN MARTINEZ, an individual; JOYCE ROBLES, an individual; and DOES 1 through 30, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          23STCV12948

 Hearing Date:   October 3, 2024

 Trial Date:        February 21, 2025

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication [RES ID # 0226]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Wawanesa General Insurance Company (Wawanesa) sues Defendants Cheryl Lyn Martinez (Martinez), Joyce Robles (Robles), (collectively, Defendants), and Does 1 through 30 pursuant to a June 7, 2023, Complaint alleging a single claim for declaratory relief.

The claim arises from the following series of events. On September 5, 2021, Martinez purchased an automobile insurance policy from Wawanesa. On January 10, 2022, Defendants Martinez and Robles were involved in a collision while operating the subject vehicle. Defendants filed a claim with Wawanesa for injuries and damages, which Wawanesa denied.  

On September 15, 2022, Defendants filed a Petition to Compel Arbitration. On December 8, 2022, the Court granted Defendants’ petition. The Court notes that on January 13, 2024, Department 53 denied relation of that action (case no. 22STCP03407) to the instant action.

Wawanesa seeks a declaration of rights and duties between the parties in relation to insurance coverage bargained for by Defendants with Wawanesa and pursuant to which Defendants seek a payout connected to a January 10, 2022, accident involving Defendant Martinez and an uninsured motorist. Specifically, Wawanesa seeks a judgment declaring that no rights, duties, or obligations arose, exist, or may in the future arise or exist under and by virtue of the provisions of the Wawanesa policy and with respect to the claims for benefits and/or coverage under the Wawanesa policy made by Defendants and Does 1 through 30, that arose out of the alleged January 10, 2022, accident.

B. Motion Before the Court

On June 24, 2024, Wawanesa filed the instant motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication on the grounds that there are no triable issues of material fact regarding whether Defendants Martinez and Robles breached either the (1) "Duties After An Accident or A Loss" condition, or (2) "Fraud and Misrepresentation" condition of the policy with Wawanesa, thereby eliminating further right to policy benefits relating to the alleged January 10, 2022 accident. Wawanesa requests that the Court “declare that Defendants Cheryl Lyn Martinez and Joyce Robles, and each of them, are not entitled to any benefits or coverage under the Wawanesa policy issued to Defendant Cheryl Lyn Martinez, relating to or arising out of the alleged accident of January 10, 2022, including claims for Uninsured/Underinsured Motorists Coverage and Medical Payments Coverage.” (Mot. p. 11:11-15.)

On September 19, 2024, the Defendants opposed the motion on the grounds that there are genuine issues of material fact concerning Defendants’ compliance with the insurance policy and Wawanesa’s handling of the claim.

On September 26, 2024, Wawanesa submitted a Further Declaration of counsel Richard Knapp attaching the full auto insurance policy. The Declaration of Michelle Gonzalez, submitted with the June 24, 2024 motion, inadvertently only attached part of the policy.  

On September 30, 2024, Wawanesa replied to the motion.

Wawanesa’s motion is now before the Court.

II. Motion

A. Requests for Judicial Notice

Pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d), the Court may take judicial notice of “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States”. The court however may not take judicial notice of the truth of the contents of the documents. (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) Documents are only judicially noticeable to show their existence and what orders were made such that the truth of the facts and findings within the documents are not judicially noticeable. (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 885.) 

Per Wawanesa’s request, the Court takes judicial notice of the following:

(1)   (Tentative) Order on Petition to Compel Arbitration dated December 8, 2022, issued in Martinez v. Wawanesa, Los Angeles County Superior Court case no. 22STCP03407, adopted as the final Order. (MSJ, Request for Judicial Notice.)

(2)   Notice of Petition and Petition to Compel Arbitration filed in Martinez v. Wawanesa, Los Angeles County Superior Court case no. 22STCP03407. (Further Request for Judicial Notice.)

The Court notes that on January 13, 2024, Department 53 denied relation of the present action to the above-mentioned action, case no. 22STCP03407.

B. Evidentiary Objections

Opposition Evidentiary Objections:

(1)   Exhibit 2, Wawanesa Policy: OVERRULED

(2)   Exhibit 14, Forensic Report: Not ruled on as not material to disposition of this motion.  

C. Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact for trial or that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) A party may also seek summary adjudication of select causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty, which may be made by a standalone motion or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and proceeds in all procedural respects like a motion for summary judgment, but which must completely dispose of the challenged cause of action, affirmative defense, claim for damages, or issue of duty. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (f)(1)-(2), (t).)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) To meet this burden, a moving party must show not only “that the [responding party] does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the [responding party] cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the moving party to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The moving party “must also produce evidence that the [responding party] cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

“Once the [moving party] … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the [responding party] … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The responding may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the [responding party] cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

D. Analysis

Wawanesa moves for summary judgment, or in the alternative summary adjudication, on the grounds that there is no triable issue regarding the following facts material to the declaratory relief action:

(1)   Defendants Cheryl Lyn Martinez and Joyce Robles breached the "Duties After An Accident or A Loss" condition of the policy with Wawanesa by failing to fully cooperate with Wawanesa's claim investigation, thereby eliminating further right to policy benefits relating to the alleged accident of January 10, 2022; and

(2)   Defendants Cheryl Lyn Martinez and Joyce Robles breached the "Fraud and Misrepresentation" condition of the policy with Wawanesa, thereby eliminating further right to policy benefits relating to the alleged accident of January 10, 2022.

The parties do not dispute that Defendant Martinez purchased an automobile policy, policy number 38369770, from Wawanesa on September 5, 2021. (Undisputed Material Fact (UMF) No. 1.) The relevant provisions of the policy are as follows:

 

“Duties After An Accident Or A Loss: We have no duty to provide coverage under this policy unless there has been full compliance with the following duties…

B. A ‘person’ seeking any coverage must:

1. Cooperate with us in the investigation, settlement or defense of any claim or suit.

2. Promptly send us copies of any notices or legal papers received in connection with the accident or loss.

3. Submit, as often as we reasonably require:

a. To physical exams by physicians we select. We will pay for these exams.

b. To examination under oath and sign, date and return same.

4. Authorize us to obtain:

a. Medical reports; and

b. Other pertinent records.

5. Submit a sworn proof of loss when required by us. This proof of loss may require a signed affidavit.” (Mot. pp. 3-4.)

“Legal Action Against Us:

A. No legal action may be brought against us until there has been full compliance with all the terms of this policy ....” (Mot. p. 5.)

“Fraud and Misrepresentation: Coverage is not provided to any ‘person’ who conceals or misrepresents any material fact or circumstance relating to this insurance:

1. At the time application is made; or

2. At any time during the policy period; or

3. In connection with the presentation, investigation or settlement of a claim.” (Mot. p. 8.)

 

Wawanesa states that Defendants Martinez and Robles violated the “Duties After An Accident Or A Loss” provision because they failed or refused to provide separate recorded statements, separate examinations, or the claim related documents as requested by Wawanesa. (Mot. p. 6; Knapp Decl., Ex. 15, Martinez's Response to Requests for Admission, Set One, (Martinez RFA) nos. 5-10, Ex. 16, Robles's Response to Requests for Admission, Set One, (Robles RFA), nos. 5-10.). Wawensa further alleges that the failure or refusal to provide the requested items violates the “Fraud and Misrepresentation” provision of the policy because the Defendants “misrepresented and concealed the accident's occurrence, including the totality of facts and circumstances surrounding the loss.” (Mot. p. 10.)

Thus, because Wawanesa has established the above provisions in the insurance policy and the Defendants’ failure to comply with the provisions, Wawanesa has met its initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists.

The burden then shifts to the Defendants to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action.

Defendants argue that the motion is defective because Wawanesa only attached the policy cover page and declaration pages to its motion but neglected to attach the complete policy. (See Gonzalez Decl., Ex 2.) However, the provisions at issue are copied in full in the body of Wawanesa’s motion. Further, Defendants do not argue that they are unaware of the policy or are not in possession of the full policy themselves. Although Wawanesa initially attached only part of the policy, Wawanesa subsequently submitted the complete policy. (See Knapp Further Decl., Ex. 2, filed September 26, 2024.) Wawanesa’s error has not prejudiced the Defendants from meeting their burden of showing that no triable issue of material fact exists.

Defendants further assert that there are issues of material fact because Wawanesa attempted to schedule an examination under oath before Defendants made a formal demand for arbitration in July 2022, and that Wawanesa denied coverage for the claim in August 2022. (Opp. pp. 11-12.) The Defendants argue that it is unclear if Wawanesa is arguing that the Defendants breached the policy prior to the demand for arbitration. (Opp. p. 12.) Otherwise, they contend that Wawanesa has failed to show that an examination under oath must precede a demand for arbitration.

However, the scope, applicability, or circumstances surrounding the demand to arbitrate are not at issue in this action. This action was brought to decide the question of other rights and obligations between the parties. While some overlap exists, Wawanesa moves for summary judgment in this action on the grounds that the Defendants violated the express terms of the insurance policy by failing to cooperate with Wawanesa’s investigation, and thus the Defendants are not entitled to policy benefits. Consequently, Defendants’ arguments pertaining to the arbitration demand do not create a triable issue of material fact in this action.

Lastly, Defendants argue that Wawanesa has not shown that the Defendants concealed or misrepresented a material fact or circumstance in violation of the “Fraud and Misrepresentation” provision. Defendant’s failure or refusal to provide the information requested by Wawanesa or submit to examinations under oath does not establish concealment or misrepresentation. Some of the facts Wawanesa describes, for example, that Robles was listed as a “transient” in the police report or the timing of when Martinez obtained the insurance policy, imply that Wawanesa believes there to have been fraud involved. (Reply p. 14; see also, UMF No. 13.) However, Wawanesa has not proven its implications of fraud such that a factfinder could not reasonably find otherwise. There is a dispute of material fact as to whether the Defendants violated the “Fraud and Misrepresentation” provision of the insurance policy.

Nonetheless, Wawanesa established that there is no triable issue of material fact that the Defendants violated the “Duties After An Accident Or A Loss” provision of the insurance policy. That provision requires Defendants to cooperate with Wawanesa in the investigation, including providing documents as requested and submitting to examination under oath. Defendants do not dispute that they failed, at least in part, to provide the documents or examinations. (UMF 15-20.) None of their additional material facts indicate any effort to cooperate that materially disputes their failure to cooperate. Accordingly, Defendants have not met their burden to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether they breached the terms of the insurance policy.

Accordingly, Wawanesa’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff Wawanesa General Insurance Company’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

ERIN JOYCE dba ERIN JOYCE LAW, PC, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

DEREK KHANNA, an individual; DOES 1 through 100, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          23STCV28020

 Hearing Date:   October 3, 2024

 Trial Date:        None Set

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Sealing Limited Portions of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint [RES ID # 1353]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Erin Joyce, dba Erin Joyce Law (Joyce), sues Defendant Derek Khanna (Khanna), and Does 1 through 100 pursuant to a November 15, 2023, Complaint and February 13, 2024, First Amended Complaint, alleging claims of (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Quantum Meruit, and (3) Common Count: Services Provided.

The claims arise from allegations that Khanna retained Joyce as legal counsel pursuant to a legal representation agreement (the Agreement). The Complaint alleges that Khanna owes Joyce an outstanding balance of $51,571.00 under the Agreement.

B. Motion Before the Court

On August 26, 2024, Joyce filed a motion to seal two sections of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint.  Specifically, Page 1, lines 24-25 and Page 3, lines 7-12 (Paragraph 12) in both the Complaint and First Amended Complaint.

On September 16, 2024, Khanna filed a “motion” (without a reservation number) in support of Joyce’s motion to seal.

On September 19, 2024, Khanna filed a second, longer “motion” (again, without a separate reservation number) in support of Joyce’s motion to seal.

 

II. Motion to Seal

A. Procedural Requirements 

1. Motion to Seal – Moving Papers 

A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.551, subd. (b)(1).) 

 Here, Joyce has filed a motion to seal supported by points and authorities. (Mot. pp. 3-7.)

2. Motion to Seal – Service 

A copy of the motion or application must be served on all parties that have appeared in the case. Unless the court orders otherwise, any party that already possesses copies of the records to be placed under seal must be served with a complete, unredacted version of all papers as well as a redacted version.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551, subd. (b)(2).) 

Here, the motion to seal was served on Khanna on August 26, 2024. (6/26/2024 Proof of Service.)

3. Motion to Seal – Lodging 

Unless good cause exists, a record to be filed under seal must be put in an envelope or other appropriate container labeled ‘CONDITIONALLY UNDER SEAL,’ have a cover sheet affixed thereto with an appropriate caption page stating the envelope contains a motion to file a record under seal and be lodged in the sealed envelope with the Court. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551, subds. (b)(4), (d)(2), (d)(4).) 

 Here, this requirement need not be satisfied because Joyce has previously filed the Complaint and FAC at issue in the public record.

4. Motion to Seal – Redacted and Unredacted Versions 

If necessary to prevent disclosure, any motion to seal and any supporting documents must be filed in a public redacted version and lodged in a complete, unredacted version conditionally under seal. The cover of the redacted version must identify it as “Public-Redacts materials from conditionally sealed record” while the cover of the unredacted version must identify it as “May Not Be Examined Without Court Order-Contains material from conditionally sealed record.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551, subd. (b)(5).) 

 Here, because nondisclosure cannot be effected by filing unredacted copies of documents already in the public record, this requirement need not be satisfied. 

B. Substantive Discussion 

1. Motion to Seal – Findings 

“The public has a First Amendment right of access to civil litigation documents filed in court and used at trial or submitted as a basis for adjudication.” (Savaglio v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 588, 596 (Savaglio), citing NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1208-1209, fn. 25.) 

Therefore, before a trial court orders a record sealed, it must hold a hearing and make express findings set forth in California Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subdivision (d): (1) there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. (Savaglio, supra, at p. 596; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d).) A court’s order allowing documents to be filed under seal must contain express factual findings establishing these five factors. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d).)  

Joyce states that there is an overriding interest that supports sealing the record because although disclosure of otherwise confidential information “is permitted in an action by an attorney to collect fees owed pursuant to Evidence Code section 958, there are in such cases two important principles that are in conflict, i.e., the attorney’s duty of confidentiality owed to a client, and the attorney’s right to seek payment for legal services rendered. That conflict may be reconciled in this case by permitting the redaction and sealing of specific portions of the complaints filed.” (Mot. p. 6:14-19.) Joyce asserts that because Khanna has expressed an objection to the language included in the Complaint and FAC, he will not be prejudiced by the redaction. (Mot. p. 6: 21-26.)

Further the “proposed sealing is narrowly tailored to serve the overriding interest, as the motion only requests the redaction and sealing of information that Defendant believes could embarrass him.” Joyce states that no less restrictive means exist because Joyce is only requesting redaction of one paragraph that contains “information that could potentially embarrass Defendant as a professional. This is the least restrictive means that achieves both the minimizing of embarrassment to the Defendant and ensures the Court records remain public.” (Mot. p. 7:9-11.)

In Khanna’s amended “motion” in support of the motion to seal, he emphasizes that the language at issue constitutes protected client secrets that warrant redaction. (Mot. in Support, pp. 5-7.) The language at issue pertains to information about “allegations by the State Bar (due to a false complaint of Defendant’s former business partner)” that Joyce came to know of during her representation of Khanna. (Mot. in Support, p. 5:14-15.) Khanna also notes that the California State Bar’s Special Deputy Trial Counsel investigated whether the cited language disclosed privileged information, and asked Joyce to file a motion to seal both complaints. (Mot. in Support, pp. 3:16-4:2.) Khanna argues that there is an overriding interest in protecting attorney-client privileged information and that he will suffer “sever reputational and professional harm” if the complaints remain unredacted (Mot. in Support, p. 14:14-15.)

Here, the Court finds that there is an overriding interest in the portions to be redacted. Detrimental client information learned by a lawyer during representation must be protected as a client secret. Protecting confidential attorney-client information is an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the information. The interest supports redacting the complaints to this limited extent. There is a substantial probability that Khanna will suffer ongoing prejudice by not redacting the complaints, the proposed redactions are narrowly tailored to protecting the confidential attorney-client information at issue, and no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.

 Thus, Joyce’s motion to seal is GRANTED.

The following portions of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint are to be redacted: Complaint, Page 1, lines 24-25 (from “for amounts. . . charges.”) and Page 3, lines 7-18 (Paragraph 12); First Amended Complaint, Page 1, lines 24-25 and Page 3, lines 7-18 (Paragraph 12).

The Plaintiff is ordered to file a redacted version of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint, redacting those portions only, within five court days, and the Court will upon receipt of those redacted documents, file them nunc pro tunc and seal the original filings. 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff Erin Joyce’s Motion to Seal Limited Portions of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint is GRANTED.


Case Number: 24STCV02455    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

IVAN D. RODRIGUEZ,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

EVARISTO RODRIGUEZ GONZALEZ; LENEICE WHITE; DOES 1 through 100,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          24STCV02455

 Hearing Date:   October 3, 2024

 Trial Date:        None Set

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Set Aside Default [RES ID # 7096]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Ivan Rodriguez (Rodriguez) sues Defendant Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez (Gonzalez), Leneice White, and Does 1 through 100 pursuant to a January 31, 2024, Complaint alleging causes of action for: (1) Quiet Title, (2) Cancellation of Instrument, (3) Declaratory Relief, (4) Negligence, and (5) Payment on Bond.

Rodriguez owns real property located at 3280 Folsom Street, Los Angeles, California 90063 which he leased to Defendant Gonzalez. On or around December 28, 2023, Rodriguez listed the property for sale. On or around January 17, 2024, the real estate listing agent for the property conducted a title search and discovered a grant deed recorded on December 29, 2023, which grants Gonzalez a 50% interest in the property as joint tenants with Rodriguez. Rodriguez alleges that Gonzalez forged his signature on the grant deed and that he did not grant Gonzalez any ownership interest in the property.

B. Relevant Procedural History

Gonzalez was served with the Complaint on February 3, 2024. He failed to timely respond to the Complaint, prompting Rodriguez to seek entry of default against Gonzalez. The Court entered default on March 6, 2024.

On April 10, 2024, the parties signed a joint stipulation to set aside the default to allow Gonzalez ten days to file responsive pleadings. Gonzalez filed a demurrer on April 19, 2024.

On July 12, 2024, the Court overruled the demurrer and ordered Gonzalez to file an answer within 14 days.

No answer was filed, once again prompting Rodriguez to seek entry of default. The Court entered default against Gonzalez on July 29, 2024.

C. Motion Before the Court

On September 9, 2024, Gonzalez filed the instant motion to set aside the default on the grounds that “he reasonably believed that Attorney Monica Graham would file an Answer for him as technically she was his Attorney of Record until July 29, 2024, the date the Court granted her Order to be relieved as his Attorney of Record.”  (Mot. p. 4:16-20.)

On September 20, 2024, Rodriguez opposed the motion on the grounds that Gonzalez has failed to show any excusable neglect or offer a meritorious defense, and that Gonzalez should be ordered to pay Rodriguez’s counsel's attorney's fees in the sum of at least $1,000.

No reply has been filed.

Gonzalez’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion

A. Legal Standard

“A motion for relief under section 473 is addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court and an appellate court will not interfere unless there is a clear showing of an abuse. [Citation.] The statute is remedial and should be liberally applied to carry out the policy of permitting trial on the merits, but the moving party has the burden of showing good cause. [Citations.]” (David v. Thayer (1980) 133 Cal.App.3d 892, 904-905.) 

Section 473 subdivision (b) of the Code of Civil Procedure provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief from a judgment, dismissal, and/or order or other proceeding taken against a party through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b) [mandatory relief more narrowly targeted to defaults, default judgments, and dismissals]; Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) 

The discretionary provision of section 473, subdivision (b), states that “[t]he court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein and be made within a reasonable time not exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

Relief is mandatory pursuant to this statutory section when a proper application for relief is accompanied by a sworn affidavit from an attorney attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect in causing the adverse judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding against the defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

B. Analysis

Gonzalez brings this motion on the grounds that was default was entered against him based on excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and excusable neglect. This position is supported by the Declaration of Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez: “I did not file an Answer by July 26, 2024 as I reasonably believed that Attorney Monica Graham would file an Answer for me as technically she was my Attorney of Record until July 29, 2024, the date the Court granted her Order to be relieved as my Attorney of Record.”  (Gonzalez Decl. ¶ 3.) Gonzalez brings this motion well within six months after the default was entered and the motion is accompanied by a copy of the answer proposed to be filed herein (Mot., Ex. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

These grounds are sufficient to support a finding that Gonzalez’s excusable neglect led to entry of default against him.

In opposition, Rodriguez argues that Gonzalez has not shown excusable neglect because he was represented by counsel at the time his Answer was due and there is no affidavit of fault from Gonzalez’s former counsel. Rodriguez also argues that Gonzalez cannot show he has a meritorious defense because in his proposed answer, Gonzalez does not deny that the allegedly forged deed is not a forgery. (Opp., pp. 5:20-6:8.)

Gonzalez does not argue that his former counsel made a mistake in not filing an answer, but that he mistakenly assumed his counsel was filing an answer despite that assumption apparently being incorrect. Given that his counsel had filed a motion to be relieved as counsel, there is a stronger basis to assert a mistake on this ground. Upon review of the proposed answer, while Gonzalez does not explicitly state that the allegedly forged deed is not a forgery, he does not admit that it is a forgery. Gonzalez claims he is a 50% owner in the property and that a controversy exists between the parties as to each party’s respective rights in the property. Considering Gonzalez’s diligence in timely filing the instant motion and strong policy favoring resolutions on the merits, the motion to set aside is GRANTED.

However, Rodriguez is correct in arguing that he is entitled to compensation for the work performed by his counsel in securing entry of default against Gonzalez.  (Opp., p.6; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c)(1)(A).) 

The Court thus GRANTS the motion to set aside default against Defendant Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez but also AWARDS Plaintiff Ivan Rodriguez with $1,000.00 in compensation for reasonable attorney’s fees and costs related to Rodriguez’s counsel work in entering default against Defendant Gonzalez.

III. Conclusion

Defendant Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez’s Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED because the entry of default against him resulted from his excusable neglect in failing to realize that his attorney would not be filing an answer to the Complaint for this action. 

               The Entry of Default entered by the Clerk against Defendant Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez on July 29, 2024, is VACATED. 

               Defendant Evaristo Rodriguez Gonzalez is ORDERED to remit payment of $1,000.00 to Plaintiff Ivan Rodriguez within fourteen (14) calendar days of notice of entry of this Order. He is ordered to file his Answer attached to his Declaration as Exhibit 2 within 5 court days. Failure to timely file the Answer could result in further default.
