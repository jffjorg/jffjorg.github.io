DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV21799A    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 14, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV21799A

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Susan Ahn and Serrano & Sixth, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs 3800 West Sixth Street, LLC and Leo Lee

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs 3800 West Sixth Street, LLC (“3800 West Sixth”) and Leo Lee (“Lee,” collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Susan Ahn (“Ahn”), Serrano & Sixth, LLC (“Serrano,” collectively, Defendants), and Steven C. Kim (“Kim”), asserting causes of action for misappropriation of trade secrets, breach of fiduciary duty, unfair competition, and interference with prospective economic advantage.  Kim is an attorney, and Plaintiffs allege they hired him to serve as counsel to advise them regarding assembling certain parcels of property in the Koreatown area of Los Angeles. (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 6.)  With Kim’s assistance, Plaintiffs acquired numerous parcels for a total purchase price of approximately $34 million. (Id., ¶ 7.)  After Plaintiffs acquired the last parcel, a dispute arose between Plaintiffs and a tenant of the property, and Kim continued to represent Plaintiffs.  (Id., ¶ 8.)  Because Kim represented Plaintiffs, Kim was aware of Plaintiffs’ plans to develop the assembled parcel, including the permitting, structural upgrades, and utility upgrades that were to take place. (Id., ¶ 9.)  Further, Kim learned Plaintiffs would require the “undergrounding” of electrical wires that traversed Defendants’ property.  (Id., ¶  10.)

Kim represented Plaintiffs in discussions with Defendants to obtain Defendants’ consent for the undergrounding, and billed Plaintiffs 2.3 hours for these discussions with Defendants and Ahn’s pastor.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 10.)  Subsequently, Kim agreed to represent Defendants in negotiations concerning the placement of the utility wires.  (Id., ¶ 13.)  Plaintiffs allege Kim violated the California Rules of Professional Conduct by representing Defendants, including rule 1.7 by simultaneously representing two clients with adverse interests and, even if Kim had terminated his representation of Plaintiffs before representing Defendants, he violated rule 1.9 by not obtaining Plaintiffs’ informed written consent. (Id., ¶¶ 17-18.)  

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision(f)(1) permits a party to move for summary adjudication “a to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim of damages, . . . or that  one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.  A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of  cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”   

EVIDENCE

             Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections Nos. 1 and 2 to the declaration of Paul Park are sustained.  Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections No. 5 to the declaration of Susan Ahn is sustained.  Plaintiffs’ other evidentiary objections are overruled.[1]

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

            To prevail on the cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets, Plaintiffs must show: (1) They owned a trade secret; (2) Defendants acquired, disclosed, or used Plaintiffs’ trade secret through improper means; and (3) Defendants’ actions damaged Plaintiffs.  (Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.1; Sargent Fletcher, Inc. v. Able Corp. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1658, 1665.)  Civil Code section 3426.1(section 3426.1), subdivision (d) defines a “trade secret” as “information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.”  

In the first amended complaint, Plaintiffs allege Kim disclosed trade secrets to Defendants, including “the value of the project, the amounts already expended or committed to the assemblage of land, and the need for access to adjoining properties in order to run utility cables needed for the project.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 24.)  Specifically, Plaintiffs allege Kim “used or disclosed to [Defendants] Plaintiffs’ need to run utility cables beneath the Ahn Property and that Plaintiff had no other viable alternatives to run utility cables if Ahn failed to cooperate.” (Id., ¶ 27.)  Defendants argue Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets because Kim did not disclose any trade secrets to Defendants.

In support of their contention, Defendants submit Ahn’s declaration, wherein Ahn avers she learned of Plaintiffs’ plans to run utility cables underneath her property in October 2019, before Ahn hired Kim, and she hired other attorneys to send letters to Plaintiffs regarding their trespass on Ahn’s property before hiring Kim.  (Declaration of Susan Ahn, ¶ 15; Appendix of Exhibits in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment (Appendix), Exhibits 10, 11, 12.)   With such evidence, Defendants have met their burden of showing Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets because Defendants did not obtain any such trade secrets.  Defendants, therefore, have shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs can prevail on this claim.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue an irrebuttable presumption arises that Kim used confidential information he obtained from Plaintiffs in his representation of Defendants.  As Plaintiffs point out, a presumption the client disclosed confidences arises from the existence of an attorney-client relationship.  (People ex rel. Deukmejian v. Brown (1981) 29 Cal.3d 150, 156.)  This presumption does not, however, excuse Plaintiffs from having to show Defendants obtained information that constitutes a trade secret; Defendants disclosed that trade secret; and Plaintiffs sustained damages as a result.

Plaintiffs advance Lee’s declaration in which Lee states, “[Plaintiffs] explained to Kim the importance of access to Ahn’s Property to underground the utility lines and asked him to draft an agreement that was as simple as possible to minimize the chance that Ahn would back out if she saw a complex document.  Kim provided an initial draft in March 2018, and then revised it at [Plaintiffs’] request in May 2018 to add a provision that would allow shoring to be constructed on the Ahn property.  Again with the amendment, [Plaintiffs] had to explain to Defendant why [Plaintiffs] needed the agreement and, with respect to the revision, why shoring was necessary.  Ahn ultimately refused to sign it.”  (Declaration of Leo Lee, ¶ 9.)  Plaintiffs contend “how badly [Plaintiffs] needed that easement” for the underground utility lines to cross Ahn’s property constitutes a trade secret.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 12.)  It does not. 

Plaintiffs’ need for the underground utility lines to cross Ahn’s property does not fall under the definition of a trade secret as related in section 3426.1.  That provision defines a “trade secret” as “information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process . . . .”  (Civ. Code, § 3426.1, subd. (d).)  A trade secret is not “simply information as to single or ephemeral events in the conduct of the business, as, for example, the amount or other terms of a secret bid for a contract or the salary of certain employees, or the security investments made or contemplated, or the date fixed for the announcement of a new policy or for bringing out a new model or the like.  A trade secret is a process or device for continuous use in the operation of the business.”  (Cal Francisco Inv. Corp. v. Vrionis (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 318, 322, internal quotations omitted.)  Plaintiffs fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs can prevail on the first cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets.  Thus, the Court grants Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication concerning it.

Third Cause of Action: Unfair Competition

In support of their cause of action for unfair competition, Plaintiffs allege, “By exploiting confidential information of Plaintiffs in order to extort an exorbitant payment from Plaintiffs for the right to install subterranean utility lines across the Ahn property, Defendants engaged in a business practice that was unfair, unlawful and fraudulent.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 37.) 

Defendants advance Ahn’s declaration wherein Ahn states she and Lee agreed to all material terms of the purchase agreement between Lee and Ahn and Serrano without Kim’s involvement.  (Declaration of Susan Ahn, ¶¶ 8-9.)  Although, Defendants have provided no contemporaneous documents to corroborate their position, and only relies on the timing when Ahn first met Kim and hired him and when the purchase agreement was signed, her testimony satisfies Defendants’ burden of showing Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their claim for unfair competition because it suggests Defendants’ unfair competition, if any, did not cause damage to Plaintiffs.  Defendants shift the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages such that Plaintiffs can prevail on this claim.

Plaintiffs again point to Lee’s declaration.  Lee counters by asserting he and Ahn did not agree to the purchase price prior to Kim’s involvement.  (Declaration of Leo Lee, ¶ 14.)  Plaintiffs also do not present any contemporaneous documents in support of this contention.  Nonetheless, such evidence is sufficient to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages due to Defendants’ unfair competition, and the trier of fact is to make that determination. 

Fourth Cause of Action: Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

In opposing Defendants’ motion challenging their claim for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage against Defendants, Plaintiffs concede they cannot state such a claim.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 14.)  The Court, therefore, grants the motion for summary adjudication of the fourth cause of action against Defendants, as stipulated.

Lack of Standing

            Defendants purport to move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether 3800 West Sixth lacks standing. This is not a proper issue for summary adjudication.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1) [“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages”].)

Federal Preemption

Defendants argue the Uniform Trade Secrets Act preempts Plaintiffs’ third and fourth causes of action.  The Uniform Trade Secrets Act preempts common law claims based on the same nucleus of facts as a misappropriation of trade secrets claim.  (See K.C. Multimedia, Inc. v. Bank of America Technology & Operations, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 939, 955-959.)  However, as this Court noted in its ruling on the demurrer to Plaintiffs’ original complaint, there is only preemption when the information is, in fact, a trade secret.  (See December 6, 2022 Minute Order.)  As set forth above, the information at issue is not a trade secret.  As such, preemption is inapplicable. 

Unclean Hands

Defendants contend unclean hands bar all of Plaintiffs’ claims.  For that bar to be applicable, Defendants must show Plaintiffs engaged in conduct that was unconscionable, in bad faith, or inequitable in connection with the matter in controversy, and it would, therefore, be inequitable for the Court to provide relief to Plaintiffs.  (Fladeboe v. Amer. Isuzu Motors Inc. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 42, 56.) 

Defendants point to two documents Plaintiffs asserted granted Plaintiffs the right to enter Defendants’ property.  The documents purport to bear Ahn’s signatures, but Ahn states the signatures on them are forgeries.  (Ahn Decl., ¶¶ 16-17; Appendix, Exhibits 13, 14.)  If Plaintiffs forged Ahn’s signature to attempt to gain access to Ahn’s property without payment, the doctrine of unclean hands would bar Plaintiffs from any recovery in this action.  Defendants, therefore, have shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether unclean hands bar Plaintiffs’ recovery.

In opposition, Plaintiffs again rely on Lee’s testimony.  Lee states he paid a real estate agent, John Rhee (“Rhee”), to negotiate with Ahn the terms reflected in these documents; he paid Rhee $290,000 to obtain Ahn’s consent to the documents; and he assumed Rhee paid a substantial portion of those funds to Ahn in exchange for her agreement.  (Lee Decl., ¶ 17.)  If Plaintiffs did not forge Ahn’s signature or did not know her signature was not genuine, Plaintiffs do not have unclean hands.  This evidence raises triable issues of material fact as to whether unclean hands bar Plaintiffs’ recovery in this case.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.  Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the third cause of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint is denied.  Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication of the first and fourth causes of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint is granted.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Steven C. Kim

OPPOSING PARTY

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs 3800 West Sixth Street, LLC (“3800 West Sixth”) and Leo Lee (“Lee,” collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Steven C. Kim (“Defendant”), Susan Ahn (“Ahn”), and Serrano & Sixth, LLC (“Serrano”) asserting causes of action for misappropriation of trade secrets, breach of fiduciary duty, unfair competition, and interference with prospective economic advantage.  Defendant is an attorney, and Plaintiffs allege they hired him to serve as counsel to advise them regarding assembling certain parcels of property in the Koreatown area of Los Angeles. (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 6.)  With Defendant’s assistance, Plaintiffs acquired numerous parcels for a total purchase price of approximately $34 million. (Id., ¶ 7.)  After Plaintiffs acquired the last parcel, a dispute arose between Plaintiffs and a tenant of the property, and Defendant continued to represent Plaintiffs.  (Id., ¶ 8.)  Because Defendant represented Plaintiffs, Defendant was aware of Plaintiffs’ plans to develop the assembled parcel, including the permitting, structural upgrades, and utility upgrades that were to take place. (Id., ¶ 9.)  Further, Defendant learned Plaintiffs would require the “undergrounding” of electrical wires that traversed Ahn’s property.  (Id., ¶ 10.)

Defendant represented Plaintiffs in discussions with Ahn to obtain her consent for the undergrounding, and billed Plaintiffs 2.3 hours for these discussions with Ahn and her pastor.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 10.)  Subsequently, Defendant agreed to represent Ahn and Serrano in negotiations concerning the placement of the utility wires.  (Id., ¶ 13.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendant violated the California Rules of Professional Conduct by representing Ahn and Serrano, including rule 1.7 by simultaneously representing two clients with adverse interests and, even if Defendant had terminated his representation of Plaintiffs before representing Ahn, he violated rule 1.9 by not obtaining Plaintiffs’ informed written consent. (Id., ¶¶ 17-18.)  

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision(f)(1) permits a party to move for summary adjudication “a to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim of damages, . . . or that  one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.  A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of  cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”   

EVIDENCE

             Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections Nos. 15, 16, 19, 20, 21, 22 to Defendant’s declaration are sustained.  Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections No. 5 to the declaration of Susan Ahn’s declaration is sustained.  Plaintiffs’ other evidentiary objections are overruled.[1]

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Misappropriation of Trade Secrets

            To prevail on the cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets, Plaintiffs must show: (1) They owned a trade secret; (2) Defendant acquired, disclosed, or used Plaintiffs’ trade secret through improper means; and (3) Defendant’s actions damaged Plaintiffs.  (Cal. Civ. Code § 3426.1; Sargent Fletcher, Inc. v. Able Corp. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1658, 1665.)  Civil Code section 3426.1(section 3426.1), subdivision (d) defines a “trade secret” as “information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.”  

In the first amended complaint, Plaintiffs allege Defendant disclosed trade secrets to Ahn, including “the value of the project, the amounts already expended or committed to the assemblage of land, and the need for access to adjoining properties in order to run utility cables needed for the project.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 24.)  Specifically, Plaintiffs allege Defendant “used or disclosed to Ahn and Serrano Plaintiffs’ need to run utility cables beneath the Ahn Property and that Plaintiffs had no other viable alternatives to run utility cables if Ahn failed to cooperate.”  (Id., Amended Complaint, ¶ 27.)  Defendant argues Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets because Defendant did not disclose any trade secrets to Ahn. 

In support of his contentions, Defendant advances Ahn’s declaration, wherein Ahn avers she learned of Plaintiffs’ plans to run utility cables underneath her property in October 2019, before Ahn hired Defendant, and she hired other attorneys to communicate with Plaintiffs regarding their trespass on Ahn’s property before Ahn hired Defendant.  (Declaration of Susan Ahn, ¶ 14; Appendix of Exhibits in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment (Appendix), Exhibits 10, 11, 12.)  This evidence satisfies Defendant’s burden of showing Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets because Defendant did not disclose trade secrets to Ahn.  Defendant, therefore, has shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on this claim.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue an irrebuttable presumption arises that Defendant used confidential information he obtained from Plaintiffs in his representation of Ahn.  As Plaintiffs point out, a presumption the client disclosed confidences arises from the existence of an attorney-client relationship.  (People ex rel. Deukmejian v. Brown (1981) 29 Cal.3d 150, 156.)  This presumption does not, however, excuse Plaintiffs from having to show Defendant obtained information that constitutes a trade secret; Defendant disclosed that trade secret; and Plaintiffs sustained damages as a result.

Plaintiffs advance Lee’s declaration in which Lee states, “[Plaintiffs] explained to [Defendant] the importance of access to Ahn’s Property to underground the utility lines and asked him to draft an agreement that was as simple as possible to minimize the chance that Ahn would back out if she saw a complex document.  [Defendant] provided an initial draft in March 2018, and then revised it at [Plaintiffs’] request in May 2018 to add a provision that would allow shoring to be constructed on the Ahn property.  Again with the amendment, [Plaintiffs] had to explain to [Defendant] why [Plaintiffs] needed the agreement and, with respect to the revision, why shoring was necessary.  Ahn ultimately refused to sign it.”  (Declaration of Leo Lee, ¶ 9.)  Plaintiffs contend “how badly [Plaintiffs] needed that easement” for the underground utility lines to cross Ahn’s property constitutes a trade secret.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 12.)  It does not. 

Plaintiffs’ need for the underground utility lines to cross Ahn’s property does not fall under the definition of a trade secret as related in section 3426.1  That provision defines a “trade secret” as “information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process . . . .”  (Civ. Code, § 3426.1, subd. (d).)  A trade secret is not “simply information as to single or ephemeral events in the conduct of the business, as, for example, the amount or other terms of a secret bid for a contract or the salary of certain employees, or the security investments made or contemplated, or the date fixed for the announcement of a new policy or for bringing out a new model or the like.  A trade secret is a process or device for continuous use in the operation of the business.”  (Cal Francisco Inv. Corp. v. Vrionis (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 318, 322, internal quotations omitted.)  Plaintiffs fail to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs can prevail on the first cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets.  Thus, the Court grants Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication concerning it.

Second Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

To prevail on their claim for breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiffs must show Defendant owed a fiduciary duty to Plaintiffs; he breached that duty; and the breach caused damages. (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.)  In support of this claim, Plaintiffs allege, “[Defendant] breached his fiduciary duties to Plaintiffs by utilizing against Plaintiffs information that he gained in his capacity as counsel for Plaintiffs.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 33.) 

Defendant contends Plaintiffs waived any conflict inherent in Defendant’s representation of Ahn.  A former client must agree in writing to an attorneys’ representation of a client with conflicting interests after the attorney explains the “relevant circumstances” and the material risks, including any “actual and reasonably foreseeable adverse consequences” to the former client.  (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, rule 1.0.1, subds. (e), (n).)  In section 20.11 of the purchase agreement by which Lee purchased property from Ahn and Serrano, the parties agreed, “[Defendant] represented [Lee] in the past in his plan to build a hotel and residential units on the adjacent property known as 3800 West Sixth Street, Los Angeles and in his legal action to remove a tenant Smiling Tree Preschool on the property located on Hobart Blvd.  [Defendant] has not worked for [Lee] since the settlement with the tenant in March 2019.  [Defendant] now represents [Ahn and Serrano] in this transaction, and [Ahn and Serrano] and [Lee] have been advised of [Defendant]'s prior representation of [Lee] and the resulting potential conflict of interest.  [Lee] is now represented by another attorney.”  (Appendix of Exhibits in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment, Exhibit 4.)   However, by agreeing to these terms, Lee did not agree Defendant could use confidential information Defendant obtained while representing Plaintiffs to assist Defendant in his representation of Ahn.  Notwithstanding any waiver of Defendant’s representation of Ahn, Defendant had a duty “[t]o maintain inviolate the confidence, and at every peril to himself or herself to preserve the secrets, of” Plaintiffs.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6068, subd. (e)(1).)  As set forth above, a presumption Plaintiffs disclosed confidences to Defendant arises from the attorney-client relationship between Plaintiffs and Defendant.  (People ex rel. Deukmejian v. Brown, supra, 29 Cal.3d at p. 156.)  The waiver Lee executed is not dispositive of the issue of whether Defendant breached his fiduciary duty to Plaintiffs.

Defendant also advances Ahn’s testimony who states Lee and Ahn agreed to all material terms of the purchase agreement between Lee and Ahn and Serrano without Defendant’s involvement.  (Declaration of Susan Ahn, ¶¶ 8-9.)  This evidence satisfies Defendant’s burden of showing Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their claim for breach of fiduciary duty, because it suggests Defendant’s breach of fiduciary duty, if any, did not cause damages to Plaintiffs.  Defendant, therefore, has shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages such that Plaintiffs can prevail on the claim.

Plaintiffs advance Lee’s declaration in which Lee states he and Ahn did not agree to the purchase price prior to Defendant’s involvement.  (Declaration of Leo Lee, ¶ 14.)  This evidence is sufficient to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages due to Defendant’s breach of fiduciary duty, and the trier of fact is to make that determination. 

Third Cause of Action: Unfair Competition

In support of their cause of action for unfair competition, Plaintiffs allege, “By exploiting confidential information of Plaintiffs in order to extort an exorbitant payment from Plaintiffs for the right to install subterranean utility lines across the Ahn property, Defendants engaged in a business practice that was unfair, unlawful and fraudulent.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 37.)  Defendant repeats the same arguments with respect to this claim as Defendant made in challenging Plaintiffs’ second cause of action.  As set forth above, Ahn’s testimony she and Lee agreed to all material terms of the purchase agreement between Lee and Ahn and Serrano without the involvement of Defendant meets Defendant’s burden of showing Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their claim for unfair competition because it suggests Defendant’s unfair competition, if any, did not cause damage to Plaintiffs.  Defendant shifts the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages such that Plaintiffs can prevail on this claim.

As set forth above, Lee states he and Ahn did not agree to the purchase price prior to Defendant’s involvement.  (Declaration of Leo Lee, ¶ 14.)  This evidence raises triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs sustained damages due to Defendant’s unfair competition.

Fourth Cause of Action: Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

To state a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, Plaintiffs must allege an economic relationship exists between Plaintiffs and a third party with the probability of future economic benefit to Plaintiffs; Defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; Defendant’s intentional acts to disrupt the relationship that result in actual disruption of the relationship; and resulting harm to Plaintiffs.  (Salma v. Capon (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1275, 1290.) 

Defendant repeats the same arguments set forth above.  Defendant also argues, “The only reason the sale was not completed was that Plaintiffs cancelled the contract after they had already constructed the underground electrical conduit, and thus no longer had any interest in completing the purchase.”  (Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 19.)  Plaintiffs concede this point in their operative first amended complaint.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 20.)  Defendant has thus shown Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage because Plaintiffs, not Defendant, disrupted the relationship between Plaintiffs and Ahn by cancelling the purchase agreement.  Defendant, therefore, has  shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiffs can prevail on this claim. 

Plaintiffs fail to address this issue in any meaningful way in their opposition brief. Instead, Plaintiffs argue Defendant “placed Plaintiffs at a disadvantage in that business relationship” with Ahn.  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, p. 15.)  This is insufficient.  Plaintiffs must raise triable issues of fact to demonstrate Defendant’s actions somehow cut off the future economic benefit to be derived from the relationship between Plaintiffs and Ahn.  Plaintiffs fail to do so thereby compelling this Court to grant summary adjudication as to the intentional interference with prospective economic advantage claim.

Lack of Standing

            Defendant purports to move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether 3800 West Sixth lacks standing. This is not a proper issue for summary adjudication.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1) [“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages”].)

Federal Preemption

Defendant argues the Uniform Trade Secrets Act preempts Plaintiffs’ second, third, and fourth causes of action.  The Uniform Trade Secrets Act preempts common law claims based on the same nucleus of facts as a misappropriation of trade secrets claim.  (See K.C. Multimedia, Inc. v. Bank of America Technology & Operations, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 939, 955-959.)  However, as this Court noted in its ruling on Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ original complaint, there is only preemption when the information is, in fact, a trade secret.  (See December 6, 2022 Minute Order.)  As set forth above, the information at issue is not a trade secret.  As such, preemption is inapplicable. 

Unclean Hands

Defendant contends unclean hands bar all of Plaintiffs’ claims.  For that bar to be applicable, Defendants must show Plaintiffs engaged in conduct that was unconscionable, in bad faith, or inequitable in connection with the matter in controversy, and it would, therefore, be inequitable for the Court to provide relief to Plaintiffs.  (Fladeboe v. Amer. Isuzu Motors Inc. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 42, 56.) 

Defendant points to two documents that Plaintiffs asserted granted Plaintiffs the right to enter Ahn’s property.  The documents purport to bear Ahn’s signatures, but Ahn states the signatures are forgeries.  (Ahn Decl., ¶¶ 16-17; Appendix, Exhibits 13, 14.)  If Plaintiffs forged Ahn’s signature to attempt to gain access to Ahn’s property without payment, the doctrine of unclean hands would bar Plaintiffs from any recovery in this action.  Defendant, therefore, has shifted the burden to Plaintiffs to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether unclean hands bar Plaintiffs’ recovery.

In opposition, Plaintiffs again rely on Lee’s testimony.  Lee states that he paid a real estate agent, John Rhee (“Rhee”), to negotiate with Ahn the terms reflected in these documents; he paid Rhee $290,000 to obtain Ahn’s consent to the documents; and he assumed Rhee paid a substantial portion of those funds to Ahn in exchange for her agreement.  (Leo Decl., ¶ 17.)  If Plaintiffs did not forge Ahn’s signature or did not know her signature was not genuine, Plaintiffs do not have unclean hands.  This evidence raises triable issues of material fact as to whether unclean hands bar Plaintiffs’ recovery in this case.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is denied.  Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the second and third causes of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint is denied.  Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the first and fourth causes of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint is granted.

EVIDENCE

             Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections Nos. 1 and 2 to the declaration of Paul Park are sustained.  Plaintiffs’ other evidentiary objections are overruled.[1]

DISCUSSION

Trespass to Land

            To prevail on their cause of action for trespass to land, Defendants must show: (1) Defendants’ ownership or control of the property; (2) Plaintiffs’ intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) Defendants did not give Plaintiffs permission to enter the property, or Plaintiffs exceeded the scope of permission; (4) Defendants were harmed by the trespass; and (5) the trespass was a substantial factor in causing Defendants’ harm.  (Ralphs Grocery Company v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262.) 

In the cross-complaint, Defendants allege Plaintiffs “used [a] forged easement to intentionally enter the [Defendants’] Property, without [Defendants’] consent, and performed excavation and construction work on the property to the disruption of its tenants.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 22.)  In support of their motion, Defendants present Ahn’s declaration wherein she  states she learned of unauthorized construction and excavation on her property in October 2019, and she hired attorneys to send letters to Plaintiffs regarding their trespass on her property.  (Declaration of Susan Ahn, ¶¶ 4, 5; Index of Exhibits in Support of Cross-Complainants Motion for Summary Judgment (Index), Exhibits 2, 3, 4.)  Defendants also offer three documents that purportedly granted Plaintiffs the right to enter Defendants’ property.  The documents bear Ahn’s signatures, but Ahn states the signatures are forgeries.  (Ahn Decl., ¶¶ 6, 7; Index, Exhibits 5, 6, 7.)

However, Defendants do not address the amount of damages they seek.  Defendants cannot obtain judgment on a trespass cause of action in an amount of damages to be determined later because the statutory language in Code of Civil Procedure section 437 does not provide for partial summary judgment or summary adjudication as to liability only.  (See, e. g., Paramount Petroleum Corp. v. Superior Court (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 226, 241-243 [in context of a breach of contract claim, which has damages as an element, a party cannot obtain summary judgment or summary adjudication in an amount of damages to be determined later].)

In any event, in that Defendants have not set forth the damages they have sustained, they have not satisfied their “burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that [they are] entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c) [“The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law].)[2]

Accordingly, the motion for summary judgment is denied. 

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]Plaintiffs request the Court take judicial notice, pursuant to Evidence Code sections 452 and 453, of a set of declarations filed in a case in the Los Angeles County Superior Court.  The Court does so; however, the Court  does not notice the truth of the contents of such pleadings or documents.  (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank national Trust Company (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) 

 

               [2]The Court also notes Plaintiffs respond to Defendant’s contention the signatures on the documents that purportedly authorized Plaintiffs’ entry onto Defendants’ property by refuting the claim the documents bore forged signatures.  At a minimum, this issue is far from undisputed.  As this constitutes a disputed issue of a material fact related to whether or not Defendants granted Plaintiffs permission to enter Defendants’ property, summary judgment as to the trespass cause of action is not warranted, and a determination by the trier of fact of the issue is necessitated.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 14, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV04117

MOTION

Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Harold Burton Igadaloff

OPPOSING PARTY

No Opposition Filed

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Harold Burton Igdaloff, individually and on behalf of the Estate of Evelyn Igdaloff, and the Igdaloff 1993 Family Trust (“Plaintiff”) moves to enforce a settlement agreement with Defendants Hany S. Fangary and Fangary Law Group (“Defendants”) under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.  Defendants have not opposed the motion.

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 (section 664.6 )provides “[i]f parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  In ruling on a motion to enter judgment, the Court acts as a trier of fact.  The Court must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement.  To do so, the Court may receive oral testimony in addition to declarations.  (Kohn v. Jaymar-Ruby, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1530, 1533.)

Plaintiff does not advance a settlement agreement that Defendants signed, or one the parties entered in open court.  (Declaration of Steven C. Glickman, ¶ 7.)  As such, the Court cannot enforce a settlement agreement under section 664.6, as Plaintiff fails to present a signed agreement to the Court.  The motion to enforce the settlement is denied without prejudice.

 If Plaintiff believes Defendants entered into an enforceable settlement agreement with Plaintiff, Plaintiff’s remedy is to assert a claim for breach of contract against Defendant.  (Smith v. Golden Eagle Ins. Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1371, 1374.)  While Plaintiff states Plaintiff seeks leave to assert such a claim in the alternative to Plaintiff’s motion to enforce the settlement agreement, Plaintiff’s motion does not meet the procedural requirements for a motion for leave to amend.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324.)

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV27311    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 14, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV27311

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Teresita Mahayag Teichman

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendants Farivar Gouhari, Eric D. Beck, and Mag Estate Property LLC

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Teresita Mahayag Teichmann (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Farivar Gouhari, Eric D. Beck, and Mag Estate Property LLC (“Defendants”), alleging tenant harassment.  Plaintiff moves the court for an order granting her trial preference due to her age and poor health pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivisions (a) and (e).  Defendants oppose the motion. 

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure section 36 (section 36) provides, in part: “A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings: (1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole. [and]  (2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation.”   (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a)(1)(2).)  In addition, Section 36, subdivision (e) states: “Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the court may in its discretion grant a motion for preference that is supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting this preference.”

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36.5, “An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.” (See also Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534 [“a motion under subdivision (a) [of section 36] may be supported by nothing more than an attorney’s declaration ‘based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party’”].)  However, “[a]dmissible evidence is still required as to the party's age (e.g., declarations by party or admissible records showing he or she is over 70).  The attorney's declaration is not sufficient for this purpose.”  (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2024) ¶ 12:247.3, italics in original.)

In the instant case, Plaintiff is 75 years old.  Plaintiff has scoliosis, which impacts her posture and walking and arthritis, which causes pain in her joints.  (See Declaration of Teresita Mahayag Teichmann, ¶ 3.)  Plaintiff also indicates her deposition on August 27, 2024 was stressful, and she is worried the litigation will worsen her health.  (Id., at ¶¶ 32, 33.)  But this evidence fails to demonstrate Plaintiff’s health necessitates trial preference.  For example, while Plaintiff establishes Plaintiff has medical conditions, she has not explained how they impact her participation in this litigation. 

Plaintiff also advances the declaration of her counsel, Andres Rapoport (“Counsel”) who states “[h]aving to litigate three separate actions with her landlord at her advanced age has left [Plaintiff] diminished”; and Plaintiff is “tired, physically and emotionally drained, and her memory has begun to decline . . . ,” although “there are days where her memory is sharp and reliable, and there are days that she struggles to recount the chronology and details of events from the last couple of years as accurately as she once could.”  (Declaration of Andres Rapoport, ¶¶ 6-7.)  Counsel’s declaration establishes Plaintiff experiences stress and is overwhelmed by the pending litigation, which any litigant might experience. 

As such, Counsel’s testimony has also not established preference is necessary to protect Plaintiff’s interests in this litigation nor that the interests of justice require preference.  Accordingly, the Court denies the motion, but without prejudice to its renewal to the extent Plaintiff is able to address the matters the Court notes above. 

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
