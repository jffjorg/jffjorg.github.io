DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20SMUD00869    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

November 3, 2023

CASE NUMBER

20SMUD00869

MOTION

Demurrer to Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Birgitta Lauren and Eric K. Yohan AKA Eric Knipe

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant American Financial Center and Cross-Defendant Mark Crittenden aka Mark Afincen

 

MOTION

 

This case arises from a dispute over real property.  Plaintiff American Financial Center alleges Defendants Birgitta Lauren (“Lauren”) and Erik K. Yohan AKA Eric Knipe (“Knipe”) (“Defendants”) unlawfully continued to occupy the subject condominium following its foreclosure of the property and subsequent purchase of the property at the foreclosure sale.  With possession no longer at issue, Plaintiff’s operative complaint seeks damages.

 

Defendants have now brought a cross-complaint against Plaintiff and Mark Crittenden aka Mark Afincen (“Cross-Defendant”), alleging that they fraudulently acquired an interest in the subject property upon which they initiated the foreclosure proceedings. 

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant demur to the cross-complaint (which alleges a single cause of action for fraud) for uncertainty, for failure to state a cause of action with sufficient particularity, and as being barred by the applicable statute of limitations and laches.

 

Defendants oppose the demurrer, and Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant reply.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant request the Court to take judicial notice of a Deed of Trust recorded with the Official Records of the Recorder’s Office of Los Angeles County, California, on September 3, 2019 (No. 20190895516). 

 

Courts can take judicial notice of the existence and recordation of real property records, including deeds, if authenticity is not reasonably disputed.  (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 264-265.)  “The official act of recordation and the common use of a notary public in the execution of such documents assure their reliability, and the maintenance of the documents in the recorder’s office makes their existence and text capable of ready confirmation, thereby placing such documents beyond reasonable dispute.”  (Ibid.)  Moreover, courts can take judicial notice not only of the existence and recordation of recorded documents but also matters that can be deduced from the documents, including the parties, dates, and legal consequences of recorded documents relating to real estate transactions.  (Ibid.)

 

            Here, Defendants contest the “accuracy” of the deed, but not its authenticity.  In other words, Defendants do not contend that the deed attached to the Request for Judicial notice is not a true and correct copy of the Deed recorded in the Los Angeles Recorder’s Office on September 3, 2019 as No. 20190895516.  Rather, Defendants argue that the deed transferring an interest in the subject property to Plaintiff was fraudulent.  Therefore, the Court takes judicial notice of the existence and recordation of the deed, but not the truth of the matters asserted therein.

 

ANALYSIS

1.      DEMURRER

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant demur to the single cause of action in Defendants’ cross-complaint for fraud on the bases that (1) it is uncertain; (2) it fails to state a cause of action; (3) it fails to plead fraud with sufficient particularity; (4) it contradicts facts of which the Court may take judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code section 452; (5) it is barred by the applicable statute of limitations; and (6) it is barred by the doctrine of laches. 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10 outlines the applicable bases for a demurrer as follows:

 

The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object, by demurrer or answer as provided in Section 430.30, to the pleading on any one or more of the following grounds:

 

(a)   The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.

 

(b)   The person who filed the pleading does not have the legal capacity to sue.

 

(c)   There is another action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.

 

(d)   There is a defect or misjoinder of parties.

 

(e)   The pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

(f)    The pleading is uncertain. As used in this subdivision, “uncertain” includes ambiguous and unintelligible.

 

(g)   In an action founded upon a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct.

 

(h)   No certificate was filed as required by Section 411.35.

 

Thus, the Court construes the demurrer as being brought pursuant to subdivision (f) for uncertainty and subdivision (e) for failure to state a cause of action. 

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.) 

 

A.    UNCERTAINTY

 

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that the responding party cannot reasonably respond - i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Where a demurrer is made upon the ground of uncertainty, the demurrer must distinctly specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain, and where such uncertainty appears by reference to page and line numbers.  (See Fenton v. Groveland Comm. Services Dist. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 797, 809.) 

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant do not demonstrate that any portions of the Cross-Complaint are so bad that they cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against them.  The Court thus declines to sustain the demurrer on the basis of uncertainty. 

 

B.     FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

                                                                    i.            Particularity

 

“The essential elements of fraud, generally, are (1) a misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage.”  (City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 191, 211.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

            Here, Defendants allege, in pertinent part, the following:

 

In or about January 2020, Cross Defendants AFC and CRITTENDEN offered to refinance the subject condominium located at 2010 S. Beverly Glen Blvd., Unit 101, Los Angeles, CA 90025 and owned solely by LAUREN in order to lessen LAUREN’s financial burden. LAUREN, in good faith, trusted CRITTENDEN and his company, AFC, and signed a Deed of Trust and several blank documents which CRITTENDEN stated were necessary for the refinancing and which would give favorable terms to LAUREN.

 

Unbeknownst to LAUREN and KNIPE, CRITTENDEN and AFC doctored the documents and, instead of providing a refinancing for LAUREN with favorable financial terms, they prepared documents without LAUREN’s knowledge or consent wherein LAUREN became the guarantor of an unrelated unsecured “factoring” agreement with a company known as Strong Arm LLC and which was affiliated with KNIPE. This guarantor agreement was also not disclosed to KNIPE, who is LAUREN’s husband. Cross complainants discovered the wrongful and fraudulent actions of cross defendants in or about February 2021, through the original unlawful detainer action, which is part of this case.

 

(Cross-Complaint, 2:15-3:6.)  Defendants also allege: 

 

AFC and Crittenden promised to refinance the subject condominium for LAUREN at a lower interest rate that would help LAUREN and KNIPE out financially. AFC and CRITTENDEN presented documents to LAUREN to sign, including but not limited to a Deed of Trust and various blank papers with the representation that Cross Defendants would help Cross Plaintiffs get out of the financial troubles they were in. LAUREN relied on these promises and signed the documents.

 

The truth was that AFC and CRITTENDEN has no intention of helping Cross Plaintiffs. Rather, AFC and CRITTENDEN doctored the documents to reflect that LAUREN was the guarantor for Strong Arm LLC who held an unsecured loan with AFC and CRITTENDEN. As such, LAUREN was not "on the hook" for securing a loan between Strong Arm LLC and AFC/CRITTENDEN and was not benefitting at all from lower monthly payments on condo.

 

AFC and CRITTENDEN promised to refinance the subject condominium to lower LAUREN's monthly payments and help her get through hard financial times as well as Covid struggles. No such refinancing between LAUREN and AFC/CRITTENDEN took place. No disclosure of the fact that at all times AFC/CRITTENDEN intended to substitute LAUREN as the guarantor of an unrelated unsecured factoring agreement between Strong Arm LLC and AFC/CRITTENDEN was made.

 

LAUREN signed all the documents presented to her by AFC/CRITTENDEN thereby causing her to become a guarantor of an unsecured factoring agreement between Strong Arm and AFC/CRITTENDEN.

 

As a result, LAUREN and KNIPE lost condominium through foreclosure and were ultimately removed from the premises through the Unlawful Detainer Action which initiated the within lawsuit. Moreover, LAUREN and KNIPE are now subject to a lawsuit in the Amended Complaint for monetary damages related to the wrongful acts of AFC and CRITTENDEN.

 

(Cross-Complaint, Attachment, pp. 1-2.) 

 

            Based upon those allegations, the Court finds that Defendants have alleged the elements of a fraud cause of action with the required particularity for pleading purposes.

 

                                                                  ii.            Contradicted by Judicially Noticed Deed

 

The Court rejects Plaintiff’s and Cross-Defendant’s argument that the Cross-Complaint fails to state a cause of action because it is contradicted by the judicially noticed deed.  Defendants dispute the validity of the contents of the deed.  Therefore, the Court finds that the deed evidences a potential factual dispute.  At this juncture, the Court cannot determine that the Cross-Complaint fails to state a cause of action as a matter of law when Defendants contest the genesis of the deed.

 

                                                                iii.            Statute of Limitations

 

A demurrer lies where the dates alleged in the complaint show the cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations.  (Saliter v. Pierce Bros. Mortuaries (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 292, 300.)  The running of the statute must appear “clearly and affirmatively” from the face of the complaint. (Committee for Green Foothills v. Santa Clara County Board of Supervisors (2010) 48 Cal.4h 32, 42.) 

 

Fraud claims are subject to a three year statute of limitations  -- “An action for relief on the ground of fraud or mistake. The cause of action in that case is not deemed to have accrued until the discovery, by the aggrieved party, of the facts constituting the fraud or mistake.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) 

 

First, Plaintiff’s and Cross-Defendant’s statute of limitations argument relies upon the date of the contested deed, August 30, 2019.  But Defendants challenge the validity of the deed which they claim was the result of fraudulent acts of Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant.  As such, the Court finds Plaintiff’s and Cross-Defendant’s argument on that basis to be fatally flawed.

 

Second, Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant argue that even if the Court does not consider the August 2019 deed, the claim is time barred by the face of the complaint, which alleges the fraud occurred in January 2020, yet Defendants did not file the cross-complaint until August 9, 2023. 

 

However, the delayed discovery rule “postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.”  (Stella v. Asset Management Consultants, Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 181, 191–192.)  “In order to rely on the discovery rule for delayed accrual of a cause of action, “[a] plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.”  (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 808.)

 

Here, the cross-complaint alleges that Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant “concealed or suppressed material acts” by not showing Defendants the doctored documents (see Cross-Complaint ¶ FR-3) and that Defendants did not discover the fraud until “February 2021, through the unlawful detainer action, which is part of this case.”  (Cross-complaint at p. 3:3-5.)

 

  Thus, Defendants have adequately alleged that their fraud claim is not time barred by virtue of the delayed discovery rule.[1]

 

                                                                iv.            Laches

 

“If, in light of the lapse of time and other relevant circumstances, a court concludes that a party's failure to assert a right has caused prejudice to an adverse party, the court may apply the equitable defense of laches to bar further assertion of the right.”  (In re Marriage of Fellows (2006) 39 Cal.4th 179, 183.)  “The doctrine of laches is to actions in equity what statutes of limitations are to actions in law.”  (In re Marriage of Parker (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 681, 687.) 

 

“The defense of laches requires unreasonable delay plus either acquiescence in the act about which plaintiff complains or prejudice to the defendant resulting from the delay.”  (Johnson v. City of Loma Linda (2000) 24 Cal.4th 61, 68.)  “Laches may be raised by demurrer, but only if the complaint shows on its face unreasonable delay plus prejudice or acquiescence. In the absence of prejudice or acquiescence delay does not establish a defense . . . .”  (Conti v. Board of Civil Serv. Com. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 351, 362, citations omitted & emphasis added.)   

 

Here, Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant argue that the deed demonstrates Defendants secured a loan in the amount of $250,000 on August 30, 2019, yet Defendants did not raise the issue of fraud or misrepresentation throughout the foreclosure process in 2020, the unlawful detainer process in 2020, or any of the pleadings in this case until four years later.  (Demurrer at p. 9.)

 

As discussed above, the disputed deed Defendants allege Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant doctored and concealed from them, and which they did not allegedly discover until February 2021, in connection with the unlawful detainer action in this case, does not demonstrate an unreasonable delay as a matter of law.

 

With respect to the foreclosure process in 2020 and the unlawful detainer process in 2020, the face of the cross-complaint does not indicate those events occurred in 2020, or that Defendants were aware of those events in 2020.  Moreover, there is nothing to suggest Defendants acquiesced or that Plaintiff or Cross-Defendant were prejudiced by the delay.  To wit, the allegations that Defendants remained in possession of the property until after the resolution of the unlawful detainer matter suggests Defendants did not acquiesce. 

 

As for prejudice, “Prejudice is never presumed; rather it must be affirmatively demonstrated by the defendant in order to sustain his burdens of proof and the production of evidence on the issue.”  (Miller v. Eisenhower Medical Center (1980) 27 Cal.3d 614, 624.)  Here, the only prejudice Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant raise is “the unnecessary time and expense defending against a non-sensical Cross-Complaint that appears frivolous on its face.”  (Demurrer at p. 9.)  But Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant cite no authority supporting their contention that having to defend against a cross-complaint constitutes prejudice in and of itself. 

 

Therefore, the Court cannot sustain the demurrer on the basis of laches.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Plaintiff’s and Cross-Defendant’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint in its entirety. 

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant shall file and serve an answer to the Cross-Complaint on or before November 17, 2023.    

 

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

CASE NUMBER

21STCV37567

MOTION

Demurrer

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Soma Surgery Center, Inc.; Melanie Kerr; and Sean Ravaei

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Pericles Chamis

 

MOTION

 

Defendants Soma Surgery Center, Inc. (“Soma”); Melanie Kerr (“Kerr”); and Sean Ravaei (“Defendants”) demur to all three causes of action alleged in Plaintiff’s Fifth Amended Complaint (“5AC”) on the grounds of uncertainty pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f).  Defendants additionally demur to the second and third causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and intentional misrepresentation for failure to state causes of action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e). 

 

Plaintiff opposes the demurrer and Defendants reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.) 

 

A.    DEMURRER FOLLOWING ANSWER

 

As a threshold issue, Plaintiff argues in opposition that the demurrer is procedurally improper because Defendants have already answered the third amended complaint.

 

By way of background, on April 11, 2022, Plaintiff filed the second amended complaint, alleging three causes of action for (1) professional negligence [medical malpractice]; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; and (3) intentional misrepresentation.  Defendant Soma Surgery Center, Inc. demurred to the second and third causes of action in Plaintiff’s second amended complaint, which the Court sustained, on the basis that all allegations were as to Dr. Prokupek himself, and there were insufficient allegations as to Soma.  (July 1, 2022 Minute Order.)

 

On September 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed the third amended complaint, alleging the same three causes of action.  On October 31, 2022, Soma again demurred to the third amended complaint, which the Court overruled on the basis that the new allegations sufficiently alleged agency as to Soma.  (December 7, 2022 Minute Order.) 

 

On November 18, 2022, Defendant Kerr was named as Doe defendant 2 and on December 1, 2022, Defendant Ravaei was named as Doe defendant 5.  Defendants Soma, Kerr, and Ravaei each answered the third amended complaint on January 11, 2023.

 

Plaintiff’s fifth amended complaint alleges the same three causes of action as the third amended complaint,  But Plaintiff added a paragraph describing each of the Defendants that were subsequently added to the lawsuit via Doe amendments; inserted more specific allegations in paragraphs 40-62, and included information Plaintiff apparently learned in discovery.

 

Defendants contend that they have a statutory right to demur to the fifth amended complaint, arguing “If it were otherwise, Plaintiff would, for example, (1) be able to file a ‘vanilla’ complaint, (2) have the defendants answer, and (3) amend it to add improper material, but without fear of a demurrer to that later objectionable complaint.”  The Court disagrees.  The purpose of a demurrer is to test the legal sufficiency of the complaint.  The Court already determined that the allegations in the Third Amended Complaint were legally sufficient as to Soma, and all three Defendants have already answered as to these allegations and causes of action.  Plaintiff’s inclusion of specific facts learned through discovery do not generally operate to undermine allegations the Court already deemed to be legally sufficient.

 

Therefore, it is improper for Defendants to demur to the same allegations and causes of action to which they have already Answered.

 

B.     UNCERTAINTY

 

Even if it were proper for Defendants to demur to allegations and causes of action they already answered, or if the Court were to construe the demurrer as a motion for judgment on the pleadings, Defendants’ arguments are without merit.

 

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that the responding party cannot reasonably respond - i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Where a demurrer is made upon the ground of uncertainty, the demurrer must distinctly specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain, and where such uncertainty appears by reference to page and line numbers.  (See Fenton v. Groveland Comm. Services Dist. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 797, 809.) 

 

Defendants argue that the 5AC conflates the conduct of all of the defendants, making the 5AC uncertain.  The Court disagrees.  The 5AC clearly alleges that Defendant Kerr is the nurse who assisted Dr. Prokupek during the procedure (5AC ¶¶ 79) and Defendant Ravaei owns and operates Soma Surgery Center “without proper certification” (5AC ¶ 56) and the “mastermind” who directed the other Defendants’ conduct (5AC ¶¶ 56, 86.) 

 

Soma also renews its arguments that the allegations are too conclusory to establish respondeat superior, and therefore demurs to all causes of action for uncertainty.  The Court already specifically rejected these arguments in its decision overruling Soma’s demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint.  (See December 7, 2022 Minute Order.) 

 

Therefore, Defendants do not demonstrate that any of the portions of the 5AC are so bad that they cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against which Defendants.  The Court thus declines to sustain the demurrers on the basis of uncertainty.

 

C.    FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

                                                                    i.            Second Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty – Lack of Informed Consent

 

To prevail on a claim of breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) breach of the fiduciary duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by the breach. (Jameson v. Desta (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1144, 1164.) “A physician has a fiduciary duty to disclose all information material to the patient’s decision when soliciting a patient’s consent to a medical procedure. (Ibid.)

 

“A claim based on lack of informed consent—which sounds in negligence—arises when the doctor performs a procedure without first adequately disclosing the risks and alternatives.”  (Saxena v. Goffney (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 316, 324; see also Moore v. Regents of University of California (1990) 51 Cal.3d 120, 129 [“[I]n soliciting the patient's consent, a physician has a fiduciary duty to disclose all information material to the patient's decision”].)  “[W]e hold, as an integral part of the physician's overall obligation to the patient there is a duty of reasonable disclosure of the available choices with respect to proposed therapy and of the dangers inherently and potentially involved in each.” (Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 243.) 

 

[T]he patient's right of self-decision is the measure of the physician's duty to reveal.  That right can be effectively exercised only if the patient possesses adequate information to enable an intelligent choice. The scope of the physician's communications to the patient, then, must be measured by the patient's need, and that need is whatever information is material to the decision.  Thus the test for determining whether a potential peril must be divulged is its materiality to the patient's decision.  . . .   There must be a causal relationship between the physician's failure to inform and the injury to the plaintiff. Such causal connection arises only if it is established that had revelation been made consent to treatment would not have been given. Here the record discloses no testimony that had plaintiff been informed of the risks of surgery he would not have consented to the operation.

 

(Id. at p. 245 [cleaned up]

 

To prevail on a claim for failure to obtain informed consent, a plaintiff must prove (1) the defendant performed a medical procedure on Plaintiff; (2) the defendant did not disclose to Plaintiff the important potential results and risks of and alternatives to the medical procedure; (3) a reasonable person in Plaintiff’s position would not have agreed to the medical procedure if that person had been adequately informed; and (4) Plaintiff was harmed by a result or risk that the defendant should have explained.  (CACI No. 533.)

 

Defendants argue that there was no fiduciary duty here.  In so arguing, Defendants again attempt to renew Soma’s argument that the allegations are too conclusory to establish respondeat superior.  This Court already found the allegations sufficient to allege an agency relationship between Dr. Prokupek and Soma to withstand demurrer.  Similarly, the new allegations demonstrate that Ravaei “owns and operates [Soma] unlawfully and without proper licensing or insurance.  Ravaei sought out doctors in financial distress to undertake risking outpatient procedures often with no medical necessity” [5AC ¶ 16] and is the “mastermind” who directed Dr. Prokupek and the other Defendants’ actions.  (5AC ¶¶ 40, 56, 106.)  Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient to withstand demurrer.

 

Moreover, Plaintiff alleges that Kerr was the treating nurse during the procedure [5AC ¶ 79], that Kerr chose to move forward participating in the procedure, “[k]nowing that there was a risk of potential complications” [5AC ¶ 25] without disclosing the risks to Plaintiff, and when complications did arise, Kerr did not administer emergency treatment, and in fact left Plaintiff alone in the emergency room “for twenty minutes while his vital signs were dropping” [5AC ¶ 27.]  Thus, Plaintiff has alleged Kerr personally participated in performing a medical procedure on Plaintiff, and Defendants’ case law standing for the proposition that only the treating physician can breach a fiduciary duty applies with equal force to Kerr, as the treating nurse during the procedure.

 

The Court therefore overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty – lack of informed consent.

 

                                                                  ii.            Third Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384, as modified on denial of reh'g (Mar. 18, 2009).)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff fails to plead the elements of intentional misrepresentation with the requisite specificity. 

 

The Court disagrees.  The 5AC alleges that in June 2020 and October 2020, Dr. Prokupek and Hershey Weber, acting as agents for Soma, and at the direction of Defendant Ravaei, who owns and operates Soma, “falsely advised Plaintiff that it was safe for him to stop taking Xarelto for one week prior to his procedure[.]”  (5AC ¶¶ 86, 108.)  Plaintiff further alleges “Plaintiff reasonably relied on Defendants fraudulent statements and stopped taking his Xarelto one week prior to his surgery.  This resulted in Plaintiff suffering a massive stroke.”  (5AC ¶ 109.) 

 

As to Defendant Kerr, the 5AC alleges “Kerr met with the Plaintiff individually, on November 4, 2020, prior to the procedure, to ensure there would be no complications with the procedure” and “indicated that Plaintiff had been off his Xarelto for four days.”  (5AC ¶¶ 116-117.)  Thus, all Defendants are alleged, with specificity, to have informed Plaintiff that it was safe to go forward with the surgery after being off Xarelto for 4 days to a week prior to surgery, which Plaintiff relied on to his detriment.

 

Plaintiff further alleges Dr. Prokupek and Soma “doctored and manipulated” Dr. Prokupek’s notes “to remove the fact that he told Plaintiff to stop taking his blood thinner medication Xarelto for one week prior to the scheduled procedure” and acted “in concert to obscure the cause of Plaintiff’s stroke.”  (5AC ¶¶ 111-112.)  This delayed Plaintiff and Plaintiffs’ representations from uncovering the truth behind Plaintiff’s condition by approximately a year, delaying Plaintiff’s treatment which “further exasperated his injuries.”  (5AC ¶ 112.)   

 

Thus, the 5AC adequately alleges the elements of intentional misrepresentation with sufficient particularity.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Defendants’ Demurrer to the Fifth Amended Complaint in its entirety.

Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

MOTIONS

 

This case stems from a dispute over the remodel of Plaintiff Glenn Vallecillos, M.D., Inc.’s (“Plaintiff”) surgical center.  Defendants Pacific Source Insurance Agency, Inc. (“Pacific”) and Antoinette Turner (“Turner”), who is the Chief Executive Officer, Secretary, Chief Financial Officer, and Director of Pacific Source Insurance Agency (collectively, “Defendants”) demur to the second, twelfth, fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action alleged in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on the basis that they fail to state a cause of action, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e). 

 

Plaintiff opposes the demurrer and Defendants reply.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

            Defendants object to the Declarations of David Glaubiger and the proposed Second Amended Complaint filed in opposition to the demurrer on the basis that demurrers test the legal sufficiency of the operative complaint, and it is improper for the Court to consider extrinsic evidence.

 

            The Court agrees that with respect to the demurrer, intrinsic evidence is improper, and the Court will not consider the intrinsic evidence with respect to its determination of the demurrers.  However, to the extent Plaintiff has presented the Court with additional facts it could add to cure any deficiencies identified in the operative complaint, the Court may properly consider such additional facts in determining whether granting Plaintiff leave to amend is proper.

 

            Therefore, the Court overrules Defendants’ objections to the Glaubiger Declarations and proposed Second Amended Complaint, in accordance with these principles.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Paragraph 2 of the Glaubiger Declaration requests that the Court take judicial notice of its April 13, 2023 Minute Order.  California Rules of Court, rule 8.252(a)(1) requires “To obtain judicial notice by a reviewing court under Evidence Code section 459, a party must serve and file a separate motion with a proposed order.”  Because Plaintiff’s request does not conform with the procedural requirements, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for Judicial Notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.) 

 

A.    Second Cause of Action – Unjust Enrichment

 

“The elements for a claim of unjust enrichment are ‘receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.”  (Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 769.)  “The theory of unjust enrichment requires one who acquires a benefit which may not justly be retained, to return either the thing or its equivalent to the aggrieved party so as not to be unjustly enriched.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Defendants argue that the FAC fails to allege any benefit that they, as the Keyes Defendants’ insurance provider on the project, received.  Rather, Defendants contend, the FAC merely alleges that the other Defendants received money from Plaintiff, for construction services they did not render or did not render adequately.  But, as Defendants contend, there is no direct relationship between Plaintiff and Defendants, and Plaintiff does not allege Defendants received any benefit.

 

The FAC alleges that “defendants Keyes, Pacific and Turner benefited from the monies plaintiff paid to defendant Keyes pursuant to the construction contracts Keyes was able to secure with the aid of defendants Pacific and Turner” and “defendants Keyes, Pacific and Turner unlawfully accepted the benefits of that which plaintiff furnished” and “have been unjustly enriched” as a result.  (FAC ¶¶ 27-29.)

 

Thus, the FAC alleges sufficient “ultimate facts” to withstand demurrer.[1]  The specific extent to which Pacific and/or Turner received sums from or at the expense of Plaintiff is a factual issue to be fleshed out in discovery. 

 

B.     Twelfth Cause of Action – Negligence

 

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.”  (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.)

 

Defendants first argue that insurance brokers owe only a limited duty to their clients, to “use reasonable care, diligence and judgment in procuring the insurance required by an insured.”  (Demurrer at p. 6.)  Because there is no direct relationship between Plaintiff and Defendants, Defendants argue, they did not owe Plaintiff any duty.  The Court disagrees.

 

“To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must establish the defendant owed a legal duty of care.  Generally speaking, all persons have a duty to take reasonable care in their activities to avoid causing injury, though particular policy considerations may weigh in favor of limiting that duty in certain circumstances.”  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 209 [hereafter, “Brown”].)  In general, “[e]veryone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.”  (Civ. Code, § 1714.)  Section 1714 “establishes the default rule that each person has a duty to exercise, in his or her activities, reasonable care for the safety of others.”  (Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 214.) 

 

“Section 1714 states a broad rule, but it has limits. We have explained that the law imposes a general duty of care on a defendant only when it is the defendant who has created a risk of harm to the plaintiff, including when the defendant is responsible for making the plaintiff's position worse.   The law does not impose the same duty on a defendant who did not contribute to the risk that the plaintiff would suffer the harm alleged.  Generally, the person who has not created a peril is not liable in tort merely for failure to take affirmative action to assist or protect another from that peril.”  (Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 214 [cleaned up].)

 

In the case cited by Defendants, the court determined the insurance broker, that was not a party to the underlying contract, owed no duty to notify the insured of the insurer’s post-issuance insolvency, especially where the party to the contract was contractually obligated to do so.  (Pacific Rim Mechanical Contractors, Inc. v. Aon Risk Ins. Services West, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 1278, 1282 (hereafter Pacific Rim).)  It does not stand for the proposition, as Defendants contend, that insurance brokers owe no general duty of care to third parties, even when they stand to benefit from the insurance.  Indeed, a broker can be held liable where the broker “misrepresents the nature, extent or scope of the coverage being offered or provided[.]”  (Pacific Rim, supra, 203 Cal.App.4th at p. 1283.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges Defendants “misrepresented the terms of worker’s compensation policy no. 9217479 by claiming that ‘Global Ocean and Land Construction’ was the named insured (when it was not the named insured on that policy.)”  (FAC ¶ 84.)  Thus, based on the allegations the law imposes a general duty of care on Defendants because they in conjunction with Keyes created a risk of harm to Plaintiff.  As such, the Court finds that Plaintiff  has adequately alleged a duty of due care and a breach of that duty. 

 

Defendants next argue that Plaintiff does not allege any damages caused by the alleged misrepresentation of the worker’s compensation insurance policy, because Plaintiff’s $252,020 in alleged damages stem from Keyes’ faulty and incomplete construction work, plus an additional $100,000 to hire alternate contractors to fix and complete the project do not bear any connection to Defendants’ misrepresentation on the initial certificate of insurance.  (Demurrer at p. 6.)  Moreover, Defendants contend that the initial certificate of insurance was issued a year after Keyes was hired and months after he abandoned the project.  (Id. at p. 7.) 

 

The FAC alleges “as a direct and proximate result of the aforementioned conduct [Defendants’ misrepresentation regarding the worker’s compensation policy], plaintiff has been damaged in a sum to be determined at time of trial in this matter, but in excess of $250,000.”  Stated differently, but for Defendants’ breach of the duty of due care, Plaintiff would not have hired Keyes and would not have suffered the alleged harm. 

 

Thus, Plaintiff has pleaded sufficient ultimate facts to withstand demurrer as to the twelfth cause of action.

 

C.    Fourteenth Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendants contend Plaintiff fails to plead the elements for a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation against them with the requisite particularity.  Specifically, Defendants contend they made no misrepresentation to Plaintiff, and when they created the certificate of insurance, they did not have knowledge that Keyes lacked worker’s compensation coverage or that having worker’s compensation insurance was a pre-condition of Keyes being hired.

 

The FAC alleges: “At the time Pacific and Turner ‘created’ the Certificate of Insurance bearing the date April 1, 2022, they knew that the Certificate of Insurance contained false statements and that the false statements were material, i.e. Global Ocean and Land Construction was the named insured on worker’s compensation policy no. 9217479.”  (FAC ¶ 91.)  Thus, Plaintiff has pleaded specific facts with respect to the first two elements of misrepresentation and knowledge of falsity.

 

As for the third and fourth elements, intent to induce reliance and justifiable reliance, the FAC alleges, “Defendants tendered the aforementioned misrepresentation to plaintiff with the intention to induce plaintiff’s reliance on the false statement by hiring defendant Keyes (dba Global Ocean and Land Construction) to remodel the surgical center and, thereafter, paying him hundreds of thousands of dollars.”  However, Plaintiff alleges to have paid Keyes a sum of $258,200 between June 2021 and January 2022 for work on the project, and that Keyes abandoned the job in December 2021.  (FAC ¶¶ 15, 17.)  Thus, Defendants’ purported April 1, 2022 misrepresentation occurred long after Plaintiff hired and paid Keyes to construct the project.  As such, the timing of the allegations precludes an inference that in April 2022, Defendants intentionally misrepresented the worker’s compensation coverage in order to induce Plaintiff to hire and pay Keyes, which Plaintiff had already done. 

 

Therefore, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the fourteenth cause of action.

 

D.    Fifteenth Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

“The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231.)   Like intentional misrepresentation, causes of action for negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud, and must also, therefore, be pleaded with particularity.  (Ibid.) 

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s fifteenth cause of action fails for the same reasons as the fourteenth cause of action—namely, the allegations do not demonstrate an intent to induce reliance or actual reliance on the April 2022 misrepresentation.

 

E.     Sixteenth Cause of Action – Conspiracy to Commit Fraud

 

“The elements of an action for civil conspiracy are (1) formation and operation of the conspiracy and (2) damage resulting to plaintiff (3) from a wrongful act done in furtherance of the common design.”  (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1136 (hereinafter Prakashpalan).)  “There is no separate tort of civil conspiracy and no action for conspiracy to commit a tort unless the underlying tort is committed and damage results therefrom.”  (Ibid.)  “The significance of a conspiracy theory of liability is that each member may be held jointly liable as a tortfeasor, even though he or she may not have participated directly in the underlying tort.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendants first contend that Plaintiff has not alleged any underlying fraud tort.  As discussed above, Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action for intentional or negligent misrepresentation.  Thus, without a valid underlying fraud tort, Plaintiff’s conspiracy to commit fraud cause of action also fails. 

 

Moreover, “[w]here fraud is alleged to be the object of the conspiracy, the claim must be pleaded with particularity.”  (Prakashpalan, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at 1136.)  Here, Plaintiff’s conspiracy allegations are all alleged on information and belief.  (See FAC ¶¶ 101, 102, 103, 104.)  “[I]t is not sufficient to allege fraud or its elements upon information and belief unless the facts upon which the belief is founded are stated in the pleading.”  (Dowling v. Spring Val. Water Co. (1917) 174 Cal.218, 221.)  As discussed above, the purportedly fraudulent certificate of insurance was created and presented to Plaintiff long after Plaintiff hired and paid Keyes, and therefore could not have formed the basis of their alleged scheme to induce Plaintiff to hire and pay Keyes.  (See FAC ¶ 102 [These defendants agreed or understood that the purpose of their meetings and agreements was as described above, i.e. to induce plaintiff to agree to hire defendant Keyes to remodel the surgical center, make payment to defendant Keyes, etc.”].)

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s sixteenth cause of action is flawed.

 

2.      MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Defendants move to strike from the FAC references to punitive damages and attorneys’ fees.

 

Punitive Damages

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.) 

 

In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  Per Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)   As set forth in the Civil Code,

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.  (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

(Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1)-(3), emphasis added.) 

 

Further, a plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud or malice.  To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.  (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)  “When nondeliberate injury is charged, allegations that the defendant’s conduct was wrongful, willful, wanton, reckless or unlawful do not support a claim for exemplary damages; such allegations do not charge malice.  When a defendant must produce evidence in defense of an exemplary damage claim, fairness demands that he receive adequate notice of the kind of conduct charged against him.” (G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29 [cleaned up].)  In Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp, the Court of Appeal noted that the plaintiffs’ assertions related to their claim for punitive damages were “insufficient to meet the specific pleading requirement.”  (Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 598, 643 [plaintiffs alleged “the conduct of Defendants was intentional, and done willfully, maliciously, with ill will towards Plaintiffs, and with conscious disregard for Plaintiff's rights. Plaintiff's injuries were exacerbated by the malicious conduct of Defendants. Defendants' conduct justifies an award of exemplary and punitive damages”]; see also Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 [“The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim”].) 

 

Here, as discussed above, Plaintiff has not alleged facts with requisite specificity to state a cause of action for Intentional or Negligent Misrepresentation or Conspiracy to Commit Fraud to support a claim of punitive damages.  Similarly, there are no other allegations that Defendants intended to cause Plaintiff injury or acted with a willful and conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights or safety, or that Defendants subjected Plaintiff to despicable conduct resulting in cruel and unjust hardship. 

 

Attorneys’ Fees

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, which outlines recoverable costs to a prevailing party under section 1032, permits the recovery of attorney fees when authorized by contract, statute, or law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)  Code of Civil Procedure section 1021 provides “[e]xcept as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties [….]”  Similarly, Civil Code section 1717 provides “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.”  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not cited to any governing statute or agreement entitling Plaintiff to recover attorneys’ fees from Defendants. 

 

3.      LEAVE TO AMEND

 

A plaintiff has the burden of showing in what manner the complaint could be amended and how the amendment would change the legal effect of the complaint, i.e., state a cause of action. (See The Inland Oversight Committee v City of San Bernardino (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 771, 779; PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 156, 189.) A plaintiff must not only state the legal basis for the amendment, but also the factual allegations sufficient to state a cause of action or claim. (See PGA West Residential Assn., Inc. v Hulven Int'l, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.App.5th at p. 189.) Moreover, a plaintiff does not meet his or her burden by merely stating in the opposition to a demurrer or motion to strike that “if the Court finds the operative complaint deficient, plaintiff respectfully requests leave to amend.” (See Major Clients Agency v Diemer (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1133; Graham v Bank of America (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 618 [asserting an abstract right to amend does not satisfy the burden].)

 

Causes of Action 14, 15, 16, and Punitive Damages

 

Here, Plaintiff has provided additional allegations that (1) Defendants first issued a certificate of insurance misrepresenting that Keyes carried the requisite insurance on June 17, 2021, which they communicated to Welltower, the manager of the subject property, who, in turn, communicated to plaintiff that Keyes had the necessary insurance; (2) the language of the Certificate of Insurance issued by Defendants contains the exact same language as the Welltower Tenant Improvement Project Guide, which also indicated Welltower required its contractors to carry worker’s compensation insurance, suggesting that Defendants knew about the worker’s compensation insurance requirement when they issued the certificate misrepresenting that Keyes had such insurance; and (3) the October 2021 end date alleged in paragraph 16 of the FAC is an error, and in fact, Keyes continued to actively work on the construction project through April 2022.  Plaintiff has also filed a proposed second amended complaint, with additional allegations.

 

Thus, Plaintiff has met its burden to demonstrate there are additional, specific facts it can add to the FAC to cure the deficiencies identified by the Court with respect to the Fourteenth, Fifteenth, and Sixteenth causes of action, and Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. 

 

Attorneys’ Fees

 

With respect to attorneys’ fees, Plaintiff points to Business & Professions Code section 7160, which provides, “Any person who is induced to contract for a work of improvement … in reliance on false or fraudulent representations or false statements knowingly made, may sue and recover from such contractor … reasonable attorneys’ fees” and Code of Civil Procedure, section 1029.8, subd. (a), which provides “Any unlicensed person who causes injury or damage to another person as a result of providing goods or performing services for which a license is required … shall be liable to the injured person for treble the amount of damages assessed in a civil action in any court having proper jurisdiction.  The court may … award all costs and attorney’s fees to the injured person….”  While these provisions could apply to Keyes, as the “contractor” who “performed services,” they do not, on their face, provide a basis to hold the moving Defendants liable for Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees. 

 

Plaintiff cites to Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudia Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 510-511 (hereafter Applied Equipment) for the proposition that “a coconspirator incurs tort liability co-equal with the immediate tortfeasors.”  Even if Plaintiff is able to cure the deficiencies identified in the conspiracy to commit fraud cause of action, Applied Equipment does not support Plaintiff’s contention that the statutory fee shifting provisions can be expanded to other parties not contemplated by the statutes. 

 

First, Applied Equipment determined that there was no co-conspirator liability at all.  (Applied Equipment, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 519.)  Second, the damages at issue in Applied Equipment were punitive damages, not attorneys’ fees.  Finally, and most notably, the appellate court held:   “we decline to allow a conspiracy theory to accomplish an ‘end run’ around the statutory rule that punitive damages are per se not recoverable in ordinary actions for breach of contract.”  (Ibid.)  Similarly, here, Plaintiff has provided no legal basis to allow a conspiracy theory to accomplish an “end run” around the statutory rule shifting attorneys’ fees to the “contractor” who “provid[es] goods or perform[s] services for which a license is required.” 

 

Thus, even if Plaintiff is able to revive the sixteenth cause of action for conspiracy, Plaintiff has not established a contractual or statutory basis to recover attorneys’ fees from the Defendants.  

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second and Twelfth cause of action, and sustains Defendants’ Demurrer to the Fourteenth, Fifteenth, and Sixteenth causes of action with leave to amend.      

 

Further, the Court grants Defendants’ Motion to Strike in its entirety, but grants Plaintiff leave to amend with respect to the claim for punitive damages.

 

Further, Plaintiff shall file and serve a second amended complaint in conformance with the Court’s ruling on or before November 27, 2023.    

 

Defendants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

MOTION

 

Defendant Shawn King (“Defendant”) moves to quash service of the summons and complaint.  The motion is unopposed.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:  (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).) 

 

“A general appearance by a party is equivalent to personal service of summons on such party.”  (Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. v. Sparks Construction, Inc. (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1135, 1145.)  “A general appearance operates as a consent to jurisdiction of the person, dispensing with the requirement of service of process, and curing defects in service.”  (Ibid.)  “An appearance is general if the party contests the merits of the case or raises other than jurisdictional objections.”  (Ibid.)  In other words, “[i]f the defendant raises an issue for resolution or seeks relief available only if the court has jurisdiction over the defendant, then the appearance is a general one.”  (Factor Health Management v. Superior Court (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 246, 250.) 

 

“In the absence of a voluntary submission to the authority of the court, compliance with the statutes governing service of process is essential to establish that court's personal jurisdiction over a defendant. When a defendant challenges that jurisdiction by bringing a motion to quash,  the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.”  (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1439–1440; accord Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1160 [“It was incumbent upon plaintiff, after the filing of defendant's motion to quash, to present evidence discharging her burden to establish the requisites of valid service on defendant”]; Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413 [“when a defendant challenges the court's personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service’ ”].)   A declaration of service by a registered process server establishes a presumption that the facts stated in the declaration are true. (Evid. Code, § 647; Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)

 

            “In order to obtain in personam jurisdiction through any form of constructive service there must be strict compliance with the requisite statutory procedures.  (Zirbes v. Stratton (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 1407, 1417, quoting Stamps v. Superior Court (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 108, 110.) 

 

For service on persons within California, generally, service of summons and complaint must be done by personal service.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)  However, “[i]f a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served,” a plaintiff may serve an individual defendant “by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person's dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address . . . , in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address . . . , at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20, subd. (b).) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Here, Defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint on the basis that the proof of service filed on August 30, 2023 indicates he was personally served at an address he has never lived at and has no association with.  (King Decl. ¶¶ 1-3.)

 

However, on September 21, 2023, Defendant filed an answer to the complaint.  The Court notes that Defendant’s answer was filed before the motion to quash was filed on September 28, 2023.  Thus, Defendant made a general appearance in advance of challenging the service of the summons and complaint, and in so doing, Defendant voluntarily submitted to the jurisdiction of this Court. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            The Court denies Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and Complaint.
