DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV23028    Hearing Date: January 23, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CHRISTIAN OCHOA

                          

         vs.

 

THE REGENTS OF THE UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA, et al.

 

 

 Case No.:  22STCV23028

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 23, 2024

 

            Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED. Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication is DENIED.

 

            On 7/18/2022, Christian Ochoa (Plaintiff) filed suit against the Regents of the University of California aka University of California, Los Angeles (Defendant or UCLA), alleging: (1) discrimination; (2) harassment; (3) retaliation; (4) failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, and retaliation; (5) failure to accommodate; (6) failure to engage in interactive process; and (7) whistleblower retaliation.

 

            Now, Defendant moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication.

 

Factual Background

 

            Plaintiff was a career food services worker at UCLA. He alleges that he was fired based on his disability and related protected activity.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that summary judgment is appropriate here because there are no genuine issues of material fact as to any of Plaintiff’s claims.

 

            The Court addresses each cause of action in turn.

 

            As a preliminary matter, Defendant argues that all of Plaintiff’s FEHA-related allegations predating 1/9/2019 are time-barred. Defendant argues that the continuing violation doctrine cannot be applied to save any of these allegations from untimeliness because “Plaintiff admitted, as of 2017, Defendants’ conduct toward him had reached a degree of permanence and would not change regardless of any effort on his part to report or stop the conduct.” (Motion, 17: 15-17.) In support, Defendant cited Richards v. CH2M Hill, Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 798, 822-23.

 

            However, as noted by Plaintiff in opposition, even applying the standard set forth in Richard, none of Plaintiff’s claims are time-barred because actionable conduct supporting each cause of action occurred well within the 2019 statutory period proffered by Defendant:

 

First, all termination claims are timely given the termination events in May and June 2019; this applies to the first, third, fourth, and seventh causes of action for discrimination, retaliation, failure to prevent, and whistleblower retaliation respectively. Next, as to the second cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that he was harassed based on his disability after his return to work in April 2019 and through his termination date, including being called a “bitch”, a “pussy” and that he was doing “woman’s job” because he required accommodations. (PF 39, 41, 65.) Finally, the fifth and sixth causes of action for failure to engage and failure to accommodate are both supported by the conduct alleged in 2019, including Plaintiff’s termination just days after his doctor reported he was permanent and stationery, and required continued accommodations.

 

(Opp., 9: 18-24.)

 

            Moreover, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that there is at the very least a triable issue as to whether or not the alleged conduct achieved “permanence” before 2019, as defined in Richards. As argued by Plaintiff:

 

Plaintiff did not previously make any informal conciliation effort surrounding his harassment or failures to engage/accommodate, and therefore Defendant did not and could not make any statements or actions in response to those efforts, whereby it was made clear that further efforts would be futile. Moreover, Plaintiff’s conduct confirms that he did believe there was hope, even to the bitter end, that Defendant would do right by him and comply with the law. He continued to present his medical restrictions in hopes of being accommodated, even as the restrictions became permanent and stationery on April 29, 2019, and even though UCLA proceeded to start the termination machinery just days later on May 3, 2019. Plaintiff also then grieved his termination with the union. He had not taken any such steps at informal conciliation before those actions in 2019, and legal ‘permanence’ was therefore impossible in 2017 or 2018 as UCLA argues.

(Opp., 10: 17-25.)

 

            Accordingly, the Court cannot conclude that Plaintiff’s pre-1/9/2019 claims are time-barred as a matter of law.

 

I.                   Discrimination

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s disability discrimination claim fails because Defendant had a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for terminating Plaintiff.

 

To establish a claim for disability discrimination, the plaintiff initially has the burden to establish a prima facie case of discrimination. The plaintiff can meet this burden by presenting evidence that demonstrates, circumstantially or by inference, that he or she (1) suffered from a disability, or was regarded as suffering from a disability; (2) could perform the essential duties of the job with or without reasonable accommodations, and (3) was subjected to an adverse employment action because of the disability or perceived disability. (Jensen v. Wells Fargo Bank (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 245, 254.)

 

The McDonnell-Douglas framework is modified when the employer moves for summary judgment. “[T]he employer, as the moving party, has the initial burden to present admissible evidence showing either that one or more elements of plaintiff’s prima facie case is lacking or that the adverse employment action was based upon legitimate, nondiscriminatory factors.” (Serri v. Santa Clara Univ. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 861 [internal quotation omitted]; accord Dep’t of Fair Employment & Hous. v. Lucent Techs., Inc. (9th Cir. 2011) 642 F.3d 728, 745. If the employer meets this burden, “it is incumbent upon the employee to produce ‘substantial responsive evidence’ demonstrating the existence of a material triable controversy as to pretext or discriminatory animus on the part of the employer.” (Serri, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at 862.) “Legitimate” reasons in this context are reasons that are factually unrelated to prohibited bias, and therefore, if true, would preclude a finding of discrimination. (Guz v. Bechtel Nat’l, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 358.)

 

      In support, Defendant submitted evidence that Plaintiff was terminated because he pushed another employee, Mr. Juarez, in violation of the Workplace Violence Prevention Policy. Defendant submitted evidence that:

 

-         The University had grounds for believing Plaintiff had violated the Workplace Violence Prevention Policy based on Plaintiff’s admission to Mino Bhathena, immediately following the altercation, that he pushed Mr. Juarez. (UFs 66, 72-74.)

 

-         Mr. Juarez made a statement to Mr. Bhathena, memorialized in writing, wherein Mr. Juarez stated that he had asked Plaintiff, “Why are you pushing me?” (UF 84.)

 

-         Plaintiff again admitted he pushed Mr. Juarez in a later investigatory interview. (UF 77.)

 

-         Elijah Eggleston, an Acrobat employee also working in the same area, who was not involved in the disagreement between Plaintiff and Mr. Juarez, saw Plaintiff push Mr. Juarez. (UF 79-81.)

 

-         Immediately thereafter, Mr. Eggleston provided a statement, memorialized in writing, of what he had observed. (UF 79-81.)

 

Defendant’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that Plaintiff was terminated because he violated the Workplace Violence Prevention Policy, not because of his disability. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to disclose a triable issue of material fact.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that he has direct evidence of discriminatory animus towards him, and that this is sufficient to show pretext. In support, Plaintiff supports six cases: Kelly v. Stamps.com Inc. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1101; Sada v. Robert F. Kennedy Medical Center (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 138, 145; DeJung v. Sup. Ct. (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 533, 550; Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc. (2000) 530 U.S. 133, 151; Ash v. Tyson Foods, Inc. (2006) 546 U.S. 454, 456; and Cordova v. State Farm Ins. Cos. (9th Cir. 1997) 124 F.3d 1145, 1150.

 

Biased remarks at any stage, even outside the context of the employment decision, may be direct or indirect evidence of discrimination and prove discrimination. Both federal and California state law rejects the “stray remarks doctrine.” (Reid v. Google (2010) 50 Cal.4th 512, 538-540; Gov. Code § 12923(c)) [confirming Reid’s rejection of the stray remarks doctrine, such that on summary judgment the totality of evidence in the record must be considered, including any relevant discriminatory remark.] Determining the weight of discriminatory or ambiguous remarks is a role reserved for the jury.” (Reid, supra, 50 Cal.4th at 541.)

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-         Mino Bhathena (Mr. Bhathena), Plaintiff’s supervisor, was admitted in Defendant’s verified discovery responses to be a decision-maker in Plaintiff’s termination. (PF 100.) While Defendant has since tried to walk this back, the Court agrees there is at minimum a triable issue as to this fact.

 

-         Mr. Bhathena called him “bitch” and “pussy,” and that he was doing a “woman’s job,” in or around February 2017. (PSS ¶ 23.)

 

The Court agrees that this evidence—which shows discriminatory animus from a decision-maker directed at Plaintiff’s disability—is sufficient to disclose a triable issue as to whether his termination for a workplace incident was pretextual. This is especially true given: (1) Plaintiff’s evidence that this incident took place just four days after Plaintiff submitted his permanent disability accommodations; (2) Plaintiff’s evidence that other employees were not terminated even though they violated the same policy; and (3) given that he was protected by a “just cause” standard for termination under his union agreement that was not followed. Plaintiff submitted evidence that there was no progressive discipline for Plaintiff. In particular, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-         There was no progressive discipline for Plaintiff, though testimony confirms he was entitled to it (PF 6), and the conduct at issue did not rise to the level of seriousness to justify an immediate termination. (PF 5-7.)

 

-         Even a perceived “serious enough” violation would be subject to further review, including the employee’s length of employment, prior history of discipline, and similar factors. (PF 92.)

 

-         Juarez was not terminated but rather received just a one-day suspension. (PF 93, 109-112.)

 

-         Multiple other employees were not fired though they were found to have violated the same policy, some multiple times. (PF 91, 119-121, 129.)

 

-         Plaintiff had no major performance issues over his nearly ten-year career prior to his workplace injury and disability, but once he requested accommodations he was subjected to near-constant harassment (including disability-based comments). (PF 16-53, 65, 101.)

                 

Based on the foregoing, the Court agrees that this evidence is sufficient to show a triable issue of material fact as to whether or not Defendant’s proffered reason for termination is pretextual.

 

II.               Harassment

 

 Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s harassment claim fails as a matter of law because the majority of his supporting allegations are time-barred, and because the remaining claims fail to rise to the level of severe and pervasive.

 

To be actionable, harassment based on a disability or medical condition must be sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create a hostile working environment: “[The] acts of harassment cannot be occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial; rather the plaintiff must show a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature.” (Muller v. Automobile Club of Southern Calif. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 431, 446 [internal quotes omitted] [disapproved on other grounds by Colmenares v. Braemar Country Club, Inc. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1019,].) “[H]arassment focuses on situations in which the social environment of the workplace becomes intolerable because the harassment (whether verbal, physical, or visual) communicates an offensive message to the harassed employee.” (Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 706; Serri v. Santa Clara University (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 830, 869.)

 

As set forth above, the Court disagrees that Plaintiff’s pre-1/9/2019 allegations are time-barred. Accordingly, the Court considers all supporting allegations.

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence that Bhathena harassed Plaintiff based on his disability from about February 2017 to May 2019, routinely calling him names like “pussy” and “bitch” and referring derogatorily to his modified duties as a “woman’s job.” (PF 22, 23, 39, 40, 41, 42 65.) Whether or not this conduct rises to the level of actionable harassment is a triable issue of material fact. (Harris v. Forklift Systems (1993) 510 U.S. 17, 23, nothing that whether conduct is harassing is “usually a question of fact” for the jury.

 

Moreover, an employer is strictly liable for its supervisors’ actions. (Myers v. Trendwest Resorts (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1421.) Accordingly, Bhathena’s conduct is imputable to Defendant. 

 

      The Court agrees that there is a triable issue as to whether or not Plaintiff’s supervisor’s alleged conduct over the course of two years rises to the level of severe and pervasive conduct.

 

III.            Retaliation

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s retaliation claim fails because Plaintiff did not complain of FEHA violations, and because it had a legitimate non-discriminatory reason to terminate Plaintiff’s employment.

 

To establish a prima facie case of retaliation under FEHA, the “plaintiff must show: (1) he or she engaged in a ‘protected activity, (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer’s action.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.) If the plaintiff successfully establishes these elements, the burden shifts to the employer to provide evidence that there was a legitimate, non-retaliatory reason for the adverse employment action. (Morgan v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2000) 88 Cal.App.4th 52, 68.) If the employer produces evidence showing a legitimate reason for the adverse employment action, “the presumption of retaliation ‘drops out of the picture,’” and the burden shifts back to the employee to provide “substantial responsive evidence” the employer’s proffered reasons were untrue or pretextual. (Martin v. Lockheed Missiles & Space Co. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1718, 1735.)

 

In support, Defendant submitted evidence that:

 

-         Regarding complaints of FEHA violations, Plaintiff admitted he never complained to anyone at UCLA about alleged harassing comments made by his supervisor, Mr. Bhathena, purportedly connected to Plaintiff’s modified work (UFs 42-43.)

 

-         Plaintiff never sought a transfer to any other department on campus, applied for any other positions at the University while he was employed, or asked to work in any of the other dining commons where he could have a different supervisor. (UF 52.)

 

-         Plaintiff further admits he never reported any comments from his other supervisors, Glendis Alvarado or Juan Alvarado, about the severity of his work restrictions, injuries, or leaves of absence. (UFs 49- 51.) Plaintiff did not ask for any other accommodations beyond the modified work he was given per his numerous Transitional Return-to-Work Agreements. (UFs 18, 22, 25, 28.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that “Plaintiff engaged in several protected acts which a reasonable jury could find motivated UCLA’s decision to terminate his employment. First, Plaintiff made multiple requests for reasonable accommodations arising from his protected disability. This includes the final one, supported by Dr. Bolden’s report of April 29, 2019, in which Plaintiff’s restrictions were confirmed to be permanent. Rather than continue to accommodate Plaintiff, Defendant found a way to terminate him based on an incident that occurred on May 3, just four days after his doctor’s note. Next, Plaintiff complained to Bhathena about the constant harassment and name calling and Bhathena told Plaintiff, “There’s nothing you can do about it and you and I know that.” (Opp., 17: 13-18.)

 

      A request for an accommodation is protected activity. (See e.g. Martinez v. Costco Wholesale Corporation (S.D. Cal. 2020) 481 F.Supp.3d 107.) Moreover, as set forth above, there is a triable issue as to whether or not Defendant’s proffered reason for termination was pretextual. Taken together, Plaintiff has submitted evidence that supports a reasonable inference that he was retaliated against for requesting disability accommodations, and was pretextually fired for a workplace incident.

 

IV.            Failure to Prevent

 

Given the Court’s conclusion that there is a triable issue of fact as to Plaintiff’s discrimination, harassment, and retaliation claims, there is necessarily a triable issue as to the claim for failure to prevent.

 

V.                Failure to Accommodate and Failure to Engage in Interactive Process

 

Defendant argues that these claims fails because he cannot establish that he was not fully and consistently accommodated by the University.

 

To establish a disability discrimination claim based on a failure to accommodate, the plaintiff must prove that: 1) he has a disability covered by the FEHA; 2) he can perform the essential functions of the position; and 3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate the plaintiff’s disability. (Scotch v. Art Institute of Cal. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 986, 1009-1010.)

 

      In support, Defendant submitted evidence that:

 

-         The University provided Plaintiff with modified work through ten Transitional Return-to-Work Agreements—dated or prepared 1) September 26, 2017; 2) October 2, 2017; 3) October 10, 2017; 4) October 16, 2017; 5) November 6, 2017; 6) November 17, 2017; 7) March 22, 2018; 8) May 1, 2018; 9) September 20, 2018; and 10) April 4, 2019. (UFs 14-32, 61.)

 

-         Plaintiff admits that he did not ask for any other accommodations during the times he was on modified work. (UFs 18, 22, 25, 28.)

 

-         When the University could not find a reasonable accommodation to modify Plaintiff’s job duties within the limitations his doctor required, Plaintiff was approved for leaves of absence that were extended as needed as part of the ongoing interactive process. (UFs 33-39.)

 

-         Plaintiff admits that other than provide the University with doctor’s notes, Plaintiff never communicated to anyone at the University about a need for additional leave or changes in his work restrictions. (UFs 38-39.)

 

-         Plaintiff attended an interactive process meeting with his union representative present where he said he did not agree with the work restrictions from his doctor and said he could perform all the essential functions of his job without any accommodation. (UFs 54- 57.)

 

-         In accordance with Plaintiff’s indication that he would follow up with his doctor to review his permanent work restrictions, the University then provided him temporary accommodations while he did so. (UFs 57-58, 61.)

 

-         At no point in the April 3, 2019, interactive process meeting did Plaintiff ever say that the University was not accommodating his work restrictions, nor did Plaintiff say that anyone at the University was retaliating against him for having work restrictions or for requesting accommodations. (UFs 59-60.)

 

-         After his doctor released him to work, Plaintiff returned to his previous position in April 2019. (UFs 62-64.)

 

Together, Defendant’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that it accommodated Plaintiff for a period of approximately two years until he returned to his position in the De Neve dining hall, with Plaintiff never once communicating a need for anything beyond the accommodations provided. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to disclose a triable issue of material fact.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that “[s]ummary adjudication of Plaintiff’s fifth and sixth causes of action for FEHA Failure to Accommodate and FEHA Failure to Engage must be denied because Defendant required Plaintiff to work outside his medical restrictions in 2017, forced him on a leave in late 2018 where other accommodations were available, threatened his job security by calling his leave “personal” and unprotected (ignoring his FEHA rights) (PF 16-53) and later terminated Plaintiff’s employment in part based on his having sought accommodations and taken medical leave.” (Opp., 19:4-7.)

 

In support, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-         On April 3, 2019, UCLA only agreed to “temporarily” accommodate Plaintiff’s restrictions set forth in a March 21, 2019 report of Dr. Borden; they asked Dr. Borden to clarify Plaintiff’s restrictions.

 

-         On Monday April 29, 2019, Dr. Borden clarified the lifting restrictions but left the restrictions as permanent, to UCLA’s great dismay

 

-         Plaintiff was terminated arising from an incident occurring just four days later, on May 3, 2019

 

As such, Plaintiff has submitted evidence which supports a reasonable inference that Defendant pretextually terminated Plaintiff rather than accommodate, or engage in the interactive process around, the permanent restrictions he had submitted just four days prior.

 

In other words, even assuming Defendant did accommodate Plaintiff as to all prior accommodations, Plaintiff submitted a notice for permanent accommodations four days before his termination. If, in fact, Defendant terminated Plaintiff as a way to avoid providing these accommodations, this would amount to a failure to accommodate and a failure to engage in the interactive process.

 

Accordingly, a triable issue of material fact exists as to this claim.

 

VI.            Whistleblower Retaliation

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot state a claim because Defendant had legitimate non-discriminatory reasons for terminating him.

 

To establish a prima facie claim for retaliation under § 1102.5, an employee must show “(1) that he engaged in protected activity, (2) that he was thereafter subjected to an adverse employment action by his employer, and (3) that there was a causal link between the protected activity and the adverse employment action.” (Morgan v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2000) 88 Cal. App. 4th 52, 69.)

 

As set forth above, the Court concluded that Plaintiff has disclosed sufficient evidence to support a reasonable inference of pretext. Accordingly, there is a triable issue of material fact as to whether or not Plaintiff was terminated for legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons.

 

VII.         Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is denied. Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

It is so ordered.

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DAVID CURTIS GRIFFITH, et al.

 

         vs.

 

JAGUAR LAND ROVER NORTH AMERICA

 

 Case No.:  22STCV01149

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 23, 2024

 

Plaintiff is awarded $22,050.00 in reasonable attorney fees.          

 

Given that Plaintiffs’ motion did not include a verified memorandum of cost, the Court does not consider the cost award at this time. 

 

On 1/11/2022, Plaintiffs David Curtis and Stephanie Griffith (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC (Defendant), alleging violations of the Song-Beverly Act.

 

Now, Plaintiffs move for $87,821.25 in attorney fees and costs totaling $1,535.53.

 

Legal Standard

 

The party claiming attorneys’ fees must establish entitlement to such fees and the reasonableness of the fees claimed.  (Civic Western Corporation v. Zila Industries, Inc. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 1, 16.) “Except as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties[.]” (CCP § 1021.)

 

“It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.”  (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623.)  In exercising its discretion, the court should consider a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in handling the matter, the attention given, the success or failure, and the resulting judgment.  (Ibid.)

 

In determining what constitutes a reasonable compensation for an attorney who has rendered services in connection with a legal proceeding, the court may and should consider the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required and the skill employed in handling the litigation, the attention given, the success of the attorneys’ efforts, their learning, their age, and their experience in the particular type of work demanded the intricacies and importance of the litigation, the labor and necessity for skilled legal training and ability in trying the cause, and the time consumed. (Stokus v. Marsh (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 647, 657.)

 

In determining the proper amount of fees to award, courts use the lodestar method.  The lodestar figure is calculated by multiplying the total number of reasonable hours expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  “Fundamental to its determination … [is] a careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney … in the presentation of the case.”  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48 (Serrano III).)  A reasonable hourly rate must reflect the skill and experience of the attorney.  (Id. at 49.)  “Prevailing parties are compensated for hours reasonably spent on fee-related issues.  A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.”  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635 (Serrano IV); see also Weber v. Langholz (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1578, 1587 (“The trial court could make its own evaluation of the reasonable worth of the work done in light of the nature of the case, and of the credibility of counsel’s declaration unsubstantiated by time records and billing statements.”)

 

Reasonable attorney fees should be based on an objective standard of reasonableness, i.e., the market value of services rendered, not on some notion of cost incurred. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1090.)  The value of legal services performed in a case is a matter in which the trial court has its own expertise.  (Id. at 1096.)  The trial court may make its own determination of the value of the services contrary to, or without the necessity for, expert testimony.  (Ibid.)  The trial court makes its determination after consideration of a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.  (Ibid.)

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiffs seek $87,821.25 in attorney fees. This is based on a lodestar of $58,547.50, and a requested lodestar enhancement of 0.5 in the amount of $29,273.75.

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel claims the following hourly rates for the attorneys who worked on this case:

 

-         Sepehr Daghighian: pre-1/1/2023: $575/hr; post 1/1/2023: $625/hr

 

-         Michael H. Rosenstein: $650-$700/hr[1]

 

-         Brian T. Shippen-Murray: rate until 3/20/2022: $450/hr; for remainder of 2022: $500/hr; after 1/1/2023: $550/hr

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ counsel’s rates are excessive, given that lemon law cases generally are not complex. The Court agrees. In Morris v. Hyundai Motor Am. (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 24, 41, the trial court reduced the rates of Bryan Altman of the Altman Law Group, who has 30-plus years of litigation experience with over 100 jury trials, of which 20-plus were as lead trial counsel in lemon law cases, from $650 to $500 per hour, reduced Steve Mikhov of the Knight Law Group from $500 to $400, and reduced all the associates’ rates that ranged from $450 to $350 to $300 per hour. (Id. at 8-9.) In support of the lower court decision, the court of appeal held that:

 

[E]ven if Morris established that her attorneys’ rates were generally commensurate with other consumer law attorneys with the same level of experience and skill, Morris ignores that there are a number of factors that the trial court may have taken into consideration in determining that reductions in the attorneys’ hourly rates were warranted. The court reasonably could have reduced the rates based on its finding that the matter was not complex; that it did not go to trial; that the name partners were doing work that could have been done by lower-billing attorneys; and that all the attorneys were doing work that could have been done by paralegals.

 

            (Id. at pp. 23-24.)

 

            Similarly, Judge Randolph Hammock of the Los Angeles County Superior Court recently found rates of $350 per hour appropriate for an “experienced” lawyer to take a lemon law case all the way through trial. (See Mikhaeilpoor v. BMW of N. Am., LLC (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 240, 245-46 [“The court found the requested fee amount “was just not reasonable.”… The court decided that $350 “is a reasonable hourly rate for the services that were done.”].)

 

            Here, the Court finds the hourly rates to be unreasonable, and reduces the hourly rates for each of plaintiffs’ attorneys to a reasonable blended $350 per hour for all attorneys who litigated this case, consistent with Mikhaeilpoor. This is based the fact that this case did not address novel or difficult questions, and was straightforward and simple. No depositions took place and no trial documents were prepared.

 

Hours

 

            Plaintiff’s counsel claims a total of 102.6 hours. A review of the billing records indicates padding and duplicative and unnecessary billings. For example, a substantial portion of the billing records are for “reviewing,” “analyzing,” and “conferring.” Given the simplicity of the case, and the limited discovery, the quantity of such billings indicate padding. Moreover, there are multiple billings for the same meet and confer. For example, on 7/11/2023, there are three separate billings for the same meet and confer. After review, the Court agrees that Plaintiff’s fee award should be reduced consistent with the proposed reductions in Defendant’s Exhibit A. Accordingly, the Court reduces the reasonable hours to 63, and thus awards $22,050.00 in reasonable attorney fees.

 

            Plaintiff did not submit a verified memorandum of costs and thus the Court does not consider the cost request at this time.

 

 

It is so ordered.
