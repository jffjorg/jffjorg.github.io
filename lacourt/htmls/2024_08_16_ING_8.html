I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On March 16, 2023, Plaintiffs, Christina Maier, Tierney Mauge, and Traci Maier (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Kate Dunitz, and DOES 1 through 10. On August 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Conversion; (2) Defamation (Slander); and (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

On December 4, 2023, Plaintiff, Christina Maier propounded Form Interrogatories, Set Three and Requests for Admission on Defendant. Responses by Defendant were due on or before January 5, 2024. However, on January 14, 2024, Plaintiff notes that Defense Counsel emailed objections with no responses to each of the discovery requests. Plaintiffs assert in their moving papers that on February 1, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter, but that despite this, there has been no response or any attempt to resolve the matter.

 

In Defendant’s opposition papers, and specifically in the Declaration of Thomas Carter (“Carter Decl.”), defense counsel, it notes that since the filing of the motions, Defendants have served verified, amended responses to each set of discovery that is subject to each of the motions. (Carter Decl., ¶ 2.) Carter further notes that he has provided Plaintiffs with verifications for each previously served response as well as the amended responses on the very day those verifications became available. (Carter Decl., ¶¶ 2, 3.)

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On March 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their motions to compel Defendant’s initial and further responses. On April 2, 2024, Defendant filed opposition briefs. On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff Plaintiffs filed reply briefs.

 

On May 21, 2024, this Court held an IDC with the parties, continued the motions, and ordered counsel to contact the Court staff by July 19, 2024.

Case Number: 23TRCV03890    Hearing Date: August 16, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 August 16, 2024

¿¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   23TRCV03890

¿¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Onni Manhattan Towers Limited Partnership v. Matern Law Group, PC, et al.

¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendants, Matern Law Group, PC and Matthew J. Matern

¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff, Onni Manhattan Towers Limited Partnership

 

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set.

¿¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              Demurrer

¿¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                  Defendants’ Demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action, SUSTAINED with twenty (20) days leave to amend as to the third, and OVERRRULED as to the fourth, and fifth causes of action.  

 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On November 21, 2023, Plaintiff, Onni Manhattan Towers Limited Partnership (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants, Matern Law Group, PC, Matthew J. Matern, and DOES 1 through 10. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Guaranty; (3) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Declaratory Relief RE Lease; and (5) Declaratory Relief RE Guaranty.

 

Now, Defendants, Matern Law Group, PC and Matthew J. Matern (collectively, “Defendants”) file a demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On July 16, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint. On August 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On August 12, 2024, Defendants filed a reply brief.

 

¿II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

           

            With Plaintiff’s opposition papers, Plaintiff requested this Court take judicial notice of the following:

 

1.     Plaintiff Onni Manhattan Towers Limited Partnership’s Complaint (the “Complaint”) filed in this action on November 21, 2023, (Exhibit 1.) 

2.     Urgency Ordinance No. 23-0005-U of the Manhattan Beach City Council (the “MB Ordinance”) adopted February 7, 2023. (Exhibit 2.)

 

The Court GRANTS this request and takes judicial notice of the above.

 

III. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

 A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)¿¿ 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Defendants demur to the original complaint on the grounds they argue the first, second, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against Defendants and are uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible.

 

i.              Meet and Confer Requirement

                      

The declaration of Scott J. Kalter is offered in support of Defendants’ counsel’s compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. Kalter declares that on February 14, 2024, he spoke over the phone with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the defects that he anticipated raising in Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint. (Declaration of Scott J. Kalter (“Kalter Decl.”), ¶ 4.) However, Kalter contends that the parties were unable to reach an agreement which necessitated the filing of the demurrer. (Kalter Decl., ¶ 4.)  

 

Thus, this Court finds that the parties have met and conferred in good faith prior to filing the demurrer, and the meet and confer requirements have been met.

 

 

 

ii.               Breach of Contract

 

First, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for breach of contract because, as pled, the cause of action is uncertain. To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that it and Defendants entered into the lease whereby Defendant, Matern Law, agreed to lease suite 200 and suite 210 at Manhattan Towers and pay rent, and other sums due thereunder, to Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 28.) Plaintiff alleges that it performed all conditions, covenants, obligations, and promises required to be performed in accordance with the terms and conditions of the lease and the amendments. (Complaint, ¶ 29.) Plaintiff further alleges that at no time did it excuse or waive any obligations or breaches of Matern Law under the lease. (Complaint, ¶ 30.) Plaintiff asserts that Matern Law and DOES 1 through 10 breached their obligations in the lease and amendments thereto, without cause and by failing to comply with their obligations including the requirement to make monthly payments and communicated it would not comply with any of its obligations related to Suite 210. (Complaint, ¶ 31.) As a result of the alleged breach of contract by Defendants, Plaintiff contends that it has been damaged by losing revenue as a result of Defendants’ failure to make payments of rent, common area maintenance charges, taxes, and interest among other costs, and has incurred attorneys’ fees and costs to enforce the Lease and Guaranty. (Complaint, ¶ 32.) The total amount of damages Plaintiff claims is owed by Defendants is in excess of $1,668,620.50. (Complaint, ¶ 32.)

 

In their moving papers, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s complaint states that Defendant did not pay rent beginning on or about April 1, 2020 as a result of the COVID-19 pandemic Complaint, ¶ 17), and that if Defendant had a valid excuse for not paying rent, then Defendant could not be in breach of the lease.  Accordingly, Defendants argue that the allegations they wrongfully relied upon Manhattan Beach Ordinance No. 20-00012-U are conclusory and deductions and this Court does not have to accept said allegations as true for purposes of a demurrer. Preliminarily, the Court is confused by Defendants’ argument. A demurrer tests the sufficiency of the pleadings on their face, not whether the Court can resolve factual disputes between the parties at the pleading stage. As noted by Plaintiff, “a demurrer based on an affirmative dense will be sustained only where the face of the complaint discloses that the action is necessarily barred by the defense.”

 

However, Plaintiff points out in their opposition that even if Defendants’ purported excuse for non-payment  - COVID-19 – was legitimate, this would only serve to postpone, and not eliminate, Defendant’ payment obligations. The Court agrees. Filed concurrently with Plaintiff’s opposition, is the judicially noticed City of Manhattan Beach, “Urgency Ordinance No. 23-0005-U,” which notes that on December 20, 2022, the City council voted to end the City’s local emergency on February 28, 2023, and directed staff to prepare an ordinance further amending the 2020 Eviction Ordinance to rescind certain regulations pertaining to commercial tenants. This noted that the language of Manhattan Beach Ordinance No. 20-0012-U stated, “[n]othing in this Ordinance shall relieve the commercial tenant of liability for the unpaid rent that became due during the period from March 17, 2020 to February 7, 2023, which the tenant must pay on or before September 1, 2023.”

 

This Court notes that based on the complaint itself, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiff’s complaint and request for judicial notice contain the lease agreement, guaranty agreement, and assignment to Plaintiff, first amended lease agreement, and second amendment to the lease agreement. Based on Defendants’ arguments on demurrer and their reply, they have not persuaded this Court to rule in favor of sustaining demurrer as they do not illustrate how the first cause of action for breach of contract is uncertain.

 

As such, the demurrer as to the first cause of action for Breach of Contract is OVERRULED.

 

iii.             Breach of Guaranty

 

Next, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for Breach of Guaranty fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action against Defendant because no Guaranty between Plaintiff and Defendant, Matthew J. Matern was ever had. The Court disagrees. As alleged in the Complaint and its attached exhibit, on April 10, 2013, Defendant, Matthew J. Matern entered into a Guaranty Agreement. Section P of the lease agreement indicates, “Guarantor Matthew Matern, a single man, pursuant to the Guaranty Agreement in the form attached hereto as Exhibit F.” (Complaint, Exhibit A.) That further Guaranty Agreement, signed by Defendant, Matthew J. Matern, states: “The Guaranty Agreement is for the benefit of Landlord, its successors and assigns…” (Complaint, Exhibit A, Lease Agreement, Exhibit F, ¶ 6.) Next, the First and Second Amendment to the Lease (Complaint, Exhibits B & C), assert that it is being entered into on March 30, 2016 between Onni Manhattan Towers Limited Partnership, and the Law Offices of Matthew J. Matern.

 

In their reply brief, Defendants argue that the Guaranty Agreement is not incorporated into the lease agreement, but was instead attached to it and that the First Amendment to the Lease did not reference the Guaranty executed between the previous landlord and individual, Defendant, Matthew Matern. However, this is again offered without any evidence or showing thereof. Instead, the Court’s review of the First Amended Lease agreement, at paragraph 9, states: “Except as expressly amended herein, the Lease shall remain in full force and effect and, as hereby amended, is ratified and confirmed by the parties hereto. In the event of a conflict between the provisions of this First Amendment and those of the Lease, the provisions of this First Amendment shall control. The Second Amended Lease states the same, and neither amendment includes a new and amended section for the guaranty issue.

 

Further, as noted above, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged duty, breach, causation, and damages.  Thus, the demurrer as to the second cause of action for Breach of Guaranty is OVERRULED.

 

iv.             Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Third, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently allege its third cause of action for Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing. “A breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing involves something beyond breach of the contractual duty itself and it has been held that bad faith implies unfair dealing rather than mistaken judgment.” (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1394.) “If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated . . . [T]he only justification for asserting a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant is to obtain a tort recovery.” (Id. at pp. 1394-1395.) To recover in tort for breach of the implied covenant, the defendant must “have acted unreasonably or without proper cause.” (Id. at p. 1395, citations and italics omitted.)

 

Here, this Court notes that Plaintiff’s allegations regarding this cause of action do not seem to go beyond the statement of a contract or guaranty breach. In fact, the facts alleged in this section contend that Defendants failed to make payments due under the lease or guaranty agreement, and in doing so, did not act fairly or in good faith. (Complaint, ¶¶ 11-12.) In opposition, Plaintiff cites to Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230 (“Thrifty Payless”) to argue that conduct indicating “unfair dealing” because of conduct – like refusing to pay rent – can constitute breach of both the express terms of the contract and the implied terms. (Opp. at 8.) Although the Court in Thrifty Payless found this to be the case when it came to the allegation that the Thrifty Payless defendant breached the agreement when it improperly exercised its discretion in allocating costs between retail and nonretail space, constituting both breach of contract and breach of the implied covenant. However, this Court notes that additional bad faith was sufficiently alleged in Thrifty Payless as to not only constitute the defendant’s behavior in that case to be a breach of a paragraph of the contract, but also by using defendant’s discretion in a way that breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

In the case at bar, although Plaintiff’s allegations allege bad faith, none of the allegations in this section differ from what the plain language of the contract or its subsequent integrations included. Defendant Law Firm allegedly breached the contract by failing to pay rent, and Defendant, Matthew J. Matern breached the Guaranty Agreement when failing to pay rent in lieu of his law firm paying. There are no allegations such as those made in Thrifty Payless, where there was further wrongdoing alleged in a sufficient way as to make this breach not only be in contract, but also be in the covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Is there any additional remedy Plaintiff seeks for the 3rd cause of action that is not covered by the first two causes of action?  As such, this Court SUSTAINS this cause of action on demurrer and allows twenty (20) days to amend.

 

 

v.               Declaratory Relief RE Lease and Declaratory Relief RE Guaranty

 

Lastly, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s fourth and fifth causes of action for declaratory relief are derivative and redundant of Plaintiff’s other claims, and therefore fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.) A cause of action for declaratory relief should not be used as a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues raised in another cause of action. (General of America Insurance Co. v. Lilly (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 465, 470.) “The availability of another form of relief that is adequate will usually justify refusal to grant declaratory relief” (California Insurance Guarantee Association v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1617, 1624), and a duplicative cause of action is subject to demurrer (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290). Further, “there is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.” (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, quotation marks omitted.)

 

However, because this Court found that the first and second causes of action are sufficiently alleged, this argument is unconvincing. Plaintiff also argues, on opposition, that the declaratory relief causes of action seek to adjudicate the parties’ ongoing relationship and may be advanced either alone, or with other relief.  While both causes of action are almost identical to that of the first and second causes of action, the declaratory relief claims appear to go not only to historical alleged breaches but also anticipate a controversy as to future months lease payments. This Court thus overrules the demurrer as to the fourth and fifth causes of action.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action, SUSTAINED with twenty (20) days leave to amend as to the third, and OVERRULED as to the fourth and fifth causes of action.   

 

            Defendants are ordered to give notice.

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Omar Garcia v. General Motors, LLC, et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiffs, Omar Garcia

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant, General Motors, LLC (No Opposition)

 

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set.

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant's PMK

                                                (2)  CMC

 

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) GRANT as to the PMK deposition and the Court will discuss a date by which the deposition is to be taken.  The Motion is DENIED as to compelling the documents sought due to the lack of a Separate Statement or other showing of good cause for every category sought. Monetary sanction of $1500 are to be payable by GM or its counsel to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days

                                                (2)  The parties submitted to typical unhelpful CMC statements in this Song-Beverly Act case involving a 2018 Silverado.  Plaintiff is to provide a summary of the nature of the Lemon Law case (i.e., is it a presumption case, a single recurring defect case, a constellation of claimed defects case, an over-30-days to repair case, etc.  and a discussion of efforts to have this case placed in a future mediation block.

¿¿ 

¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff, Omar Garcia (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant, General Motors, LLC, and DOES 1 through 10. The complaint alleges a violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff contends that he noticed the deposition of GM’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”). Along with Plaintiff’s notice, Plaintiff asserts that a meet and confer letter was also served requesting Defendant provide additional dates to conduct the deposition by March 6, 2024, if the noticed date did not work with it or it’s counsel’s schedule. However, by March 6, 2024, Plaintiff contends that Defendant failed to respond to Plaintiff’s meet and confer letter nor did Defendant indicate that the parties would need to meet and confer to establish a mutually agreeable date and time for the deposition. Similarly, Plaintiff contends that GM failed to provide objections to the deposition notice.

 

            Plaintiff asserts that assuming the deposition was confirmed, on March 27, 2024, two days prior to the noticed deposition, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to opposing counsel to confirm the deposition was moving forward. In response, Defendant’s counsel advised tat the deposition would not move forward, notwithstanding the fact that Defendant did not serve objections.

 

            As such, Plaintiff has brought this Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant GM’s PMK and Request for Production of Documents.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

            On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Compel Deposition of GM’s PMK and Request for Production of Documents. To date, no opposition has been filed. On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Non-Opposition. In fact, other than a minimalist CMC statement, GM has not filed anything in this case besides its Answer.

¿ ¿¿ 

¿II. MEET AND CONFER

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(b)(2) requires that a motion to compel a part deponent to appear or produce documents “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (C.C.P. §2025.450(b)(2).) The attempt to resolve informally may be made either by conferring “in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney.”  (C.C.P. §2023.010(i), [failure to make such attempt constitutes “misuse of discovery process”].)

 

            Here, Plaintiff notes that on March 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel requested an alternative date in which to conduct the deposition of Defendant’s PMK via email and establishing an April 5, 2024 deadline to provide alternative dates. By April 5, 2024, Plaintiff contends that no alternative dates were provided, nor were any attempts to meet and confer made by Defendant’s counsel. Further, on April 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel served a second meet and confer letter requesting three days in which to conduct the deposition of Defendant’s PMK, and establishing a April 19, 2024 deadline to provide alternative dates. However, by April 19, 2024, no alternative dates were provided, nor attempts to meet and confer from Defense Counsel. Additionally, on April 24, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel notes that a request for alternative dates was also sent, establishing a May 1, 2024 deadline to provide, but by then, no alternative dates or meet and confer dates were provided by Defendant’s counsel.

 

            This Court finds that Plaintiff’s attorney sufficiently attempted to meet and confer prior to bringing this motion.

 

III. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A.    Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, section (a) provides:  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  

 

The motion must “be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition…by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (2).) A court shall impose monetary sanctions if the motion to compel is granted unless the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or other circumstances would make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code. Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)  

 

B.    Discussion

¿ 

            Here, because GM failed to object to the notice of deposition, and also failed to provide alternative dates for the taking of the deposition, the Court notes that it would typically GRANT such a motion. However, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2045.450 subdivision (b)(1), Plaintiff’s motion was required to set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2045.450, subd. (b)(1).  Plaintiff’s motion is missing this analysis. While the Court can compel the parties to set discovery for a mutually agreeable date, the good faith of the categories of discovery remains. Further, pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1020(a)(5), a motion to compel the production of documents at a deposition must be accompanied by a separate statement. Plaintiff has not filed a separate statement in the bringing of this motion, but the motion is also for the production of documents at the deposition. As such, this Court GRANTS the motion to compel the deposition of GM”s PMK but DENIES this motion to compel the production of the requested documents. The denial of the motion to compel the documents is without prejudice to Plaintiff serving a new or the same document demand before the date set for the deposition, putting GM to the task of asserting timely objections to any specific category or categories of requested documents.  Monetary sanction of $1500 are to be payable by GM or its counsel to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days.
