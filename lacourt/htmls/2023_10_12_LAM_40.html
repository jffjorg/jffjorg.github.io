DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV35458    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

GUADALUPE MAXEY,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KND REAL ESTATE 40, LLC dba KINDRED HOSPITAL – PARAMOUNT; KND SNF REAL ESTATE HOLDINGS, LLC; KND REAL ESTATE HOLDINGS, LLC; KINDRED HEALTHCARE OPERATING, LLC; AGVA, LLC dba KEI-AI SOUTH BAY HEALTHCARE CENTER; ASPEN SKILLED HEALTHCARE, INC., AND DOES 1 THROUGH 50, INCLUSIVE,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          21STCV35458

 Hearing Date:   10/12/23

 Trial Date:        2/20/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Guadalupe Maxey’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Defendant Kindred Healthcare Operating, LLC to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production, Set Two, and Request for Sanctions.

 

Background

Pleadings

Plaintiff Guadalupe Maxey sues Defendants KND Real Estate 40, LLC (Kindred Paramount), KND SNF Real Estate Holdings, LLC (since dismissed), KND Real Estate Holdings, LLC (since dismissed), Kindred Healthcare Operating, LLC (KHO), Agva, LLC, Aspen Skilled Healthcare, Inc., and Does 1 through 50 pursuant to a September 24, 2021 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Dependent Adult Abuse & Neglect, (2) Negligence, and (3) Violation of Residents Rights.

The claims arise from allegations that Plaintiff Maxey failed to receive proper treatment while under Defendants’ care, resulting in, among other things, a long-standing contracture condition of her left arm, which, to this day, has left her with no function in that arm.

The relationship between Defendants, as alleged in the Complaint, is that KHO is the parent company of KND Real Estate Holdings, LLC, which is, in turn, the parent company of KND SNF Real Estate Holdings, LLC, which is, in turn, the parent company of Kindred Paramount. KHO is thus the ultimate parent company of Kindred Paramount.

Motion Before the Court

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff Maxey moved to compel further responses and production from KHO related to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production (RPDs), Set Two, Nos.115-119, 121-126, 129-132, and 137-153.

On September 27, 2023, KHO opposed Plaintiff’s motion.

On October 5, 2023, Plaintiff Maxey replied to the opposition.

Plaintiff Maxey’s motion is now before the Court.

 

Motion to Compel Further Production

Legal Standard

A motion to compel a further response is used when a party gives unsatisfactory answers or makes untenable objections to interrogatories, demands to produce, or requests for admission. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)

To request further production, a movant must establish: (1) good cause for the production (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1); Sinaiko, supra, at p. 403); and (2) that a further response is needed because (a) the responding party’s statement of compliance with the demand to produce is incomplete Code Civ. Anti-SLAPP Motion

Legal Standard

Anti-SLAPP analysis under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 proceeds in two familiar steps. In the first step, the defendant or moving party must make “a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one ‘arising from’ protected activity.” (Barry v. State Bar of California (2017) 2 Cal.5th 318, 321, quotations omitted (Barry).) In this context, the term “protected activity” refers to speech or petitioning activities. (Barry, 2 Cal.5th at p. 321.) A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim; otherwise stated, “the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action [must] itself [be] … an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1063, quotations omitted (Park).) “[T]he focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’” (Ibid.) In teasing out whether protected conduct exists, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability. (Ibid.)

If the court finds the defendant or moving party succeeds at the first step, then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to “demonstrate[] a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Ibid. [quotations omitted].) At the second step, courts “evaluate the defendants’ evidence only to determine if it defeats that submitted by the plaintiff as a matter of law.’ [Citation.] ‘[I]n order to establish the requisite probability of prevailing [citation], the plaintiff need only have “‘stated and substantiated a legally sufficient claim.’” [Citation.] “Put another way, the plaintiff ‘must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.’”’ [Citation.] … That burden [is] not particularly high.” (Area 51 Productions, Inc. v. City of Alameda (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 581, 602 (Area 51 Productions, Inc.).) “Claims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 94 (Navallier).) If the cause of action satisfies both prongs of the anti-SLAPP statute, then it is subject to being struck. (Barry, supra, 2 Cal.5th at p. 321.)

As stated by our State’s highest Court: “This is a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure at an early stage of the litigation.’” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 192 (Varian Medical Systems, Inc.).)

Anti-SLAPP Motion: DENIED.

I. First Prong, Protected Activity

A defendant meets his burden of showing that a plaintiff’s claim arises from that defendant’s exercise of free speech or petition rights by making a prima facie showing that the act or conduct underlying the plaintiff’s claims falls within one of the four categories found in Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e). (Navellier, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 88.) “Courts should analyze each claim for relief — each act or set of acts supplying a basis for relief, of which there may be several in a single pleaded cause of action — to determine whether the acts are protected [the first prong of the analysis] and, if so, whether the claim they give rise to has the requisite degree of merit to survive the motion [the second prong of the analysis].” (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010.) “[W]hen the allegations referring to an arguably protected activity are only incidental to a cause of action based essentially on non-protective activity, collateral allusions to protected activity should not subject the cause of action to the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Martinez v. Metabolife Internat., Inc. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 188 (Martinez).)

Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e) states:

As used in this section, “act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes:

(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,

(2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law,

(3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or

(4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e) (italics added.)

In its motion, CAB argues that it is being sued based on allegations that involve the litigation privilege. In support, CAB compares the Cross-Complaint’s allegations to cases finding that anti-SLAPP protections applied to pleadings where the defendant was alleged to have, through a tort or otherwise, obtained of a writ of execution or levied property pursuant to that writ of execution. CAB also argues that nothing in this Court’s June 9, 2023 order directed return of the levied monies to J&S, for which reason J&S can only be suing CAB based on lawful activity undertaken pursuant to a valid judgment. (Mot., pp. 4-9.)

In opposition, J&S distinguishes the case law cited by CAB in the anti-SLAPP motion. J&S also argues that CAB is not being sued for having secured the writ or levying funds thereafter but is instead being sued because CAB did not release the monies to J&S after the writ of execution was quashed by the Court, which, by operation of law, should have led CAB to revert the $98,969.45 in levied monies back to J&S. (Opp’n, pp. 3-7.)

In reply, CAB reiterates its points on motion and cites case law not raised in the anti-SLAPP motion. These latter points are not considered due to being raised for the first time on reply. (Reply, pp. 2-6; Nordstrom Com. Cases (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 576, 583 [Courts generally need not accept arguments raised for the first time on reply without good cause explanation as to why points were not raised earlier].)

The Court finds in favor of J&S.

The arguments and case law raised by CAB are distinguishable. The cases cited by CAB appear to involve questions of whether protected activity arises from allegations that a party, through tortious conduct or otherwise, secured a writ of execution or levied monies. However, the claims in the Cross-Complaint are not based on whether CAB unlawfully secured the writ of execution or levied monies from J&S’s bank account. Instead, the claims are based on CAB’s failure to return the levied funds to J&S after the writ of execution pursuant to which those monies were levied was quashed by the Court on June 9, 2023. In essence, J&S is suing CAB for failure to follow the spirit of the Court’s June 9, 2023 order. The Court cannot find that such alleged conduct involves protected activity.

The Court also disagrees with CAB’s position that this Court needed to explicitly command CAB to return the levied monies in the June 9, 2023 order. Such a condition was implicit in the June 9, 2023 order because if the writ of execution failed, then the levy that followed also failed, and any levied monies needed to be returned. CAB does not provide authority to the contrary, instead arguing that unless it was explicitly told to return the $98,969.45 in levied monies, it did not need to do so. Such an interpretation of the Court’s June 9th order is narrow and incorrect.

CAB’s anti-SLAPP motion is therefore DENIED because the Cross-Complaint’s claims do not arise from protected activities within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16.  

Conclusion

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc.’s Motion to Strike Cross-Complaint of J&S Painting, Inc. is DENIED.

Demurrer

Demurrer Sufficiency Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) This device can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a [general] demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

I.

Second Cause of Action, Invasion of Privacy: SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

To allege an invasion of privacy in violation of the state constitutional right, a plaintiff “must establish each of the following: (1) a legally protected privacy interest; (2) a reasonable expectation of privacy in the circumstances; and (3) conduct by defendant constituting a serious invasion of privacy.” (Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 39-40 (Hill).) Defendants may prevail by negating any element or “by pleading and proving, as an affirmative defense, that the invasion of privacy is justified because it substantively furthers one or more countervailing interests. Plaintiff, in turn, may rebut a defendant’s assertion of countervailing interests by showing there are feasible and effective alternatives to defendant’s conduct which have a lesser impact on privacy interests.” (Id. at p. 40.) “Actionable invasions of privacy must be sufficiently serious in their nature, scope, and actual or potential impact to constitute an egregious breach of the social norms underlying the privacy right.” (Id. at p. 37.)

The Complaint’s second cause of action alleges an invasion of privacy by Defendants through Defendants obtaining and furnishing investigative consumer reports about Plaintiffs without complying with mandatory requirements of the ICRAA relating to such reports. (Complaint, ¶¶ 78-79; see also Complaint, ¶¶ 42-57.)

In its demurrer, ERM argues that the second cause of action is not sufficiently stated for two reasons. The first is that the Complaint’s allegations show that Plaintiffs did not have a reasonable expectation of privacy in the information obtained by the investigative reports because the Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs consented to release their information and acknowledge that such background checks are common practice in rental applications. Second, ERM argues that any invasion of privacy alleged in the Complaint was not serious for the reasons discussed in the preceding sentence. ERM compares to case law to support these points. (Demurrer, pp. 6-7.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs cite to case law to support the position that they have a reasonable expectation of privacy in investigative consumer reports about them and that such reports will not be obtained without compliance with the ICRAA, for which reason ERM committed a serious invasion of privacy. (Opp’n, p. 2.)

In reply, ERM argues that Plaintiffs opposition arguments are conclusory because they cite to case law but do not tie that law to the allegations in the Complaint to show where the elements of this tort are pleaded. ERM also argues that Plaintiffs fail to distinguish case law cited by ERM in its demurrer. (Reply, pp. 2-3.)

The Court finds in favor of ERM.

A reasonable expectation of privacy exists where the Plaintiff “[has] not manifested by his or her conduct a voluntary consent to the invasive actions of defendant.” (Hill, supra, 7 Cal.4th at p. 26.) Here, the Complaint is founded on allegations that Plaintiffs were harmed when ERM obtained investigative consumer reports regarding Plaintiffs without complying with the terms of the ICRAA. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 42-57, 58-77, 78-79.) While Plaintiffs signed applications that stated that ERM may screen for criminal background and previous evictions and that Plaintiffs consented to and did release information to ERM, Plaintiffs also allege that ERM then requested and obtained consumer reports “without complying with the mandatory requirements, disclosures and authorizations required under the ICRAA.” (Complaint, ¶¶ 41-44.)

The ICRAA provides that its remedy is not exclusive, and thus does not prevent any consumer from maintaining an action for invasion of privacy, among other claims. (Civil Code section 1786.52.) Thus, to the extent Plaintiffs have alleged a violation of ICRAA, they have also alleged, at least facially, a protected privacy interest and reasonable expectation of privacy.

However, the Court agrees with ERM that Plaintiffs have not alleged a serious invasion of privacy, as required for an invasion of privacy tort claim. (Compare Folgelstrom v. Lamps Plus, Inc. (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 986, 992 [relating to obtaining zip code under false pretenses].)  

ERM’s demurrer is thus SUSTAINED as to the Complaint’s second cause of action but with leave to amend. It is possible Plaintiffs may be able to allege how one or more of the violations of ICRAA constituted a “serious” invasion within the meaning of California caselaw.

II.

Third Cause of Action, Declaratory Relief: OVERRULED.

A complaint for declaratory relief must demonstrate: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the rights or obligations of a party. (Brownfield v. Daniel Freeman Marina Hosp. (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 405, 410.) The fundamental basis of declaratory relief is a present and actual controversy between the parties over a proper subject. (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 80; see Code Civ. Proc., §1060.)

The Complaint alleges various controversies, which blend into each other to various degrees: (1) a controversy regarding the legality and effect of Defendants’ housing application, where Plaintiffs demand that all leases must be renewed or re-certified, and that a judicial determination be made to prevent the Defendants’ continued violations of the ICRAA; (2) a controversy regarding how to correct and mitigate the damages to Plaintiffs and other similar persons from Defendants’ illegal actions in violation of the ICRAA statutes, including by injunction running with the land; and (3) a controversy whether the application and re-certification of leases violated the ICRAA, making the application and re-certifications illegal and void. (Complaint, ¶¶ 81-83; see Complaint, ¶ 84.)

In its demurrer, ERM argues that the third cause of action is not sufficiently stated because it requests relief for hypothetical controversies that would require an impermissible advisory opinion. More specifically, ERM argues that the relief sought by Plaintiffs—renewal or re-certification of leases—is hypothetical because housing applications need only be filled out before becoming a tenant, after which Plaintiffs need not file new applications to renew their lease, particularly where ERM does not ask tenants to file new applications for that purpose. ERM also argues that it does not require tenants to renew leases and Plaintiffs have not alleged an intention to renew their lease, making the claim advisory. (Demurrer, pp. 8-9.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the Complaint alleges that Defendants demand that all leases be renewed or re-certified, which is accomplished by completing the same form as the housing application, where that form authorizes Defendants to obtain investigative consumer reports about Plaintiffs without complying with the ICRAA. Plaintiffs argue that they seek a declaration of rights as to the illegality of the renewal and re-certification form. (Demurrer Opp’n, p. 3.)

In reply, ERM argues that Plaintiffs conflate two separate applications: the form for housing application; and the lease forms. ERM argues that the complained-of language only exists in the housing application. ERM also argues that the renewal of lease process does not require a form. (Demurrer Reply, pp. 3-4.)

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiffs.

The Court recognizes that the third cause of action is somewhat confusing. The third cause of action requests certain types of injunctive relief that are not wholly consistent with declaratory relief. For example, a declaration that Plaintiffs leases must be renewed or re-certified or that, in effect, ERM must use different forms in the future. (Complaint, ¶¶ 81.) Such relief is not a declaration of rights, but an order requiring that a party act or refrain from acting in a particular way. ERM challenges such injunctive relief in its motion to strike.

However, the third cause of action is clear enough to convey that it seeks a declaration of rights as to the legality or voidness of the housing application and lease renewal form used by Defendants when managing housing applications or renewal leases. (Complaint, ¶¶ 81, 83.) Such a question is different than whether Defendants violated the ICRAA against the Plaintiffs, which is the subject matter of the Complaint’s first cause of action, and to which ERM has not here demurred. Moreover, ERM’s arguments lie outside of the pleadings. ERM argues that the housing application and lease renewal forms, to the degree that they exist, do not involve the same form. Yet, the Complaint appears to allege that the housing application and lease renewal form are one in the same and contain the same investigative report clause. (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶ 81 [“same forms are always used”].) ERM has not rebutted this representation by requesting judicial notice of the housing application form and any lease renewal forms, to the extent that such renewal forms exist. The proper place for ERM’s arguments is thus an evidence-based motion, not one challenging the pleadings.

ERM’s demurrer is thus OVERRULED as to the Complaint’s third cause of action.

 

Motion to Strike

Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)

For the purposes of a motion to strike pursuant to Sections 435 to 437 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the term “pleading” generally means a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint, (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (a)), and an immaterial allegation or irrelevant matter in a pleading entails (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subds. (b)(1)-(3), (c)).

I.

Order Striking Injunctive Relief under ICRAA Claim: GRANTED, With Leave to Amend.

In its motion, ERM moves to strike injunctive relief requested by the first cause of action because that claim is founded in the ICRAA, which limits relief to damages and attorney’s fees pursuant to Civil Code section 1786.50. (Strike Mot., p. 2.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that they are entitled to seek injunctive relief before harm occurs and that the Complaint alleges ongoing violations of the ICRAA at paragraph 81. Plaintiffs also argue, without citing authority, that because they have requested actual damages (attorney’s fees), they may also seek injunctive relief as a remedy for continuing violations of the ICRAA. (Strike Opp’n, pp. 3-4.)

In reply, ERM argues that Plaintiffs provide no authority to support injunctive relief under their ICRAA claim. (Strike Reply, p. 4.)

The Court finds in favor of ERM.

Civil Code section 1786.50 details the kind of liability that can arise from the use of consumer reporting agency information in violation of the ICRAA and only contemplates actual damages, attorney’s fees and costs, and punitive damages where applicable. No injunctive relief is set forth in the statute. Though Plaintiffs are generally otherwise able to bring a petition or motion for injunctive relief, ICRAA does not appear to be the proper statutory ground for such relief. (See Civ. Code, § 1786.50.)

ERM’s motion is therefore GRANTED as to STRIKING paragraph 77 of the Complaint.

II.

Order Striking Declaratory and Injunctive Relief: GRANTED in Part [Injunctive Relief], With Leave to Amend; DENIED, in Part [Declaratory Relief].

ERM also seeks an order striking paragraphs 1, 5, 56, and 75 and Prayer paragraphs 8 through 10 from the Complaint. ERM argues that this relief is merited because the third cause of action failed on demurrer, making these allegations and prayers irrelevant. (Strike Mot., p. 2.)

The opposition and reply make arguments against and in favor of striking these matters. (Strike Opp’n, p. 4; Strike Reply, p. 4.)

The Court finds in favor of ERM.

As discussed in relation to the third cause of action, a declaration of rights pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1060 is declaratory relief different than coercive relief, such as an order for monetary damages or injunctive relief. (See Demurrer discussion supra at § II.) The injunctive relief sought in the Complaint is not proper as to the first cause of action. (See Strike Mot. discussion supra at § I.) And the second cause of action failed on demurrer. (See Demurrer discussion supra at § I.)

As a result, no claims exist to support the injunctive relief sought in the Complaint. ERM’s motion is thus GRANTED as to STRIKING injunctive relief from paragraphs 1, 5, 56, and 75 and Prayer paragraphs 8 through 10 of the Complaint.

However, the Court adopts the outcome in Section II of the demurrer discussion above to find that any declaration of rights alleged in the Complaint at paragraphs 1, 5, 56, and 75 and Prayer paragraphs 8 through 10 should not be stricken as irrelevant.

III.

Order Striking Punitive Damages: GRANTED, With Leave to Amend.

ERM last argues that punitive damages are improper and must be stricken from the Complaint because the knowing violations of the ICRAA pleaded in the Complaint do not connote oppression, fraud, or malice against Plaintiffs. ERM also argues that the Complaint fails to allege conduct by ERM’s senior management, thus undercutting punitive damages against ERM. (Strike Mot., pp. 3-4.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that they have sufficiently alleged malice based on ERM’s allegedly knowing violation of the ICRAA and based on ERM obtaining highly confidential and private background information relating to Plaintiffs by way of improper documents. (Strike Opp’n, pp. 2-3.)

In reply, ERM argues against Plaintiffs’ opposition points and reiterates its points in the demurrer. (Strike Reply, pp. 2-3.)

The Court finds in favor of ERM.

“When the defendant is a corporation, ‘[a]n award of punitive damages … must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees’” specifically, “the oppression, fraud, or malice perpetrated, authorized, or knowingly ratified by an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation,” where a managing agent “include[s] only those corporate employees who exercise substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decisionmaking so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy.’” (Wilson v. Southern California Edison Co. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 123, 164, citations omitted.)

Here, the Complaint lacks allegations of involvement by ERM’s corporate decisionmakers or only alleges conclusory involvement. (See Complaint, ¶ 36.) Moreover, the Complaint does not sufficiently elaborate on the grounds for finding that ERM acted with malice other than by alleging a knowing violation of the ICRAA. Under the circumstances, the Court finds that malice is lacking in the pleadings.

As a result, the Court the Court GRANTS ERM’s motion to strike as to punitive damages and STRIKES paragraphs 5, 56, and 76 and Prayer paragraph 4 of the Complaint.

 

Conclusion

Defendant Equity Residential Management, L.L.C.’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint is SUSTAINED, in Part, and OVERRULED, in Part, as follows:

(1) SUSTAINED, With Leave to Amend, as to the Complaint’s second cause of action

(2) OVERRULED as to the Complaint’s third cause of action.

Defendant Equity Residential Management, L.L.C.’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint is GRANTED, in Part, and DENIED, in Part, as follows:

(1) GRANTED as to STRIKING injunctive relief allegations and prayers from the first cause of action, With Leave to Amend, as stated above;

(2) GRANTED as to striking other injunctive relief allegations and prayers from the Complaint, With Leave to Amend, as stated above;

(3) DENIED as to striking declaratory relief allegations and prayers from the Complaint, as stated above; and

(4) GRANTED as to striking punitive damages from the Complaint, as stated above.

Plaintiffs are granted leave to file an amended pleading within 10 days of this ruling.
