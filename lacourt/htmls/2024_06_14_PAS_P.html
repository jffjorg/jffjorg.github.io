DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01076    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING THE DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT WITH LEAVE TO AMEND AS TO THE FIRST CAUSE OF ACTION AND OVERRULING THE DEMURRER TO THE SECOND CAUSE OF ACTION AS MOOT

I.        INTRODUCTION

          In this personal injury action, Plaintiff Ruben Trinidad alleges that, on or about November 13, 2022, he sustained a fall on an unleveled, uneven, and sunken roadway located at or near 528 N. Chandler Ave., Monterey Park, CA 91754 (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint, ¶ 6.)

          On May 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendants City of Monterey Park (“City”), County of Los Angeles (“County”), and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Liability for Dangerous Condition of Public Property Pursuant to Gov. Code § 835 et seq.; and (2) Public Employee Liability for the Wrongful Acts or Omissions Pursuant to Gov. Code § 840.2.

          On July 14, 2023, pursuant to Plaintiff’s Request for Dismissal, the Court dismissed the second cause of action without prejudice.

          Also, on July 14, 2023, Defendant City filed a Cross-Complaint against ROES 1 through 10, inclusive. Also, on such date, Defendant City filed an Answer to the Complaint.

On March 21, 2024, Defendant County filed and served the instant demurrer to the first and second causes of action in the Complaint on the grounds that the Complaint contains a misjoinder of Defendant County pursuant to CCP § 430.10(d).

As of June 12, 2024, the demurrer is unopposed. Any opposition to the demurrer was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing pursuant to CCP § 1005(b).

II.      DEMURRER

A. Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. (CCP § 430.10(e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Aubry v. Tri-Defendants Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.) A demurrer may also be brought on the grounds that “[t]here is a defect or misjoinder of parties.” (CCP § 430.10 (d).)

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.)

Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.) 

          Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) 

          B. Meet and Confer Requirement Not Met

          The Court notes that the meet and confer requirement has not been met. Counsel for Defendant County, Tasha T. Salveron, Esq. (“Salveron”), declares that on March 12 and 13, 2024, she attempted to contact Plaintiff’s counsel to meet and confer to attempt to resolve the matters raised in the demurrer. (Salveron Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel failed to respond to the request to meet and confer. (Id.)

The meet and confer requirement has not been met pursuant to CCP § 430.41. The lack of meet and confer, however, does not affect the merits of the Court’s analysis as to the demurrer. The Court, however, reminds the parties of the need to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure.  

C. Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant County’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)  

Initially, the Court notes that one of the documents to which Defendant County requests judicial notice of is the Declaration of Carol Chaparro (“Chaparro”). Defendant County offers such declaration to support the argument that the Subject Property is located entirely within the jurisdiction of Defendant City. (Request for Judicial Notice at p. 2:1-6.)

While the Court can and does take judicial notice of the existence of the statements contained in the declaration of Ms. Chapparo, the truth of the statements in the declaration of Ms. Chaparro are not subject to judicial notice. (Big Valley Band of Pomo Indians v. Superior Court (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1185, 1192.)

          D. Demurrer

          Defendant County contends that the demurrer to the entire Complaint should be sustained because various judicially noticeable documents indicate that it has no jurisdiction over the Subject Property.

          According to the judicially noticeable documents, the Subject Property is located in the jurisdiction of Defendant City. (RJN at ¶¶ 2-4; Exhs. B, C, and D to Salveron Decl.)

          As to the first cause of action, Plaintiff must allege that Defendant County owned the Subject Property. (Gov. Code § 835.)

          As to the second cause of action for public employee liability for wrongful acts or omissions, such cause of action was dismissed on July 14, 2023. (07/14/23 Request for Dismissal.)

          Here, judicially noticeable documents indicate that Defendant County does not own the Subject Property as it is in the jurisdiction of Defendant City.

          Furthermore, given that the demurrer is unopposed, the Court finds that Plaintiff has conceded to the arguments raised therein as “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

          The demurrer is sustained as to the first cause of action with leave to amend. There is a possibility, with successful amendment, that Plaintiff may be able to state a cause of action against Defendant County.

          As to the second cause of action, Defendant County’s demurrer thereto is overruled as moot due to Plaintiff’s dismissal of such cause of action.

III.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Defendant County’s demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the first cause of action and overruled as moot as to the second cause of action.

Plaintiff Ruben Trinidad is given 30 days to amend his Complaint.

Case Number: 23AHCV01742    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This action arises from an automobile accident that occurred on or about April 30, 2020. On July 31, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Complaint against Defendant State Farm Insurance alleging causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and (3) Fraud.

          On August 16, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Motion to Compel Arbitration.

          On December 5, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint against Defendants State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance and State Farm General Insurance Company alleging causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and (3) Fraud.

          On December 14, 2023, pursuant to a Request for Dismissal, Defendant State Farm General Insurance Company was dismissed from this action without prejudice.

          On January 4, 2024, Defendant State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“State Farm”) filed an Answer to the First Amended Complaint.

          On March 29, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Honorable Margaret L. Oldendorf, sitting in this department, denied Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Arbitration. (03/29/24 Minute Order.)

          On April 4, 2024, Defendant State Farm filed and served a Notice of Ruling as to the Court’s March 29, 2024 order. The Notice of Ruling provides that “[t]he Court denied the motion and specifically indicated that in determining the motions ruling did not accept or consider the declarations of Barry Zelner and Lidia Ramirez because they were not properly filed.” (04/04/24 Notice of Ruling at p. 1:21-23.)

          On April 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration and moved the Court for an order reconsidering its ruling of March 29, 2024, where it denied Plaintiffs’ motion to compel arbitration. The motion is made on the grounds “that the Court did not allow the declarations of Barry Zelner and Lidia Ramirez to be considered which clearly establish new facts that plaintiff’s counsel believe should have granted the motion to compel arbitration.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:25-27.)  

          On May 31, 2024, Defendant State Farm filed an opposition to the motion for reconsideration, to which Plaintiffs replied on June 4, 2024.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD     

          Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(a) “requires that a motion for reconsideration be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212.) “A party seeking reconsideration must also provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce the evidence at an earlier time.” (Ibid.) “A trial court’s ruling on a motion for reconsideration is reviewed under the abuse of discretion standard.”  (Ibid., citation omitted.)

California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 1008(a) provides that “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order . . . [may] make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.” “The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) A trial court has discretion with respect to granting a motion for reconsideration. (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212.)

“The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13, citation omitted.) A “party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.) “If the trial court believes reconsideration is warranted, it can amend, modify or revoke its previous order.” (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457, citation omitted.) In the context of motions for reconsideration, there is “a strict requirement of diligence.” (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.)  

III.     ANALYSIS

A. Evidence in Support of Reconsideration

In support of the instant motion, Plaintiffs’ counsel, Barry Zelner (“Zelner”), declares that Plaintiffs’ motion to compel arbitration was specifically based on the fact that Plaintiffs’ counsel believed there was clearly an open extension over the two-year statute of limitations to compel arbitration. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 2.) In response to the opposition, Plaintiff filed reply declarations. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 3.) Unfortunately, Plaintiffs’ counsel’s legal secretary, Lidia Ramirez, was out sick with COVID and was unable to file the declarations. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 4.) Another secretary in Plaintiffs’ counsel’s office, who had never filed legal papers before, filed the paperwork and Mr. Zelner was not in the office when they were filed. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 4.)

Mr. Zelner further states that: during the hearing on the motion, defense counsel argued that Mr. Knapp, the handling attorney for the underlying action, told Mr. Zelner that the two-year statute was going to run before it actually ran, which was a lie. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 5.) The responding declarations that were attached in response to the opposition clearly indicates that it was one hundred percent false and a lie and that there was an open extension in ongoing conversations about the two-year statute of limitations to compel arbitration. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 5.) The facts are there was a letter regarding the open extension with defense counsel and State Farm. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 5.) Mr. Zelner states that it was agreed by counsel Mr. Knapp and State Farm that there was an ongoing investigation of the claims and to continue examinations under oath beyond two years after the statute of limitations. (Zelner Decl., ¶ 5.)

          B. Evidence in Opposition to Reconsideration

          In opposition, counsel for Defendant State Farm, Tod M. Castronovo (“Castronovo”), provides a declaration. Mr. Castronovo relevantly declares that: as a recorded note to his office’s file in this mater, Judge Oldendorf advised that she had in fact reviewed the subject declarations and she was not considering them in her ruling; however, Judge Oldendorf also advised that even if she were to have considered the declarations, this would not have caused her to grant Plaintiffs’ motion. (Castronovo Decl., ¶ 11.) Mr. Castronovo states that Judge Oldendorf advised the parties that she read the declarations. (Castronovo Decl., ¶ 11.)

          C. Appropriateness of Setting Aside the Court’s March 29, 2024 Order

          Initially, the Court notes that on reply Plaintiffs argue, for the first time, that the Court’s March 29, 2024 order should be set aside under CCP § 473(b). CCP § 473(b) allows the Court to “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, or order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (CCP § 473(b).)

          Here, Plaintiffs move for reconsideration under CCP § 1008 seeking to set aside the order denying their motion to compel arbitration based on the grounds of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. The Court finds that neither the declaration of Mr. Zelner submitted in support of the moving nor Defendants’ reply papers make a showing of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Additionally, the act at issue—the improper filing of declarations—was not done by Mr. Zelner or Plaintiffs but instead was done by an unnamed legal secretary.

          As to Plaintiffs’ request for reconsideration, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not made the required showing to warrant reconsideration. The declarations of Mr. Zelner in support of the motion do not state what new or different facts, law, or circumstances are claimed to be shown. Plaintiffs were required to state, by affidavit, “what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown” pursuant to CCP § 1008(a). Additionally, Plaintiffs have not made any showing of diligence as they have failed to state, by affidavit, why the declarations at issue were not filed at an earlier point in time or present a satisfactory explanation for the failure present the declarations at an earlier point in time. Moreover, the purported declarations are not “new” evidence as such declarations were previously presented to the Court but were deemed improperly filed.  

          IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

Case Number: 24NNCV00030    Hearing Date: June 14, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE

I.        INTRODUCTION

          Plaintiffs Lucinda Sky and Leonardo Nishanov (collectively “Plaintiffs”) are parties to three matters with the court: 24NNCV00030, 23PDUD04606, and 24BBCV00353. None of these matters were related at the time of filing this motion.

          On December 28, 2023, Symphony Asset Pool XIII LLC (“Symphony”) filed an unlawful detainer complaint against Lucinda Sky (“Sky”) under case number 23PDUD04606. Judgment was entered against Sky on May 15, 2024.

          On February 13, 2024, Sky filed a complaint against Foothill L.A.P.D., Officer Juan Rico, and Capitan Marco Lozano under case number 24BBCV00353. Sky alleges a police report she had taken was created falsely as it omitted evidence she emailed in, and the police detective disregarding the motives of the apartment building management harming Sky and her son. Sky raised a CA Penal Code section 13510.8(a)(1), recklessly disregarding conspiracy cause of action against Foothill L.A.P.D. Sky raised the following causes of action against Capitan Lozano: (1) abuse of power, (2) pressuring staff to commit perjury, (3) conspiracy, (4) accessory to crime, and (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress. Sky raised the following causes of action against Officer Juan Rico: (1) abuse of power, (2) perjury, (3) conspiracy, (4) concealment, (5) accessory to crime, (6) accessory to second degree murder, and (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

          On May 23, 2024, all defendants in 24BBCV00353 were dismissed without prejudice as the court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte motion to dismiss.

          On March 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Symphony and Otilia Rosenburg under case number 24NNCV00030. Plaintiffs contend numerous events have transpired at their apartment including having documents go missing from their unit, their dog being poisoned, burglary, car theft, to Plaintiffs being poisoned by the building manager, Ms. Rosenburg. Plaintiffs also contend there is a drug trafficking ring in the building.

Plaintiffs asserted the causes of action of: (1) infliction of emotion distress, (2) withholding victim’s Civil Rights, (3) concealment, (4) forgery, (5) conspiracy, (6) racketeering and criminal profiteering, (7) housing discrimination, and (8) injunctive relief against Symphony. Plaintiffs asserted the causes of action of: (1) infliction of emotion distress, (2) poisoning someone’s animal, (3) grand theft auto, (4) withholding victim’s right to their evidence, (5) concealment, (6) property damage, (7) negligence tort of habitability on the premises, (8) poisoning someone’s food or drink, (9) second degree battery, (10) contract forgery, (11) conspiracy, (12) racketeering and criminal profiteering, and (13) housing discrimination.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code Civ. Proc. §1048 provides as follows:      

(a) When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.

(b) The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.

III.     ANALYSIS

          Plaintiffs seek an order consolidating 24NNCV00030, 23PDUD04606, and 24BBCV00353.

The motion is denied. The cases have not yet been deemed related, with each of the matters in different departments, 24NNCV00030 is in Dept. P at the Pasadena Courthouse, 24BBCV00353 is in Dept. A at the Burbank Courthouse, and 23PDUD04606 is in Dept. R at the Pasadena Courthouse.  Pursuant to Local Rule 3.3(g)(1), which requires cases to be pending in the same department before being consolidated, the motion is denied. Further, judgment has been entered in 23PDUD04606 and defendants dismissed in 24BBCV00353. Therefore, there are no matters to consolidate with 24NNCV00030 at this time.        

The motion to consolidate is denied.

In Plaintiffs’ motion, Plaintiff Leonardo Nishanov states that he wishes to be represented by his mother, Lucinda Sky. (Motion, 2:6-14.) The courts DENIES this request as it would be the unauthorized practice of law under Business and Professions Code §§ 6125 & 6126.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiffs Lucinda Sky and Leonardo Nishanov’s motion to consolidate 24NNCV00030, 23PDUD04606, and 24BBCV00353 is DENIED.
