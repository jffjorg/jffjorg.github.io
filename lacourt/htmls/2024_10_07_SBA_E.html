Case Number: 22TRCV01540    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

HL GROUP 2 LIMITED PARTNERSHIP,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV01540

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

TRANG LUYEN, et al.,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                    October 7, 2024

Moving Parties:                 Plaintiff HL Group 2 Limited Partnership

Responding Party:            Defendant Huan Luyen

HEARING:                        Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted

         

The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

          The motion to deem request for admissions admitted is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff HL Group 2 Limited Partnership filed the Complaint on December 20, 2022.

On August 17, 2023, following two sustained demurrers, the operative Second Amended Complaint was filed alleging causes of action for slander of title, fraud, intentional interference with contractual relationships, intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, and notarial negligence or misconduct.

On August 6, 2024, Defendants Tranh Luyen, Truc Luyen, Huan Luyen, Loanco Mortgage Services LLC, and Stephanis Lan Nguyen filed the Answer to the Second Amended Complaint.

On July 22, 2024, Plaintiff served Huan Luyen with a set of requests for admissions numbered 20-49 on Defendant. (Boyko Decl. ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff has received no response or declaration, nor has an opposition been filed to the instant motion.

DISCUSSION

RFA

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including those based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted and for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance before the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

          The Court finds that Plaintiff has proven it is entitled to an order deeming the truth of the matters specified in the request for admission, deemed admitted. Plaintiff served the subject discovery requests on July 22, 2024, and Huan Luyen failed to provide any responses. (Boyko Decl. ¶ 5; Ex. A). Huan Luyen has not opposed this motion. The Court, therefore, orders that the truth of the matters specified in the request for admission, as to Huan Luyen, are deemed admitted. 

 

Sanctions

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) 

Here, Plaintiff requests $1,068.91 in sanctions for costs incurred following Huan Luyen’s failure to file a timely response to Plaintiff’s request for admissions. (Boyko Decl. ¶ 9.) To reach this number, Plaintiff’s counsel states that they spent 1 hour preparing the instant motion and 1 hour attending the hearing at $500 an hour plus an estimated $68.91 in filing fees. (Boyko Decl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff raised no arguments against the imposition of sanctions.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions against Huan Luyen is granted in the amount of $1068.91. Sanctions are payable within 30 days of service of this order.

            The Court considered the moving papers and opposition papers.

RULING

            The motion for leave to amend is granted.

BACKGROUND

On August 8, 2022, Plaintiff Layla Kiffin filed the complaint alleging causes of action for violation of Song-Beverly Act - breach of express warranty, violation of Song-Beverly Act - breach of implied warranty, violation of Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, violation of Consumer Legal Remedies Act, negligent repair, intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, and breach of contract against Defendant Carwell, LLC, dba Land Rover South Bay.

 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint.

 

On September 23, 2024, Defendant filed the Opposition.

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Reply.

 

Trial is set for April 1, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Code of Civil Procedure §473 subdivision (a)(1) states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars and may, upon like terms, allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”   

 

Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and therefore, the courts have held that “there is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296–297; see also Ventura v. ABM Industries, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 268) [“Trial courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial where the adverse party will not be prejudiced.”].) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend, and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 543; see also Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [“[I]t is an abuse of discretion to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.”] 

 

Pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court 3.1324(a), a motion to amend must: (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered; and (2) state what allegations are proposed to be deleted from or added to the previous pleading and where such allegations are located. Cal. Rules of Court 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration that accompanies the motion, stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Here, Plaintiff’s motion is accompanied by a copy of the First Amended Complaint “FAC” and a list of all changes made between the Complaint and the First Amended Complaint. Furthermore, Plaintiff has also provided testimony from Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the need for the amendment. (Mann Decl.) Plaintiff seeks to amend the complaint because discovery has uncovered new facts that would allow Plaintiff to augment and expand Plaintiff's allegations against Defendant. The FAC also contains a new request for punitive damages. Plaintiff states that Defendant will not be prejudiced by the granting of this motion because Plaintiff does not seek to add any additional claims, and Plaintiff sought Defendant's consent to file the Amended complaint within two weeks of the completion of relevant discovery. (Motion For Leave “MFL” p. 7,) Last, the trial is scheduled for April 1, 2025, more than six months from now.

 

            In opposition, Defendant argues that the Notice of Motion is deficient and, therefore, the instant motion must be denied. (Opposition p. 3.) The Notice of Motion and case captions must state the specific relief sought, the reasons for it, and the grounds for issuance of the order in the opening paragraph. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473; Cal. Rules of Court 3.110(a).) The court cannot grant relief that differs from what is stated in the Notice of Motion unless the parties have had the opportunity to address new grounds without objection. (Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal. App. 4th 1119, 1124-5.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to provide any grounds upon which the Court can base an order in Plaintiff’s Notice of Motion.

 

            In reply, Plaintiff argues that while the Notice of Motion does not reference Code Civ. Proc., § 473 subd. (a), the brief attached to the notice does, thus, denying the motion based on these grounds “elevates form over substance” and would go against the premise that leave to amend should be liberally granted. (Baxter v. Riverside Portland Cement Co (1913) 22 Cal. App. 199, 201.)

 

            Code of Civ. Proc. §473 subdivision (a) and California Rules of Court 3.110(a) state that the grounds for the motion must be stated in the Notice of Motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473 subd. (a); Cal. Rules of Court 3.110(a).) Here, the Notice of Motion fails to mention Code of Civil Procedure 473 subd. (a) but it is noted in the Memorandum of Points and Authority. Therefore, given the very liberal interpretation of the Code of Civ. Proc. §473(a) the Court finds the Notice of Motion is not procedurally deficient and should not be denied on such grounds.

 

            Defendant also argues that Plaintiff has failed to show sufficient evidence to justify the exercise of the Court’s discretionary power. Defendant states that while Plaintiff lays out a statement of facts, Plaintiff provides no declarations or citations to any evidence justifying the court granting leave to amend.

 

            In reply, Plaintiff lists the new allegations and supporting facts with citations to depositions and other evidence. (Reply p. 3-4.)

 

            Leave to amend should only be denied “where no showing is made to justify the exercise of the court's discretionary power” (Baxter, 22 Cal. App. 199, 201.) Here, Plaintiff has shown that discovery led to the uncovering of multiple misrepresentations that had been communicated to Plaintiff during the repair process. (Reply p. 3-4.) All of these new allegations are supported with citations to depositions and other evidence. Therefore, Plaintiff has provided legally sufficient evidence to support the Court’s discretionary power to grant leave to amend. (See, Baxter, 22 Cal. App. 199, 201.)

 

            Defendant also argues that they will be prejudiced because Plaintiff seeks to add claims for punitive damages and attorney’s fees. Defendant argues that this change will require the redeposition of parties and further discovery. Furthermore, the addition of punitive damages may drastically change the potential liability of Defendant. Last, Defendant argues that we are nearing the trial date and Plaintiff wishes to start the pleading stage again.

 

            In reply, Plaintiff argues that the only new claim added was for punitive damages, which Plaintiff decided not to plead until she had evidence to support the claim. Furthermore, the trial is not set for another six months, and Plaintiff would be open to allowing Defendant to re-depose Plaintiff if they wish to.

 

            Defendant cites Duchrow v. Forrest (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1359, 1378-1379 to argue that it would be prejudicial to allow a late amendment that drastically changes the liability of a party. However, in Duchrow, the complaint was amended on the fourth day of a five-day trial; here, there are six months until the trial, and Plaintiff is open to allowing additional discovery. (See, Duchrow, 215 Cal.App.4th 1359, 1363.)  Therefore, the Court finds the Defendant would not be prejudiced by allowing Plaintiff to amend the Complaint.

 

            Last, Defendant argues that the Court should not grant leave to amend the Complaint because the proposed amendment for punitive damages would be subject to a Motion to Strike.

 

            California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code § 3294 subd. (a).) “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294 subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code § 3294 subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code § 3294 subd. (c)(3).)  

 

            Here, Plaintiff requests punitive damages for intentional misrepresentation and violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act. In alleging these causes of actions, Plaintiff states that Defendant represented to Plaintiffs that the car had a working engine that could be serviced, the car was in good working condition, and that Defendant kept accurate records of the repair history of the car. (FAC ¶ 86, 98.) Furthermore, Plaintiff states that Defendant intended for customers to rely on their misrepresentations to induce customers to have their vehicles serviced at Defendant’s dealership. (FAC ¶ 100.) Therefore, Plaintiff has pleaded facts to allege defendant has engaged in fraud, and thus, a claim for punitive damages is justified.

           

Therefore, the motion for leave to amend is granted.

 

Defendant also requests that the Court shift all or most of any new discovery costs to Plaintiff relying on Code of Civ. Proc. § 473 subdivision (a)(2). However, Code of Civ. Proc. § 473 subdivision (a)(2) refers to costs that occur from postponement of the trial. Here, there has been no postponement of the trial. Therefore, Defendant’s request is denied.
