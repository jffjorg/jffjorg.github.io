County of Los Angeles – Central District

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

kia america, inc. , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV46989

January 3, 2025

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to bifurcate trial

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Kia America, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:     Plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios    

Motion to Bifurcate Trial

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Kia America, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order bifurcating trial in this action, filed by plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios (“Plaintiffs”), to separate the issues of (1) Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages, and (2) Plaintiffs’ claim for civil penalties.

“The court shall, on application of any defendant, preclude the admission of evidence of that defendant’s profits or financial condition until after the trier of fact returns a verdict for plaintiff awarding actual damages and finds that a defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud in accordance with Section 3294.¿ Evidence of profit and financial condition shall be admissible only as to the defendant or defendants found to be liable to the plaintiff and to be guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud.¿ Evidence of profit and financial condition shall be presented to the same trier of fact that found for the plaintiff and found one or more defendants guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud.”¿¿(Civ. Code, §¿3295, subd. (d).)¿ “While the statute refers only to evidence of the defendant’s financial condition, in practice bifurcation under this section means that all evidence relating to the amount of punitive damages is to be offered in the second phase, while the determination whether plaintiff is entitled to punitive damages (i.e., whether the defendant is guilty of malice, fraud or oppression) is decided in the first phase along with compensatory damages.”¿ (Holdgrafer v. Unocal Corp. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 907, 919.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to other issues, “trial courts have broad discretion to determine the order of proof in the interests of judicial economy.”¿ (Grappo v. Country Financial Corp. (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 496, 504.)¿ Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice . . . may order a separate trial . . . of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues . . . .”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (b).)¿ Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure section 598 provides that “[t]he court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby . . . make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case . . . .”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §¿598.)¿¿¿ 

First, the court finds that Defendant is entitled to an order bifurcating trial as to the issue of Plaintiffs’ claim for punitive damages in the manner required by Civil Code section 3295, and therefore grants that request and makes the orders set forth below.  (Civ. Code, § 3295, subd. (d); Holdgrafer, supra, 160 Cal.App.4th at p. 919.)

Second, the court finds that it is not in furtherance of convenience, the avoidance of prejudice, or judicial economy and efficiency to bifurcate trial to determine whether Plaintiffs are entitled to civil penalties from Defendant and the amount thereof after liability is determined because (1) Defendant has not shown, with adequate argument and analysis, that bifurcating trial in this manner is justified to prevent prejudice or jury confusion, and (2) the court finds that bifurcating trial to separate these additional issues would not promote the efficiency and economy of the court and judicial resources.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 598, 1048, subd. (b).)  The court further notes that Defendant has not shown that it is in furtherance of convenience and judicial efficiency and economy to bifurcate trial to determine issues relating to Plaintiffs’ claim for civil penalties (i.e., whether Defendant willfully failed to comply with the Song-Beverly Act) since the first phase of trial will require Plaintiffs to prove that Defendant is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud, which may be based on the same or similar presentations of evidence and arguments.  The court therefore exercises its discretion to deny Defendant’s request to bifurcate trial to separately determine the issue of Plaintiffs’ claim for civil penalties.  (Ibid.)

ORDER

            The court grants in part defendant Kia America, Inc.’s motion to bifurcate as follows.

            The court orders that trial of this action will be bifurcated as follows: (1) the jury will first decide the issues of whether to impose liability for compensatory damages and, if so, the amount of compensatory damages, whether to impose a civil penalty and the amount of any civil penalty, and whether defendant Kia America, Inc., is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud, and (2) only after making findings in plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios’s favor on the issues of whether to impose liability for compensatory damages, the amount of compensatory damages, and whether defendant Kia America, Inc. is guilty of malice, oppression, or fraud, the jury will then decide the issue of whether to impose punitive damages against defendant Kia America, Inc.

            The court denies all other relief requested.

            The court orders plaintiffs Adela Sanchez and Livia Larios to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOVING PARTY:                 Indira J. Cameron-Banks

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant Downtown Crenshaw Rising

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.  

DISCUSSION

Indira J. Cameron-Banks (“Defendants’ Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel for defendant Downtown Crenshaw Rising (“Defendant”).

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. 2) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’”¿ (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)¿ The court should also consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.”¿ (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

For a motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2), California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-053)).¿¿¿¿¿ 

            First, the court finds that Defendants’ Counsel has not filed “a declaration stating facts showing that” the service address is the current address for Defendant as required because Defendants’ Counsel did not, on the supporting declaration, check the box establishing that counsel confirmed within the past 30 days that the address of service is Defendant’s current address.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1362, subd. (d)(1) [declaration must state facts showing that the service address is the current address; “‘current’ means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved”]; MC-052 as to Downtown Crenshaw, ¶ 3, subd. (b).)  Thus, the declaration is incomplete.  Moreover, although Defendants’ counsel stated, under subdivision (d) (labeled “by other means”), “electronic service to Damien Goodmon at dg@libertyecosystem.org[,]” it is unclear whether Defendants’ Counsel (1) is asserting that the address was confirmed to be current by contacting defendant Damien Goodmon at his email address, or (2) is asserting that the moving papers were also served by electronic service, as stated in the accompanying proof of service.  (MC-052 as to Downtown Crenshaw, ¶ 3, subd. (b)(1)(d); POS-040, ¶¶ 4, 5 [stating service was made on defendant Goodmon as agent for all defendants “by mail and follow-up with email”].) 

Thus, the court finds that Defendants’ Counsel did not submit a declaration stating facts showing that the address of service is Defendant’s current address and therefore did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1362, subd. (d)(1)(A).)  The court therefore denies Defendants’ Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel for Defendant.

Second, to the extent that Defendants’ Counsel’s requests an order relieving them as counsel for defendants Liberty Community Land Trust (“Liberty”), and Damien Goodmon (“Goodmon”), the court denies that request. 

Defendants’ Counsel filed two separate “Declaration[s] in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” forms as to Liberty and Goodmon, each of which identified the hearing date thereon to be January 3, 2025.  (MC-052 as to Liberty, p. 1; MC-052 as to Goodmon, p. 1.)  However, Defendants’ Counsel (1) did not identify Liberty and Goodmon as clients in the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil in connection with the motion to be relieved as counsel for Defendant, and did not file separate motions to be relieved as their counsel, and (2) did not identify Liberty and Goodmon as clients in the proposed Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil.  (MC-051, ¶ 1 [identifying the client to be only Downtown Crenshaw]; MC-052 as to Liberty; MC-052 as to Goodmon; MC-053, ¶ 1 [identifying, on the proposed order, the subject motion to be the motion of Defendants’ Counsel “to be relieved as counsel of record for” Downtown Crenshaw only].)

Thus, to the extent that Defendants’ Counsel moves to be relieved as counsel of record for defendants Liberty and Goodmon as well, the court finds that Defendants’ Counsel did not file all required documents (i.e., the Notice of Motion and Motion and proposed Order on Judicial Council forms MC-051 and MC-053) and therefore did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.

ORDER

The court denies Indira J. Cameron-Banks’s motion to be relieved as counsel for defendant Downtown Crenshaw Rising, without prejudice to Indira J. Cameron-Banks’s filing a new motion to be relieved as counsel that complies with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.

            The court orders Indira J. Cameron-Banks to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

(1)   CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO SECOND AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT

(2)   CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE SECOND AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Cross-defendants McGausa LLC and Joshua Melville                       

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Cross-Complainant Michelle S. Liu

(1)   Demurrer to Second Amended Cross-Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike Second Amended Cross-Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with the demurrer and motion to strike.  

BACKGROUND

Michelle S. Liu (“Cross-Complainant”) filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint in this action on June 12, 2024, against plaintiff and cross-defendant McGausa, LLC (“McGausa”) and cross-defendant Joshua Melville (“Melville”) (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”).  Cross-Complainant alleges four causes of action for (1) breach of lease covenants, (2) breach of covenant restricting transfer, (3) nuisance, and (4) harassment.

Cross-Defendants now move the court for an order (1) sustaining their demurrer to each cause of action alleged against them in the Second Amended Cross-Complaint, and (2) striking from the Second Amended Cross-Complaint various of Cross-Complainant’s prayers for relief.

DEMURRER

The court overrules cross-defendant McGausa’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of covenants of contract because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402 [elements of breach of contract].) 

The court notes, as McGausa argues, that the parties’ lease, which is attached to the cross-complaint, states that the agreed use for the subject premises includes “permitted smoking,” and that Cross-Complainant alleges that McGausa breached the lease by, inter alia, using the premises as a cigar lounge.  (SACC Ex. A, p. 1, ¶ 1.8; SACC ¶ 14.)  But (1) the Second Amended Cross-Complaint does not allege what conduct constitutes “permitted smoking” (SACC Ex. A, ¶ 1.8), and the allegations that McGausa (i) covenanted that it would use the premises for the specified activity purposes only (SACC ¶ 13) and (ii) breached that covenant by using the premises as a cigar lounge (SACC ¶ 14), liberally construed, sufficiently allege that the use of the premises as a cigar lounge does not constitute “permitted smoking,” and (2) even if McGausa had shown that the use of the premises as a cigar lounge could, as a matter of law, be considered a permitted use pursuant to the parties’ lease, Cross-Complainant also bases this cause of action on other alleged breaches by McGausa (e.g., renting the premises out for parties, failing to pay the amount due for increased insurance premiums, and failing to ensure that its customers would park only in certain assigned spaces (SACC ¶¶ 14, 16, 18)), such that McGausa has not shown that this cause of action fails in its entirety.[1]  (Munoz v. Patel (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 761, 780, n. 9 [“a general demurrer does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action and if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the demurrer will be overruled”] [internal quotation marks omitted].)

            The court sustains cross-defendant Melville’s demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of covenants of contract, to the extent that it is alleged against Melville,[2] because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against him since (1) Cross-Complainant does not allege that Melville is a party to the lease that is the subject of this cause of action (SACC ¶ 10 and Ex. A, ¶ 1), and the lease attached to the pleading shows that Melville was not a party thereto, and (2) the allegations in support of this cause of action set forth the alleged breaches of contract on the part of McGausa, not Melville (SACC ¶¶  11-19).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Miles, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 402.)

            The court overrules cross-defendant McGausa’s demurrer to the second cause of action for breach of covenant restricting transfer because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it since Cross-Complainant has alleged that (1) the parties agreed that the leased premises would not be assigned or sublet without Cross-Complainant’s written consent (SACC ¶ 21), and (2) McGausa breached the lease by assigning, transferring, and/or subletting the leased premises without Cross-Complainant’s written consent (SACC ¶ 23).[3]  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Miles, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 402.)

            The court sustains cross-defendant Melville’s demurrer to the to the second cause of action for breach of covenant restricting transfer because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, to the extent it is alleged against Melville, since Cross-Complainant did not allege that Melville is a party to the contract that is the subject of this cause of action (SACC ¶¶ 10, 23), as set forth in connection with the court’s ruling on the first cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Miles, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at p. 402.)

            The court overrules cross-defendant McGausa’s demurrer to the third cause of action for nuisance because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainant has alleged that (1) McGausa’s conduct, which produced and caused noxious odors from excessive smoke, loud music, and the excessive trash around the building, interfered with Cross-Complainant’s use and enjoyment of the land as a property owner (SACC ¶ 26), (2) the invasion of Cross-Complainant’s interest caused her actual damage (SACC ¶ 29), and (3) the interference was unreasonable (SACC ¶¶ 28-30).[4]  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1176 [elements of private nuisance].)

            The court sustains cross-defendant Melville’s demurrer to the third cause of action for nuisance because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Melville, to the extent that it is asserted against him, since Cross-Complainant has not alleged facts establishing that Melville created the subject nuisance, and instead has alleged facts establishing that cross-defendant McGausa is the entity that “changed the permitted use of the Premises from a workspace, office and business incubator, to a cigar lounge and a rental space” which led to the creation of the nuisance (SACC ¶ 26).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Today’s IV, Inc., supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at p. 1176.)

            The court sustains Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action for harassment because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

            Cross-Complainant appears to allege this cause of action pursuant to section 12940 of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (Gov. Code, § 12900 et seq.) (“FEHA”).  (SACC ¶ 32 [alleging that Cross-Complainant was a person providing services under a contract pursuant to section 12940].)  Under that statute, it is an unlawful employment practice “[f]or an employer, labor organization, employment agency, apprenticeship training program or any training program leading to employment, or any other person, because of[,]” inter alia, race, national origin, marital status, sex, gender, or gender identity or expression, “to harass an employee, an applicant, an unpaid intern or volunteer, or a person providing services pursuant to a contract.”  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1).)  

            Cross-Complainant does not allege that Cross-Defendants acted as her employer, labor organization, employment agency, or apprenticeship training program or any training program leading to employment, or that she is an employee, applicant, or unpaid intern or volunteer of Cross-Defendants’.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1).)  While Cross-Complainant has asserted that she is a “person providing services pursuant to a contract” (SACC ¶ 32), the court finds that those allegations are conclusory, unsupported by sufficient factual allegations, and therefore do not show that Cross-Complainant may bring a cause of action for harassment under FEHA.  Specifically, a “person providing services pursuant to a contract” means a person who (1) has the right to control the performance of the contract for services and discretion as to the manner of performance, (2) is customarily engaged in an independently established business, and (3) has control over the time and place the work is performed, supplies the tools and instruments in the work, and performs work that requires a particular skill not ordinarily used in the course of the employer’s work.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(5).)  Cross-Complainant did not allege specific facts establishing that she was providing services to Cross-Defendants—instead acting as a landlord to McGausa (SACC ¶ 6 and Ex. A)—or that she had control over the time and place such services were performed, and that the work requires a particular skill not ordinarily used in the course of the employer’s work.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subds. (j)(5)(A), (j)(5)(C).)  Moreover, Cross-Complainant did not present adequate authority and analysis to show that a landlord may sue a tenant for harassment under section 12940 of FEHA, which sets forth unlawful employment practices.

            The court notes that McGausa has also argued that Cross-Complainant’s requests for damages and other relief are defective.  However, those arguments are not properly raised on demurrer.  (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1547, 1562 [“a demurrer tests the sufficiency of the factual allegations of the complaint rather than the relief suggested in the prayer of the complaint”].)

The burden is on the plaintiff or cross-complainant “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff or cross-complainant “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  The court finds that Cross-Complainant has not met her burden to articulate how she can amend (1) her cross-complaint to render it sufficient against cross-defendant Melville, and (2) her fourth cause of action to render it sufficient against McGausa. 

The court therefore sustains (1) Melville’s demurrer to the first through fourth causes of action without leave to amend, and (2) McGausa’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action without leave to amend.

MOTION TO STRIKE

Cross-Defendants move the court for an order striking Cross-Complainant’s (1) prayer for termination of the lease (Prayer, ¶ 3), (2) prayer for diminution, impairment, or depreciation of the value and/or rental value of the premises (Prayer, ¶ 4), (3) prayer for discomfort, annoyance, mental and physical distress (Prayer, ¶ 5), (4) prayer for medical expenses (Prayer, ¶ 6), (5) prayer for statutory fines (Prayer, ¶ 7), and (6) prayer for punitive damages (Prayer, ¶ 8).

First, the court denies as moot cross-defendant Melville’s motion to strike because the court has sustained Melville’s demurrer to the Second Amended Cross-Complaint without leave to amend.

Second, the court finds that McGausa has not shown that Cross-Complainant’s prayer for termination of the lease is improper on the ground that Cross-Complainant did not file an unlawful detainer action since (1) Cross-Complainant does not pray for possession of the premises using the unlawful detainer statutory scheme, and (2) McGausa did not present adequate authority, argument, and analysis establishing that Cross-Complainant may not seek to terminate the parties’ lease.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; SACC, Prayer, ¶ 3.)  The court therefore denies McGausa’s motion to strike that request.

Third, the court denies cross-defendant McGausa’s motion to strike the prayers for diminution, impairment, or depreciation of the value and/or rental value of the premises, discomfort, annoyance, mental and physical distress damages, medical expenses, and statutory fines because McGausa did not meet its burden to present argument and authority establishing that those requests for relief are irrelevant, improper, or not made in conformity with the laws of this state.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1683 [uses of motions to strike should be cautious and sparing, and should not be used as a procedural line-item veto for the civil defendant].)

Fourth, the court denies cross-defendant McGausa’s motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages because punitive damages may be recovered in connection with a cause of action for nuisance.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Hassoldt v. Patrick Media Group, Inc. (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 153, 168, n. 13, abrogated on other grounds as set forth in People v. Rogers (2013) 57 Cal.4th 296, 330; Hutcherson v. Alexander (1968) 264 Cal.App.2d 126, 136 [“when the facts warrant, exemplary or punitive damages may be recovered in a nuisance case”]; Mot., p. 9:19-20 [arguing that punitive damages cannot be sought because this case involves a breach of lease].)  The court notes that McGausa did not argue that Cross-Complainant’s prayer for punitive damages should be stricken for any other reason.

ORDER

            The court overrules in part and sustains in part plaintiff and cross-defendant McGausa, LLC and cross-defendant Joshua Melville’s demurrer as follows.

The court sustains cross-defendant Joshua Melville’s demurrer to cross-complainant Michelle S. Liu’s Second Amended Cross-Complaint without leave to amend.

            The court sustains plaintiff and cross-defendant McGausa, LLC’s demurrer to cross-complainant Michelle S. Liu’s fourth cause of action for harassment without leave to amend.

            The court denies as moot cross-defendant Joshua Melville’s motion to strike.

            The court denies plaintiff and cross-defendant McGausa, LLC’s motion to strike.

            The court orders cross-defendant Joshua Melville to prepare, serve, and lodge a proposed judgment of dismissal from cross-complainant Michelle S. Liu’s Second Amended Cross-Complaint within 10 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders plaintiff and cross-defendant McGausa, LLC to file an answer to cross-complainant Michelle S. Liu’s Second Amended Cross-Complaint within 10 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders cross-defendant Joshua Melville to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
