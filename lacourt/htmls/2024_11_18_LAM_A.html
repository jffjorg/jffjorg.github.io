DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV18241    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: A

23STCV18241 Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio v. Khowsrow Benji et al.

Monday, November 18, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      The first amended complaint alleges 21 causes of action arising from alleged harassment, discrimination, retaliation and other claims in violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”). Plaintiff, who was employed as a housekeeper, also alleges assault and battery and wage and hour claims in violation of the Labor Code.

      Khosrow Benji (“Cross-Complainant” or “Benji”), initially filed a cross-complaint alleging claims for negligence, assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress and physical abuse. On July 11, 2024, the Hon. Anne Richardson, sustained demurrer with leave to amend the claims for intentional infliction of emotional distress, battery and for physical elder abuse. (M.O. 7/11/24, p. 4-6.).

      On July 29, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed a first amended cross-complaint (“FAXC”) against Plaintiff (“Cross-Defendant”) alleging three causes of action limited to negligence, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and physical elder abuse arising from injuries suffered by Cross-Complainant, who slipped on the tile patio at his residence after Cross-Defendant washed it.

 

II.     ARGUMENTS

      Cross-Defendant demurs to the emotional distress and elder abuse claims. The FAXC was filed five days late. The Hon. Anne Richardson sustained demurrer as noted on July 11, 2024, granting 14 days leave to amend the claims at issue here.

      Cross-Defendant contends that Benji did not allege extreme and outrageous conduct to support the claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. The alleged facts do not support a conclusion that Cross-Defendant acted “outrageously.” The court should not grant further leave to amend.  As to the elder abuse claim, Cross-Defendant did not allege facts to support the “physical abuse” allegation.

      In opposition, Benji argues that the claims are adequately alleged. The elder abuse claim is alleged with required specificity.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing the complaint’s sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations as well as facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded facts. The court may also consider matters properly subject to judicial notice. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

      The court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) Plaintiff is required to allege facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126.)

      Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

      A demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

      A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.) However, unlike federal courts, California state courts are not a notice pleading jurisdiction, and notice alone is not a sufficient basis for any pleading. California is a fact pleading jurisdiction. Merely putting an opposing party on notice is not sufficient. (Bach v. County of Butte (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 554, 561; see Diodes, Inc. v. Franzen (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 244, 250.)

 

IV.   DISCUSSION

A.     Demurrer is sustained as to the second cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress

      To prevail on a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, plaintiff must allege facts showing (1) extreme and outrageous conduct with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress, (2) that plaintiff suffered severe or extreme emotional distress, and (3) actual and proximate causation. (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.)

      Behavior is considered sufficiently “outrageous” if defendant “(1) abuses a relation or position which gives him power to damages the plaintiff’s interest; (2) knows the plaintiff is susceptible to injuries through mental distress; or (3) acts intentionally or unreasonably with the recognition that the acts are likely to result in illness through mental distress.” (Agarwal v. Johnson (1979) 25 Cal.3d 932, 946.) “Outrageous conduct” may be considered “irritating, insulting or even distressing but it is not actionable and must simply be endured without resort to legal redress.” (Yurick v. Superior Court (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 1116, 1129.)

      Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendant washed the stone tile patio despite being requested not to; washed the area with soap and water, making the area slippery and a fall hazard; failed to notify Cross-Complainant that she had washed the patio, and that the area was wet and slippery. (FAXC, ¶ 26.) These allegations do not reach the level of being so extreme as to "exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community." (Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197, 210 ["The tort calls for intentional, or at least reckless conduct—conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result."].)  Accordingly, demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

B.     Demurrer to the elder abuse claim based on physical abuse is SUSTAINED.

      A defendant is liable for physical abuse of an elder person where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of such abuse. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657.) “Physical abuse” is specifically defined by statute to mean assault, battery, assault with a deadly weapon, unreasonable physical restraint, prolonged or continual deprivation of food or water, sexual assault, or use of a physical or chemical restraint or psychotropic medication. (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.63.)

      Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendant committed physical abuse “by an unlawful attempt, coupled with a present ability, to commit a violent injury on Cross-Complainant, and/or a willful and unlawful use of force or violence” by washing the floor although asked not to, using water and liquid dish soap on the stone tile floor, making the area slippery and a fall hazard, and not warning that the area was wet. (FAXC ¶ 33.)

      These allegations do not fit within the definition of “physical abuse” as specifically described. Therefore, demurrer is SUSTAINED.

V.     CONCLUSION

      Judge Richardson previously evaluated these claims, found them defective, and gave Cross-Complainant leave to amend. The claims remain defective. Accordingly, demurrer to the second and third causes of action alleged in the first amended cross-complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

Case Number: 24STCV25138    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: A

24STCV25138 Temple West Plaza v. Augusto Corpus Valino, et al.

Monday, November 18, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      This is an action for unlawful detainer arising from Defendant’s alleged failure to pay rent pursuant to a commercial lease agreement. Plaintiff moves for summary judgment of all claims for possession of the property, past due rent, and damages.      

      Defendant, Augusto C. Valino, dba Norma’s Income Tax & Bookkeeping Services & Philca Enterprises and Service Bureau (“Defendant”), filed an answer on October 11, 2024. Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default on October 28, 2024, against all unnamed occupants, which the clerk rejected. Plaintiff timely served the motion on Defendant, who did not file an opposition.

II.     ARGUMENTS

      In the context of an unlawful detainer action, a motion for summary judgment may be made at any time after the answer is filed upon five days’ notice. The motion shall be granted or denied on the same basis as a motion under Code of Civil Procedure, section 437c. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1170.7.) An order granting the motion is appropriate if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c).) Plaintiff’s burden is to establish that there is no defense to a cause of action if Plaintiff can prove each element of the cause of action entitling Plaintiff to judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).) If that threshold burden is established, the burden shifts to Defendant to demonstrate with admissible evidence that a triable issue of one or more material facts exist. (Id.)

      A tenant of real property is guilty of unlawful detainer where the tenant continues in possession after failing to pay rent pursuant to the lease agreement, and after the expiration of the three-day notice to pay rent or quit. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1161 subd(2). ) To prevail on the motion, Plaintiff must demonstrate strict compliance with the statutory notice requirements including providing the tenant with three days' written notice to pay rent or quit the premises and proper service of the notice. (Culver Center Partners East #1, L.P. v. Baja Fresh Westlake Village, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 744, 749–750.)

III.    DISCUSSION

      Plaintiff served Defendant with a three-day notice to pay rent or quit on September 20, 2024, requiring Defendant to pay $33,384.00 in back rent or to surrender the premises within three business days. (Mot. Exhs, D and E.) The notice indicated that if Defendant failed to pay back rent, Plaintiff would elect forfeiture of the lease, which will terminate. (Ex. D.)

      The notice provided that Plaintiff would accept any partial payments made after service of the notice, but Plaintiff may file legal action to terminate the lease, recover possession, and for damages, attorney’s fees, and costs. (Id.)

      Plaintiff acknowledges acceptance of a partial payment by Defendant of $5,600 before the three-day notice expired but commenced legal action for the difference as permitted by statute and as alleged in the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1161.1 subd. (b) ["If the landlord accepts a partial payment of rent, including any payment pursuant to subdivision (a), after serving notice pursuant to Section 1161, the landlord, without any further notice to the tenant, may commence and pursue an action under this chapter to recover the difference between the amount demanded in that notice and the payment actually received, and this shall be specified in the complaint."]

      Plaintiff submits a copy of the signed leased agreement and amendments thereto. (Ex. A.)  Defendant defaulted on his obligations to pay rent since June 21, 2023. (Ex. B.) The complaint alleges holdover damages accruing at $91.46 for each day Defendant remains in possession from October 1, 2024, until the date of judgment. (Complaint, 4:3-6.) Defendant remains in possession. (Beth Bamba decl. ¶ 9.) 

      The undisputed evidence supports damages as calculated below. The proposed order conforms with this calculation.  

Back rent per 3-day notice

$33,384.00

Partial payment

-5,600.00

Back rent due

$27,784.00

Holdover damages  ($91.46 x 49 days)

4,481.54

Total Damages

$32,265.54

 

IV.   CONCLUSION

      Plaintiff has met its burden of establishing that the undisputed facts entitle Plaintiff to summary judgment. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s for summary judgment is GRANTED.
