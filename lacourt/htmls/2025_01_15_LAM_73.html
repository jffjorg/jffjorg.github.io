DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV17765    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: 73

01/15/2025

Dept. 73

Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

LATOYA S. REDD V. ANNA MEZHERITSKY, ET AL. (23STCV17765)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: LaToya S. Redd, Esq. in Pro Per

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party: Anna Mezheritsky in Pro Per

 

MOTION TO COMPEL PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS FROM NON-PARTY SAFECO INSURANCE COMPANY OF AMERICA AND REQUEST FOR ORDER AWARDING MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST SAFECO INSURANCE COMPANY OF AMERICA IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,574.92 (filed 11/13/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Motion to Compel Production of Documents from Non-Party Safeco Insurance Company of America and Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against Safeco Insurance Company of America in the Amount of $324.92 is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 28, 2023, plaintiff LaToya Reed (Plaintiff) filed her initial complaint against defendant Anna Mezheritsky (Defendant) and Does 1 through 10, inclusive. (Compl. p. 1.) On January 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed her first amended complaint, alleging the following: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Obligation to Pay for Legal Services Rendered and Costs Under Written Contract; (3) Common Count: Quantum Meruit: Costs and Services Rendered; and (4) Declaratory Relief. (First Amend. Compl. p. 1.)

 

The causes of action arise after Defendant hired Plaintiff to represent her in the case of Anna Mezheritsky v. Dorothy Kovich Klien (Underlying Case). (First Amend. Compl. ¶ 8.) On September 23, 2019, the attorney-client relationship ended, and Defendant owed Plaintiff a fee for her legal services and other incurred costs. (First Amend. Compl. ¶ 8.) Defendant claims Plaintiff owes her an outstanding balance of $35,489.51 plus interest. (First Amend. Compl. ¶ 9.) On September 25, 2019, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Attorney Lien on the Underlying Case and served it on Defendant’s new counsel. (First Amend. Compl. ¶ 10.)

 

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed and was granted an entry for default. (Entry of Default p. 1.)

 

On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Does 1 through 10 only. (Dismissal p. 1.)

 

On March 15, 2024, the Court entered the default judgment against Defendant. (Default Judgment p. 1.)

 

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff served a Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records on Safeco Insurance Company of America (Safeco). (Decl. Redd Exh. A.) Safeco was to produce the documents on September 23, 2024. (Decl. Redd Exh. A.) Safeco did not serve an objection prior to the date. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.) Safeco did not produce the documents on the prescribed date and location. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.)

 

From September 24, 2024 to October 22, 2024, a deposition officer attempted to obtain the documents requested in the subpoena. (Decl. Redd ¶ 5, Exh. B.) The deposition officer eventually reached Safeco on October 11, 2024. (Decl. Redd Exh. B.) The Subpoena Response Team stated that “Defense Counsel in this case has filed an interpleader to resolve this matter per Court Order, [and] therefore we cannot respond to this Subpoena.” (Decl. Redd Exh. B.)

 

On November 8, 2024, Plaintiff states she told Safeco’s counsel that she would be filing a motion to compel should Safeco not produce the documents requested by November 12, 2024 at noon. (Decl. Redd ¶ 4.)

 

As time of filing, November 13, 2024, Safeco has not produced the documents requested under the subpoena. (Decl. Redd ¶ 5.)

 

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion to Compel Production of Documents from Non-Party Safeco Insurance Company of America and Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against Safeco Insurance Company of America in the Amount of $1,574.92, arguing:

·                The information requested in the subpoena will directly assist Plaintiff with discovering the existence and monies that will satisfy the judgment in the case. (Motion p. 4.)

·                Good cause exists for production of all documents because “the documents are relevant and material to the material to the existence and location of movies to satisfy the judgment” and “the different categories listed in the subpoena will aid Plaintiff … in satisfying the judgment.” (Motion p. 5.)

·                Plaintiff and the deposition report met and conferred with Safeco’s counsel and agents regarding the subpoena. (Motion p. 6.)

·                $74.92 should be awarded to Plaintiff against Safeco for its failure to comply with the subpoena. Plaintiffs incurred costs in connection with preparing and filing the motion. Plaintiff incurred $60 for filing this motion and a $14.92 E-Filing Fee, resulting in $74.92. (Motion p. 6; Decl. Redd ¶ 7.)

·                Under California Code of Civil Procedure[1] section 177.5, the Court should impose sanctions in the amount of $1,500 against Safeco for not complying with a court order, the subpoena. (Motion p. 7-8.)

There is no opposition or reply.

 

 

 

 

 

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Deposition Subpoena

 

“[D]iscovery from a nonparty may be obtained only by ‘deposition subpoena’ (§ 2020.010, subd. (b)).” (Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 130 (Unzipped), italics removed.)¿¿ “A business records subpoena directs the nonparty’s custodian of records (or other qualified person) to deliver the requested documents (in person, by messenger, or by mail) to the ‘deposition officer’ specified in the subpoena.¿ (§ 2020.430, subd. (a).)”¿ (Id. at p. 131.)¿¿¿ 

 

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”¿ (§ 2025.480, subd.¿ (a).)¿ “This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ (§ 2025.480, subd. (b).)

 

“In Unzipped, the court held that the record of the deposition was complete, and the 60-day clock began to run, on the deposition subpoena's date of production when the nonparty deponents asserted objections and declined to produce responsive documents.” (In re Marriage of Moore, 102 Cal.App.5th 1275, 1287, citing Unzipped, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at pp. 128, 131–136.)

  

Section 1987.1, subdivision (a), provides that if a subpoena requires the production of documents, the Court may, upon motion reasonably made by a party, make an order “directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare.” (§ 1987.1, subd. (a).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel an answer or production, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (§ 2025.480, subd. (j).)

 

Furthermore, under section 2023.030, subdivision (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.  If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (§ 2023.030, subd. (a).)

 

Section 2023.010 defines misuses of the discovery process to include “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery[,]” “[m]aking an evasive response to discovery[,]” and “[f]ailing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery, if the section governing a particular discovery motion requires to filing of a declaration stating facts showing that an attempt at informal resolution has been made.” (§ 2023.010, subds. (d), (f), (i).)

 

“A judicial officer shall have the power to impose reasonable money sanctions, not to exceed fifteen hundred dollars ($1,500), notwithstanding any other provision of law, payable to the court, for any violation of a lawful court order by a person, done without good cause or substantial justification. This power shall not apply to advocacy of counsel before the court. For the purposes of this section, the term ‘person’ includes a witness, a party, a party’s attorney, or both.” (§ 177.5.)

 

A subpoena is considered a court order under section 1985, which defines a subpoena as “a writ or order directed to a person and requiring the person's attendance at a particular time and place to testify as a witness.” (§ 1985.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

1.                  Safeco Did Not Respond to the Subpoena.

 

Under section 2025.480, subdivision¿(a), the Court finds that Safeco did not produce any document specified within the deposition subpoena, because Safeco failed to produce any documents at the location and date specified on the subpoena. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.) Safeco also failed to produce any documents after Plaintiff had granted them an extension. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.) The Court will grant the motion to compel if the motion was filed timely and Plaintiff demonstrates a good faith attempt to meet and confer as required under section 2025.480, subdivision (b). These requirements are met.

 

2.                  Motion Was Filed Timely.

 

Because this motion was made before the 60 days deadline, the motion was timely filed.

 

Here, Plaintiff filed the motion on November 13, 2024, and served the motion on Safeco on November 19, 2024. (Motion p. 1; Decl. Redd Exh. A.) As discussed, the deadline runs when the nonparty deponents assert objections and declines to produce responsive documents. (In re Marriage of Moore, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at 1287, citing Unzipped, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at pp. 128, 131–136.) Safeco declined to produce the documents on September 23, 2024 by not appearing at the scheduled location. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.) Accordingly, Plaintiff would have to file and serve the motion by November 22, 2024 per the 60-day deadline. As Plaintiff served the motion on November 19, 2024, Plaintiff’s motion was made before the 60-day deadline.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff timely filed this motion.

 

3.                  Meet and Confer Requirement Met.

 

Here, Plaintiff has presented sufficient facts to demonstrate a good faith attempt to meet and confer to solve the issues informally.

 

From September 24, 2024 to October 22, 2024, a deposition officer attempted to obtain the documents requested in the subpoena after Safeco failed to produce documents on the specified date. (Decl. Redd ¶ 5, Exh. B.) The deposition officer called three times, September 24, October 1, and October 7, 2024, to speak with someone from Safeco. (Decl. Redd Exh. B.) The deposition officer emailed Safeco about filing a motion to compel due to Safeco’s incompliance. (Decl. Redd Exh. B.) On November 8, 2024, having not received the requested documents, Plaintiff spoke with Safeco’s counsel about filing a motion to compel should Safeco not produce the documents requested by November 12, 2024 at noon. (Decl. Redd ¶ 4.) Again, Safeco did not provide the requested documents within the extension of time provided to avoid filing a motion. (Decl. Redd ¶ 3.)

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff satisfies the meet and confer requirement.

 

As all requirements are met, the Court GRANTS the Motion to Compel Production of Documents from Non-Party Safeco Insurance Company of America.

 

4.                  Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process under sections 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d). If a motion to compel is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction is unjust.

 

Here, the facts described above, as well as Safeco’s failure to oppose Plaintiff’s motion, justify the imposition of monetary sanctions against Safeco for misuse of the discovery process. Plaintiff asks for $74.92 for the cost of filing this motion and the associated E-Filing Fee. (Motion p. 6; Decl. Redd ¶ 7.) This amount is granted.

 

As discussed, the Court may also impose a sanction for violating a lawful court order, including a subpoena, by a person, done without good cause or substantial justification. (§§ 177.5; 1985.) Here, Safeco did not comply with the order or provide cause or substantial justification as to why they could not comply. Safeco did not serve objections to the subpoena. Safeco simply said, “Defense Counsel in this case has filed an interpleader to resolve this matter per Court Order, [and] therefore we cannot respond to this Subpoena.” (Decl. Redd Exh. B.) Safeco’s lack of communication with Plaintiff influenced the filing of this motion. Plaintiff asks for sanctions be imposed against Safeco, a witness in this action, in the amount of $1,500. However, the Court finds this amount to be excessive for their first violation. The Court finds $250 reasonable.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against Safeco Insurance Company of America in the Amount of $324.92.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS the Motion to Compel Production of Documents from Non-Party Safeco Insurance Company of America. Safeco is hereby ordered to provide verified responses, without objections, to Plaintiff’s Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s Request for Order Awarding Monetary Sanctions Against Safeco Insurance Company of America in the Amount of $324.92. $74.92 in monetary sanctions is to be paid to Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. $250 in monetary sanctions is to be paid to the Court within 30 days of the date of this order.

Claire Donahue v. 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP, et al. (24STCV02046)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Claire Donahue is represented by Armand J. Jaafari

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP, is represented by David Stuart Canter

 

Motion to compel plaintiff’s further responses to form interrogatories, set one and request for monetary sanctions (filed 08/06/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to provide further verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff Claire Donahue (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendants 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP; LA Urban Living LLC; Jeclim LLC; Jeremy Miller; Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

The Complaint alleges the following: Plaintiff was an occupant of real property located at 427 S Mariposa, Unit 511, Los Angeles, CA (the “Property”) as a lessee/tenant under a written lease with Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that on April 6, 2020, Plaintiff reported an active leak and water damage in the bedroom ceiling to management, who acknowledged the issue but failed to conduct repairs despite multiple follow-ups. On January 25, 2022, Plaintiff discovered additional mold growth in the bedroom after heavy rains. Plaintiff informed the building manager and requested repairs. During Plaintiff’s tenancy, Defendants failed to maintain the Property in a safe and habitable condition. Plaintiff was eventually forced to vacate the Property due to health issues while living in the Property. Defendants constructively evicted Plaintiff from the Property—having refused to make the required repairs to the Property and ensure the Property be made safe and habitable, and after Plaintiff told Defendants that Plaintiff suspected that the fungal growth at the Property was making Plaintiff sick.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1. Breach of the implied warranty of habitability;

2. Nuisance;

3. Breach of contract;

4. Negligence;

5. Negligent misrepresentation;

6. Constructive eviction;

7. Premises liability;

8. Fraud; and

9. Intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On August 6, 2024, Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP (“Defendant” or “Mariposa”) filed a motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to form interrogatories, set one, and a request for monetary sanctions, arguing:

·                Defendant properly electronically served Plaintiff with its Form Interrogatories, Set One, on March 22, 2024.

·                On April 25, 2024, Defendant sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel to confer about Plaintiff’s failure to respond.

·                On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel claimed that Defendant failed to properly serve their written discovery requests.

·                On April 30, 2024, based on Plaintiff’s contention that service of the requests was improper, Defendant granted Plaintiff an extension of time to provide Defendant with her responses based on a service date of April 30, 2024, making Plaintiff’s responses due on or before June 4, 2024.

·                On June 7, 2024, after still not receiving Plaintiff’s responses, Defendant granted Plaintiff an extension of time to provide Defendant with her responses until June 14, 2024.

·                On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff electronically served Plaintiff’s Responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories. However, Plaintiff failed to tailor her responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories. Instead, Plaintiff provided one responsive document with evasive, non-responsive, and/or incomplete responses, and in some cases, Plaintiff failed to respond to an interrogatory.

·                On July 29, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel a formal meet and confer letter identifying the deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses. Defendant requested code-compliant responses by Friday, August 2, 2024.

·                On Monday, August 5, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant wrote to Plaintiff’s counsel to arrange an informal discovery conference and an extension of time to file any required motions to compel further responses.

·                On Tuesday, August 6, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel further responses.

No opposition has been filed. On January 8, 2025, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery…by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.” (CCP § 2030.010(a).) “An interrogatory may relate to whether another party is making a certain contention, or to the facts, witnesses, and writings on which a contention is based. An interrogatory is not objectionable because an answer to it involves an opinion or contention that relates to fact or the application of law to fact, or would be based on information obtained or legal theories developed in anticipation of litigation or in preparation for trial.” (CCP § 2030.010(b).)

 

The propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response to the interrogatories if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) an answer is particularly evasive or incomplete; (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate; and (3) an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (CCP § 2030.300(a)(1)-(3).)  The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2030.300(b)(1).)

 

The Code of Civil Procedure requires the following for answers to interrogatories: “(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits. (b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible. (c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” (CCP § 2030.220.)

 

When responding to interrogatories, the Discovery Act requires a party to make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information before responding to the interrogatories. (Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504.) A party cannot plead ignorance to information which can be obtained from sources under his control. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

In this case, Defendant has demonstrated that Plaintiff’s responses to the interrogatories were deficient and were evasive and incomplete. (CCP § 2030.300(a)(1).)

 

In support of its motion, Defendant presented the Declaration of Christine R. Ghattas (“Ghattas Decl.”), Defendant’s counsel of record, which states the following facts:

 

On March 22, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff electronically with Form Interrogatories. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. A.) Plaintiff’s responses were due on April 24, 2024, 30 days following service, plus an additional two court days for electronic service. (CCP §§ 2030.260(a); 1013 (a).) However, Plaintiff failed to provide responses by this date. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 3.) After communications between counsel for Defendant and Plaintiff where Defendant’s counsel granted Plaintiff multiple extensions to her deadline to provide responses, on June 7, 2024, Defendant ultimately granted Plaintiff an extension to provide her responses without objections by June 14, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 4-9; Ex. B.)

 

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff electronically served her responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 10; Ex. C.) However, such responses were deficient for multiple reasons, including the following:

             

1.      Plaintiff failed to answer or even reference Form Interrogatories Nos. 14.2, 17.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, and 50.6.

2.      Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory No. 6.5 is incomplete because Plaintiff was asked to provide information about the medications she was taking, but all she did was say she was taking medications that could be found in the medical records that she produced, but she did not produce any medical records.

3.      Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 6.7, 7.1, and 12.6 are incomplete and evasive because Plaintiff failed to completely answer the interrogatories.

4.      Finally, Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory No. 8.3 is incomplete and non-responsive because it conflicts with another of Plaintiff’s responses.

 

On July 29, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a formal meet and confer letter identifying Defendant’s claimed deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses and requesting that Plaintiff provide code-compliant responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories by Friday, August 2, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. D.) On August 5, 2024, having not received any communications from Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant’s counsel sent an additional meet and confer correspondence requesting an informal discovery conference and an extension of time to file any motions to compel further responses. (Id., ¶ 12, Ex. E.) Again, Plaintiff’s counsel did not provide any response. (Id., ¶ 12.)

 

This evidence is sufficient to justify Plaintiff’s further response, or in some cases initial response, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories. Further, Plaintiff’s lack of opposition waives any potential objections Plaintiff may have had to any of Defendant’s Form Interrogatories.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to form interrogatories, set one.

 

C.      Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

In connection with its motion, Defendant also requests the imposition of monetary sanctions on Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel for their misuse of the discovery process. Defendant’s request totals $2,140.00, representing 4.5 hours preparing this motion and related documents, 1.0 hours anticipated to prepare a reply, and 2.5 hours anticipated to prepare for and attend the hearing on this motion charged at a $260 per hour fee, as well as a $60.00 filing fee for this motion. (Ghattas Decl., ¶¶ 14-15.)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (CCP § 2030.290(c).)

 

“To the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing may impose … sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process…” (CCP § 2023.030.) “Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to . . . (e) Making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery . . . (f) Making an evasive response to discovery . . . (h) Making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or to limit discovery….” (CCP § 2023.010.)

 

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that the one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (CCP § 2023.030(a).)

 

Here, the facts described above, as well as Plaintiff’s failure to oppose Defendant’s motion, justify the imposition of monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel for misuse of the discovery process. However, since the motion is unopposed, the Court will not award Defendant’s anticipated fees for preparing a Reply. Further, the Court finds that 2.5 anticipated hours to prepare for and attend the hearing on an unopposed motion is unreasonable, and that 4.5 hours to prepare a relatively simple discovery motion is not reasonable given counsel’s level of expertise. The Court awards Defendant’s attorney’s fees for 5.0 hours of attorney work and for the filing fee. Thus, monetary sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $1,360.00.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiff is hereby ordered to provide further verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendant 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the reduced amount of $1,360.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendant’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.

Case Number: 24STCV20566    Hearing Date: January 15, 2025    Dept: 73

Dimas M Figueroa, An Individual, et al. vs. American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (24STCV20566)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing parties: Eleazar D. Kim and Matt Xie (Downtown L.A. Law Group)

Counsel for Defendants/demurring parties: Elizabeth V. McNulty, Christine D. Reynolds, and Samantha M. Geraghty (Fraser Watson & Croutch, LLP)

 

demurrer (filed by Defendant American Honda Motor Co. on October 28, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court sustains the Demurrer to the third cause of action, with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Background

 

On February 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Dimas and Martha Figueroa purchased a 2021 CR-V Honda distributed by Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Complaint ¶ 5.) Plaintiff alleges that during the warranty period, the vehicle developed defects in the body system, powertrain system, safety system, electrical system, braking system, and noise system. (Complaint ¶ 11.) Plaintiffs further allege that Defendant was unable to repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts. (Complaint ¶ 12.) On August 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for violation of subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, violation of subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, breach of express written warranty, and breach of the implied warranty of merchantability. On October 28, 2024, Defendant filed the Demurrer. On January 2, 2025, Plaintiffs filed the Opposition. On January 8, 2025, Defendant filed the reply.

 

Arguments

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2.  In support of their demurrer, Defendant argues:

·                Plaintiffs’ claim for violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2 fails to articulate the required factual allegations to plead a cause of action.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue:

·                Plaintiffs have adequately pleaded a claim for violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2.

Defendants reply:

 

·                Plaintiffs’ Opposition failed to state a single fact that can support a cause of action for violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2.

ANALYSIS

 

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

A party is required to meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The meet and confer effort must occur at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).)

 

The parties met and conferred by email on September 18, October 10, and October 15.  (Geraghty Decl. ¶¶ 2-5.)  These meet and confer efforts are sufficient under § 430.41. 

 

B.    Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 (in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents).)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)   

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 (court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”); Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1037 (“A demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint, liberally construed, can state a cause of action under any theory or if there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured by amendment.”).)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

 

C.    Analysis

 

 

Defendant argues that a complaint must provide sufficient factual detail to alert the defendant to the cause of action. (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal. App. 5th 989, 996.) Specifically, when statutory remedies are invoked, the facts must be pleaded with particularity. (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396,410.)

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiffs' Third Cause of Action fails to meet these standards. The Complaint merely recites provisions of Civil Code section 1793.2 and offers conclusory statements without specifying facts, such as how Defendant allegedly violated the Civil Code, what service literature or replacement parts were unavailable, or how this failure caused damages.

 

Defendant further argues that the Complaint does not provide any factual allegations to support the claim for damages or civil penalties. The generalized assertions, such as entitlement to "a civil penalty of two times Plaintiffs' actual damages,” fail to meet the pleading standard necessary to sustain the claim.

 

Defendant concludes that the lack of factual detail prevents them from being apprised of the factual basis for the claims, and thus, the Demurrer to Plaintiffs' Third Cause of Action should be sustained.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the Complaint sufficiently alleges a cause of action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Contrary to Defendant's assertion that the complaint lacks specificity and contains mere conclusions, Plaintiff contends that it pleads sufficient facts to meet the standard required for a demurrer.

 

Plaintiffs emphasize that a general demurrer should not be sustained if the complaint states a cause of action on any theory. (Gomez v. Volkswagen of Am., Inc. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 921, 924-925.)  They assert that the Song-Beverly Act is a remedial measure intended to protect consumers and should be liberally construed to bring its benefits into action. (Kwan v. Mercedes-Benz of N.Am., Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 174, 184.) The Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs purchased a vehicle with an express warranty, encountered defects during the warranty period, and that Defendant failed to make sufficient service literature and replacement parts available, resulting in damages. (Complaint ¶ 5, 9, 10-11, and 30-31.)  These allegations, Plaintiff argues, provide the essential facts needed to state a claim.

 

Plaintiffs further note that Defendant’s demand for heightened specificity, such as identifying specific service literature or parts, is unfounded. They argue that the Song-Beverly Act does not require this level of detail at the pleading stage, as no case law or statutory authority supports such a requirement. Moreover, Plaintiff points out that Defendant possesses superior knowledge of the relevant facts, such as the repair history of the vehicle, and it would be unreasonable to expect Plaintiff to have access to this information prior to discovery.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ Opposition fails to address the fundamental deficiencies in their Third Cause of Action, which alleges a violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(a)(3). Defendant contends that Plaintiffs merely repeat the statutory language without providing factual support, failing to meet the required pleading standard. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) Statutory claims must be pleaded with particularity, including specific facts rather than conclusory statements. Plaintiffs’ complaint lacks allegations detailing what service literature or replacement parts were unavailable and when.

 

Defendant further argues that Plaintiffs fail to allege any specific damages suffered as a result of the purported violation. A general statement claiming damages without factual support is insufficient to establish a cause of action. Defendant asserts that Plaintiffs’ argument relies on unsubstantiated inferences, which do not meet established pleading requirements.

 

Moreover, Plaintiffs do not address Defendant’s argument that there is no factual basis to support their claims of wrongdoing or damages, nor do they provide a valid argument for leave to amend. Defendant urges the court to sustain the demurrer without leave to amend, as Plaintiffs have not demonstrated any ability to remedy the deficiencies in their complaint.

 

Civil Code section 1793.2 subdivision (a)(3) “Every manufacturer of consumer goods sold in this state and for which the manufacturer has made an express warranty shall: Make available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period.” (Civ. Code § 1793.2 subd. (a)(3).)

In the Complaint, Plaintiffs pleaded that Defendant “Failed to make available to the privately owned service and repair shops sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period” and that “Plaintiffs have been damaged by Defendant AHM’s failure to comply with its obligations.” (Complaint ¶ 31.) While all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, the Court does not consider conclusions of law. (Aubry, supra, 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) Here, the Plaintiffs have pleaded that they were damaged but have not provided any facts to show how they were damaged. Thus, Plaintiffs’ claim for damages is a conclusion of law and is not considered by the Court. Consequently, Plaintiffs have not pleaded sufficient facts to support a cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1793.2 subdivision (a)(3). (Lewis, supra, 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the third cause of action, with 20 days leave to amend.

Ciro Martinez v. Sheny Maradiaga (24STCV20593)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff Ciro Martinez/opposing party: Law Offices of D. Hess Panah & Associates

Counsel for Defendant Sheny Maradiaga/moving party: Courtney D. Flannery & Associates

 

[motion TO COMPEL SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE] (filed December 13, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s motion to compel Special Interrogatories, set one is GRANTED. Defendant’s request for sanctions is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from a motor vehicle accident between Plaintiff Ciro Martinez (Plaintiff) and Defendant Sheny Maradiaga (Defendant).

 

On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant, alleging a single cause of action for Negligence.

 

On December 13, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories, set one (SROGs). No opposition has been filed.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A responding party has 30 days after service of interrogatories to serve their responses on the propounding party. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.260(a).) However, if interrogatories are served by electronic service, a responding party has an additional two (2) court days to respond. (Id. § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)¿ 

 

If the responding party fails to timely respond to the interrogatories, “the party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010).” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)¿ 

 

Furthermore, the party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(b), 2031.300(b).) And “[u]nlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet and confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)¿¿ 

 

Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1)-(2).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff to provide verified responses without objections to SROGs.

 

Here, on October 3, 2024, Defendant served upon Plaintiff the SROGs. (Chan Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s responses were thus due no later than November 4, 2024.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff is compelled to serve verified responses to Defendant’s SROGs, without objections.

 

 

Sanctions

 

When a request for monetary sanctions is concurrently filed with a motion to compel responses, “The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(a).) Additionally, “If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanction unjust.” (Ibid.)¿ 

 

As explained above, Plaintiffs did not serve timely responses to Defendant’s SROGs. Plaintiff has also not filed an opposition to provide the Court with any substantial justification as to why the responses were not timely given. Thus, sanctions against Plaintiff are appropriate. 

 

Defendant requests $455.34 in sanctions.

 

Counsel for Defendant declares that she incurred two (2) hours of fees to meet and confer, review the motion and declaration, and appear at the hearing, at $160.17 per hour, plus one (1) hour of legal assistant time to reserve the hearing and draft motion, at $75.00 per hour. The Court finds that the amount of time spent in association with the instant motion and the hourly rates to be reasonable. Thus, the Court will award Defendant the requested $455.34 in sanctions, which includes the $60.00 filing fee.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel Special Interrogatories, set one is GRANTED. Plaintiff must provide code-compliant verified responses, without objection, to Defendant’s SROGs within 30 days’ notice of this order.

 

Defendant’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $455.34. Sanctions are payable within 30 days’ notice of this order.

 

Defendant is to give notice.
