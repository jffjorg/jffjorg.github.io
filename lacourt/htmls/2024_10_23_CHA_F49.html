DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 23CHCV03144    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: F49

Dept. F49

Date: 10/23/24

Case Name: BMO Bank N.A. v. Medina Truck & Transportation, Inc., Salvador Medina, and Does 1-20

Case No. 23CHCV03144

 

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

 

OCTOBER 23, 2024

 

MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23CHCV03144

 

Motion filed: 6/17/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff BMO Bank N.A.  

RESPONDING PARTY: None

NOTICE: OK.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order from this Court to compel Defendant Salvador Medina to respond to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and to impose monetary sanctions against Defendant Salvador Medina and his counsel in the amount of $1,847.50.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is GRANTED. The request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an alleged breach of contract.

 

On October 18, 2023, Plaintiff BMO Bank N.A. (“Plaintiff” or “BMO Bank”) initiated the action against Defendants Medina Truck & Transport, Inc. (“MT&T”), Salvador Medina (“Medina”) (collectively, “Defendants”) and Does 1-20. The Complaint alleges: (1) Breach of Written Agreement (against MT&T); (2) Breach of Continuing Guaranty (against Medina); (3) Claim and Delivery (against all Defendants); and (4) Conversion (against all Defendants).

 

On May 30, 2024, the Court overruled a demurrer with motion to strike, filed by Medina on December 2, 2023. Subsequently, Defendants filed their Answer to the Complaint on June 12, 2024.

 

On the same day, May 30, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s application for writ of possession against Medina.

 

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Reponses to Form Interrogatories, Set One (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition or Reply papers have been received by the Court.

 

ANALYSIS

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that needs to be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Additionally, “[i]f a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response ... [t]he party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product...” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

 

A.    Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One

 

Defendants’ counsel, Michael Brown (“Brown”), attests that on or about December 20, 2023, Defendant Medina was served with the first set of Form Interrogatories by both mail and email. (Brown Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. “1.”) This service established that responses were due on January 24, 2024. On February 6, 2024, Brown’s send a meet and confer letter to Medina’s counsel requesting responses. (Id. ¶ 5.)  However, as of the date of filing of the Motion, Medina has not served any responses. (Id. ¶ 6.)

 

Based on the above records, the Court determines that Defendant Medina failed to serve a timely response, thereby waiving any objection to the interrogatories, including those based on privilege or the protection for work product, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, subdivision (a).

 

Nevertheless, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, subdivision (a), “[t]he court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: ¶ (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. ¶ (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”

 

Here, Defendant Medina did not file an Opposition to the Motion. Nor did he move separately for relief from the waiver. Additionally, there is no record indicating any responses have been subsequently served by Medina prior to the hearing. Consequently, the Court finds that relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, subdivision(a) is unavailable in this circumstance.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the unopposed Motion.

 

A.    Monetary Sanctions

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, subdivision (c), “[t]he Court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust....”

 

Additionally, California Rules of Court rule 3.1348(A) further provides, “The Court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Underlines added.)

 

The Court finds the mandatory sanction applies in this case. Utilizing the lodestar approach, the Court determines the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the Motion to be $547.50, calculated based on a reasonable hourly rate of $325.00 for 1.5 hours reasonably spent, plus a $60.00 filing fee.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS IN PART Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff BMO Bank N.A.’s unopposed Motion to Compel Defendant Salvador Medina to Respond to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is GRANTED.

 

Defendant Salvador Medina is ordered to serve verified responses, without objection, to the Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days.

 

Plaintiff BMO Bank N.A.’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED IN PART.

 

Defendant Salvador Medina and his attorney of record are ordered to jointly and severally pay $547.50 to Plaintiff BMO Bank N.A.’s counsel within 20 days.

DEMURRER

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order sustaining Defendant’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The Demurrer is SUSTAINED IN PART and OVERRULED IN PART.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a breach of contract and tort case between a canned beverage manufacturer, Plaintiff DrinkPAK LLC (“Plaintiff” or “DrinkPAK”), and a payroll services provider, Defendant Paylocity Corp. (“Defendant” or “Paylocity”).

 

On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 10, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Implied Indemnity, (4) Equitable Indemnity, (5) Contribution, and (6) Declaratory Relief. Subsequently, on October 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Request for Dismissal, dismissing the following causes of action in the Complaint: Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing (Second Cause of Action), Equitable Indemnity (Fourth Cause of Action), and Declaratory Relief (Sixth Cause of Action). As a result, the remaining causes of action in the Complaint are Breach of Contract, Implied Indemnity, and Contribution.

 

On September 9, 2024, Defendant Paylocity filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint (the “Demurrer”). Following this, Plaintiff filed its Opposition on October 10, 2024, and Defendant replied on October 16, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.)

 

At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law[.]” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525 (Berkley).) In applying these standards, the court liberally construes the complaint to determine whether a cause of action has been stated. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School Dist. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 733.)

 

A.    Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of the following matters:

 

1.      Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”) No. 1: Paylocity Corporation Full Bundle Subscription Agreement between the parties. (RJN Ex. “A.”)

 

2.      RJN No. 2: the complaint filed on September 1, 2023, in Veliz v. DrinkPak, LLC, Case No. 22STCV37384 in the Superior Court of Los Angeles County, California (RJN Ex. “B.”)

 

3.      RJN No. 3: the complaint filed on January 13, 2023, in Bazzy v. DrinkPak, LLC, Case No. 23STCV00766 in the Superior Court of Los Angeles County, California. (RJN Ex. “C.”)

 

Evidence Code section 425 provides, in pertinent part, “Judicial notice may be taken of the following matters: ... (d) Records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States....(h) Facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” Additionally, a court may take judicial notice of a contract referred to in the complaint. (See, e.g., Align Tech., Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 956 fn. 6.)

 

            However, the case file does not indicate that Defendant has included the exhibits as claimed in its request. As a result, Defendant has not provided sufficient information to enable the Court to take judicial notice of the matter. (Evid. Code, § 453.)

 

            Accordingly, the RJN is DENIED.

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

A party filing a demurrer “shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.41(a).) A failure to meet and confer does not constitute grounds to sustain or overrule a demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., sections 430.41 (a)(4).)

 

Here, Defendant’s good faith efforts to meet and confer were sufficient. (Casillas Decl. ¶¶ 3-4, 6-7.)

 

C.    Application of Illinois Law to the Causes of Action

 

“California law recognizes a strong public policy favoring enforcement of choice-of-law provisions[.]” (Colaco v. Cavotec SA (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1172, 1177.) California courts enforce choice of law provisions, subject to two qualifications. (Nedlloyd Lines B.V. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 459, 465.) More specifically:

 

If the parties state their intention in an express choice-of-law clause, California courts ordinarily will enforce the parties’ stated intention unless (1) the chosen state has no substantial relationship to the parties or their transaction, and there is no other reasonable basis for the parties’ choice, or (2) the chosen state’s law is contrary to a fundamental policy of the state whose law otherwise would apply, and the latter state has a materially greater interest in the matter than does the chosen state.

 

(Frontier Oil Corp. v. RLI Ins. Co. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1436, 1451, fn. 7 [citing Nedlloyd Lines B.V. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 459].)

 

Although Defendant has not presented the actual copy of the purported agreement to the Court, it argues that the parties agreed in the Paylocity Corporation Full Bundle Subscription Agreement (“Agreement”) that “Agreement shall be construed according to the laws of the State of Illinois.” (Dem. at p. 2.) Additionally, Defendant maintains that substantial relationship exists as it is headquartered in Illinois. (Dem. at p. 8, see also Casillas Decl. Ex. “D.”) Therefore, the Court finds that the express choice-of-law clause reflects the parties’ intention and establishes a substantial relationship between the chosen state and the parties.

 

Plaintiff does not dispute the application of Illinois law, nor does it argue that Illinois law is contrary to any fundamental policy of the State of California. Instead, Plaintiff relies on legal arguments based on Illinois precedent.

 

            Accordingly, the Court reviews the Demurrer under Illinois law.

 

D.    Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Defendant first argues that the Breach of Contract claim is barred by the Agreement’s exculpatory clauses.

 

Defendant presents that the Agreement contains the following provisions:

 

“§6: CLIENT AGREES THAT PAYLOCITY IS NOT . . . RESPONSIBLE FOR

CLIENT’S COMPLIANCE WITH FEDERAL, STATE, OR LOCAL STATUTES, REGULATIONS, OR ORDINANCES, INCLUDING, BUT NOT LIMITED TO . . . ANY WAGE AND HOUR LAWS . . . PAYLOCITY SHALL NOT UNDER ANY CIRCUMSTANCES BE LIABLE FOR SPECIAL, INDIRECT, INCIDENTAL, OR CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES OR OTHER SIMILAR DAMAGES (INCLUDING LOST PROFITS) EVEN IF PAYLOCITY HAS BEEN ADVISED OF THE POSSIBILITY OF SUCH DAMAGES.”

 

“§5: Client is solely responsible for the content and accuracy of all data input and then subsequently processed by Paylocity.

 

. . .

 

It is the CLIENT’s responsibility to review the processed payroll and other information and to promptly identify any errors. If the data submitted to Paylocity is incorrect, incomplete, or not in proper form, then CLIENT agrees to pay additional charges then in effect for the corrections to said data.

 

…

 

CLIENT IS SOLELY RESPONSIBLE FOR ANY LIABILITY TO EMPLOYEES FOR FAILURE TO COMPLY WITH FEDERAL, STATE OR LOCAL LAWS.”

 

(Dem. at p. 9.)

 

In response, Plaintiff contends that the exculpatory clauses in the Agreement do not apply because they render Defendant’s contractual duties illusory and that they are invalid under the exception of willful and wanton conduct. (Opp’n., at p. 10.)

 

The Court proceeds to address the two contentions separately.

 

1)      Exculpatory Clauses Rendering Promises Illusory

 

           

 

            Although exculpatory clauses are generally enforced under Illinois’s public policy that strongly favors freedom to contract, “exculpatory clauses are not favored in Illinois, and are to be strictly construed against the party they benefit, especially when the party was also the drafters[.]” (Horne v. Electric Eel Manufacturing Company, Inc. (2021) 987 F.3d 704, 718 (Horne), citing Avery v. State Farm Mut. Ins. Co. (2015) 216 Ill.2d 100.)

 

In Jewelers Mut. Ins. Co. v. Firstar Bank Illinois (2004) 213 Ill.2d 58 (Jewelers), the plaintiffs were insurers of individuals and businesses that rented safety deposit boxes at a bank. The rental agreement contained a broad exculpatory clause, denying liability for loss of or injury to the contents of the box unless the lessee entered into a separate agreement with the bank to that effect. But the contract also provided that the “liability of said bank is limited to the exercise of ordinary care to prevent the opening of said safe by any person not authorized[.]” The Illinois Supreme Court stated that “[a] party cannot promise to act in a certain manner in one portion of a contract and then exculpate itself from liability for breach of that very promise in another part of the contract.... [T]he exclusion of its specifically articulated obligations ... would render [a] defendant's contractual duties illusory.” (Id. at pp. 415-416.)

 

Conversely, Illinois courts have upheld exculpatory clauses that do not absolve a party from liability for breach of a promise made in another part of the contract. In Home Healthcare of Ill., Inc. v. Jesk (2017) 112 N.E.3d 594 (Jesk), an escrow agreement contained two exculpatory clauses: (1) “the Escrow Agent shall incur no liability whatsoever except for willful misconduct or gross negligence,” and (2) “[t]he Seller and Purchaser each release the Escrow Agent from any act done or omitted to be done by the Escrow Agent in good faith in the performance of its duties hereunder.” (Id. at p. 603.) The Illinois appellate court reasoned that “[t]he net effect of the two relevant sentences in that clause is that [the defendant] is not liable for acts of ordinary negligence committed in good faith but is still liable for actions taken in bad faith, i.e., acts constituting ‘willful misconduct or gross negligence,’” concluding that these provisions did not render the contract illusory. (Ibid.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges that “[a]s a result of the errors in Paylocity’s software, Paylocity’s software was erroneously calculating and reporting to DrinkPAK the wages earned by DrinkPAK’s employees. Eventually, DrinkPAK became aware of the errors contained in Paylocity’s software, and DrinkPAK made multiple requests to Paylocity to correct the errors inherent in its software. By way of example, on August 5, 2021, DrinkPAK’s general counsel, Jer Monson, emailed Ana Espinoza at Paylocity and asked for a call to ‘address several outstanding issues about which we have previously contacted customer service.’ One of the issues that Mr. Monson identified was the ‘[m]ethodology for calculating “regular rate of pay” overtime compensation and meal/rest period premium payments.’ Indeed, Mr. Monson and DrinkPAK’s Chief Financial Officer, Christina Kim, called Paylocity on August 9, 2021, to try to have Paylocity correct the issue with its software. However, despite multiple assurances by Paylocity to DrinkPAK that the problems would be rectified, the issues with Paylocity’s software and services continued to persist, and the software continued to improperly calculate the wages owed to DrinkPAK’s employees.” (Compl. ¶ 17.) “Despite DrinkPAK’s best efforts to have Paylocity correct the problems, the issues with Paylocity’s software persisted—even after the lawsuits were initiated against DrinkPAK.” (Id. ¶ 20.)

 

Plaintiff contends that, based on the allegations, the exculpatory clauses in the Agreement effectively negate Defendant’s obligations to exercise due care and to correct errors, rendering its promises illusory. The Court agrees.

 

The Court finds that the net effect of the provision that states “Client is solely responsible for the content and accuracy of all data input and then subsequently processed by Paylocity” (Dem. at p. 9) effectively negates Paylocity’s specific contractual duty to “use due care in processing [DrinkPAK’s] work” and that Paylocity “shall be responsible for correcting errors” caused by Paylocity. (Opp’n., at p. 6.) (Underlines added.)

 

Unlike the exculpatory clauses in Jesk, Paylocity’s promise to exercise due care and to correct errors conflicts with the exculpatory clauses in the Agreement, which place sole responsibility for the content and accuracy of the data processed by Paylocity.

 

Furthermore, much like the defendant in Jewelers, where the bank was prohibited from exculpating itself from all liability after breaching an express obligation to exercise ordinary care to prevent unauthorized persons from opening a safe, Paylocity allegedly breached its express duty to use due care in processing DrinkPAK’s work and correcting errors after DrinkPAK identified and reported the issue. Following the reasoning in Jewelers, the Court finds Defendant’s argument unpersuasive, as the exculpatory clauses do not shield Defendant from liability when it is alleged to have materially breached its express obligations.

 

As for Defendant’s argument that the exculpatory clauses at issue are narrower than those in Jewelers, claiming that they do not bar all liabilities “from any causes whatever,” the Court finds this argument unconvincing in light of its prior findings.

 

The primary obligation of Paylocity under the Agreement was to provide the payroll services, which specifically included exercising due care in processing DrinkPAK’s data and correcting errors. If Paylocity’s breach of this core provision is the cause of the injury, then, as established in Jewelers, it cannot exculpate itself from liability for that breach, even if the exculpatory clauses limit other liabilities.

 

            Accordingly, based on the argument presented, the Court concludes that the exculpatory clauses render a primary obligation of Paylocity illusory.

 

2)      Willful and Wanton Conduct Exception

 

In Illinois, when a defendant has engaged in willful and wanton conduct, an exculpatory clause will not bar claims for injuries derived from such gross negligence (See Garrison v. Combined Fitness Centre, Ltd. (1990) 201 Ill.App.3d 581; see also Hawkins v. Cap. Fitness, Inc. (2015) 29 N.E.3d 442 [“Absent fraud or willful and wanton negligence, a contract’s exculpatory clause will be valid ...”])

 

Willful and wanton acts show “actual or deliberate intent to harm” or, if not intentional, show “an utter indifference to or conscious disregard for a person's own safety or the safety or property of others.” (Pfister v. Shusta (1995) 167 Ill.2d 417, 421 (Pfister).)

 

Here, Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not allege willful and wanton conduct, asserting that the Complaint contains no allegations that “Paylocity chose to ‘ignore the problem’ or ‘act in a way that put [another] at risk,” citing Munoz v. Nucor Steel Kankakee, Inc. (2022) 44 F.4th 595.) (Reply at p. 5.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant’s argument unpersuasive in concluding that Plaintiff’s allegations lack any showing of conscious disregard or indifference. The Supreme Court of Illinois recognized that “[w]illful and wanton conduct is ‘a hybrid between acts considered negligence and behavior found to be intentionally tortious.... Under the facts of one case, willful and wanton misconduct may be only degrees more than ordinary negligence, while under the facts of another case, willful and wanton acts may be only degrees less than intentional wrongdoing. [Citations.]” (Pfister, supra, 167 Ill.2d at p. 422.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges that DrinkPAK became aware of the errors contained in Paylocity’s software and made multiple requests to Paylocity to correct the errors. (Compl. ¶ 17.) One of the issues identified by DrinkPAK was the “[m]ethodology for calculating ‘regular rate of pay’ overtime compensation and meal/rest period premium payments.” (Ibid.) However, despite multiple assurances by Paylocity to DrinkPAK that the problems would be rectified, the issue with Paylocity’s software and services continued to persist, and the software continued to improperly calculate the wages owed to DrinkPAK’s employees. (Ibid.) Later, DrinkPAK was named in two class action lawsuits directly stemming from the failures with Paylocity’s software. The lawsuits alleged failure to pay various wages, including improper calculations of overtime, rest periods, and meal periods, and failure to supply accurate wage statements. (Compl. ¶ 18.) The issues persisted even after the lawsuits were initiated against DrinkPAK. (Id. ¶ 20.) Paylocity’s own employees admitted that they had caused the errors. (Id. ¶ 21.)

 

Based on these allegations, and applying the reviewing standards for a demurrer, in which the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded” (Berkley, supra, 152 Cal.App.4th at p. 525), the Court finds that the Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts to survive the demurrer. The issue of whether Paylocity’s conduct rises to the level of willful and wanton misconduct is a question of fact that should be resolved through factual findings, rather than being dismissed as a matter of law.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the at this stage of the pleading, the allegations in the Complaint are sufficient to invoke the willful and wanton conduct exception, providing an alternative basis to overrule the Demurrer on this claim.

 

Therefore, the Court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the Cause of Action for Breach of Contract.

 

E.     Cause of Action – Implied Indemnity

 

Defendant argues that Illinois law divides implied indemnity claims into two buckets: implied contractual indemnity (see Zielinski v. Miller (1995) 277 Ill.App.3d 735 (Zielinski), 740 [stating that implied contractual indemnity applies “where one party’s breach of contract causes a second party to breach a separate contract with a third party.”]), and implied tort indemnity (see Id. at p. 739, see also UIRC-GSA Holdings Inc. v. Willaim Blair & Co., L.L.C (N.D. Ill.2018) 289 F.Sup.3d 852, 861-63 [holding that the party seeking indemnification must also be blameless while the other party is at fault.]) Defendant maintains that Plaintiff’s claim does not fit into either bucket.

 

            In its Opposition, Plaintiff contends that the underlying lawsuits gave rise to Defendant’s liability for implied indemnity, as these underlying lawsuits are borne out of DrinkPAK’s “nondelegable duty arising out of statutory law,” namely the California Labor Code, the California Business and Professions Code, and the Fair Labor Standard Act. (Frazer v. A.F. Munsterman, Inc. (1988) 123 Ill.2d 245, 255.) In Frazer, the Supreme Court of Illinois provided an example where “a landowner held responsible for failure to perform the duty imposed by law to maintain his premises in a reasonably safe condition is allowed indemnity from the negligent party who created the dangerous condition.” (Id. at p. 255, citing Rovekamp v. Central Construction Co. (1964) 45 Ill.App.2d 441 (Rovekamp).)

 

            The Court distinguishes Rovekamp. In that case, the Appellate Court of Illinois held that a general contractor was entitled to maintain third party action against subcontractor on theory that general contractor was exposed to statutory liability only because of its technical violation of an [Scaffold] Act, but subcontractor was primary tort-feasor wholly responsible for employee’s injuries. (Rovekamp, supra, 4 Ill.App.2d at pp. 450-451.) The Court finds Defendant’s following argument persuasive.

 

            First, while the underlying lawsuits in this case also involved nondelegable duties under the California Labor Code, the California Business and Professions Code, and the Fair Labor Standard Act, these do not sound in tort (see Reynolds v. Bement (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1075, 1090), distinguishing them from cases like Rovekamp, in which the underlying lawsuit was specifically to recover damages for personal injuries resulted from a fall from a scaffold.

 

Second, Paylocity cannot be considered a “tortfeasor” in the underlying lawsuits because the Labor Code impose liability solely on “employer,” and Paylocity did not have an employment relationship with the plaintiffs in the underlying lawsuits. (See Martinez v. Combs (2010) 49 Cal.4th 35, 49 [“That only an employer can be liable ...as no generally applicable rule of law imposes on anyone other than an employer a duty to pay wages.”])

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff does not allege an implied tort indemnity under Illinois law.

 

            Furthermore, the Complaint does not allege that the Paylocity’s breach of Agreement caused DrinkPAK to breach a separate contract with a third party, as required under Zielinski. Nor does the Complaint present any legal argument that its violation of statutory law, allegedly caused by Defendant’s breach of the Agreement, satisfy the Zielinski element or is recognized under any legal theory to adequately state a cause of action for implied contractual indemnify under Illinois law.

 

            Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the Cause of Action for Implied Indemnity.

 

F.     Cause of Action - Contribution

 

“[i]f the parties are not subject to liability in tort for the plaintiff's injuries at issue in the underlying action, there is no right of contribution between those parties.” (Giordano v. Morgan (1990) 197 Ill.App.3d 543, 548.)

 

“As established, liability in tort governing contribution has been interpreted to mean ‘potential’ tort liability of the party, determined at the time of the injury to the initial plaintiff. [Citation]” (Raab v. Frank (2019) 157 N.E.3d 470, 477(Raab).) “Although a breach of contract is a nontort theory, that alone is not determinative as to whether parties might also be subject to liability for contribution. [Citation]” (Ibid.) However, “[a]bsent potential liability in tort, a breach of contract claim does not warrant third-party contribution.” (Id. at p. 478.)

 

            Here, as analyzed above, Plaintiff’s claims sound in contract and the underlying lawsuit involved claims for statutory violations, not tort. Therefore, unless Plaintiff can sufficiently allege any “potential” tort liability, on which a claim for Contribution may be premised (see Raab, supra), the Complaint’s allegations do not give rise to liability for contribution. 

 

            Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the Cause of Action for Contribution.

 

            Based on the forgoing, the Court SUSTAINS IN PART the Demurrer and OVERRULES IN PART the Demurrer.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Paylocity Corp’s Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED as to the Cause of Action for Breach of Contract.

 

Defendant Paylocity Corp’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED as to the Causes of Action for Implied Indemnity and Contribution.

 

Plaintiff DrinkPAK, LLC is GRANTED 30 days’ leave to amend.
