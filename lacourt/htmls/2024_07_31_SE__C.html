Case Number: 22NWCV00320    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: C

PACIFIC SOUTHWEST DISTRICT OF LUTHERAN CHURCH-MISSOURI SYNOD v. IRENE JENSEN, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00320

HEARING:  7/31/24 @ 9:30 A.M.

 

#2

TENTATIVE RULING

 

     I.        Plaintiff Pacific Southwest District of Lutheran Church-Missouri Synod’s motion for leave to file the first amended complaint is GRANTED.

    II.        Plaintiff Pacific Southwest District of Lutheran Church-Missouri Synod’s motion for service by publication is DENIED without prejudice. 

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

 

Background

 

This is a quiet title action filed by Pacific Southwest District of the Lutheran Church-Missouri Synod (PSD) seeking to determine the ownership of the church where Defendant Peace Lutheran Church (PLC) gathers.  PSD contends that it now owns the property pursuant to PLC’s constitution because PLC’s congregation no longer exists.

 

PSD moves for leave to file the first amended complaint and for service by publication.

 

Motion for Leave to File the First Amended Complaint

 

Legal Standard

 

The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a).) The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a).)

 

A court can deny leave to amend after long, inexcusable delay, where there is cognizable prejudice, such as discovery needed, trial delay, critical evidence lost, or added preparation expense. (Solit v. Tokai Bank (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

 

Discussion

 

On April 26, 2022, Plaintiff filed the complaint. Plaintiff proposes the following changes:

 

(1) Identify “Doe 1” as “all persons unknown, claiming any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest in the property described in the complaint adverse to Plaintiff’s title or any cloud upon Plaintiff’s title thereto” (hereinafter referred to as “Unknown Claimants”).

(2) Add a seventh cause of action for “declaratory relief” against Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, Unknown Claimants, and Does 2-50.

(3) Add an eighth cause of action for “imposition of constructive trust” against Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, Unknown Claimants, and Does 2-50.

(4) Add a ninth cause of action for “ejectment” against Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, Unknown Claimants, and Does 2-50. 5.

(5) Add a twelfth paragraph to the Prayer “For a judicial determination and declaration that defendants Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, Unknown Claimants, and Does 2-50 hold the subject property and funds as constructive trustee(s) for Plaintiff’s benefit; that a “constructive” or “resulting” trust be imposed for the benefit of Plaintiff; and that an order be entered compelling Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, and Does 1-50 to transfer said property to Plaintiff.”

(6) Add a thirteenth paragraph to the Prayer “For an order directing defendants Chastity Carvel, Peace Lutheran Church, Incorporated, of South Gate, California, Unknown Claimants, and Does 2-50, and each of them, to surrender possession of all real and personal property of Peace Lutheran Church to Plaintiff.”

 

Plaintiff has included a copy of the proposed amended pleading. (Decl. Leverone, ¶ 2, Ex. A.)

 

California recognizes “a general rule of…liberal allowance of amendments…” (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.) It has also long been recognized that “even if the proposed legal theory is a novel one, ‘the preferable practice would be to permit the amendment and allow the parties to test its legal sufficiency by demurrer, motion for judgment on the pleadings or other appropriate proceedings.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.) In light of great liberality employed when ruling on a motion for leave to amend, the court will not normally consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading since grounds for demurrer or motion to strike are premature. Thus, absent prejudice to the opposing party, courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (emphasis added.) (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.)

 

In light of the liberality associated with granting motions for leave to amend up to and including the time of trial, the Court finds that PSD should be afforded the opportunity to make the proposed amendments. Defendants maintain their right to compel discovery, demur, move for judgment on the pleadings, move to strike, or move for summary judgment.

 

The motion for leave to file the First Amended Complaint is GRANTED. 

 

Motion for Service by Publication

 

Legal Standard

 

If the Court determines that the plaintiff has made a showing that plaintiff used reasonable diligence to ascertain the identity and residence of and to serve summons on the persons named as unknown defendants, the court shall order service by publication pursuant to Section 415.50 and the provisions of this article. (Code Civ. Proc., § 763.010, subd. (b).)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff’s counsel conclusorily states that Plaintiff and its counsel have used reasonable diligence to identify and locate all persons Plaintiff reasonably suspects might claim an interest in the subject property. (Decl. Leverone, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s showing is not sufficient.

 

The motion for service by publication is DENIED without prejudice.

Case Number: 22NWCV00741    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: C

HODGES, ET AL. v. ZEV BROOKS, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00741

HEARING:  7/31/24 @ 10:30 A.M.

 

#8

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Zev Brooks’ and the Law Offices of Zev Brooks’ motion for summary judgment is deemed to be a motion for judgment on the pleadings.  The motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff Jane Hodges (“Plaintiff”) sues Defendants Zev Brooks and the Law Offices of Zev Brooks for legal malpractice and breach of fiduciary duty.  She does not sue in her individual capacity.  Instead, she sues in her capacity as trustee of her family’s trust and as special administrator of her mother’s estate. 

 

The Complaint alleges that in 2019, Defendants represented Plaintiff’s mother, Rose Mary Ollison (deceased), for the purpose of drafting an amendment to the family trust and other documents.  The documents removed Plaintiff from all fiduciary positions and named Plaintiff’s sister, Marian Cecilia Contreras, as the primary fiduciary.  Plaintiff alleges that all of this was accomplished under Marian’s direction.  After Plaintiff discovered what had happened, Plaintiff and Marian filed competing petitions in the Orange County Superior Court regarding the validity of the 2019 documents.  The Court found the documents were void due to Marian’s undue influence, and the Court restored Plaintiff’s position as trustee in charge of the trust.  In this lawsuit, Plaintiff seeks damages which include the cost of Plaintiff’s attorney fees and Marian’s attorney fees, both of which were (or will likely be) paid out of the trust. 

 

Defendants move for summary judgment and, in the alternative, summary adjudication on the grounds that Defendants did not owe a duty to Plaintiff and Plaintiff lacks standing.  Defendants also assert that Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the statute of limitations. 

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of the following:

 

(1) verified petition filed in San Diego County Superior Court, Case No. 37-2019-00015566-PR-TR-CTL, transferred to Orange County Superior Court as case number 30-201901098095-PR-TR-CJC, and consolidated with case number 30-2019-01059566;

(2) verified petition filed by Marian Contreras in Orange County Superior Court, Case No. 30-2019-01059566-PR-Tr-CJC on March 27, 2019;

(3) verified petition filed by Jane Hodges in Orange County Superior Court, case number 30-2019-01059566-PR-TR-CJC on June 19, 2020;

(4) order for probate – special administrator, filed February 3, 2022 in Los Angeles Superior Court, Case No. 21STPB11209;

(5) letters of special administration filed February 18, 2022 in Los Angeles Superior Court, case number 21STPB11209;

(6) order appointing counsel as guardian ad litem for Rose Marie Ollison, filed July 9, 2019 in Orange County Superior Court, Case No. 30-2019-01059566-PR-TR-CJC;

(7) report by court-appointed guardian ad litem for Rose Mary Ollison filed June 3, 2020 in Orange County Superior Court, Case No. 30-2019-01059566-PR-TR-CJC;

(8) petition for request and declaration of Linda K. Ross for fees as court appointed guardian ad litem for Rose Mary Ollison filed September 23, 2020 in Orange County Superior Court, Case No. 30-2019-01059566-PR-TR-CJC;

(9) Final Statement of Decision filed May 3, 2021, Orange County Superior Court Case No. 30-2019-01059566;

(10) judgment filed August 23, 2021, Orange County Superior Court Case No. 30-2019-01059566;

(11) Complaint for Damages filed in the instant action; and

(12) Answer filed in the instant action.

 

The court takes judicial notice of the existence of the documents identified above. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d)(1).) But it does not take judicial notice of any factual findings. Factual findings in prior judicial orders, opinions, or decisions are not a proper subject of judicial notice. (Kilroy v. State of Calif. (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 140, 148.)

 

Legal Standard

 

A defendant’s motion for summary judgment ‘necessarily includes a test of the sufficiency of the complaint.  Motions for summary judgment in such situations have otherwise been allowed as being in legal effect motions for judgment on the pleadings. Thus, if the reviewing court finds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action as a matter of law, it need not reach the question whether plaintiff's opposition to the summary judgment motion raises a triable issue of fact. (Hansra v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 630, 638.)

 

Standing

 

In the Complaint, Plaintiff sues in her capacity as trustee on behalf of the Ollison Family Trust and as special administrator on behalf of the Estate of Rose Mary Ollison, Deceased.  Defendants argue that they only represented Rose Mary Ollison, not the trust or a trustee.  Plaintiff concedes this in her opposition brief. (Opp., p. 7:23-24.) However, the Complaint is inconsistent in this regard.  For example, the Complaint alleges, “Defendant had a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as members of the legal profession commonly possess and exercise, in providing legal services to Plaintiffs (Complaint, ¶ 37, italics added).  The following paragraph alleges, “Defendant represented Rose Mary Ollison (who is now deceased) and also the Ollison Family Trust in his preparation of and facilitating the execution of the March 2019 Documents described above.” (Id., ¶ 38, italics added.) Thus, it is unclear from the Complaint precisely whom the Defendants undertook to represent.  Until this aspect of the attorney client relationship is clarified, no determination can be made regarding standing. 

 

Accordingly, the Court determines that the Complaint does not sufficiently allege Plaintiff’s standing to pursue this litigation.  The motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED with 30 days leave to amend.  Because lack of standing is dispositive of the motion, the Court does not address Defendants’ statute of limitations argument. 

 


Case Number: 22NWCV01502    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: C

FUENTES v. H.P. PALOMINO, INC., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV01502

HEARING:  7/31/24 @ 10:30 A.M.

 

#3

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants H.P. Palomino, Inc. and Rafiq Shah’s demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Background

 

Defendants H.P. Palomino, Inc., and Rafiq Shah demur to the Second Amended Complaint on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

 

On December 6, 2022, Plaintiff Victor Fuentes brought the instant lawsuit for wage-an-hour violations against his former employers, Defendants H.P. Palomino, Inc., and Rafiq Shah.  Plaintiff alleges he was employed by Defendants as a maintenance manager from 1999 until September 2021.   

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request judicial notice of the following:

 

(1) settlement agreement in the prior action; and  

(2) Articles of Incorporation of H.P. Palomino, Inc.

 

Courts properly take judicial notice of facts in legal, operative documents, where the complainant alleges no facts inferring a contrary conclusion, or where the existence and effect is not the subject of a reasonable dispute.  (Intengan v. Bac Home Loans Servicing LP (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1054.)  Thus, the Court grants judicial notice of the first request.  

 

The Court will not adjudicate the second request because it is not relevant to this matter. (Mangini v. R.J. Reynolds Tobacco Co. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1057, 1063.)

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.”  (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.)  The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. . .”  (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525 (Berkley).) 

 

The burden is on the complainant to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully, to obtain an order allowing leave to amend.  (McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co.  (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 78.) “Leave to amend should be denied where the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear, but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Lawrence v. Bank of America (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 431, 436.) 

 

Discussion

 

Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s claims were settled in the unlawful detainer action entitled Shah v. Fuentes, Case No. 22NWUD01483.  Defendants rely upon the Unlawful Detainer Stipulated Judgment entered on September 13, 2022. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. B.) Page Two of the Stipulated Judgment contains the following handwritten language:

 

“Concurrent with the execution of this settlement agreement, Plaintiff [Rafiq Shah] shall pay to Defendants [Victor Fuentes and Guillermina Figueroa], jointly, the sum of $2500.00.  The said sum is intended to be relocation expenses and in exchange for a waiver of all claims known and unknown.  Defendants waive protections under Civil Code section 1542, which provides that ‘A general release does not extend to claims that the creditor or releasing party does not know or suspect to exist in his or her favor at the time of executing the release and that, if known by him or her, would have materially affected his or her settlement with the debtor or released party.’  Additionally, Defendants release any claims for habitability of the leased premises.”

 

The Stipulated Judgment was entered 84 days before Fuentes filed this lawsuit. 

 

A general release that explicitly covers known and unknown claims and specifically waives the provisions of Civil Code Section 1542 is enforceable and acts as a complete bar to all claims, whether known or unknown at the time of release. (San Diego Hospice v. Cnty. of San Diego (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1048, 1053-1054.)

 

In Opposition, Fuentes argues that he did not waive or settle the claims at issue in this lawsuit.  Fuentes points to language in the SAC which alleges that Shah’s attorney specifically told Fuentes that the eviction settlement only applied to matters involving the eviction and no other matters. (SAC ¶ 34).  The SAC further alleges that Fuentes did not review the revised version of the settlement agreement and Fuentes believes the Section 1542 waiver was added without his knowledge. (SAC ¶ 35.) 

 

This language was added after the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the First Amended Complaint on September 5, 2023.  In its ruling, the Court noted that the FAC lacked any allegation that Fuentes did not review the written portion of the agreement.  Fuentes attempts to remedy this deficiency in the SAC.  However, after further review, the Court determines that the additional language in the SAC is still insufficient to state a cause of action.  That is because the Section 1542 waiver is enforceable as part of the judgment entered by the unlawful detainer court.  Page Three of the Stipulated Judgment was signed by Commissioner Bruguera and the Court entered the following order: “The Court hereby orders the above-named parties to comply with the terms of the stipulation, and the clerk is directed to enter this stipulation as judgment.” (Orloff Decl., ¶3, Ex. B.) Thus, absent a court order vacating that portion of the judgment, the Section 1542 waiver is dispositive of this lawsuit. 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

BARMAAN VS. CITY OF HUNTINGTON PARK, ET AL.

CASE NO.:  23NWCV00134

HEARING:  7/31/24 @ 9:30 A.M.

 

#4

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Complainant City of Huntington Park’s motion for preliminary injunction is GRANTED.

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

 

Background

 

Cross-Complainant City of Huntington Park (“The City”) moves for a preliminary injunction to enjoin Cross-Defendants Jamshid Barman Saeideh Aryaee from continually violating Huntington Park municipal code. According to the Cross-Complainant, Cross-Defendants have not sought the required building permits and approvals to zone the property located at 2551 Clarendon Avenue, Huntington Park, California (“Subject Property”). The City maintains that the Subject Property should be strictly used commercial purposes, but Cross-Defendants have used it for commercial and residential purposes.  The City also alleges that Cross-Defendants have violated property maintenance and building permit requirements.

 

The Subject Property is in the City’s District C, and residential uses are permitted in District C at the discretion of the City, or when conditionally permitted. (See Downtown Huntington Park Specific Plan, § 4-6 [Allowable Land Uses By District], figure 4.58 [Land Use Table].) The City’s investigation revealed that the Subject Property contains extensive building alterations completed without the required building permits. (See, e.g., Zapien Decl., ¶ 20, Ex. V [September 2022 Post-Inspection Diagram].) These unpermitted alterations include the following: (1) metal carport/cover patio located at the northwest end of the building; (2) laundry connections under the metal cover patio of the first floor; (3) shower in the permitted half bath on the first floor; (4) kitchen cabinets and sink in the first floor between the two businesses; (5) partition wall to the rear of one of the businesses on the first floor; (6) a partition wall to the rear of the second business on the first floor next to the half bath; (7) a full bath inside unit #1 on the second floor; (8) a shower in the half batch on the second floor; (9) partition wall inside unit #3 on the second floor; (10) partition wall inside unit #5 on the second floor; (11) metal bars in the east balcony area on the second floor, and (12) the first floor tenants spaces have been unlawfully connected by the unpermitted removal of the wall next to the full bath area. (Zapien Decl., ¶¶ 16–20, 27(b); Bollier Decl., ¶¶ 12.)

 

Cross-Complainant argues that these violations are a nuisance, while Cross-Defendants oppose on the grounds of state preemption of municipal law.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Cross-Complainant requests judicial notice of the following: (1) grant deed dated March 10, 2005 as instrument number 050572725; (2) quitclaim deed dated December 16, 2019 as instrument number 202110607595; (3) Huntington Park Downtown Specific Plan, adopted by City of Huntington Park on August 4, 2008; and (4) Huntington Park Municipal Code sections 1-2.01, 1-2.02, 5-35.01, 5-35.02, 5-35.03, 5-35.04, 5-35.17, 5-35.17, 8-1.02, 8-1.05, 9-1.106, 9-2.2406, 9- 2.2407, 9-3.602, 9-3.603, 9-3.604, 9-3.611, 9-3.612, 9-3.613, and 9-3.614.

 

Courts properly take judicial notice of facts in legal, operative documents, where the complainant alleges no facts inferring a contrary conclusion, or where the existence and effect is not the subject of a reasonable dispute.  (Intengan v. Bac Home Loans Servicing LP (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1054.)  Thus, the Court grants judicial notice of the first three requests.

 

Also, judicial notice may be taken of regulations issued by any public entity in the United States. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (b).)  Thus, the Court grants judicial notice of the fourth request.

 

Legal Standard

 

In determining whether to issue a preliminary injunction, the trial court considers two factors: (1) the reasonable probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial; and (2) a balancing of the “irreparable harm” that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants a preliminary injunction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 526, subd. (a); 14859 Moorpark Homeowner’s Assn. v. VRT Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1396, 1402; Pillsbury, Madison & Sutro v. Schectman (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1279, 1283.)

 

The Court’s determination is guided by a “mix” of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.  (Butt v. State of Calif. (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 678.)  However, a trial court may not grant a preliminary injunction, regardless of the balance of interim harm, unless there is some possibility that the plaintiff would ultimately prevail on the merits of the claim. (Ibid.) 

 

Likelihood of Success

 

The underlying causes of action for the injunction relief sought by Cross-Complainant are the nuisance and nuisance per se causes of action.

 

The concept of a nuisance per se arises when a legislative body with appropriate jurisdiction, in the exercise of the police power, expressly declares a particular object or substance, activity, or circumstance, to be a nuisance. (Beck Dev. Co. v. S. Pac. Transportation Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1206.) Generally a nuisance is defined as “[a]nything which is injurious to health, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, or unlawfully obstructs the free passage or use, in the customary manner, of any navigable lake, or river, bay, stream, canal, or basin, or any public park, square, street, or highway, ...” (Civ. Code, § 3479.) This requires consideration and balancing of a variety of factors. But where the law expressly declares something to be a nuisance, then no inquiry beyond its existence need be made; thus, its mere existence is said to be a nuisance per se. (Beck Dev. Co. v. S. Pac. Transportation Co., supra, 44 Cal.App.4th at pg.1207.)

 

Government Code sections 38771 and 38773.5 authorize cities to define those conditions deemed to constitute a public nuisance. Sections 1-2.01, 5-35.04, 8-1.05(c) of Huntington Park Municipal Code provide that any violation of the Huntington Park Municipal Code constitutes a public nuisance. (RJN ¶ 4, Ex. D [HPMC Sections].) Thus, the City only needs to establish that a single violation of the HPMC exists to prove its public nuisance claim. (Gov. Code, §§ 38771, 38773.5; Huntington Park Mun. Code, §§ 1-2.01, 5-35.04, 8-1.05(c); City of Costa Mesa v. Soffer (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 378, 382.)

 

Cross-Complainant alleges that Cross-Defendants have violated the following:

 

(a) HPMC sections 9-1.106(3) and 9-1.106(6), which prohibit any land, building, structure or premises in the City to be used, designed, moved, altered, added to, enlarged, or intended to be used for any purpose or in any manner other than a lawful use as listed in the HPMC;

(b) HPMC section 9-2.1003, which require City approval of a Development Permit for certain scopes of work and certain changes in use for certain types of structures;

(c) HPMC section 9-3.603, which prohibits the use of property for any purposes except those uses permitted in the applicable zoning district;

(d) HPMC section 9-3.604, which restricts changes to nonconforming uses and structures;

(e) HPMC section 9-3.611, which regulates the maintenance, repair, and alteration of nonconforming uses and structures;

(f) HPMC section 9-3.612, which permits an existing legal nonconforming use to be minimally expanded or changed subject to the granting of a Minor Conditional Use Permit and certain finding requirements;

(g) HPMC section 9-3.613, which automatically terminates the right to operate a nonconformity upon occurrence of certain conditions;

(h) HPMC section 9-3.614, which provides a process for legalization of an illegal nonconforming structure or use;

(i) Huntington Park Building Code sections 1.8.4.1 and 105.1, which require that a written construction permit shall be obtained from the City before the erection, construction, reconstruction, installation, moving or alteration of any building or structure; and

(j) Other violations, according to proof. (Cross-Compl., ¶ 33.)

 

According to the Cross-Complainant, the Subject Property is used for residential purposes without required zoning approvals. (See generally Martinez Decl.; see also generally Bollier Decl.) No development or conditional use permit has been granted by the City for the residential use of the Subject Property, which is required under the DTSP regulations. (Martinez Decl., ¶ 27.) The Subject Property also contains extensive building alterations completed without the required building permits. (See, e.g., Zapien Decl., ¶ 20, Ex. V [September 2022 Post-Inspection Diagram].) These unpermitted alterations include the following: (1) metal carport/cover patio located at the northwest end of the building; (2) laundry connections under the metal cover patio of the first floor; (3) shower in the permitted half bath on the first floor; (4) kitchen cabinets and sink in the first floor between the two businesses; (5) partition wall to the rear of one of the businesses on the first floor; (6) a partition wall to the rear of the second business on the first floor next to the half bath; (7) a full bath inside unit #1 on the second floor; (8) a shower in the half batch on the second floor; (9) partition wall inside unit #3 on the second floor; (10) partition wall inside unit #5 on the second floor; (11) metal bars in the east balcony area on the second floor, and (12) the first floor tenants spaces have been unlawfully connected by the unpermitted removal of the wall next to the full bath area. (Zapien Decl., ¶¶ 27(b); Bollier Decl., ¶¶ 12.)

 

          Preemption

 

In opposition, Cross-Defendants argue that the State Housing Element Law preempts the requirement to apply for a conditional use permit. Cross-Defendants also argue that the State of California regulates building construction and development and thus preempts the City of Huntington Park’s ordinances.

 

State law may occupy a field either expressly or by implication. (Candid Enters., Inc. v. Grossmont Union High Sch. Dist. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 878, 886.)  When the state law contains provisions that expressly declare the legislature’s intent to preempt local regulation, a local law will be invalidated unless it is found to regulate a different “field” of regulation. (Ibid.) In determining whether the legislature has impliedly occupied or preempted a field to the exclusion of local regulation, the court must look at “the whole purpose and scope of the legislative scheme.” (Candid Enters., Inc. v. Grossmont Union High Sch. Dist., supra, 39 Cal.3d at pg. 886.) An intent to occupy the field will be found in any one of the following circumstances:

 

(1) the subject matter has been so fully and completely covered by general law as to clearly indicate that it has become exclusively a matter of state concern;

(2) the subject matter has been partially covered by general law couched in such terms as to indicate clearly that a paramount state concern will not tolerate further or additional local action; or

(3) the subject matter has been partially covered by general law, and the subject is of such a nature that the adverse effect of a local ordinance on the transient citizens of the state outweighs the possible benefit to the municipality.

 

(In re Hubbard (1964) 62 Cal.2d 119, 128.)

 

Preemption will not be implied when the legislative scheme either permits or recognizes local regulation (People ex rel Deukmejian v. County  of Mendicino (1990) 36 Cal.3d 476, 485); also when there is a significant local interest that may differ from one locality to another (Fisher v. City of Berkeley (1984) 37 Cal.3d 644, 707; Gluck v County of L.A. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 121, 133); or

When there is a long tradition of local regulation and a legislatively imposed duty to preserve and protect the public health (People ex rel Deukmejian v. County of Mendicino, supra, 36 Cal.3d at 484).

 

Because a local law can be invalidated as preempted only when it regulates the same “field” in which the legislature has evinced its intent to occupy, either expressly or by implication, the way in which the relevant field is described is often crucial to the result: a narrow definition of the field limits the preemptive effect of state law, and a broad definition increases its preemptive reach. (Candid Enters., Inc. v Grossmont Union High Sch. Dist. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 878, 886 n4; Cal. Water & Tel. Co. v County of L. A. (1967) 253 Cal.App.2d 16, 27–28.) A local law may be found to regulate a separate field, and thus not be preempted by state law when it has a separate and independent purpose from the allegedly preemptive state law. (Santa Monica Pines, Ltd. v Rent Control Bd. (1984) 35 Cal.3d 858, 869. 

 

In defining a “field” for purposes of preemption, a court or a local legislative body can apply a patterned approach, i.e., a field should not be defined merely by uniting a series of statutes containing a common noun such as “rent” (see Fisher v. City of Berkeley (1984) 37 Cal.3d 644, 707); rather, an area of state legislation that is logically related and reflects a patterned approach to the subject constitutes a potentially preempted field. (Galvan v Super. Ct. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 851, 862.)

 

Recognizing that the availability of housing is vitally important to the state, the California legislature passed the housing element law to do the following: (a) assure that counties and cities recognize their responsibilities in contributing to the attainment of the state housing goal; (b) assure that counties and cities will prepare and implement housing elements which, along with federal and state programs, will move toward attainment of the state housing goal; (c) recognize that each locality is best capable of determining what efforts are required by it to contribute to the attainment of the state housing goal, provided such a determination is compatible with the state housing goal and regional housing needs; (d) ensure that each local government cooperates with other local governments in order to address regional housing needs. (Gov. Code., § 65580, 65581.)

 

Cross-Defendants’ preemption argument is faulty because the housing element law’s legislative scheme both permits and recognizes local regulation. There is no explicit preemption or implied preemption.

 

Cross-Defendants also argue that requiring conditional use violates the state housing element law, citing Government Code section 65583.2, subdivision (i). This law states that the local government's review of the owner-occupied or multifamily residential use may not require a conditional use permit. However, the law does not apply to the present circumstances because it is designed to address the development of a local government’s comprehensive, long-term general plan. (Martinez v. City of Clovis (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 193.)

 

Cross-Defendants further argue that Cross-Complainant violated the state’s certificate of occupancy requirements. But this is not relevant to the nuisance per se issue.

 

Thus, the City has made a sufficient showing of prevailing on the merits of the negligence per se cause of action.

 

Balancing of the Harm

 

When a governmental entity is suing to enjoin violation of a statute or ordinance that specifically authorizes injunctive relief and the government shows a “reasonable probability” of prevailing on the merits, a rebuttable presumption arises that the potential harm to the public outweighs the potential harm to the defendant. (IT Corp. v. County of Imperial (1983) 35 Cal.3d 63, 69.) 

 

Here, HPMC sections 5-35.18, 8-1.05, and 9-2.2407 together authorize the City to enforce any violation of the HPMC by seeking a court injunction. Thus, the rebuttable presumption arises.

 

The burden then shifts to the cross-defendants to prove that they would suffer “grave or irreparable harm” from issuance of the preliminary injunction. (IT Corp. v. County of Imperial, supra, 35 Cal.3d 63 at pg. 69.) Only if such harm can be shown will the court proceed to examine the relative actual harms to the parties. (Ibid.) 

 

Cross-Defendants conclusorily state that the seven tenants would experience irreparable harm. (Opp., pg. 12.) They have not established that they would suffer grave or irreparable harm.

 

Other

 

Cross-Defendants argue laches and res judicata. A long wait before filing suit or applying for injunctive relief may convince the trial court that there is no irreparable harm. (See Youngblood v. Wilcox (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 1368, 1376.) But Cross-Defendants do not argue how the City delayed in filing the cross-complaint or applying for injunctive relief. A judge in a civil case is not "'obligated to seek out theories [a party] might have advanced, or to articulate … that which … [a party] has left unspoken.'"  (Mesecher v. County of San Diego (1992) 9 Cal. App. 4th 1677, 1686.)

 

There is no support for adjudicating a res judicata argument in a preliminary injunction, and Cross-Defendants cite no support for it.

 

Bond

 

Because Cross-Complainant is a public entity, it does not need to provide an undertaking. (Code Civ. Proc., § 529, subd. (b)(3).)

 

Based on all the above, the motion for preliminary junction is GRANTED.
