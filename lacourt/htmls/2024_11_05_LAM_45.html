Case Number: 23STCV18258    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

RAVAND CONSTRUCTION, INC., a California Corporation; HASSAN MOHAMMADKHANIGHIYASVAND aka RAMIN GHIYASVAND, an individual,

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

ALEN AIR SYSTEMS, a California Corporation; ALEN MANOOKIAN, an individual; and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive,

 

                              Defendants.
Case No.: 23STCV18258

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed: 8/2/2023

[1st Amended Complaint Filed: N/A]

Trial Date: 6/30/2025

 

Hearing date: November 5, 2024

Moving Party: Defendant Madlen Manoukian

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Ravand Construction, Inc. and Hassan Mohammadkhanighiy Asvand

Defendant Madlen Manoukian’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint

The Court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Background

            This action arises from an alleged failure to repay three separate loans made by Plaintiffs Ravand Construction, Inc. (“Construction”) and Hassan Mohammadkhanighiy Asvand aka Ramin Ghiyasvand (“RG”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”). On August 2, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint against Defendants Alen Air Systems (“AAS”), Alen Manookian (“Defendant Alen”), and Does 1 through 50, inclusive, alleging the following causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) check fraud (Civ. Code § 1719); (3) fraud and deceit – intentional misrepresentation (erroneously spelled “decient”); (4) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (5) quantum meruit; (6) unjust enrichment (erroneously listed as the “fifth cause of action”); (7) an accounting (erroneously listed as the “fifth cause of action”); and (8) intentional infliction of emotional distress (erroneously listed as the “fifth cause of action”).

            On October 6, 2023, Plaintiffs filed two amendments to the complaint indicating that Doe 1’s true name is “Madlen Manoukian” and Doe 2’s true name is “Alem Manoukian Senior.”

            On December 21, 2023, Defendant Madlen Manoukian (“Defendant Madlen”) filed the instant demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint.

            On October 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition. And on October 24, 2024, Defendant replied.

 

Legal Standard

            “[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, the court “ ‘ “treat[s] the demurrer  as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law….” ’ ” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

            A party may also demur to a complaint where “[t]he pleading is uncertain,” meaning also that it is “ambiguous [or] unintelligible.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10 subd. (f).) The law encourages courts to liberally construe pleadings and disfavors demurrers for uncertainty. (Morris v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 279, 292.) That said, where the complaint does not properly distinguish between multiple defendants, such that it is not clear which factual allegations are made as to which parties, the complaint fails for ambiguity. (See Hawley Bros. Hardware Co. v. Brownstone (1899) 123 Cal. 643, 646-47.) Similarly, allegations made against multiple defendants are fatally uncertain where plaintiff alleges the same harm against multiple defendants but makes no effort to either apportion their liability or show why it cannot be apportioned in the complaint. (See Landau v. Salam (1971) 4 Cal.3d 901, 909.)

 

Discussion

            Defendant Madlen demurs to Plaintiffs’ Complaint on the grounds that (1) each cause of action alleges insufficient facts to state a valid claim against Defendant Madlen, and (2) each cause of action is uncertain or ambiguous as to Defendant Madlen. Here, the Court finds that the allegations of Plaintiffs’ Complaint are insufficient and uncertain as to Defendant Madlen because they fail to provide her with notice of the basis for her liability.

 

Plaintiffs’ Allegations are Insufficient to Establish Valid Claims Against Defendant Madlen

            Defendant Madlen contends that the allegations in Plaintiffs’ Complaint are insufficient to state a valid claim against her because the only allegations that could be construed as imputing liability against her would contradict other express allegations of the Complaint.

Plaintiffs contend that the properly pled allegations in the Complaint are sufficient to establish the causes of action against Defendant Madlen because the allegations against Defendant Alen are valid, and the Complaint, as alleged, makes the same allegations against Defendant Madlen as it does Defendant Alen. In their moving papers, Plaintiffs contend that they properly identified Defendant Madlen as a named defendant, by amending her into the Complaint, because Plaintiffs discovered that the fraudulent scheme perpetrated by Defendants was a family business. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant Alen received assistance from his father, Alen Manoukian Senior, and sister, Defendant Madlen, to execute his scheme to defraud Plaintiffs.

However, these allegations appear nowhere in the Complaint’s allegations. Instead, Plaintiff relies on various paragraphs of the Complaint identifying the parties to establish its claims against Defendant Madlen. First, Paragraph 9 of the Complaint states as follows:

The true names and capacities, whether individual, official, corporate, associate, or otherwise, of Defendants sued in this Complaint as DOES 1 to 50, inclusive, are unknown to Complainants, who sue such Defendants by such fictitious names. Each of the Defendants designated herein as a Doe is legally responsible in some manner for the unlawful acts referred to herein. Complainants will seek leave of Court to amend this Complaint to reflect the true names and capacities of the Defendants designated as Does when such identities become known.

(Ibid.)

Next, Plaintiff relies on Paragraph 10 of the Complaint, which states as follows:

At all time mentioned in this Complaint, Defendants were the agents, servants, employees, partners, joint venturers or representatives of each of the remaining Defendants, and were at all times acting in the course and scope of said agency, service, employment, partnership, joint venture or representation. Defendants ratified and approved the acts of said agents, servants, employees, partners, joint ventures, or representatives.

(Ibid.) 

And, finally, Paragraph 11 of the Complaint states as follows:

Plaintiff [sic] are informed and believe, and based thereon allege that, at all relevant times, Defendants, and each of them, were acting in concert or participation with each other, and/or were joint participants and collaborators in doing the acts complained of, and as such, each Defendant is jointly and severally liable to Complainants.

(Ibid.)

Plaintiffs contend that these paragraphs of the Complaint are proper basis to impute liability for each of the allegations against Defendant Alen and AAS to Defendant Madlen. However, this argument is non-meritorious. First, this argument contradicts certain express allegations of the Complaint, which explicitly state that Defendant Alen or AAS were the parties who performed certain acts, not all Defendants or Doe defendants. Paragraph 6 of the Complaint states that “Plaintiff [sic] are informed and believe and thereon allege that ALEN owns all the capital stock of ALEN AIR, and that ALEN controls, dominates, and manages ALEN AIR and that the two entities are one and the same person.” (Ibid.) This indicates that only the actions of Defendant Alen are directly imputable to AAS, or, conversely, that the actions of AAS are only directly imputable to Defendant Alen.

Further, Paragraph 7 also states that “Defendants ALEN, ALEN AIR shall be referred to collectively as ‘Defendant.’” Thus, the allegations of the Complaint common to all causes of action that generally refer to “Defendant,” by the express allegations of the Complaint, do not reach Defendant Madlen. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 15, 18, 20, 21, 22, 24, 25, 26, 27.)

And merely Doing in Defendant Madlen does not rectify this issue. As the Complaint explicitly states, “Complainants will seek leave of Court to amend this Complaint to reflect the true names and capacities of the Defendants designated as Does when such identities become known.” (Id., ¶ 9 (emphasis added).) Here, Plaintiffs’ amendments to the complaint merely identified the names of Alen Manoukian Senior and Defendant Madlen, but did not reflect the capacities of these Doe Defendants, or their liability for the allegations against the original named Defendants. Although Plaintiffs’ moving papers identify a potential basis for liability against Defendant Madlen, such contentions may not be considered as the basis for granting or denying relief in a demurrer as they are outside the allegations of the complaint. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co., supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.)

However, where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) The basis Plaintiffs identify for establishing liability against Defendant Madlen in their moving papers may be sufficient to establish liability against Defendant Madlen, by identifying her role in the fraudulent scheme perpetrated by Defendant Alen and AAS against Plaintiffs.

Accordingly, Defendant Madlen’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint based on insufficiency is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

            Plaintiffs’ Allegations are Uncertain or Ambiguous as to Defendant Madlen

            Defendant Madlen further contends that the allegations in Plaintiffs’ Complaint are uncertain or ambiguous because, without actually identifying which allegations are specifically made against Defendant Madlen, Plaintiffs do not provide proper notice of the basis for liability against Defendant Madlen.

            The Court notes, as Defendant Madlen concedes, demurrers for uncertainty are generally disfavored because the defects may be inconsequential, and any confusion may be possible to resolve through the liberal discovery process. (See Lickiss v. Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1125, 1135.) But as discussed above, a demurrer for uncertainty may lie where the failure to label parties and claims makes a pleading so defective that the opposing party is unable to determine what he or she is supposed to respond to. (See Hawley Bros. Hardware Co. v. Brownstone, supra, 123 Cal. at p. 646-47; Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

            Here, the Complaint is uncertain or ambiguous as to Defendant Madlen because the express allegations of the Complaint, as discussed above, limit the effect of the Plaintiffs’ allegations, and do not properly impute liability to Defendant Madlen. Plaintiffs’ Doing in of Defendant Madlen was improper because Plaintiffs’ amendment did not indicate the capacity of Defendant Madlen or the basis for attaching liability against her.

            However, a demurrer on the ground of improper joinder of parties is merely a pleading error, and typically should not be sustained without leave to amend when it appears that the defect can be cured by amendment and “removing mere uncertainties in the phraseology of expression of their causes of action.” (Kraft v. Smith, (1944) 24 Cal.2d 124, 132.) Here, as indicated above, the primary basis for granting Defendant Madlen’s demurrer to the instant Complaint is a contradiction in the phraseology of those allegations that would establish liability against Defendant Madlen. Further, Plaintiffs’ moving papers identify a reasonable basis for amending the Complaint that may be sufficient to establish its claims against Defendant Madlen that would not be uncertain or ambiguous.

            Accordingly, Defendant Madlen’s demurrer to the Plaintiffs’ Complaint based on uncertainty is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

            In all, Defendant Madlen Manoukian’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

            It is so ordered.

Motion for Charging Order Against Eugenio Gonzalez

The Court considered the moving papers. No opposition or reply was filed.

            The Judgement Creditor’s motion is GRANTED.

Background

            On June 5, Petitioners Tierra Holdings Co., LLC, FEM Ventures, LLC, and QBN Capital, LLC obtained a judgment from this Court against Respondents G2K Holdings, LLC, Eugenio Gonzalez, Monica Gonzalez, and Robert Knohl, jointly and severally, in the total sum of $3,279,720.93.

             

            On June 27, 2024, Petitioner FEM Ventures, LLC (“Judgment Creditor”) filed an Application for Appearance and Examination of Respondent Eugenio Gonzalez (“Gonzalez”) set for hearing on September 12, 2024. On that date, Gonzalez failed to appear. The Court ordered a bench warrant and set bail in the amount of $5,000. The hearing was continued to  October 17, 2024.

On October 2, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed this Motion for Charging Order against Gonzalez to satisfy the judgment.

On October 17, 2024, Gonzalez again failed to appear. The hearing was continued to November 20, 2024.

            No opposition or reply was filed as of October 29, 2024.

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.310 provides:¿ 

“If a money judgment is rendered against a partner or member but not against the partnership or limited liability company, the judgment debtor’s interest in the partnership or limited liability company may be applied toward the satisfaction of the judgment by an order charging the judgment debtor’s interest. . .”

Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.320, subdivision (a) provides:¿ 

“A lien on a judgment debtor's interest in a partnership or limited liability company is created by service of a notice of motion for a charging order on the judgment debtor and on either of the following: (2) All members or the limited liability company.”

Corporations Code, section 17705.03, subdivision (a) provides, in relevant part:¿¿ 

“On application by a judgment creditor of a member or transferee, a court may enter a charging order against the transferable interest of the judgment debtor for the unsatisfied amount of the judgment.¿¿A charging order constitutes a lien on a judgment debtor’s transferable interest and requires the limited liability company to pay over to the person to which the charging order was issued any distribution that would otherwise be paid to the judgment debtor.”

Discussion

On June 5, the Court entered a judgment confirming an arbitration award on this matter in the total sum of $3,279,720.93. (Bequer Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) As of the motion’s filing date, September 11, 2024, Respondents have not paid any portion of the judgment. (Mot., 3:5.) Petitioner/Judgment Creditor FEM Ventures, LLC moves the Court to issue a charging order against Respondent/Judgment Debtor Gonzalez’s transferable interest in two limited liability companies (LLCs).

The Court finds that Judgment Creditor has served the Gonzalez and the two LLCs with the Notice of Motion for Charging Order and filed proof of service with the Court in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure, section 708.320, subdivision (a).

Judgment Creditor has identified two limited liability companies in which Gonzalez owns an interest: Sunflower Apartments, LLC (“Sunflower”) and Avalon Cove Communities, LLC (“Avalon”). Prior to the entry of judgment, Gonzalez gave Benjamin Bequer, principal of Judgment Creditor, a property/asset listing schedule. (Bequer Decl., ¶ 3.) Sunflower and Avalon were listed on the schedule as entities that Gonzalez owns an interest in and owns property through. (Ibid.) The California Secretary of State’s website indicates that Sunflower and Avalon both have Matthew Sullivan listed as their agent. (Bequer Decl., ¶¶ 4-6, Exhs. B, C.) Further, both entities have the same mailing address. (Bequer Decl., ¶ 6.) Such commonalities indicate common ownership by Gonzalez. (Ibid.) The Court finds Judgment Creditor has established Gonzalez’s interest in the two limited liability companies.

Conclusion

            The Court GRANTS the Motion for Charging Order.

            It is so ordered.

Motion for Order for Discharge and Payment of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs  

 

The court has considered the moving papers. No opposition was filed.

The motion is DENIED without prejudice.

Background

            On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff Universal Bank (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint in Interpleader against Defendants Idabel Guzman Domantay (“Domantay”), Kemar Porteous (“Porteous”), Shannon Griffith (“Griffith”), Neville Lewis (“Lewis”), Monique Maniquiz Busby (“Busby”), Lydia Rigor (“Rigor”), and Adult Protective Services of Los Angeles County (“APS”) (collectively, “Defendants”).  

            Plaintiff alleges that it is a Federal Savings Bank that holds an account for Defendant Domantay. (Complaint, ¶ 1.) Defendant Domantay is the account holder and is a victim of elder financial abuse. (Complaint, ¶ 2.) Defendants Porteous, Griffith, and Lewis were all recipients of cashier’s checks from Defendant Domantay’s account. (Complaint, ¶¶ 3-5.) Defendant Busby is the niece of Defendant Domantay. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) Defendant Rigor is the sister-in-law of Defendant Domantay. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) A dispute has arisen between the Defendants with respect to the funds in the account. (Complaint, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff is holding the sum of $198,151.51 in Defendant Domantay’s account, as of June 25, 2024. (Complaint, ¶ 10.) Plaintiff cannot resolve the conflicting demands and, therefore, is unable to determine to whom the $198,151.51 should rightfully be delivered. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges that it has no interest in the funds except for attorneys’ fees and expenses incurred in filing the complaint. (Complaint, ¶ 13.)

            On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Order for Discharge and Payment of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs, which was unopposed.

            On September 26, 2024, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Defendant APS was dismissed from the complaint with prejudice.

            On October 3, 2024, after hearing, the Court denied Plaintiff’s Motion for Order for Discharge and Payment of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs without prejudice. (See 10/03/24 Minute Order.)

            On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Order for Discharge and Payment of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs.  

            As of October 24, 2024, the motion is unopposed. Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

Legal Standard

An interpleader action is initiated when a party, who has no interest in the disputed funds or property but is subject to conflicting claims, deposits those funds or property with the court and seeks to compel the claimants to litigate their claims among themselves. (Tri-State, Inc. v. Long Beach Community College Dist. (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 224, 227 (Tri-State).) This is done to prevent multiple lawsuits and double vexation. (Principal Life Ins. Co. v. Peterson (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 676, 682.)

The interpleader process involves a two-step procedure. (City of Morgan Hill v. Brown (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1114, 1126 (Morgan Hill).) The court first determines whether the plaintiff has the right to interplead the funds or property. (Id. at p. 1126-27.)  “[I]f that right is sustained, an interlocutory decree is entered with requires the defendants to interplead and litigate their claims to the funds.” (Virtanen v. O’Connell (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 688, 698.) “Second, it if it is so determined, then the court will discharge the plaintiff from liability and the action may proceed for the determination of the rights of the various claimants to the property which is then in the custody of the court.” (Morgan Hill, supra, 71 Cal.App.4th 1114 at p. 1127.) “The effect of such an order is to preserve the fund, discharge the stakeholder from further liability, and to keep the fund in the court’s custody until the rights of the potential claimants of the monies can be adjudicated.” (Virtanen v. O’Connell, supra, 140 Cal.App.4th 688 at p. 698.)

“Where the only relief sought against one of the defendants is the payment of a stated amount of money alleged to be wrongfully withheld, such defendant may, upon affidavit that he is a mere stakeholder with no interest in the amount or any portion thereof and that conflicting demands have been made upon him for the amount by parties to the action, upon notice to such parties, apply to the court for an order discharging him from liability and dismissing him from the action on his depositing with the clerk of the court the amount in dispute and the court may, in its discretion, make such order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386.5.) An interpleader plaintiff must follow all requirements of Code of Civil Procedure sections 386 and 386.5. (Hood v. Gonzales (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 57, 81-82.)

 

 

 

Discussion

            Discharge from Interpleader Action

            Plaintiff has met the statutory requirements to be released from the current interpleader action. Plaintiff has a right to interplead pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 386. (See CCP § 386(b) (“Any person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom double or multiple claims are made, or may be made, by two or more persons which are such that they may give rise to double or multiple liability, may bring an action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims.”).) Plaintiff has established no interest in the funds. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff has also deposited the disputed funds under Code of Civil Procedure section 386(c). (Complaint, ¶ 13.)

Plaintiff has named all defendants in this action in its complaint and served Defendants Domantay, Busby, and Rigor. (Mot. for Discharge, Proof of Service.) However, Plaintiff has not served Defendants Porteous, Griffith, or Lewis with either the complaint or the instant motion. Plaintiff alleges that it does not have sufficient information to have them served. (Mot. for Discharge, p. 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel, Anthony Rothman (“Rothman”), declares that on October 7, 2024, a request for dismissal was filed for Defendants Porteous, Griffith, and Lewis. (Rothman Decl., ¶ 4.) Such defendants were perpetrators of the elder abuse Defendant Domantay. (Rothman Decl., ¶ 4.) Mr. Rothman states that “[t]heir addresses and whereabouts are unknown, and were unable to be uncovered by an attorney service. It was determined the best course of action was to dismiss these parties.” (Rothman Decl., ¶ 4.)

Based on a review of the register of actions in this matter, the Court notes that dismissal was not entered as to Defendants Porteous, Griffith, and Lewis. The lack of dismissal was due to errors on the request for dismissal form. It follows that Defendants Porteous, Griffith, and Lewis are still parties to this action. As such, those defendants are entitled to notice of this action and the instant motion. “[D]ue process requires a party be fully advised of the issues to be addressed and be given adequate notice of what facts it must rebut in order to prevail.” (Fenn v. Sherriff (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1481.)

            As such, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion for discharge from liability without prejudice.

            Attorneys’ Fees and Costs

            “A party to an action who follows the procedure set forth in Section 386 or 386.5 may insert in his motion, petition, complaint, or cross complaint a request for allowance of his costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred in such action.” (CCP § 386.6(a).) “In ordering the discharge of such party, the court may, in its discretion, award such party his costs and reasonable attorney fees from the amount in dispute which has been deposited with the court.” (CCP § 386.6(a).)

            Mr. Rothman states that his hourly rate is $460.00 per hour and that he “will have billed 21 hours, including drafting the new version of this motion.” (Rothman Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff is requesting $10,385.00 in fees and costs. (Mot. for Discharge, p. 5.) Given that Plaintiff cannot be discharged at this time due to the lack of service on all interested parties, the Court finds that it is inappropriate to award Plaintiff its fees and costs at this time under CCP § 386.6(a).

            The Court therefore DENIES Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees and costs without prejudice.    

It is so ordered.
