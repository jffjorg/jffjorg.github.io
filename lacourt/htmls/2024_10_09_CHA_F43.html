DEPARTMENT F43 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21CHCV00215    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: F43

MOTION TO DISMISS THE CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiffs Ryan Canning and Patricia Bogarin

RESPONDING PARTY: No response has been filed.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Plaintiffs have requested that the Court dismiss Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint for failure to prosecute.

 

RULING: Motion to dismiss is denied without prejudice.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On July 2, 2024, Plaintiffs Ryan Canning and Patricia Bogarin (Plaintiffs) filed a motion to dismiss Defendant and Cross-Complainant Shawn Kerwin’s (Kerwin) cross-complaint for failure to prosecute. The only argument in Plaintiffs’ motion is that the cross-complaint should be dismissed because Kerwin filed for bankruptcy in the Eastern District of California on or about November 2023, and this action was stayed as result. Plaintiffs provided no authority that would allow them to dismiss Kerwin’s case for failure to prosecute in such an instance.

 

The Court will also note that on May 22, 2024, this Court found that Kerwin’s bankruptcy had been discharged and the stay on the case was lifted. A case management conference was scheduled for November 5, 2024. Additionally, in his status report filed prior to that hearing, Kerwin indicated that he wishes to proceed on his cross-complaint against Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs’ July 2 motion makes no mention of this.

 

On July 10, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended motion to dismiss. In that motion, Plaintiffs indicated that at the hearing on May 22, this Court requested Plaintiffs’ counsel to address the issues of preemption and standing by the bankruptcy court in a written noticed motion. In this motion, Plaintiffs also cite several cases indicating that the cross-complaint should be dismissed because the bankruptcy trustee had taken control of the case, but none of these cases cited by Plaintiffs are binding upon this Court.

 

It appears, perhaps, that Plaintiffs are attempting to argue that Kerwin no longer has standing in this case and that his standing now belongs to the bankruptcy trustee. However, that is not clear from their motion, nor have they supported their motion with any binding authority. Plaintiffs have also not addressed what effect, if any, the discharge of Kerwin’s bankruptcy has on his ability to proceed.

 

Accordingly, the Court denies the motion to dismiss without prejudice.

 

CONCLUSION

The motion to dismiss is denied without prejudice.

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 21STCV28908    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: F43

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

MOVING PARTY: Attorneys for Defendant PDQ Pickup, LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: No response has been filed.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Attorneys for Defendant are requesting to be relieved as counsel.

 

RULING: Motion to be relieved as counsel is granted

 

Nannina L. Angioni and Sarah A. Swanson of Kaedian LLP, counsel of record for Defendant PDQ Pickup, LLC, seek to be relieved as counsel for that party.¿Counsel filed a motion with the required Forms MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053. Counsel’s declaration states that there has been a breakdown in the attorney client relationship and that Defendant’s counsel are no longer able to work with and communicate with their client on this case and that the client has refused to pay invoices or communicate with its attorneys. The client has also not provided any contact information for any other representatives besides Jacob A. Sanchez.

 

Absent a showing of resulting prejudice, an attorney’s request for withdrawal should be granted.¿(People v. Prince¿(1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406.)¿There is no showing of prejudice, and counsel’s motion complies with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362 and meets its requirements.

 

However, the Court will note that corporations may not represent themselves. (Merco Constr. Engineers, Inc. v. Municipal Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 724.) Defendant PDQ Pickup, LLC, should immediately find new counsel if it has not already done so.

 

Motion to be relieved as counsel is granted.

 

Moving party to give notice.

SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Granada Hills Charter

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs Iris Arnold, et al.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication

 

RULING: Motion is granted in part and denied in part.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On April 22, 2022, Plaintiffs Iris Arnold, et al. (Plaintiffs) filed their original complaint. Plaintiffs filed this action because they were fired from their jobs at Defendant Granada Hills Charter (Defendant). Plaintiffs were teachers and other employees at Defendant’s school. Plaintiffs were terminated because they refused to get the COVID-19 vaccine as required by LAUSD.

 

In the fall of 2021, Defendant required all of its employees to get the COVID-19 vaccine in order to keep its school open and prevent outbreaks of the virus that might force the school to close again. Defendant’s administrators were concerned that if they did not enforce this mandate, then they could lose their charter based on the terms of that charter. Defendant provided an interactive process by which employees who wanted an exemption from the mandate could provide information to the administration for consideration. The five Plaintiffs in this case used that process. However, during that process Defendant determined that Plaintiffs’ essential job functions required them to be in person on Defendant’s campus. Defendant determined that because of the terms of the vaccine mandate, it could not allow Plaintiffs to be on campus without being vaccinated, so Plaintiffs were terminated. Defendant also determined that any attempts to accommodate Plaintiffs’ requests would lead to undue hardship for Defendant because it would either require Defendant to hire more employees if Plaintiffs attempted to work remotely or were placed on leave, or it could lead to Defendant losing its charter if they were on campus without vaccinations.

 

Defendant argues in its motion for summary judgment that the submissions by Plaintiffs to Defendant confirmed that Plaintiffs cannot make a prima facie case of religious or disability/medical discrimination. Defendant also argues that Plaintiffs cannot seek emotional distress damages because four of the five Plaintiffs signed a release of these claims in exchange for payment in the settlement of Worker’s Compensation claims that they had filed against Defendant. Plaintiffs oppose Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

 

Because there was more evidence and testimony that the parties wished to gather, the Court reset this MSJ hearing for October 10 and set a supplemental briefing schedule. The parties addressed the additional evidence and testimony in their supplemental briefs.

ANALYSIS

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) “On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; see also CCP § 437c(c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.” (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

Issue 1

Defendant argues that the First Cause of Action for religious discrimination in violation of California’s FEHA fails as a matter of law because Plaintiffs have failed to show a sincerely held religious belief that conflicts with Defendant’s vaccination policy and because Defendant could not reasonably accommodated Plaintiffs without undue hardship.

 

When evaluating whether an employer failed to accommodate an employee’s religious beliefs, first “the employee must establish a prima facie case that he or she had bona fide religious belief, of which the employer was aware that conflicts with an employment requirement.” (Soldinger v. Northwest Airlines, Inc. (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 345, 370.) “Once the employee establishes a prima facie case, then the employer must establish it initiated good faith efforts to accommodate or no accommodation was possible without producing undue hardship.” (Id.)

 

In this case, there is no dispute that the majority of the Plaintiffs told Defendant that they had bona fide religious beliefs that conflicted with the vaccine requirement. Because questioning the sincerity of beliefs is fraught with potential bias, it is presumed as a matter of law that religious beliefs are sincere. (Friedman v. Southern Cal. Permanent Medical Group (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 39, 69.) Therefore, it is not for the Court to question whether Plaintiffs’ beliefs were sincere. Furthermore, there is no dispute that Defendant was aware of Plaintiffs’ beliefs when Plaintiffs explained their reasons for not getting the COVID-19 vaccine. In fact, Plaintiffs described their beliefs to Bauer three years ago, and Bauer testified that he did not have any reason to doubt Plaintiffs’ sincerity.

 

Also, though Defendant argues that it is relevant that Plaintiffs’ religions do not require them to abstain from the vaccine, it does not matter whether everyone in Plaintiffs’ particular religious sects hold the same beliefs because the U.S. Supreme Court has held that “a person’s belief can be religious even if it is not shared by all of the members of [their] religious sect.” (Thomas v. Review Bd. of Ind. Emp’t Sec. Div. (1981) 450 U.S. 707, 715-716 (quotations omitted).)

 

The issue then becomes whether Defendant has established that it initiated good faith efforts to accommodate Plaintiffs and that no accommodations were possible without undue hardship to Defendant. “The extent of undue hardship on the employer’s business is at issue only where the employer claims that it is unable to offer any reasonable accommodation without such hardship.” (Soldinger, 51 Cal.App.4th at 371 (quotations omitted).) The law “requires that an employer reasonably accommodate an employee’s practice of religion, not merely that it assess the reasonableness of a particular possible accommodation or accommodations.” (Groff v. DeJoy (2023) 600 U.S. 447, 473.)

 

As to whether the accommodations that were proposed by Plaintiffs and rejected by Defendant would have placed an undue hardship on Defendant, as will be discussed, the only one that would have certainly caused a hardship is allowing the Plaintiffs to work on-campus.

 

Defendant has argued that the LAUSD mandate required all on-campus employees of LAUSD schools, including charter schools, to be vaccinated. In response, Plaintiffs presented evidence that another area charter school, Vaughn, allowed staff with religious objections to vaccinations to work in person during the 2021-2022 school year while testing weekly for COVID-19. (PAOE 232.) That being said, Defendant submitted a declaration from the CEO of Vaughn with its reply indicating that Vaughn did not authorize any unvaccinated employees to enter or access LAUSD-owned property while the vaccine policy was operative. (Ramirez Decl., ¶ 4.) The deposition testimony of Ramirez submitted with the parties’ supplemental briefing confirms that Vaughn did not authorize unvaccinated employees to work on campus because of its charter with LAUSD. It appears that there was distinction between Vaughn’s LAUSD-owned campus and a separate, privately owned Vaughn campus that may have caused this difference in testimony. No unvaccinated employees were allowed on Vaughn’s LAUSD-owned campus.

 

Furthermore, the Chief Human Resources Officer for LAUSD, Dr. Francisco Serrato, stated in his September 5 deposition that unvaccinated charter school employees could not remain on campus even with accommodations. (Roth Decl., Ex. A, pp. 57:10-58:1.) When asked whether an on-campus accommodations that involved masking, social distancing, and weekly COVID testing could have been granted, he explicitly answer no because on-campus employees were required to be vaccinated. (Roth Decl., Ex A, pp. 40:12-41:8.)

 

However, Serrato also said that this did not prohibit charter schools from granting other accommodations with respect to the vaccine mandate for qualified employees. (Roth Decl., Ex. A., p. 34:2-17.) This is in line with Serrato’s Declaration, submitted with Plaintiffs’ opposition, which states that “The District expected independent charter schools to comply with all applicable laws in providing reasonable accommodations to their qualified employees…Additionally, the District never prohibited any independent charter school from providing reasonable accommodations to its qualified employees, nor did the District ever prohibit independent charter school employees from participating in their employer’s reasonable accommodation process. (Serrato Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.) All of these things are evidence for the finder of fact to weigh when deciding whether Defendant could have provided reasonable accommodations for Plaintiffs that did not involve them being on campus.

 

Based on the testimony of Ramirez and Serrato, the question in this case becomes whether Defendant could have made some kind of remote accommodations or other accommodations for Plaintiffs, rather than taking the extreme measure of terminating their employment. This is a question of fact for the finder of fact to weigh and not appropriate for a motion for summary judgment, as Defendant has not presented evidence that the off-campus options would produce significant hardship for it. (See Ruiz v. Paradigmworks Grp., Inc. (S.D. Cal. Jan. 13, 2020) No. 16-CV-2993-CAB-BGS, 2020 WL 133905, at *4.) Instead, Defendant only offers speculation of what might have happened had it tried to accommodate Plaintiffs.

 

Defendant simply decided that people should work in-person. Plaintiffs’ original opposition mentions remote work or administrative leave as potential alternatives to working on-campus. (Plaintiffs’ Opposition, p. 26, fn. 5; Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement ¶¶ 29, 30, 38, 86.) Those types of accommodations would have been possible under LAUSD’s rules. (Roth Decl., Ex. A., p. 34:2-17.) Accordingly, whether Defendant could have made other accommodations for Plaintiffs that did not involve them remaining on-campus remains a question for the finder of fact. The purpose of the interactive process is to find an arrangement that works for all parties. Even if Plaintiffs would have preferred to work on-campus and Defendant would have preferred for them to be there and vaccinated, there could potentially have been a compromise that did not involve the termination of Plaintiffs’ employment.

 

While the other Plaintiffs’ reasons for not getting the vaccine were religious, Plaintiffs Beckman and Olczak’s primary basis for not getting the vaccine was their medical conditions, though Beckman also cited religions reasons in her deposition and declaration. For Olczak, her reasons were almost entirely medical and were not based on the teachings of any church or religion. (UMF 64.) Her decision not to get the vaccine was based on her personal beliefs about vaccination, not on any religious beliefs. (Crochetiere Decl., Ex. A., 20:7-16.) Based on these statements from Olczak, Olczak cannot claim religious discrimination as a matter of law because her decision to not get the vaccine was not based on any religious beliefs or convictions.

 

Accordingly, there are triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant could have made off-campus accommodations for Plaintiffs’ religious beliefs. Defendant’s motion is denied on this issue for most of the Plaintiffs, but it is granted for Olczak.

 

Issue 2

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs Olczak and Beckman’s Second Cause of Action for disability discrimination in violation of FEHA fails as a matter of law because they have not established a disability or qualifying medical condition and because Defendant could not reasonably accommodate them without undue hardship.

 

An employer cannot fire somebody because of her “medical condition ….” (Gov. Code § 12940(a).) FEHA requires that employers “engage in a timely, good faith, interactive process with the employee or applicant to determine effective reasonable accommodations, if any, in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee or applicant with a known physical or mental disability or known medical condition.” (Gov. Code § 12940(n).)

 

“‘Medical condition’ means … (1) Any health impairment related to or associated with a diagnosis of cancer or a record or history of cancer.” (Gov. Code § 12926(i)(1).) California courts have held that “medical condition” as used in FEHA covers “any health impairment related to or associated with a diagnosis of cancer, for which a person has been rehabilitated or cured, based on competent medical evidence.” (Gosvener v. Coastal Corp. (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 805, 812-813 (emphasis added) (quotations omitted).) FEHA covers both cancer and other health impairments not related to cancer. (See Jensen v. Wells Fargo Bank (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 245, 257.)

 

Despite Plaintiffs Beckman and Olczak claiming that they could not take the COVID-19 vaccine due to their particular medical conditions, a skin condition and prior cancer respectively, these Plaintiffs were fired. Defendant is claiming that there was no disability discrimination because Plaintiffs have not established a disability or qualifying medical condition. However, it should be noted that at the time, Plaintiff’s provided medical records, including doctors’ notes, to support their requests for disability accommodations. (PAOE, 67, 164.)

 

Rather than present medical evidence, Defendant has questioned the veracity and seriousness of Plaintiffs’ claimed conditions and has argued that as a matter of law, it had no duty to even consider accommodating the Plaintiffs’ conditions. That does not change the fact that Defendant fired Plaintiffs despite their claimed medical impairments that they believed were preventing them from getting the vaccine.

 

Whether Defendant’s firing of Plaintiffs amounted to disability discrimination because they did not want to get the vaccine because of their medical conditions is a question of fact for the jury. Defendant’s motion is denied for this issue.

 

Issue 3

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs Olczak and Beckman’s Third Cause of Action for failure to engaged in the interactive process fails as a matter of law because there were not available reasonable accommodations for any of the Plaintiffs.

 

Similar to the religious discrimination cause of action, no accommodations were made for Plaintiffs’ claimed medical conditions. Defendant has also not produced any evidence that engaging in the interactive process or accommodating Plaintiffs’ medical conditions would have caused it undue hardship.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion is denied for this issue.

 

Issue 4

Defendant argues that the claims by Plaintiffs Olczak, Beckman, Arnold, and Karapetyan for emotional distress damages fail as a matter of law because these Plaintiffs released Defendant for these damages in their worker’s compensation settlements with Defendant.

 

Plaintiffs argue in their opposition that Defendant cannot raise this issue for the first time in its motion for summary judgment. However, Defendant cites a case in its reply that demonstrates that courts generally have allowed an affirmative defense to be asserted for the first time in a motion for summary judgment “absent a showing of prejudice.” (Nieto v. Blue Shield of California Life & Health Ins. Co. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 60, 75.) “Given the long-standing California court policy of exercising liberality in permitting amendments to pleadings at any stage of the proceedings ... we believe that a party should be permitted to introduce [a] defense ... in a summary judgment procedure so long as the opposing party has adequate notice and opportunity to respond.” (Cruey v. Gannett Co. (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 356.)

 

Given that case law, the Court will allow Defendant to make this argument in its summary judgment motion.

 

As for the merits of Defendant’s argument, in 2004, the California Supreme Court clarified that “the standard language of the preprinted form used in settling workers’ compensation claims releases only those claims that are within the scope of the workers’ compensation system, and does not apply to claims asserted in separate civil actions.” (Claxton v. Waters (2004) 34 Cal.4th 367, 376.) To release the other claims, the parties must “execute another document expressing that agreement.” (Id. at 378). And they must demonstrate their intention “in clear and non-technical language.” (Id.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that no separate agreement exists, and the only one Defendant can point to is Addendum A to the pre-printed release form. However, courts have rejected arguments based on similar addenda before. (See Camacho v. Target Corp. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 291, 312 (reversing summary judgment on this issue based on similar addendum).) Moreover, each release executed by the plaintiffs here states specifically that they were not releasing any claims related to the facts being litigated in this action. (PAOE 44, 50, 96, 134, 196, 200.)

 

Defendant argues in its reply that the workers’ compensation settlements broadly release Defendant “from all claims and causes of action, whether now known or ascertained or which may hereafter arise or develop as a result of the above-referenced injury(ies).” (UMF 47-54.) Defendant further argues that this broad language defeats Plaintiffs’ after-the-fact effort to claim that they were only releasing clams for emotional distress before a certain date. (POAE 13, 56 -57, 103-104, 146.)

 

The only carve-out in the releases was for “Claims outside the jurisdiction of the WCAB,” and Defendant argues that it is not contending that the release precludes Plaintiffs from asserting a discrimination claim in Superior Court. The issue thus is whether Plaintiffs carved-out their right to seek emotional distress damages a second time here, after Plaintiffs agreed that claims for that injury were within the WCAB’s jurisdiction. Defendant argues that there is no such carve-out in the releases. (See Jefferson v. California Dept. of Youth Authority (2002) 28 Cal.4th 299, 303-304 (“We have been particularly rigorous about strictly releasing broad release language in workers’ compensation settlements, because, in that context, WCAB oversight helps ensure fairness”).)

 

However, it should be noted that in the worker’s compensation releases, they were for specific dates and were for injuries “arising out of and in the course of employment.” (See, e.g., Vu Decl., Ex. E, Arnold Release.) The emotional damages in Plaintiffs’ current complaint appear to be for all emotional damages that Plaintiffs have allegedly suffered, including those that they have suffered after their employment was terminated. (See Plaintiffs’ Fourth Amended Comp.) The worker’s compensation settlements were only for injuries arising during the course of their employment, while their current complaint and claim for damages is for emotional damages arising from their terminations.

 

Because Plaintiffs’ claim for emotional damages is for damages beyond those covered by their worker’s compensation settlements, summary judgment is not appropriate. Defendant’s motion is denied for this issue.

 

Conclusion

The motion for summary judgment is denied for all issues except the religious discrimination issue, which is granted as to Plaintiff Olczak only.

 

Trial remains set for November 4, 2024.
