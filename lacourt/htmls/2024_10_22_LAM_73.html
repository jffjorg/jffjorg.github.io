DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV11579    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: 73

10/22/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

TRAVELERS CASUALTY AND SURETY COMPANY OF AMERICA v. VSD LIVING TRUST, et al.

(23STCV11579) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Carleton Burch (Anderson, McPharlin & Conners LLP) 

Counsel for Defendant:  None

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 9/30/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff Travelers Casualty and Surety Company of America filed the instant action against Defendant VSD Living Trust, a living trust organized under the laws of the State of California; Gintaras Vilutis, an individual; Gintaras Vilutis, as trustee of the VSD Living Trust; and V Scape Design, a California Corporation.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.     Conversion

2.     Unjust Enrichment - Restitution

3.     Money Had and Received

 

Plaintiff alleges that this action was brought to recover funds that were wire transferred to Defendant’s bank account by mistake to which Defendant was not entitled and Defendant has refused to return the funds. (Complaint, ¶ 1.)

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Request for Default Judgment. Plaintiff seeks judgment against Defendant in the principal amount of $1,075,908.48, costs of $3,061.80, plus pre-judgment interest at the annual rate of 7% for the period of May 26, 2021 to August 9, 2024, for a total of $241,612.43 in pre-judgment interest, for a total judgment amount of $1,320,582.71.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.     Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.     Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                      Yes 

2.     Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                    Yes 

3.     Declaration of non-military status for each defendant                     Yes 

4.     Summary of the case                                                                          Yes 

5.     585(d) declarations/admissible evidence in support                        Yes 

6.     Exhibits (as necessary)                                                                       Yes 

7.     Interest computation (as necessary)                                                 Yes 

8.     Cost memorandum                                                                            Yes

9.     Request for attorney fees (Local Rule 3.214)                                     N/A

 

Damages Summary: 

 

Damages 

$ 1,075,908.48 

Punitive Damages  

 N/A

Interest 

$ 241,612.43

Attorneys’ fees 

 N/A

Costs 

$ 3,061.80     

TOTAL 

$ 1,320,582.71   

Other relief requested 

 N/A

 

 

Here, Plaintiff complied with all requirements for a default judgment.

 

Plaintiff submitted a summary of the case. According to Plaintiff, Defendants have refused to return funds that they are not entitled to and that were inadvertently transferred to Defendants’ bank account. II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.  In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

A defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also, Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) 

 

“The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But… the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id., at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action . . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and “avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

  

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

D.     First and Second Causes of Action: Retaliation and Wrongful Termination

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the first and second causes of action for retaliation and wrongful termination. Defendant argues that both causes of action fail because: (1) Plaintiff was terminated for good cause and (2) Plaintiff cannot establish a nexus between the alleged protected activity and his termination.

 

Section 1102.5(c) states, “An employer…shall not retaliate against an employee for refusing to participate in an activity that would result in a violation of state or federal statute, or a violation of or noncompliance with a local, state, or federal rule or regulation.” (Cal. Lab. Code § 1102.5(c).) Where a plaintiff alleges violation of 1102.5(c), she must establish that an actual violation of law would result from an activity to prove retaliation under § 1102.5(c). (Ibid.) An employee’s reasonable but incorrect suspicion of illegality is not sufficient; the activity must actually result in the violation of a statute, rule, or regulation. (Nejadian v. County of Los Angeles, 40 Cal. App. 5th 703, 719 (2019).) The employee must identify the specific activity in which the employee refused to participate, and what specific statute, rule, or regulation would have been violated. (Id.)

 

Once an employee makes a prima facie case under section 1102.5, the employer must do more than show it had a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse employment action. It must, per section 1102.6, “demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.” (Vatalaro v. Cnty. of Sacramento (2022) 79 Cal. App. 5th 367, 383.) And, as the Lawson court explained, the employee need not “show that the employer's nonretaliatory reason was pretextual.” (Ibid.)

“The elements of a claim for wrongful discharge in violation of public policy are (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employer terminated the plaintiff’s employment, (3) the termination was substantially motivated by a violation of public policy, and (4) the discharge caused the plaintiff harm.  It is well established that a termination premised on an employee’s refusal to violate either a statute or an administrative regulation may support a claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.”  (Nosal-Tabor v. Sharp Chula Vista Medical Center (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1224, 1234, cleaned up.)

 

The Complaint alleges that that Defendant did not provide proper meal and rest breaks to their employees. Plaintiff reported that he was unable to take meal and rest breaks to his supervisors, but Plaintiff’s supervisors instructed him to change his time records to show that he was taking meal breaks. Plaintiff alleges he was wrongfully terminated in retaliation for complaining about not being able to take meal breaks and having to falsify his time records.

 

Defendant argues that even if Plaintiff had not complained about the alleged unlawful conduct, Plaintiff would have been terminated nevertheless for good cause. Defendant asserts that it implemented and enforced its written Mobile Device Distracted Driving Policy (“Distracted Driving Policy”). (Okum Decl. ¶ 3, Distracted Driving Policy. Exh. 2.) Under the Distracted Driving Policy, if employees like Plaintiff took their personal phones into the field with them on their service calls, they were required to download software (“TRUCE”) onto the phone which blocked incoming and outgoing calls while drivers were driving. (Okum Decl. ¶ 3; Distracted Driving Policy, Exh. 2 at 1-2; Perez Depo., 18:2-7.) The Distracted Driving Policy provides in relevant part:

 

“Disciplinary measures within a rolling 12 months

First occurrence          Written Corrective Action

Second occurrence     Termination.”

 

(Okum Decl. ¶ 3; Distracted Driving Policy, Exh. 2 at 2.) Defendant asserts that Plaintiff did not comply with the Distracted Driving Policy because the TRUCE application was not installed and working on Plaintiff’s phone. (Okum Decl. ¶ 5.) Branch Manager Benjamin Okum issued Plaintiff two written warnings, on May 13, 2022 and June 2, 2022, for noncompliance with the policy. (Okum Decl. ¶¶ 6-7; Exh. 3.) Despite these warnings, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff still failed to download the TRUCE app to comply with the policy. (Okum Decl. ¶ 7.) On June 10, 2022 Okum met with Plaintiff and told him he was being terminated because of his failure to comply with the Distracted Driver Policy. (Perez Depo., 11:22-12:1; Okum Decl. ¶ 7.) In support, Defendant cites to Okum’s declaration and Plaintiff’s deposition testimony. Plaintiff concedes that he was issued warnings for failure to comply with the policy and that Okum told him the reason for his termination was the failure to comply with the policy. (Perez Depo., 15:15-17:25; 28:21-29:4; 11:22-12:1.) The Court finds that Defendant has carried its burden in establishing that it had good cause to terminate Plaintiff. Therefore, the burden shifts to Plaintiff.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues he has established a prima facie case of retaliation. Plaintiff states that when he complained to supervisor Fonseca about missing meal periods and being instructed to change time records, Fonseca simply told Mr. Perez that “it is what it is.” (Perez Decl. ¶ 5.) Supervisor Valdez told Plaintiff to simply “get the stops done” and ignored Plaintiff’s complaints. (Perez Decl. ¶ 5.) Plaintiff also states he complained to Okum on a weekly basis about having to miss meal periods and falsify time sheets, Okum told Plaintiff that he simply could not put down that he did not take a meal break. (Perez Dep., 37:25-38:4.) Plaintiff further states that after complaining about meal breaks and having to falsify time records, Fonseca and Valdez began to retaliate against Plaintiff by demanding that he spend less time at stops and get more stops done on his route. (Perez Depo., 61:2-24; Perez Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff states that even though he explained to Valdez that repairs take time, and reiterated to Valdez that he wanted to keep his route, Valdez took away Plaintiff’s route and reassigned Plaintiff to a smaller, less profitable route. (Perez Depo 61:2-24) As a result, Plaintiff’s pay was substantially reduced, his hours were cut, and the time Plaintiff had spent cultivating relationships with his customers was rendered moot, as Plaintiff was forced to re-start the long process of building relationships with new customers in an unfamiliar territory. (Perez Depo 62:16-25.)

 

Plaintiff also argues that Defendant has not established by clear and convincing evidence that it would have terminated Plaintiff for a legitimate, independent reason. Section 1102.6 requires whistleblower plaintiffs to show that retaliation was a ‘contributing factor’ in their termination, demotion, or other adverse action. This means plaintiffs may satisfy their burden of proving unlawful retaliation even when other, legitimate factors also contributed to the adverse action.” (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 713-714.) Plaintiff disputes Defendant’s assertion that Plaintiff did not have TRUCE downloaded on his phone. He states that a week after the Distracted Driving Policy was implemented, he provided Defendant with his personal cell number and downloaded the TRUCE app from a text message link sent to him. (Perez Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiff asserts that initially TRUCE was functioning properly, but a few weeks later TRUCE started malfunctioning. (Perez Decl. ¶ 8.) Plaintiff states that prior to May 13, 2022, he informed Okum that he was having issues with the TRUCE app. (Perez Decl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff contends that in response, Okum told Plaintiff to delete the version of TRUCE on his phone, Okum sent a new link to Plaintiff’s phone and Okum personally installed and configured the settings of TRUCE on Plaintiff’s phone. (Perez Decl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff states that Okum informed Plaintiff that TRUCE should work properly going forward. (Perez Decl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff cites to Okum’s deposition testimony in which he states that Plaintiff had showed Okum a notification on Plaintiff’s phone showing that Plaintiff had downloaded the TRUCE app. (Okum Depo 84:4-22.) However, Okum states he did not verify that it was working on his phone. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant has not established by clear and convincing evidence that Plaintiff did not have TRUCE downloaded on his phone. According to Plaintiff, he complained to supervisors about missing meal periods and falsifying documents, supervisor Valdez then removed Plaintiff from his route, and Plaintiff did have TRUCE installed on his phone, thus, a reasonable jury could conclude that Plaintiff’s complaints were a contributing factor to his termination.

 

Therefore, summary adjudication as to the first and second causes of action in the Complaint is DENIED.

 

E.     Third, Fourth, Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action: Failure to Provide Meal and Rest Periods, Failure to Provide Accurate Wage Statements, Waiting Time Penalty and Failure to Indemnify Employee

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the third, fourth, fifth and sixth causes of action. Defendant argues that Plaintiffs individual wage and hour claims are barred as a matter of law due to his failure to timely opt out of the certified class action in which the same claims are asserted.

 

Defendant contends that Christian Sanchez and Frankie Escobar brought a class action lawsuit against Defendant Auto-Chlor in Los Angeles Superior Court alleging causes of action for unpaid overtime, unpaid meal period premiums, unpaid rest period premiums, unpaid minimum wages, waiting time penalties, non compliant wage statements, unreimbursed business expenses, and violation of California Business & Professions Code §§ 17200 et seq. (Clayton Decl. ¶ 3.) The Court, the Honorable Yvette Palazuelos presiding, certified a class and subclasses on July 23, 2023. (Clayton Decl. ¶ 3.) On August 9, 2023, the third party administrator CPT Group, Inc. mailed the Notice of Certified Class Action Lawsuit to Plaintiff Adrian Perez. (Clayton Decl. ¶ 3; Notice of Class Action, Exh. 4.) The Notice of Certified Class Action Lawsuit informed Plaintiff that he had until August 9, 2023 to optout of the class and if he did not do so he would “[b]e bound by any decision by the Court whether adverse or favorable.” (Clayton Decl. ¶ 3; Notice of Class Action, Exh. 4.) Defendant asserts that Plaintiff received the Notice but did not opt out of the class, citing to Plaintiff’s deposition testimony in support. (Perez Depo., 97:9-18.) Defendant further asserts that Plaintiff’s counsel stated in writing on December 19, 2023 that Plaintiff would seek relief from the judge handling the class action, but Plaintiff never did so. (Clayton Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. 5.)

 

“After the members of the class have been properly notified of the action, they are required to decide whether to remain members of the class represented by plaintiffs' counsel and become bound by a favorable or unfavorable judgment in the action, whether to intervene in the action through counsel of their own choosing, or whether to ‘opt out’ of the action and pursue their own independent remedies, such as negotiation with defendants, initiation of their own action, or intervention in some other action.” (Home Sav. & Loan Assn. v. Superior Court (1974) 42 Cal.App.3d 1006, 1010; accord, Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial, supra, ¶ 14:133, p. 14–80 (rev.# 1, 2010).) “The critical reason for notification of members of the class on whose behalf a class action has been brought is that notification makes possible a binding adjudication and an enforceable judgment with respect to the rights of the members of the class. Absent such notification no member of the class need be bound by the result of the litigation.” (Home Sav. & Loan Assn., supra, at p. 1011.)

 

Here, Defendant has sufficiently established that Plaintiff did not opt out of the other pending class action alleging the same causes of action against Defendant. Plaintiff stated in his deposition testimony that he received the Notice of the class action but did not opt out. (Perez Depo., 97:9-18.) Plaintiff’s opposition fails to address Defendant’s argument, and his separate statement does not dispute these facts. Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff’s individual wage and hour claims are barred due to his failure to timely opt out of the certified class action in which the same claims are asserted.

 

As such, summary adjudication as to the third, fourth, fifth and sixth causes of action in the Complaint are GRANTED.

 

F.      Seventh Cause of Action: PAGA Cause of Action

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the seventh cause of action on several grounds. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s PAGA cause of action is barred as a matter of law because: (1) the PAGA claim is subsumed in the class action in which Plaintiff is a certified class member, (2) Defendant could be double penalized with Labor Code penalties for the same conduct as in the certified Class Action and (3) Plaintiff has no evidence of Labor Code violations under PAGA within the PAGA period as to the alleged representative class of aggrieved employees.

 

As discussed above, Defendant sufficiently established that Plaintiff failed to timely opt out of the other pending class action. (Perez Depo., 97:9-18.) Thus, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s PAGA claim is subsumed in the class action in which Plaintiff is a certified class member. Defendant further asserts that Plaintiff has no evidence of Defendant violating any class of alleged aggrieved employees’ rights under Labor Code §§ 2699. (Clayton Decl. ¶ 9, Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Document Request, at 10-12, 16, 17, 19, Exh. 6.) The Court finds that Defendant has carried its burden.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff states he disputes Defendant’s material facts on the PAGA issue in his separate statement. However, Plaintiff fails to present any evidence in support. Therefore, Plaintiff fails to establish the existence of a triable issue of material fact. 

 

Accordingly, summary adjudication as to the seventh cause of action in the Complaint is GRANTED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first and second causes of action is DENIED.

Defendant's motion for summary adjudication as to the third, fourth, fifth, sixth and seventh causes of action is GRANTED.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendants in the reduced amount of $1,807.26. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs Superstar Monitoring LLC; Annmarie Baranik (“Baranik”); Raymond Anthony Lerma; Samantha Brown; Adrian Napolitano and Roman Moreno filed this premises liability action against Defendants Sharp Group LLC and Rozita Natanzi.

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges the following causes of action against the Defendants: (1) Breach of Express and Implied Contract; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing/Breach of Express and Implied Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment, Trespass, Nuisance; (3) Nuisance; (4) Fraud, Negligent Misrepresentation, Concealment; (5) Negligence – Premises Liability; (6) Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Negligent Management; (7) Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200; (8) Violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiffs were tenants/occupants of the subject premises, at Defendants’ Oakridge Plaza with the street address of 14336 Ventura Boulevard. Plaintiff Baranik was the lessee of the premises. Defendants entered into a written lease with Plaintiff Baranik, which provided for her to have the exclusive use of and to occupy 1,117 square feet of retail space, parking and amenities located at the premises for the use of her business Superstar Monitoring LLC, and her employees, occupants and clientele. Defendants breached the lease and their duties to Plaintiffs, including failing to remediate water intrusion on the premises which caused the premises to be unfit for use, unhealthy and unsafe.

 

On September 30, 2024, Defendants filed a Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One) and a Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s Responses to Requests for Documents (Set One), arguing:

·       Counsel for Defendants provided six extensions over the three-month period in which to provide the responses. Despite the multiple extensions given, and notice by counsel for Defendants that they would have no choice but to file a motion to compel if responses were not served, counsel for Plaintiff filed one single omnibus objection for all Plaintiffs as to all request for production of documents and all form interrogatories.

·       Defendants contend the omnibus objections were improper and made in bad faith.

·       Defendants requests sanctions in the sum of $342 for each motion.

 

Plaintiff filed a combined opposition to both motions, arguing that Defendants failed to meet and confer and failed to file a Separate Statement. Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendants in the sum of $2,700.

 

Defendants filed replies arguing that Plaintiff did not sufficiently provide responses because the responses were only blanket objections, thus there was no need to meet and confer.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Procedural Issues

 

First, the Court notes that although Defendants’ motions are styled as motions to compel initial responses, they would in fact be motions to compel further responses.

 

Defendants served their discovery requests on June 17, 2024. Defendants contend that they granted Plaintiff multiple extensions to respond. (Nusbaum Decl., ¶¶ 4-11.) On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff served omnibus objections to Defendants’ discovery requests. (Nusbaum Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. H.) Therefore, Plaintiff had served responses to the initial discovery requests, even if the responses consisted of only objections. Thus, Defendants’ motions are in fact motions to compel further responses.

 

a.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further responses “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b); 2031.310, subd. (b).)  The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)  “[A] reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with [opposing] counsel….  Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.”  (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294.)  “A determination of whether an attempt at informal resolution is adequate involves the exercise of discretion.”  (Stewart v. Colonial W. Agency (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016, internal ellipses omitted.)  Where a party fails to make any real effort at informal resolution, a particularly egregious failure may justify an immediate and outright denial of further discovery.  (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 433-34, citing Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1437.)   

 

Here, Defendants failed to file a meet and confer declaration. Defendants argue in their reply that they were not obligated to meet and confer as Plaintiff only served unmeritorious objections. The Court disagrees. Even if Plaintiff’s responses were unmeritorious, Defendants were still obligated to file a meet and confer declaration.

 

Defendants also contend that even if they were required to meet and confer over those legal issues, the Court is allowed to evaluate whether, from the perspective of a reasonable person in the position of the discovering party, additional effort appeared likely to bear fruit. (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal. App. 4th 1277, 1285.) Here, the Court finds that Defendants should have attempted to meet and confer, and thus, have failed to satisfy their meet and confer requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b); 2031.310, subd. (b).) 

 

b.     Separate Statement

 

Motions to compel further responses must be accompanied by a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(3).) A properly-prepared Separate Statement enables both the parties and the Court to accurately analyze relevance and objections. The substantial rights of the parties are affected when there is no separate statement. Here, Defendants did not file separate statements.

 

Due to these procedural deficiencies, the Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

B.     Sanctions

 

As stated in Section 2023.030 (a), the court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.

 

Further, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand or interrogatory, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code of Civil Procedure §2031.310(h), §2030.300(d).)

 

Here, both parties seek monetary sanctions. Defendants request sanctions in the sum of $342 for each motion. Plaintiff requests sanctions in the sum of $2,707.26. Since the Court denies Defendants’ motions, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for sanctions. Plaintiff’s counsel states he has and will spend a total of six hours on opposing Defendants’ motions at a rate of $450 per hour and costs in the amount of $7.26. (Rand-Lewis Decl. ¶ 25.) Since both motions are not substantively different and Plaintiff only filed one collective opposition in response, the Court is inclined to award sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,807.26.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Plaintiff Superstar Monitoring LLC’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendants in the reduced amount of $1,807.26. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.
