May 2, 2024

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant Yang Lu

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc.’s evidentiary objections, filed on April 25, 2024, as follows:

Objections Nos. 1 and 8 are overruled.

Objections Nos. 2-7 and 9 are sustained.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc. (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Yang Lu (“Defendant”) to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set Ten, numbers 136 and 137, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $3,621.65.

The court finds that Defendant has not shown that Plaintiff’s motion is untimely because it was filed after Defendant filed a motion for a protective order.  The court also finds that, for purposes of this motion, Defendant has not shown that she was not required to answer the subject two special interrogatories on the ground that Plaintiff propounded more than 35 interrogatories because Plaintiff has submitted evidence establishing that it submitted a declaration that satisfies Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.030 and 2030.050.  (Chan Decl., Ex. A, p. 4 [Chan declaration regarding additional discovery].)  Although Defendant filed a motion for a protective order on December 20, 2023, Defendant did not move to advance the September 30, 2024 hearing date on her motion until April 24, 2024 (1) because she believed that the court would grant her motion for summary judgment, and (2) two days before the first hearing on Plaintiff’s pending motions to compel her further responses to discovery.[1]  (Apr. 24, 2024 Ex Parte App., p. 6:3-18.)

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 136 and 137 because Defendant’s objections to those interrogatories are without merit.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(3).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)  The court finds that $1,661.65 ((4 hours x $400 hourly rate) + $61.65 filing fee) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Defendant in connection with this motion.  (Chan Decl., ¶¶ 23-25.)

The court denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc.’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, the court orders defendant Yang Lu to serve further, full and complete answers to plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc.’s Special Interrogatories, numbers 136 and 137, which comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.210-2030.250, within 20 days of the date of service of this order.

            The court orders defendant Yang Lu to pay monetary sanctions to plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc. in the amount of $1,661.65 within 30 days of the date of service of this order.

The court orders plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc.  to give notice of this ruling.

The court considered the moving, consolidated opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with each motion.

 

 

HOWARD’S MOTION TO TAX COSTS

Defendant and cross-complainant Howard Management Group (“Howard”) moves the court for an order (1) striking the memorandum of costs filed by cross-defendant Pacific Coast Elevator Corporation d/b/a Amtech Elevator Services (“Amtech”) or, alternatively, (2) taxing various costs.

First, the court finds that Howard has not shown that Amtech’s memorandum of costs was not filed within the time limits set forth by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1700.

“A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.5 or the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first.”  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1700, subd. (a)(1).)

The parties do not dispute that (1) Howard filed a Request for Dismissal of its Cross-Complaint, which alleged causes of action against defendant and cross-defendant 1544 9th Street, LLC (“9th Street”) and Amtech, on February 9, 2024, (2) pursuant to that request, the clerk dismissed Howard’s Cross-Complaint on February 13, 2024, and (3) Amtech filed and electronically served its memorandum of costs on February 26, 2024.  (Feb. 9, 2024 Request for Dismissal; Feb. 26, 2024 Memo. of Costs, MC-010.)  The parties do dispute, however, whether the court’s January 5, 2024 order granting Amtech’s motion for determination of good faith regarding the settlement that it entered into with plaintiff Sofia Nemzer (“Plaintiff’) constitutes a dismissal of Howard’s Cross-Complaint.  The court finds that it does not.

The court acknowledges that a “trial court has the inherent power to dismiss a cross-complaint for equitable indemnity, without notice, when the alleged indemnitor has entered into a good faith settlement with the claimant.”  (Hale v. Laden (1986) 178 Cal.App.3d 668, 670.)  However, the cases relied on by Howard concerned a trial court’s authority (1) to dismiss a cross-complaint in connection with motions for determination of good faith settlement and a request for dismissal or order barring a claim for comparative indemnity, and (2) to dismiss a cross-complaint upon an ex parte request to do so based on a prior determination of a good faith settlement.  (Greshko v. County of Los Angeles (1987) 194 Cal.App.3d 822, 828 [city and county cross-defendants filed motions for determination of good faith settlement, and (1) city cross-defendant “also moved for dismissal[,]” and (2) county cross-defendant “sought an order barring [the] claim for comparative indemnity”], 830 [determining that the court did not err in striking the cross-complaint “which, after the good faith determination, became irrelevant to resolution of the remaining issues”]; Hale, supra, 178 Cal.App.3d at p. 672.)  In those instances, the trial court was determined to have been permitted to strike the cross-complaints because they had become “irrelevant to resolution of the remaining issues” upon the good faith determination.  (Hale, supra, 178 Cal.App.3d at p. 673.)

Here, the court’s January 5, 2024 order did not grant a request by Amtech to dismiss Howard’s Cross-Complaint, although the court may have had the authority to do so.  Thus, the court finds that its January 5, 2024 order does not constitute a dismissal of Howard’s Cross-Complaint, such that the 15-day deadline to file the memorandum of costs began to run on the date of service of the notice of entry of the order.  Amtech filed its memorandum of costs on February 26, 2024, within 15 days after the date that Howard’s Cross-Complaint was dismissed (i.e., February 13, 2024).  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1700, subd. (a)(1).)

Second, the court finds that Howard has not shown that Amtech is not a prevailing party on its Cross-Complaint.  Howard dismissed its Cross-Complaint against, inter alia, Amtech, with prejudice, on February 13, 2024.  (Feb. 9, 2024 Request for Dismissal.)  Thus, Amtech is a prevailing party on Howard’s Cross-Complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4) [prevailing party includes “a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered”].)

Third, the court finds that Howard has not shown that Amtech’s requested costs should be offset by the settlement amount of $35,000 with Plaintiff pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 877.  That statute “directs that ‘claims’ against nonsettling defendants be reduced by the amount of the settlement consideration.”  (Reed v. Wilson (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 439, 444.)  “[T]he word ‘claims’ in section 877 generally refers to a claim of liability for damages and not to costs awarded to a prevailing party after a claim of liability has been adjudicated.”  (Id. at pp. 444-445.)  Thus, section 877 does not allow for an offset of costs.  (Ibid.)

Fourth, the court grants Howard’s request to tax $613.35 in costs.

The court finds that Howard has met its burden to show that the following costs were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation regarding Howard’s Cross-Complaint against Amtech: (1) $37.95 in costs incurred in filing Amtech’s answer to 9th Street’s Cross-Complaint; (2) $513.75 in fees to reserve a hearing on a motion for summary judgment, which Amtech never filed; and (3) $61.65 for “Good Faith Settlement” dated November 6, 2023, since (i) Amtech did not file its motion for determination of good faith settlement until December 8, 2023 and (ii) Amtech appears to have also requested $37.95 in filing costs for that motion on December 15, 2023.  (Memo. of Costs, MC-011, ¶¶ 1, subds. (c), (e), (f); Kelly Decl., ¶ 6 [Amtech paid the $513.75 nonrefundable reservation fee but did not file the motion]; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c); Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1486 [the burden is on the party challenging costs “to demonstrate that they were not reasonable or necessary”].)  

The court notes that Howard has also argued that the $3,150 in mediation costs were not reasonably necessary because (1) the referenced April 5, 2023 mediation did not occur, and        (2) no mediation was conducted by Adjudicate, Inc.  (Memo. of Costs, MC-011, ¶ 16.)  Amtech has submitted evidence, in its opposition, explaining that it incorrectly identified the mediation provider to be Adjudicate, Inc. and listed the invoice date of April 5, 2023, instead of the date on which the mediation occurred.  (Kelly Decl., ¶ 7; Kelly Decl., Ex. C [Judicate West invoice in the amount of $3,150].)  The court finds that the costs incurred in mediation were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation and are reasonable in amount, and therefore exercises its discretion to allow these costs.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c).)

9TH STREET’S MOTION TO STRIKE COSTS OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, TAX COSTS

Defendant and cross-complainant 9th Street moves the court for an order (1) striking the memorandum of costs filed by Amtech or, alternatively, (2) taxing various costs.

 

First, the court finds that 9th Street has not met its burden to show that Amtech’s memorandum of costs was untimely on the ground that it was filed more than 15 days after notice of entry of the court’s January 5, 2024 order granting its motion for determination of good faith settlement for the same reasons set forth above in connection with Howard’s motion to tax costs.  (Feb. 9, 2024 Request for Dismissal of 9th Street’s Cross-Complaint [dismissing 9th Street’s Cross-Complaint on February 13, 2024]; Memo. of Costs filed Feb. 26, 2024; Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1700, subd. (a).)

Second, the court finds that 9th Street has not shown that it may enforce the waiver provision of the settlement agreement entered into by and between Plaintiff and Amtech.  

The court acknowledges that the “General Release and Settlement Agreement” between Plaintiff and Amtech (1) states that the parties “will each pay their own costs, attorney fees, and expenses with respect to the ACTION[,]” and (2) defines “ACTION” to be Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint, Howard’s cross-action, and 9th Street’s cross-action.  (River Decl., Ex. K, pp. 3, 1.)  However, the settlement agreement was entered into by and between Plaintiff and Amtech.  (Id. at p. 1.)  9th Street has not presented evidence or argument establishing that it—a nonsignatory and nonparty—may enforce the terms of the settlement agreement as a third-party beneficiary or pursuant to any other legal doctrine.  9th Street, for example, did not present evidence or argument establishing that a motivating purpose of Plaintiff and Amtech was to benefit it, or whether permitting 9th Street to enforce this provision is consistent with the objectives of their settlement agreement, such that the court could find that 9th Street is permitted to enforce the waiver of costs provision against Amtech.  (Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 917, 829-830 [factors to determine existence of a third-party beneficiary].)

Third, the court grants 9th Street’s request to tax $1,225.21 in costs.

The court finds that 9th Street has met its burden to show that the following costs were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation as to 9th Street’s Cross-Complaint against Amtech: (1) $510.24 in costs incurred to file an answer to Howard’s Cross-Complaint; (2) $37.95 in filing fees to file an opposition to Howard’s motion to compel; (3) $37.95 in filing fees to file, on March 30, 2023,[1] a notice of entry of the court’s order denying Howard’s motion; (4) $513.75 in fees to reserve a hearing on a motion for summary judgment, which Amtech never filed; (5) $61.65 in fees for “Good Faith Settlement” dated November 6, 2023, since Amtech     (i) did not file its motion for determination of good faith settlement until December 8, 2023 and   (ii) appears to have also requested $37.95 in filing costs for that motion on December 15, 2023; (6) $37.65 in filing fees to file an opposition to Howard’s motion to compel on December 5, 2023; (7) $13.01 in filing fees to file a statement of non-opposition to Howard’s motion to compel on December 5, 2023; and (8) $13.01 in filing fees to file a statement of non-opposition to Howard’s motion to compel on December 5, 2023.  (MC-011, ¶ 1, subds. (a), (b), (d), (e), (f), (g), (h), (i); Mar. 30, 2023 Notice of Entry of Order filed by Amtech [providing notice that the court denied Howard’s motion to compel]; Kelly Decl., ¶ 6; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c); Adams, supra, 199 Cal.App.4th at p. 1486.)

The court finds that 9th Street has not met its burden to show that the costs incurred in connection with various depositions was not reasonably necessary to the conduct of this litigation.  Although 9th Street asserts that those depositions were taken by or of other parties, 9th Street has not shown that Amtech’s costs in obtaining those deposition transcripts were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of this litigation, and Amtech has asserted that, in order to defend itself against 9th Street’s Cross-Complaint, it had to attend and participate in various depositions.  (Opp., p. 6:11-15; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c).)

Further, as to the costs incurred in mediation, the court has found, as set forth above in connection with Howard’s motion to strike, that (1) the costs incurred in mediation were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation and are reasonable in amount, and               (2) Amtech presented evidence showing that it erroneously identified the incorrect mediation provider, but supported its request for costs of the July 21, 2023 mediation.  (Code Civ. Request for Court Judgment by Default

Plaintiff Yessenia Perez (“Plaintiff”) requests that the court enter default judgment in her favor and against defendant Dr. Sebi’s Cell Food, LLC (“Defendant”) in the total amount of $286,858.47, consisting of (1) $8,480 in special damages, (2) $250,000 in general damages,      (3) $25,000 in attorney’s fees, (4) $834.47 in costs, and (5) $2,544 in prejudgment interest.  (Memo., pp. 6:22-7:5.)

The court notes two defects in Plaintiff’s request for default judgment.

First, Plaintiff did not file with the court the mandatory Request for Entry of Default (made on Judicial Council form CIV-100), required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a).

Second, even if Plaintiff had filed that document, the court finds that it would not be able to enter default judgment against Defendant in any amount because Plaintiff’s Complaint does not specify the amount of damages sought against Defendant.   

“The relief granted to the plaintiff, if there is no answer, cannot exceed that demanded in the complaint . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 580, subd. (a).)  “Thus, ‘in all default judgments the demand sets a ceiling on recovery,’ and a judgment purporting to grant relief beyond that ceiling is void for being in excess of jurisdiction.  [Citation.]”  (Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 863.)  “Where no amount of damages is demanded any amount awarded is by definition greater than the amount demanded.”  (Falahati v. Kondo (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 823, 830-831.)

Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint does not demand a certain amount of damages against Defendant, and instead (1) alleges that Plaintiff has suffered damages “in an amount according to proof[,]” and (2) prays “[f]or damages according to proof[.]”  (Compl., ¶¶ 32, 42, 49, 56, 70, 79, 90; Compl., Prayer, ¶¶ 1, 4, 5.)  Thus, the entry of default judgment awarding any amount of damages in favor of Plaintiff against Defendant would be void for being in excess of jurisdiction.  (Sass, supra, 10 Cal.5th at p. 863; Falahati, supra, 127 Cal.App.4th at pp. 830-831.)

The court therefore denies plaintiff Yessenia Perez’s request for court judgment by default against defendant Dr. Sebi’s Cell Food, LLC.

Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 863.)  “Where no amount of damages is demanded any amount awarded is by definition greater than the amount demanded.”  (Falahati v. Kondo (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 823, 830-831.)

Here, Plaintiff’s Complaint does not demand a certain amount of damages against Defendant, and instead (1) alleges that Plaintiff has suffered damages “in an amount according to proof[,]” and (2) prays “[f]or damages according to proof[.]”  (Compl., ¶¶ 32, 42, 49, 56, 70, 79, 90; Compl., Prayer, ¶¶ 1, 4, 5.)  Thus, the entry of default judgment awarding any amount of damages in favor of Plaintiff against Defendant would be void for being in excess of jurisdiction.  (Sass, supra, 10 Cal.5th at p. 863; Falahati, supra, 127 Cal.App.4th at pp. 830-831.)

The court therefore denies plaintiff Yessenia Perez’s request for court judgment by default against defendant Dr. Sebi’s Cell Food, LLC.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
