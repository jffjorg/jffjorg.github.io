Hearing Date:                          January 23, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Ford Motor Co. and Auto Nation Ford Valencia

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Karen McManus

Demurrer to Complaint

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the Fifth and Sixth causes of action in the complaint.  Defendants are ordered to file answers within 20 days.

BACKGROUND

On October 2, 2023, plaintiff Karen McManus filed a complaint against Ford Motor Company and Auto Nation Ford Valencia for (1) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(d), (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(b), (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(a)(3), (4) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, (5) fraudulent inducement – concealment, and (6) negligent repair.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Ford Motor Company demurs to the 5th cause of action for fraudulent inducement - concealment on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.  Defendant Auto Nation Ford Valencia demurs to the 6th cause of action for negligent repair on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that on October 28, 2019, plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with defendant Ford regarding a 2019 Ford Ranger, which was manufactured and/or distributed by defendant Ford.  Complaint, ¶9.  The warranty contract contained various warranties, including the bumper-bumper warranty, powertrain warranty, and emission warrant.  Id., ¶10.  Prior to plaintiff purchasing the vehicle, defendant Ford knew that vehicles equipped with the same 10-speed transmission as the vehicle suffered one or more defects that can cause the vehicles and their 10-speed transmissions to experience hesitation and/or delay acceleration; harsh and/or hard shifting; jerking; shuddering; and/or juddering (“Transmission Defect”).  Id., ¶25.  Defendant acquired this knowledge prior to plaintiff purchasing the vehicle through various sources of information, including pre-production testing, pre-production design failure mode and analysis data, production failure mode and analysis data, early consumer complaints made exclusively to Ford’s network of dealers and directly to Ford, aggregate warranty data complied from Ford’s network of dealers, testing conducted by Ford in response to consumer complaints, and repair orders and parts data received by Ford from Ford’s network of dealers.  Id., ¶26.  Defendant subsequently issued technical service bulletins (“TSBs”) concerning the Transmission Defect.  Id., ¶27.  On March 2, 2018, Defendant issued TSB 18-2079, which covers the same type of transmission in the subject vehicle.  Id., ¶28.  On September 7, 2018, defendant issued TSB 18-2274 regarding same type of transmission.  Id., ¶30.  On September 27, 2021, defendant issued TSB 21-2315 regarding same type of transmission.  Id., ¶32.  However, they fail to fix the transmission defects which affects the subject vehicle.  Id., 34.  Plaintiff would not have purchased the subject vehicle, or would have paid less for it, had plaintiff known of the Transmission Defect, given the unsafe nature of the defect.  Plaintiff unknowingly exposed themselves to the risk of accident, injury, and/or liability to others as a result of the nature of the Transmission Defect, which can lead to hesitation, loss of power, and other shifting issues while driving at highway speeds.  Id., ¶35.

 

 

Fifth Cause of Action for Fraudulent Inducement - Concealment

“’The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of fact.’”  Hambridge v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 124, 162 (citation omitted).  “One who willfully deceives another with intent to induce him to alter his position to his injury or risk, is liable for any damage which he thereby suffers.”  Civil Code §1709.

“[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’”  OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 845.  Nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud when:  (1) there is a fiduciary relationship between the parties; (2) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.  Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Commission v. Insomniac, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal. App. 4th 803, 831.

Rules of specifically pleading how, when, where, to whom, and by what means, misrepresentations were communicated, is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations, and not to concealment.  See Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal. App. 4th 1356, 1384.  The specificity requirement of fraud is “relaxed when it is apparent from the allegations that the defendant necessarily possesses knowledge of the facts.”  Quelimane Co. v. Steward Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 27.

The complaint alleges that plaintiff purchased the vehicle as manufactured with defendant’s 10-speed automatic transmission.  Complaint, ¶57.  Defendant committed fraud by allowing the subject vehicle to be sold to plaintiff without disclosing that the subject vehicle and its transmission was defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure.  Id., ¶58.  Prior to plaintiff acquiring the vehicle, plaintiff was well aware and knew that the transmission installed in the vehicle was defective but failed to disclose this fact to plaintiff at the time of sale and thereafter.  Id., ¶59.  Specifically, Ford knew that vehicles equipped with the same 10-speed transmission as the vehicle suffered from one or more defects that can cause the vehicles and their 10-speed transmissions to experience hesitation and/or delayed acceleration; harsh and/or hard shifting; jerking, shuddering, and/or juddering.  These conditions present a safety hazard and are unreasonably dangerous to consumers because they can suddenly and unexpectedly affect the driver’s ability to control the vehicle’s speed, acceleration, deceleration, and/or overall responsiveness of the vehicle in various driving conditions.  Id., ¶60.  Ford acquired its knowledge of the Transmission Defect prior to plaintiff acquiring the subject vehicle.  Id., ¶61.  Defendant knew about the Transmission Defect and its safety risks but nevertheless concealed and failed to disclose the defective nature of the vehicle and its transmission to plaintiff at the time of sale.  Had plaintiff known, they would not have purchased the subject vehicle.  Id., ¶62.  Defendant knew that the vehicle and its transmission suffered from an inherent defect, was defective, would fail prematurely, and was not suitable for its intended use.  Id., ¶63.  Defendant was under a duty to plaintiff to disclose the defective nature, its safety consequences, and/or the associated repair costs.  Id., ¶64.  Plaintiff only became suspicious that the vehicle suffered from the transmission defect after presenting the vehicle to defendant for a reasonable number of repair attempts to no avail.  Id., ¶68.  Plaintiff has suffered and will continue to suffer actual damages.  Id., ¶69.  Plaintiff was harmed by purchasing a vehicle that plaintiff would not have leased and/or purchased had plaintiff known the true facts about the Transmission Defect.  Plaintiff unknowingly exposed themselves to the risk of liability, accident, and injury.  Id., ¶70.

Defendant Ford argues that plaintiff fails to plead sufficient facts to support the elements and to plead with the requisite specificity.  Ford also contends that the claim is barred by the economic loss rule but acknowledges that that the court in Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal. App. 4th 828, held otherwise, that the economic loss rule does not bar fraudulent inducement claims, noting that the California Supreme Court granted a petition for review of that case.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that she sufficiently alleged the elements, including a direct contractual, transactional relationship, which gives rise to a duty to disclose.  Plaintiff also argues that the economic loss rule does not apply, citing to Dhital.

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient to support the elements at the pleading stage for fraudulent concealment.  Plaintiff alleges that defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact.  See Complaint, ¶¶58-60, 62.  Plaintiff alleges that defendant had a duty to disclose the fact.  See id., ¶64.  See also OCM Principal Opportunities Fund, L.P. v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 859 (“Under California law, a vendor has a duty to disclose material facts not only to immediate purchasers, but also to subsequent purchasers when the vendor has reason to expect that the item will be resold.”);  Khan v. Shiley Inc. (1990) 217 Cal. App. 3d 848, 858 (“[A] manufacturer of a product may be liable for fraud when it conceals material product information from potential users.”).  Plaintiff alleges that defendant knowingly and intentionally concealed the material fact.  See id., ¶65.  The court can infer that defendant placed the vehicle into the stream of commerce, presumably for profit.  Plaintiff was unaware of the fact, and she would not have purchased the vehicle, had she known of such defects and sustained damages.  See id., ¶66.  Moreover, noting that the specificity requirement is “relaxed” when defendant possesses knowledge of the facts and reading the allegations liberally and in context, the court finds that plaintiff has pled with sufficient particularity.

As to defendant’s argument as to the economic loss rule, the court follows Dhital, supra, in finding that the economic loss rule does not bar the claim for fraudulent concealment.

            Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

            Sixth Cause of Action for Negligence Repair

            The complaint alleges that plaintiff delivered the subject vehicle to defendant Valencia for substantial repair on at least one occasion.  Complaint, ¶73.  Defendant owed a duty to plaintiff to use ordinary care and skill in storage, preparation, and repair of the vehicle in accordance with industry standards.  Id., ¶74.  Defendant breached its duty.  Id., ¶75.  Defendant’s negligent breach of its duties owed to plaintiff was a proximate cause of plaintiff’s damages.  Id., ¶76.

            Defendant Valencia argues that the economic loss rule bars the cause of action against it.  Defendant also argues that plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged damages.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that under California law, economic loss rule does not apply to service contracts.  See North American Chem. Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal. App. 4th 764, 777-81.  Plaintiff also asserts that California law recognizes that the economic loss rule does not necessarily bar recovery in tort when a defective product causes damage to other portions of a larger product.  See Jimenez v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal. 4th 473, 483-84.  Further, plaintiff argues that she sufficiently alleged damages.

            The court finds that the economic loss rule does not bar the claim against defendant.  The economic loss rule provides that, “[i]n general, there is no recovery in tort for negligently inflicted ‘purely economic losses,’ meaning financial harm unaccompanied by physical or property damage.”  Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal. 5th 905, 922.  There is, however, “a recognized exception to the economic loss rule for consumers who contract for certain kinds of professional services.”  Id. at 933.  Plaintiff alleges that she delivered her car for a repair service.  

As for damages, “[n]egligence may be alleged in general terms; that is, it is sufficient to allege an act was negligently done without stating the particular omission which rendered it negligent. There is no requirement that [the plaintiff] identify and allege the precise moment of the injury, or the exact nature of the wrongful act.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747-748.  Only ultimate facts need be alleged; evidentiary facts supporting the allegation of ultimate fact need not be pleaded.  McKelly v. Washington Mut., Inc. (2006) 142 Cal. App. 4th 1457, 1469.

The demurrer is OVERRULED.

Time Records and Attorney Fees

The court notes that the demurrer challenges only claims that are not Song-Beverly claims.  As such, the work devoted to opposing the demurrer should be noted in plaintiff’s counsel’s time sheets as not related to the Song-Beverly claims, as the prosecution of non-Song-Beverly claims is not entitled to recovery of attorney’s fees under Song-Beverly and hence must be separately noted for potential attorney’s fees claims.

ORDER

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the Fifth and Sixth causes of action in the complaint.  Defendants are ordered to file answers within 20 days.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

BACKGROUND

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

Request for Production of Documents

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of “good cause” is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants Mario Dominguez and Lamngoc T. Nguyen request that the court compel plaintiff to serve responses without objections to defendants’ initial form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and demand for inspection and production of documents.

            Defendants contend that they served discovery requests on October 27, 2023 and responses were due by November 28, 2023.  To date, defense counsel has not received responses.

            The court finds that the discovery requests were properly served, and that plaintiff failed to serve responses.

            The motions are GRANTED.

Sanctions

Under CCP §2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” 

Defendants request sanctions against plaintiff and plaintiff’s attorney of record, Kenneth C. Yeager, in the amount of $760 for each motion.  The court finds that $530 ($175/hr. x 2 hrs.; $180 filing fees) is a reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs to be awarded against plaintiff only in total for all three motions.  No sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff’s counsel as he has sworn that he is not receiving cooperation from Plaintiff.

BACKGROUND

            On June 30, 2023, Ostralia J. Mathews and O’Myah Jackson, a minor by and through her guardian, Ostralia J. LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.  Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal. App. 2d 636, 643-44.

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  CCP § 436(a).  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  CCP §436(b).  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  CCP §436.  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendants Coastline Real Estate Advisors, Inc. and 4006 Emerald Owner LLC demur to the 5th cause of action for nuisance, 6th cause of action for wrongful eviction, and 7th cause of action for violation of Covid-19 eviction moratorium on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

The complaint alleges that plaintiffs entered into a lease agreement with defendants on February 18, 2021.  Throughout plaintiffs’ tenancy, and continuing up until the time that plaintiffs vacated the property, plaintiffs were forced to endure various unsafe, unsanitary, unhealthy, and uninhabitable conditions, all of which were caused by defendants’ failure to maintain the property in a habitable and tenantable condition.  Complaint, ¶15.  The various unsafe, unsanitary, unhealthy, and uninhabitable conditions include mold throughout inside of the property, mildew smell, bulging walls, leaking pipes, water leaks, roofing issues, damaged drywall, plumbing issues, sewage leaks, severely damaged structural wood, infestation of insects, low water pressure, broken lock on patio door, holes in the interior walls, detached and inoperable electrical outlets, inoperable dishwasher and garbage disposal and bathroom exhaust fan, lack of electrical lighting in bathroom, failure to keep basic structural elements of the building safe and intact, failure to maintain all common areas, failure to keep electrical, plumbing, sanitary, heating, ventilating systems and elevators operating safely, and foul urine smell.  Id., ¶16.  Despite repeated requests, defendants failed to effectuate the necessary repairs and/or take necessary measures to timely render the property in a habitable condition.  Id., ¶17.  Instead of repairing the many items in need of repair, defendants began to serve serial notices to quit in an effort to force plaintiff form the locale.  Id., ¶18.

Fifth Cause of Action for Nuisance

“A public nuisance is one which affects at the same time an entire community or neighborhood, or any considerable number of persons, although the extent of the annoyance or damage inflicted upon individuals may be unequal.”  Civil Code §3480.  “Of course, not every interference with collective social interest constitutes a public nuisance.  To qualify, and thus be enjoinable, the interference must be both substantial and unreasonable.”  People Ex Rel. Gallo v. Acuna (1997) 14 Cal. 4th 1090, 1105.

The essential factual elements of a public nuisance claim are that (1) defendant created a condition or permitted a condition to exist that was harmful to health, was indecent or offensive to the senses, or was an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property; (2) that the condition affected a substantial number of people at the same time; (3) that an ordinary person would be reasonably annoyed or disturbed by the condition; (4) that the seriousness of the harm outweighs the social utility of defendant’s conduct; (5) that plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; and (6) that defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  CACI 2020.

Under this cause of action, plaintiffs allege that the uninhabitable conditions, including mold contamination, at the property as described above constituted a nuisance within the meaning of Civil Code §§3479, et sec. in that these defective conditions were injurious to the health and safety of plaintiffs and the public at large, indecent, and offensive to the senses of plaintiffs and the public at large, and interfered substantially with plaintiffs’ comfortable enjoyment of the premises.  Id., ¶45.  Despite being required to abate the nuisance, defendants failed to correct the conditions.  Id., ¶47.

Defendants argue that this claim is duplicative of the cause of action for negligence and, therefore, redundant, because it relies on the same set of facts.  “Where negligence and nuisance causes of action rely on the same facts about lack of due care, the nuisance claim is a negligence claim.”  El Escorial Owners’ Assn. v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal. App. 4th 1337, 1349 (citations omitted).  Defendant also argues that plaintiffs fail to plead facts to establish the elements required for a public nuisance claim.  Rather, defendants contend, plaintiffs only allege facts as to plaintiffs’ injuries and the uninhabitable conditions are alleged to only have existed in plaintiffs’ unit and therefore could not have affected a substantial number of people at the same time.

In opposition, plaintiffs only reiterate the allegations at paras. 14-19 and make no other arguments.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action for nuisance, whether it be public or private nuisance.  Plaintiffs have not alleged any facts to support that the conditions affected a substantial number of people at the same time.  Further, the allegations are based on the same facts as the 4th cause of action for negligence and thus are duplicative.  “This court has recognized [that a cause of action that appears to be duplicative of another cause of action] [is] a basis for sustaining a demurrer.” Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal. App. 4th 268, 290, citing to Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal. App. 3d 494, 501 (finding demurrer was properly sustained without leave to amend as to cause of action that contained allegations of other causes and “thus added nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery.”).

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Defendants contend that plaintiffs fail to allege any facts which support a claim under Civil Code §1947.10, which is related to an eviction based on the owner’s intention to occupy a rent controlled unit.

            Motion to Strike

Defendants request that the court strike punitive damages at paras. 30, 36, 49, 52, and 58 and prayer for statutory damages at no. 4, punitive damages at no. 5, and treble damages at no. 6.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

A claim for punitive damages may not be based on conclusory allegations of oppression, fraud or malice, but instead must be based on factual allegations which support such a conclusion.  See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-42 (Court of Appeal issued a peremptory writ directing trial court to issue order striking plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages because “[t]he sole basis for seeking punitive damages are . . . conclusory allegations” which were “devoid of any factual assertions supporting a conclusion [defendants] acted with oppression, fraud, or malice”).

A tenant may state a cause of action in tort against his landlord for damages resulting from a breach of the implied warranty of habitability.”  Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal. App. 3d 903, 918-19; see also Smith v. David (1981) 120 Cal. App. 3d 101, 112 n.3 (a tenant “may seek general and punitive damages for . . . breach of warranty [of habitability]”).  The court in Stoiber found that plaintiff stated sufficient facts to support exemplary damages when she “alleged that defendant had actual knowledge of defective conditions in the premises including leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions . . . [and] alleged defendants ‘In maintaining said nuisance, . . . acted with full knowledge of the consequences thereof and the damage being caused to plaintiff, and their conduct was willful, oppressive and malicious.’”  Stoiber, supra, at 920 (citation omitted).

Defendants argue that the allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages as they are vague and conclusory.  Defendants also contend that the allegations are insufficient to support any statutory or treble damages.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that the allegations are conclusory and do not show oppression, fraud, or malice.  Further, there is no cause of action that supports statutory or treble damages.

The motion is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

ORDER

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Fifth and Sixth causes of action.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TEN DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Seventh cause of action.  

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.
