DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV07287    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: 73

9/05/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

CHICAGO TITLE INSURANCE COMPANY v. SOLUTION4HOME.COM, LLC, et al. (20STCV07287) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Joseph Dorfman (Fidelity National Law Group) 

Counsel for Defendants: N/A

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO VACATE DISMISSAL AND ENTER JUDGMENT (filed 6/14/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 21, 2020, Plaintiff Chicago Title Insurance Company filed the instant action against Defendants Solution4Home.com, LLC, a limited liability company fdba Giant Earth Property, LLC; Gene Choe aka Gene Wook Choe aka Eugene Wook Choe aka Jin C. Choe aka Jin Hoon Choe aka Jin Chan Choe; April Cabuena aka April L. Cabuena aka April Lou Cabuena; Victor Matthews; Victor Hammond; Motsoasele Nkhereanye; Gold Mine Investments and Development Corporation; and Sonny Seyedi.  

 

The Complaint alleges that in 1999, Sheila Fletcher inherited the subject real property located at 214 Horizon Avenue, Venice, CA 90291. Fletcher was defrauded which resulted in title insurance claims made by Plaintiff’s insureds. Plaintiff incurred damages resolving its insureds claims and sought recovery in the instant action.

 

Defendants Solution4Home.com, LLC, a limited liability company fdba Giant Earth Property, LLC; Gene Choe aka Gene Wook Choe aka Eugene Wook Choe aka Jin C. Choe aka Jin Hoon Choe aka Jin Chan Choe (collectively “Defendants”) settled with Plaintiff under a Stipulation for Entry of Judgment (the “Stipulation”).  Pursuant to the terms of the Stipulation, Defendants agreed to pay Plaintiff $75,000 under a payment plan.

 

On March 21, 2022, the Court ordered Defendants dismissed without prejudice, but the Court retained jurisdiction to make orders to enforce any and all terms of settlement, including judgment, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 664.6.

 

On June 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment against Defendants.  

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP section 664.6 provides that “[i]f parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.  If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6.)

 

B.     Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of: (1) The Minute Order dated March 21, 2022, and (2) The Order Approving Joint Stipulation for Entry of Judgment and Order for the Court to Retain Jurisdiction to Enforce Parties Stipulation for Entry of Judgment Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) The Court may take judicial notice of its own file. (See Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.)

 

The Court grants Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice.

 

C.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to fulfill the obligations agreed to in the parties’ Stipulation. As a result, Plaintiff requests that the Court vacate the dismissal previously entered in this case pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, and enter Judgment in the amount of $23,370 pursuant to the terms of the written Stipulation. 

 

In support of its motion, Plaintiff filed the Stipulation showing the agreed upon terms. The Stipulation provides for the Court to retain jurisdiction pursuant to CCP section 664.6 to enforce the agreement. (Dorfman Decl., Ex. A, ¶ C.) Pursuant to the Stipulation, “Defendants shall pay to Chicago the settlement amount of $75,000.00 with 3% interest payable over monthly installments of $3,225.00 each commencing on November 1, 2021 and continuing the 1st day of each consecutive month thereafter until the settlement sum is paid in full pursuant to the Payment Schedule.” (Dorfman Decl., Ex. A, ¶ A.) Further, pursuant to the Stipulation, in the event of default by the Defendants, Plaintiff is entitled to request entry of judgment against Defendants for the amount of principal plus court costs, less a credit for all payments received under the terms of the Stipulation. (Keith Decl., Ex. A, ¶ B.)

Plaintiff asserts that Defendants made payments totaling $51,630 and then stopped making payments required by the Stipulation. (Dorfman Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendants defaulted under the terms of the Stipulation by failing to pay the payment due April 1, 2023, and all subsequent payments due under the Stipulation. (Ibid.)

 

Notably, Defendants failed to file an opposition.  

 

As Defendants have defaulted, the Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to entry of judgment under the Stipulation. 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the Motion to vacate dismissal and enters judgment in the principal sum of $75,000, less credits of $51,630, for a total of $23,370.00.   

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment.

SUNDO HONG v. SUN BE HONG (23STCV12280) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Jacob Iloulian (Law Office of Jacob Illoulian) 

Counsel for Defendant:  Justin Rodriguez (Justice Law Partners, Inc.)

 

MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION OF PRIOR ORDER FOR APPOINTMENT OF REFEREE (filed 5/09/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 1, 2023, Plaintiff Sundo Hong (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant Sun Be Hong (“Defendant”).

 

This is an action for partition by sale of the commercial property at 6840 S. Crenshaw Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90043, held equally as joint tenants by Plaintiff and Defendant. (FAC, ¶ 1.) Plaintiff alleges Defendant refused to voluntarily sell the real property in question or buy out the share of Plaintiff’s interest. (Ibid.)

 

On March 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Motion for Interlocutory Judgment of Partition by Sale and Appointment of Partition Referee.  Defendant did not file an opposition. On May 1, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion.

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of the Complaint for Interpleader filed by Fenix Entrepreneur Inc., Case No. 24STCV17180.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ 

 

The Court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

B.      Motion for Reconsideration

 

Defendant moves for reconsideration of the Court’s May 1, 2024 order granting Plaintiff’s Motion for Interlocutory Judgment of Partition by Sale and Appointment of Partition Referee.

 

The moving party must present new facts, circumstances or law in order to grant a motion for reconsideration.  (See CCP § 1008(a); see also Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.)  The party seeking reconsideration of an order shall state by affidavit what application was made before, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts or circumstances are claimed to be shown.  (CCP § 1008(a).)  Further, “…the party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457 [emphasis added].)  The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.) 

 

Defendant asserts that new or different circumstances exist warranting reconsideration. Defendant contends that an interpleader was filed after the time of Plaintiff’s Motion “whereby a Buyer who previously entered into a Purchase Agreement with the Defendant, prior to the filing of the Complaint for Partition, declared a stake in the instant case.” (Motion, p. 3.) Defendant also states that “counsel for Defendant was limited in his ability to participate meaningfully in the May 1st hearing as he was engaged in a trial matter and Defendant’s opposition had not been effectively filed for the Court’s consideration.” (Motion, p. 4.)

 

The Court does not find that Defendant has sufficiently established new or different circumstances that could not have been offered earlier. According to Defendant, Martin Fierro entered into a January 20, 2023 Purchase Agreement to buy Defendant's 1/2 Joint Tenant interest in the subject real property owned with Plaintiff Sun Do Hong, thereby severing the joint tenancy. (Motion, Ex. B.) Thus, Defendant had this information in January 2023, before Plaintiff’s Motion was heard on May 1, 2024. Therefore, Defendant could have offered this information earlier at the time of Plaintiff’s Motion. Yet, Defendant failed to file an opposition. Therefore, the Court does not find reconsideration is warranted.   

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

BROOKS v. HARDAGE HOSPITALITY (24STCV00516)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Brent Marlis (The Work Justice Firm)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Edwin Pairavi (Pairavi Law PC)

 

DEFENDANT’S motion to DECLARE RELATED AND CONSOLIDATE CASE NOS. 24STCV00516 AND 24STCV00523 (filed 07/24/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion to Relate and Consolidate Case Nos. 24STCV00516 and 24STCV00523. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff Ana Sabala Brooks filed this action against Defendant Hardage Hospitality.

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint, alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Disability Discrimination;

2.      Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation;

3.      Failure to Engage in Good Faith Interactive Process;

4.      Retaliation; and

5.      Wrongful Termination.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff was employed by Defendants from July 5, 2021 until January 26, 2022. At the time of her termination, Plaintiff worked as a Room Attendant. On or about September 2021, Plaintiff’s back began to hurt and on or about October 2021, her right wrist also started hurting. Plaintiff notified her supervisors about her injuries, but they did not take any action. On January 26, 2022, Plaintiff was terminated after being accused of sexual harassment by a co-worker. Plaintiff alleges she was actually terminated in retaliation for her request for reasonable accommodations.

 

On July 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Relate and Consolidate Case Nos. 24STCV00516 and 24STCV00523, arguing:

·       The Brooks and Alcerro actions should be related and consolidated because they seek the same types of damages from identical causes of action against the same Defendant based on a single incident that resulted in Plaintiffs’ simultaneous suspension and termination

·       The benefits of consolidation outweigh any burden

 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends:

·       On May 8, 2024, the Court already determined the cases were not related.

·       Defendant’s motion is an improper motion for reconsideration

·       Defendant has failed to file a notice of appeal to the denial of the notice to relate cases

·       Nevertheless, the facts of the cases are different. Both Plaintiffs have different injuries. One Plaintiff was sent to a company doctor and given work restrictions which Defendant did not honor.

·       Consolidating the cases would create prejudice. The credibility or lack of potential credibility by one Plaintiff might negatively affect the other Plaintiff’s case

 

 In reply, Defendant argues:

·       Plaintiff did not provide defense counsel with any authority supporting their position that a case must first be ordered “related” before it can be ordered “consolidated”

·       Thus, the cases should be consolidated

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendant objects to the declaration of Rami Kayyali in support of Plaintiff’s Opposition.

 

The Court overrules Defendant’s objections.

 

B.      Legal Standard

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).) “Coordination of civil actions sharing a common question of fact or law is appropriate if one judge hearing all of the actions . . . will promote the ends of justice taking into account whether the common question of fact or law is predominating and significant to the litigation; the convenience of the parties, witnesses, and counsel; the relative development of the actions and the work product of counsel; the efficient utilization; the calendar of the courts; the disadvantages of duplicative and inconsistent rulings, orders, or judgments; and, the likelihood of settlement of the actions without further litigation should coordination be denied.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 404.1.)  

“The purpose of consolidation is merely to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.”  (Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485 [citation omitted].) “A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.” (Ibid.)  Consolidation does not require identical causes of action in each case, absolute identity of the parties, or identical allegations. (Ibid.)  Where common issues are present in two cases then consolidation is appropriate.  (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-79.)   

C.      Discussion

 

Defendant seeks to relate and consolidate Case Nos. 24STCV00516 and 24STCV00523. 

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that the instant Motion to Consolidate Cases is procedurally deficient and does not comply with Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1).

Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1) provides that “[c]ases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related in a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”  

On May 8, 2024, the Court found that the following cases, 24STCV00516 and 24STCV00523, are not related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a), stating that Plaintiff in each case is different. (Court’s 5/8/24 Minute Order.) Defendant has not filed a motion for reconsideration or appealed the Court’s order. In addition, the cases are not assigned to the same department. Case No. 24STCV00516 is in this department, Department 73, and Case No. 24STCV00523 is in a different department, Department 32. Therefore, the cases cannot be consolidated because they are not in the same department and have not been related in a single department.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion is DENIED.

 

DISPOSITION

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion to Relate and Consolidate Case Nos. 24STCV00516 and 24STCV00523.

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

IMMIGRANT RIGHTS DEFENSE COUNCIL, LLC v. SKLAR, et al. (24STCV04208) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff: Sebastian Medvei (Medvei Law Group, APC)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Robert Gentino (Law Office of Robert Gentino)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES (filed 06/24/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff $40,785 in attorney fees and costs. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 20, 2020, Plaintiff Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Izabella Sklar, Access Legal Services and Same Day Marriage in Los Angeles, Inc. (“Defendants”) and Does 1 through 100.

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges a cause of action for Violation of the Immigration Consultant Act (“ICA”). 

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Commencing in or about 2003, Defendants started offering immigration services. The business offers immigration legal services in exchange for compensation, including services such as assistance with the preparation of petitions and applications for immigration benefits, as well as the provision of legal advice. However, during the time the website and Defendants’ business has existed, Defendants did not obtain immigration consultant bonds, in violation of Business and Professions Code section 22443.3. Moreover, throughout the website, Defendants create an “aura of expertise” and refer to themselves as professionals experienced in immigration laws, despite not taking the immigration specialization exam, or even obtaining a law degree or even maintaining the required immigration consultant bond. Nowhere on the website is there a clear and conspicuous statement that Defendants cannot provide legal advice, in violation of Business and Professions Code section 22442.2. Plaintiff seeks injunctive relief and reasonable fees/costs.

 

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Acceptance of Defendants’ Statutory Offer to Compromise Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 998.

 

On April 12, 2024, the Court entered judgment enjoining Defendants from violating the ICA and from engaging in the business of an immigration consultant as well as entitling Plaintiff to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs.

 

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served notice of entry of judgment and Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees, arguing:

·       Plaintiff is entitled to recover its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs under Business and Professions Code section 22446.5

·       Plaintiff’s counsel’s hours spent/hourly rate is reasonable

·       This action serves the public interest. Plaintiff should be awarded a lodestar multiplier of 1.5.

·       Plaintiff requests attorney’s fees and costs for a total of $40,785.

 

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s counsel seeks the outrageous sum of $40,785 in attorney fees notwithstanding Block Billing, Inflated Billing, Piggybacked Work, No Complex Issues, Unreasonable Hourly Rate, Unnecessary Work Performed After Defendants’ Settlement Offers for Full Relief and after Defendants’ Section 998 Offer for Full Relief. The Court should reduce the award or deny it altogether.

 

Plaintiff filed a reply arguing that the Court should disregard Defendants’ late filed opposition, that Defendants fail to specify which Defendant is bringing the opposition, and Plaintiff maintains that the requested fees/costs are reasonable.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Defendants’ Late Filed Opposition

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Defendants failed to timely file its opposition.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). A court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.)  The appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on the merits is a waiver of any procedural defects or irregularities in a motion.  (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.)

 

Here, Defendants filed their opposition on August 28, 2024, after the opposition was due. Defendants assert the opposition was filed late because the electronic filing was initially rejected. (Gentino Decl., ¶ 1.) However, a court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.) Since Plaintiff were able to respond to the opposition in its Reply, the Court, in its discretion, considers the arguments raised in Defendants’ late filed opposition.

 

B.      Objections to Evidence

 

Plaintiff’s objections to Defendant’s evidence are overruled.

 

C.      Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

D.     Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees

 

Business and Professions Code section 22446.5 “sets forth three classes of plaintiffs who may pursue actions against immigration consultants who violate the ICA: (1) ‘A person claiming to be aggrieved by a violation of this chapter by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action or injunctive relief or damages, or both’ (subd. (a)); ‘Any other party who, upon information and belief, claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action for injunctive relief on behalf of the general public’ (subd. (b)); and (3) ‘The Attorney General, a district attorney, or a city attorney who claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant, may bring a civil action for injunctive relief, restitution, and other equitable relief against the immigration consultant in the name of the people of the State of California’ (subd. (c)).” (Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC v. Hudson Ins. Co. (2022) 84 Cal. App. 5th 305, 309-310.) “The first two categories of plaintiffs who prevail in their actions are entitled to ‘reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.’” (Id. at 310.) Indeed, such fees and costs are mandatory, as opposed to other statutes that provide for discretionary awards. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 22446.5(b) (stating that a private attorney general plaintiff, “upon prevailing, shall recover reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs”—use of the word shall makes such fees and costs mandatory); see e.g. Grossman v. Park Fort Washington Assn. (2012) 212 Cal. App. 4th 1128, 1133 (where statutory conditions for an award of statutory attorney fees is met, award is “mandatory”); Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal. 3d 621, 633.)

 

Here, the parties’ stipulation provides that Plaintiff was awarded an injunction against Defendants and Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees and costs. Thus, Plaintiff is a prevailing party in this action and the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to its reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

E.      Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) An attorney’s own billing rates carry a presumption of reasonableness. (Mandel v. Lackner (1979) 92 Cal.App.3d 747, 761)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel asserts his hourly rate of $750 is a reasonable base rate for the lodestar calculation. Plaintiff’s counsel states this hourly rate is well within the range of current market rates in Los Angeles for attorneys of similar experience. (Medvei Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

The Court finds that the rate requested by Plaintiff’s counsel is reasonable. Counsel’s declaration sufficiently attests to his experience and the reasonableness of the rates. (Medvei Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

F.       Reasonable Hours Incurred and Costs

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

Plaintiff’s counsel states that he expended 36.2 hours litigating this case. Plaintiff also seeks a lodestar enhancement of 1.5 due to: 1) the contingent nature of Plaintiff’s counsel’s compensation and likely delays in receiving payment; and 2) the public interest nature of the litigation.

 

The Court has reviewed all of the claimed hours. Utilizing a lodestar method, the Court finds that the total amount of reasonable hours spent by Plaintiff’s counsel in this matter to date is 36.2 hours. Hence, the lodestar attorney’s fees award is $27,150 (36.2 x $750/hr.). Plaintiff’s request for a lodestar enhancement/multiplier of 1.5 is granted.

 

Plaintiff also filed a Memorandum of Costs seeking the recovery of its filing and service costs in connection with this matter. The “prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) Thus, the Court further awards Plaintiff’s requested costs for $635.66 plus $60 in the filing of the instant Motion. 

 

In sum, this Court grants Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees and costs totaling $40,785.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiff $40,785 in attorney fees and costs.

EMMA T. MALLOY v. EARL HALL (24STCV04870)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Gary Farwell (The Law Offices of Gary A. Farwell)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Eric Davis (Lara & Davis LLP)

 

Demurrer to FIRST AMENDED complaint (filed 07/30/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff Emma T. Malloy (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Earl Hall (“Defendant”)

 

On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

            C/A 1: Financial Elder Abuse

            C/A 2: Quiet Title

            C/A 3: Fraud

            C/A 4: Declaratory and Injunctive Relief

 

The FAC alleges the following. On November 28, 1969, Charlie and Emma T. Malloy purchased the residential property located at 1751 W. 38th Street, Los Angeles, CA 90062. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Title recorded under Charlie and Emma T. Malloy continued to remain in both names until October 31, 2002, when a Quitclaim Deed was signed, transferring title from Charlie Malloy and Emma T. Malloy to Defendant Earl Hall. (FAC, ¶ 11.) Plaintiff became aware of the fraudulent transfer of title to the Defendant in May 2019, when her daughter, Tracy Hall, learned that her brother, Defendant, had taken loans out on the property. (FAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff did not agree nor intend to ever quitclaim her property to the Defendant and does not remember signing the Quitclaim Deed, nor did Plaintiff agree to the multiple encumbrances identified in the Title Point Property Search prior to this fraudulent real property transfer. (FAC, ¶ 16.)

 

On July 30, 2024, Defendant filed a Demurrer to the FAC, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action are facially barred by the statutes of limitations

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff is eligible for tolling of the statute of limitations pursuant to Emergency Rule 9. (See Cal. R. Ct., Emergency Rule 9.) In addition, Plaintiff has in the past and continues to suffer from an inability to handle her business affairs as a competent adult. Plaintiff was legally incapacitated due to illness and infirmity, not due to age. All of Plaintiff’s causes of action should be allowed to proceed based on the foregoing.

Defendant filed a reply, arguing:

·       The FAC is devoid of any facts Plaintiff was legally incapacitated. There is no basis for the causes of action in the FAC to be tolled due to any alleged legal incapacity.

·       Further, any amendment that Plaintiff was legally incapacitated would be would be factually inconsistent with prior verified pleadings wherein Plaintiff alleged that she personally became aware of  the alleged fraudulent transfer in May 2019.

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone on July 5, 2024 regarding the alleged deficiencies in the FAC. (Davis Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.) The Court finds the moving party has met its obligation to meet and confer.

 

C.     Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of the following:

1.     Trust Deed recorded on June 13, 1984 as Instrument No. 84703287 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

2.     Trust Deed recorded on August 29, 1986 as Instrument No. 861135722 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

3.     Trust Deed recorded on December 9, 1987 as Instrument No. 871943799 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

4.     Trust Deed recorded on May 11, 1989 as Instrument No. 89756981 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

5.     Trust Deed recorded on October 25, 1991 as Instrument No. 1688751 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

6.     Trust Deed recorded on October 25, 1991 as Instrument No. 1688752 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California.

 

The Court may take judicial notice of “facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452(h).) While courts take judicial notice of public records, they do not take judicial notice of the truth of matters stated therein. [Citation.] ‘When judicial notice is taken of a document,…the truthfulness and proper interpretation of the document are disputable.’ [Citation.]” (Herrera v. Deutsche National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) Further, “courts have taken judicial notice of the existence and recordation of real property records...[because] [t]he official act of recordation and the common use of a notary public in the execution of such documents assure their reliability, and the maintenance of the documents in the recorder’s office makes their existence and text capable of ready confirmation, thereby placing such documents beyond reasonable dispute.” (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 264.)

 

The Court judicially notices all of Defendant’s requests. 

 

D.     Demurrer to First, Second, Third and Fourth Causes of Action: Statute of Limitations

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s causes of actions are facially barred by the statutes of limitations.

For a statute of limitations to bar a claim on demurrer, “the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.” (Committee for Green Foothills v. Santa Clara County Bd. of Supervisors (2010) 48 Cal.4th 32, 42, internal quotation marks omitted.) In general, a statute of limitations begins to run “when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements,” namely, wrongdoing, causation, and resulting harm. (Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 397 (Norgart).)  

 

The statute of limitations for financial abuse of an elder or dependent adult is four years after the plaintiff discovers, or through the exercise of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the financial abuse. (Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code§ 15657.7).

 

The statute of limitations for a cause of action for fraud is three years. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) If an action is brought more than three years after commission of the fraud, the plaintiff has the burden of pleading and proving that he did not make the discovery until within three years prior to the filing of his complaint. (Hobart v. Hobart Estate Co. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 412, 437.)

 

A claim for declaratory relief is subject to the same limitations period as the legal or equitable claim on which it is based. (Bank of N.Y. Mellon v. Citibank, N.A. (2017) 8 Cal. App.5th 935, 943; Ginsburg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal. App. 4th 873, 883.) Similarly, the theory of relief underlying the action for quiet title determines which statute of limitations applies. (Leeper v. Beltrami (1959) 53 Cal. 2d 195, 214; Ankoanda v. Walker-Smith (1996) 44 Cal. App. 4th 610, 615.)

 

As the Court previously found when it sustained the original Complaint, Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action are barred under the statute of limitations. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff discovered the facts constituting the alleged fraudulent transfer of property in May 2019. The Complaint states: “Plaintiff became aware of the fraudulent transfer of title to the Defendant in May 2019, when her daughter, Tracy Hall, learned that her brother, Earl Hall, had taken loans out on the property.” (FAC ¶¶ 13, 24.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s first cause of action for financial elder abuse would have needed to be brought by May 2023, within four years of her discovery. Plaintiff’s third cause of action for fraud would have needed to be brought by May 2022, within three years of her discovery. Plaintiff’s second cause of action for quiet title and declaratory relief are based on Plaintiff’s financial elder abuse or fraud claims and subject to the same statute of limitations. Here, Plaintiff filed this action February 27, 2024. Thus, over four years have passed since the discovery of the alleged financial abuse, which is past the statute of limitations for Plaintiff’s first, second, third, and fourth causes of action.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues she is eligible for tolling of the statute of limitations pursuant to Emergency Rule 9. California¿Rules¿of Court, appendix I (Emergency¿Rule¿9), which provides that, “[n]otwithstanding any other law, the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.”¿(Cal.¿Rules¿of Court, appen. I,¿Emergency¿Rule¿9(a).) On May 29, 2020, the Judicial Council added an Advisory Committee comment to the¿rule¿explaining that the¿rule¿“is intended to apply broadly to toll any statute of limitations on the filing of a pleading in court asserting a civil cause of action,” and that “[t]he term ‘civil causes of action’ includes special proceedings.”¿(Advisory Com. com, Cal.¿Rules¿of Court, appen, I,¿emergency¿rule¿9, (emphasis added).) Accordingly, the statute of limitations could not expire on any civil causes of action between April 6, 2020 and October 1, 2020. (Lantzy v. Centex Homes (2003) 31 Cal.4th 363, 370 (“the limitations period stops running during the tolling event, and begins to run again only when the tolling event has concluded.”).) Here, Plaintiff only states she is eligible for tolling pursuant to this rule, without explaining why or how. As such, the Court does not find this rule tolled the statute of limitations here.

Next, Plaintiff argues she has in the past and continues to suffer from an inability to handle her business affairs as a competent adult. Plaintiff asserts in her opposition that was legally incapacitated due to illness and infirmity, not due to age. However, Plaintiff’s assertion is not verified or supported by a declaration under penalty of perjury. Moreover, the FAC does not contain any allegations that Plaintiff was legally incapacitated in May 2019, the date she discovered the alleged fraudulent transfer and her causes of action against Defendant began to accrue. Thus, the Court does not find any alleged legal incapacity tolled the statute of limitations.

The Court does not find Plaintiff has shown that the pleading can be amended successfully. Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) Not only did Plaintiff not request leave to amend in her Opposition, but there was no indication in the Opposition that Plaintiff would be able to cure the defects of the FAC. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Service (2000) 81 Cal. App. 4th 39, 43, 96 Cal.Rptr.2d 354, 356). Plaintiff’s claim that she was legally incapacitated is factually inconsistent with the allegations in the prior pleadings that state when and how Plaintiff became aware of the fraudulent transfer. (FAC ¶¶ 13, 24.) A proposed amendment to a pleading that is "factually inconsistent" with prior pleadings and there is no plausible explanation for the inconsistency, should be denied by the Court as a sham pleading. (See Smyth v. Berman (2019) 31 Cal. App. 14 5th 183, 195-196.) Therefore, the Court does not find amendment of the FAC should be permitted. There is no basis in law to grant leave to amend where, “the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Casterson v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 190).

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.  

 

E.     Conclusion

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
