On November 27, 2019, Plaintiff Hyung Koo filed this personal injury action.  Plaintiff later identified Defendant Doe 1 as Jamison Services Inc. (“Jamison Services”), Doe 2 as Central Plaza LLC (“Central Plaza”), and Doe 3 as Jamison Realty Advisors LLC (“JRA”).

On July 24, 2023, JRA filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.

On November 30, 2023, Jamison Services and Central Plaza filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.

LEGAL STANDARD

For each claim in the complaint, the defendant moving for adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by showing that one or more elements of a cause of action cannot be established or that there is a complete defense to a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520 (Scalf).)  Then the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf, supra, 128 Cal.App.4th at p. 1520.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.”  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162-163.)

JRA’S MOTION

The first and second causes of action allege general negligence and premises liability.  (The third cause of action is not brought against JRA.)  JRA argues that it owed no legal duty to Plaintiff and did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries.

A.        Request for Judicial Notice

JRA’s request for judicial notice of the Baycap LLC Financing Statement listing the subject cross-over machine as collateral issued by Baycap LLC, recorded on August 16, 2012, is granted.

B.        Evidentiary Objections

JRA’s Objection Nos. 1-8:  Sustained.  (Summers v. A.L. Gilbert Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1155, 1180 [expert opinion on the legal interpretation of contracts is inadmissible].)

JRA’s Objection Nos. 9-29:  Overruled.

C.        Background Facts

Plaintiff’s accident occurred on November 8, 2019, while he was using a cable crossover machine at the Wilfit Sports Club (“Gym”) located in Suite 100 of 3470 Wilshire Blvd, Los Angeles, California 90010.  (Undisputed Material Facts “UMF” 1-2.)  Central Fitness LP (“Central Fitness”) dba Wilfit Sports Club, formerly Central Fitness LLC, was the owner of the Gym.  (UMF 3.)  The Gym was located in a building owned and leased by Central Plaza LLC (“Central Plaza”), and Jamison Services Inc. (“Jamison Services”) was the sole property management company for the building.  (UMF 4-5; see UMF 7.)  JRA was not involved in the management of the Central Plaza buildings.  (UMF 9.)

JRA is a placeholder entity to assist with real estate acquisitions.  (UMF 6.)  JRA has never owned the Gym, was not involved in its maintenance, and did not exercise control over the Gym.  (UMF 8, 10-11.)

TechnoGym USA Corp. (“TechnoGym”) sold the Machine to Central Fitness, and BayCap financed the purchase of the Machine.  (UMF 12.)  The only payments made for the purchase of the Machine were made by Central Fitness.  (UMF 13.) JRA never owned, assembled, installed, or maintained the Machine.  (UMF 14-17.)

D.        Discussion

JRA argues that Plaintiff cannot establish its duty when it had no ownership, possession, or control over the premises.  (Motion at pp. 3-4.)  JRA argues that “the undisputed evidence shows that JRA did not own, possess or even manage the Gym or the building where Plaintiff was injured, much less had anything to do with the subject crossover machine.”  (Motion at p. 3.)

A cause of action for negligence requires (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) causation, and (4) damage to the plaintiff.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.)  The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same.”  (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158.)  “In premises liability cases, summary judgment properly may be granted where a defendant unequivocally establishes its lack of ownership, possession, or control of property alleged to be in a dangerous or defective condition.”  (Lopez v. Superior Court (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 705, 714.)

JRA’s evidence shows that it never owned, assembled, installed, or maintained the Machine.  (UMF 14-17.)  JRA did not own or manage the building, never owned the Gym, was not involved in its maintenance, and did not exercise control over the Gym.  (UMF 4-5, 7-11.)

JRA has met its moving burden of showing that Plaintiff cannot establish all elements of his claims—namely JRA’s duty to Plaintiff and causation of Plaintiff’s harm.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Plaintiff argues that JRA, Central Plaza, Jamison Services, and Central Fitness are indistinguishable entities operating as a single enterprise who built the Gym as part of a joint venture.  (Opposition at pp. 7, 10-14.)  Single enterprise is a theory of liability, not a cause of action.  (See Las Palmas Associates v. Las Palmas Center Associates (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1220.)  “Two conditions are generally required for the application of the [single enterprise] doctrine to two related corporations: (1) such a unity of interest and ownership that the separate corporate personalities are merged, so that one corporation is a mere adjunct of another or the two companies form a single enterprise; and (2) an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.”  (Tran v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1219.)  “A joint venture exists when there is ‘an agreement between the parties under which they have a community of interest, that is, a joint interest, in a common business undertaking, an understanding as to the sharing of profits and losses, and a right of joint control.’”  (Connor v. Great Western Sav. & Loan Ass’n (1968) 69 Cal.2d 850, 863.)

This theory of liability was not alleged in the Complaint.  (See Simmons v. Ware (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1050 [“[The] complaint was wholly inadequate to raise a joint venture and at no time did [he] seek to amend [his] complaint to conform to proof.”].)  “[A] party cannot successfully resist summary judgment on a theory not pleaded.”  (Whelihan v. Espinoza (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1566, 1576.)

Even considering Plaintiff’s argument, Plaintiff has not shown a triable issue of material fact regarding a single enterprise or joint venture.  Plaintiff contends that JRA “either entered into a joint venture or was a single enterprise with respect to Wilfit.  Ownership of the relevant companies, including Jamison Realty Advisors, were all owned, organized, and operated by Dr. David Lee and his family.”  (E.g., Response to UMF 3.)  Plaintiff also contends that “Central Plaza and Jamison Services shared in the profits and losses of Wilfit,” without presenting evidence about JRA’s profit sharing.  (E.g., Response to UMF 3.)  When arguing that JRA “along with the other entities, controlled, funded, and oversaw Wilfit’s construction,” Plaintiff cites evidence about Jamison Services hiring Philmont Management for construction, Central Plaza being the sole entity paying for construction, and Jamison Services’ property management and inspection—without evidence of JRA’s involvement.  (E.g., Response to UMF 3.)

Plaintiff also argues that there are triable issues regarding JRA’s ownership of the Machine.  (Opposition at pp. 15-16.)  Plaintiff generally relies on the Baycap Equipment Leasing Agreement, under which Central Plaza, Jamison Services, and “Jamison Realty Inc.” agreed to make payments for the Machine.  (Opposition at p. 16; Opposition, Ex. 31.)  JRA is not a party to this agreement.

Summary judgment is granted in favor of JRA.

E.        Conclusion

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  JRA is ordered to submit a proposed judgment within five days.

A Non-Appearance Case Review Re: Submission of Proposed Judgment is scheduled for May 22, 2024 at 9:00 a.m.

JAMISON SERVICES’ AND CENTRAL PLAZA’S MOTION

The first and second causes of action allege general negligence and premises liability.  (The third cause of action is not brought against Jamison Services and Central Plaza.)  Jamison Services and Central Plaza (“Defendants”) argue that they owed no legal duty to Plaintiff and did not cause Plaintiff’s injuries.

A.        Evidentiary Objections

Defendants’ Objection Nos. 1-8:  Sustained.  (Summers v. A.L. Gilbert Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1155, 1180 [expert opinion on the legal interpretation of contracts is inadmissible].)

Defendants’ Objection Nos. 9-29:  Overruled.

B.        Background Facts

Jamison Services is a property management company, and Central Plaza was the owner of the relevant property.  (Undisputed Material Facts “UMF” 1-3.)  As property manager, Jamison Services would conduct inspections of Central Plaza premises, including when construction was done for tenant improvements.  (UMF 4.)  Jamison Services would also inspect the premises before the tenant took possession, in accordance with tenant lease agreements.  (UMF 5, 8.)  Jamison Services would not inspect tenant-specific business equipment and/or fixtures, and it was not responsible for ensuring that a tenant correctly installed its business equipment.  (UMF 6-7.)

In April 2011, Wilfit entered into a lease agreement with Defendants, with the lease beginning on July 1, 2011.  (UMF 9-10.)  Per the lease agreement, Wilfit agreed to maintain equipment and fixtures in good repair.  (UMF 42.)  The parties’ lease further provides that “[a]ll business and trade fixtures, machinery and equipment, furniture, movable partitions and items of personal property owned by the Tenant or installed by the Tenant at its expense in the Premises shall be and remain the property of the Tenant.”   (UMF 43.)  Before Wilfit took final possession of the premises, Jonathan Kim from Jamison Services conducted a walkthrough of the space with a Wilfit representative.  (UMF 11.)  Jamison Services inspected only those parts of the construction related to the premises to make sure that everything regarding the building premises was done properly.  (UMF 16.)

Defendants did not purchase any gym equipment for or on behalf of Wilfit, and they were not involved in the selection or purchase of equipment from TechnoGym.  (UMF 17.)  The subject cable crossover machine was purchased on approximately August 11, 2011, and Wilfit financed the purchase of the machine.  (UMF 18.)  The cable crossover machine was delivered to Wilfit on July 18, 2012.  (UMF 19.)  Jamison Services employee Jin Soo Choi gave the delivery company access to Wilfit so that the machine could be delivered.  (UMF 20.)  Mr. Choi did not supervise the delivery company’s delivery or accompany the delivery company to Wilfit; he merely let them into the building.  (UMF 23.)  At the time of delivery, Mr. Choi signed what he believed was a document confirming the delivery.  (UMF 25, 30.)  He did not believe that either he or his company was taking responsibility for the installation of the cable crossover machine, and he did not tell anyone at Jamison Services that he had signed this form.  (UMF 26-27.)

C.        Discussion

Defendants argue that they had no actual knowledge that the machine needed to be secured to the premises or that the machine was not properly installed or secured.  (Motion at pp. 8-10.)  Defendants also argue that they cannot be liable for accidents after the tenant took possession.  (Motion at pp. 10-12.)  “‘[I]t is the general rule that [the landlord] is under no obligation to anyone to look after the premises or keep them in repair, and is not responsible, either to persons injured on the land or to those outside of it, for conditions which develop or are created by the tenant after possession has been transferred.’”  (Uccello v. Laudenslayer (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 504, 511.)  “Public policy precludes landlord liability for a dangerous condition on the premises which came into existence after possession has passed to a tenant.”  (Garcia v. Holt (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 600, 604.)  “[B]efore liability may be thrust on a landlord for a third party’s injury due to a dangerous condition on the land, the plaintiff must show that the landlord had actual knowledge of the dangerous condition in question, plus the right and ability to cure the condition.”  (Ibid., quotation marks omitted.)  “‘A landlord cannot be held to be responsible for all dangers inherent in a dangerous business,’” including those inherent dangers at a gym.  (Day v. Lupo Vine Street, L.P. (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 62, 73.)

Wilfit’s lease began on July 1, 2011.  (UMF 10.)  Jamison Services conducted a walkthrough of the space with a Wilfit representative before Wilfit took final possession.  (UMF 11.)  The machine was purchased on August 11, 2011 and delivered on July 18, 2012—after Wilfit took possession.  (UMF 18-19.)  Defendants did not purchase any gym equipment for or on behalf of Wilfit, and they were not involved in the selection or purchase of equipment from TechnoGym.  (UMF 17.)  Jamison Services is not involved in the operation of the gym or operating a gym for the tenant.  (UMF 44.)  Under the lease, Wilfit was responsible for maintain its fixtures, and ““[a]ll business and trade fixtures, machinery and equipment” installed by Wilfit remained its own property.  (UMF 42-43.)

Defendants have met their moving burden of showing that Plaintiff cannot establish all elements of his claims—namely Defendants’ duty to Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Plaintiff argues that Defendants, JRA, and Central Fitness are indistinguishable entities operating as a single enterprise who built the gym as part of a joint venture.  (Opposition at pp. 7-9, 13-14.)  Single enterprise is a theory of liability, not a cause of action.  (See Las Palmas Associates v. Las Palmas Center Associates (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1220.)  “Two conditions are generally required for the application of the [single enterprise] doctrine to two related corporations: (1) such a unity of interest and ownership that the separate corporate personalities are merged, so that one corporation is a mere adjunct of another or the two companies form a single enterprise; and (2) an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.”  (Tran v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1219.)  “A joint venture exists when there is ‘an agreement between the parties under which they have a community of interest, that is, a joint interest, in a common business undertaking, an understanding as to the sharing of profits and losses, and a right of joint control.’”  (Connor v. Great Western Sav. & Loan Ass’n (1968) 69 Cal.2d 850, 863.)

This theory of liability was not alleged in the Complaint.  (See Simmons v. Ware (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 1035, 1050 [“[The] complaint was wholly inadequate to raise a joint venture and at no time did [he] seek to amend [his] complaint to conform to proof.”].)  “[A] party cannot successfully resist summary judgment on a theory not pleaded.”  (Whelihan v. Espinoza (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1566, 1576.)

Even considering Plaintiff’s argument, Plaintiff has not shown a triable issue of material fact regarding a single enterprise or joint venture.  Plaintiff contends that Defendants “either entered into a joint venture or were a single enterprise with respect to Wilfit Sports Club.  Ownership of the relevant companies, including Defendants, were all owned, organized, and operated by Dr. David Lee and his family.”  (E.g., Response to UMF 7.)   However, Defendants were not involved with the purchase of any equipment, and Jamison Services is not involved in the operation of the gym.  (UMF 17, 44.)  Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of material fact regarding unity of interest and ownership or a common business undertaking to establish a single enterprise or joint venture.  (See Reply at pp. 8-12.)

Plaintiff also argues negligent undertaking when an employee signed the “Securing Equipment Disclaimer” from TechnoGym and provides access to the gym for the delivery of the machine.  (Opposition at p. 27.)  However, Defendants’ alleged liability arises from failure to anchor/secure the machine and failure to warn.  (Complaint at p. 5.)  “A moving party seeking summary judgment or adjudication is not required to go beyond the allegations of the pleading, with respect to new theories that could have been pled, but for which no motion to amend or supplement the pleading was brought, prior to the hearing on the dispositive motion.”  (Irvine Company LLC v. Superior Court of Orange County (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 858, 875, quotation marks omitted.)  When a complaint does not suggest a negligence claim based on anything other than physical conditions or certain conduct, a motion for summary judgment need not address an unpleaded claim for negligent undertaking.  (Ibid.)

Summary judgment is granted in favor of Defendants.

D.        Conclusion

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Jamison Services and Central Plaza are ordered to submit a proposed judgment within five days.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 14, 2024

 

On April 20, 2023, the Court granted Defendant Razmer #3 LLC’s motion for summary judgment of Plaintiff Varouj Krajian’s first amended complaint.  The Court entered judgment on May 8, 2023.

On July 7, 2023, Defendant filed a motion for attorney fees.  Defendant seeks $184,251.00 in attorney fees.

A.        The Motion is Not Premature.

Plaintiff argues that the motion is premature because an appeal is pending.  (Opposition at pp. 4-5.)  However, “the filing of a notice of appeal does not deprive the trial court of jurisdiction to award attorney fees as costs post trial.  Although a prevailing party at trial may not be the prevailing party after an appeal, it has been held that a motion for attorney fees is not premature despite the filing of a notice of appeal.”  (Bankes v. Lucas (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 365, 368.)

B.        Defendant May Recover Attorney Fees Pursuant to the License Agreement.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant is not entitled to attorney fees under the contract.  (Opposition at pp. 3-4.)  A prevailing party may recover reasonable attorney fees in any action on a contract where the contract specifically provides that attorney fees and costs incurred to enforce that contract shall be awarded, whether that party is specified in the contract or not.  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a).)  “California courts liberally construe the term ‘“‘on a contract””’ as used within section 1717.  [Citation.]  As long as the action ‘involve[s]’ a contract it is ‘on [the] contract’ within the meaning of Section 1717.  [Citations.]”  (Dell Merk, Inc. v. Franzia (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 443, 455.)

The License Agreement provides for an award of attorney fees to the prevailing party “[i]n the event litigation is necessary for any party to commence any action to enforce any of the provisions of this agreement.”  (FAC, Ex. C at p. 3.)  According to Plaintiff, “the attorneys’ fees clause in the License Agreement is strictly limited to actions on a contract,” but here, he used the existence of the License Agreement as evidence of his predecessor-in-interest’s granting of Krajian permission to use the license area.  Krajian’s expenditure of time and money in reasonable reliance on the permission then caused the revocable license granted under the License Agreement to become an irrevocable license.”  (Opposition at pp. 3-4.)  However, the FAC alleged that “Defendants’ Property and Plaintiff’s Property are subject to an irrevocable license agreement.”  (FAC ¶ 14.)  The first cause of action sought to establish Plaintiff’s continued right to use the license area under the “irrevocable License Agreement between Plaintiff’s and Defendants’ predecessors that was intended to run with the land.”  (FAC ¶¶ 38-43.)  Accordingly, this action was one intended to “enforce any of the provisions of this agreement.”

C.        The Court Awards Defendant $165,000 in Attorney Fees.

California courts apply the “lodestar” approach to determine what fees are reasonable.  (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.)  This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)  From there, the “lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”  (Ibid.)  Relevant factors include “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.”  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)  The party seeking fees has the burden of documenting the appropriate hours expended and hourly rates.  (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 784.)

Defendant’s counsel charge various hourly rates: $575 or $595 for Elsa Horowitz; $675 for Christopher Heck; $595 for Mark J. Rosenbaum; $285 or $295 for Vanessa H. Widener; $250 or $265 for Ali Vaqar; and $250 or $265 for Jesse Yanco.  (Horowitz Decl. ¶ 10; Heck Decl. ¶ 5; Rosenbaum Decl. ¶ 5; Widener Decl. ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff argues that some deductions are warranted for duplicative work, work relating to coverage and communication with an insurance carrier, and inflated time.  (Opposition at pp. 6-7.)  Indeed, one firm “has several entries wherein a call with Vanessa is billed,” and the billing records for each firm for the same call differ in the amount of time recorded.  (Id. at p. 7.)  Accordingly, the Court will reduce the award for some of this time.

Plaintiff also argues that the motion for summary judgment was “limited to a narrow set of issues – namely the issue of notice,” so Defendant’s recovery should be limited to $33,511.50, for just the work on the motion for that issue.  (Opposition at pp. 7-8.)  Plaintiff cites no authority for this limitation.  Much of litigation, including discovery, is necessary before reaching the summary judgment stage.  The Court will not limit Defendant’s attorney fees award to time spent on a single issue in the motion for summary judgment.

Considering the type of case, complexity of the case, length of litigation, and the record as a whole, the Court concludes that a reasonable amount of attorney fees is $165,000.00.  (See Kerkeles v. City of San Jose (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 88, 102 [“When a ‘voluminous fee application’ is made, the court may . . . ‘make across-the-board percentage cuts either in the number of hours claimed or in the final lodestar figure.’”].)

D.        Conclusion

The motion for attorney fees is GRANTED IN PART.  The Court awards Defendant $165,000.00 in attorney fees.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING IN PART DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 14, 2024

 

On April 25, 2022, Cohaus LLC filed this action against Family Living Vineyard Avenue LLC, Family Living Avenue 53 LLC, Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC, and others.

On January 11, 2024, Family Living Vineyard Avenue LLC, Family Living Avenue 53 LLC, and Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) filed a first amended cross-complaint (“FACC”) against Lindon Shiao, Cohaus LLC, Young Min Chun, and Sung Ki Min (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”).

On February 9, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed a demurrer.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Cross-Defendants ask the Court to take judicial notice of the Collaboration Agreement.  Because the FACC refers to this agreement, which was also attached to the Complaint, it is an appropriate matter for judicial notice.  (Align Technology, Inc. v. Tran (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 949, 956 fn. 6.) The request is granted.

Cross-Defendants also ask the Court to take judicial notice of the Secretary of State of California business filing records for Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC, showing that entity was created on January 10, 2020.  The request is granted.

DEMURRER

Cross-Defendants demur to the first four causes of action.

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce ex rel. Arce v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 556.)

A.        Cross-Complainants Cannot Allege Breach of Contract by Family Living Vineyard Avenue LLC and Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC (First Cause of Action).

Cross-Defendants argue that Family Living Vineyard Avenue LLC and Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC are not parties to the contract.  (Demurrer at p. 4.)  Only Family Living Avenue 53 LLC and Cohaus LLC are parties to the Collaboration Agreement.  (Complaint, Ex. A; Demurrer RJN, Ex. A.)  Cross-Complainants do not address this in their opposition.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend with respect to Cross-Complainants Family Living Vineyard Avenue LLC’s and Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC’s first cause of action.

B.        The FACC Sufficiently Alleges the Elements for Breach of Contract (First Cause of Action).

The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

Cross-Defendants argue that the FACC does not allege that Cross-Complainants performed under the contract or that the breach was a substantial factor in causing their harm.  (Demurrer at pp. 5-6.)  The FACC does, however, allege that “Cross-Complainants fully performed their obligations under the Collaboration Agreement,” and “The Family Living Entities satisfied all material obligations they owed to Cohaus in the Collaboration Agreement.”  (FACC ¶¶ 71, 74.)  The FACC also alleges, “As the proximate result of the foregoing breaches of contract by Cohaus, Cross-Complainants have suffered damages . . . .”  (FACC ¶ 75.)

The demurrer is overruled on this ground.

C.        The FACC Pleads Some, But Not All, Fraud with Specificity (Second Cause of Action).

Cross-Defendants argue that the FACC does not allege fraud with specificity.  (Demurrer at pp. 7-11.)

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231.)  Fraud must be pleaded with specificity.  (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.)  “‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’  [Citation.]  A plaintiff’s burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater.  In such a case, the plaintiff must ‘allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.’  [Citation.]”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 (Lazar).)

The FACC alleges that Shiao and Chun, on behalf of Cohaus made certain representations in October 2019, and Mr. von Watts established the Cross-Complainant entities in reliance on those representations.  (FACC ¶¶ 22-23, 77.)  Other representations were made in the written Collaboration Agreement.  (FACC ¶¶ 25-30, 77.)  Cohaus and its principals (Shiao and Chun) made additional specific representations during meetings in September, October, and November 2019.  (FACC ¶¶ 31-39, 77.)  These representations ended up being false.  (See FACC ¶¶ 54-60, 78.)  Cross-Complainants allege that they “justifiably and reasonably relied on these misrepresentations” to their detriment.  (FACC ¶¶ 79-80.)  Although there are not facts about Cross-Complainants’ reliance on most representations, there are facts about reliance for at least one representation.  (FACC ¶¶ 22-23.)  “Ordinarily, a general demurrer does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action, and if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the demurrer will be overruled.”  (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.)  The demurrer is therefore overruled on this ground.

Cross-Defendants argue that Family Living Berryman LLC was not in existence until January 14, 2020.  (Demurrer at p. 8; see Demurrer RJN, Ex. B.)  The demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend with respect to Cross-Complainant Family Living Berryman Avenue LLC’s second cause of action.

Cross-Defendants argue that there is no specific allegation of intentional misrepresentation by Min.  (Demurrer at p. 8.)  The demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend with respect to allegations about Sung Ki Min.

D.        The Cause of Action for Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing is Superfluous (Third Cause of Action).

Cross-Defendants argue that the third cause of action is superfluous because there are no allegations about what benefits they prevented Cross-Complainants from receiving in addition to the contract damages.  (Demurrer at pp. 11-12.)

“If the allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated.  Thus, absent those limited cases where a breach of a consensual contract term is not claimed or alleged, the only justification for asserting a separate cause of action for breach of the implied covenant is to obtain a tort recovery.”  (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.)

The FACC alleges that the Collaboration Agreement “contains an implied covenant to seek out and deliver to the Family Living Entities qualified investment opportunities and to manage the development and construction of their operation in an ethical, safe, and appropriate manner.”  (FACC ¶ 84.)  The agreement contains an express term that “COHAUS will present qualified projects meeting the investment criteria for consideration by Family Living Avenue LLC.”  (Demurrer RJN, Ex. A, § 3(a).)  Additionally, “[o]nce a property undergoes conversion into a coliving facility and becomes operational, COHAUS will manage all aspects of operations.”  (Id., § 5(a).)  The alleged implied covenant is therefore superfluous of the breach of contract claim.

The demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

E.        The FACC Sufficiently Alleges Unfair Business Practices (Fourth Cause of Action).

California’s Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”) includes any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading advertising.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.)  The UCL embraces “anything that can properly be called a business practice and that at the same time is forbidden by law.”  (Cel-Tech Communications, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 180.)  “By proscribing any unlawful business practice, section 17200 borrows violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices that the unfair competition law makes independently actionable.”  (Ibid.; see Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969 [“Virtually any law can serve as the predicate for a section 17200 action.”].)

Cross-Defendants argue that “[n]o specific unlawful, fraudulent and/or unfair business practices are attributed to them, and the request to enjoin conduct and disgorge profits is so vague as to be unenforceable.”  (Demurrer at p. 12.)  The fourth cause of action incorporates all prior allegations, including those about fraud.  (FACC ¶¶ 89-90.)  The FACC alleges that “Cross-Defendants have reaped and continue to reap unfair benefits and illegal profits at the expense of Cross-Complainants.  Cross-Defendants should be enjoined from such conduct and made to disgorge their ill-gotten gains and restore to Cross-Complainants all wrongfully obtained benefits and profits.  Cross-Defendants should also disgorge their ill-gotten gains kept from any monies they wrongfully obtained though their wrongful actions.”  (FACC ¶ 92.)  This is sufficient at the pleading stage.

The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer is SUSTAINED IN PART and OVERRULED IN PART as set forth above.  Cross-Complainants are granted 30 days’ leave to amend as set forth above.

A party may move to compel a further response to interrogatories if the demanding party deems an answer to be evasive or incomplete, if an exercise of the option to produce documents is unwarranted or inadequate, or if objection is without merit or too general.  (Code Civ. FILING DEFICIENCIES

Each set of discovery should have been filed as a separate motion, with separate filing fees and hearing reservations.  Despite being scheduled as only three motions and three hearings, the total substance is that of six motions.  This unfairly allows the parties to take only three hearing reservations (instead of six) and results in an inaccurate projection and accounting of the Court’s workload, inconveniencing both the Court and other litigants.

All parties are ordered not to do this again and are warned that continued action of this type may result in monetary sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 177.5.  For any future discovery motions, the parties must file a separate motion for each set of discovery, or the Court may strike or deny the motions for being improperly filed.
