DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV17411    Hearing Date: January 18, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Veronica Maes filed this action against Defendant, Harbor Industrial Services Corporation on 5/10/21.  Plaintiff’s judicial council form complaint includes causes of action for general negligence and premises liability, and alleges Plaintiff was injured when Defendant’s crane operator struck her with a crane due to a computer malfunction. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

a.     Standard on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)

Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

c.     Initial Note re: Summary Adjudication

Defendant, in its notice of motion, indicates it seeks summary adjudication of each cause of action in Plaintiff’s complaint.  Defendant’s separate statement is not technically compliant with CRC 3.1350(b) which requires each issue to be adjudicated to be set forth “verbatim” in the notice of motion and separate statement.  The Court will rule on the issues despite the defect, but asks Counsel to ensure compliance with all Rules of Court in the future in connection with this and other actions. 

 

d.     Initial Notes re: Separate Statement

Defendant consistently refers to deposition testimony as a “material fact.”  See facts 10-12, 55-61, 63-67, 69-71, 93-95, 139-145, 147-151, and 153-155.  Pursuant to Reeves v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 95, 105-106, what someone said in deposition is not a material fact.  It may be evidence of a material fact, but the statement itself is not the fact. 

 

Additionally, it appears Defendant used the same 81 facts to support each of its requests for summary adjudication.  CRC 3.1350(d)(2) requires the separate statement to ONLY include facts material to disposition of the issue to be adjudicated.  By way of example, Defendant’s facts 4 and 5, found in connection with the separate statement concerning the negligence cause of action, states that Plaintiff, at page 5 of her complaint, sues for products liability and does so under a manufacturing, design, and/or sale theory.  These facts cannot, in any way, relate to the NEGLIGENCE cause of action, which is the cause of action that is the subject of the first half of the separate statement. 

 

Pursuant to Beltran v. Hard Rock Hotel Licensing, Inc., 2023 WL 8430581:

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (b)(1), requires each motion for summary judgment to be accompanied by a separate statement “setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.”  (Italics added.)  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1350(d)(2)[1] states:  “The separate statement should include only material facts and not any facts that are not pertinent to the disposition of the motion.”  Under the Rules of Court, “‘Material facts’ are facts that relate to the cause of action, claim for damages, issue of duty, or affirmative defense that is the subject of the motion and that could make a difference in the disposition of the motion.”  (Rule 3.1350(a)(2).)

What neither the rule nor the statute states is that the moving party must include in the separate statement every fact they intend to include in their motion, regardless of its materiality.  For example, HRH’s very first “material fact” in its separate statement is:  “The Hard Rock brand is known worldwide for its connection to music, fashion, and entertainment.”  Under no interpretation of “material” does this qualify – it is merely background information that has no relevance to any cause of action or defense.

This is far from the only paragraph in the three separate statements that make absolutely no difference in the disposition of the motion.  The paragraphs in a separate statement should be limited to facts that address the elements of a cause of action or an affirmative defense.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1); rule 3.1350(a)(2), (d)(2).)  The statute and Rules of Court do not preclude litigants from including background, nonmaterial information in their papers as long as they include a cite to the evidence, but nonmaterial facts should not be included in the separate statement.  The point of the separate statement is not to craft a narrative, but to be a concise list of the material facts and the evidence that supports them.  “The separate statement serves two important functions in a summary judgment proceeding:  It notifies the parties which material facts are at issue, and it provides a convenient and expeditious vehicle permitting the trial court to hone in on the truly disputed facts.”  (Collins v. Hertz Corp. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 64, 74.)  There is nothing convenient or expeditious about the separate statements submitted in this case.

 

Similar to the defects in Beltran, Defendant in this motion includes myriad facts in its separate statement that are not ultimately material to resolution of the motion.  The only facts material to resolution of the negligence cause of action are facts relating to whether there was a computer malfunction of the subject crane.  Facts concerning, for example, Plaintiff’s employment, what a chassis is, what a bomb cart is, etc., are all background facts, but are not material to resolution of the merits of the motion. 

 

The motion is summarily denied.  Pursuant to Beltran, supra, “Trial courts should not hesitate to deny summary judgment motions when the moving party fails to draft a compliant separate statement – and an inappropriate separate statement includes an overly long document that includes multiple nonmaterial facts in violation of the Rules of Court.”

Plaintiff, Martin Delgado filed this action against Defendant, Super Center Concepts, Inc. on 9/29/21 for damages arising out of a fall Plaintiff sustained while shopping at Defendant’s store.  Specifically, Plaintiff alleges he was using a mobility scooter and, upon standing to exit the scooter, the scooter seat base broke and/or detached from its base, causing Plaintiff to sustain injuries.

1.     Motion to Quash

Amigo propounded a deposition subpoena for production of business records on Plaintiff’s primary care physician.  Plaintiff moves to quash the subpoena. 

 

a.     Parties’ Positions

The subpoena at issue is directed at Dr. Cynthia Miller.  Dr. Miller is Plaintiff’s primary care medical provider, and has been since at least 2011.  The subpoena seeks production of Plaintiff’s complete medical records from 2011 forward, with no other limitations.  Plaintiff argues the subpoena should be limited to records relating to the body parts at issue in this litigation, which include his left knee, left shoulder, back, neck, and left hip.    

 

Defendant, in opposition to the motion, contends Plaintiff suffers from numerous chronic conditions, including arthritis, diabetes, hypertension, obesity, heart disease, asthma, severe sleep apnea, neuropathy, depression, and anxiety.  Defendant contends it is likely those conditions have contributed to any complaints Plaintiff raises about his knee, shoulder, back, neck, and/or hip, such that his comprehensive medical records should be produced.    

 

i.              Law Governing Discovery of Medical Records

For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.  Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.  (Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301.)  These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery (Colonial Life & Accident Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 785, 790), and again (contrary to popular belief), fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.  (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 385.)

 

By filing a personal injury action, plaintiffs place in issue their past and present physical and/or mental conditions related to the injury sued upon.  All medical and/or psychological records relating to the claimed injuries are thus discoverable.  (Evidence Code §§ 996, 1016; Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 862–864.)  Normally, information about medical conditions entirely different from the injury sued upon is beyond the scope of discovery.  However, medical records pertaining to an unrelated condition are discoverable on a showing of “good cause” if the condition is relevant to the issue of proximate causation.  (Evidence Code §999; Slagle v. Superior Court (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1309, 1314–1315 [good cause shown by info that plaintiff was blind 6 months before accident].)

 

ii.             Analysis

The Court finds Defendant’s position is well-taken.  Plaintiff seems to be misunderstanding Defendant’s position.  Defendant’s position is not that Plaintiff’s myriad chronic conditions were caused by the accident, as argued at page 2, lines 16-19 of the reply, but instead that Plaintiff’s myriad chronic conditions likely contributed to the very same damages he is contending resulted from the accident.  By way of example, if Plaintiff has chronic arthritis, his arthritis could play a role in the numerous skeletal-muscular issues he claims were caused by the accident itself.  Especially where, as here, Plaintiff is claiming almost a million dollars in past and future medical expenses, Defendant is entitled to discover and argue some of those expenses would have been incurred regardless of the injuries sustained in the lawsuit. 

 

The Court does, however, find the duration of the subpoena is excessive.  Defendant seeks records from 2011 forward.  The accident occurred in 2021.  In general, the Court believes medical records more than five years old are not relevant to Plaintiff’s current and/or probable future medical condition.  If Plaintiff complained of something more than five years prior to the accident, and it was not raised again during the subsequent five years, it appears to have fully resolved such that it would no longer be relevant.  The Court therefore asks the parties to meet and confer to draft a new deposition subpoena that is limited in duration to no more than five years.   

 

iii.            Sanctions 

Both parties seek sanctions in connection with the motion and opposition.  CCP §1987.2(a) permits the Court, in its discretion, to award sanctions in connection with a motion to quash if the Court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification. 

 

The Court finds both parties took their positions in good faith and sanctions are not justified.  Issues relating to patient privacy are complicated.  Plaintiffs are entitled to defend their rights to privacy, and defendants are entitled to defend the allegations made against them.  No sanctions are imposed.

b.     Standard on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)

Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

d.     Law re: Standard of Care

The standard of care against which the acts of health care providers are to be measured is a matter within the knowledge of experts.  Elcome v. Chin (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 310, 317.  Unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman, the standard of care in a malpractice action can only be proved by an expert’s testimony.  Id.  If the “common knowledge” exception does not apply to a medical malpractice action, expert evidence is conclusive and cannot be disregarded.  Id.  A medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because he chooses one medically acceptable method of treatment or diagnosis and it turns out that another medically accepted method would have been a better choice.  CACI 506.  Likewise, a medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because his efforts are unsuccessful, or he makes an error that was reasonable under the circumstances.  CACI 505.

 

Whether the standard of care in the community has been breached presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by opinion testimony unless the medical question is within the common knowledge of laypersons.  See Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App4th 836, 844. “‘When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.’“ (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985.)

 

e.     Law re: Causation

In order to establish that defendant's negligence was a “substantial factor” in causing injury or death, the plaintiff must prove the negligence was of itself sufficient to bring about that harm.  “The law is well settled that in a personal injury action causation must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based upon competent expert testimony.  Mere possibility alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case.  [Citations.]  That there is a distinction between a reasonable medical ‘probability’ and a medical 'possibility' needs little discussion.  There can be many possible ‘causes,’ indeed, an infinite number of circumstances which can produce an injury or disease. A possible cause only becomes ‘probable’ when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action.  This is the outer limit of inference upon which an issue may be submitted to the jury.” Bromme v. Pavitt (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1498; citing Jones v. Ortho Pharmaceutical Corp. (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 396, 402 403.

 

f.      Initial Note re: Summary Adjudication

Defendants indicate, in their notices of motion, that they seek summary judgment or summary adjudication for multiple reasons.  They then list the reasons.  They never, however, list what issues are to be adjudicated.  Notably, CCP §437c(f) permits a motion for summary adjudication of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.  It does not permit summary adjudication of a “reason.”  Defendants’ notices of motion never indicate which causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, and/or issues of duty they wish to have adjudicated.  The Court therefore will consider only the motions for summary judgment, and not the purported alternative motions for summary adjudication. 

 

g.     Dr. Levine’s Moving Burden

Defendant provides the Expert Declaration of Dr. Christopher J.L. Newth, M.D. in support of his motion.  Dr. Newth sets forth his expert qualifications, details Defendant’s care and treatment of Decedent, and ultimately concludes Dr. Levine did not breach the standard of care in connection with his care and treatment, and nothing he did caused or contributed to Decedent’s death.  Dr. Levine therefore met his moving burden to show that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law on the negligence claims against him.  Additionally, Dr. Levine establishes that the crux of Plaintiff’s fraud claim is that Dr. Levine failed to disclose his own negligence; however, if there was no negligence, there can be no failure to disclose.  The fraud claim therefore rises and falls with the negligence claim, and Dr. Levine met his burden to negate the claim.    

 

h.     Opposing Burden

As noted above, the Court refuses to consider Plaintiff’s opposition papers because they exceed the page limit.  Additionally, the Court notes that Plaintiff presented the Declaration of Jill Glenn, MSN-ED., R.N., in support of her opposition to the motion.  In Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 970, the Court of Appeals considered a situation where the plaintiff opposing a summary judgment motion submitted the expert declaration of a doctor in support of her position that the defendant hospital, through its nursing staff, breached the standard of care.  The Court of Appeals found the doctor failed to show he possessed any certification, expertise, or relevant knowledge of the standards of care attributable to nurses, hospitals, or hospital employees, other than physicians and surgeons.  The Court therefore found the doctor’s declaration failed to raise a triable issue of material fact as to the hospital, and the summary judgment motion was properly granted. 

 

In this case, we have the reverse situation.  We have a nurse purporting to opine as to the standard of care that applies to a physician.  The same logic, however, governs the Court’s inquiry.  Glenn failed to show she is familiar with the standard of care that governs physicians, and therefore, even if the Court were to consider the opposition, it would find the Declaration of Glenn insufficient to raise a triable issue of material fact in opposition to the motion. 

 

i.      Dr. Javahery’s Moving Burden

Dr. Javahery supports his motion with the expert declaration of Nouzhan Sehati, M.D.  Dr. Sehati sets forth his expert qualifications, details Dr. Javahery’s care and treatment of Decedent, and ultimately concludes Dr. Javahery complied with the standard of care and all times and nothing he did caused or contributed to Decedent’s death.  Dr. Javahery therefore met his moving burden to show he is not liable on Plaintiff’s negligence claims pled against him.  Additionally, for the reasons detailed above in connection with Dr. Levine’s motion, Dr. Javahery showed that the fraud causes of action pled against him also fail.  Dr. Javahery therefore met his moving burden to show he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

k.     Dr. Attinasi’s Moving Burden

Plaintiff’s sole claim against Dr. Attinasi is for negligence.  Dr. Attinasi supports her motion with the Declaration of Christopher J.L. Newth, M.D.  Dr. Attinasi met her moving burden to show she is entitled to judgment as a matter of law for the reasons detailed in connection with Dr. Levine’s motion, above. 

 

l.      Plaintiff’s Shifted Burden

Each of the three moving defendants met the initial burden to show entitlement to judgment as a matter of law.  Plaintiff failed to file proper opposition to any motion.  Additionally, the submission of Jill Glenn’s declaration is insufficient, as a matter of law, to raise a triable issue of material fact.  The motions are therefore granted.

2.     Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares multiple attempts to meet and confer were made, but Plaintiff did not respond.  The Court previously advised Plaintiff she must meaningfully participate in meet and confer communications, but she continues to fail to do so. 

 

  1. Initial Notes

The Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the original complaint on 9/19/23.  Plaintiff filed a document captioned amended complaint on 10/19/23.  She then filed two documents, both captioned opposition to demurrer to answer, on 10/19/23 and 10/30/23.  There was no demurrer to the answer pending at the time Plaintiff filed these documents, and it appears the documents are intended to supplement the amended complaint in some way. 

 

  1. Analysis

Plaintiff’s purported amended complaint is not filed in conformity with the law, is not a proper complaint, does not state a cause of action, and is uncertain.  The amended complaint does not comply with the California Rules of Court.  CRC 2.112 requires the complaint to separately identify each cause of action being pled.  Plaintiff’s amended complaint does not even purport to state any cause of action.  Instead, it includes only one caption, which is “memorandum.”  A memorandum is not a cause of action. 

 

The demurrer is sustained.  Because Plaintiff was previously given leave to amend, and because a review of the purported amended complaint reveals an inability to articulate a cause of action against Defendant, leave to amend is denied.

Case Number: 22STCV11375    Hearing Date: January 18, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, ZRP Group, LLC filed this action against Defendants, City of Long Beach and Sami Kherimi for damages arising out of a flood that occurred when the City energized the fire suppression system at Plaintiff’s business location.  Plaintiff alleges Kherimi is the City employee who energized the system.  The City filed a cross-complaint against Long Beach Acquisitions, LLC and Shirley Vancamp for indemnification and related claims.  Long Beach Acquisitions filed a cross-complaint against the City.      

 

2.     Motion to Compel Deposition

a.     Summary Judgment Motion

The City filed a summary judgment motion on 9/20/23, setting it for hearing on 2/22/24.  The City supports its motion, in part, with the Declaration of Todd LeBerge.  LeBerge is an expert fire marshal and managing fire protection engineer.  LeBerge opines, in his declaration, that the City (through its employee Kherimi) did nothing wrong in connection with the flood, and that Plaintiff failed to adequately maintain the fire sprinkler system in operational condition, which caused the flood. 

 

b.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff moves to compel LeBerge to sit for deposition and produce documents.  It contends a deposition is necessary for the purpose of impeaching LeBerge’s declaration.  It contends LeBerge improperly attempts to interpret codes, ordinances, and safety regulations, which is the province of the trial court.  It contends each of LeBerge’s four major opinions asserted in the declaration lack proper foundation. 

 

The City opposes the motion.  It argues there are no circumstances present that permit an early expert deposition.  It argues all of the major opinions are proper and, in any event, a deposition is not the proper way to challenge the opinions at this stage of the proceedings.  It argues that, in the event the deposition is compelled, it should be limited in scope.

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues LeBerge’s testimony regarding operating a valve is not correct based on all available evidence, and also argues his testimony re: Plaintiff’s negligence is not correct based on all available evidence. 

 

c.     Law Governing Early Expert Depositions in Opposition to Summary Judgment

The parties agree that St. Mary Medical Center v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1531 sets forth the test for determining whether an early expert deposition can be conducted.  Where a party has submitted the opinion of the expert in opposition to a motion for summary judgment brought prior to designation, and there is a legitimate question regarding the foundation of the opinion, reasonable discovery may be allowed. Id. at 1540.

 

However, "[t]here must be objective facts presented which create a significant question regarding the validity of the affidavit or declaration which, if successfully pursued, will impeach the foundational basis of the affidavit or declaration in question." Id. at 1540-1541.  In St. Mary, those objective facts existed; there was evidence that one of the defendant doctors had not participated in the procedure at issue, and the defendants' expert submitted evidence that plaintiff's expert was relying on a hypothetical unsupported by the facts of the case.  Under these circumstances, the Court of Appeal concluded defendants should have been permitted to depose plaintiff's expert, as it was possible that plaintiff's expert would retreat from the statements in his declaration, thereby allowing the case to be resolved on summary judgment.  Id. at pp. 1538-1539. The appellate court was clear that this discovery would be allowed only under limited circumstances.

 

d.     Analysis

Plaintiff failed to show the need for an expert deposition prior to the hearing on the summary judgment motion.  Plaintiff showed, at most, a variety of factual disputes that would defeat summary judgment.  None of these disputes, however, goes to the foundation of the expert opinion set forth in the moving declaration. 

 

Plaintiff does not point to any failure on the part of Defendant’s expert to review necessary evidence or to have necessary experience, both of which go to foundation.  Plaintiff, instead, argues the expert has not properly reviewed the evidence, has reached improper conclusions from the evidence, and/or is misconstruing the evidence.  All of this is the proper subject of evidentiary objections and/or submission of contrary factual evidence to defeat the summary judgment motion, but no deposition is necessary in order to submit the objections and evidence. 

 

The motion is denied.
