DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV15786    Hearing Date: December 6, 2023    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     December 6, 2023                  TRIAL DATE: January 23, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         James Weldon Schott v. Miguel A. Melgoza

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV15786           

 

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

MOVING PARTY:               Attorney Christopher L. Hoglin, counsel for Defendant Miguel Melgoza.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 12/1/23.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/24/20: Complaint filed.

·         09/27/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for partition of a parcel of real property and for fraud, conversion, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant induced him to loan money for the purchase of the property to operate it as a dispensary.

 

Attorney Christopher L. Hoglin, counsel for Defendant Miguel Melgoza, moves to be relieved as counsel.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Attorney Christopher L. Hoglin, counsel for Defendant Miguel Melgoza, moves to be relieved as counsel.

 

Moving counsel filed all three required forms (MC-051, -052, and -053) and included a proof of service as required by California Rules of Court rule 3.1362(d).  Moving counsel’s declaration states that he served Defendant by mail at his last known address but could not confirm the address was current beyond a person locator search via LexisNexis showing Defendant is still at his last known address. (MC-052 ¶ 3.)

 

In general, an attorney may withdraw with or without cause as long as the withdrawal would not result in undue prejudice to the client’s interest – i.e., counsel cannot withdraw at a critical point in the litigation, because that would prejudice client, but can withdraw otherwise. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915.) The court has discretion to deny an attorney’s request to withdraw where the withdrawal would work an injustice or cause undue delay in the proceeding, but the court’s discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)   

 

Here, the matter set for trial on January 23, 2024 at 10:00 AM. (MC-052 ¶ 6.) The next hearing on calendar is a Post-Mediation Status Conference set for the same date and time as this motion, followed by a Final Status Conference on January 8, 2024 at 9:00 AM. (MC-052 ¶ 4.). There is a high risk of prejudice to Defendant due to the relative proximity of trial. Moving Counsel’s declaration states, however, that he is no longer able to represent Defendant due to a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship in that Defendant has ceased communicating with Moving Counsel entirely.  (MC-052 ¶ 2.) Despite the risk of prejudice to Defendant, no purpose would be served by forcing Moving Counsel to continue to represent a client at trial when that client is no longer communicating with his counsel. The Court therefore finds that the circumstances of this case warrant withdrawal.

 

            Accordingly, Attorney Christopher L. Hoglin’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant Miguel Melgoza is GRANTED, conditioned on Moving Counsel serving notice on all parties within 10 days of this order.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

 

 

 

Dated:  December 6, 2023                              ___________________________________

                                                                                    Theresa M. Traber

                                                                                    Judge of the Superior Court

CASE:                         Gladys Navarro v. South Bay Safety Inc., et al

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV38140           

 

(1) MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION (SET ONE); REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(2) MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – EMPLOYMENT; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(3) MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES – GENERAL; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Gladys Navarro

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 12/1/23

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         10/05/20: Complaint filed.

·         10/06/20: First Amended Complaint Filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for wrongful termination. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants terminated her employment in retaliation for taking sick days.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to requests for production and form interrogatories propounded to Defendant South Bay Safety, Inc.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production (Set One) is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories – General is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories – Employment is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant South Bay Safety Inc. is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,530 against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally. Payment is to be made within 20 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to requests for production and both general and employment form interrogatories propounded to Defendant South Bay Safety, Inc. In the interest of brevity, the Court will address all three motions together.

 

Legal Standard

 

When a party to whom an inspection demand or interrogatories are directed fails to respond, a party making the demand may move for an order compelling a response to the discovery propounded. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(b); 2031.300(b).) A party who fails to provide timely responses waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(a); 2031.300(a).) For a motion to compel initial responses, no meet and confer is required. All that must be shown is that a set of requests for production was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response has been served. (Leach v. Sup. Ct. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-06.)

 

Entitlement to Order Compelling Response

 

            Plaintiff propounded the discovery at issue on these motions via U.S. mail on Defendants on July 31, 2023. (See, e.g., Declaration of Melanie Rodriguez ISO Mot. Compel Response to RFPs ¶ 7, Exh. 1.) Defendant’s counsel requested and received two discovery extensions, with the final deadline being September 15, 2023. (Id. ¶¶ 13-19, Exhs. 3-6.) However, no responses were ever provided. (Id. ¶ 27.) As Defendant has not responded to these motions, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling responses to this discovery.

 

Entitlement to Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the amount of $2,310 on each of the three motions against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally, for failure to respond to discovery.

 

            Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery is a misuse of the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subd. (a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories or requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290 (c), 2030.300(d); 2031.300 (c); 2031.310(h).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Id.)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subdivision (a) states: “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

 

Here, Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $2,310 on each of the three motions, for a total of $6,930, representing 2 hours of attorney time preparing each motion, plus 3 anticipated hours plus $60 in filing fees for each motion at a rate of $450 per hour. (Rodriguez Decl. ¶ 29.) The Court is not inclined to award anticipated hours which were not actually billed. Moreover, the Court finds that awarding Plaintiff’s counsel for six hours of attorney time on three substantially identical motions would be inappropriately duplicative. The Court will therefore award reduced sanctions in the total amount of $1,530, representing 3 total hours of attorney time at $450 per hour, plus $180 in filing fees for the three motions.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production (Set One) is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories – General is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories – Employment is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant South Bay Safety Inc. is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,530 against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally. Payment is to be made within 20 days of this order.

The court has discretion to deny an attorney’s request to withdraw where the withdrawal would work an injustice or cause undue delay in the proceeding, but the court’s discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably. Here, the matter is set for trial on May 7, 2024. (MC-052 ¶ 6.) Moreover, in light of the breakdown of the attorney-client relationship, the Court finds these circumstances warrant permitting withdrawal.

CASE:                         3BTech Inc. v. Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV39646           

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant West American Ins. Co.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff 3BTech, Inc.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         12/21/22: Complaint filed.

·         05/19/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

This is a breach of contract action. Plaintiff seeks coverage for the loss of inventory destroyed by a fire while in the care of the insured non-party Pro-Com Products, Inc.

 

Defendant West American Insurance Co. demurs to the First Amended Complaint in its entirety.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

            Defendant West American Insurance Co.’s Demurrer to the First Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant West American Insurance Co. demurs to the First Amended Complaint in its entirety.

 

//

 

//

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Attorney Patricia A. Daza-Luu states that the parties met and conferred telephonically and by email on June 6, 2023 to attempt to informally resolve the issues presented in this demurrer. (Declaration of Patricia A. Daza-Luu ISO Demurrer ¶ 3.) The Court therefore finds Defendant has satisfied its statutory meet-and-confer obligations.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests judicial notice of (1) the pertinent portions of the Insurance Policy issued by West American to Pro-Com, Policy No. BKW (21) 59288577, effective from January 11, 2020 to January 11, 2021; (2) the transcript from the April 19, 2023 hearing on the Demurrer to the Complaint; (3) the Court’s April 19, 2023 Minute Order sustaining the demurrer with leave to amend; (4) Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint; and (5) the March 14, 2023 Letter from West American to 3BTech referenced in the First Amended Complaint.  With respect to the Policy in Request No. 1 Defendant’s request is GRANTED as a legally operative document referenced in and relied upon in the pleadings. (FAC ¶ 3, Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 754; Salvaty v. Falcon Cable Television (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 798, 800 n.1.) With respect to the materials referenced in Requests Nos. 2 through 4, those requests are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records). Finally, with respect to Request No. 5, Defendant’s request is GRANTED as a document referenced in and relied on in the pleadings. (FAC ¶¶ 39-40, Ingram v. Flippo (1999) 74 Cal. App. 4th 1280, 1285 n.3.)

 

Standing

 

            Defendants demur to each of the three causes of action asserted in the First Amended Complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff does not have standing under the insurance policy purchased by non-party Pro-Com Products Inc from Defendant West American Insurance Company. The parties agree that each of the three causes of action survive or fail together.

 

            A person who is not insured under an insurance policy generally does not have standing to bring an action for breach of the policy. (Republic Indemnity Company of America v. Schofield (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 220, 227; see also Harper v. Wasau Ins. Co. (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1086.) That said, a third party may bring an action against an insurer as an intended third-party beneficiary if “an intent to make an obligation inure to the benefit of a third party . . . clearly appear[s].” (Rupley v. Huntsman (1958) 159 Cal.App.2d 307, 312.) “[T]hird party beneficiary status is a matter of contract interpretation.” (Schauer v. Mandarin Gems of Cal., Inc. (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 949, 957.)

 

            “When a dispute arises over the meaning of contract language, the first question to be decided is whether the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged by the party. If it is not, the case is over.” (S. Cal. Edison Co. v. Super. Ct., (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 839, 847-48.) “If the court decides the language is reasonably susceptible to the interpretation urged, the court moves to the second question: what did the parties intend the language to mean? ... Thus, where contract language is clear and explicit and does not lead to absurd results, we ascertain intent from the written terms and go no further. If the contract is capable of more than one reasonable interpretation, it is ambiguous.”  (Department of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Lawrence Livermore National Security, LLC (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1060, 1066 [internal quotation marks, ellipses, and citations omitted].) Interpretation of an ambiguous contract requires the consideration of extrinsic evidence, and is therefore not well-suited to resolution via demurrer. (See Brown v. Goldstein (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 418, 432-33 [describing evidentiary considerations necessary for determination of the legal question of the meaning of a contract via motion for summary judgment].)

 

            The parties agree the relevant provision in the Policy is the “Personal Property of Others” provision within Policy’s Commercial Property Coverage. This provision states:

 

Personal Property of Others that is:

 

            (1) in your care, custody, or control; and

 

(2) Located in or on the building or structure described in the Declarations or in the open (or in a vehicle) within 100 feet of the building or structure or within 100 feet of the premises described in the Declarations, whichever distance is greater.

 

However, our payment for loss of or damage to personal property of others will only be for the account of the owner of the property.

 

(FAC ¶ 3, RJN Exh. A p. 2 § A.1.c.) It is alleged that this language “required that some or all of the [$7 million Defendant paid for the loss here] be paid to 3BTech,” so as to comply with the clause requirement any “payment for loss” of the property of others “will only be for the account of the owner of the property.” (Id., ¶ 6.)

 

            Although Plaintiff states in the Complaint that it brings this action as a party “directly entitled to benefits under the policy” and “as a third party beneficiary of the policy issued by Defendants to PROCOM” (FAC ¶ 36), the thrust of Plaintiff’s legal theory advanced in the pleadings and in its opposition is not that it is a party to the policy, but that it is an intended third-party beneficiary to the policy under the provision for Personal Property of Others. (See generally Opposition.) Thus, the question raised by the demurrer is, as before, whether the referenced policy language makes Plaintiff a third-party beneficiary of the insurance policy, such that it has standing to sue the insurer for a perceived breach of the Policy’s terms.

 

            Defendant previously demurred to the Complaint on the same basis asserted here arguing Plaintiff does not have standing under the insurance policy to bring its claims. (See February 7, 2023 Demurrer.) The Court concluded that, under the language of the Policy, Plaintiff was not an intended third-party beneficiary, but rather an incidental beneficiary, and therefore sustained the demurrer for lack of standing. (April 19, 2023 Minute Order.) The Court granted Plaintiff leave to allege additional extrinsic facts which might support Plaintiff’s interpretation of the relevant provisions in the insurance policy. (Id.) Plaintiff filed its First Amended Complaint on May 19, 2023, incorporating new factual allegations. (See FAC.) Defendant contends that these new allegations still do not suffice to establish standing as an intended third-party beneficiary.

 

            The new allegations added in the First Amended Complaint, as Defendant describes, can be separated into three general categories: (1) allegations regarding the structure of the Insurance Policy (FAC ¶¶ 2-4); (2) references to out-of-state authorities supporting Plaintiff’s position (FAC ¶ 5); and (3) allegations pertaining to the parties’ discussion of Plaintiff’s claims. (FAC ¶¶ 28-30, 38-40, 52, 58-60, 84-86.)

 

            The California Supreme Court addressed the circumstances when a nonsignatory has standing to assert rights under a contract as a third-party beneficiary in Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817.  Under Goonewardene, a non-party to a contract is a third party beneficiary if it demonstrates “not only (1) that it is likely to benefit from the contract, but also (2) that a motivating purpose of the contracting parties is to provide a benefit to the third party, and further (3) that permitting the third party to [assert rights under the contract] against a contracting party is consistent with the objectives of the contract and the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties.”  (Id., at p. 821.)

 

Defendant contends, as it did previously, that Plaintiff does not have standing under the Policy to bring this action because Plaintiff is neither a party to the Policy nor an intended third-party beneficiary of the Policy. Instead, Defendant argues, Plaintiff is merely an incidental beneficiary of the Policy. Plaintiff, in opposition, again argues that the plain meaning of the phrase “for the account of the owner of the property” means that payment on claims for damage to the personal property of others are payable not to the policy holder, but to the injured third party directly, thereby creating standing to pursue this action. Defendant again analogizes this case to LaBarbera v. Security National Ins. Co., a recent appellate opinion concerning a similar “insured contract” benefit in which the Court of Appeal held that the benefit, which provided coverage to the insured for defense of a third party indemnified by the insured in a contract arising from the insured party’s business, did not confer standing on the named insured’s contractual indemnitee. (LaBarbera v. Security National Ins. Co. (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 1329, 1345.) In LaBarbera, the Court of Appeal concluded that an indemnitee defense clause was principally for the benefit of the contracting parties, even though it also conferred an incidental benefit on the indemnitee. (Id. at 1343-44.) Specifically, the LaBarbera Court found that that it was in the insured party’s interest to have the insurance company pay for the defense of the indemnitee in litigation arising out of the underlying construction contract without reducing the insured party’s policy limits, and that it was in the interest of the insurer to minimize its obligation with the efficiency of a combined defense where feasible. (Id.) Thus, the Court of Appeal concluded that the plaintiff was not the intended beneficiary on the insurance policy and therefore did not have standing to sue the insurance company. (Id. at 1345.)

 

            Here, Defendant argues that the purpose of the Personal Property of Others benefit is similar to the benefit at issue in LaBarbera, in that, as evidenced by the phrase “for the account of the owner of the property,” the purpose of the provision was to protect Pro-Com from harm by providing Pro-Com with funds to settle any account with a third party for loss of the personal property of others. Defendant also argues that this case is factually analogous to LaBarbera in that this provision, like the insured contract provision, only provides coverage when certain conditions are satisfied, and does not provide general coverage of the property of others. (See FAC ¶ 3, LaBarbera, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at 1334-35.)

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff reasserts the argument that the plain meaning of the phrase “for the account of the owner of the property” means that payment on claims for damage to the personal property of others are payable not to the policy holder, but to the injured third party directly, thereby creating standing to pursue this action. Plaintiff cites the out-of-state authorities previously referenced in support of its position on the Demurrer to the original Complaint. (See United Natl’ Ins. Co. v. Mundell Terminal Servs. (5th Cir. 2014) 740 F.3d 1022, 1030; Peters v. Employers Mut. Cas. Co. (Mo. 1993) 853 S.W.2d 300.) The Court found these authorities to have little persuasive force in its previous ruling because they concerned different policy language, addressed different legal questions, or were simply devoid of legal reasoning. (See April 19, 2023 Minute Order.) Plaintiff’s arguments in opposition to this demurrer offer no justification for the Court to depart from those conclusions.

 

Plaintiff also argues that LaBarbera is distinguishable because that opinion concerned indemnification for coverage based on liability, rather than the fault-free property coverage at issue here, which Plaintiff contends creates a right for Plaintiff to recover under the Policy. The Court agrees with Plaintiff that the coverage offered in the Policy for the property of others is not predicated on legal liability to any party, as the indemnification at issue in LaBarbera was. (See LaBarbera, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at 1335.) Indeed, Defendant freely concedes both in the moving papers and in the reply that coverage for liability of Pro-Com to Plaintiff is not at issue in this case. (See, e.g., Reply pp. 6:26-7:1.) However, the factual differences from LaBarbera identified by Plaintiff do not defeat the structural similarity between the two policies described above. Defendant did not contend that LaBarbera was identical to this case, merely analogous in that both policies had coverage provisions that were concerned principally with mitigating the risk to the policy-holder from losses suffered by others. (See FAC ¶ 3, LaBarbera, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at 1334-35.) Moreover, Plaintiff’s position is based on the circular argument that it has a right to recover under the Policy because it is entitled to coverage, and that if it does not have standing under the Policy to sue Defendant, then there is no mechanism to enforce the benefit. Indeed, that is precisely Defendant’s position, which the Court had previously adopted based on its interpretation of the policy language and the absence of allegations of extrinsic evidence that might support an alternative construction.

 

            Plaintiff asserted new factual allegations in the First Amended Complaint in response to the Court’s ruling granting leave to add allegations of extrinsic facts which might bear on the interpretation of the Policy. Defendant contends that the new factual allegations in the First Amended Complaint do not support the interpretation of the contract advanced by Plaintiff in its pleadings and opposition papers. Plaintiff’s principal allegations pertaining to the interpretation of the Policy concern the correspondence between the parties after the loss regarding the effect of the insurance policy. (FAC ¶¶ 28-30, 38-40, 52, 58-60, 84-86.) As Defendant correctly observes, the allegation which directly addresses the interpretation of the policy is contained in paragraphs 39 and 40, wherein Plaintiff alleges:

 

39. More than a year after formal written request by Plaintiff for the disclosure of all policies and potentially available limits of coverage, and more than five months after Defendant wrote a letter denying any coverage existed under the policy for 3BTECH’s losses, on March 14, 2023, Defendant wrote a letter to Plaintiff 3BTECH stating that it conducted a review of its policy for benefits available to 3BTECH under the Policy’s Property Coverage and confirmed that coverage was available for 3BTECH’s losses.

 

40.  After the filing of this lawsuit, by letter dated March 14, 2023, Defendant admitted to Plaintiff, contrary to pre-litigation denials, that property coverage purchased by PROCOM does provide coverage to 3BTECH’s losses in the subject fire.

 

(FAC ¶¶ 39-40 [emphasis in original].) If true, these allegations would certainly support the interpretation advanced by Plaintiff. However, as Defendant states, the documents themselves, which are subject to judicial notice, do not show that Plaintiff is entitled to coverage under the Policy. Defendant’s letter dated March 14, 2023, states that coverage is available for the property under the Coverage Extension for Personal Property of Others as described in section A.5.b of the Policy but does not concede that Plaintiff is entitled to coverage under that provision, and expressly denies that Plaintiff is entitled to any benefits under the policy. (RJN Exh. E. p.5.) Defendant does not dispute, for the purpose of this demurrer, that Plaintiff’s property would fall within the Policy’s definitions of “Property of Others,” but denies that Plaintiff has standing to sue under the policy as a third-party beneficiary. Plaintiff does not address this argument in opposition. The Court thus concludes that these allegations offer no insight as to the interpretation of the relevant policy language.

 

As there are no other factual allegations which appear to pertain to the interpretation of the policy, such as custom and practice in interpreting the relevant provisions, or trade usage distinct from common parlance or legal meaning, the Court concludes that there is no basis to depart from its previous ruling that Plaintiff lacks standing to sue under the Policy.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to Amend

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            The Court has already provided Plaintiff an opportunity to amend the pleadings to offer allegations of extrinsic evidence that would support its interpretation of the Policy. (April 19, 2023 Minute Order.) Although Plaintiff certainly attempted to meet that objective, the new allegations are not sufficient to cure the defects identified in both demurrers brought by Defendant. The Court is therefore forced to conclude that extending further leave to amend would be futile.

 

CONCLUSION:

            Accordingly, Defendant West American Insurance Co.’s Demurrer to the First Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

            Any party may submit on the tentative ruling by contacting the courtroom via email at Smcdept47@lacourt.org by no later than 4:00 p.m. the day before the hearing. All interested parties must be copied on the email. It should be noted that if you submit on a tentative ruling the court will still conduct a hearing if any party appears. By submitting on the tentative you have, in essence, waived your right to be present at the hearing, and you should be aware that the court may not adopt the tentative, and may issue an order which modifies the tentative ruling in whole or in part.
