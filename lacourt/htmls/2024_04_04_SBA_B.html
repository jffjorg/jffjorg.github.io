THE CITY OF RANCHO PALOS VERDES vs DONALD WEITZMAN

CASE NO. 23TRCP00265

MOTION FOR RELIEF PURSUANT TO CCP SECTION 473 FROM SANCTION FOR FAILURE TO APPEAR

On June 26, 2023 Petitioner City of Rancho Palos Verdes filed an Application for Abatement Warrant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1822 et seq.  The same date that the Application for Abatement was filed, the Court issued the Order for Abatement.  On July 13, 2023 the Return to Warrant was executed by the Court.  (A copy does not appear in the Court’s file int this matter.)  This concluded the proceedings in this case under CCP § 1822 et seq.

However, the Court on June 26, 2023 had also issued its Notice of Case Management Conference and Order to Show Cause re Failure to file Proof of Service, Failure to Prosecute Case, and Failure to File a Request for Entry of Default.  Both matters were set for hearing on October 24, 2023.

Since the proceedings under CCP § 1822 had been completed, counsel failed to appear at the October 24, 2023 hearing.  As a result, the Court issued its Order sanctioning counsel $250 for failure to appear, failure to file proof of service and failure to prosecute.

Counsel for Petitioner now seeks relief from the October 24, 2023 Order that he pay $250 as a sanction. The Court GRANTS the motion as it finds that counsel for Petitioner reasonably believed that all matters related to this case had been concluded and thus there was no need to attend a hearing.

ORDER:

The Motion is GRANTED and no sanctions are ordered.

The Clerk shall record this case as completed.

Demurrer

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

On May 16, 2023, plaintiff Christine Rush filed a complaint against Brant Griffin for premises liability based on a slip and fall on April 24, 2021.  Plaintiff alleges that she was a guest at defendant’s property in Palm Springs to celebrate a birthday.  On the night of the incident, plaintiff arrived at the property around 10:00 p.m. and took her dog outside to relieve itself.  Plaintiff’s dog wandered into the swimming pool and began to drown.  Plaintiff entered the pool to rescue her dog, and after successfully doing so, she exited the pool and walked toward the overhead where towels were kept.  As plaintiff was walking on the tiled surface, she slipped and fell due to the tiles being slippery when wet.          

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Brant Griffin demurs to the complaint on the ground that it fails to state a cause of action against him because it is barred by the two year statute of limitations under CCP §335.1.

Defendant asserts that plaintiff alleges that the incident occurred on April 24, 2021 when she allegedly sustained injuries but the complaint was not filed until more than two years later on May 16, 2023. 

In opposition, plaintiff contends that the complaint was “initially filed on April 18, 2023,” but that due to technical deficiencies in the filing, the court’s clerk rejected the initial filing on April 19, 2023 because the filing was missing page two of the Civil Case Cover Sheet (Form CM-010) and the Civil Case Cover Sheet Addendum and Statement of Location (LASC Form CIV 109).  Plaintiff asserts that “plaintiff’s counsel promptly rectified these deficiencies as soon as he was aware and refiled the complaint on May 5, 2023” but that the May 5, 2023 filing was similarly rejected, “and promptly rectified by counsel” and the court “officially accepted the filing on May 16, 2024.”  Plaintiff requests that the court take “judicial notice of the fact” the complaint was filed on April 18, 2023 before the statute of limitations expired.  Plaintiff argues that the court’s clerk was required to file the complaint when plaintiff submitted the complaint, citing to Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.30(c).  Plaintiff also seeks relief under CCP §473(b) based on mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect and CCP §471.5 based on the relation back doctrine.

In reply, defendant argues that even assuming that CCP §473 applies, plaintiff failed to seek relief within six months.  Further, defendant contends, there is no evidence of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect as to why plaintiff failed to file a timely complaint after the clerk rejected the filing on April 19, 2023.  Defendant also argues that CCP §471.5 is not applicable because no “amended complaint” had been filed.

The court rules as follows:  The complaint is barred on its face by the two-year statute of limitations under CCP §335.1.  Plaintiff alleges that the incident where she was injured occurred on April 24, 2021.  The complaint was not filed until May 16, 2023.

As for relief for untimely filing or to reinstate or correct the purported original filing date, “[t]he functions of the clerk are purely ministerial.  The clerk has no discretion to reject a complaint that substantially conforms to the local rules.”  Rojas v. Cutsforth (1998) 67 Cal. App. 4th 774, 777 (citation omitted).  In Rojas, the plaintiff’s attorney had mailed the complaint for filing before the statute of limitations expired, but did not receive the rejection by the clerk until after the statute of limitations expired.  The court had found that the defect was insubstantial. 

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.220(c) states:  “If a party that is required to provide a cover sheet under this rule or a similar local rule fails to do so or provides a defective or incomplete cover sheet at the time the party's first paper is submitted for filing, the clerk of the court must file the paper.” 

CCP §1010.6(e)(4)(E) states:

“If the clerk of the court does not file a complaint or cross complaint because the complaint or cross complaint does not comply with applicable filing requirements or the required filing fee has not been paid, any statute of limitations applicable to the causes of action alleged in the complaint or cross complaint shall be tolled for the period beginning on the date on which the court received the document and as shown on the confirmation of receipt described in subparagraph (A), through the later of either the date on which the clerk of the court sent the notice of rejection described in subparagraph (C) or the date on which the electronic filing service provider or electronic filing manager sent the notice of rejection as described in subparagraph (D), plus one additional day if the complaint or cross complaint is subsequently submitted in a form that corrects the errors which caused the document to be rejected. The party filing the complaint or cross complaint shall not make any change to the complaint or cross complaint other than those required to correct the errors which caused the document to be rejected.”

The court notes that plaintiff did not file a motion to correct the filing date or to reinstate the original filing date.  In any event, the court finds that such a request would have no ground.  As defendant points out, this case is distinguishable from Rojas, and plaintiff does not address in the opposition to argue otherwise.  In the herein case, plaintiff’s counsel received the clerk rejection four days before the statute of limitations expired and the defect was not insubstantial.  Plaintiff has not shown that he filed a complaint that substantially complied with the local rules, until after the statute of limitations expired.  Even considering CCP §1010.6, and if the statute of limitations was extended, plaintiff still did not file a timely complaint.  CCP §473 is not applicable because plaintiff did not seek relief within six months.  This case does not involve an amended complaint and thus CCP §471.5 does not apply.

The demurrer is thus SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ORDER

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as it is time barred.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

            The demurrers to the second, third, and seventh causes of action in the cross-complaint are SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The demurrer to the eighth cause of action is OVERRULED.  The motions to strike are GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to claim for punitive damages.

BACKGROUND

            On August 17, 2023, plaintiff JG Builders, Inc., dba Ecosmart Builders filed a complaint against Kimberly Cates and Nancy Dodd for breach of contract.

            On November 2, 2023, the court sustained defendants Cates and Dodd’s demurrer to the complaint with leave to amend.

            On November 2, 2023, defendants Cates and Dodd filed a cross-complaint against Ecosmart Builders, LLC, Kamyar K. Rezani, Farzad Zamani, JG Builders, Inc., and Jose Guadalupe Ramirez for (1) breach of written contract, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) misrepresentation, (4) negligence, (5) statutory recovery of compensation paid, (6) recission, (7) fraud, and (8) unfair business practices.

            On November 22, 2023, plaintiff JG Builders, Inc. dba Ecosmart Builders filed a FAC for breach of contract.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  CCP § 436(a).  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  CCP §436(b).  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  CCP §436.  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrers

Cross-complainants demur to the second, third, seventh, and eighth causes of action on the grounds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

FAC

As context, the FAC alleges that on December 4, 2021, plaintiff and defendants executed a written contract wherein plaintiff was to construct an Accessory Dwelling Unit (“ADU”) on defendants’ property located at 624 W. Acacia Ave., El Segundo.  Plaintiff agreed to provide all services, materials, and labor for the conversion of defendant’s garage into an ADU pursuant to a Term Sheet.  FAC, ¶15, Exh. 4.  Plaintiff was to be paid by defendants pursuant to a schedule of progress payment per certain “Milestones.”  Id., ¶16.  On December 17, 2021, defendants submitted payment by way of defendant Cates’ check in the sum of $10,000 for the initial deposit and Milestone 1 payment.  Id., ¶17.  On June 16, 2022, pursuant to the December 4, 2021 written contract, plaintiff completed Milestone 2 by obtaining the necessary permits from the City of El Segundo.  At this time, defendants’ payment for Milestone 2 came due.  Defendants did not submit payment for Milestone 2 until July 18, 2022.  This was in breach of the December 4, 2021 contract.  Id., ¶14.  On July 17, 2022, defendants gave plaintiff access to defendants’ property and plaintiff began construction at defendants’ property.  Id., ¶20.  Defendants submitted checks totaling $54,040 for Milestone 3.  Id., ¶22.  On January 10, 2023, defendants took issue with the kitchen cabinets, bookshelf and materials that were going to be used within the ADU.  Defendants took issue with quality of materials they never saw.  Id., ¶23.  On March 1, 2023, defendants sent notice that they were terminating their agreement with plaintiff and would not allow plaintiff to complete the work.  Plaintiff stopped work per defendants’ demand.  Id., ¶28.  Defendants breached the December 1, 2021 agreement by failing to submit timely payment, forbid plaintiff to complete the kitchen, forbid plaintiff to complete the bookshelf, and unnecessarily placed a stop work order on the entire project.  Id., ¶31.  Defendants breached the contract by failing to pay the unpaid principal balance of $68,170 despite plaintiff’s demand.  Id., ¶34.

            Cross-Complaint

The cross-complaint alleges that Ecosmart is an LLC.  Its members are defendants Rezani, Zamani, and Ramirez.  In 2012, Ecosmart applied for a contractor’s license.  Cross-complaint, ¶13.  The Cal. State License Board issued a general B contractor’s license number 971444 to Ecosmart in 2012.  Id., ¶14.  On December 2018, Ramirez applied for a separate contractor’s license with JG Builders.  The Contractor’s board assigned license number 1049536 to JG Builders.  Id., ¶15.  On November 1, 2020, defendants sent the contractor’s board a license cancellation request to cancel 97114 used by Ecosmart, and it was cancelled.  Id., ¶16.  Despite no longer possessing a valid contractor license, Ecosmart continued to perform construction services in the Los Angeles area.  Id., ¶17.

The cross-complaint further alleges that in November 2021, cross-complainants were interviewing general contractors to hire to convert their existing detached garage into an ADU.  Cross-complainants spoke with Zamani who represented he was the CEO and owner of Ecosmart and that Ecosmart was a licened general contractor.  Id., ¶18.  In December 2021, Zamani presented a contract for Ecosmart to convert the garage into an ADU; the contract price was $130,000 for the construction of a “premium” ADU which was to include a kitchen, laundry room, and bathroom.  It was signed by Zamani on behalf of Ecosmart.  Id., ¶19.  Nowhere in the agreement is JG Builders, Inc. or Jose Guadalupe Ramirez.  Id., ¶20.  The contract listed contractor license number 1049536, which is the license belonging to JG Builders.  Id., ¶21.  On June 16, 2022, Ecosmart applied for a building permit, which listed the general contractor for the project as JG Builders and its address in El Monte.  Id., ¶24.  Between July 2022 and March 2023, Ecosmart performed services on the subject property and did not maintain a valid contractor license, collecting $103,750 in payments.  Id., ¶25.

The cross-complaint further alleges that Ecosmart failed to complete the project as required under the agreement.  The project took much longer than represented, and it failed to construct the project within the standard of care.  It was notified of the numerous defects and errors in its work but refused to make the required repairs.  Id., ¶26.  Ramirez is the responsible managing owner for JG Builders contractor’s license and failed to supervise the work of the employees of Ecosmart and the work of Ecosmart’s subcontractors.  Id., ¶27.  Ecosmart collected more from cross-complainants than the work performed at the property in violation of Bus. and Prof. Code §7159.  Id., ¶28.  Due to the numerous problems and issues with Ecosmart’s performance of the work and its numerous delays and inability to properly repair the many defects reported to it, cross-complainants terminated Ecosmart’s contract on March 1, 2023.  Id., ¶29.

            Second cause of action for breach of oral contract

“To state a cause of action for breach of contract, [plaintiff] must plead the contract, his performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, [defendant’s] breach and the resulting damage.”  Lortz v. Connell (1969) 273 Cal. App. 2d 286, 290.  “To state a cause of action for breach of contract, it is absolutely essential to plead the terms of the contract either in haec verba or according to legal effect.”  Twaite v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1989) 216 Cal. App. 3d 239, 252.    

The cross-complaint alleges that between December 2021 through March 2023, cross-defendants entered into several oral contracts with cross-complainants to perform the above described construction services.  Cross-complaint, ¶35.  They breached the oral contracts by failing to perform the services described in the contract in a good and workmanlike manner in accordance with accepted trade practices and free from defects.  Id., ¶37.  In addition, cross-defendants breached their agreement to obtain and carry the requisite general contractor’s license.  Id., ¶38.

Cross-defendants argue that the cross-complaint does not allege what the oral contracts were or the essential terms of the oral contracts.

In opposition, cross-complainants argue that the allegations are sufficient because they allege that Zamani made oral statements and misrepresentations about his role with Ecosmart and about Ecosmart having a contractor’s license, “which form the oral contract at issue were made in furtherance of the overall fraud by all cross-defendants.” 

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient and are uncertain and cross-complainants appear to be conflating a breach of oral contract with allegations of fraud.  The cross-complaint alleges “several oral contracts” but does not provide any allegations to identify them or the terms.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the second cause of action.

            Third cause of action for misrepresentation

The elements of negligent misrepresentation are: (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed, and (5) damages. Where the defendant makes false statements, honestly believing that they are true, but without reasonable ground for such belief, he may be liable for negligent misrepresentation, a form of deceit.  B.L.M. v. Sabo & Deitsch (1997) 55 Cal. App. 4th 823, 834.  “To withstand demurrer, the facts constituting every element of fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings.”  Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal. App. 4th 772, 782.  The particularity requirement necessitates pleadings facts that “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal. 4th 631, 645.      

The cross-complaint alleges that cross-defendants through their word and deeds made misrepresentations to cross-complainants about their intent and ability to construct the work at the property in a good and workmanlike manner, free from defects.  They promised via its contracts that the property would be constructed in a workmanlike manner and that cross-defendants’ would be properly licensed and carry the required workman’s compensation insurance.  Cross-complaint, ¶43.  Cross-defendants lacked reasonable grounds for the representations that they were willing to construct the ADU in a good and workmanlike manner, free from defects.  In addition, they never possessed the required general contractors’ license.  They illegally used JG Builders’ contractor license number.  They made numerous false statements and used these false promises and reassurances to obtain additional funds from cross-complainants for construction work performed at the subject property.  Id., ¶45.

Cross-defendants argue that the cross-complaint appears to be alleging nothing more than breach of an alleged agreement to construct an ADU, and that there is no allegation of a misrepresentation of any past or existing material fact.  Cross-defendants further contend that the cause of action is uncertain because the cross-complaint “mush and reference all five” cross-defendants and assert that they made false statements.  Cross-defendants also assert that the cross-complaint fails to identify the statements and promises, outside of those stated in the written contract.

In opposition, cross-complainants argue that the allegations are sufficient and that “[a]s is clear from the entirety of the cross-complaint, the negligent misrepresentations and fraud have to do with the cross-defendants’ illegal renting of the contractor license and go well beyond the construction of the ADU.”

 

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient and have not been plead with the requisite particularity.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the third cause of action.

            Seventh cause of action for fraud [misnumbered in the cross-complaint]

The elements of a fraud claim are (1) misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to deceive; and (4) reliance and resulting damage.  Vega v. Jones, Day, Reavis & Pogue (2004) 121 Cal. App. 4th 282, 290.  “To withstand demurrer, the facts constituting every element of fraud must be alleged with particularity, and the claim cannot be salvaged by references to the general policy favoring the liberal construction of pleadings.”  Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal. App. 4th 772, 782.  The particularity requirement necessitates pleadings facts that “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal. 4th 631, 645.

The cross-complaint alleges that prior to entering into the agreement, cross-defendants represented to cross-complainants that Ecosmart possessed the experience, expertise and as a licensed California contractor would be capable of converting the existing garage into an ADU at the property.  Cross-defendants further represented that as a licensed California general contractor, a qualified individual would also supervise the construction work to be performed at the property.  These representations about Ecosmart’s license and qualifications as a general contractor were made directly to cross-complainants and to the general public.  At all times Ecosmart performed work under the contract, Ecosmart was not a licensed general contractor.  Cross-complaint, ¶71.

Cross-defendants argue that the allegations lack specificity.

In opposition, cross-complainants argue that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient and have not been plead with the requisite particularity.  The cross-complaint does not allege how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the misrepresentations were made.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the seventh cause of action.

            Eighth cause of action for unfair business practices

            The purpose of the Unfair Business Practices Act (Bus. & Prof. Code §17000, et. seq.) is to “safeguard the public against the creation or perpetuation of monopolies and to foster and encourage competition, by prohibiting unfair, dishonest, deceptive, destructive, fraudulent and discriminatory practices by which fair and honest competition is destroyed or prevented.”  Bus. & Prof. Code §17001.  The Unfair Business Practices Act shall include “any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.”  Bus. & Prof. Code §17200.  The Unfair Business Practices Act is a tool with which to enjoin deceptive or sharp practices.  Samura v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (1993) 17 Cal. App. 4th 1284, 1299, fn. 6.  A plaintiff alleging unfair business practices under these statutes must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.  Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal. App. 4th 612, 619.  

The cross-complaint alleges that cross-defendants engaged and continue to engage in business practices which are unfair and unlawful and in violation of Bus. and Prof. Code §17200, et seq. by fraudulently representing to cross-complainants and the general public that it was a properly licensed general contractor.  They also violated the Bus. and Prof. Code by failing to have an actual RMO supervise, coordinate, and inspect the construction services being performed under JG Builders’ contractor’s license.  Cross-complaint, ¶79.

Cross-defendants argue that the cause of action is uncertain because it incorporates all the prior allegations and causes of action and fails to allege who on behalf of the two corporate cross-defendants engaged in unfair and unlawful business practice.

In opposition, cross-defendants argue that the allegations are sufficient.

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient to plead violation of Bus. and Prof. Code based on Ecosmart’s alleged illegal use of JG Builders’ contractor’s license and fraudulently representing that it had a contractor’s license. 

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the eighth cause of action.

 

Motion to Strike

Cross-defendants request that the court strike the prayer for punitive and exemplary damages.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  “’Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(3).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

A claim for punitive damages may not be based on conclusory allegations of oppression, fraud or malice, but instead must be based on factual allegations which support such a conclusion.  See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-42 (Court of Appeal issued a peremptory writ directing trial court to issue order striking plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages because “[t]he sole basis for seeking punitive damages are . . . conclusory allegations” which were “devoid of any factual assertions supporting a conclusion [defendants] acted with oppression, fraud, or malice”).

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  See also the demurrer ruling under the third and seventh causes of action.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ORDER

The demurrers to the second, third, and seventh causes of action in the cross-complaint are SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  The demurrer to the eighth cause of action is OVERRULED.  The motions to strike are GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to claim for punitive damages.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.
