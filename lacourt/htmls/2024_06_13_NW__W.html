DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03792    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: W

SAJJAD TAKALLOU v. WALMART INC.

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT RAFAEL DELGADO TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES

 

Date of Hearing:        June 13, 2024                                                Trial Date:       None set 

Department:              W                                                                    Case No:         23VECV03792

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Keon Hollifield      

Responding Party:     None

Meet and Confer:      Attempted

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

This action involves a motor vehicle collision. On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff Keon Hollifield filed a complaint against Defendant Rafael Delgado for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Rafael Delgado to Provide Further Responses to Request for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED.

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant Rafael Delgado to Provide Further Responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

III.                Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT RAFAEL DELGADO TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR ADMISSIONS, SET ONE

 

Plaintiff Keon Hollifield moves the court for an order compelling Defendant Rafael Delgado to serve on Plaintiff further answers to Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One and for an order imposing a monetary sanction of $2,560.00 against Defendant Rafael Delgado and his counsel for record Chad G. Chapman for failure to provide a response. The motion is made on the grounds Defendant has served evasive objection only responses.

 

A motion to compel further responses be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (CCP § 2033.290(a).)  However, a motion to compel further responses cannot compel the admission of matters already denied.  (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 821.) 

 

On January 9, 2024, Plaintiff propounded a set of written discovery to Defendant Delgado, including Request for Admissions, Set One. (Green Decl., ¶3, Exh. 1.) Defense requested a 30 day extension to respond to the discovery, making them due on March 12, 2024. (Green Decl. ¶4, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel agreed. (Green Decl. ¶5, Exh 3.) On March 15, 2024, three days after the extension had expired, Defendant served objection only responses to the written discovery. (Green Decl. ¶6, Exh. 4.) Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter stating the responses were objection only, which started Plaintiff’s 45 day to compel. (Grene Decl. ¶7.) Defendant never responded. Upon review of the Request for Admissions, Set One, the court finds the objections without merit.

 

The court therefore GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Admission, Set One. 

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290(d) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2033.290(d).)  

 

Plaintiff requests $2,560.00 in sanctions for 3 hours preparing the motion, 1 hour in anticipation of reviewing opposition and preparing reply, and 1 hour attending the hearing we as well as the $60 filing fee. Counsel’s hourly rate is $500.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,560.00. 

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT RAFAEL DELGADO TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES AND SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE

 

 

Plaintiff Keon Hollifield moves the court for an order compelling Defendant Rafael Delgado to serve on Plaintiff further answers to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One and for an order imposing a monetary sanction of $3,560.00 against Defendant Rafael Delgado and his counsel for record Chad G. Chapman for failure to provide a response. The motion is made on the grounds Defendant has served non-compliant responses. 

 

A motion to compel further responses to form or specially prepared interrogatories may be brought if the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2) an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2030.300(a).)  

 

On January 9, 2024, Plaintiff propounded a set of written discovery to Defendant Delgado, including Form Interrogatories, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Green Decl., ¶3, Exh. 1.) Defense requested a 30-day extension to respond to the discovery, making them due on March 12, 2024. (Green Decl. ¶4, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel agreed. (Green Decl. ¶5, Exh 3.) On March 15, 2024, three days after the extension had expired, Defendant served objection only responses to the written discovery. (Green Decl. ¶6, Exh. 4.) Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter stating the responses were objection only, which started Plaintiff’s 45 day to compel. (Grene Decl. ¶7.) Defendant never responded. (Green Decl. ¶8.)

 

The court finds the objections without merit. As for the Form Interrogatories, these requests are not so vague, overbroad, or irrelevant. They seek background information and information regrading the incident. Moreover, none of them request attorney client privileged responses. Similarly, the objections to Special Interrogatories, Set One are without merit. The information sought is relevant and discoverable.

 

The court therefore GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One. 

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(d) provides that for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP §2030.300(d).)  

 

Plaintiff requests $3,560.00 in sanctions for 5 hours preparing the motion, 1 hour in anticipation of reviewing opposition and preparing reply, and 1 hour attending the hearing we as well as the $60 filing fee. Counsel’s hourly rate is $500.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,060.00. 

 

III.                PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE

 

Plaintiff Keon Hollifield moves the court for an order compelling Defendant Rafael Delgado to serve on Plaintiff answer to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One and for an order imposing monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,560.00 against Defendant Delgado and his counsel of record, Chad G. Chapman for failure to provide code-compliant, substantive responses.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1).)

 

As noted above, on January 9, 2024, Plaintiff propounded a set of written discovery to Defendant Delgado, including Request for Production of Documents, Set One. (Green Decl., ¶3, Exh. 1.) Defense requested a 30-day extension to respond to the discovery, making them due on March 12, 2024. (Green Decl. ¶4, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel agreed. (Green Decl. ¶5, Exh 3.) On March 15, 2024, three days after the extension had expired, Defendant served objection only responses to the written discovery. (Green Decl. ¶6, Exh. 4.) Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter stating the responses were objection only, which started Plaintiff’s 45 day to compel. (Grene Decl. ¶7.) Defendant never responded. (Green Decl. ¶8.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause for the documents. They are relevant and material to this litigation involving a motor vehicle collision. Moreover, the objections are without merit. Defendant submits boilerplate objections without any effort to actually respond to the demand.

 

The court therefore GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One. 

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).)   

 

Plaintiff requests $2,560.00 in sanctions for 3 hours preparing the motion, 1 hour in anticipation of reviewing opposition and preparing reply, and 1 hour attending the hearing we as well as the $60 filing fee. Counsel’s hourly rate is $500.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $560.00. 

 

 


Case Number: 24VECV00591    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: W

CURTIS A. JOHNSON vs MARIA L. KILE

 

SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE THE COMPLAINT AND EACH CAUSE OF ACTION AND DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        June 13, 2024                                                 Trial Date:       N/A 

Department:              W                                                                    Case No:         24VECV00591

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Maria L. Kile  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Curtis A. Johnson

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff Curtis A. Johnson filed a complaint against Defendant Maria L. Kile for slander, libel, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff alleges Defendant spoke to third persons about Plaintiff including that Plaintiff protected his alleged good friend, Rene Tenas, who was accused of molesting girls in his elementary school class and that Plaintiff took no initiative to protect students at the school where Plaintiff was principal.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike the Complaint and Each Cause of Action for Slander, Libel, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant Maria L. Kile requests this court take judicial notice of the following documents: (1) Damages for Negligence filed on August 3, 2022 in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 22STCV25004 (Exh. A); (2) Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. LAVLA087395-01, The People of the State of California vs. TENAS, RENE (Exh. B); (3) Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. LAVLA097646-01, The People of the State of California vs. TENAS, RENE (Exh. C); (4) Tenas’ teaching credentials issued by the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (Exh. D); and (5) LAUSD’s Policy Bulletin re Protocols and Procedures to Report, Reassign, and Investigate Allegations of Employee Misconduct, BUL-044381.0 (Exh. E).

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the demurrer of Defenant Maria L. Kile on April 22, 2024 (Exh. 1). The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Defendant Maria L. Kile submits objections to the Declarations of Curtis A. Johnson and Craig B. Forry by Plaintiff Curtis A. Johnson in opposition to Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike.

 

Objections Nos. 1 – 10 are OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Defendant Maria L. Kile moves to strike the Complaint for Slander, Libel, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. Defendant also seeks attorney fees under 425.16 against Plaintiff and his counsel in the amount of $17,777.50.

 

On a special motion to strike pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, also known as an anti-SLAPP motion, moving parties have the initial burden to demonstrate that a cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike. (Martinez v. Metabolife Inter. Ins. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 186; Fox Searchlight Pictures Inc. v. Paladino (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 294, 304.) First, the court must determine whether moving parties have made a prima facie showing that the attacked claims arise from a protected activity, including defendants’ right of petition, or free speech, under a constitution, in connection with issues of public interest. (Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1, 5; Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278; CCP § 425.16(e).)

 

If the court finds this showing has been made, it must dismiss the cause of action unless the plaintiff meets its burden to demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the claim. (CCP §425.16(b)(1); Balzaga v. Fox News Network, LLC (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1325, 1336.) This means that the plaintiff must state a legally sufficient claim and must then present evidence that substantiates or sustains the claim. (Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 61; see also Wilson v. Parker, Covert & Chidester (2002) 28 Cal.4th 811, 821 [plaintiff “must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited”].)

 

i.                    Defendant’s Burden

 

An act in furtherance of a person's right to petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or California Constitution includes “(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.” (CCP §425.16(e).)

 

In the instant matter, the conduct Plaintiff complains of are statements Defendant made to third persons regarding Plaintiff’s alleged knowledge of Tenas’ sexual abuse of students and failure to protect the students on campus as principal. As alleged, it would appear that such statements do not fall within the meaning of protected activity within the meaning of the Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. The allegations are vague as it is impossible to determine when Defendant made the statements or under what context Defendant made the statements.

 

Defendant presents testimony, however, that in November of 2022, she was contacted while at school and informed that she needed to participate in an investigation being conducted by the District’s Student Safety Investigation Team (“SSIT”). (Kile Decl. ¶6.) Defendant was required to participate and informed the investigation was confidential nor would she be retaliated against for participating. (Kile Decl. ¶6.) Defendant states the interview was conducted on the school campus and answered only questions posed to her. (Kile Decl. ¶6.) Moreover, she did not discuss the interview, investigation or information requested or provided to the investigators with anyone. (Kile Decl. ¶6.) In March of 2023, Defendant again was asked to participate in an interview with SSIT related to the same investigation. (Kile Decl. ¶7.) Defendant did so and again answered questions that were posed. (Kile Decl. ¶7.) She did not discuss the interview with anyone nor did she make any public negative statements about Plaintiff. (Kile Decl. ¶7.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues none of the causes of action are intended to be alleged against Defendant based on her conduct as an employee of LAUSD, as a mandated reporter under CANRA, or on statements that may have bene made during an investigation by LAUSD. Moreover, the complaint is not intended to be alleged on any lawsuit or any statements made by Defendant in a judicial or quasi-judicial proceeding.

 

It is true that vague complaint makes it impossible to determine if acts “occurred in a place open to the public or in a public forum.” (Turnbull v. Lucerne Valley Unified School District (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 522, 534.) However, that does not mean extrinsic evidence cannot be used to clarify whether the wrongful acts at issue concern protected activity.  (See Optional Capital, Inc. v. Akin Gump Strauss, Hauer & Feld LLP (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 95, 111.)  The court finds that defendant has presented sufficient evidence to establish that the only statements she made were as part of the administrative investigation and that she made no other statements.  Plaintiff does not counter that evidence with any evidence that any statement was made by Kile outside of the administrative process.  Plaintiff’s declaration does not suffice, since it has no content and provides no contrary evidence.   

 

An action arising from statements made during an administrative investigation are undoubtedly protected, defendant has met their burden under the first prong. (See CCP §425.16(e)(1), (2); see also Laker v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 745, 765 holding statements made directly to internal investigators during an investigation into discrimination are protected.)

 

ii.                  Plaintiff’s Burden

 

On the second component of the analysis, courts employ a “summary-judgment-like” procedure, “accepting as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff and evaluating the defendant’s evidence only to determine whether the defendant has defeated the plaintiff’s evidence as a matter of law.” (Gerbosi v. Gaims, Weil, West & Epstein, LLP (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 435, 444.) In other words, the court does not assess credibility, and the plaintiff is not required to meet the preponderance of the evidence standard.  The court accepts as true the evidence favorable to the plaintiff, who need only establish that his or her claim has “minimal merit” to avoid being stricken as a SLAPP.  (Soukup, supra, 39 Cal.4th at 291.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the absolute privilege of Civil Code section 47(b) and for failure to comply with Government Claims Act. Defendant contends because the alleged statements are all in the context of one or more official proceedings, they are absolutely privileged and cannot form the basis of any tort. (See Laker, supra, 32 Cal.App.5th at 768.) Moreover, Plaintiff was required to comply with Government Tort Claims Act as Defendant is a public employee.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues there are no claims against Defendant as a public employee and moreover, the statements were not made in the context of a judicial proceeding. Plaintiff provides testimony stating that none of the causes of action are intended to be alleged against LAUSD or  Defendant as a mandated reporter, for statements Defendant made during the LAUSD investigation, lawsuit, or judicial or quasi-judicial proceeding.

 

The court finds Plaintiff has not met their evidentiary burden. Although a plaintiff need only establish minimal merit, “plaintiff must present credible evidence that satisfies the standard of proof required by the substantive law of the cause of action the anti-SLAPP motion challenges.” (De Havilland v. FX Networks, LLC (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 845, 856.) A declaration providing testimony that the allegations are not based on the SSIT investigation does not demonstrate a probability of prevailing on the merits. A judge must resolve factual conflicts in the plaintiff’s favor, however, a judge is not required to disregard the defendant's evidence when there is no conflict. (Litinsky v. Kaplan (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 970, 982-83.) Here, Plaintiff fails to present any evidence regarding the libelous or slanderous statements made outside of the investigation process.

 

Moreover, the court finds the Plaintiff’s claimed barred by Civil Code section 47(b). The litigation privilege is codified in Civil Code section 47(b) and “immuniz[es] participants from liability for torts arising from communications made during judicial proceedings[.]” (Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205, 214.) The privilege applies to any “communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action.” (Id. at p. 212.) Here, Defendant’s statements made during the SSIT investigation are privileged. Lastly, “the Legislature included in the Tort Claims Act what amounts to a requirement that ….. one who sues a public employee on the basis of acts or omissions in the scope of the defendant's employment have filed a claim against the public-entity employer pursuant to the procedure for claims against public entities. [Citations.] Failure to allege compliance renders the complaint in such an action subject to general demurrer.” (Briggs v. Lawrence (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 605, 613.) Defendant has presented evidence the statements were made during her employment as a public school teacher.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike the Complaint and Each Cause of Action for Slander, Libel, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress is GRANTED.

 

iii.                Attorney Fees

 

“[A] party who partially prevails on an anti-SLAPP motion must generally be considered a prevailing party unless the results of the motion were so insignificant that the party did not achieve any practical benefit from bringing the motion. The determination whether a party prevailed on an anti-SLAPP motion lies within the broad discretion of a trial court.” (Mann v. Quality Old Time Service, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 328, 340.)

 

Defendant is ordered to file a separate motion for attorney fees.

 

II.                  DEMURRER

 

Defendant demurs to each cause of action on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant, the claims are uncertain and fails to identify a mandatory duty breached by Defendant.

 

Pursuant to the ruling in the special motion to strike, the court finds the demurrer MOOT.
