DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02926    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: W

MELISSA DENISE MEJIA vs NADER FARSAR

 

MOTION OF DEFENDANT/CROSS-DEFENDANT HOSSEIN AMIRI TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF DEFENDANT/CROSS-COMPLAINANT NADER FARSAR’S CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        July 2, 2024                                        Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV02926

 

Moving Party:             Defendant/Cross-Defendant Hossein Amiri

Responding Party:     No opposition.

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Rossi Decl. ¶¶6-8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action involves a motor vehicle collision. On July 6, 2023, Plaintiff Melissa Denise Mejia filed a complaint against Defendant Nader Farsar for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff named Hossein Amiri as Defendant Doe 1 and Uber Technologies, Inc. as Doe 2. Plaintiff dismissed Uber Technologies on April 30, 2024.

 

On October 24, 2023, Nader Farsar filed a cross-complaint against Hossein Amiri for indemnity.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant/Cross-Defendant Hossein Amiri’s Motion to Strike Portions of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Nader Farsar’s Cross-Complaint is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant/Cross-Defendant Hossein Amiri moves this court to strike portions of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Nader Farsar’s Cross-Complaint including Paragraph 3 and Farsar’s request for attorney fees. Amiri makes the motion on the grounds attorney fees are not allowed in the instant action.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.) 

 

Cross-Defendant Amiri argues the inclusion of attorney fees is improper matter as Cross-Complainant Farsar has not provided any statutory or case law establishing that he is entitled to attorney fees from Amiri nor does Farsar identify any contractual or factual basis justifying attorney fees. Moreover, Farsar’s claims are based on equitable indemnity and contribution, none of which include a right to attorney fees.

 

The court agrees. Cross-Complainant Farsar has not established any grounds for a request for attorney fees. These claims arise out of a motor vehicle collision and indemnification/contribution. Accordingly, the court GRANTS Cross-Defendant Amiri’s Motion to Strike.

BACKGROUND

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiffs Bruce Altschuld and Camille Altschuld filed a second amended complaint against Defendants Kaivan Nownejad and Saturn New Generation LLC for Breach of Written Lease Contract; Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability; Breach of Statutory Warranty of Habitability; Negligence; Nuisance; Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; Violation of California Civil Code §1942.4; Elder Abuse; Fraud; and Breach of Civil Code §1950. Plaintiffs were tenants pursuant to a lease agreement with Defendant Saturn New Generation, LLC. Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached the implied warranty of habitability and committed other wrongful, tortious acts.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Kaivan Nownejad and Saturn New Generation LLC’s Demurrer to the Eighth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND; OVERRULED as to the Ninth, Tenth and Eleventh Causes of Action. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

discussion

 

Defendants Kaivan Nownejad and Saturn New Generation LLC demur to the Second Amended Complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs have failed to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for Violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, Elder Abuse, Fraud, and Breach of Statutory Requirement to Return Security Deposit.

 

Eighth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the eighth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4 on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

 

A landlord violates Civil Code section 1942.4 if (1) the dwelling lacks any affirmative standard characteristics listed in Civil Code section 1941.1, (2) the landlord has received a notice to abate from a public officer after an inspection has taken place, (3) the conditions have not been abated within 35 days of the notice being mailed, and (4) the conditions were not caused by the tenants. If all four of these conditions are met, a landlord is liable to the tenant for actual damages of not less than $100 and not more than $5,000. (Civil Code §1942.4(b)(1).) “Actual damages” under the statute includes damages for emotional distress suffered by tenants who claimed “embarrassment, anger, fear, stress, helplessness, and frustration from unsanitary conditions landlord failed to remedy.” (McNairy v. C.K Really, (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1500, 1506.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ SAC still presents no facts that any public housing official inspected the subject property and notified Defendants to abate the nuisance or repair substandard conditions therein. Plaintiffs’ SAC only states that Plaintiffs reported Defendants violations, which does not allege facts necessary to support a claim under 1942.4.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they have properly pled that Defendants were notified that there was a demand to inspect the premises by a government official, but that Defendants have refused to let the government agent inspect the property.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have failed to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4. An essential element of this claim is that the landlord received written notice from a public officer or employee responsible for enforcement of any housing law informing Defendants they need to abate the nuisance or repair the substandard condition. Although, as alleged, Defendants have refused to permit the City of Los Angeles Building and Safety Department to access the premises, this does not demonstrate that Defendants were made aware of the defects due to the citations. This is a requirement under Civil Code section 1942.4.   The court does not understand how plaintiff can amend this cause of action since one of the requisite elements is an inspection by the City and a refusal to abate after an inspection has been conducted.  Should the city in the future send an inspector, issue an abatement order and defendant fail to abate, plaintiff can seek leave to amend to add this cause of action back in.  

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED  WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Ninth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the ninth cause of action for elder abuse on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

 

To constitute abuse and neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act, thereby triggering the enhanced remedies available under the Act, Plaintiff “must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 88-89, quotation marks omitted.)  

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ cause of action for Elder Abuse is again not pled with the requisite specificity and is uncertain as it contains no references to the statute violated. Defendants contend the allegations are unspecified, vague, and uncertain, and they do not rise to a level to sufficiently impute liability.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue their allegations are sufficient and cite to paragraphs 60 and 63 of the complaint.

 

The court agrees that plaintiff has stated facts that meet the heightened standard of pleading or malicious conduct for elder abuse and neglect. (See Covenant Care, supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 790 [“statutory causes of action must be pleaded with particularity”].) Plaintiffs point to many instances including Defendants smelling a gas leak yet doing nothing about it, Defendants knowledge of ants in the pantry during hot days yet failed to call any exterminator, and Defendants knew that the attic had mold that was festering the whole house. Plaintiff alleges that this conduct was reckless and it would appear to meet the definition of “deliberate disregard” that injury was likely to occur to plaintiff, in particular with respect to allegations of a gas leak and mold infestation.  

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the ninth cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Tenth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the tenth cause of action for fraud on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

 

To establish a fraud cause of action, Plaintiff must allege (1) misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to defraud or to induce reliance, (4) justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage. (See Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.) 

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ SAC stated that Defendants’ conduct is fraudulent because of various representations. However, Plaintiffs have again failed to allege specific facts versus conclusory statements and assumptions. Plaintiffs make no mention of what representations were made by Defendants. In addition, Plaintiffs’ fraud claim is not pled with the requisite specificity.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the cause of action is pled only against individual Defendant Nownejad and specifically states in paragraph 65, prior to signing, Nownejad made representations to Plaintiff including that the house was habitable.

 

The court finds the allegations are sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Nownejad made the representations prior to signing the lease and exactly what those misrepresentations were and that Plaintiffs relied on Defendant Nownejad’s representations. 

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the tenth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Eleventh Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action for Breach of Statutory Requirement to Return the Security Deposit on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

 

Civil Code section 1950.5 provides a statutory cause of action against a landlord who does not return a security deposit to a tenant. (Civ. Code § 1950.5.) Civil Code section 1950.5 subdivision (g)(1) states “No later than 21 calendar days after the tenant has vacated the premises… the landlord shall furnish the tenant, by personal delivery or by first-class mail, postage prepaid, a copy of an itemized statement indicating the basis for, and the amount of, any security received and the disposition of the security, and shall return any remaining portion of the security to the tenant.” A landlord may claim an amount of the security deposit for amounts that are reasonably necessary for the purposes of: (1) The compensation of a landlord for a tenant’s default in the payment of rent; (2) The repair of damages to the premises, exclusive of ordinary wear and tear, caused by the tenant or by a guest or licensee of the tenant; (3) The cleaning of the premises upon termination of the tenancy necessary to return the unit to the same level of cleanliness it was in at the inception of the tenancy; (4) To remedy future defaults by the tenant in any obligation under the rental agreement to restore, replace, or return personal property or appurtenances, exclusive of ordinary wear and tear, if the security deposit is authorized to be applied thereto by the rental agreement. (Civ. Code § 1950.5, subds. (b), (e).)

 

Defendants argue California contains no such cause of action for Breach of Statutory Requirement to return the security deposit. In addition, Plaintiffs’ claim is not pled with the requisite specificity and contains no references to the statute violated.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they are not required to name the exact statute under which they are bringing an action and have sufficiently alleged the elements required for a violation of Civil Code section 1950.5.

 

The court agrees the allegations are sufficient to support a claim under violation of Civil Code section 1950.5. Although not explicitly referenced in the eleventh cause of action, the caption of the complaint discloses this cause of action is for violation of Section 1950.5. When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.)¿

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants seek to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ SAC on the grounds that the SAC fails to state sufficient facts to sustain its allegations for punitive damages and attorney’s fees as against Defendants.

 

While the parties agree that Plaintiff Bruce Altschuld cannot recover attorney fees, the parties dispute whether his wife can. Plaintiff Bruce Altschuld is acting as attorney for his wife, Plaintiff Camille Altschuld. At this stage, Plaintiff Camille Altschuld can pray for attorney fees. However, whether or not she may actually incur any attorney fees may be determined by a motion for attorney fees at the appropriate time.

 

Next, Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ punitive damage claims against Defendants must be stricken pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, Section 436 (a), (b). When read as a whole, Plaintiffs’ SAC alleges in vague and general terms that Defendants kept the subject property in an uninhabitable condition; however, there are not enough facts stating how the property was uninhabitable. Moreover, the SAC in conclusory terms alleges intent, willfulness, recklessness, and wantonness.

 

The court finds the punitive damages allegations sufficient for the purposes of a motion to strike. Plaintiffs have alleged Defendants acted with despicable conduct.

 

Accordingly, the motion to strike is DENIED.
