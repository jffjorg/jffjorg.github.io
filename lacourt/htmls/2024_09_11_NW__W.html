DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV01514    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: W

OREN KONENKOV, ET AL. V. EXPO DESIGN & DEVELOPMENT, INC., DBA EXPO CONSTRUCTION, ET AL.

 

MOTION TO QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA FOR PRODUCTION OF BUSINESS REOCRDS

 

Date of Hearing:        September 11, 2024                         Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21VECV01514

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Avraham Gutman and Sharon Trumer

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Oren Konenkov and Lina Konenkov

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs Oren and Lina Konenkov allege on August 10, 2020, Plaintiffs and Defendants Expo Design & Development, Inc. dba Expo Construction, Avraham Gutman, Sharon Trumer, and Celio Eugenio Cruz entered into an agreement by which Defendants agreed to furnish certain labor, services, equipment and materials for construction work pertaining to improvement to Plaintiffs’ property located in Woodland Hills. The agreed upon price was $348,809.00. Between the time that Plaintiffs first entered into the Agreement and October 26, 2020, Plaintiffs paid Defendants the sum of $256,244.90. Plaintiffs further allege any work that Defendants have performed at the Property to date, was not permitted by the relevant governmental agencies as required by law and in November 2020, Plaintiffs terminated Defendants’ access to the Property and requested that Defendants comply with their statutory obligation to return the $256,244.90 that Plaintiffs had paid Defendants to date. However, Defendants refused to return any of money Plaintiffs had paid Defendants.

 

On November 5, 2021, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for 1) Disgorgement of Compensation Paid to Unlicensed Contractor (B&P Code §7031); 2) Fraud; 3) Breach of Contract; 4) Breach of Implied Warranty and Implied Covenant to Perform Work in a Good and Competent Manner; 5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 6) Negligence; and 7) Unfair Business Practices (B&P Code §17200).

 

On April 21, 2022, Defendant Expo Construction was dismissed.

 

On August 26, 2022, default judgment was entered against Defendants Avraham Gutman and Sharon Trumer. On February 6, 2023, the default judgment was set aside.

 

TENTATIVE RULLING

 

Defendants’ Avraham Gutman and Sharon Trumer’s Motions to Quash Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Avraham Gutman and Sharon Trumer both move the court for an order quashing the deposition subpoena issued and served on JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. for production of business records regarding Defendants Gutman and Trumer. Defendants make the motion on the grounds that 1) the subpoena seeks the private financial records of Defendants Gutman and Trumer, 2) is overbroad in seeking all records for all accounts regardless of any connection to any issues in this case, 3) seeks records outside of the time period set forth in the Complaint, and 4) seeks records that could potentially disclose the financial information of third parties that have not been put on notice. Defendants also seek attorney fees.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1(a) provides: “If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

 

The right to privacy set forth in the California Constitution (at Art. I, § 1) extends to one's financial records. (Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court of San Joaquin County (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 656.) “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court . . . any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved . . . if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010.) “Even highly relevant, nonprivileged information may be shielded from discovery if its disclosure would impair a person’s ‘inalienable right of privacy’ provided by” Article 1, section 1 of the California Constitution. (See Britt v. Superior Court (San Diego Unified Port Dist.) (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  

 

The California Supreme Court in Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35, “established a framework for evaluating potential invasions of privacy. The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious. [Citation.] The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. A court must then balance these competing considerations.” (Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) If any of the threshold Hill requirements are absent, the court need not move on to a balancing of interests. (Id. at p. 555.) 

 

Defendants argue balancing their right to privacy against Plaintiff’s interest in obtaining their private financial records tips in favor of protecting Defendants’ constitutional right to privacy. Defendants claim Plaintiffs are seeking their personal bank records and are seeking documents regarding all accounts held in Defendants’ name without any limitation in scope and overbroad in time. Specifically, Plaintiffs seek financial records as well as applications of all accounts regardless of whether said accounts are associated with Expo Design & Development, Inc. dba Expo Construction. In other words, Plaintiffs seek any and all financial information regarding Defendants held by JPMorgan Chase regardless of any connection said information has on any issue in this case. Defendants deny doing business through Expo and even if they did so, engaging in the normal course of business through a corporation does not render one’s personal financial information open and available. Lastly, Defendants assert that the subpoenas improperly seek private financial information of third parties as it is likely Defendants conducted incoming and outgoing transfers through Zelle, Venmo and other money applications from their personal bank accounts to the personal money applications and/or bank accounts of his family, friends and associates.

 

In opposition[1], Plaintiffs argue the documents sought are relevant given there is sufficient evidence to link Trumer, and by extension her husband Gutman, to an account at JPMorgan Chase bank where Plaintiff’s checks were likely deposited. This evidence would prove that Trumer and Gutman have misrepresented their continued involvement of Expo after November 18, 2019 as well as their denial of performing work in Plaintiffs’ property in 2020. Moreover, disclosure weighs in Defendants’ favor because the information is not being sought to annoy, embarrass or oppress Trumer or Gutman, but because the records go the issue of their credibility and affirmative defenses, including that they have not received any compensation from Expo or any one else related to the construction project at the Property and that they had no involvement with EXPO’s operation after November 18, 2019.

 

The court finds the deposition subpoenas seek relevant information but are currently overbroad and should be modified. First, the records sought are from the time period of August 1, 2020 to December 31, 2021. The court sees no justification for extending the period all the way through December 31, 2021, as the interactions between the parties ended in November 2020. Accordingly, the time period for which bank records should be produced is August 1 2020 to December 31, 2020.  Second, seeking ‘any and all’ documents, applications related to ‘any and all’ transactions, wire transfer, and cred card accounts is extremely overboard. The court agrees the financial records are relevant to the causes of action and affirmative defenses being asserted, specifically, to Defendants’ denial of their involvement in  Expo and performing work at Plaintiffs’ property. However, this does not entitle Plaintiffs to Defendants’ full bank records.  Accordingly, the court orders the subpoenas as follows:  All cancelled checks signed by Grutman or Turner, all bank records referencing Expo, all records related to plaintiff’s property, and any wire transfers to or from Turner and Grutman.    Plaintiffs have not demonstrated how account applications or credit cards are necessary.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Avraham Gutman and Sharon Trumer’s Motions to Quash Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records is GRANTED, in part.

 

Sanctions

 

With respect to sanctions, a court may “in its discretion award the amount of reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing [a motion to quash], including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”¿ (CCP § 1987.2(a).)

 

Both parties request sanctions. However, the court finds Plaintiffs did have a legitimate discovery interest in seeking certain bank records of Defendants and Defendants did have a legitimate interest in protecting their financial privacy rights. Accordingly, both requests for sanctions are denied.

 


 


Case Number: 23VECV00091    Hearing Date: September 11, 2024    Dept: W

ROBIN LA DUE vs SHAHE BOYADIJAN, et al.

 

defendants’ MOTION TO DISMISS FOR PLAINTIFF’S FAILURE TO AMEND FOLLOWING SUSTAINING OF DEMURRER

 

Date of Hearing:        Sept. 11, 2024                                   Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV00091

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Robin La Due

Responding Party:     Shahe Boyadjian and Maral Boyadjian

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 9, 2023, Plaintiff Robin La Due filed a complaint against Defendants Shahe Boyadjian and Maral Boyadjian. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint on August 24, 2023 for Breach of Contract, Stolen Property, and Malicious Sabotage. Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on January 8, 2024, adding a cause of action for Fraud on the Court.

 

The parties have a history of litigation between them that began prior to Plaintiffs filing the instant action. In January 2022, Boyadjian filed a small claims action against Robin La Due to recover unpaid rent. Judgment was ultimately entered in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. On June 21, 2022, Boyadjian filed an unlawful detainer action against Robin La Due, stemming from his longstanding unjustified failure to pay rent at the subject property, in violation of the lease agreement. That matter was also fully litigated and resulted in another judgment in favor of Boyadjian, against La Due. Following those two actions by Boyadjian, Plaintiff subsequently brought two civil actions against the Boyadjians. In this action, Plaintiff alleges Defendants did not properly give Plaintiff enough time to vacate the premises upon the Sheriffs’ arrival, resulting in their leaving behind certain personal property at the unit, which Defendants have refused to return. Plaintiff also alleges Defendant withheld Plaintiff’s security deposit.

 

On April 30, 2024, defendants filed a motion to dismiss, which was granted on June 10, 2024.  The court ordered plaintiff’s complaint dismissed with prejudice as plaintiff failed to amend the complaint within the time allowed by the court, as required by C.C.P. Section 581(f).  While dismissal under Code of Civil Procedure section 581(f) is discretionary, any such dismissal must be with prejudice. (Cano v. Glover (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 326, 330.) “No cases have held that a dismissal pursuant to section 581, subdivision (f)(2) may be without prejudice. The right to dismiss without prejudice is expressly permitted by other subdivisions of section 581 but section 581, subdivision (f)(2) does not so provide.”¿(Id. at 229.)  Plaintiff failed to file an opposition to the motion to dismiss and the case was dismissed. 

 

Plaintiff now moves for reconsideration of that order.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.  

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff filed the instant motion for reconsideration on June 21, 2024.   It cannot fairly be construed as a motion for reconsideration, as it does not comply with the requirements of C.C.P. Section 1008, namely that the motion be based “upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law.”  Rather, this motion is in truth a motion for relief from error pursuant to C.C.P. 473(b), which provides in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” 

 

Plaintiff asserts that he should be relieved of his mistake in failing to file the amended complaint because he is self-represented and is burdened by the care of his mother and other responsibilities. While this court is sensitive to the difficulties of appearing without counsel and the financial and personal difficulties plaintiff alleges, plaintiff must still conform to the legal requirements for the motions he brings.   He has not complied with the mandatory requirements of Section 473(b) that he submit a copy of the amended complaint with the motion for relief.  As that section makes clear: the “[a]pplication for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.”   

 

But even had such a document been filed, the court would not find an adequate basis for relief.  Plaintiff knew that he had to file the amended complaint by April 29, 2024.   He was in court when the order was issued.  Further, he filed a declaration on April 26, 2024, informing the court that he had no intention of filing the amended complaint, as he had been ordered.  He informed the court that the case was not “going anywhere,” and he wanted to “stop wasting the time of everyone involved.”  (La Due Declaration filed April 26, 2024.)   Given this admission that he knew he was obligated to file the amended complaint and he elected not to at the time, either because he thought it was a waste of time or thought he would not get a fair hearing, the court does not find his subsequent change of heart entitled to the relief contemplated for true mistake, surprise or excusable neglect.  The deliberate decision to not file a pleading despite a court order and deadline, is not excusable neglect, nor can it be deemed a true mistake or surprise.

 

The Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.
