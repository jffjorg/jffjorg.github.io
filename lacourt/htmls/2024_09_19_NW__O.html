I.               BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Level 8 Management, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) brings a breach of contract action against Defendants Nicola Di Lello a/k/a Nick Di Lello (“Di Lello”) and Ital Concepts, LLC d/b/a Bottega Pizzeria Ristorante (“Bottega”) (collectively, “Defendants”) arising out of Plaintiff’s alleged performance of professional accounting services to assist Defendants apply for and obtain Employee Retention Tax Credits.

Defendants now move for a judgment on the pleadings. Plaintiff also moves to compel Defendants’ further responses to eight separate discovery requests. The Court will address both sets of motions below.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed its Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Common Counts.

On April 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed its First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging (1) Breach of Contract; and (2) Common Counts.

On May 22, 2023, Defendants filed their Demurrer with Motion to Strike.

On August 7, 2023, the court overruled Defendants’ Demurrer and denied Defendants’ Motion to Strike.

On September 6, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer.

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Motions to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admission, Set One (“RFAs”), as to both Di Lello (“Di Lello RFAs”) and Bottega (“Bottega RFAs”).

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Motions to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One (“RFPs”) as to both Di Lello (“Di Lello RFPs”) and Bottega (“Bottega RFPs”).

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Motions to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories-General, Set One (“FROGS”), as to both Di Lello (“Di Lello FROGS”) and Bottega (“Bottega FROGS”).

On July 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Motions to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One (“SROGS”), as to both Di Lello (“Di Lello SROGS”) and Bottega (“Bottega SROGS”).

On August 15, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (“Motion”).

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Opposition to Defendants’ Motion.

On September 6, 2024, Defendants filed their eight Oppositions to Plaintiff’s eight motions to compel further.

On September 12, 2024, Defendants filed their Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to their Motion.

On September 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Reply to Defendants’ Opposition to Plaintiff’s eight motions to compel further.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Judgment on the Pleadings

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1064.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether the factual allegations, assumed true, are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452-53.) Thus, it may be granted if, from the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 438(d)[1]; Saltarelli & Steponovich v. Douglas (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1, 5.)

    A defendant or plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 438(f).)

B.      Request for Production of Documents

In responding to a request for production of documents, a party must provide one of the following: (1) a legally adequate statement of compliance; (2) a statement of inability to comply; or (3) an objection to the particular demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210(a).) If any objections are made, they must: (1) “[i]dentify with particularity any document … falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made”; and (2) “[s]et forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection.”¿(Id. at § 2031.240(b).) A statement that the responding party is unable to comply with a particular demand “shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand”; “shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party”;  and “shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Id. at § 2031.230.) 

A party who believes a responding party has served (1) an incomplete statement of compliance, (2) an inadequate, incomplete, or evasive statement of compliance, or (3) an unmeritorious objection can move for an order compelling further responses but must “set forth specific acts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (See id. at §§ 2031.310(a)-(b)(1).)

C.             Interrogatories

A propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses to interrogatories if the responding party produced an evasive or incomplete answer or a meritless or overly general objection in response to an interrogatory. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(a).) A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories lies where the party to whom the interrogatories were directed gave responses deemed improper by the propounding party; e.g., objections, or evasive or incomplete answers. (See id. at § 2030.300.)¿The moving party must also include reasons why further answers should be ordered: legal or factual arguments why the answers given were incomplete or nonresponsive, or the objections invalid.¿(See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(c).)

However, where a timely motion to has been filed, “the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer the interrogatories.”¿(Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civil Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2017) ¶ 8:1179.)¿“The ruling usually is based on consideration of the following factors: [¶] the relationship of the information sought to the issues framed in the pleadings; [¶] the likelihood that disclosure will be of practical benefit to the party seeking discovery; [¶] the burden or expense likely to be encountered by the responding party in furnishing the information sought.”¿(Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court (Rolfe) (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19.)

D.      Requests for Admissions

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.290, a requesting party may move for further responses to requests for admissions when responses have been timely served but the requesting party finds the requests evasive or incomplete or objections without merit or too general. No further responses can be ordered, however, to RFAs that have been unqualifiedly denied, even if the facts are unquestionably true. (See Holguin v. Superior Court (Hoage) (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 820.)

IV.          MEET AND CONFER

A.             Judgment on the Pleadings

“Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 439(a) (emphasis added).)         

Defendants have not complied with their meet and confer obligations and did not address these obligations in either a declaration or their Motion. Defendants’ counsel thus made absolutely no attempt to informally avoid this Court’s intervention as required. Accordingly, the Motion is procedurally defective and is denied on that basis alone. Nevertheless, the Court will address the merits below.   

B.             Compel Further Discovery Responses

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b)(1), and 2033.290(b)(1).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Id. at § 2016.040.) However, a discovery motion should not be denied automatically based upon the reason that the moving parties failed to meet and confer in good faith. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.)

Plaintiff provides a meet and confer declaration attesting to the parties meet and confer efforts, which consists of one meet and confer letter and a reply. (See, e.g., Bottega RFAs Declaration of Simon Song filed July 9, 2024, ¶ 7, Exs. E & F.)

As a general rule, this Court doesn’t find a single letter and reply adequate to satisfy the meet and confer requirements under this rule. Particularly unproductive – not to mention ironic – was how Plaintiff’s counsel’s purported “refus[al] to meet and confer in person or telephonically as required before filing any such motions to compel”, id. at Ex. F, drew Defendants’ counsel’s ire when (a) no such in person or telephonic requirement exists and (b) Defendant’s counsel didn’t even bother to send a letter before filing its Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings despite the fact that motion does require meeting and conferring to be done in person or by telephone.

The Court will address the merits of Plaintiff’s motions in the interest of judicial efficiency. However, the Court expects more collaborative efforts from both counsel going forward that are more than box-checking exercises designed to merely comply with the letter of the law, but, more importantly, comply with its spirit as well.

V.             ANALYSIS

There are nine motions presently before this Court. The Court will first address Defendants’ Motion. The Court will next address Plaintiff’s eight discovery motions, first addressing those propounded upon Di Lello, and next addressing those propounded upon Bottega.

A.             Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

Defendants request this Court take judicial notice of the following: (1) Secretary of State’s Certificate of No Record, which states no record was “found in the Limited Liability Company files of this office…of the name: LEVEL 8 MANAGEMENT, LLC;” (2) County of Los Angeles’ Certificate of Fact of Non-Filing, indicating no record of “Level 8 Management, LLC” could be found; (3) 31 CFR § 10.27; (4) Treasury Department Circular No. 230, 31 C.F.R. part 10 (Circular 230); (5) “That no entities called Level 8 Management LLC or Level 8 Management, Inc. appear in a California Department of Consumer Affairs, Board of Accountancy License Search, demonstrating that Plaintiff does not have a license to perform accountant services in California;” and (6) “That no individuals named Sam Derakshan appear in a California Department of Consumer Affairs, Board of Accountancy License Search, demonstrating that Plaintiff’s principal, Sam Derakshan does not have a license to perform accountant services in California.” (Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”) Nos. 1-6, Exs. A-D.)

In opposition, Plaintiff objects to Defendants 5th and 6th request for judicial notice, on the basis that Plaintiff already addressed and proved his status as a licensed tax prepare via declaration in opposition to Defendants’ previous Demurrer. (See Plaintiff’s Objection to RJN Nos. 5-6.)

The Court will address each request in turn.

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ RJN Nos. 1 and 2 as official acts of the state, per Evidence Code § 452.

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ RJN Nos. 3 and 4 per Evidence Code § 452.

The Court OVERRULES Plaintiff’s objections but DENIES Defendants’ RJN Nos. 5 and 6 because Defendants have not attached any Exhibits to these requests. As such, the Court assumes Defendants are requesting this Court take judicial notice of statements that, based on Plaintiff’s objections, are reasonably subject to dispute and thus inappropriate for judicial notice. (See Evid. Code § 452.)

Finally, in their Reply, Defendants appear to request this Court take judicial notice of the fact that “Level 8 Management, Inc. was formed on March 8, 2024, as confirmed by the California Secretary of State.” (See Reply, pg. 5, fn. 1.) The Court DENIES this request because Defendants did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(l) (requiring a separate statement) and Code of Civil Procedure § 438(d) (requiring it to be specified in the notice of motion) as to that fact.

2.               The Court Denies Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

Defendants move for a judgment on the pleadings arguing Plaintiff’s first cause of action for breach of contract fails to state a cause of action as (1) Plaintiff lacks standing to sue; (2) the contract is void; and (3) Plaintiff operated as an unlicensed contractor. (See Motion, pp. 6-10.) Defendants also argue Plaintiff’s second cause of action for common counts fails to state a cause of action because Plaintiff lacks standing to sue. (See id. at 10-11.)

First, Defendants’ Motion is improper per Code of Civil Procedure § 438(g)(1). Section 438(g)(1) allows a party who has already demurred to a complaint to bring a motion for judgment on the pleadings on the same grounds as the demurrer “provided that there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer” (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(g)(1) (emphasis added).)

 Here, Defendants’ Motion relies on the exact same grounds as their previously overruled Demurrer: (1) Plaintiff lacks standing to sue; (2) The contract is void; (3) Plaintiff operated as an unlicensed contractor. (See Demurrer.) Defendants attempt to bolster their identical grounds with what appears to be rebuttal to the court’s ruling on their Demurrer, but these additional arguments are just that—additional arguments. (See Minute Order dated August 7, 2023; see, e.g., Motion, pg. 8:8-28, 9:1-10.) Additional arguments are not material changes in case law or statute and thus are inappropriate for a subsequent motion for judgment on the pleadings. The Court denies Defendants’ Motion on this ground alone.

The only new facts Defendants submit, and which this Court granted judicial notice of, are their RJN Nos. 1 and 2. Even if this Court were to disregard the glaring deficiencies in Defendants’ Motion and consider their Motion on the merits, which it is not, the Motion would fail. RJN Nos. 1 and 2 do not implicate Plaintiff’s standing to sue as they address the existence of Level 8 Management, LLC—not Plaintiff, Level 8 Management, Inc. Plaintiff alleges “although Plaintiff was not a party to the original contract, it is the real party in interest and a proper plaintiff because Level 8 Management, LLC transferred and/or assigned its contractual duties and rights under the Agreement to Plaintiff.” (See FAC, ¶ 4.) “The question for purposes of standing is not the name used by the party suing but whether the party suing is the party possessing the right sued upon.” (The Rossdale Group, LLC v. Walton (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 936, 945–946 (citations omitted).) Thus, Plaintiff has properly alleged standing at the pleading stage.[2]

In reply, Defendants reiterate their arguments concerning Level 8 Management, LLC’s nonexistence and assert their Motion is brought on non-statutory grounds and so is not subject to Code of Civil Procedure § 438(g)’s restrictions. (See Reply, pp. 3-4.) The Court is unpersuaded. In their Motion and Reply, Defendants ask this Court to make the leap from (1) Level 8 Management, LLC, does not turn up in searches of the California Secretary of State or County of Los Angeles records to (2) Plaintiff does not have standing. The Court will not make such a leap at the pleading stage, even if this Motion had been properly brought.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion.

B.             Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses Are Granted.

1.               Plaintiff’s Motions Are Timely

Notice of a motion to compel further responses must be given within 45 days of service of verified responses or supplemental responses, or on or before any specific later date to which the parties have agreed in writing. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.230(c), 2030.300(c), 2033.290(c).) When responses are served by mail, that time is extended by five days. (See id. at 1013(a).) If such notice is not given timely, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (See id.)¿¿ ¿¿

Here, Defendants served their responses to all eight discovery requests on May 21, 2024, via mail. (See, e.g., Bottega SROGS Declaration of Simon Song filed July 9, 2024, pg. 13, ¶ 6.) Plaintiff filed the instant Motions on July 9, 2024 – 49 days later and within the applicable 50-day window. (See, e.g., Bottega SROGS.) Therefore, the motions are timely.

2.               Compel Further Discovery Responses from Di Lello

Plaintiff moves to compel Di Lello’s further discovery responses to its RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs. (See, e.g., Di Lello RFPs.) After several extensions, Di Lello provided the same unverified response to each: “Defendant NICOLA DILELLO aka NICK DILELLO (“Defendant”) objects to the entirety of Plaintiffs [RFPs or FROGS or SROGS or RFAs] propounded by Plaintiff LEVEL 8 MANAGEMENT, INC. There are no allegations in the First Amended Complaint against Defendant. Defendant is not liable to Plaintiff under any theory and any information requested from Defendant violates his right to privacy and is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Defendant will not provide any substantive responses and demand is reiterated that he be dismissed from this case.” (See, e.g., Di Lello RFPs Declaration of Simon Song filed July 9, 2024, pg. 13, ¶ 6, Ex. D.)

Plaintiff argues further responses are required because “Defendant is a named defendant in this action, and the person who executed the MSA on behalf of Bottega. Plaintiff is clearly entitled to seek discovery from Defendant regarding his claims and defenses.” (See, e.g., Di Lello RFPs, pp. 4-5.)

In opposition, Di Lello argues “The services were allegedly performed by Level 8 Management, LLC and then the collection rights assigned to Plaintiff. However, Level 8 Management, LLC does not exist and never existed. That entity cannot enforce any contractual or common law rights. As such, any assignment of such rights is non-existent… Regardless, Defendant still fully supplemented the discovery responses in dispute and served those responses on September 5, 2024.” (See, e.g., Di Lello RFPs Opposition, pp. 2-3.)

The Court is unpersuaded by Di Lello’s objections or arguments. As Plaintiff notes, Di Lello is a named defendant and the individual who signed the contract attached to Plaintiff’s FAC on behalf of Bottega. As such, his copy-and-paste objection is completely meritless as are his attestations to serving supplemental discovery after Plaintiff filed the instant motions.[3] Plaintiff is entitled to code-compliant responses without objection and responsive documents for each of its four motions pursuant to §§ 2031.310, 2030.300, and 2033.290.  

Accordingly, the Court overrules all of Di Lello’s objections and orders Di Lello to provide code-compliant responses without any further objection – including privilege because that was not asserted in their initial responses. Furthermore, the Court orders Di Lello to produce all responsive documents to Plaintiff’s RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs on or before October 11, 2024. In doing so, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion RFPs, Motion FROGs, Motion SROGS, and Motion RFAs as to Di Lello.

3.               The Court Grants Plaintiff’s Motions To Compel Further Responses From Bottega.

a.               RFPs

Plaintiff moves to compel Bottega’s further responses to RFPs Nos. 1-9. (See Bottega’s Separate Statement in Support of Opposition to Bottega RFPs (“SS Bottega RFPs”), pp. 2-25.)

To each, Bottega did not object but simply provided the following response: “Defendant has performed a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and will produce any and all non-privileged documents responsive to this Request that are in its possession, custody, or control. Responding Party reserves the right to produce further documents when they become available.” (See id.)

Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to its RFPs. Simply put, section 2031.210(a) presents a multiple-choice question requiring respondents to select one of three specific options; it does not allow for fill in the blank style responses. Rather than do that, Bottega’s responses blend the gist of subdivision (a) with language from section 2031.230 by referring to having made “a diligent search and reasonable inquiry.” Moreover, Bottega’s attempt to avail itself of any privilege is not well taken because they never raised an objection based on a specific privilege. Therefore, Plaintiff is unable to determine what category of documents is being withheld, if any,  and why. Finally, as of the date of the filing of the Motion RFPs on July 9, 2024, Bottega had failed to produce any documents whatsoever despite 49 days having passed.[4] In summary, Bottega RFP responses are not code compliant because they fail to provide (1) a legally adequate statement of compliance; (2) a statement of inability to comply; or (3) an objection to the particular demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210(a).)

Accordingly, the Court orders Bottega to provide code-compliant responses without objection – including as to any privileges – and produce all responsive documents to Plaintiff’s RFPs on or before October 11, 2024.

b.              FROGS

Plaintiff moves to compel Bottega’s further responses to FROGS Nos. 3.4-3.7, 15.1, 17.1, and 50.5. (See Bottega’s Separate Statement in Support of Opposition to Bottega FROGS (“SS Bottega FROGS”), pp. 2-46.) The Court has grouped the FROGS at issue and will address each in turn.

                                                                            i.                    FROGS Nos. 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, and 17.1 (RFAs Nos. 19-28)

Bottega did not provide a response to FROGS Nos. 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, and 17.1 (RFAs Nos. 19-28).

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion FROGs as to these FROG. The Court orders Bottega to provide code compliant responses without objection to its FROGS Nos. 3.4, 3.5, 3.6, 3.7, and 17.1 (RFAs Nos. 19-28) pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.220 on or before October 11, 2024.

                                                                          ii.                    FROG No. 15.1

FROG No. 15.1 asks Bottega to “[i]dentify each denial of a material allegation and each special or affirmative defense in your pleadings and for each: (a) state all facts upon which you base the denial or special or affirmative defense; (b) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and (3) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your denial or special or affirmative defense, and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT.” (SS Bottega FROGS, pg. 15.)

          Rather than provide separate responses to its general denial and each of its 30 affirmative defenses, Bottega provides one single longform response to subpart (a). As to subpart (b), Bottega’s response is “Nicola and Christine DiLello, who may be contacted via counsel, Gregory F. Wilder, Saman Derakhshan, and perhaps others in the employ of Wildflower Legacy and Wealth Planning, LLC and the Level 8 entities. Bottega’s response to subpart (c) is “All documents referenced above, the agreement between the parties and all correspondences exchanged amongst the parties and other related to this project. All documents are either publicly available or equally available to Plaintiff.” (SS Bottega FROGS, pp. 15-18.)

Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to its FROG No. 15.1 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.220 and 2030.300. The whole point of 15.1 is to force a responding party to provide specific facts relating to each affirmative defense so that the propounding party may evaluate the strength of each one separately. Doing them separately also enables the propounding party to evaluate the responsiveness of each response to the affirmative defense for which it is given. Bottega’s approach is incomplete at best and purposely obfuscatory and evasive at worst.[5] To provide code-complaint responses, Bottega must either respond completely as to each affirmative defense or state it does not have sufficient personal knowledge to answer the interrogatory and that it made a reasonable and good-faith effort to obtain the requested information from other individuals and organizations. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220(c).) Bottega has not done either.

                                                                        iii.                    FROG No. 17.1 (RFAs Nos. 2-11, 15-16, 18)

FROG No. 17.1 states “[i]s your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories at unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission: (b) state all facts upon which you base your response; (d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.” (SS Bottega FROGS, pp. 21-35.)

In response to 17.1(b) (RFAs Nos. 2-11, 15-16), Bottega refers to either its responses to its RFAs or previous argument but does not otherwise provide a response. (See id. at 27, 31.) In response to 17.1(b) (RFA No. 18), Bottega states “Defendant admits that it has not tendered payment for any portion of the $59,858.04 but denies that said amount was due.” (Id. at 35.)

In response to 17.1(d) (RFAs Nos. 2-11, 15-16, and 18), Bottega states “All documents referenced above, the agreement between the parties and all correspondences exchanged amongst the parties and others related to this project. All documents are either publicly available or equally available to Plaintiff.” (Id.)

Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to its FROG 17.1(b) (RFAs Nos. 2-11, 15-16) and 17.1(d) (RFAs Nos. 2-11, 15-16, and 18), but not 17.1(b) (RFA No. 18) as it finds Bottega’s response to this subpart sufficient. For the rest, the Court reiterates: Bottega’s response is incomplete at best and intentionally obfuscatory and evasive at worst. To provide code-complaint responses, Bottega must either respond completely or state it does not have sufficient personal knowledge to answer the interrogatory and that it made a reasonable and good-faith effort to obtain the requested information from other individuals and organizations. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220(c).) Bottega has not done so here.

                                                                         iv.                    FROG No. 50.5

FROG No. 50.5 asks, “Is any agreement alleged in the pleadings unenforceable? If so, identify each unenforceable agreement and state why it is unenforceable.” (SS Bottega FROGS, pg. 43.)

In response, Bottega states “[y]es. The entire agreement is unenforceable for the reasons stated above and herein.” (Id.)

Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to its FROG No. 50.5. Since this response incorporates by reference responses the Court has already found defective for the reasons stated above, the Court reincorporates its analysis regarding those defective responses here.

d.              RFAS

Plaintiff moves to compel Bottega’s further responses to RFAs Nos. 2-11 and 25-27. (See Bottega’s Separate Statement in Support of Opposition to Bottega RFAs (“SS Bottega RFAs”), pp. 1-54.)

In response to each, Bottega offers objections only, such as the request is “impermissibly compound,” “asks Defendant to admit to something that is within the sole knowledge…of Level 8,” and “calls for a legal conclusion and the opinion of an expert.” (SS Bottega RFAs.)

Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to its RFAs Nos. 2-11 and 25-27. As a preliminary matter, Plaintiff’s RFAs are not impermissibly compound. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.060(f).) Additionally, Bottega’s objections are improper. It is not ground for an objection that an RFA calls for an opinion, is ambiguous, or that the facts are known to the propounder of the request. (See Cembrook v. Superior Court (Sterling Drug, Inc.) (1961) 56 Cal.2d 423, 430; see Hillman v. Stults (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 848, 885.) These responses miss the entire point of RFAs: to find as many potentially undisputed factual and legal issues as possible.

Accordingly, the Court overrules all of Bottega’s objections and orders Bottega to provide code-compliant responses without objection to Plaintiff’s RFAs on or before October 11, 2024.[6]

VI.          SANCTIONS

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction¿. . .¿against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel¿. . .” responses to interrogatories, requests for production, or request for admission, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.¿(Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), & 2033.290(d).) Similarly,¿“[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying¿a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)

Plaintiff seeks $2,800.00 in discovery sanctions for its Di Lello FROGS, which accounts for 4 hours to draft the motion, 2 hours to prepare a potential reply, 1 hour to attend the hearing, and a billing rate of $400.00 an hour.

For the rest of its motions, Plaintiff seeks $2,000.00 per motion, which accounts for 2 hours to draft the motion, 2 hours to prepare a potential reply, 1 hour to attend the hearing, and a billing rate of $400.00 an hour.

In total, Plaintiff seeks $16,800.00 in discovery sanctions.

Defendants also request discovery sanctions.

The Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rate of $400 reasonable given the prevailing rates in the Greater Los Angeles area for attorneys with his level of experience. However, given the overlapping issues, Plaintiff’s duplicated hearing requests, and Plaintiff’s “omnibus” Reply, the Court finds it appropriate to award Plaintiff’s counsel $7,660.00 in discovery sanctions, which accounts for 2 hours of drafting time per motion [16 total hours], 2 hours to draft its omnibus Reply, 1 hour to attend the hearing, plus the $60.00 filing fee. This breaks down to $950 per motion.

Defendants’ counsel shall pay Plaintiff’s counsel monetary sanctions in the amount of $7,660.00 on or before October 31, 2024.

Defendants are not entitled to sanctions as they did not prevail on their Motion or Plaintiff’s motions.
