Case Number: 21STCV33172    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

agavni sagryan ;

 

Plaintiff,

 

 

vs.

 

 

general motors, llc , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

21STCV33172

 

 

Hearing Date:

October 21, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Agavni Sagryan       

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant General Motors LLC

Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on plaintiff Agavni Sagryan’s evidentiary objections, filed on October 14, 2024, as follows:

Objections Nos. 1-23 are overruled.

The court denies plaintiff Agavni Sagryan’s request that the court strike the declaration of Xylon Quezada in its entirety.

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Agavni Sagryan (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding attorney’s fees and costs in her favor, to be paid by defendant General Motors LLC (“Defendant”), in the total amount of $86,721.61, consisting of $82,152.50 in attorney’s fees and $4,569.11 in costs.

First, the court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses from Defendant pursuant to the Song-Beverly Act.

Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d), provides:¿ “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.”¿¿ 

            Plaintiff has presented evidence establishing that, pursuant to the parties’ “Settlement Agreement and Release of All Claims,” the parties agreed to settle this action, and Defendant shall pay (1) $36,464.28 to Plaintiff and $18,535.72 to Chase Auto Finance, and (2) Plaintiff’s attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses.[1]  (Barry Decl., Ex. 6, ¶ 2, subd. (b).)  The parties further agreed that “Plaintiff is the prevailing party for purposes of the motion” for attorney’s fees.  (Id. at ¶ 2, subd. (b)(iii).)  The court therefore finds that Plaintiff may recover from Defendant attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses that were reasonably incurred in connection with the commencement and prosecution of this action.  (Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (d).)

            Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has established, as to the attorney’s fees incurred to commence and prosecute this action and to prepare the pending motion for attorney’s fees, a lodestar amount of $68,095.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted).)¿ “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿¿ 

Plaintiff has submitted the declarations of David N. Barry, Elizabeth Quinn, Otis R. Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Defendants

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Matthew D. Rifat and Law Offices of Matthew D. Rifat APC (“Defendants’ Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel for defendants SRRA, Inc., Mohammad Amir Hossain, and Sura Shirin (“Defendants”) in this action.

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. 2) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’”¿ (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)¿ The court should also consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.”¿ (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

For a motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2), California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil form (MC-053)).¿¿¿ 

The court finds that Defendants’ Counsel has not complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 and therefore denies Defendants’ Counsel’s motion.

First, although Defendants’ Counsel filed the notice of motion and motion directed to Defendants on Judicial Council form MC-051, Defendants’ Counsel did not (1) file and serve the Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil on mandatory Judicial Council form MC-052, or (2) lodge with the court and serve on the parties the proposed Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel -- Civil on Judicial Council form MC-053.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1362, subds. (c), (e).)  While Defendants’ Counsel did file and serve a declaration in support of this motion, the California Rules of Court require the declaration made in support of a motion to be relieved as counsel to be made on Judicial Council form MC-052.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1362, subd. (c) [the motion “must” be accompanied by the MC-052 declaration].) 

Second, while Defendants’ Counsel has filed a proof of service of this motion on Defendants, Defendants’ Counsel did not submit a declaration stating facts showing that counsel served Defendants at their last known addresses, which Defendants’ Counsel has confirmed within the past 30 days is current or has been unable to do so after making reasonable efforts.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)

The court therefore denies Defendants’ Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel for Defendants, without prejudice to Defendants’ Counsel’s filing a new motion that complies with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.

ORDER

            The court denies, without prejudice, Matthew D. Rifat and Law Offices of Matthew D. Rifat APC’s motion to be relieved as counsel for defendants.

            The court orders Matthew D. Rifat and Law Offices of Matthew D. Rifat APC to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

(2)   Motion to Tax Costs

 

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with each motion.

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

Plaintiff Bonnie Duboff (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding her attorney’s fees and costs, to be paid from the recovery obtained by the limited partnership, 245 Spalding Partners, L.P., in this action.  Defendant Linda Schermer, individually, as trustee of the Surviving Trustor’s Trust under the Schermer Family Trust, as trustee of the Deceased Trustor’s Trust under the Schermer Family Trust, and as trustee of the Marital Trust under the Schermer Family Trust (“Defendant”) has opposed the motion.

First, the court (1) finds that Plaintiff’s derivative action was successful, and therefore  (2) exercises its discretion to award to Plaintiff reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, from the recovery obtained by 245 Spalding Partners, L.P. in this action.

“If a derivative action is successful in whole or in part, the court may award the plaintiff reasonable expenses, including reasonable attorney’s fees, from the recovery of the limited partnership.”  (Corp. Code, § 15910.05, subd. (b).)  

The court finds, based on a review of Plaintiff’s Complaint, the court’s Statements of Decision, and the Judgment entered in this action, that Plaintiff’s action is derivative.  (Compl., ¶¶ 1 [“Plaintiff brings this action as a derivative action on behalf of 245 Spalding Partners, L.P.], 21 [“Plaintiff brings this action, in part, derivatively in the right, and for the benefit, of 245 Spalding Partners, L.P. . . .”]; Compl., pp. 24:15-18 [asserting first cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 26:13-16 [asserting second cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 27:1-5 [asserting third cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 28:1-5 [asserting fourth cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 28:22-26 [asserting fifth cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 29:16-20 [asserting sixth cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”], 30:9-13 [asserting seventh cause of action “by Plaintiff Derivatively”]; Statement of Decision (Phase Two), pp. 24:15-25:4 [finding in favor of Plaintiff and awarding to Plaintiff, derivatively on behalf of 245 Spalding Partners, L.P., damages and restitution]; Oct. 9, 2023 Judgment, pp. 3:1-5, 3:10-14, 5:3-7 [finding in favor of Plaintiff, derivatively on behalf of 245 Spalding Partners LP].)  The court notes that Defendant contends that Plaintiff may not recover attorney’s fees because she “only sought to advance her own personal interests and did not advance the interests of a class of others.”  (Opp., p. 2:18-20 [emphasis in original omitted].)  The court disagrees.

The court acknowledges that, when evaluating a request for attorney’s fees pursuant to the common fund or substantial benefit principles, a court may decline to award attorney’s fees if the movant’s objective was not to secure a common fund but to establish personal adverse interests.  (Baker v. Pratt (1986) 76 Cal.App.3d 370, 379; Cziraki v. Thunder Cats, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 552, 563 [“When a corporation is the sole beneficiary of a derivative shareholder suit, we conclude it should bear the costs associated with obtaining that benefit”].)  However, as noted by Plaintiff, the common fund and substantial benefit theories provide for the recovery of attorney’s fees separate from Corporations Code section 15910.05.  Even if the court were to find that the reasoning set forth in the cases discussing those theories could be applied here, Defendant has not shown, and the court does not find, that Plaintiff obtained personal interests adverse to 245 Spalding Partners, L.P., such that the court should exercise its discretion to deny Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees.  (Corp. Code, § 15910.05, subd. (b) [“the court may award” a successful plaintiff in a derivative action reasonable expenses] [emphasis added].)

The court further finds that (1) Plaintiff’s derivative action was successful, and (2) it is equitable to award Plaintiff reasonable expenses incurred in litigating this action.  (Oct. 9, 2023 Judgment, p. 5:3-16 [awarding damages in favor of Plaintiff, derivatively on behalf of 245 Spading Partners, L.P., and against Defendant in the amount of $5,930,072]; Corp. Code, § 15910.05, subd. (b).)  The court therefore exercises its discretion to award Plaintiff reasonable expenses from the recovery obtained by 245 Spalding Partners, LP.  (Corp. Code, § 15910.05, subd. (b).)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has established a lodestar amount of $775,162.50.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted).)¿ “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿¿¿ 

Plaintiff has submitted the declarations of attorneys Stephen D. Weisskopf and David C. Nelson, in which counsel attest to the qualifications, skill, and experience of the attorneys and legal staff that worked on this action.  (Weisskopf Decl., ¶¶ 4-7 [attesting to his qualifications], 10-15 [attesting to the qualifications of counsel and legal staff from his firm]; Nelson Decl., ¶¶ 4, 6 [attesting to his qualifications and hourly rates], 10-12 [attesting to qualifications and rates of other attorneys at Loeb & Loeb LLP].)  The court finds that the hourly rates requested ($375 as to attorney Weisskopf; $375 as to attorney Kent; $250 to $325 for attorney Daubs; $250 for attorney Stiner; $150-175 for Charne as a paralegal and $275 as an associate; $200-$225 for attorney Ovespian; $175 for paralegal Spicher; $150 for paralegal Bevan; $845-$1,095 for attorney Nelson; $780 for attorney Vidal; $690 for attorney Givens; and $965 for attorney Catalano) are reasonable in light of the qualifications, experience, and skill of counsel.

The court has reviewed the billing statements submitted by Plaintiff’s attorneys, Defendant’s opposition, and the declaration of André Jardini submitted by Defendant in support of her opposition to this motion.  The court finds that (1) of the 59.3 hours billed by attorney Weisskopf to prepare the moving papers in connection with Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment, 19.3 of those hours were not reasonably expended; (2) of the 12.3 hours billed by attorney Stiner to oppose Defendant’s motion to file an amended answer, 2.0 of those hours were not reasonably expended; (3) of the 23 hours billed by attorney Stiner to prepare the reply to the motion for summary judgment, 5 of those hours were not reasonably expended; (4) of the total 10.8 hours billed by attorney Weisskopf to file a reply to the motion for sanctions and preliminary injunction, 2.8 of those hours were not reasonably incurred; (5) of the total 8.8 hours billed by attorney Stiner to file a reply to the motion for sanctions and preliminary injunction, 2.4 of those hours were not reasonably expended; (6) of the 8.1 hours billed by attorney Stiner to oppose the motion to transfer, 4.1 of those hours were not reasonably expended; and (7) of the 16.3 hours billed by paralegal Bevan to draft the memorandum of costs, 3.3 of those hours were not reasonably expended.  (Weisskopf Decl., Ex. A.)

The court finds that the work performed and hours billed by attorneys at Loeb & Loeb LLP were reasonable because Plaintiff has submitted evidence establishing that Defendant attempted to transfer this action to the Probate Court or to relate this action to another case pending in the Probate Court, such that the court finds that it was reasonable for Plaintiff to engage additional attorneys with experience with probate and trust matters.  (Weisskopf Decl., ¶ 17; Nelson Decl., ¶ 7; Jardini Decl., ¶¶ 46, 79 [requesting the fees incurred by Loeb & Loeb LLP be excluded].)  The court also finds that (1) the staffing of attorneys was not unreasonable and therefore declines to decrease the award by 10.3 percent as requested by Defendant, and (2) the entries to which Defendant objects as vague are not vague since they sufficiently describe the work performed (e.g., preparation for trial).  (Jardini Decl., ¶¶ 62-68; Jardini Decl., p. 76 [Vague Billing].)  

The court finds, and Plaintiff does not appear to dispute, that Plaintiff’s attorneys erroneously billed for identical charges on March 16, 2022.  The court therefore excludes from the lodestar 0.30 hours billed by attorney Kent.  (Jardini Decl., p. 78 [Error Billing].)  However, the court finds that Defendant has not shown that the April 1, 2018 entries by attorney Stiner were duplicative.  Specifically, although Stiner had two entries described as “Research and analyze issues re standing[,]” Stiner separately billed 1.10 hours and 3.10 hours for such time, and therefore does not appear to have been an erroneously duplicative charge.  (Ibid.; Weisskopf Decl., Ex. A, p. 150.)

The court therefore finds that Plaintiff has established that her attorneys reasonably expended 2,231.3 hours in litigating this action (58.2 hours expended by Loeb & Loeb LLP and 2,170.8 hours expended by LevatoLaw).  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has established a lodestar figure of $775,162.50 and grants Plaintiff’s motion to award her attorney’s fees in that amount, to be paid from the recovery obtained by 245 Spalding Partners, L.P.

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO TAX COSTS

On October 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Memorandum of Costs, claiming $51,981.47 in costs.  Defendant now moves the court for an order striking from Plaintiff’s Memorandum of Costs (1) a total of $1,103.20 in filing fees (Item 1g); (2) a total of $214.36 in service of process fees (Item 5d); (3) $11,364.60 in court ordered transcripts (Item 9); (4) attorney’s fees (Item 10); and (5) a total of $528.96 in electronic filing or service fees (Item 14).

“‘If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.’”  (Lowry v. Port San Luis Harbor District (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 211, 222 [internal citation omitted].)

            The court finds that Defendant has not met her burden to show that the costs incurred in connection with filing fees, service of process fees, and electronic filing or service fees, even though incurred to file and serve motions on which Plaintiff did not prevail, were not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and therefore denies Defendant’s motion to tax $1,041.65 in filing fees, $112.76 in service of process fees, and $528.96 in electronic filing or service fees.  (Lowry, supra, 56 Cal.App.5th at p. 222; MC-010, ¶¶ 1, 5, 14; Bohm Decl., ¶ 4 [asserting that these costs are associated with unsuccessful motions and ex parte applications].)

            The court finds that Defendant has met her burden to show that the $61.55 in court reservation fees to reserve a hearing for a motion for a preliminary injunction that Plaintiff did not file was not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation and therefore grants Defendant’s motion to tax those costs.  (Bohm Decl., ¶ 4; Opp., p. 2:12-13 [withdrawing that request for costs].)

            The court grants Defendant’s motion to tax the request for $101.54 in costs to serve supplemental discovery because Plaintiff appears to concede that it was not reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation.  (Opp., p. 2:13-14 [withdrawing that request for costs].)

            The court denies as moot Defendant’s motion to tax the request for attorney’s fees because the court has concluded that Plaintiff may be awarded attorney’s fees pursuant to Corporations Code section 15910.05 for the reasons set forth above.  (MC-010, ¶ 10 [requesting attorney’s fees on noticed motion].)  

            The court finds that Defendant has met her burden to show that the $11,346.50 in costs for court-ordered transcripts is not allowable as costs because (1) Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 allows for costs incurred for transcripts of court proceedings as ordered by the court, and (2) Defendant has shown that the court did not order transcripts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subds. (a)(9) [allowing costs for transcripts of court proceedings ordered by the court], (b)(5) [disallowing as costs transcripts of court proceedings not ordered by the court]; Bohm Decl., ¶ 3 [“It is my recollection that the court did not order any transcripts”].)  

In her opposition, Plaintiff “concedes that transcripts from the first phase one trial . . . were not ordered by the court and withdraws” her request for those costs in the amount of $472.10.  (Opp., p. 1:23-25.)  The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion to tax costs in the amount of $472.10.

However, Plaintiff contends that the court ordered the preparation of transcripts at the end of the second phase of trial.  The court agrees and therefore finds that Plaintiff has met her burden to show that she may recover the remaining $10,892.50 in those costs.  (Weisskopf Decl., Ex. A, p. 86:6-10 [“Pursuant to stipulation, the court orders that Plaintiff shall lodge certified copies of the final reporter’s transcript of the trial with the court in Department 53 no later than October 24, 2022”]; Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(9) [allowing costs for transcripts of court proceedings ordered by the court]; Lowry, supra, 56 Cal.App.5th at p. 222.)

The court orders that plaintiff Bonnie Duboff, on behalf of herself and all other limited partners of 245 Spalding Partners, L.P., shall recover a total of $775,162.50 in attorney’s fees from the recovery obtained by 245 Spalding Partners, L.P. in this action.  (Corp. Code, § 15910.05, subd. (b).)

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Lorraine Ann Escobar (“Defendant”) to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set Three, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $310.66.

The court finds that the discovery motion cutoff date has passed and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)

Except as otherwise provided, “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)  “Thus, if a party properly notices a discovery motion to be heard on or before the discovery motion cutoff date, that party has a right to have the motion heard.  By negative implication, a party who notices a discovery motion to be heard after the discovery motion cutoff date does not have a right to have the motion heard.”  (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1586 [emphasis in original].) 

On March 6, 2023, the court set this case for trial on May 15, 2024.  (March 6, 2023 Minute Order, p. 1.)  On May 2, 2024, the court (1) continued trial, pursuant to the request of Plaintiff, from May 15, 2024 to July 10, 2024, and (2) ordered that “[a]ll discovery cut-off and discovery motion cut-off dates, and deadlines for the exchange of information concerning expert trial witnesses, shall be based on the date initially set for trial of this action (5/15/24) and shall not be reopened or based on the new trial date, except that Defendants may take the deposition of Plaintiff Emilio Reyes pursuant [to] the Court’s 5/1/24 order.”  (May 2, 2024 Minute Order, pp. 1-2.)  On June 27, 2024, the court continued trial from July 10, 2024 to July 17, 2024 on the court’s own motion.  (June 27, 2024 Minute Order, p. 2.)  The court did not reopen discovery or extend the discovery and discovery motion cutoff dates in that order.  (Ibid.)  Thereafter, on July 15, 2024, the court issued a minute order (1) continuing trial from July 17, 2024 to October 16, 2024, and (2) ordering that “the discovery cut-off and discovery motion cut-off dates are only extended with respect to the deposition of Plaintiff, Emilio Reyes.”  (July 15, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)  On October 9, 2024, the court issued an order granting defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc. and Andrew Salas’s motion for order continuing trial and final status conference, and ordered that (1) trial in this action is continued from October 16, 2024 to January 29, 2025, and (2) the discovery cutoff and discovery motion cutoff dates (i) were not extended by the continuance and (ii) shall be based on the date initially set for trial of this action, i.e., May 15, 2024.  (Oct. 9, 2024 Order, p. 2:6-13.)

            Thus, the discovery motion cutoff date passed as of April 30, 2024, i.e., 15 days before May 15, 2024.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a).)  Because Plaintiff noticed this motion for hearing after the discovery motion cutoff date had passed, and because Plaintiff did not file a motion requesting that the court reopen discovery pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050, the court finds that Plaintiff does not have a right to have this motion heard.  (Ibid.; Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc., supra, 165 Cal.App.4th at p. 1586.)  

The court denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Opp., p. 3:3-16.)

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Emilio Reyes’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents.

            The court orders defendant Lorraine Ann Escobar to give notice of this ruling.
