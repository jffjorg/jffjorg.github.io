DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24NNCV00253    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: P

 [TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF'SMOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES - SET ONE and GRANTING MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a collection action for recovery of unpaid nursing home bills. Plaintiff, AG Arcadia, LLC, a California Limited Liability Company doing business as Huntington Drive Health and Rehabilitation Center (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendant Rebecca Schasa, also known as Rebecca Rodriguez, individually and as trustee of the Abraham Rodriguez and Stella Rodriguez Trusts (“Defendant”), seeking damages for the unpaid balance of nursing home services provided to Defendant’s mother, Stella Rodriguez.

On March 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the original complaint, and on November 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC).

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories - Set One. On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition, noting that Defendant failed to oppose the motion.

 

Meet and confer

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating, “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)

Here, On October 28, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed a meet-and-confer letter to Defendant’s counsel addressing the deficiencies in Defendant’s responses. Plaintiff’s counsel requested that Defendant provide verified, code-compliant responses by November 7, 2024, or Plaintiff would proceed with this motion. Despite this effort, Defendant’s counsel failed to respond or provide any further responses (Wilson Decl., ¶ 7).

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A motion to compel a further response to interrogatories or production of documents must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c); see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685).) Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further responses. (Id.) The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  (Sexton, supra,58 Cal. App. 4th at 1410.)   

            An unverified response is tantamount to no response at all. (See Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)  However, objections to interrogatories and requests for production are not required to be verified because “objections are legal conclusions interposed by counsel, not factual assertions by a party.” (Blue Ridge Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 339, 345.)

III.      ANALYSIS

            Plaintiff moves the court to compel Defendant to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories to Rebecca Schasa - Set One and for an award of monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,018.50. 

Plaintiff propounded the Special Interrogatories on August 20, 2024. After granting Defendant multiple extensions, the responses were due by October 22, 2024. However, Defendant’s responses were incomplete, unverified, and contained meritless objections. Additionally, Defendant failed to provide any substantive responses to Special Interrogatory Nos. 9 through 25. (Motion, p. 3; Wilson Decl.¶ 6)

Specifically, Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to respond entirely to Special Interrogatory Nos. 9 through 25. Defendant’s failure to respond constitutes a violation of California Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.250(a), which mandates that responses be verified under oath. Unverified responses are tantamount to no response at all (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636). Moreover, by failing to timely provide substantive responses, Defendant waived all objections to these interrogatories, including those asserted in her deficient responses (CCP § 2030.290(a); see also Leach v. Sup. Ct. (Markum) (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906) (Separate Statement, pp. 6–8)

The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling verified, code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories to Rebecca Schasa - Set One. Given that this motion is unopposed, Defendant’s failure to provide substantive answers to these interrogatories, coupled with her failure to verify the responses, establishes grounds for granting Plaintiff’s motion. 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further response to Special Interrogatories to Rebecca Schasa - Set One.

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030 grants the court discretion to impose sanctions against any party engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. Misuse includes the failure to respond to authorized discovery methods or disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., §2023.010, subds. (d), (g).) Further, CCP §2030.300(d) mandates that the court impose monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories, unless that party acted with substantial justification or if imposing sanctions would be unjust.

Here, the motion is unopposed, and Defendant has provided no substantial justification for her failure to respond to Plaintiff’s discovery requests or to verify her responses. Defendant’s conduct constitutes misuse of the discovery process under CCP sections 2023.010(d) and 2023.030(a).

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,018.50 for attorney’s fees and costs incurred in bringing this motion. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that Plaintiff has incurred 2.7 hours of attorney time at $355.00 per hour and a filing fee of $60.00, for a total of $1,018.50 (Wilson Decl., ¶ 9). Additionally, Plaintiff anticipates spending another 2.5 hours to review any opposition, prepare a reply, and attend the hearing, and therefore requests an additional $887.50 in sanctions if the motion is opposed, for a total of $1,906.00 (Wilson Decl., ¶ 9).

The Court finds that Defendant’s failure to provide verified, substantive responses and her failure to oppose this motion justify the imposition of sanctions. Accordingly, the Court reduces the requested sanctions and finds that $355 per hour is a reasonable hourly rate. The Court awards 1.5 hours for drafting this motion and an additional 1 hour for attending the hearing, along with the $60 filing fee, for a total of $887.50 in sanctions.

Since this motion is unopposed, the Court declines to grant Plaintiff’s additional request for sanctions. Defendant Rebecca Schasa is ordered to pay $887.50 to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of this order.

            IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories to Rebecca Schasa - Set One. Defendant is to provide verified, code-compliant responses, without objections, within 20 days of this order.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff monetary sanctions in the amount of $887.50 against Defendant. Defendant is ordered to pay the sanctions within 20 days of this order.

            Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 24NNCV01444    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT CAL LAND ENGINEERING INC’S DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT

I.         INTRODUCTION

This case arises from allegations of property damage and interference with property rights stemming from construction activities. Plaintiff Maria Davidson, Trustee of the Maria Lynn Davidson 2022 Trust (“Plaintiff”), owns property adjacent to a construction site where Defendants Ashot Sandoyan, Rodeo Construction and Management, a California Corporation, Cal Land Engineering, Inc., a California Corporation, dba Quartech Consultants (“Cal Land”), and GR Martinez, Inc., a California Corporation, allegedly conducted or facilitated activities that caused harm to Plaintiff’s property.

Plaintiff initiated this action on May 7, 2024. On July 26, 2024, Defendant Cal Land filed a demurrer to the Complaint. Plaintiff filed an opposition on December 2, 2024, and Cal Land submitted a reply on December 6, 2024.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, on July 19, 2024, Cal Land’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet-and-confer letter. On July 25, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded. (Sanders Decl., ¶¶ 3-6) However, as no meeting occurred in person, by telephone, or via video conference, the Court finds that counsel failed to comply with the meet-and-confer requirements under section 430.41.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Pursuant to CCP § 335.1, an action for injury to an individual cause by the neglect of another must be filed within two years. 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

Cal Land demurs to the second, third, and fourth causes of action—Private Nuisance, Public Nuisance, and Trespass—arguing that Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts to state a valid cause of action against Cal Land.

 

Second cause of action: Private Nuisance

To establish an action for private nuisance, (1) “the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of his property”; (2) “the invasion of the plaintiff’s interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, that is, that it causes the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage”; (3) “the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, but it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.”  (Mendez v. Rancho Valencia Resort Partners, LLC (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 248, 262-263)

Cal Land demurs to this cause of action, asserting that Plaintiff fails to allege specific facts demonstrating how Cal Land’s conduct caused substantial and unreasonable interference with her property (Demurrer, p. 3). Cal Land further contends that its geotechnical report and related activities were merely advisory and did not involve control or oversight of the construction methods allegedly responsible for the harm (Demurrer, pp. 7-8). Moreover, Cal Land argues that the alleged interference, including vibrations and noise, is inherently associated with permitted construction activities and, therefore, cannot constitute a nuisance (Demurrer, p. 8).

In opposition, Plaintiff maintains that they sufficiently allege a cause of action for private nuisance. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant’s negligent preparation of the geotechnical report failed to account for known soil conditions, directly leading to property damage (Opposition, pp. 3) Plaintiff further argues that Cal Land report enabled improper construction methods by other parties, causing substantial and unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of Plaintiff’s property (Opposition pp.5-6)

In reply, Cal Land argues the lack of proximate causation, asserting that Plaintiff has not shown a direct connection between the report and the alleged interference. (Reply, pp. 2-3) Cal Land distinguished its role as an advisory party, not a direct participant in the construction activities responsible for the alleged harm (Reply pp.3-4) 

The Court has reviewed the Complaint and finds that Plaintiff alleges interference due to constant noise, vibrations, and other noxious conditions (Complaint, ¶ 33). Plaintiff also claims an inability to enjoy her backyard and worries about structural damage to her property (Complaint, ¶¶ 40(a), 40(c)). While these allegations describe interference with Plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of her property, the connection between Cal Land’s conduct and the alleged interference is too attenuated to sustain a claim for private nuisance.

For substantial actual damage, Plaintiff alleges physical damage, including structural and cosmetic cracking (Complaint, ¶¶ 35(A), 40(b)), as well as emotional and mental distress caused by the interference (Complaint, ¶ 39). However, Plaintiff fails to sufficiently allege how Cal Land’s geotechnical report proximately caused these damages.

For unreasonableness, Plaintiff claims Defendants failed to implement reasonable construction measures, such as monitoring activities or accounting for known unstable soil conditions (Complaint, ¶¶ 35(B)-(D)), and alleges that Defendants' conduct exceeded the scope of their construction permits (Complaint, ¶ 36). While Plaintiff asserts that Cal Land’s report inadequately addressed known soil conditions, this claim lacks the specificity needed to establish that Cal Land’s actions were a substantial factor in the alleged interference. At the pleading stage, the Court assumes the truth of all properly pled factual allegations; however, Plaintiff fails to sufficiently allege how Cal Land’s geotechnical report proximately caused these damages.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS this cause of action with leave to amend.

Third cause of action: Public Nuisance

Public nuisances are “substantial and unreasonable” “offenses against, or interferences with, the exercise of rights common to the public.”  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 305.)

Cal Land demurs to this cause of action, arguing that the Complaint fails to allege how their conduct had any involvement or control to constitute public nuisance (Demurrer, p. 6; Reply p.4). In opposition, Plaintiff contends that Cal Land’s geotechnical report facilitated construction activities that created widespread conditions, including noise and dust pollution, which affected the surrounding community (Opposition, p. 7). In reply, Cal Land emphasizes the absence of allegations demonstrating how their specific actions resulted in interference with public rights (Reply, p. 5).

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations primarily address private property damage and fail to establish how Cal Land’s conduct interfered with a right common to the public. Although Plaintiff alleges that “this condition affected a substantial number of people, including Plaintiff and the properties located at 837 and 843 Monte Verde Drive, Arcadia, California 91007” (Complaint, ¶ 49), this statement lacks sufficient detail to demonstrate a substantial and unreasonable interference with public rights. Therefore, Plaintiff fails to sufficiently plead a cause of action for public nuisance.

Given this, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to this cause of action with leave to amend. 

Fourth cause of action: Trespass

To set forth a cause of action for trespass, a plaintiff must allege (1) the plaintiff’s lawful possession or right to possession of real property; (2) the defendant’s wrongful, intentional, reckless, or negligent act of trespass on the property; (3) the plaintiff did not give permission for the entry or scope of permission was exceeded; and (4) damage to the plaintiff caused by the trespass.  (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262.)  

            Cal Land demurrers to this cause of action arguing that Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a trespass. Specifically, Cal Land argues that Plaintiff fails to allege that Cal Land engaged in any physical entry onto Plaintiff’s property (Demurrer, p. 7). The Complaint does not establish how Cal Land’s actions—limited to preparing a geotechnical report—constituted an unauthorized or wrongful intrusion (Demurrer, p. 9). And Vibrations and debris resulting from construction activities performed by others cannot support a trespass claim against Cal Land (Demurrer, p. 10)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues vibrations and debris caused by construction activities facilitated by Cal Land’s geotechnical report constitute a trespass (Opposition, p. 9). And Plaintiff asserts that Cal Land knew or should have known its report would lead to harmful activities, resulting in an invasion of Plaintiff’s property (Opposition, pp. 9-10)

In reply, Cal Land argues that trespass requires a direct and physical intrusion, which Plaintiff fails to allege against Cal Land (Reply, p. 5). Further, Cal Land emphasizes its role was advisory and did not involve participation in or control over construction activities alleged to have caused the harm (Reply, pp. 5-6).

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not sufficiently pleaded a cause of action for trespass against Cal Land. Plaintiff fails to allege a direct or tangible intrusion onto the property by Cal Land, as required to sustain this claim. Given Cal Land’s role in providing geotechnical reports, as Plaintiff alleges, the Court sustains this claim without leave to amend, as Cal Land was not directly involved in the construction activities that allegedly caused the harm.

The Court SUSTAINS this cause of action without leave to amend. 

IV.       ORDER

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the second and third causes of action, with 20 days leave to amend.

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to the fourth cause of action, without leave to amend.

Cal Land to give notice.
