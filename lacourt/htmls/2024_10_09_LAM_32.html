DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV26639    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 32

 

MARYAM KHANI,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

BOBBY BABAK SAADIAN, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV26639

  Hearing Date:  October 9, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ motion for summary judgment

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed this legal malpractice action against Defendants Bobby Babak Saadian and Wilshire Law Firm, PLC. Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint on December 6, 2022, asserting causes of action for (1) legal malpractice, (2) breach of fiduciary duty, and (3) fraud. The third cause of action has been stricken by demurrer.

            Plaintiff was injured in an auto accident in September 2020 and hired the Defendants to represent her in the underlying action. (Def.’s Stmt. of Undisputed Facts (UF) 1.) The underlying action settled in June 2021 for $250,000, the insurance policy limit. (UF 3.) Afterwards, Plaintiff complained to Defendants that the amount was insufficient to cover her injuries and inquired about the other driver contributing to the settlement. (UF 4.) Defendants explained to Plaintiff that the settlement had been finalized and that there would be no contribution from the other driver. (UF 5-7.) Defendants reminded Plaintiff that she had been informed of this prior to signing the settlement. (Ibid.) Ultimately, Plaintiff signed an “Acknowledgment of Understanding Settlement Terms,” acknowledging that she had been fully informed of the settlement terms and was fully agreeing to every term. (UF 10.) The acknowledgment also released Defendants from any further obligations related to the underlying claim. (Ibid.)   

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ negligent representation and advice caused her to: obtain an insufficient amount from the underlying action; be exposed to unnecessary medical bills; bear responsibility for liens in favor of certain health care providers and incur attorney’s fees to resolve such obligations; and suffer emotional distress. (FAC ¶ 13.)  

            On May 28, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion for summary judgment. Plaintiff filed her opposition on September 25, 2024. Defendants filed their reply on October 2, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.” (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff’s Objections

  • Plaintiff did not file any objections. Arguments in the opposition brief or separate statement do not constitute proper objections. “All written objections to evidence must be served and filed separately from the other papers in support of or in opposition to the motion.” (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1354(b).) “[O]bjections must not be restated or reargued in the separate statement.” (Ibid.)

Defendants’ Objections

  • Defendants’ objections are also improperly stated. Each objection must “[q]uote or set forth the objectionable statement or material.” (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1354(b)(3).) Defendants merely identify the paragraph number without setting forth the objectionable material.  

DISCUSSION

I. Statute of Limitations

“An action against an attorney for a wrongful act or omission, other than for actual fraud, arising in the performance of professional services shall be commenced within one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission, or four years from the date of the wrongful act or omission, whichever occurs first.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.6(a).)

“Actual injury occurs when the client suffers any loss or injury legally cognizable as damages in a legal malpractice action based on the asserted errors or omissions.” (Jordache Enterprises, Inc. v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 18 Cal.4th 739, 743.) “Negligent legal advice which induces a client to enter into a binding contract resolving [a dispute] results in actual injury at the point of entry.” (Hensley v. Caietti (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 1165, 1175.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff knew or should have known of her injury at the time the settlement agreement was executed, in June 2021. (UF 3.) Defendants contend that Plaintiff had knowledge of her injury in June 2021 because she complained to Defendants about the inadequacy of the settlement amount and their failure to pursue the other driver’s assets. (UF 5-8.) Plaintiff filed this action in August 2022, more than one year after June 2021. This satisfies Defendants’ initial burden of showing that the action is time-barred as a matter of law.

Plaintiff argues that the statute of limitations does not apply due to tolling and delayed discovery.

a. Tolling

The statute of limitations is tolled while “[t]he attorney continues to represent the plaintiff regarding the specific subject matter in which the alleged wrongful act or omission occurred.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.6(a)(2).) Tolling ends “[a]fter a client has no reasonable expectation that the attorney will provide further legal services.” (Flake v. Neumiller & Beardslee (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 223, 231.)

In June 2021, Plaintiff signed the aforementioned distribution and acknowledgment releasing Defendants from any further obligations related to the underlying case. (UF 10.) At that point, Plaintiff had “no reasonable expectation that the attorney will provide further legal services.” (See Flake, supra, 9 Cal.App.5th at p. 231.) At the latest, Defendants aver that they stopped all work on the underlying matter upon filing for dismissal in July 2021. (UF 12.)

Plaintiff does not dispute signing the distribution and acknowledgment but avers that she was told the case was not finalized and that Defendants would continue to handle medical bills and liens. (Khani Decl. ¶ 19.) Plaintiff avers that she was told the process would take several months before she would receive the final distribution and accounting. (Ibid.) Thus, Plaintiff understood that Defendants continued to represent her while they maintained her settlement funds in trust and negotiated her medical liens. (Ibid.)   

Plaintiff’s evidence is insufficient to raise an inference that the statute was tolled. Plaintiff merely avers that she was “informed” by some unspecified person that the case was not finalized. Plaintiff has no evidence that Defendants in fact continued working on her case past June or July 2021. Plaintiff also does not dispute receiving an email informing her that “all reductions have been negotiated, re-negotiated, and finalized.” (UF 5.) Plaintiff does not dispute receiving another email informing her that the medical bills could not be reduced any further and asking her to sign the distribution agreement “so we can send you the check.” (UF 9.) Plaintiff’s vague reference to being told that Defendants would continue to negotiate the medical bills on her behalf does not constitute “substantial responsive evidence” sufficient to raise a factual dispute. (See Sangster, supra, 68 Cal.App.4th at p. 163.)  

Even assuming the statute was tolled until July 2021, Plaintiff still filed this action more than one year later. Thus, tolling does not save the claim from the statute of limitations.

b. Delayed Discovery

“[T]he one-year limitations period of section 340.6 is triggered by the client's discovery of the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission, not by his discovery that such facts constitute professional negligence.” (Peregrine Funding, Inc. v. Sheppard Mullin Richter & Hampton LLP (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 658, 685, internal citations omitted.) “The test is whether the plaintiff has information of circumstances sufficient to put a reasonable person on inquiry, or has the opportunity to obtain knowledge from sources open to his or her investigation.” (Ibid.) “A plaintiff need not be aware of the specific ‘facts’ necessary to establish the claim; that is a process contemplated by pretrial discovery. Once the plaintiff has a suspicion of wrongdoing, and therefore an incentive to sue,” the statute begins running. (Genisman v. Carley (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 45, 51.)

Plaintiff argues that delayed discovery applies because she did not receive her medical records until June 2022, which revealed that her medical providers had estimated her cost of care at $1.2 million. (Khani Decl. ¶ 22.) However, the medical records and precise amount are “ancillary to the allegations of misconduct.” (See Peregrine, supra, 133 Cal.App.4th at p. 685.) They are “merely evidence” of Defendants’ purported wrongdoing. (Ibid.) Plaintiff already believed as early as June 2021 that the settlement amount was insufficient and that Defendants had failed to pursue a higher amount. (UF 4-7.) It is immaterial that Plaintiff did not know the full extent of the deficiency until she obtained her medical records. The discovery rule does not require a plaintiff’s knowledge of all facts supporting a claim. (Genisman, supra, 29 Cal.App.5th at p. 51.)  

By clearly expressing concern over the $250,000 settlement amount and accusing Defendants of failing to pursue the other driver’s assets, Plaintiff demonstrated her knowledge that Defendants “had done something wrong.” (See Peregrine, supra, 133 Cal.App.4th at p. 685.) In other words, Plaintiff was on inquiry notice. (Ibid.) Thus, the statute of limitations began running in June 2021, making the complaint untimely.   

In sum, Defendants have demonstrated that the complaint is barred by the one-year statute of limitations, and Plaintiff has failed to raise a factual dispute as to tolling or delayed discovery. Therefore, the claims fail as a matter of law regardless of the substantive merits. 

 

II. Causation and Damages

“In a litigation malpractice action, the plaintiff must establish that but for the alleged negligence of the defendant attorney, the plaintiff would have obtained a more favorable judgment or settlement in the action in which the malpractice allegedly occurred.” (Viner v. Sweet (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1232, 1241.) “[T]he crucial causation inquiry is what would have happened if the defendant attorney had not been negligent.” (Id. at p. 1242.) “The plaintiff in a malpractice action must establish that the underlying judgment lost as the result of the attorney's error could have been collected.” (Wise v. DLA Piper LLP (US) (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1180, 1190.) “The element of collectibility requires a showing of the debtor's solvency.” (Ibid.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot prove she would have received a judgment or settlement greater than the policy limit of $250,000 because in her deposition, Plaintiff was unable to identify facts showing that the other driver possessed assets beyond $250,000, or refused to disclose such facts under the attorney-client privilege. (UF 15; see also Xebec Development Partners, Ltd. v. National Union Fire Ins. Co. (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 501, 569 [“A party who asserts a privilege as to evidence essential to some element of his or her case will usually be obliged as a practical matter to forsake that element”].) This satisfies Defendants’ initial burden of showing that Plaintiff cannot meet the causation element of her claim.  

In opposition, Plaintiff’s counsel avers that “[a]n investigation and asset search was conducted through the resources available to my office including, interviews and records searches and we were able to determine that the underlying defendant owned real property and had assets that could have be utilized to satisfy a judgement above the $250,000 policy limits.” (Seuthe Decl. ¶ 8.)  

However, Plaintiff presents no evidence of the other driver’s assets, nor evidence that the assets could have been applied to the underlying judgment (e.g., not exempt). Counsel’s bare declaration does not constitute “substantial responsive evidence” sufficient to raise a triable issue. (See Sangster, supra, 68 Cal.App.4th at p. 163.) Moreover, Plaintiff “cannot have it both ways.” (See Xebec, supra, 12 Cal.App.4th at p. 569.) She cannot assert the attorney-client privilege in discovery to prevent Defendants from obtaining critical evidence, and then offer that evidence in support of her claims. (Ibid.)     

Ultimately, it remains undisputed that the policy limit was $250,000 and that Plaintiff identified no facts demonstrating the other driver’s ability to pay beyond that amount. (See Lona v. Citibank, N.A. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 89, 110 [defendant may obtain summary judgment based on plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses, deposition testimony, or admissions showing that plaintiff has not discovered anything supporting the essential elements of the claim].)

Without the essential elements of causation and damages, Plaintiff’s claims fail as a matter of law. This independently warrants summary judgment of the claims.

CONCLUSION

            Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motion to compel depositions  

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Cornelia Rosales, Jose Juventino Rosales, and Jose Alfredo Rosales filed this landlord-tenant action against Defendant Vermont City Lights LP alleging uninhabitable conditions.

            On August 7, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiffs’ depositions, ordering Plaintiffs to appear for deposition within 40 days. The notice of ruling also states that the Court ordered the depositions to take place in person, as specified in the notices of deposition.

            On September 6, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiffs’ depositions for failure to comply with the Court’s order. Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition.   

LEGAL STANDARD

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination . . . or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(a).)

DISCUSSION

The extensive history of Defendant’s attempts to ascertain Plaintiffs’ availability leading up to the first motion to compel has been summarized in the Court’s initial order. The Court will not repeat that history herein, but it should be noted that Defendant attempted numerous times to ascertain Plaintiffs’ availability, and Plaintiff ignored most of the communications and then proposed an unreasonable date/time for the depositions.

After the Court granted Defendant’s motion, Defendant once again began inquiring as to Plaintiffs’ availability. (McPhillips Decl., Ex. D.) Plaintiffs initially offered Wednesdays at noon via Zoom. (Id., Ex. E.) Defendant responded that the Court ordered the depositions to be in person and asked for dates for in-person depositions. (Id., Ex. F.) Plaintiff then proposed October 29, 2024 at 11am. (Id., Ex. G.) Defendant responded that a single half-day was insufficient to depose all three Plaintiffs and noted that the proposed date was past the discovery cutoff. (Id., Ex. H.) Defendant also asked for verification that the proposed date was for an in-person deposition. (Ibid.) Plaintiffs then offered September 16, 2024 at 11am for an in-person deposition. (Id., Ex. I.) Defendant responded, again taking issue with the offer of a single half-day to depose all three Plaintiffs. (Id., Ex. J.)

This demonstrates that Plaintiffs have not been forthcoming with their availability and are essentially evading their depositions. Offering a single date with insufficient time to depose all three Plaintiffs does not constitute good faith cooperation in the discovery process. Plaintiffs chose to file this lawsuit and cannot avoid the associated discovery obligations. Defendant is entitled to depose each Plaintiff for up to seven hours, and may take that deposition in person at a location “either within 75 miles of the deponent’s residence, or within the county where the action is pending and within 150 miles of the deponent’s residence.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.250(a), 2025.290(a).)

Sanctions are warranted as Plaintiffs have “[f]ail[ed] to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010(d).) Defendant requests the imposition of $1,340 against Plaintiffs’ counsel. (Mtn. 2:11-13; McPhillips Decl. ¶ 13.) The Court finds this reasonable.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motion to compel depositions is GRANTED. Plaintiffs are to appear for their depositions in person at defense counsel’s office on __________________ at 9:30 a.m. The Court sanctions Plaintiffs’ counsel in the amount of $1,340, to be paid within 30 days of this order.
