DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01458    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY DAVIS, et al. v. PAIGE HEMMIS, et al.

 

PLAINTIFFS KIMBERLY DAVIS AND JONATHAN DAVIS’ MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE OF BUSINESS RECORDS DEPOSITION SUBPOENA PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE § 2020.410 AND 2025.480

 

Date of Hearing:        May 15, 2024                         Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV01458

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis 

Responding Party:     No opposition.

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Marko Decl. ¶3.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a third amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125). On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution. Cross-Complainants dismissed their cause of action for express indemnification.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis’ Motion to Compel Compliance of Business Records Deposition Subpoena Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2020.410 and 2025.480 is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of several court documents submitted in support of Plaintiffs’ motion to compel compliance of business records deposition subpoena (RJN Exhs. 1-7.) Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis move the court for an order compelling AT&T Corporation to provide requested documents. Plaintiffs make the motion on the grounds AT&T has failed to provide the requested documents, which seek basic information that is highly material to the adjudication of this matter. Plaintiffs also request that this Court award Plaintiffs an award of $10,915 against AT&T as sanctions for having to file this motion.

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records.¿ (CCP § 2020.010.)¿ A deposition subpoena may command: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent, (2) only the production of business records for copying, or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things.¿ (CCP § 2020.020.)¿  

 

A service of a deposition subpoena shall be affected a sufficient time in advance of the deposition to provide the deponent a reasonable opportunity to locate and produce any designated documents and, where personal attendance is commanded, a reasonable time to travel to the place of deposition.¿ (CCP § 2020.220(a).)¿ Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies.¿ (CCP § 2020.220(c).) A deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena may be punished for contempt without the necessity of a prior order of the court directing compliance by the witness.¿ (CCP § 2020.240.)¿A motion to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena must be made within 60 days after completion of the deposition record, the date objections are served, or the date specified for production. (CCP §2025.480(b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.) 

 

The court finds good cause for production of the requested documents. As contend by Plaintiffs, the identity of the person(s) who have used the phone numbers requested is highly relevant to this case. Similarly, the IP addresses requested are highly relevant as Plaintiffs contend they were used to impersonate Plaintiff and sign the lease on Plaintiff’s behalf. The court has previously determined the requested time periods are relevant.

 

The motion is unopposed. The court notes AT&T has waived objections pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.410(b).

 

Sanctions

 

In their moving papers, Plaintiffs request $10,915 in monetary sanctions to be awarded against AT&T Corporation for AT&T Corporation’s failure to provide code compliant responses.

 

The amount sought in sanctions in unreasonable, given that this is an unopposed motion about a single subpoena.  Further, the court does not believe that parties should recover for time spent in the requisite meet and confer process; the meet and confer process is required by law.  The reasonable amount of attorney fees for the filing of this motion is $2500.  That amount is ordered payable by AT&T within 30 days.

II.                  MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES AND REQUESTS FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST U.S. BANK AND NATIONSTAR AND ANDREA HICKS

 

Plaintiffs move this court for an order compelling Defendants U.S. Bank National Association and Nationstar to provide further responses to certain requests contained in Plaintiffs’ First Set of Special Interrogatories and First Set of Requests for Production. Plaintiffs make the motion on the grounds each request is necessary to discern the basis, if any, for the factual contentions set forth in Defendant’s pleadings, Defendant’s claims, Defendant's defenses thereto, and the responses they did serve do not satisfy Defendant’s discovery obligations. Plaintiff also requests that this Court award Plaintiff an award of $11,240.00 against Defendants U.S. Bank and Nationstar and its counsel, Andrea Hicks, as sanctions for having to file this motion.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1).)

 

A motion to compel further responses to form or specially prepared interrogatories may be brought if the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2) an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2030.300(a).)

As for Special Interrogatory Nos. 15, 16, and 17, Defendants state they have produced a June 14, 2019 payoff statement. Thus, no further response is necessary. As to Special Interrogatory No. 25, as set forth in Defendants’ response, Defendants do not have any identifying information for any individual that visited the property after June 2019.

 

As for Plaintiffs’ RFPs, Defendants argue RFP Nos. 12, 13, 14, 15, 16, 22, and 25 are complete. ELLEN A. SENTIMORE v. SHREE RAM KRISHNA HARI, LLC., et al.

 

Motion TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF plaintiff’s COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        May 15, 2024                                                 Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        23VECV04358

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Ellen A. Sentimore

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case involves a claim of bed bug infestation in a hotel. On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff Ellen A. Sentimore filed a first amended complaint against B&M Capital Group, LLC and Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC for (1) Negligence - Premises Liability/Failure to Warn/ Negligence Per Se; (2) Nuisance; (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (4) Breach of Contract; and (5) Fraudulent Concealment. Plaintiff seeks damages from Defendants for their alleged wanton failure to properly maintain, control or repair Plaintiff’s rented hotel room number 501.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is DENIED.  

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of the Tentative Ruling regarding the Motion to Strike Hearing, which became the court’s ruling on January 29th, 2024, attached herein as Exhibit A. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Shree Ram Krishna Hari, LLC moves this court for an order striking portions of Plaintiff’s first amended complaint on the grounds Plaintiff’s claims for punitive damages are not based on ultimate facts, are conclusory, and the first amended complaint fails to plead specific facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages.

 

 The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.)

 

To sufficiently plead a claim for punitive damages pursuant to Civil Code §3294, a plaintiff must satisfy circumstances of “malice, oppression, or fraud,” supported by facts alleged with sufficient particularity. (G.D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29.) These allegations are held to a heightened pleading standard: a plaintiff may not state a mere conclusion of law to support a cause of action. (Perkins v. Sup. Ct. (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6.) More importantly, the plaintiff may not simply "plead . . . a claim for damages in the language authorizing such damages." (Id.) While some conclusory statements may be permitted, they must make sense in the context of the Complaint taken as a whole. (Id.)

 

The court previously granted Defendant’s motion to strike on the grounds “Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged Zagar was an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation or that an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. The complaint generally alleges he was a manager, employee, or agent.” (Minute Order, January 29, 2024.) The court noted, however, Plaintiff did sufficiently allege more than mere negligence and that the conduct alleged “could certainly be found to be so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” (Minute Order, January 29 2024.)

 

Upon review of the first amended complaint, the court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged Defendants’ managing agents authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct constituting malice, oppression and fraud by a corporate defendant. Plaintiff alleges the corporate managing agents, including Benik Navi, Mitesh Kalthia and Ashu Kalthia, ratified Zagar’s conduct. Plaintiff alleges Zagar was either Defendants’ managing agent or authorized to act on behalf of Defendants’ managing members regarding the Subject Property. This is sufficient for the purposes of a motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike the first amended complaint is DENIED.
