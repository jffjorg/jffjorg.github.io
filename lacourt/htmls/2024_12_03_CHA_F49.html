Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 23CHCV00262    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: F49

Dept. F49

Date: 12/3/24

Case Name: Taylor Peacock and Austin Smith v. Ford Motor Company, AutoNation Ford Valencia, and Does 1 to 10

Case No. 23CHCV00262

 

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

 

DECEMBER 3, 2024

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 23CHCV00262

 

Motion filed: 8/29/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Ford Motor Company

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs Taylor Peacock and Austin Smith

NOTICE: OK.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Summary Adjudication on the First, Fourth, and Seventh Causes of Action in Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs Taylor Peacock (“Peacock”) and Austin Smith (“Smith”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) brought this action under Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Lemon Law), Consumer Legal Remedies Act, and Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act over alleged defects in their 2020 Ford Expedition (the “Subject Vehicle”), which was manufactured by Defendant Ford Motor Company (“FMC”). Plaintiffs allege that they entered into a warranty contract with Defendant FMC on or about December 19, 2020.

 

On January 31, 2023, Plaintiffs initiated this action. Subsequently, on May 26, 2023, the Department F47 Court sustained a demurrer filed by Defendant FMC on April 10, 2023.

 

On June 23, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants FMC and AutoNation Ford Valencia (“AutoNation Ford”), and Does 1 through 10, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (2) Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (3) Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2, (4) Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civ. Code §§ 1791.1, 1794, 1795.5), (5) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment, (6) Negligent Repair, (7) Violation of Consumer Legal Remedies Act (Civ. Code § 1750 et seq.), and (8) Violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act. The First through Fifth, and Seventh through Eighth Causes of Action are alleged against Defendant FMC. The Sixth Cause of Action is alleged against AutoNation Ford. Subsequently, on December 5, 2023, the Department F47 Court sustained FMC’s demurrer to the Third and Fifth Causes of Action without leave to amend.

 

On August 29, 2024, Defendant FMC filed the instant Motion for Summary Adjudication on the First, Fourth, and Seventh Causes of Action in the FAC. Subsequently, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition on November 19, 2024.

 

            No Reply papers have been received by the Court.  

 

ANALYSIS

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).)

 

“The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

“[I]f the moving papers establish a prima facie showing that justifies a [ruling] in the [moving party’s] favor, the burden then shifts to the [opposing party] to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable material factual issue.’ [Citation.]" (See's Candy Shops, Inc. v. Superior Court (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 889, 900, quoting Rehmani v. Superior Court (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 945, 950.) “The defendant or cross-defendant shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) 

 

A.    Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court OVERRULES Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections.

 

B.     First Cause of Action: Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2

 

Civil Code section 1793.2 provides special consumer remedies to purchasers of new vehicles covered by express warranties. The remedy at issue here, commonly referred to as the “refund-or-replace” provision, requires a manufacturer to replace a defective “new motor vehicle” or make restitution if, after a reasonable number of attempts, the manufacturer is unable to repair the vehicle to conform to the applicable express warranty. (Civ. Code, § 1793.2, subd. (d)(2).)

 

FMC contends that that it is entitled to summary adjudication on the First Cause of Action on the grounds that the Subject Vehicle does not qualify as a “new motor vehicle” and therefore, remedies provided under Civil Code section 1793.2 are therefore inapplicable. (Mot. at pp. 3-9.)

 

The Court will examine this argument as the sole basis for adjudicating the First Cause of Action.

 

1)      FMC’s Initial Burden  

 

Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (d)(1), provides, in pertinent part, that “[i]f the manufacturer or its representative in this state is unable to service or repair a new motor vehicle ...  to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of attempts, the manufacturer shall either promptly replace the new motor vehicle in accordance with subparagraph (A) or promptly make restitution to the buyer in accordance with subparagraph (B).”

 

For the purpose of this section, “‘New motor vehicle’ means a new motor vehicle that is bought or used primarily for personal, family, or household purposes. ‘New motor vehicle’ also means a new motor vehicle with a gross vehicle weight under 10,000 pounds that is bought or used primarily for business purposes by a person ... or any other legal entity, to which not more than five motor vehicles are registered in this state. ‘New motor vehicle’ includes the chassis, chassis cab, and that portion of a motor home devoted to its propulsion, ... [and] a dealer-owned vehicle and a ‘demonstrator’ or other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer's new car warranty .... A demonstrator is a vehicle assigned by a dealer for the purpose of demonstrating qualities and characteristics common to vehicles of the same or similar model and type.” (Civ. Code § 1793.22, subd. (e)(2).) (Underlines added.)

 

In Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 209, review granted July 13, 2022, S274625 (Rodriguez), the Court of Appeal upheld the trial court’s finding that a two-year-old Dodge truck purchased from a used car dealership, with 55,000 miles on it and a remaining unexpired limited powertrain warranty, did not qualify as a ”new motor vehicle” within the definition of Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (e)(2). (Ibid.) The Rodriguez Court determined that the phrase “other motor vehicles sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty” applies to cars sold with a full warranty, not to previously sold cars accompanied by some balance of the original warranty.” (Rodriguez, supra, at p. 225.)

 

Here, FMC presents evidence to establish that the Subject Vehicle was initially sold to EAN Holdings, Inc. (“Enterprise”) on October 7, 2019. (FMC’s Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. “1,” Geraghty Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. “2.”) On November 24, 2020, the Subject Vehicle was sold by Enterprise to Chino Hills Ford, an independently operated dealership. (FMC’s UMF No. “2,” Geraghty Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. “3.”) Subsequently, on December 19, 2020, Chino Hills Ford in turn sold the Subject Vehicle to Plaintiffs with 36,182 miles on the odometer. (FMC’s UMF No. “3,” Geraghty Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. “4.”) Additionally, FMC asserts that Plaintiffs did not receive new or additional warranty coverage from FMC at the time of their purchase of the Subject Vehicle; instead, they received only the remaining balance of the original warranty issued by FMC in connection with the delivery of the Subject Vehicle to its original purchaser, Enterprise. (FMC’s UMF No. “7,” Geraghty Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. “4.”)

 

The Court finds that FMC’s UMFs and supporting evidence demonstrate that the Subject Vehicle was purchased by Plaintiffs in “USED” condition, without any additional new warranties issued by FMC.

 

The Court acknowledges that, although Rodriguez is pending review by the California Supreme Court, it remains citable for its persuasive value and to establish the existence of a conflict of authority, thereby allowing trial courts to exercise discretion under Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 456.

 

The Court finds the Rodriguez decision to be directly applicable to the present facts. Based on Rodriguez, the Court concludes that the Subject Vehicle does not qualify as a “new motor vehicle” under the Song-Beverly Act. Consequently, the “refund-or-replace” remedy provided under Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (d) is unavailable.

 

Accordingly, the Court determines that FMC has met its initial burden of making a prima facie showing that Plaintiffs cannot establish their First Cause of Action. As a result, the burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to demonstrate a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable material factual issue.

           

2)      Plaintiffs’ Burden of Showing Existence of a Triable Material Factual Issue

     

Plaintiffs argue that they purchased the Subject Vehicle from FMC’s authorized retailer, and they received various warranties, including a 3-year/36,000-mile bumper-to-bumper express warranty, a 5-year/60,000-mile express powertrain warranty, and a 8-year/80,000-mile California emissions warranties directly from FMC. (Pl.’s UMF Nos. “2,” “3,” and “6,” Peacock & Smith Decl. ¶ “6,” Ex. “2.”)

 

However, the Court finds this evidence insufficient to establish the existence of a trial issue of material fact as to whether the Subject Vehicle qualifies as a “new motor vehicle” under the definition provided in Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (e)(2), or in alignment with the ruling in Rodriguez.    

 

Just like the plaintiffs in Rodriguez who purchased their car from a used car dealership with the basic bumper-to-bumper warranty expired but with an unspecified balance remained on the powertrain warranty, Plaintiffs purchased the Subject Vehicle in “USED” condition (Geraghty Decl. Ex. “4”) The basic bumper-to-bumper warranty had expired due to the odometer exceeding 36,000 miles, although an unspecified balance remained on the powertrain warranties and California emissions warranties. (Pl.’s UMF No. “7.”)

 

Additionally, while Plaintiffs purchased the Subject Vehicle from FMC’s authorized retailer, their own evidence – specifically, the Purchase Agreement between Plaintiffs and Chino Hills Ford – indicates that the Subject Vehicle was sold in “USED” condition. (Peacock and Smith Decl. Ex. “1.”) Furthermore, Plaintiffs have not claimed or provided evidence to support that the Subject Vehicle qualified as a dealer-owned “demonstrator” under Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (e)(2).

 

The Court concludes that Plaintiffs’ UMFs and supporting evidence do not distinguish their case from Rodriguez. Consequently, Plaintiffs have not satisfied their burden to demonstrate the existence of a triable material factual issue on the question of whether the Subject Vehicle qualified as a “new motor vehicle,” a prerequisite for availing the “refund-or-replace” remedy sought in the First Cause of Action under Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (d).

 

            Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the First Cause of Action.

 

C.    Fourth Cause of Action – Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civ. Code §§ 1791.1, 1794, and 1795.5.)

 

The Song-Beverly Act provides for implied warranties of merchantability and fitness for “consumer goods.” (Civ. Code, § 1791.1, subd. (a).) Importantly, “Consumer goods” means any new product or part thereof that is used, bought, or leased for use primarily for personal, family, or household purposes, except for clothing and consumables. (Civ. Code, § 1791, subd. (a).) (Underlines added.)

 

The Song-Beverly Act also provides for implied warranties for used goods, albeit for a shorter duration compared to new products – specifically, the implied warranties for used goods are limited to a maximum duration of three months. (Civ. Code, § 1795.5, subd. (c).) “As is the case with liability for breach of express warranties, ‘in the sale of used consumer goods, liability for breach of implied warranty lies with distributors and retailers, not the manufacturer,’ unless the manufacturer issues a new warranty along with the sale of the used good.” (Rodriguez, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at p. 218, internal citations omitted.) (Underlines added.) “The Song-Beverly Act provides similar remedies in the context of the sale of used goods, except that the manufacturer is generally off the hook.” (Kiluk v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 334, 339–340 (Kiluk).)

 

1)      FMC’s Initial Burden 

 

Here, FMC argues that Plaintiffs’ Fourth Cause of Action must fail because no defects were manifested within the three-month implied warranty period. (Mot. at pp. 9-12.) Additionally, FMC asserts the seller of Plaintiffs’ Subject Vehicle – Chino Hills Ford – is an independently operated dealership. (FMC’s UMF No. “2,” Geraghty Decl, Ex. “3.”) FMC further maintains that it did not issue any new or additional warranty at the time of Plaintiffs’ purchase of the Subject Vehicle from Chino Hills Ford. (FMC’s UMF No. “7,” Geraghty Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. “4.”)

 

As the Court has determined that the Subject Vehicle does not qualify as a “new motor vehicle” under the Song-Beverly Act, it further concludes that FMC has met its initial burden to show that liability for breach of implied warranty does not attach to FMC. The Court finds that FMC, as the manufacturer, is not liable under the implied warranty provisions because it did not issue a new warranty at the time of the Subject Vehicle's sale by Chino Hills Ford, an independent retailer.

 

2)      Plaintiffs’ Burden of Showing Existence of a Triable Material Factual Issue

 

In their Opposition, Plaintiffs argue that their protection under the implied warranty is not time-barred due to the latent defects that were undiscoverable at the time of sale. (Opp’n. at p. 7.) Plaintiffs further contend that when a warrantor partners with a dealership in the sale of its vehicles, the warrantor legally steps into the shoe of a “retailer” within the meaning of Civil Code section 1795.5, and is treated the same as a retailer, citing Kiluk, supra.

 

The Court acknowledges that Plaintiffs’ second argument is dispositive because, if FMC is exempted from the Civil Code section 1795.5, the liability does not attach to FMC even if there may be a latent defect.

 

The Court, however, finds Kiluk distinguishable. In Kiluk, the plaintiff purchased a certified preowned (“CPO”) Mercedez-Benz vehicle that included a new CPO warranty issued by Mercedez-Benz, extending coverage for one year beyond the expiration of the original new car warranty. (Kiluk, supra, 43 Cal.App.5th at p. 337.) The Kiluk court held that Mercedez-Benz was not exempted from liability under Civil Code section 1795.5 because “it partnered with a dealership to sell used vehicle directly to the public by offering an express warranty as part of the sales package[.]”

 

Here, Plaintiffs present no evidence to support that FMC issued any new warranty at the time of their purchase of the Subject Vehicle. Plaintiffs’ own evidence – the Warranty Manual – states in its Introduction:

 

“If you bought a previously owned 2020-model vehicle, you are eligible for any remaining warranty coverages.”

 

(Peacock & Smith Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. “2.”)

 

Unlike the new CPO warranty issued by the manufacturer in Kiluk, Plaintiffs’ evidence establishes only that they received the balance of the FMC’s original express warranty, issued when the Subject Vehicle was delivered to its original purchaser. Under these circumstances, both Rodriguez and Kiluk are consistent that Civil Code section 1795.5 specifically exempts manufacturers from liability, imposing warranty obligations only on the retailer or distributor.

 

Accordingly, the Court concludes that Plaintiffs have failed to meet their burden of showing the existence of a triable material factual issue.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Motion as to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

D.    Seventh Cause of Action – Violation of Consumer Legal Remedies Act (Civ. Code, §§ 1750 et seq.)

 

“[A]lthough a claim may be stated under the CLRA in terms constituting fraudulent omissions, to be actionable the omission must be contrary to a representation actually made by the defendant, or an omission of a fact the defendant was obliged to disclose.” (Daugherty v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 835 (Daugherty).) (Underlines added.)

 

Plaintiffs’ FAC alleges that FMC violated the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (“CLRA”) by “knowingly warranting and allowing to be sold to Plaintiffs the Vehicle without disclosing that the Subject Vehicle and its 10R80 10-speed (“10R80”) transmission is defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure.” (Compl. ¶ 78.) By failing to disclose and concealing the defective nature of the 10R80 transmission from Plaintiffs, Defendant violated California Civil Code § 1770(a), as it represented that the Vehicle and its 10R80 transmission had characteristics and benefits that they do not have, and ii) represented that the Vehicle and its 10R80 transmission were of a particular standard, quality, or grade when they were of another. See Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1770(a)(5) and (7).” (Id. ¶ 82.)

 

Accordingly, the Seventh Cause of Action is based on allegations of fraudulent omission or concealment.

 

1)       FMC’s Initial Burden 

 

FMC argues that there is no evidence of any fraudulent concealment on its part. FMC submits evidence that it publicly disclosed potential harsh shifting being experienced by some of its customers prior to Plaintiffs’ purchasing the Subject Vehicle. (FMC’s UMF No. “10.”)

 

FMC further argues that it had no duty to disclose, as it did not enter into a transactional relationship with Plaintiffs. FMC provides evidence that Plaintiffs did not purchase the Subject Vehicle from FMC (FMC’s UMF No. “3”), and testified that they had no contact with Ford prior to purchasing the Subject Vehicle, and that they did not review or rely on any advertising materials prior to purchasing. (Id. No. “5.”)

 

The Court finds that FMC has presented sufficient evidence to justify a ruling in its favor.

 

2)      Plaintiffs’ Burden of Showing Existence of a Triable Material Factual Issue

 

i.                    Existence of a Triable Issue as to FMC’s Misrepresentation

 

“[A]lthough a claim may be stated under the CLRA in terms constituting fraudulent omissions, to be actionable the omission must be contrary to a representation actually made by the defendant, or an omission of a fact the defendant was obliged to disclose.” (Daugherty, supra, 144 Cal.App.4th at p. 835.) (Underlines added.)

 

In Daugherty, the plaintiff “alleges Honda violated the CLRA by “[c]oncealing and failing to disclose” that the 1990–1997 model year Preludes and Accords equipped with F22 engines contain a defect, and by continuing to market and sell defective cars notwithstanding knowledge of the defect. (Id. at p. 833.) The Daugherty court upheld the trial court’s dismissal of the complaint, reasoning that “the complaint does not allege any representation of fact that ‘works a concealment of the true fact.’ (Citation omitted.) In other words, and as Outboard Marine [internal citation omitted] also opined, the CLRA proscribes a concealment of characteristics or quality “contrary to that represented,” but in Daugherty's case, no representation was made to which the alleged concealment was contrary.” (Id. at p. 834.) (Underlines added.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs presented evidence that when they were shopping for a vehicle, they “looked online mostly at the manufacturer website or dealership websites . . . .” (Pl.’s UMF No. “5,” referencing Peacock’s Deposition Transcript at 40: 11-15.) Plaintiffs suggest that FMC’s website or dealership websites made certain representations that could potentially create a basis for concealment of characteristics or quality contrary to those represented.

 

However, Plaintiffs have failed to provide specificity regarding any affirmative representation made by FMC, or on its website. Nor does Plaintiffs’ evidence support the existence of trial issue that any specified representation made by FMC could proscribe a concealment of characteristics or quality contrary to that represented.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have failed to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable material factual issue that FMC’s alleged nondisclosure of defect was contrary to any FMC’s affirmative representation.

 

ii.                  Existence of a Triable Issue as to FMC’s Duty to Disclose

 

Alternatively, FMC’s alleged fraudulent omission may be actionable if FMC was obligated to disclose. (Daugherty, supra, 144 Cal.App.4th at p. 835.) There are “four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]” (Heliotis v. Schuman (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 646, 651.)

 

Here, in disputing FMC’s UMF No. “10,” Plaintiffs merely state the general legal principal that “[a] duty to disclose material facts may arise, inter alia, (1) when the defendant has exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; or (2) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff. [citing LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336 (LiMandri)]” (Pl.’s UMF No. “10.”) Plaintiffs, however, provide no legal analysis or evidence to support their assertion.

 

The full statement in LiMandri clarifies that “where material facts are known to one party and not to the other, failure to disclose them is not actionable fraud unless there is some relationship between the parties which gives rise to a duty to disclose such known facts.” (LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337.) (Italics in original.) In LiMandri, the Court of Appeal found that “[t]he fundamental problem with [the plaintiff’s] nondisclosure causes of action is that he alleges no such transaction or relationship with [the defendant]. The only ‘transaction’ underlying [the plaintiff’s] claims in the ... conversation in which [the defendant] asked [the plaintiff] about the state court action against Signal Landmark. [The plaintiff] alleges no existing or anticipated contractual relationship or any other relationship with [the defendant] that would give rise to a duty to disclose.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, even construing all facts and inferences in Plaintiffs’ favor as required at the summary judgment stage, Plaintiffs’ receipt of the Warranty Manual at the sale of the Subject Vehicle in used condition and their online browsing at FMC’s websites does not demonstrate the existence of a transactional relationship with FMC. Such a transactional relationship is necessary to implicate FMC’s duty to disclose on the basis of its exclusive knowledge. (LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at p. 337.)

 

Additionally, Plaintiffs do not argue that FMC was in a fiduciary relationship with them, or that FMC actively concealed and suppressed information about the alleged defects. Plaintiffs’ evidence fails to demonstrate any of the circumstances required to impose a duty to disclose.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ evidence insufficient to meet their burden to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable material factual issue that FMC had a duty to disclose.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Motion as to the Seventh Cause of Action.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Summary Adjudication on the First, Fourth, and Seventh Cause of Action in Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2023, Plaintiff Eva Cruz (“Plaintiff” or “Cruz”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Juan Antonio Mazariego Jr., Rachel Megan Corbin, Ralph Corbin, and Barbara Corbin (collectively, “Defendants”), alleging one cause of action for Motor Vehicle Liability. Subsequently, Defendants filed their Answer to the Complaint on March 20, 2023.

 

 On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Trial Setting Preference (the “Motion”). Subsequently, Defendants filed their Opposition to the Motion on November 19, 2024, and Plaintiff replied on November 25, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 36 provides, “A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court” finds the party “has a substantial interest in the action as a whole” and the party’s health “is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).)

 

“The standard under subdivision (a), unlike under subdivision (d), which is more specific and more rigorous, includes no requirement of a doctor's declaration. To the contrary, a motion under subdivision (a) may be supported by nothing more than an attorney's declaration ‘based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.’ [Citations.]” (Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534.) Once a party “meets the requisite standard for calendar preference under subdivision (a), preference must be granted. No weighing of interests is involved.” (Fox v. Super. Ct., supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at 535.) This is so, even against the opposing party’s “interest in having adequate time to prepare for trial.” (Ibid.)

 

A case that is granted preference must be set for trial “not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party's attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (f).)

 

A.    Party 70 Years of Age or Older

Here, Plaintiff moves for preference applicable to a party 70 years of age or older. According to the driver's license lodged as Exhibit “A” to her counsel’s declaration, Plaintiff’s date of birth is January 4, 1946. (10/23/24 Darbinyan Decl. Ex. “A.”) In her Reply, Plaintiff presents her verified response to Form Interrogatories, Set One, No. 2.3 which states that her birth date is “1/4/1946.” (11/22/24 Darbinyan Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. “B.”)

The Court finds this evidence sufficient to establish Plaintiff’s date of birth, making her 78 years old.

 

B.     Party’s Substantial Interest in the Action

 

It is undisputed that Plaintiff has a substantial interest in the action as a whole, as she is the injured party.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a)(1) is satisfied.

 

C.    Proof of Party’s Health Condition

 

The moving party has a burden to show that her health is “such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a)(2).) (Underlines added.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s moving papers rely solely on her counsel’s declaration, which asserts that “Plaintiff suffers from diabetes, DM 2 with hyperglycemia, hypertension and hyperlipidemia.” (10/23/24 Darbinyan Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

The Court notes that the relevant standard under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a) accommodates a wide range of circumstances. As stated in Fox v. Superior Court, supra, “The issue under subdivision (a) is not whether an elderly litigant might die before trial or become so disabled ... [but] all subdivision (a) requires is a showing that that party's ‘health ... is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing [her] interest in the litigation.’” (Fox v. Super. Ct., supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at 534, italics omitted.) Additionally, a declaration supporting a motion for preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a) “may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 36.5.) (Underlines added.) While section 36.5 permits such a declaration, it does not eliminate the need to establish a reasonable connection between the party’s health and the necessity for trial preference.

 

The declaration, however, does not state that it is made “based on Plaintiff’s counsel’s information and belief,” leaving it unclear whether the declaration meets even the lowered evidentiary standard of personal assessment permitted under section 36.5. Moreover, it provides no indication that Plaintiff’s health condition is deteriorating or that it presents circumstances beyond chronic and managed conditions. As such, it lacks sufficient context or explanation to support a reasonable inference that Plaintiff’s health necessitates trial preference to prevent prejudicing her interest in the action. Finally, the declaration fails to demonstrate any prejudice Plaintiff would suffer if preference is unavailable.

 

Plaintiff’s Reply unfortunately has failed to address these deficiencies, leaving the Motion unsupported and insufficient to meet all statutory requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a).

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the Motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Preference is DENIED.
