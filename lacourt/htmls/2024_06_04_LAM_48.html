[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PETITIONS TO APPROVE MINOR’S COMPROMISE

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

June 4, 2024

 

Claimant Alexia Lopez, a minor, by and through her parent and guardian ad litem, Sonia Calderon, has agreed to settle her claims against Defendants Leonidas Ramirez, Dolores Ramirez, and Rafael Ramirez in exchange for $70,000.00.  Of this amount, $1,208.76 is allocated for reimbursement of medical expenses to Medi-Cal and $28,000.00 is allocated for attorney fees, leaving a balance of $40,791.24 for Claimant.  If approved, the funds will be deposited into a blocked account, subject to withdrawal only upon authorization of the court.

Claimant Genesis Lopez, a minor, by and through her parent and guardian ad litem, Sonia Calderon, has agreed to settle her claims against Defendants Leonidas Ramirez, Dolores Ramirez, and Rafael Ramirez in exchange for $35,000.00.  Of this amount, $122.28 is allocated for reimbursement of medical expenses to Medi-Cal and $14,000.00 is allocated for attorney fees, leaving a balance of $20,877.72 for Claimant.  If approved, the funds will be deposited into a blocked account, subject to withdrawal only upon authorization of the court.

Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim.  (Probate Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code Civ. Proc. § 372.)  The Court has reviewed the proposed settlement and finds that it is fair and reasonable.  The Court also finds that the attorney fees, which are 40% of the funds, are fair and reasonable.  Additionally, they were agreed to by the parent and guardian ad litem.

Accordingly, the Petitions to Approve Minor’s Compromise are GRANTED.  The Court sets an Order to Show Cause Re: Proof of Deposit Into Blocked Accounts for September 6, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  (California Rules of Court, rule 7.953(a).)  If acknowledgements of receipt by the financial institution are filed before that date, no appearance will be required.

Moving party to give notice.

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the Court at SMCDEPT48@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit.  If all parties in the case submit on the tentative ruling, no appearances before the Court are required unless a companion hearing (for example, a Case Management Conference) is also on calendar.

 

         Dated this 4th day of June 2024

 

 

 

 

Hon. Thomas D. Long

Judge of the Superior Court

On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff Jane Doe L.P. served a Notice of Taking Deposition of Defendant Downey Unified School District’s Person Most Knowledgeable and Fact Witness Principal Tom Houts and Request for Production of Documents (“RFPs”) on Defendant Downey Unified School District.  On February 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to RFP No. 4.

At the March 19, 2024, hearing, the Court found that some information about K.G., such as that related to Defendant’s knowledge of his prior similar misconduct and need for supervision, may be relevant.  However, the Court found that RFP No. 4 was overbroad.  The Court also noted that the request infringed on K.G.’s privacy rights without giving him notice before the disclosure.  Accordingly, the Court continued the hearing, ordered the parties to meet and confer about the scope of RFP No. 4, and ordered Defendant to provide K.G.’s guardian’s last known address and phone number to Plaintiff so that Plaintiff could provide notice of the motion and the continued hearing date.

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel filed a supplemental declaration.  Plaintiff has served K.G.’s guardian with notice of the motion via U.S. mail and email.  (Baldermann Suppl. Decl. ¶ 13 & Ex. 13.)  Since the last hearing, Plaintiff and Defendant have been discussing the scope of RFP No. 4.  (Baldermann Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 6-10.)  However, from April 18, 2024 through May 1, 2024, Defendant’s counsel stopped responding to Plaintiff’s counsel.  (Baldermann Suppl. Decl. ¶ 11.)

As propounded, RFP No. 4 requests “Any and all SCHOOL RECORDS in YOUR possession, custody or control for K.G.”  “SCHOOL RECORDS” is defined as “any and all written notes, memos, reports, or other writings regarding a student including but not limited to report cards, progress reports, letters, notes, nurses’ notes, attendance records, health records, incident reports, IEP reports, correspondence, class schedules, or any other written material having anything whatsoever to do with the student in YOUR possession, custody or control.”

Defendant’s last specific offer (March 26, 2024) was for K.G.’s “student profile, enrollment history, attendance history, entry into the special education program dates, and the September 2019 IEP outlining the supervision/services that his IEP called for at the time of the incident.”  (Baldermann Suppl. Decl. ¶ 6 & Ex. 7.)

Plaintiff’s last request (April 25, 2024) was for “K.G.’s student profile, enrollment history, class schedule for the 2019-2020 school year, entry into the special education program dates, IEP/504 Triennial Plans for the time period of August 1, 2012 - December 6, 2019, behavioral and psychosocial reports up until December 6, 2019, Disciplinary Records from August 1, 2012 - December 6, 2019, if any, which shall include written/recording verbal warnings, written warnings, incident reports, suspensions, detentions, complaints regarding behavioral issues and incidents, reprimands, and any similar admonishments of inappropriate behavior demonstrated by K.G.”  (Baldermann Suppl. Decl. ¶ 10 & Ex. 11.)

Plaintiff alleges that on December 6, 2019, her 1-on-l aide abandoned her, resulting in Plaintiff being assaulted by K.G.  (Complaint ¶ 22.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant “negligently failed to carry out its duties to properly and adequately supervise Plaintiff L.P.” and “failed to discipline, investigate, warn, appropriately supervise, or suspend [K.G.] despite having actual or constructive knowledge that he had engaged in inappropriate conduct with other minor student.”  (Complaint ¶¶ 23, 46.)  “California courts have long recognized that a student may recover for injuries proximately caused by a breach of [a school’s] duty to supervise.”  (M. W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 508, 518.)  “The existence of a duty of care of a school district toward a student depends, in part, on whether the particular harm to the student is reasonably foreseeable.”  (Ibid.)  The harm may be foreseeable when the alleged perpetrator was repeatedly disciplined for “grave acts of defiance and inappropriate and violent behavior.”  (Id. at p. 520.)  Accordingly, Defendant’s limitation to K.G.’s student profile, enrollment/attendance history, entry into the special education program, and the September 2019 IEP is too narrow.

Defendant did not file any supplemental information, and K.G.’s guardian did not file any objections.

The motion to compel further is GRANTED IN PART.

Defendant is ordered to respond to RFP No. 4 by producing the following documents to Plaintiff within 30 days:  K.G.’s student profile; enrollment history; class schedule for the 2019-2020 school year; dates of entry into the special education program; IEP/504 Triennial Plans for the time period of August 1, 2012 to December 6, 2019; behavioral and psychosocial reports up until December 6, 2019; and Disciplinary Records from August 1, 2012 to December 6, 2019, if any, including written/recording verbal warnings, written warnings, incident reports, suspensions, detentions, complaints regarding behavioral issues and incidents, reprimands, and any similar admonishments of inappropriate behavior demonstrated by K.G.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING MOTION TO SEAL

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

June 6, 2024

 

On May 6, 2024, Cross-Defendant Oscar de la Hoya filed a redacted Trial Brief and redacted supporting documents.  Cross-Defendant also filed a motion to seal.

A record must not be filed under seal without a court order, and a party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(a)-(b).)  The party requesting sealing must also file a public redacted version and lodge conditionally under seal with the court a complete, unredacted version.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(4)-(b)(5).)

The Court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it finds that (1) there is an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced absent sealing, (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exists to achieve the overriding interest.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  A motion seeking an order sealing records must be accompanied by a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)

Additionally, if a motion requires reliance on confidential and/or lodged filings, the unredacted versions must be emailed to smcdept48@lacourt.org, in accordance with Department 48’s Courtroom Information, available on the court’s website (www.lacourt.org).

Cross-Defendant purported to lodge his Trial Brief and Appendix of Exhibits conditionally under seal.  Cross-Defendant did not provide unredacted electronic copies.  Because the documents were not properly lodged with the Court, the Court cannot review them in connection with the request for sealing.

Even if Cross-Defendant had followed the proper procedures, the accompanying declaration does not contain facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (See California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)  Counsel’s declaration states only that the motion to seal was filed “in order to protect the personal privacy rights of himself, [Cross-Complainant] Ruccione, and of third parties to this Action.  The overriding interest overcomes the right to public access to said records in that the public disclosure of these documents may infringe upon the privacy rights of Defendants and third parties to this Action.”  (Troupson Decl. ¶ 6.)  Counsel also quotes a federal case from the Seventh Circuit to support sealing records that contain “highly sensitive and potentially embarrassingly personal information.”  (Troupson Decl. ¶ 7.)  The motion contains similar conclusory statements about privacy interests, with the only specific fact being third parties’ “phone numbers, email addresses and private personal information.”  (Motion at p. 4.)  Because Cross-Defendant did not properly lodge the unredacted documents, the Court cannot even independently assess the nature of the personal information and privacy rights.

Based on the current record, the Court is inclined to deny the motion.  However, the Court will continue the hearing to allow Cross-Defendant to supplement the motion.

The Hearing on Motion to Seal Trial Brief and Exhibits Thereto is CONTINUED July 11, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. in Department 48 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse.

No later than five court days before the hearing, Cross-Defendant is ordered to (1) electronically lodge the unredacted documents in accordance with Department 48’s procedures, and (2) file a supplemental declaration with facts sufficient to justify the sealing.

If Cross-Defendant does not follow this Department’s procedures for lodging or does not file a supplemental declaration that satisfies all requirements for sealing, the Court will strike the Trial Brief and related documents for not conforming with the California Rules of Court and this order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

June 4, 2024

 

On July 5, 2022, Plaintiff Commercial Waste Services Inc. filed this action against Defendant IPL Inc. (“IPL”), alleging breach of written contract and breach of implied warranty.

After IPL’s demurrer was overruled, “IPL North America Inc., erroneously named as IPL Inc.” (“IPL NA”) filed an answer.

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike IPL NA’s answer.

Plaintiff’s and IPL NA’s requests for judicial notice are granted.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff argues that IPL has forfeited its right to do business in California, and IPL NA improperly filed an answer without seeking leave to intervene in the action under Code of Civil Procedure section 387.  IPL NA argues that it is IPL’s assignee and successor in interest.

A.        A Motion to Strike is Procedurally Proper.

IPL NA argues that this motion is procedurally improper because “Plaintiff does not identify any violation of a court rule or Court Order,” lack of capacity to defend is an issue for summary judgment, and California has a longstanding policy of deciding cases on the merits.  (Opposition at pp. 4-5.)

A party may file a motion to strike an answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subds. (a)(2), (b)(1).)  The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

According to IPL NA, “[t]he only law of the state that [Plaintiff] arguably references is that a forfeited entity cannot defend itself.”  (Opposition at p. 4.)  IPL NA admits that “IPL, Inc. is a forfeited entity,” but “[t]hat entity did not, however, file the Answer here.  IPL NA did, and that entity is in good standing with the State of California.”  (Id. at p. 5.)

Plaintiff does not contend that IPL NA is a forfeited entity that lacks capacity to defend itself.  Rather, Plaintiff argues that IPL NA is a non-party who filed an answer that does not conform with the laws governing intervention by a non-party.  (Motion at pp. 4-6.)  A motion to strike is therefore an appropriate method of challenging this alleged non-conformity.

Additionally, IPL NA purports to file the answer on behalf of IPL as its assignee.  Had IPL filed an answer itself, that answer would also be subject to a motion to strike for being filed in violation of California law prohibiting defense of an action by a suspended or forfeited entity.  (See Motion at pp. 7-8.)  This is another reason why the Court finds that a motion to strike is appropriate for consideration.

B.        Assignee IPL NA is Subject to the Same Limitations as Forfeited Entity Assignor IPL, Including the Inability to Defend this Action.

It is undisputed that IPL, a Canadian corporation, cannot defend itself in this action.  On August 25, 2020, the California Secretary of State forfeited IPL’s right to do business in California.  (Motion, RJN, Ex. 2.)  “[A] suspended corporation may not prosecute or defend an action in a California court.”  (Timberline, Inc. v. Jaisinghani (1997) 54 Cal. App. 4th 1361, 1365 (Timberline).)

IPL NA argues that it is IPL’s successor in interest and assignee to its North American operations, and it contracted for IPL’s rights and obligations.  (Opposition at pp. 5-7.)  This is also not in dispute.  In October 2020, IPL merged into IPL Plastics Inc., IPL Plastics Inc. assigned all of its liability and obligations with respect to IPL’s North American operations to IPL St. Damien Inc., and IPL St. Damien Inc. underwent two name changes—resulting in IPL NA being the assignee and successor in interest to IPL.  (Darling Decl. ¶¶ 3-7.)

Plaintiff argues that“[d]espite the fact that IPL North America has active status, it must suffer the consequences of IPL Inc.’s forfeited status,” including the loss of the ability to defend this action.  (Reply at p. 7; see id. at pp. 4-7.)

An assignee is subject to all claims and defenses existing at or before the notice of the assignment.  (Cal-Western Business Services, Inc. v. Corning Capital Group (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 304, 310-312 (Cal-Western).)  IPL could no longer do business in California as of August 25, 2020.  The mergers, assignments, and name changes that led to IPL NA being the assignee of IPL occurred later, in October 2020.  (Darling Decl. ¶¶ 3-6.)  IPL NA is correct that it contracted for IPL’s rights and obligations.  (See Opposition at p. 6.)  Among those was IPL’s liability as a forfeited entity in California.   Accordingly, because IPL lacked capacity to defend itself in court at the time of notice of the assignment, IPL NA (as IPL’s assignee) is subject to the same limitation.  (See Cal-Western, supra, 221 Cal.App.4th at p. 312; Wanke, Industrial, Commercial, Residential, Inc. v. AV Builder Corp. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 466, 477 [discussing Cal-Western].)

 “[I]f the corporation’s status only comes to light during litigation, the normal practice is for the trial court to permit a short continuance to enable the suspended corporation to effect reinstatement (by paying back taxes, interest and penalties) to defend itself in court.”  (Timberline, supra, 54 Cal. App. 4th at p. 1366.)

Here, however, a continuance would be futile.  IPL no longer exists and does not have any assets.  (Darling Decl. ¶ 9.)  The Court therefore declines to continue the hearing on this motion.  There does not appear to be any way for IPL to revive its status so that IPL NA (as assignee) can defend this action on behalf of IPL.

CONCLUSION

The motion to strike is GRANTED.  The Court orders the answer filed by IPL NA on September 29, 2023 STRICKEN.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT THE WALT DISNEY COMPANY’S MOTION TO DECLARE VEXATIOUS LITIGANT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

On May 8, 2024, Defendant the Walt Disney Company’s filed the Motion to Declare Dylan Scott Perry a Vexatious Litigant. On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition. On May 28, 2024, Defendant filed the reply. 

The Walt Disney Company (erroneously named as “d.b.a. Twentieth Century Studios”) moves the Court pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 391 et seq. for an Order designating Plaintiff Dylan Scott Perry a “vexatious litigant” under Code of Civil Procedure sections 391 et seq. and entering a pre-filing order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391.7 that prohibits Perry from filing any new litigation in the courts of California in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the presiding justice or presiding judge of the court where there the litigation is proposed to be filed.

Section 391.7 allows courts to take preemptive action to curb future litigation abuse by a vexatious litigant. Code Civ. Proc. § 391.7. The Court, on its own motion or the motion of a party, may enter a pre-filing order that prohibits a vexatious litigant from filing new litigation without the prior authorization of the presiding justice or judge of the court in which the litigation is proposed to be filed. Id. § 391.7(a). A vexatious litigant subject to a prefiling order may only bring suit by employing a lawyer, or by proceeding pro se and demonstrating to the presiding judge or justice of the relevant court that the proposed “litigation has merit and has not been filed for the purposes of harassment or delay.” Id. § 391.7(b); see also Bravo, 99 Cal. App. 4th at 221. Code of Civil Procedure section 391(b) defines a “vexatious litigant” as a pro se litigant who, among other things, “repeatedly files unmeritorious papers or engages in other frivolous litigation tactics.” Code Civ. Proc. § 391(b)(3).

Here, Perry filed a nearly identical complaint in a new action (23STCP01900) on June 1, 2023. Defendant filed demurrers to Perry’s complaints in both cases on June 23, 2023. While those demurrers were pending, Perry filed a third complaint in a third case (23STCV15449, filed July 10, 2023). That complaint alleged the same basic conduct that Perry had previously alleged in his prior two cases and sought $1 billion in monetary relief to be able to afford security, including “electric fences, razor wire, top of the line security, attack dogs, a moat, alligators, a drawbridge, nuclear defenses, anti-air missiles, and to hire 4 or more Mercenaries wielding M16s to secure the perimeter of my home.” (23STCV15449 Compl. at p. 4.) Defendant filed a demurrer to this complaint on September 8, 2023. At the May 7, 2024, hearing on Defendant’s demurrers, Perry sought leave to file an amended complaint that would “no longer alleg[e] anything” and would instead seek declaratory relief in the form of answers to 25 questions. (Payne Decl. ¶¶ 2–3 & Ex. A; see also Payne Decl. ¶ 4 & Ex. D.) At the same hearing, Perry insisted that he needed certain information from Defendant before he could “file articles of impeachment with congress.” (Payne Decl. ¶ 3.)

On May 7, 2024, this Court sustained all three demurrers without leave to amend. Defendant’s counsel also submitted an additional supplemental declaration where he states that after filing the instant motion, he was threatened by Plaintiff. (Payne Decl. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s one-line opposition was “Penis boner banana.” (Opp’n at p.2.)

Accordingly, the motion to declare Plaintiff Dylan Scott Perry a vexatious litigant is GRANTED.
