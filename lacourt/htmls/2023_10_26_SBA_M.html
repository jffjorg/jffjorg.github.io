Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

GENERAL MOTORS LLC,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                         October 26, 2023

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant General Motors LLC

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Amr Mohamed Henigal and Amr Henigal LLC

Motion for Summary Judgment or, Alternatively, for Summary Adjudication

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  The motion for summary adjudication is MOOT in light of the ruling on the motion for summary judgment.

BACKGROUND

On January 26, 2022, plaintiffs Amr Mohamed Henigal and Amr Henigal LLC filed a complaint against General Motors LLC for violations of statutory obligations under Song Beverly Act and Magnuson Moss Act.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  Id.  “’An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.’”  Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 1001, 1014 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

Defendant General Motors LLC requests that the court enter summary judgment in its favor and against plaintiffs pursuant to CCP §437c as to each of the causes of action on the ground that there is no triable issue of material fact and defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

The complaint alleges that on June 21, 2021, plaintiffs purchased a 2020 Cadillac Escalade from Penske Chevrolet of Cerritos.  The vehicle was manufactured and/or distributed and warranted by GM.  Complaint, ¶4.  In connection with the transaction, GM issued to plaintiffs express warranties within the meaning of Civil Code §1791.2.  Id., ¶7.  Subsequent to plaintiffs’ purchase, the vehicle exhibited numerous defects and nonconformities covered by the warranties, which substantially impair the use, value, and safety of the motor vehicle.  Plaintiffs visited the servicing GM dealership on July 14, 2021 (47,916 miles), October 25, 2021 (48,273 miles), and December 16, 2021 (48,715 miles).  Id., ¶8.  GM’s authorized repair facilities failed to properly inspect the vehicle and failed to properly diagnose the severe issues plaguing the vehicle.  GM is well aware of the several chronic issues with the engine, transmission, power steering, hitch, park assist, mirrors, and windows that have plagued the vehicle.  Id., ¶9.  The vehicle is a “new motor vehicle under Civil Code §1790 et seq.  Id., ¶13.

The parties do not dispute that the subject vehicle is a 2020 Cadillac Escalade, VIN 1GYS4HKJ8LR103193.  Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (DUMF) 1.  Plaintiffs bought the Escalade used, with 47,718 miles, on June 30, 2021.  DUMF 2.  The Escalade had a cash price of $68,882.  DUMF 4.  With the Escalade, plaintiffs bought two non-GM products totaling $5890:  (1) an optional service contract from “PHD Productions” for $4995 and (2) a debt cancellation agreement for $895.  DUMF 5.  Factoring in these two non-GM products, all taxes and fees, including more than $12,600 in finance charges, the Total Sales Price came to $95,457.92.  DUMF 6.  Plaintiffs were not the Escalade’s first owners; the Escalade was delivered to its original owner as a daily rental in Missouri on July 2, 2019, with nine miles on its odometer.  DUMF 7.  In connection with the Escalade’s delivery to its original owner(s), GM issued a New Vehicle Limited Warranty with bumper-to-bumper coverage for the earlier of 48 months or 50,000 miles and powertrain coverage for the earlier of 72 months or 70,000 miles.  DUMF 8.

1st cause of action for violation of Song Beverly Act – breach of express warranty

Civil Code §1793.2 states, in part:  “(d)(2) If the manufacturer or its representative in this state is unable to service or repair a new motor vehicle, as that term is defined in paragraph (2) of subdivision (e) of Section 1793.22, to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of attempts, the manufacturer shall either promptly replace the new motor vehicle in accordance with subparagraph (A) or promptly make restitution to the buyer in accordance with subparagraph (B). . . .”

Under Civil Code §1793.22, “(e) For the purposes of subdivision (d) of Section 1793.2 . . . , the following terms have the following meanings:  . . . . (2) ‘New motor vehicle’ means a new motor vehicle that is bought or used primarily for personal, family, or household purposes. . .  . ‘New motor vehicle’ includes . . . a dealer-owned vehicle and a ‘demonstrator’ or other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty . . . .”

Defendant argues that this claim fails because plaintiffs did not buy a “new motor vehicle” or receive a “new car warranty” from GM, citing to Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC (2022) 77 Cal. App. 5th 209, 215, review granted July 13, 2022 (the Song-Beverly Act does not apply to “previously owned motor vehicles with some balances remaining on the manufacturer’s express warranty . . . .”). 

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the court should follow Jensen v. BMW of N. Am., Inc. (1995) 35 Cal. App. 4th 112, 123 (“We conclude the words of section 1793.22 are reasonably free from ambiguity and cars sold with a balance remaining on the manufacturer’s new motor vehicle warranty are included with its definition of ‘new motor vehicle.’”) and the subsequent cases that followed Jensen.  See, e.g., R & R Auto Center, Inc. v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2006) 140 Cal. App. 4th 327, 335.  Plaintiffs assert that Jensen is consistent with legislative intent and furthers the goals of the Song Beverly Act.  Plaintiff also contends that the plain language of the Act supports used car coverage.  Plaintiffs further argue that Rodriguez is distinguishable because plaintiffs’ vehicle was purchased from defendant GM’s authorized retailer with its knowledge and its warranty, like in Jensen.

In reply, defendant reiterates that the parties do not dispute that the vehicle was not purchased new in California, and thus plaintiffs lack standing under Song Beverly Act.  As to the Rodriguez case, defendant argues that the court of appeal’s holding is legally sound and directly on point, and that several courts have cited to Rodriguez for its persuasive value.  Defendant also asserts that the “facts here look nothing like the facts in Jensen.”

The court rules as follows:  In exercising its discretion, (see Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal. 2d 450, 456), the court follows Rodriguez as more persuasive than Jensen, and the Rodriguez facts are more analogous to the alleged facts in the herein case—a used vehicle with some balance remaining on the manufacturer’s express warranty.  The Rodriguez court, in distinguishing itself from the Jensen case, explained that Jensen “must be read in light of the facts then before the court and are limited in that respect” and that the Jensen court “was not asked to decide whether a used car with an unexpired warranty sold by a third party reseller qualifies as a ‘new motor vehicle.’”  Rodriguez, supra, at 224.  The Rodriguez court concluded based on “the statutory provision, its place within [Song-Beverly] as a whole, and its legislative history,” the phrase “other motor vehicles sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty” refers to cars sold with a full warranty, not to previously sold cars accompanied by some balance of the original warranty.  Rodriguez, at 225.

 

2nd cause of action for violation of Song Beverly Act – breach of implied warranty

“[E]very sale of consumer goods that are sold at retail in the state shall be accompanied by the manufacture’s and the retail seller’s implied warranty that the goods are merchantable.”  Civil Code §1792.  “Consumer goods” is defined as “any new product or part thereof that is used, bought, or leased for use primarily for personal, family, or household purposes . . . .” Civil Code §1791(a). 

Defendant argues that the implied warranties under Song-Beverly do not apply to manufacturers with respect to the sale of used goods, citing to Civil Code §1795.5 and Ruiz Nunez v. FCA US LLC (2021) 61 Cal. App. 5th 385, 399 (“It is evident from these provisions that only distributors or sellers of used goods—not manufacturers of new goods—have implied warranty obligations in the sale of used goods.”).  Thus, defendant argues, because plaintiffs bought the Escalade used and defendant is a manufacturer, this cause of action fails as a matter of law.

Plaintiff does not address in the opposition.

The court finds that defendant has met its burden of showing that plaintiff cannot establish the elements under this cause of action as it is undisputed that the vehicle was purchased used and defendant is a manufacturer.  See Nunez, supra; Kiluk v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (2019) 43 Cal. App. 5th 334, 337 (Song-Beverly Act “generally binds only distributors and retail sellers in the sale of used goods.”).  Plaintiff has not presented any evidence that defendant was “a distributor or retail seller of used consumer goods” or in any way acted as such.

 

3rd cause of action for Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act – breach of express warranty and 4th cause of action for Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act – breach of implied warranty

“Magnuson-Moss ‘calls for the application of state written and implied warranty law, not the creation of additional federal law,’ except in specific instances in which it expressly prescribes a regulating rule.”  Daugherty v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 824, 833 (citation omitted).  “[F]ailure to state a warranty claim under state law necessarily constituted a failure to state a claim under Magnuson-Moss.”  law constitutes a failure to state a claim under Magnuson-Moss.  Id.  See also Arana v. Tesla Motors, Inc. (C.D. Cal. Feb. 7, 2023) 2023 U.S. Dist. LEXIS 20847 (“[A]n MMWA claim typically succeeds or fails along with a plaintiff’s state warranty claims.”).

Defendant argues that because plaintiffs’ claims fail under the Song Beverly Act, they also fail under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

In opposition, plaintiffs argue that Magnuson-Moss creates a federal claim for relief that has its own elements   Plaintiffs also contend that defendant failed to present sufficient evidence to establish that the vehicle was not given a warranty when it was purchased by plaintiffs, noting a GM warranty booklet.

In reply, defendant contends that it showed that it did not provide new or additional warranty coverage with plaintiffs’ purchase, and that plaintiffs have not presented admissible evidence that GM did provide new coverage.

The court finds that defendant has shown that plaintiff cannot establish the elements under Magnuson-Moss in that the underlying state warranty claims fail. 

 

Request for continuance

Plaintiffs contend that they have sought to depose defendant’s PMQ on matters pertaining to the warranties applicable to the vehicle, the sale of the vehicle, the certified pre-owned procedures and coverage, among other things, and that defendant has precluded plaintiff from taking such deposition.  Plaintiff also asserts that defendant failed to respond to plaintiffs’ discovery requests with substantive verified responses or a responsive document production.

In the reply, defendant argues that no additional testimony or documents would change the grounds upon which GM is seeking summary judgment, and that no discovery will change the fact that plaintiffs did not buy the vehicle “new.”  Also, defendant points out, the discovery cut-off passed on July 17, 2023.

The court finds that a continuance is not warranted.

            Accordingly, the motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  The motion for summary adjudication is MOOT in light of the ruling on the motion for summary judgment.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP §1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Interrogatories

CCP §2030.300 states:  “(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:   (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. . . . (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.  (b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories. . . .”

CCP §2030.220 states:  “(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.  (b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.  (c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  CCP § 2031.310(a).  A statement of compliance shall state that the production, inspection, and related activity demanded will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.  CCP § 2031.220.  “A representation of inability to comply with [a] particular demand for inspection . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.  This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.  This statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”  CCP § 2031.230.

A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).  “To establish ‘good cause,’ the burden is on the moving party to show both:  [1] Relevance to the subject matter (e.g., how the information in the documents would tend to prove or disprove some issue in the case); and [2] Specific facts justifying discovery (e.g., why such information is necessary for trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial).  The fact that there is no alternative source for the information sought is an important factor in establishing ‘good cause’ for inspection.  But it is not essential in every case.”  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1495.6 (citations omitted).  “Declarations are generally used to show the requisite ‘good cause’ for an order to compel inspection.  The declarations must contain ‘specific facts’ rather than mere conclusions.”  Id. at 8:1495.7 (citation omitted).  “The declarations may be on information and belief, if necessary.  However, in such cases, the ‘specific facts’ supporting such information and belief (the sources of the information) must also be alleged.”  Id. at 8:1495.8 (citation omitted).  “Most declarations are made by the attorney for the moving party, who is usually more familiar with the relevancy and ‘specific facts’ constituting ‘good cause’ for inspection.”  Id. at 8:1495.9.

“If ‘good cause’ is shown by the moving party, the burden is then on the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure (the same as on motions to compel answers to interrogatories or deposition questions. . . ).”  Id. at 8:1496 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant City of Hermosa Beach requests that the court compel plaintiff Jonathan Cruz to respond further to its first set of form interrogatories-general, form interrogatories-employment, special interrogatories, and requests for production of documents.

            The FAC alleges that City Manager Suja Lowenthal, who also serves as the City Manager and Personnel Officer for the City of Hermosa Beach, implemented a mandatory vaccination policy for Covid-19 and denied all sworn officers’, including plaintiff’s, request for accommodations under the policy.  FAC, ¶4.  It is believed that the City of Hermosa Beach was the only city in the South Bay to institute a mandatory Covid-19 vaccination policy; therefore, officers from other agencies that routinely worked in Hermosa Beach and/or worked with Hermosa Beach police officers to provide aid to various cities did not have to be vaccinated.  Id., ¶5.  While plaintiff was employed, after implementation of the vaccination policy, he was routinely tested to determine if he had the Covid virus whereas other vaccinated employees that were vaccinated were not tested and appeared at work in an infected and/or contagious status.  It is believed that there was no good cause for the mandatory vaccination policy.  Id., ¶6.  City Manager Lowenthal dismissed plaintiff, terminating his employment with the City of Hermosa Beach and the Hermosa Beach Police Department.  It is believed that Lowenthal acted in excess of her authority when she terminated plaintiff’s employment.  Id., ¶7.

            The FAC further alleges that pursuant to Gov. Code §3304, and his due process rights, plaintiff requested an administrative appeal of his dismissal.  Id., ¶8.  Plaintiff believed that he was discriminated against because of his deeply held beliefs that prevented him from getting vaccinated.  Id., ¶9.  Because plaintiff filed complaint with the DFEH and/or EEOC, defendants refused and continue to refuse to afford him an administrative appeal hearing from his dismissal in violation of his due process rights and his rights under Gov. Code §3304.  Id., ¶10.

            Defendant asserts that it served its form interrogatories on plaintiff by email on May 8, 2023 and responses were due by June 9, 2023.  Defendant served its special interrogatories and requests for production of documents on plaintiff by email on May 18, 2023 and responses were due by June 19, 2023.

On June 14, 2023, plaintiff’s counsel requested, and defense counsel granted, a two-week extension for discovery responses until July 3, 2023.  Defendant asserts that they were mailed on July 3 (as indicated on the proofs of service), but because of the holiday on July 4, they were not postmarked until July 5.  Defense counsel received plaintiff’s responses by mail on July 7, 2023.  Verifications were provided on July 14, 2023.  After reviewing the responses and finding them to be deficient, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter on July 20, 2023, requesting that plaintiff provide amended responses.  On July 27, 2023, plaintiff’s counsel responded declining to amend plaintiff’s responses until defendant stipulated to a protective order.  Defendant contends that plaintiff’s counsel conditioned the protective order as a waiver of defendant’s Pitchess objections for privileged third-party officer personnel and health records.  On August 16, 2023, defense counsel sent another meet and confer letter “explaining that Plaintiff’s objections lacked merit and that the responses provided were evasive and incomplete” and that it would agree to a protective order but that the protective order does not address the City’s separate Pitchess obligations with regard to its maintenance of third-party records.  In an email August 25, 2023, plaintiff’s counsel maintained that plaintiff had properly and fully responded to all questions as posed and reiterated that he believed Pitchess procedures were applicable to all discovery propounded to plaintiff.

            Defendant argues that plaintiff waived his objections because the responses were served after July 3, 2023.  Further, defendant argues, in any event, plaintiff’s objections lack merit.  Defendant asserts that plaintiff objected that the interrogatories were compound, vague, and ambiguous and that the interrogatories purportedly call for information subject to Pitchess protections and procedures.  Defendant contends that it utilized the standard Judicial Council approved definition of “incident” and that plaintiff and his counsel are presumed to understand the factual basis for their legal contentions.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff’s substantive responses are evasive and incomplete.

As to whether Pitchess procedure applies, defendant contends that Pitchess does not apply to plaintiff, as an individual party, because the “general personnel file” that is privileged from disclosure under Pitchess is “the file maintained by the agency containing the primary records specific to each peace or custodial officer’s employment, including evaluations, assignments, status changes, and imposed discipline.”  Penal Code §832.5(d)(1).  Defendant argues that plaintiff is “taking the absurd position that the City must go through the process of effectively noticing a motion against itself requesting information it does not possess, for a hearing it can waive, as a prerequisite to engaging in defensive discovery in this matter.”  Defendant asserts that it does not seek to disclose plaintiff’s personnel records but instead seeks documents not in its possession regarding plaintiff’s allegations.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the motion is untimely.  Plaintiff also contends that his responses were not untimely because they were served on July 3.  Plaintiff argues that a Pitchess motion is required because defendant is seeking to conduct discovery and disclosure from confidential personnel files and information contained in such files.

 

            Initially, the court finds that the motions are timely, as plaintiff did not serve his verifications until July 14, 2023, and the motions were filed on August 28, 2023, which is 45 days.  The court also finds that plaintiff did not waive his objections as the proofs of service indicate that he served his responses by the agreed upon date of July 3, 2023.  As to the Pitchess procedure for the discovery sought by defendant from plaintiff, the court finds that it does not apply.  Under Penal Code §832.8, “As used in Section 832.7, the following words or phrases have the following meanings:  (a) “Personnel records” means any file maintained under that individual’s name by his or her employing agency and containing records relating to any of the following:

(1) Personal data, including marital status, family members, educational and employment history, home addresses, or similar information.

(2) Medical history.

(3) Election of employee benefits.

(4) Employee advancement, appraisal, or discipline.

(5) Complaints, or investigations of complaints, concerning an event or transaction in which he or she participated, or which he or she perceived, and pertaining to the manner in which he or she performed his or her duties.

(6) Any other information the disclosure of which would constitute an unwarranted invasion of personal privacy.”

The information sought below does not appear to be implicated in the definition of “personnel records.”  Further, defendant persuasively distinguished the cases cited by plaintiff.  Also, defendant is seeking information from plaintiff, which would not be “maintained by the agency.”

No. 3 – plaintiff responded sufficiently, and defendant does not make any argument for a further response.

No. 4 – plaintiff responded by referring to his response to No. 3, which identified several individuals at police departments in other cities.  Defendant asserts that plaintiff’s response is deficient because plaintiff did not provide contact information and thus the response is incomplete. 

No. 5 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶5 – plaintiff responded by reiterating his allegation.

No. 9 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶8 – plaintiff responded by reiterating his allegation.

No. 11 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶9 – plaintiff responded by reiterating his allegation.

No. 15 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶15 – plaintiff responded by reiterating his allegation.

No. 17 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶24 (contention that City of HB and/or HB Police Dept. has a history of denying police officers the opportunity for administrative appeal when subjected to punitive action) – plaintiff responded by naming several officers but provided no other information, and thus, the response is incomplete.

No. 21 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶42 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint without providing any further, substantive information or evidence.

No. 23 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶43 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint without providing any further, substantive information or evidence.

No. 25 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶44 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint without providing any further, substantive information or evidence.

No. 27 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶45 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint without providing any further, substantive information or evidence.

No. 29 – identify all healthcare providers plaintiff has seen for treatment, consultation, or other healthcare services within the past five years.  Plaintiff objected that the interrogatory seeks to invade plaintiff’s right to privacy and the confidential medical information act/HIPPA.

Defendant argues that plaintiff has put his vaccination status at issue, “so the identification of healthcare providers will be necessary to determine the merits of plaintiff’s claimed religious objections” and that plaintiff has put his physical and mental health at issue by alleging that he suffered and continues to suffer physical, mental, and emotional injuries.

The court finds that the interrogatory is overly broad.  Counsel are to meet and confer to narrow the interrogatory as to plaintiff’s medical information.

No. 32 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶46 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint and did not provide any response to the other “coveted positions in the City” or the benefits plaintiff would have received but-for his separation from employment.

No. 34 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶47 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint and did not provide any response to the “other income and/or opportunities to obtain employment and/or increase plaintiff’s earning capacity” outside of the City.

No. 36 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶48 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint and did not provide any response as to incidental and consequential damages except that he had to make up the loss of his salary and benefits through “other measures” but does not identify such measures.

No. 37 – identify all person with knowledge supporting plaintiff’s contention that he “suffered other incidental and consequential damages.” 

Plaintiff responded only identifying himself.  Defendant argues that the response is evasive and incomplete because he had responded in No. 36 that he had to supplement the loss of his income and benefits through other measures, which “implies that he has worked or transacted with at least one person or entity, if not multiple.”

No. 42 – contention interrogatory as to Complaint, ¶54 – plaintiff responded by reiterating the complaint and added that he filed a tort claim and “he sent communications” to the City and City Council but did not provide any other details. 

No. 43 – identify all persons with knowledge supporting plaintiff’s contention that plaintiff complained to defendants about the inappropriate actions and/or retaliation, but nothing was done to investigate and/or stop the retaliation.  Plaintiff responded “Suja Lowenthal, City Clerk, Vanessa Godinez, Paul LeBaron, Hermosa Beach City Council, Plaintiff.”

The court finds that the response is sufficient.

No. 46 – state each communication plaintiff contends constitutes a request for an administrative appeal of plaintiff’s dismissal, as alleged in Complaint, ¶8.

Plaintiff responded “March 16, 2022, Appeal Letter.  Verbal and email conversations between Jennifer Misetich and Plaintiff’s counsel.”  Defendant argues that the response is vague because it fails to specify the exact communications that plaintiff claims constituted requests for an appeal.

No. 47 – state each communication plaintiff contends constitutes a disclosure to a person with authority over plaintiff, and/or to another employee who had authority to investigate, discover and/or correct violations of law/statutes/regulations, as alleged in Complaint, ¶42.

Plaintiff responded, “verbal conversation with Suja Lowenthal, Jennifer Misetich, and Dorothy Scheid; email communications to City of HB City Council, Tort Claim, Appeal Letter.”  Defendant argues that the response is vague because it fails to specify the exact communications that plaintiff claims constituted protected disclosures or complaints.

No. 48 – state each communication plaintiff contends constitutes a “complaint to defendants about the inappropriate actions and/or retaliation,” as alleged in Complaint, ¶54.  Plaintiff responded the same as No. 47. 

No. 54 – identify all persons with knowledge of the facts that support the 3rd cause of action for violation of Labor Code §1102.5.

Plaintiff responded, “City Council, the City Clerk (via Tort Claim), verbally to Suja Lowenthal, Jennifer Misetich, Vanessa Godinez, Dorothy Scheid.”

The court finds the response sufficient.

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to Nos. 1, 4, 5, 9, 11, 15, 17, 21, 23, 25, 27, 32, 34, 36, 37, 42, 46, 47, and 48 and DENIED as to Nos. 3, 29, 43, and 54.

 

 

 

 

 

                        Requests for Production of Documents

            As to each of the 44 requests, plaintiff asserted objections and responded, “Plaintiff, to the extent he understands the Request, will produce all non-privileged documents upon the issuance of a protective order as provided for under Evidence Code §1045(e).”  Plaintiff has not produced any documents.

            As stated above, Pitchess does not apply to the documents sought by defendant from plaintiff.

            The motion is GRANTED.

 

 

 

            Sanctions

            The court declines to award sanctions against plaintiff and plaintiff’s attorney of record finding that they acted with substantial justification.
