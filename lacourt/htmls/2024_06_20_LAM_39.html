Case Number: 22STCV33046    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV33046

MOTION

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Huge USA, Inc. and Jin Ser Park

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Nam Suk Park

 

MOTION

Defendants Huge USA, Inc. and Jin Ser Park (“Defendants”) move to compel further responses from Plaintiff Nam Suk Park (“Plaintiff”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set two (“RPD”) and Special Interrogatories, set two (“SROG”).  Plaintiff opposes the motions. 

In its order of March 20, 2024, the court ordered the parties to meet and confer on each outstanding discovery request on or before March 29, 2024, and to file a joint statement regarding any issues the parties could not resolve on or before April 24, 2024.  The parties filed their joint statement and indicated that they resolved all issues with respect to the RPD and SROG.  Accordingly, the Court places the motion off calendar as moot.

Defendants argue in the joint statement Defendants believe Plaintiff may have failed to preserve evidence pertinent to this case.  That issue is not developed by way of a noticed motion.  Therefore, it is not properly before the court, and the court will not consider it unless and until Defendants notice and serve a motion concerning it.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of this order and file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV27441    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV27441

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Total-Western, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Steven Hockney

 

MOTION

              Defendant Total-Western, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration with Plaintiff Steven Hockney (“Plaintiff”), and Plaintiff opposes the motion.

 ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendant contends an arbitration agreement between Defendant and Plaintiff’s union, United Steelworkers Local 1945, provides Defendant and Plaintiff’s union agree to arbitrate “any dispute arising out of the interpretation of” the collective bargaining agreement.  Defendant and Plaintiff’s union also agreed to arbitrate “any dispute or grievance regarding overtime, meal periods, rest periods or any other subject matter covered by any and all applicable Wage Orders issued by . . . California . . . , and/or by California Labor Code sections 201-204, 226, 226.7, 510, 512, 1194, 1194.2, or 1197 . . . .” 

The agreement further provides Plaintiff’s union will file a grievance with Defendant, and the parties will attempt to resolve the dispute informally.  If the parties are unable to resolve the dispute informally, Plaintiff’s union will “determine whether to continue to participate in the arbitration of” the grievance.  If Plaintiff’s union elects not to participate in the arbitration, Plaintiff “may pursue arbitration . . . .”  (Declaration of Leonard Crespo, Exhibits A, B.)  By its terms, if Plaintiff’s union elects not to participate in arbitration, Plaintiff’s participation in an arbitration is optional.  As such, Defendant fails to meet its burden to show an arbitration agreement exists, which applies to Plaintiff’s claims.  The motion to compel arbitration is, therefore, denied.

In reply, Defendant argues “[t]he use of the word ‘may’ is to state that should Plaintiff decide to continue proceeding with filing an action against Defendant, that the avenue to do so is through arbitration–not that Plaintiff may choose any other avenue of dispute resolution.”  (Reply in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, p. 7.)  However, the language of the collective bargaining agreement “is not reasonably susceptible of a meaning” Plaintiff must submit to arbitration.  (Beard v. Goodrich (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1039.)  Defendant acknowledges the language of the agreement is permissive.  (Reply in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, p. 7.)  Further, Defendant advances no extrinsic evidence to support its argument the parties intended a contrary meaning.  (Founding Members of the Newport Beach Country Club v. Newport Beach Country Club, Inc. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 944, 955.)  As such, Defendant’s argument is unsupported and not persuasive.

Therefore, the motion is denied, and Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

              Defendant Zara USA, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration with Plaintiff Adrain J. Parra (“Plaintiff”), and Plaintiff opposes the motion.

 ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

.Defendant advances an arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed.  The arbitration agreement provides Plaintiff agrees to arbitrate “any controversy, dispute, or claim that could otherwise be raised in court . . . that [Defendant] has against [Plaintiff] or [Plaintiff] [has] against [Defendant] (and/or its current or former officers, directors, members, employees, vendors, clients, customers, agents, parents, subsidiaries, affiliated companies, successors, or assigns) . . . .”  (Declaration of Patricia Sullivan, Exhibit 1.)  Accordingly, Defendant meets its burden to show that an arbitration agreement exists, which applies to Plaintiff’s claims.

Plaintiff asserts representative claims under the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004, or PAGA.  However, the arbitration agreement requires Plaintiff to bring claims “on an individual basis only . . . .”  (Declaration of Patricia Sullivan, Exhibit 1.)  Pursuant to Viking River Cruises, Inc. v. Moriana (2022) 596 U.S. 639 (Viking River Cruises), Defendant is “entitled to enforce the agreement insofar as it mandated arbitration of [Plaintiff]’s individual PAGA claim.”  (Id. at p. 662.)  Plaintiff must arbitrate his individual claim under PAGA.  Therefore, the court stays Plaintiff’s representative claim pending arbitration of Plaintiff’s individual claim.  (See Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1123.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is unconscionable under Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83.  The arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed specifies the Federal Arbitration Act governs.  A choice of law provision in an arbitration agreement is enforceable.  (See Coopers & Lybrand v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 524, 538.)  As such, the Federal Arbitration Act applies.  Under the Federal Arbitration Act, defenses that derive their meaning from the fact that an agreement to arbitrate is at issue cannot invalidate agreements to arbitrate.  (AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333,  339.)  The rules set forth in Armendariz are specific to arbitration agreements.  (See Abramson v. Juniper Networks, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 638, 661.)  These rules, therefore, cannot invalidate the arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant.  The court considers whether the arbitration agreement is unconscionable pursuant to generally applicable principles of contract law.

Civil Code section 1670.5 provides “[i]f the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract, or it may enforce the remainder of the contract without the unconscionable clause, or it may so limit the application of any unconscionable clause as to avoid any unconscionable result.”  (Civ. Code, § 1670.5, subd. (a).)  Unconscionability is defined as “an absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms which are unreasonably favorable to the other party.”  (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486 (A & M Produce).)

Unconscionability, thus, has a procedural and a substantive element.  (A & M Produce, supra, 135 Cal. App.3d at p. 486.)  The court considers the factors of oppression and surprise in determining whether a contract is procedurally unconscionable.  (Ibid.)  Oppression refers to inequality of bargaining power, which cuts off meaningful negotiation.  (Ibid.)  Surprise refers to the concealment of the agreed-upon terms in a lengthy form drafted by the party seeking to enforce them.  (Ibid.)  Substantive unconscionability refers to the extent to which a contractual term reallocates the risks of a transaction in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.  (Id. at 487.)  Both substantive and procedural unconscionability must be present for a contract to be unenforceable, and “the greater the unfair surprise or inequality of bargaining power, the less unreasonable the risk allocation which will be tolerated.”  (Ibid.)

The evidence before the court demonstrates Defendant prepared the arbitration agreement, and Plaintiff had no opportunity to negotiate or discuss the terms of the agreement.   It is, therefore, a contract of adhesion (Neal v. State Farm Ins. Companies (1961) 188 Cal.App.2d 690, 694), and this constitutes some evidence of procedural unconscionability.  (Flores v. Transamerica HomeFirst, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 846, 853.)  Plaintiff has not, however, shown the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable so as to be unenforceable.

,Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable because it is not mutual.  That contention is not borne out by a reading and interpretation of the agreement’s terms.  The arbitration agreement at issue applies to claims Defendant has against Plaintiff as well as claims Plaintiff has against Defendant.  Further, while it exempts certain claims from arbitration, the exempted claims include those that only Plaintiff could bring, such as claims for workers’ compensation or unemployment benefits, and claims before the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission.  (Declaration of Patricia Sullivan, Exhibit 1.)  The agreement is mutual.  Plaintiff’s other arguments about substantive unconscionability are specific to arbitration agreements and, therefore, inapplicable under the Federal Arbitration Act.

DPlaintiff contends Defendant has waived the right to enforce the arbitration agreement because Defendant breached the agreement.  Pursuant to the Federal Arbitration Act, the court considers the defense of waiver, rather than the arbitrator.  (Buckeye Check Cashing, Inc. v. Cardegna (2006) 546 U.S. 440, 444-446.)  As Plaintiff points out, the arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant requires mediation prior to arbitration.  The agreement provides, “To initiate mediation, a party must deliver a Request for Mediation within the time period required under the applicable statute of limitations . . . .”  (Declaration of Patricia Sullivan, Exhibit 1.)  However, the evidence presented to this court indicates because Defendant repeatedly asked Plaintiff to participate in mediation, but Plaintiff insisted both his individual and the PAGA claims had to be mediated.   Plaintiff breached the agreement, not Defendant. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate his individual claims is granted.  Plaintiff’s representative claims against Defendant are stayed pending arbitration. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of same.
