DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01618    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: W

WILMA PONY vs WESTFIELD LLC

 

DEFENDANT universal protection services, lp’s motion for relief from waiver of objections

           

Date of Hearing:          May 6, 2024                                        Trial Date:       None set.

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         22VECV01618

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Universal Protection Services LP

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Wilma Pony

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 17, 2022, Plaintiff Wilma Pony filed a complaint against Defendant Westfield LLC for negligence and premises liability. Plaintiff alleges Defendant Westfield negligently maintained and owned certain property in Canoga Park, which created a dangerous condition on the floor of the subject premises. As a result, Plaintiff slipped and fell when she encountered the dangerous condition.  On August 1, 2023, Plaintiff named Universal Protection Service, LP as DOE 1.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant UPS’ Motions for Relief from Waiver of Objections to Plaintiff’s Special and Form Interrogatories, Set One, Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Requests for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Universal Protection Services LP (“UPS”) moves the court for an order granting it relief from the waiver of objections to Set One of Special and Form Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents, and Request for Admissions propounded by Plaintiff Wilma Pony. Defendant UPS makes the motion on the grounds that they have subsequently served responses that are in substantial compliance with the Codes of Civil Procedure and Defendant’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence or excusable neglect.

 

A respondent that misses the 30-day deadline for answering interrogatories, inspection demands, or requests for admission waives all objections to said discovery requests, including objections based on privilege or on the protection for work product.¿(CCP §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a).) However, on motion, the judge may¿set¿aside¿waivers to interrogatories, inspection demands, and/or requests for admission:¿ (1) if the respondent has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with¿the relevant Code of Civil Procedure section for the particular discovery request/procedure; and¿ (2) if the respondent failed to serve a timely response because of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.¿(Id. §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a).)

 

UPS seeks relief from waiver of discovery objections. On October 4, 2023, Plaintiff served a full set of written discovery on UPS, including a first set of Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents, and Requests for Admission. (Marcoly Decl. ¶6.) However, UPS was unable to serve discovery responses at that time due to unforeseeable, extenuating circumstances including severe staffing shortages during the last few months of 2023 at UPS’ counsel’s law firm. (Marcoly Decl. ¶6.) On November 7, 2023, Plaintiff granted an extension to provide those responses by November 21, 2023. (Marcoly Decl. ¶7.) On February 28, 2024, counsel for UPS in this action, spoke with Plaintiff’s counsel on the phone regarding UPS’ responses. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) Mr. Marcoly explained that the responses were due prior to his time with UPS’ counsel’s law firm and that he understood that the law firm had been enduring staffing challenges at the time the responses were due. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.)  The supervising attorney on the file mistakenly believed that the previously handling attorney on this file had timely served responses to the written discovery in question last year. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) The unintended lapse in responding to the discovery prior to Mr. Marcoly taking over this file were due to numerous medical and maternity leaves of absence at the firm as well as some recent attrition, constituting a severe shortage of attorneys who could take over this file and other files. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.)  UPS requested that Plaintiff provide an extension to serve verified responses to Plaintiff’s first set of written discovery, including objections. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) Although Plaintiff granted UPS an extension up to March 13, 2024 to serve verified responses to the outstanding discovery, Plaintiff refused to agree that objections to the discovery requests were not waived. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) ON March 7, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel advised that if verified responses to the discovery at issue are not served by March 13, 2024 without objections, Plaintiff would file a Motion to Compel Discovery Responses. (Marcoly Decl. ¶9.) On March 19, 2024, UPS served verified responses to Plaintiff’s first set of written discovery, with objections. (Marcoly Decl. ¶10.) Because Plaintiff insists that UPS waive its objections to the subject written discovery, UPS has no alternative but to file the instant Motion seeking relief from waiver.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant has deprived them of valuable time and resources all because Defendant failed to provide any response to Plaintiff’s discovery. Plaintiff contends while they are sympathetic towards Defendant’s office affairs, this should not serve as the foundation as to why serving discovery responses six months late should be deemed as “inadvertence or excusable neglect”. Moreover, despite Defendant’s claim that their responses are in substantial compliance, they are not.

 

The court finds UPS has sufficiently demonstrated mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (See Solv-All v. Superior Court (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 1003, 1010 holding the client should be entitled to relief if the attorney admits that the inaction was his responsibility.) Mr. Marcoly, under penalty of perjury, testifies the supervising attorney on the file mistakenly believed that the previously handling attorney on this file had timely served responses to the written discovery in question last year. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) This lapse occurred due to numerous medical and maternity leaves of absence at the firm as well as recent attrition. (Marcoly Decl. ¶8.) Moreover, the responses being served six months after the original due date does not constitute an inexcusable mistake and Mr. Marcoly testifies it was only from February 28, 2024 on did they realize no discovery responses had been provided since the November 7, 2023 extension. (Marcoly Decl. ¶7, 8.) Moreover, the court finds Plaintiff will not be prejudiced by UPS asserting objections to the discovery. The parties are still exchanging written discovery and several depositions must still be noticed and taken. Lastly, Defendant’s responses to the discovery demands appear to be complete and substantially code compliant. If Plaintiff takes issue with the discovery responses, Plaintiff may file a motion to compel further responses.

 

Accordingly, Defendant UPS’ Motions for Relief from Waiver of Objections to Plaintiff’s Special and Form Interrogatories, Set One, Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, and Requests for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV00760    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: W

LILACH BASSON, ET AL. V. KRYSTAL BURNS, ET AL.

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTIONS TO COMEPL FURTHER DISCOVERY

 

Date of Hearing:        May 6, 2024                                                   Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        23VECV00760

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Lilach Basson, Yacov Serazi, and Noam Sherazi

Responding Party:     Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Lilach Basson, Yacov Serazi, and Noam Sherazi, a minor by his Guardian Ad Litem Lilach Basson, filed a complaint against Defendants Krystal Burns, Norma Burns, and Glory Project LLC for assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and interference with tenancy. Plaintiff Yacov leases property from Defendant Glory Project, LLC. Plaintiff Lilach Basson alleges when she was visiting her husband’s office with Noam, their son, Basson was physically and verbally attacked by Defendants Norma and Krystal Burns. Defendant Krystal Burns manages the property.

 

Default was entered against Defendant Norma Burns on April 13, 2023. On April 24, 2023, default was entered against Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC. The court set aside the default entered against Defendants Krystal Burns and Glory Project LLC on June 22, 2023. On December 11, 2023, the court set aside the default against Norma Burns.

 

On January 25, 2024, Krystal and Norma Burns filed a cross-complaint against Yacov Serazi and Lilach Basson for trespass, assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligence, breach of contract, and elder abuse.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Compelling Defendant Krystal Burns’ Further Response to Demand for Production and Inspection is GRANTED

II.                  Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Compelling Defendant Krystal Burns’ Further Response to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED

III.                Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Compelling Responses by Defendant Norma Burns to Plaintiff’s Demand for Production is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER COMPELLING DEFENDANT KRYSTAL BURNS’ FURTHER RESPONSE TO DEMAND FOR PRODUCTION AND INSPECTION

 

Plaintiff Lilach Basson moves this court for an order compelling Defendant/Cross-Complainant Krystal Burns to serve adequate responses and withdraw objections to Plaintiff’s Request for Production and Inspection of Documents, Set Two. Plaintiff also moves for an order requiring Krystal Burns and her attorneys, Daniel Wolfberg, Diana Rivera, and the law firm of Tharpe & Howell, LLP to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $8,700.00.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588.)

 

Plaintiff argues Krystal Bums did not provide a statement of compliance as required by Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(a) in response to Plaintiff’s second set of requests for production of documents and each of her 28 objections was based upon the grounds the requests were “vague, ambiguous, and unintelligible”. Plaintiff contends the discovery plaintiff Lilach Basson served on Krystal Burns was narrow, relevant to the subject matter, and not privileged as the request only sought documents that related directly to any affirmative defense Krystal Burns raised or any allegation she raised in her cross-complaint.

In opposition, Defendant Krystal Burns first argues there was no proper meet and confer. Defendant Krystal Burns contends Plaintiff filed the motion nine days after receiving Defendant’s responses, which indicates a lack of good faith and constitutes an abuse of discovery.

 

Plaintiff has provided a declaration of their meet and confer efforts along with the correspondence between counsel. While the two dispute whether this was sufficient, the court the correspondence makes clear that the parties have reached an impasse as to this issue, the court will address the motion on its merits.  (See Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 332.)   

 

Defendant Krystal Burns opposes the motion on the grounds the discovery responses Plaintiff complains of were the result of discovery directed to Krystal Burns, as opposed to Defendant Krystal Burns or Cross-Complainant Krystal Burns. Defendant Burns has two separate counsel (one to defend and the other to pursue the cross-complaint) and reasonably asserted objections to discovery because Plaintiff did not identify Krystal Burns as either Cross-complainant or Defendant. Moreover, the allegations that Defendant Krystal Burns failed to make proper statements of compliance is incorrect. In response to Plaintiff Basson’s Demand for Production of Documents, Krystal Burns clearly stated that all responsive documents within her possession, custody or control were already produced. 

 

The court finds the responses are not code compliant. Regardless of whether Krystal Burns was in the middle of obtaining new counsel for their cross-complaint, Krystal Burns was properly served discovery and has the capacity to respond to discovery regarding her cross-complaint and affirmative defenses. It is the party who responds, not their attorney. Moreover, the requests are not vague, ambiguous and unintelligible as they are clearly in regards to either Krystal Burns’ allegations in her cross-complaint or her affirmative defenses to Plaintiffs’ complaint and reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. As for Defendant’s contention they have produced all responsive documents in their possession, the parties need to work together to confirm what has been produced and what has not. Lastly, Burns’ objection “All proposed denials and affirmative defenses were protectively pled pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 431.30. Discovery has just begun in this matter and Responding Party (GLORY PROJECT LLC) has limited facts at this time on which to base all denials or affirmative defenses” is a proper objection.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $8,700.00 against Krystal Burns and her attorneys Daniel Wolfberg, Diana Rivera and the law firm of Tharpe & Howell, LLP on the grounds Krystal Burns and her attorneys have displayed contempt for the discovery process and have obstructed and frustrated Plaintiffs’ counsel at every opportunity to obtain legitimate discovery.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues the motion is without merit and brought without substantial justification. The discovery served by Plaintiff is confusing as to whether directed to Defendant or Cross-complainant. Plaintiff’s counsel stated no further extensions would be granted, so Responding Party supplied as much information as possible when the responses to Plaintiff’s to the discovery were made.

The court finds sanctions warranted in the reduced amount of $3,000.00. This includes Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories, discussed below.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER COMPELLING DEFENDANT KRYSTAL BURNS’ FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES

 

Plaintiff Lilach Basson moves this court for an order compelling Defendant/Cross-Complainant Krystal Burns to serve further responses a to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One. Plaintiff also moves for an order requiring Krystal Burns and her attorneys, Daniel Wolfberg, Diana Rivera, and the law firm of Tharpe & Howell, LLP to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $7,850.00.

 

A motion to compel further responses to form or specially prepared interrogatories may be brought if the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2) an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2030.300(a).)    

 

The parties make the same arguments as above. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s special interrogatories is GRANTED. The request for sanctions is granted, but reduced and included in the amount set forth above.   

 

III.                PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER COMPELLING RESPONSE BY NORMA BURNS TO DEMAND FOR PRODUCTION

 

Plaintiff requests an order compelling Defendant Norma Burns to respond to Plaintiff’s Request for Production and Inspection of Documents, Set One for Norma Burns failure to timely respond. Plaintiff also requests sanctions against Norma Burns and her attorney of record, Chritopher J. Lauria to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $4,700.00.

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

On January 26, 2024 plaintiff Lilach Basson served a Request for Production and Inspection of Documents on Norma Burns. (Klein Decl. ¶8, Exh. 2.) On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel granted Normal Burns’ new counsel an extension to respond to this discovery to March 12, 2024. (Klein Decl. ¶¶9-10, Exhs. 3, 4.) Norma Burns never responded to the Request for Production and Inspection of Documents and Norma Burns’ counsel failed to appear at the trial setting conference. (Klein Decl. ¶¶11, 12.) The motion is unopposed.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for an order compelling responses by Norma Burns to Plaintiff’s Demand for Production is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,500.00.
