DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30828    Hearing Date: October 25, 2024    Dept: 73

10/25/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/opposing party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/moving party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION (SET ONE) PROPOUNDED ON CROSS-COMPLAINANT DM COSMETICS AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed on 07/01/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Admission, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics is GRANTED.

 

Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). On August 10, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”) asserting 8 causes of action against Cross-Defendants: (1) Breach of Oral Contract I; (2) Breach of Oral Contract II; (3) Breach of Oral Contract III; (4) Breach of Oral Contract IV; (5) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (6) Trademark Infringement; (7) Unfair Competition in Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq. and California Trademark Law; and (8) Declaratory Relief.

 

The TACC alleges the following. Prior to 2017, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Choi owned Yedam. (TACC, ¶ 11.) Yedam was experiencing serious financial problems. (Ibid.) In February of 2017, Choi approached Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lee asking for help. (Id., ¶ 12.) Choi proposed Lee serve as Yedam’s general manager, whereby Lee would oversee the Korean market and Choi would oversee the Chinese market. (Ibid.) In exchange, Lee would be paid a $10,000 per month salary. (Ibid.) In addition, Choi would give Lee 49% ownership of Yedam if Lee could help Yedam “overcome [its] financial hardship and become profitable." (Ibid.) Lee agreed and moved from the US to Korea to serve as Yedam’s general manager. (Id., ¶ 13.) To help Yedam solve its financial issues, Lee loaned Yedam $70,000.00 in operating capital. (Id., ¶ 14.) Choi promised that the $70,000.00 would be paid back when Yedam become profitable, along with 7% interest per annum from April 1, 2017. (Id., ¶ 12.) Lee successfully managed Yedam thereafter, and Yedam went from net losses in 2016 and 2017 to net profits of over a million dollars per year by the end of 2019. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, in July of 2019, Yedam faced supply chain issues when the “Milk House Candle" company in the United States refused to renew its exclusive distribution contract with Yedam. (Id., ¶ 16.) In response, the Lees created a new brand of merchandise named “Dani Mackenzie" under DM Cosmetics, Inc. (Id., ¶ 17.) The Lees trademarked Dani Mackenzie in the United States in May of 2020. (Id., ¶ 18.) Around that same time, Choi proposed that Yedam purchase Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics in exchange for Yedam having the right to use the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 19.) Choi told the Lees that there was no need to have a written licensing and distribution agreement because Choi would transfer 49% of Yedam’s ownership interest to Lee by the end of December 2020. (Id., ¶ 20.)

 

Dani Mackenzie became highly popular in Korea, and Yedam’s sales and profit soared to the point that Yedam was valued at over $50 million. (Id., ¶ 22.) On December 20, 2020, Lee demanded that Choi transfer 49% of Yedam to him. (Id., ¶ 23.) Lee also demanded that Choi and Yedam repay the $70,000 operating capital loan with 7% interest from April 1, 2017. (Ibid.) However, Choi terminated Lee from Yedam shortly thereafter and refused to convey the 49% ownership share. (Id., ¶ 25.) Further, Choi and Yedam stopped purchasing Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics and started using other suppliers to manufacture products bearing the Dani Mackenzie trade name. (Id., ¶ 28.) On March 18, 2021 and April 14, 2021, Cross-Complainants demanded that Cross-Defendants cease and desist manufacturing, supplying and/or selling products under the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 32.)

 

A.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Admissions (Set One)

 

On July 1, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed the Motion to Compel Further Answers to Requests for Admissions (Set One) against DM Cosmetics, arguing:

·       Cross-Complainant improperly responded by providing a collective objection to all requests without separately stating its responses.

·       Monetary sanctions should be awarded in the amount of $4,522.50.

Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Cross-Defendants’ requests are not code compliant because each request is not full and complete. The set contains “definitions” on two pages which include subparts, compound and/or conjunctive requests.

·       Sanctions should be awarded to DM Cosmetics in the amount of $1,920.

Cross-Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       In Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal. App. 4th 1277 the responding party (to the discovery request) made an objection based on Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060(d). The court in Clement found that Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060 (d) and (f) is to prevent evasion of the statutory limit on the number of interrogatories that one party may propound to another. (Clement, at pages 1287 and 1288.)

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for Cross-Defendants submitted a declaration attesting that the parties discussed the alleged deficient responses via email but an agreement was not reached. (Muriella Decl., ¶¶ 8-11.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

B.     Legal Standard

¿  

A motion to compel further responses may be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)  However, a motion to compel further responses cannot compel the admission of matters already denied.  (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 821.) 

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

C.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admissions (Set One)

 

Cross-Defendants request further responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 1-11.

 

Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics objected to each request, stating:

 

“Objection on the grounds that this set is not in compliance with the Code in that each request is not full and complete, and this set includes subparts, or a compound or conjunctive requests by way of “DEFINITIONS” which include subparts, or a compound or conjunctive requests. CCP Section 2033.060 (d) and (f). Responding Party reserves all of his/her/its right to object on other grounds.

 

CCP Section 2033.060 (d) requires: “Each request shall be full and complete in and of itself. No preface or instruction shall be included with a set of admission requests unless it has been approved under Chapter 17 (commencing with Section 2033.710).” CCP Section 2033.060 (f) provides: “No request for admission shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive request.”

 

While the Court agrees that Cross-Defendants’ requests are not compliant with CCP Sections 2033.060 (d) and (f), the Court does not find that this deficiency warrants denial of Cross-Defendants’ motion to compel further responses. In Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal. App. 4th 1277, 1287-88, the court found that the rule that each specially prepared interrogatory must be “full and complete” by itself (§ 2030.060, subd. (d)), together with the rule that “[n]o specially prepared interrogatory shall contain subparts, or a compound, conjunctive, or disjunctive question” (§ 2030.060, subd. (f)) “prevent evasion of the statutory limit on the number of interrogatories that one party may propound to another.” As the court stated in Clement:

 

“As to the specific provision at issue in this case, the Reporter's Note continues: “Prefaces, Instructions, Definitions, and Sub–Parts. This provision is included to prevent wrangling about whether a party is evading the 35–question limit by using prefaces, instructions, definitions, and sub-parts to exceed the substance of the restriction it imposes. The Commission does not believe that boiler plate interrogatories, prefaces, instructions, definitions, or sub-parts are per se abusive. Instead, it recognizes the need to control use of these devices lest they become the vehicle for evasion of the 35–question limit.”

 

“Plaintiffs do not contend that any of the interrogatories to which they objected on this basis were unclear, or that the interrogatories, considered either singly or collectively, in any way undermined or violated the presumptive numerical limit of 35 interrogatories of section 2030.030. Yet plaintiffs seized on what might have been at most an arguable technical violation of the rule, to object to interrogatories that were clear and concise where the interrogatories did not even arguably violate the presumptive numerical limitation set by statute. In so doing, plaintiffs themselves engaged in the type of gamesmanship and delay decried by the drafters of the Act.”

 

(Ibid., (emphasis in original).) Similarly here, Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics does not argue that the requests for admissions are unclear or violated the presumptive numerical limit. Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that further responses are warranted. Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics is GRANTED.

 

                                                    i.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Both parties request the Court impose monetary sanctions. As discussed above, the Court finds further responses are warranted. However, since Cross-Defendants’ requests were not compliant with CCP Sections 2033.060 (d) and (f), the Court does not find that Cross-Complainant acted without substantial justification in objecting to Cross-Defendants’ requests. The Court declines to impose sanctions.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses to Requests for Admission, Set One, propounded on Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics is GRANTED.

 

Cross-Complainant DM Cosmetics shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied.

GARCIA, et al. v. KIA AMERICA, INC. (23STCV16333)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/ moving party:  Guy Mizrahi (JSGM Law LLP)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Matthew Proudfoot (Gates, Gonter, Guy, Proudfoot & Muench, LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE (filed 3/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 11, 28-29, and 37-78 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Request Nos. 10, 15, 22-26 and 33 is MOOT.

BACKGROUND

 

On July 12, 2023, Plaintiffs Eloy Garcia and Pauline Arredondo filed the instant action against Defendant KIA America, Inc. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.     Breach of Express Warranty under The Song-Beverly Act

2.     Breach of lmplied Warranty of Merchantability under The Song-Beverly Act

3.     Civil Code § 1793.2(b) under The Song-Beverly Act

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about March 16, 2021, Plaintiffs acquired a 2021 Kia Sorento, vehicle identification number 5XYRK4LF2MG020013 (“Vehicle”), for paid and/or payable consideration. All owners and lessees of 2021 KIA vehicles receive multiple express warranties directly from Defendant for periods of up to 10 years or 100,000 miles. The Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiffs with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to loss of power, engine and related systems, check engine light, engine misfire, engine failure, powertrain system, auto stop/start, safety recalls, transmission and related systems, excessive days out of service and other defects. However, Defendant has failed to conform the Vehicle to the applicable warranties because said defects, malfunctions, misadjustments, and/or nonconformities continue to exist even after a reasonable number of attempts to repair was given.

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs move to compel further responses to Request Nos. 10, 11, 15, 22-26, 28-29, 33, 37-78.

·       Plaintiffs requested the following categories of documents: (1) those relating specifically to Plaintiffs and the subject vehicle (Request Nos. 33); (2) those relating to Defendant’s knowledge, internal investigations, analysis and publications of defects plaguing the Vehicle (Request Nos. 15, 28-29, 37-78); and (3) those relating to Defendant’s warranty and vehicle repurchase policies, procedures, and practices (Request Nos. 10, 11, 22-26).

·       The foregoing documents are probative of the existence of engine and other related systems’ defects in 2021 Kia Sorrento vehicles, Defendant’s knowledge of those defects, Defendant’s inability to repair those defects within a reasonable number of attempts and Defendant’s willful noncompliance with its obligations under Song-Beverly.

·       Defendant responded to the requests with boilerplate objections and did not provide any emails, memoranda or studies reflecting investigations into the Vehicle’s defects. Defendant’s responses are not code compliant.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       On June 20, 2024, Defendant produced additional documents such as the Call Matrix, and Service Policy and Procedure Manual.

·       Plaintiffs numerous requests seek documents relating to vehicles of the same year, make and model as the subject vehicle) that contain an undefined and overly broad set of characteristics. Such information has no relevance to Plaintiffs’ claims.

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       Defendant produced further supplemental documents on June 20, 2024. Upon review of these documents, many requests have been remedied. However, there are a number of requests still at issue: Request Nos. 11, 28-29, and 37-78.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a meet-and confer-declaration

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel a further response to a request for production must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the response, or any supplemental response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further response to the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c); see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)  The motion must also be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of any issue presented by it. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).) Finally, a motion to compel further responses to inspection demands must be accompanied by a separate document that sets forth each item to which further response is requested and the factual and legal reasons for compelling it. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a).)  

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel states she sent a meet and confer letters to Defendant's counsel regarding deficiencies with the discovery responses at issue. (Le Decl., ¶¶ 14-19.) However, Plaintiffs’ counsel states Defendant has not provided any further supplemental responses and has failed to produce any additional documents. (Le Decl., ¶ 20.) The Court finds the moving party has met its meet and confer obligation. Accordingly, the Court will consider the merits of the motion.

 

C.     Discussion

 

In Plaintiffs’ Notice of Motion, they assert that Plaintiffs move to compel further responses to Request Nos. 10, 11, 15, 22-26, 28-29, 33, 37-78. However, in Plaintiffs’ reply, Plaintiffs contend that Defendant produced further supplemental documents on June 20, 2024. Upon review of these documents, Plaintiffs state that many requests have been remedied. In Plaintiffs reply, they specify that they only move to compel further responses to Request Nos. 11, 28-29, and 37-78. Thus, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Request Nos. 10, 15, 22-26 and 33 is MOOT.

 

a.     Request No. 11

 

Request No. 11 seeks “A copy of the Workshop Manual specifying diagnosis and repair procedures for vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the SUBJECT VEHICLE.”

 

Defendant objected to this request, including on the grounds that the request is vague, overly broad, unduly burdensome, lacks the requisite specificity, seeks confidential information, seeks documents that are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and is irrelevant.

 

Plaintiffs contend that the document is probative to establish Defendant’s inability to repair Plaintiffs’ vehicle and that it had knowledge of such inability, thereby justifying the imposition of a civil penalty. In this action, Plaintiffs must prove that Defendant failed to repair substantially impairing defects in the subject Vehicle after a reasonable number of repair attempts. (Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to their case. The requested information is relevant to Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly claims. The requests are narrowly tailored, not overbroad and Defendant has not shown that responding to these requests would be unduly burdensome. To show objectionable burden requires a declaration from the responding party specifying each of the tasks required to answer each discovery request and the number of hours required to do those tasks for each discovery request. (Coriell v. Superior Court (1974) 39 Cal. App. 3d 487, 493; Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 761, 764.) To the extent Defendant asserts a privacy privilege, it must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Here, Defendant has not provided a declaration showing undue burden, nor has Defendant provided a privilege log.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further response to request for production of document Nos. 11.

 

b.     Request Nos. 28-29, 37-78

 

Request Nos. 28-29 and 37-78 seek documents relating to Defendant’s knowledge, internal investigations, analysis and publications of defects plaguing the Vehicle.

 

Defendant objects to these requests and argues the information sought is irrelevant. Specifically, that information about other vehicles, concerns and complaints are not relevant and beyond the scope of Plaintiffs’ causes of action.

 

Plaintiffs argue that this information is relevant and may serve as proof of the existence of a defect or nonconformity, as well as Defendant’s knowledge of widespread warranty problems and Defendant’s failure to act despite this knowledge.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to their case. Regarding Defendant’s objections based on undue burden and privilege, as discussed above, Defendant has not provided a declaration showing undue burden nor has Defendant provided a privilege log.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of document Nos. 28-29 and 37-78.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 11, 28-29, and 37-78 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Request Nos. 10, 15, 22-26 and 33 is MOOT.

AISPURO, et al. v. BEVERLY COMMUNITY HOSPITAL ASSOCIATION (24STCV13872)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Stephen Garcia (Garcia & Artigliere)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Rima Badawiya (Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith, LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF THE PERSON MOST KNOWLEDGEABLE OF BEVERLY COMMUNITY HOSPITAL ASSOCIATION AND FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (filed on 09/30/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMK with Production of Documents is GRANTED.

Defendant is ordered to provide for deposition its PMK with the requested documents within 20 days of the hearing on this motion.

Sanctions are denied.

BACKGROUND

 

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiffs Gloria Aispuro, by and through her successor in interest, Jesse Aispuro; Jesse Aispuro; Peter Aispuro; Martha Aispuro and Priscilla Ortiz filed this action against Defendant Beverly Community Hospital Association (“Hospital”) and Does 1-250.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.     Elder Abuse

2.     Negligence

3.     Wrongful Death

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant Hospital neglected decedent Gloria Aispuro while she was a patient in Defendant’s care and she suffered from an avoidable aspiration event when provided solid foods in direct contravention of physician’s orders.

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion for an Order to Compel Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) and request for monetary sanctions, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs move for an order compelling the deposition of the PMK of Beverly Community Hospital Association to take place within 10 days of this hearing and on a date selected by Garcia & Artigliere. Plaintiff also seeks the imposition of sanctions in the amount of $3,860.00

·       Defendant failed to produce a deponent and failed to produce documents as requested at its legally noticed deposition as provided in the Plaintiffs’ lawful notice of deposition, as well as defense counsel’s failure to collaboratively schedule the deposition

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendant is not refusing to comply with discovery. Rather, Defendant is simply asking for some additional time to identify the potential deponent(s) and to locate the responsive documents.

·       Defendant Does Not Have A Person Most Knowledgeable or Custodian of Records to Produce.

·       Defendant’s Objections to Plaintiffs’ Notice of Deposition and Request for Production of Documents are Valid.

·       Defendant Is Obligated to Produce Documents In Its Possession, Custody Or Control, Not Documents to Which it Has “Access.”

·       As Defendant Has Shown “Substantial Justification” for its Inability to Comply with September 24, 2024 PMK Deposition Notice, Sanctions Are Not Warranted.

Plaintiffs filed a reply, maintaining that:

·       It is true that the Defendant was in Bankruptcy, which delayed the litigation of this matter, but it should have been no impediment to the Defendant collecting and safeguarding the documents they knew would be requested in the litigation.

·       Defendant presents to the Court with no documentary support or declaration under penalty of perjury that “Defendant Beverly Community Hospital Association no longer exists, and therefore has no officers, directors, managing agents, employees or agents, and only limited access to documents through management for Adventist Health.” The reality is that the Secretary of State of California reflects that this is still an active entity.

·       To justify its failure to produce a deponent as requested at deposition, Defendant needed to file a protective order or motion to quash the deposition notice. Code of Civil Procedure §§2025.420(a) and 2025.410. Here, the Defendant patently fails to justify its refusal to produce a deponent as to any topic of inquiry and failure to produce a single document requested at its lawfully noticed deposition.

ANALYSIS

A.     Legal Standard

 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.) A party desiring to take an oral deposition shall give a notice in writing which states the specification of reasonably particularly of any materials to be produced by the deponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.220, subd. (a)(4).) A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action … without having served a valid objection … fails to appear for examination, … or to produce for inspection any document, … described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)   

 

A motion brought to compel a deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition … by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiffs have lodged their counsel’s (Stephen Garcia’s) declaration in support of their motion. Mr. Garcia’s declaration states the parties participated in a meet and confer via Zoom on September 24, 2024. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 11.) Accordingly, the court finds that Plaintiffs have satisfied the meet and confer requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (d)(2).

 

C.     Discussion

 

Plaintiffs move for an order compelling the deposition of the PMK of Defendant Beverly Community Hospital Association.

 

On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff served the notice of deposition of Defendant’s PMK. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 7.) The notice of deposition compelled the attendance of the Defendant at deposition on September 24, 2024. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 7.) On September 19, 2024, Defendant served objections to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 8.) Concurrently therewith, defense counsel wrote stating that “we are working on identifying persons who may able [sic] to testify on the topics listed in the deposition notice. We do not have that information at this time but should have it by September 27, 2024.” (Garcia Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

On September 24, 2024, Defendant failed to appear at its lawfully noticed deposition. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 11.) On September 24, 2024, the parties met and conferred, and defense counsel requested a thirty day continuance of the deposition in order to seek a deponent and collect documents. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 12.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states the parties were unable to agree on a continuance and as of the filing of this motion, the Defendant has failed to provide dates for the deposition. (Garcia Decl., ¶¶ 12-13.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel should be denied because Defendant served valid objections to Plaintiffs’ Notice of Deposition. California Code of Civil Procedure 2025.450(a) provides that if a party fails to appear at a deposition noticed and served “without having served a valid objection,” the party seeking the deposition may move for an order compelling the attendance and testimony and production of documents or things. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(a) [emphasis added].) Defendant objected to the matters for examination and requests for document production on the following grounds: vague, ambiguous, overbroad, and privacy rights. Upon review of Plaintiffs’ requests, the Court finds the matters for examination and requests for document production are relevant to the subject matter of the underlying action. In Defendant’s opposition, it contends that Defendant is not refusing to comply with discovery. Rather, Defendant is simply asking for some additional time to identify the potential deponent(s) and to locate the responsive documents. However, the Court notes that Defendant did not file a protective order or motion to quash the deposition notice.

Under Code Civ. Proc., section 2025.450, when a party fails to appear for a properly noticed deposition, the Court may order the party to appear for the deposition.

 

Here, the Defendant failed to comply with a properly noticed deposition for its PMK on the topics in the Plaintiffs’ notice of deposition. Further, the Court did not find that Defendant justified its objections. (See Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.)

 

As a result, there are grounds under Code Civ. Proc., section 2025.450, to order the Defendant to produce its witness and to produce the requested documents. Therefore, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for an order compelling the deposition of the PMK of Defendant Beverly Community Hospital Association to take place within 20 days of this hearing.

 

D.     Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs request monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,860.00 in connection with this motion.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §2025.450(g)(1) states, “[i]f a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with¿Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Cal. Code Civ. Pro. §2025.450(g)(1).) 

 

Although the Court grants Plaintiffs’ motion, the Court finds that Defendant has shown substantial justification for its inability to comply with Plaintiffs’ deposition notice. Defendant asserts that on April 19, 2023, Defendant filed for Chapter 11 Bankruptcy. Defendant further asserts that as a result of the bankruptcy proceedings and the winddown process that is being overseen by the Bankruptcy Trustee, Defendant Beverly Hospital’s assets were sold, including the hospital located at 309 West Beverly Boulevard, Montebello, California. The acquiring company, Adventist Health, took possession of all documents which were relevant to the past and current patients of the hospital, including the documents relevant to Gloria Aispuro and her care and treatment at Beverly Hospital from July 25, 2022 to October 8, 2022. Thus, Defendant contends for the purposes of the subject Deposition Notice, it has no officers, directors, managing agents, employees or agents, and only limited access to documents through management for Adventist Health. Defendant’s counsel states they have been working diligently with members of the Beverly Hospital bankruptcy estate and management at Adventist Health to identify potential deponents for the PMK Deposition and to obtain the requested documents. (Aziz Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds Defendant has shown substantial justification for its inability to comply with Plaintiffs’ deposition notice. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

DISPOSITION

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMK with Production of Documents is GRANTED.

Defendant is ordered to provide for deposition its PMK with the requested documents within 20 days of the hearing on this motion.
