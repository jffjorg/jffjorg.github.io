                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

nico halekakis aka nick j. halekakis, et al.,  

 

                        Defendants.

 

Case No.: 21BBCV00565

 

  Hearing Date:  October 18, 2024

 

 [TENTATIVE] order RE:

motion for an award of post-judgment attorney’s fees

 

 

BACKGROUND

A.    Relevant Background

The trial occurred on May 6 and 7, 2024. 

On August 5, 2024, the Court issued its Final Statement of Decision and entered Judgment in this matter.  Judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiff Christo J. Halekakis and against Defendants Nico Halekakis aka Nick J. Halekakis and Megan T. Lorick Halekakis fka Megan T. Lorick for the amount of $834,718.50.  The Court found Plaintiff to be the prevailing party and stated that Plaintiff may recover attorneys’ fees by motion and costs by Bill of Costs.   

B.     Motion on Calendar

On August 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for an award of post-judgment attorney’s fees pursuant to Penal Code, § 496 in the amount of $256,725 against Defendant Nico Halekakis and Megan Halekakis (“Defendants”). 

On October 4, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition brief.

On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.

DISCUSSION

A.          Entitlement to Attorney’s Fees

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees pursuant to Penal Code, § 496. 

Penal Code, § 496 states in relevant part:

(a) Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170. …

…

(c) Any person who has been injured by a violation of subdivision (a) or (b) may bring an action for three times the amount of actual damages, if any, sustained by the plaintiff, costs of suit, and reasonable attorney's fees.

 

(Pen. Code, § 496(a), (c).) 

Here, there is a statutory basis for attorney’s fees in this action.  Plaintiff is the prevailing party on the 1st cause of action for cancellation of instrument and damages.  The Court awarded treble damages pursuant to Penal Code, § 496 in connection with the 1st cause of action.  Plaintiff was also deemed the prevailing party in the Judgment.

Defendants do not dispute that Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this action or that he is entitled to attorney’s fees pursuant to Penal Code, § 496.  Instead, Defendants argue that Plaintiff is only entitled to “reasonable” fees. 

B.     Reasonableness of Attorney’s Fees

In support of the request for attorney’s fees in the amount of $256,725, Plaintiff provides the declaration of counsel Eric A. Schreiber.

A.    Terminating Sanctions

CCP § 2023.030 permits the Court to impose terminating sanctions for discovery misuses, which are defined by CCP § 2023.010 to include the failure to respond to an authorized method of discovery and the failure to comply with a Court discovery order.  The Court weighs the following factors when considering the present motion: (1) defendants’ conduct, indicating whether their actions were willful; (2) the detriment to the party seeking discovery; and (3) the number of formal and informal unsuccessful attempts to obtain discovery.  (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)  Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.  (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.)

            By way of background, Plaintiffs argue that the RPDs at issue (regarding alter ego relationships) were propounded on August 3, 2023 on Ms. English’s then-counsel, Jennifer Gardner, such that responses were due by September 8, 2023.  Plaintiffs’ counsel granted a 45-day extension for responses.  On October 24, 2023 (beyond the 45-day extension), Ms. English served verified responses, which Plaintiffs’ counsel found inadequate. Plaintiffs’ counsel attempted meet and confer efforts, which were unsuccessful.  Plaintiffs filed a motion to compel further responses on December 8, 2023.  On February 27, 2024, Na’il Benjamin filed a Notice of Appearance for Ms. English and her affiliated companies.  Plaintiffs and Mr. Benjamin engaged in settlement negotiations, such that Plaintiffs stipulated to continue the hearing on the motion to compel further.  On May 31, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ (unopposed) motion to compel Ms. English’s responses to RPD, set one, and ordered Ms. English only to pay sanctions in the amount of $2,060 within 20 days of notice of the Court’s order.  Plaintiffs argue that Ms. English has failed to provide further verified responses to RPD as ordered by the Court and to pay monetary sanctions of $2,060, by June 24, 2024 (which was 20 days after Plaintiffs’ May 31, 2024 e-service of the Court’s order).  Plaintiffs argue that they have attempted several meet and confer attempts with Mr. Benjamin, but he has not offered any times to meet and confer or responded substantively to the meet and confer efforts.

            Plaintiffs argue that terminating sanctions are appropriate because Ms. English and her counsel Mr. Benjamin failed to comply with: (1) the March 15, 2024 Stipulation and Order[1]; (2) the April 22, 2024 Second Stipulation and Order[2]; and (3) the May 31, 2024 Court Order regarding the motion to compel further to produce documents and pay sanctions.   They also argue that there is a history of abuse by Ms. English, such that terminating sanctions are appropriate. 

            In opposition, Ms. English argues that the motion should be continued or denied.  She argues that the motion misrepresents the parties’ settlement efforts and does not consider the bankruptcy proceedings.  She also argues that she needs additional time to file an opposition due to the power outages in Georgia caused by hurricanes and her intent to retain new counsel in California.  (There is a motion to be relieved as counsel filed and scheduled regarding Ms. English for November 1, 2024.  There are also several motions to be relieved as counsel “reserved” for November 1 and 8, 2024.)  Ms. English also argues that Mr. Benjamin should not be sanctioned as he was not counsel at the time the discovery deadline was initially missed, he was not counsel at the time the motion to compel further was filed, and he entered the case and requested a continuance on the motion to evaluate opposition arguments.  Ms. English requests that the Court allow her a full opportunity to be heard on the merits of this motion through new counsel following Mr. Benjamin’s withdrawal. 

            The Court denies Ms. English’s request for a continuance of the motion.  If the motion is continued beyond the hearing date for the motion to be relieved as counsel, it appears unlikely that Ms. English will be able to present any substantive arguments to oppose the motion—she either has facts now or does not.  Further, if she retains new counsel, it is unlikely that her prior counsel would have any personal knowledge as to the events that occurred prior to substituting into the case.  As such, a continuance would only delay the case further.

            However, at this time, the Court declines to impose terminating sanctions against Ms. English.  The Court recognizes that Ms. English has been represented by counsel, has had counsel withdraw, represented herself, and has different counsel in the case.  It is unclear if the delays in producing discovery are the fault of Ms. English’s failure to comply with her discovery obligations or whether this is the fault of her counsel.  Regardless, there has been a delay in Ms. English in providing discovery responses and in complying with the Court order – whether she was represented or was not represented.  The Court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, but will decline to do so at this time.  Instead, the Court will impose issue and/or evidence sanctions in the alternative. 

B.     Issue and Evidence Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030, the Court may impose issue sanctions ordering that designated facts be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.  (CCP § 2023.030(b).)  The Court may impose an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.  (CCP § 2023.030(c).)  Any motion for issue or evidentiary sanctions must be accompanied by a separate statement.  (CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(7).) 

Evidence or issue sanctions may be imposed only after parties violated discovery orders compelling further responses, except in exceptional circumstances, including where there was sufficiently egregious misconduct regarding a failure to respond to discovery, or a prior discovery order would be futile. (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1426.)  Two prerequisites for the imposition of non-monetary sanctions are: (1) there must be a failure to comply; and (2) the failure must be willful.  (Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545.)  The party requesting sanctions under CCP §2023.030 and §2023.010(g) must establish that the other party has failed to comply or disobeyed a court order to provide discovery.  (CCP §2023.010(g); Miranda v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 913, 929 [first requirement of failure to comply].)  To avoid sanctions, the burden of proving that a discovery violation was not willful is on the party on whom the discovery was served. (Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 252- 253.)

Due to Ms. English’s failure to produce responsive documents to RPD Nos. 1-64 regarding the issue of alter ego, Plaintiffs seek issue sanctions establishing the following:

·         The Affiliated Entities failed to maintain meeting minutes. (See RFP 22.)

·          The Affiliated Entities failed to file Statements of Information or have Articles of Organization. (See RFP 23, 34.)

·         The Affiliated Entities shared leases with each other and with Ms. English. (See RFP 28-31.)

·         The Affiliated Entities shared employees and contractors with each other and with Ms. English. (See RFP 32-33.)

·         The Affiliated Entities shared members, shareholders, officers, and directors with each other. (See RFP 36-42.)

(Mot. at p. 11.)  Plaintiffs also seek evidence sanctions against Ms. English from introducing any evidence contradicting any of the issues listed above and precluding Ms. English from relying at trial on any documents responsive to the RPDs that Ms. English has not produced to-date.  (Id. at p.12.)

C.     Monetary Sanctions

CCP § 2023.030(a) states: “The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. The court may also impose this sanction on one unsuccessfully asserting that another has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process, or on any attorney who advised that assertion, or on both. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

The Court took the ex parte application under submission.  On July 8, 2024, the Court issued its ruling, setting it for hearing on July 26, 2024.  The Court ordered Defendant to file and serve an opposition brief by the end of the business day on July 19, 2024.  On July 24, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition to Plaintiff’s ex parte application.  On July 26, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the ex parte application, which the parties did not attend.  The Court continued the hearing on the ex parte application to August 2, 2024.  On August 2, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application and continued the hearing on the motion for summary judgment to October 18, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  The Court ordered Plaintiff to file and serve opposition papers by the end of the business day on October 4, 2024 and ordered Defendant to file and serve reply papers by the end of the business day on October 11, 2024. 

On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed an untimely supplemental opposition brief. As stated in the Court’s order, Plaintiff’s supplemental opposition brief was due by October 4, 2024, and Defendant’s supplemental reply brief was due by October 11, 2024. 

On October 15, 2024, Defendant filed a supplemental reply brief.  Defendant states that she was not served a copy of Plaintiff’s untimely supplemental opposition brief and that Defendant is unable to access a copy of the document on the Court’s website, such that Defendant cannot file a reply brief based on the filed supplemental opposition.  (Def.’s Suppl. Reply at p.3.)  Defendant states that she will make every effort to obtain a copy of Plaintiff’s supplemental opposition and be prepared to make oral arguments at the hearing.  (Id.)  Defendant requests that if the Court is inclined to deny the motion, Defendant be allowed to file a supplemental reply brief after obtaining Plaintiff’s supplemental opposition papers.  (Id. at p.4.)

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            Defendant requests judicial notice of: (1) Plaintiff’s verified complaint filed February 10, 2023; (2) Defendant’s verified answer filed December 27, 2023; (3) the Notice of Ruling filed April 5, 2024 regarding the Motion to Dismiss the 1st cause of action in the initial complaint; (4) the Grant Deed dated June 27, 2007 and recorded on July 20, 2007; (5) the Grant Deed dated October 8, 2008 and recorded on October 15, 2008; (6) a certified copy of Notary Book Entry of Mary Venia for Grant Deed dated October 8, 2008 and recorded on October 15, 2008; and (7) the Grant Deed dated September 16, 2022 and recorded on October 13, 2022.  The request is granted.  (Evid. Code, § 452(d); Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 264-267, disapproved on other grounds.) 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant moves for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication on the remaining cause of action for fraud and the issue of punitive damages in the complaint. 

To allege a cause of action for fraud, the requisite elements are: (1) a representation, usually of fact, which is false; (2) knowledge of its falsity; (3) intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance upon the misrepresentation; and (5) damage resulting from that justifiable reliance.  (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal. App. 3d 59, 72-73.)  The elements for fraudulent concealment are the following: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff; (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.  (Lovejoy v. AT&T Corp. (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 151, 157-158.)  The statute of limitations for a fraud cause of action is 3 years.  (CCP § 338(d).) 

            In support of the motion, Defendant submitted 15 material facts.  The Court notes that while Plaintiff filed an opposition brief, Plaintiff did not file an opposing separate statement.  CCP § 437c(b)(3) states: “The opposition papers shall include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court's discretion, for granting the motion.” (Emphasis added.)  As Plaintiff has not filed a responsive separate statement with the opposition brief, Defendant’s material facts will be deemed undisputed and may constitute a ground to grant the motion outright.  The Court notes that this issue was raised with Plaintiff at the prior hearing on this motion and Plaintiff was given sufficient time and opportunity to file additional opposition papers (including a separate statement) with the continuance requested by Plaintiff, but Plaintiff has failed to file a responsive separate statement.   

            The undisputed material facts are as follows.  On or about June 27, 2007, Defendant purchased real property located at 554 E. San Jose Avenue #204, Burbank, California 91501 (hereinafter “Subject Property”) with her own funds.  (Fact 1; Pl.’s Ex. 4 [6/27/07 First Grant Deed].)  For estate planning purposes, the Subject Property was deeded to Plaintiff and Defendant as “joint tenants.”  (Fact 2.)  Plaintiff did not make any mortgage payments, insurance payments, taxes, HOA fees, or assist in the upkeep or maintenance of the Subject Property at any time.  (Fact 3.)  In or about October 2008, Plaintiff moved out of the Subject Property and requested to be removed from title after a conflict arose between the parties.  (Fact 4.)  On October 8, 2008, the parties deeded the Subject Property to Defendant as a “bonafide gift and the Grantor received nothing in return, E&T 11911.”  (Fact 5; Pl.’s Ex. 5 [10/8/08 Second Grant Deed].)  The Second Deed of Trust was signed by Plaintiff in front of a Notary on October 8, 2008, and Plaintiff’s signature and fingerprint are included in the Notary Book. (Fact 6; Pl.’s Ex. 5, Ex. 6 [10/8/08 Notary Book].)  Despite retaining keys to the Subject Property, Plaintiff did not move back onto the Subject Property at any time.  (Fact 7.)  In the complaint, Plaintiff alleges that she had no knowledge of the Second Deed and never agreed to such a transfer.  (Fact 8, Ex. 1 [Complaint, ¶44].)  Plaintiff alleges that all Defendants had a duty of disclosure to Plaintiff and that all Defendants engaged in illegal and fraudulent conduct in order to deprive Plaintiff of her property rights and to profit from Plaintiff’s loss.  (Fact 9-10, Ex. 1 [Complaint, ¶43].)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant changed the locks to the Property, without notice to Plaintiff, in or about May 2022.  (Fact 11, Ex. 1 [Complaint, ¶44].)  On or about September 16, 2022, the Subject Property was deeded by Defendant to Juvelyn Palomique and Stephen Harrison via Grant Deed. (Fact 12; Ex. 7 [9/16/22 Third Grant Deed].)  On February 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Verified Complaint for (1) Quiet Title and (2) Fraud and on December 27, 2023, Defendant filed a Verified Answer.  (Fact 13-14, Ex. 1, Ex. 2 [Answer].)  On April 5, 2024, the Court dismissed the 1st cause of action for Quiet Title with prejudice as alleged against Defendant because Plaintiff failed to amend the Complaint within the time given after sustaining a demurrer.  (Fact 15, Ex. 3 [4/5/24 Notice of Ruling re Motion to Dismiss].)

            Defendant moves for summary judgment/adjudication on the remaining cause of action for fraud in the complaint, arguing that the material facts are undisputed that there was no fraudulent transfer of the Subject Property on October 8, 2008 as the Second Grant Deed and Notary Book show that Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to the terms of the Grant Deed and Plaintiff even signed and inputted her fingerprint into the Notary Book.  Defendant further argues that she did not owe a duty to Plaintiff to not change the locks as Defendant did not conceal the sale of the Subject Property in 2022 and, by that point, Plaintiff lacked any property rights in the Subject Property.  These facts are supported by Defendant’s separate statement. 

            In addition, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claim is time-barred by the 3-year statute of limitations period for fraud.  As indicated above, Plaintiff executed the Second Grant Deed on October 8, 2008, which is evidenced by the recorded document and the Notary Book.  Thus, Defendant has shown that Plaintiff was on notice of her claim regarding the transfer of the Subject Property by that date, which is evidenced by Plaintiff’s signature and fingerprint in the Notary Book.  Plaintiff filed the complaint on February 10, 2023, which is almost 15 years later and is thus untimely. 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the documents are forged and that she never signed any documents.  Plaintiff argues that she has a right to a jury trial to present her evidence and witnesses, and states that she is saving money to retain an attorney.  However, the time to present opposing evidence and declarations on this motion for summary judgment was at the time her opposition was due.  The Court also granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application for a continuance on August 2, 2024, gave her time to file an additional opposition brief, and set the hearing on this motion for October 18, 2024—such that she had an additional 2 months from the August 2, 2024 date to file supplemental papers.  On October 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed an untimely supplemental opposition brief (she was ordered to file a brief by October 4, 2024).  Even if the brief were considered, Plaintiff has not presented evidence of forgery.  She states that she has retained a court certified forgery and document examiner, Beth Chrisman, to testify verbally for the case and at trial, such that her case must go before a jury, but the Court specifically gave Plaintiff time to present evidence now at the motion for summary judgment stage by granting her a continuance to file supplemental opposition papers.  There is no declaration from Ms. Chrisman and it is unknown what she will testify—whether in Plaintiff’s favor that the documents were forged, or not—such that Plaintiff’s bare representation that she has hired Ms. Chrisman is not sufficient to defeat summary judgment. 

While it is unfortunate that Plaintiff does not have an attorney, the lack of attorney is not a ground to deny a motion for summary judgment.  Even considering Plaintiff’s opposition brief, the only evidence Plaintiff provides is her declaration.  She provides no documentary evidence, nor does she provide a separate statement disputing the material facts presented by Defendant.  Plaintiff’s declaration does not show if and how the documents were forged, and she does not raise any triable issues of material fact regarding the elements of fraud.  Instead, the declaration provides accusations against Defendant without any supporting evidence.  Thus, Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact in her opposition brief.

At the June 28, 2024 hearing, Plaintiff argued that the documents were forged and that the Notary Book had evidence of tampering and white-out.  However, the Court has reviewed the certified copies of the notary book pages.  There is no indication that Plaintiff and Defendant’s signatures were altered.  The signatures are in a colored box and there is no evidence of white-out being used to alter Plaintiff’s signature.  The Court has also reviewed various pages from the evidence to look at Plaintiff’s signature, but Plaintiff’s signature varies such that the Court cannot determine without the aid of a handwriting expert whether any documents were forged.  (See Def.’s Exs. 1 [Complaint], Ex. 5 [10/8/08 Grant Deed signature], Ex. 6 [Notary Book]; Pl.’s Opp. to the MSJ at p.7.)  While Plaintiff raises arguments of forgery at the hearing, she presented no evidence to support her theory and has not retained a handwriting expert to support her opposition arguments at the summary judgment stage despite being given additional time to do so.

Finally, with respect to Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages, this request fails as the underlying cause of action for fraud—upon which the punitive damages request relies—fails. 

For these reasons, the motion for summary judgment is granted in favor of Defendant. Defendant has upheld her initial burden in summary judgment, but Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of material fact in opposition to the motion.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant Sandra D. Santopietro’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication is granted.  Defendant is ordered to lodge with the Court and serve on Plaintiff a proposed judgment within ten (10) days and to provide notice of this order.

Defendant shall give notice of this order.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

Plaintiff Marco Morales (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on June 3, 2022, he visited his local Defendant 7-Eleven, Inc. (“Defendant”) retail store located at 6780 Vineland Avenue, North Hollywood, CA 91606.  He alleges that upon entering, he was approached by Defendant’s employee who became verbally aggressive to Plaintiff and began violently attacking Plaintiff.  Plaintiff alleges that the employee punched Plaintiff’s face approximately 4-5 times with a closed fist.  Plaintiff alleges that he suffered bodily injuries. 

The complaint, filed June 30, 2023, alleges causes of action for: (1) assault/battery; and (2) negligent hiring.

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff named The Eleventh Dimension, Inc. (“TED”) as Doe 1.

B.     Motion on Calendar

On June 27, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment against Plaintiff on the complaint. 

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed opposition papers.

On October 11, 2024, Defendant filed a reply brief.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant moves for summary judgment arguing that it cannot be held vicariously or directly liable the tortious act of the store’s employees because Defendant is a franchisor of the subject store, which is owned and operated by TED, an independent contractor pursuant to the franchise agreement with Defendant (franchisor) and TED (franchisee).  Defendant argues that it lacked any control over the day-to-day operations of the subject store or its employees, the employee was not an agent of Defendant, no agent/employee of Defendant was present at the store at the time of the incident, and it did not hire/employe/supervise the store’s employee. 

As a preliminary matter, in the opposition brief, Plaintiff requests that the Court either deny the motion or continue the motion under CCP § 437c(h) to permit further discovery. 

CCP § 437c(h) states:

If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.

(CCP § 437c(h).) 

Plaintiff provides the declaration of counsel Shantel Yaghoobian.  Counsel states that a continuance is necessary because the parties have not identified the cashier who allegedly assaulted and battered Plaintiff.  (Yaghoobian Decl., ¶¶3-4.)  Counsel states that without this information, it is unknown who trained and supervised the employee, or who is the employee’s employer.  (Id., ¶4.)  Counsel also states that no discovery has been obtained from TED, such that Plaintiff lacks facts regarding TED’s relationship with Defendant, Defendant’s involvement the store’s day-to-day operations, etc.  (Id., ¶5.)  Counsel states that there has been no discovery regarding Defendant’s exercise of discretion to control the store under the Franchise Agreement or its policies, how many trainings were required, how often Defendant changed its operating standards and procedures and design to the franchise, what was included in Defendant’s mandatory standards/specifications/operating procedures, and Defendant’s exercise of its own discretion to modify the manual at any time in its sole discretion as the Operations Manual or attendant documents have not yet been provided.  (Id., ¶¶6-7.)  Counsel states that Plaintiff propounded discovery on Defendant on September 11, 2023.  (Id., ¶8.) 

            Based on the affidavit of Plaintiff’s counsel, the Court finds that a continuance of the motion is appropriate to allow the parties to conduct written discovery, identify the employee of the store at the time of the subject incident, and to schedule depositions.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

Plaintiff Lisa Alfonso (“Plaintiff”) alleges that from 1996 to 2001, she was a victim of domestic violence perpetrated by an LAPD officer and a resultant cover-up of the unlawful behavior by the LAPD.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant City of Los Angeles (“City”) is a municipality and that the Los Angeles Police Department (“LAPD”) is a branch or agency of City.  Plaintiff alleges that from 1996 to 1997, Defendant Officer Anibal Michael Lopez (“Officer Lopez”) sexually assaulted and threatened to sexually assault Plaintiff while he was engaged in his official duties as an LAPD officer.  Plaintiff alleges that LAPD was aware of the sexual harassment and abuse that Plaintiff was subjected to by Officer Lopez, but that LAPD had covered-up its officers’ pattern of domestic violence during internal investigations into such misconduct. 

The complaint, filed January 3, 2024, alleges causes of action for: (1) sexual battery; (2) gender violence; (3) sexual assault; (4) negligence; (5) violation of the Bane Act; (6) violation of the Ralph Act; (7) IIED; (8) NIED; and (9) conspiracy. 

B.     Relevant Background and Motion on Calendar

On September 6, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel the personnel records of Officer Lopez (Pitchess Motion).  The Court ordered the parties to discuss proposed dates regarding when a custodian would be available to review the documents with the Court for an in camera review of the personnel records.  The parties were also ordered to meet and confer regarding the terms of an appropriate protective order and submit a proposed protective order for the Court’s signing.  The Court set a Status Conference re: Scheduling an In Camera Review Date for the Pitchess Motion for October 10, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  The parties were ordered to jointly file a brief by September 26, 2024 proposing several dates of availability of the custodian of record so that the Court could conduct the in camera review of documents. 

On September 23, 2024, City filed a motion for reconsideration of the Court Order and Minute Order re Plaintiff’s Pitchess Motion dated September 6, 2024. 

On September 30, 2024, the parties filed a Joint Status Report.  The parties stated that they could not reach an agreement about the scope of the Court’s order such that City has filed a motion for reconsideration, set for hearing on October 18, 2024.  The parties suggested October 21, 22, 23, 24, 25, 28, 29, or 30, or November 1, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 11, 12, or 13 as potential dates for the in camera review with the custodian of records.  The parties stated that they were working on a proposed protective order.  The parties intend to file another joint status report following the October 18, 2024 hearing. 

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. 

On October 11, 2024, City filed a reply brief.

A Status Conference re: Scheduling an In Camera Review Date for the Pitchess Motion is scheduled for October 24, 2024 at 1:30 p.m. 

LEGAL STANDARD

            CCP § 1008(a) states: “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  (CCP § 1008(a).)

DISCUSSION

            City moves pursuant to CCP § 1008(a) and the inherent powers of the Court for reconsideration of the September 6, 2024 order wherein the Court requested City’s custodian of record to supply the entire or whole personnel file of Officer Lopez.  City argues that the Court should amend its order so that the custodian of records need only furnish the Court with “potentially relevant” portions of Officer Lopez’s personnel file, as opposed to the “whole” personnel file. 

            City argues that the Court should consider different law under People v. Mooc (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1216 and issue an amended order.  City’s counsel Kedar S. Vishwanathan states that after receiving notice of Plaintiff’s Pitchess motion, he diligently researched statutory procedure and cases to oppose Plaintiff’s Pitchess Motion.  (Vishwanathan Decl., ¶4.)  After the hearing on the Pitchess motion and reading the Court’s September 6, 2024 ruling, City’s counsel states that he became concerned with the Court’s order granting the motion and tried to determine if the Court’s language meant that the custodian of records was required to supply Officer Lopez’s entire personnel file and assist with pointing out what was relevant within the entire personnel file during the in camera review.  (Id., ¶11.)  Counsel states that while the Pitchess Motion was under submission, he discovered different case law in Mooc that could be the basis for reconsideration.  (Id., ¶¶13-14.)  He states that he did not provide the Mooc case in the opposition brief because he believed that if the Court were to rule in Plaintiff’s favor on the Pitchess motion, then the statutory limitations under the Evidence Code would apply to prevent the production of the entire personnel file and the Court was already aware of City’s opposition brief requesting that only potentially relevant documents be produced for the in camera review.  (Id., ¶¶15, 18, 22.) 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that City’s arguments merely rehash the same arguments it made in opposing the Pitchess motion and that the Mooc case makes the same point as the Santa Cruz v. Municipal Court (1989) 49 Cal.3d 74 regarding “potentially relevant” documents, which was already presented and argued.  Plaintiff argues that if the motion for reconsideration is granted, then the Court should order the following categories of “potentially relevant” documents be produced: (1) documents memorializing, discussing, mentioning or relating to any allegations, complaints or grievances regarding Officer Lopez; (2) documents memorializing, discussing, mentioning or relating to any disciplinary actions against Officer Lopez; (3) documents memorializing, discussing, mentioning or relating to the hiring of Officer Lopez including but not limited to, background checks conducted on him; (4) documents requested by any entity, party, or individual pursuant to the Public Records Act, regarding any conduct by Defendant Lopez under California Senate Bill 1421.  (Opp. at p.4.) 

           

The Mooc case does not constitute “new or different” law that warrants reconsideration.  The Mooc case was in existence at the time of City’s opposition brief and the hearing on the Pitchess motion, as it is a 2001 case.  (Baldwin v. Home Sav. of Am. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1196 [stating it is not “new” law for purposes of a reconsideration motion when the law existed prior to the court’s ruling on the motion at issue].)  Further, while the Mooc case is a different case that was not presented at the time of the Pitchess motion, it does not offer any different or new legal proposition.  The Mooc cases states: “If the trial court concludes the defendant has fulfilled these prerequisites and made a showing of good cause, the custodian of records should bring to court all documents ‘potentially relevant’ to the defendant's motion.”  (People v. Mooc (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1216, 1226 [citing to City of Santa Cruz v. Municipal Court 1989] 49 Cal.3d 74, 84 and Evid. Code, § 1045).)  The Court notes that the Santa Cruz case and the Evidence Code cited by Mooc were previously raised by the parties and considered by the Court when ruling on the initial Pitchess motion.

            The motion for reconsideration is denied.  The Court intends to review the entire personnel file of Officer Lopez with the aid of the custodian of records to in order to determine what information is potentially relevant and discoverable.  If City intends to withhold production of documents at the in camera review, City is ordered to provide a specific description of such documents. The Court finds it a little difficult to believe that the City has a privilege relating to documents in Officer Lopez’s file, but if such a privilege is asserted, then a privilege log, which describes the documents under oath and the privilege claimed so that the Court may determine whether the document should be withheld based on privilege and should be reviewed or not.  

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant City of Los Angeles’ motion for reconsideration of the Court Order and Minute Order re Plaintiff’s Pitchess Motion dated September 6, 2024 is denied.  The Court intends to review the entire personnel file of Officer Lopez with the aid of the custodian of records to in order to determine what information is potentially relevant and discoverable.  If City intends to withhold production of documents at the in camera review, City is ordered to provide a specific description of such documents. If the City asserts any privileges, then a privilege log, which describes the documents under oath and the privilege claimed so that the Court may determine whether the document should be withheld based on privilege and should be reviewed or not.  

Defendant shall provide notice of this order.
