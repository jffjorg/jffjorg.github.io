DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00777    Hearing Date: October 16, 2023    Dept: W

VEDRES FAMILY INVESTMENT PARTNERSHIP, LP v. PAYAM MAROUNI MD, INC.

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2023                              Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00777

 

Moving Party:             Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP

Responding Party:     Cross-Complainant Payam Marouni MD, Inc.  

Meet and Confer:      Yes.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 8, 2022, Plaintiff Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP filed an unlawful detainer action against Defendant Payam Marouni MD, Inc. Plaintiff’s request for entry of default was entered against All Unknown Occupants in Possession on August 3, 2022. Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint naming Defendant Payam Marouni on October 31, 2022; however, Defendant Marouni was dismissed on February 22, 2023.

 

On February 24, 2023, Defendant Payam Marouni MD, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP and Quest Screen for (1) Conspiracy to Interfere with Prospective Economic Advantage (2) Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations and (3) Negligent Interference with Contractual Relations.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

 

ANALYSIS

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment Partnership, LP (“Vedres Family Investment”) demurs to the First Amended Complaint of Payam Marouni, MD, Inc. on the grounds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a cause of action because it fails to plead facts which, if true, would give rise to a cause of action.

 

First Cause of Action

 

Cross-Defendant Vedres Family Investment demurs to the first cause of action on the grounds the claim fails to allege an independent wrongdoing or other basis of liability. Conspiracy is not itself an independent claim; it allows for recovery against some other party based upon some other, independent tort action.

 

“‘A civil conspiracy, however atrocious, does not give rise to a cause of action unless a civil wrong has been committed resulting in damage.’”  (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 511.)

 

Here, the civil conspiracy appears linked to Cross-Complainants’ interference with prospective economic advantage claim. The elements of the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage are: (1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff, (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship, (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship, (4) actual disruption of the relationship, and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant. (Marsh v. Anesthesia Services Medical Group, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 480, 504, citing Korea Supply Company v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1153.) In Korea Supply, “the court clarified that the third element of the tort of interference with prospective economic advantage ‘also requires a plaintiff to plead intentional wrongful acts on the part of defendant designed to disrupt the relationship.’” (Marsh, 200 Cal.App.4th at 504.)

 

As discussed below in more detail, Cross-Complainants have not alleged an independent wrongful act. Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Second and Third Causes of Action

 

Cross-Defendant first demurs to the FAXC on the grounds it fails to allege an economic relationship with a cognizable third party that Cross-Defendant interfered with.

 

“The tort[s] of intentional or negligent interference with prospective economic advantage impose[] liability for improper methods of disrupting or diverting the business relationship of another which fall outside the boundaries of fair competition.” (Golden Eagle Land Investment, L.P. v. Rancho Santa Fe Assn. (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 399, 429.) “The elements of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage are ‘(1) the existence, between the plaintiff and some third party, of an economic relationship that contains the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant's knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentionally wrongful acts designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm proximately caused by the defendant's action.’ [Citations.]” (Redfearn v. Trader Joe's Co. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 989, 1005.)

 

Cross-Complainant alleges that the conduct of the Cross-Defendant interfered with a general market for COVID-19 tests. Cross-Complainant argues the cross-complaint, however, fails to allege a tangible, existing relationship with ascertainable third parties beyond a general class of consumers who might be interested in such tests. Accordingly, the Cross-Complaint fails to adequately allege the first element of prospective interference with an economic relationship.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainant argues Plaintiff need not allege an actual or inevitable breach of contract in order to state a claim for disruption of contractual relations. The court disagrees. Allegations regarding interference with potential customers are insufficient to state a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. (See Korea Supply Co., supra, 29 Cal.4th at 1164 (stating that the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage “‘protects the expectation that the relationship eventually will yield the desired benefit, not necessarily the more speculative expectation that a potentially beneficial relationship will arise” (quoting Westside Center Associates v. Safeway Stores 23, Inc. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 507, 524); Roth v. Rhodes (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 530, 546 (finding that an allegation that the defendant interfered with relations with future customers is insufficient to show an existing relationship).) Moreover, the cross-complaint contains no allegations related to any contracts between the Cross-Complainant and a third party.

 

Cross-Defendant further argues Cross-Complainant fails to allege an independent wrong. Specifically, Vedres Family Investment contends Cross-Defendant alleges that the Cross-Complainant leased space it owned to an adjacent tenant, and that that tenant set up a tenant to conduct COVID-19 testing. However, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege any determinable legal standard as to why this conduct is independently wrong.

 

In order to plead intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, a plaintiff has to plead facts showing that Defendants engaged in an independently wrongful act. (See Korea Supply Co., supra, 29 Cal.4th at 1158.) “An act is not independently wrongful merely because defendant acted with an improper motive.” (Id.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Id. at 1159.) 

 

Cross-Complainant does not address this point. Upon review of the cross-complaint, the court finds the cross-complaint does not allege an independent wrong. Cross-Complainant’s allegations that Vedres intentionally worked with Quest in the adjacent property to allow Quest to also conduct COVID-19 testing in the parking lot to confuse Cross-Complainant’s patients is not a wrong that is unlawful.

 

Lastly, Cross-Defendant argues in California, there is no cause of action for negligent interference with contractual relations. (See Davis v. Nadrich (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1, 9.) The court agrees.

 

Accordingly, the court sustains the demurrer to the second and third causes of action WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

LAUREN ELIZABETH BAHORICH, et al. v. PETROS GHARIBYAN, et al.

 

defendants Petros gHaribyan and vazrik gHaribyan’s MOTION TO STAY THE CIVIL PROCEEDING DUE TO THE PENDING CRIMINAL CASE and motion to strike portions of plaintiff’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2023                               Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:         23SMCV01521         

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Petros Gharibyan

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Lauren Elizabeth Bahorich

 

BACKGROUND

 

This lawsuit arises out of an accident that occurred on February 26, 2022 wherein Plaintiff Lauren Elizabeth Bahorich alleges Defendant Petros Gharibyan negligently drove a BMW owned by his father, Vazrik Gharibyan, and collided with Plaintiffs’ vehicle. On April 7, 2023, Plaintiff Bahorich filed a complaint against Petros Gharibyan, Vazrik Gharibyan, County of Los Angeles, and State of California, acting by and through the California Department of Transportation, for negligence, dangerous condition of public property, and punitive/exemplary damages claim.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Defendants Motion to Stay the Civil Proceeding Due to the Pending Criminal Case is GRANTED, IN PART.

II.                  Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                    MOTION TO STAY THE CIVIL PROCEEDING DUE TO THE PENDING CRIMINAL CASE

 

Defendants Petros Garibyan and Vazrik Garibyan move to stay the instant civil proceedings on the ground criminal charges have been filed against Defendant Petros Gharibyan involving the same accident and are currently pending. Defendant Petros Gharibyan contends he is an integral witness in this action, and as he is not waiving his Fifth Amendment rights, Defendants request that the court stay this case until the criminal case is completed.

 

Plaintiff Bahorich filed a notice of joinder to Plaintiff Shloss’ opposition in the related/lead case No. 23VECV00978. Plaintiff Bahorich states her interests are identical to that of Plaintiff Shloss’. Plaintiff Bahorich further contends a delay in the subject civil suit will prejudice the Bahorich in the same manner as laid out in the Opposition Papers filed by Plaintiff Shloss.

 

For reasons stated in the court’s order in Sophia Shloss v. Petros Gharibyan, et al. (23VECV00978), Defendants’ motion is GRANTED IN PART.

 

II.                  DEFENDANTS PETROS GHARIBYAN AND VAZRIK GHARIBYAN’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

 

Defendants Petros Garibyan and Vazrik Garibyan move the court for an order striking the following portions of Bahorich’s complaint: Paragraphs 62, 64, and Paragraph 17 of the Prayer for relief on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to plead specific facts sufficient to establish that Defendants Gharibyans acted with malice, as required under California law to state a claim for punitive damages.

 

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code §3294(a).) 

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice ....” (Civ. Code §3294(a).)  “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code §3294(c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code §3294(c)(2).) “‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  (Civ. Code §3294(c)(3).) 

 

Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95.)  Malice is properly pleaded by alleging the wrongful motive, intent or purpose. A general allegation of that intent is sufficient. (Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal. App. 3d 306, 316–17.)  Specific facts must be pleaded in support of punitive damages. (See Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-92.) 

 

Defendants first argue Plaintiff’s allegations are insufficient to establish that Defendant Vazrik Gharibyan had a conscious disregard or that he knew that injury would occur. At best, Defendant Vazrik Gharibyan was negligent in allowing Defendant Petros Gharibyan to operate the vehicle, but this is insufficient for punitive damages.  As for Defendant Petros, Defendants argue there are no facts pled, nor could there be, that he intended to harm Plaintiff, or that he acted in “conscious disregard” of others’ rights, including Plaintiffs. Specifically, Plaintiff’s allegations Defendant Petros was driving recklessly and that he was unable to negotiate a curve as well as ran a red light and was driving at an excessive speed reaching over 100 MPH at best involves simple negligence or at most, gross negligence or recklessness but does not amount to an evil motive or conscious disregard for the safety of others. 

 

In support of their contention, Defendants primarily rely on two cases: Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890 and Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188. Taylor held that, “[i]n order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis [conscious disregard of the safety of others], the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor, supra, 24 Cal.3d at 895-896.) Defendants argue this action is distinguishable from Taylor because there are no allegations of prior accidents of recklessness involving injury and in fact and this is not an alcohol related accident. Rather, this action is more similar to Lackner. In Lackner, the court found “the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Lackner, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at p. 1211.) Defendants contend like Lackner, although it is alleged that Defendant’s conduct may have been careless or reckless, it does not warrant punitive damages.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff Bahorich argues their allegations that Defendant Petros was driving erratic and reckless at excessive speeds, purportedly ran a red light, and was overall driving unsafe are sufficient to support a claim for damages. Plaintiff Bahorich’s opposition also states Defendant Petros was arrested and charged with two criminal counts for “murder” and “reckless driving”. Plaintiff Bahorich also argues punitive damages are appropriate for punishment and deterrence.

 

Based upon the discussion set forth above, the court finds that the allegations contained in Plaintiff Bahorich’s complaint regarding Defendant Petros’ erratic driving at a high-speed rate including running a red light and Defendant Vazrik’s negligent entrustment of the vehicle to Defendant Petros are insufficient to support the imposition of punitive damages. Although Plaintiff Bahorich does allege facts regarding Defendant Petros’ alleged reckless driving by driving at erratic high speeds, this behavior and Defendant Vazrik’s knowledge of Petros’ prior record for reckless driving – without more – has the character of ordinary negligent driving and does not show aggravating circumstances warranting punitive damages.  The court notes Plaintiff makes arguments in her opposition that are not alleged in the complaint. These “facts” are not within the four corners of the complaint and are not considered by the court.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

SOPHIA SHLOSS v. PETROS GHARIBYAN, et al.

 

defendants Petros gHaribyan and vazrik gHaribyan’s MOTION TO STAY THE CIVIL PROCEEDING DUE TO THE PENDING CRIMINAL CASE, motion to strike portions of plaintiff’s complaint, and motion to consolidate cases

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2023                                           Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        23VECV00978

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Petros Gharibyan

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Sophia Shloss

 

BACKGROUND

 

This lawsuit arises out of an accident that occurred on February 26, 2022 wherein Plaintiff Sophia Shloss alleges Defendant Petros Gharibyan negligently drove a BMW owned by his father, Vazrik Gharibyan, and collided with Plaintiffs’ vehicle. On March 6, 2023, Plaintiff Shloss filed a complaint against Petros Gharibyan, Vazrik Gharibyan, City of Agoura Hills, and County of Los Angeles, State of California for motor vehicle, general negligence, and premises liability.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Defendants Motion to Stay the Civil Proceeding Due to the Pending Criminal Case is GRANTED, IN PART.

II.                  Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

III.                Defendants’ Motion to Consolidate Cases is DENIED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.                    MOTION TO STAY THE CIVIL PROCEEDING DUE TO THE PENDING CRIMINAL CASE

 

Defendants Petros Garibyan and Vazrik Garibyan move to stay the instant civil proceedings on the ground criminal charges have been filed against Defendant Petros Gharibyan involving the same accident and are currently pending. Defendant Petros Gharibyan contends he is an integral witness in this action, and as he is not waiving his Fifth Amendment rights, Defendants request that the court stay this case until the criminal case is completed.

 

In deciding whether to stay proceedings when a civil defendant faces parallel criminal charges, courts engage in a two-step process. First, the decision should be made “‘in light of the particular circumstances and competing interests involved in the case’ . . . This means the decision maker should consider ‘the extent to which the defendant’s fifth amendment rights are implicated.’ [Citation.]” (Keating v. Office of Thrift Supervision (9th Cir. 1995) 45 F.3d 322, 324.) Second, the court should consider: “(1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.” (Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885; Keating, supra, 45 F.3d at p. 325.) “[I]t has been consistently held that when both civil and criminal proceedings arise out of the same or related transactions, an objecting party is generally entitled to a stay of discovery in the civil action until disposition of the criminal matter.”  (Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 686, 690.) 

 

Defendant Petros Gharibyan is currently facing two charges in Los Angeles County Superior Court, Case No. LA097176. A complaint was filed on July 1, 2022 for two counts: Penal Code §187(A) (murder) and Vehicle Code §23101(A) (reckless driving) for which he has pleaded not guilty. There is no doubt Defendant Petros Gharibyan’s Fifth Amendment rights would be implicated in the instant civil action. As discussed further below, the court finds the Avant! and Keating factors weigh in favor of staying the civil action as to Defendant Petros Gharibyan only. Discovery may continue against Defendant Vazrik Gharibyan.

 

Defendants first argue there is little to no prejudice to Plaintiffs in issuing the stay. All of the individuals involved in this accident are relatively young and are all under the age of 25. Moreover, the accident occurred only about a year ago, on February 26, 2022. Defendants contend Plaintiff will have their opportunity to conduct discovery and litigate this matter, but only after the criminal case is completed. Defendants also argue unless this civil action is stayed, he would be forced to choose between potentially having a judgment entered against him by not being able to participate in and defend the present case or sacrificing his Constitutional right against self-incrimination. Defendants also note they are only attempting to streamline the litigation in light of the criminal matter and protect his Fifth Amendment rights. If the court does not issue a stay, there will likely be a plethora of law and motion work on this case regarding the scope of depositions and written discovery.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff Shloss argues placing a hold on the entire civil proceeding is unnecessary and would prejudice the Plaintiff in this matter. In light of the corresponding criminal proceeding, Plaintiff contends she should still be able to engage in discovery that would not infringe upon Defendant Petros Gharibyan's Fifth Amendment right. For instance, Plaintiff contends she should still be able to request information pertaining to the Defendants’ applicable insurance policy at the time of the subject collision and police department records. Plaintiff Shloss further argues by engaging in narrow discovery, little to no burden would be placed on Defendant Petros. The fact that Defendant Petros is involved in an ongoing criminal matter does not shield the other Defendant or witnesses from complying with Plaintiff’s discovery requests. Plaintiff Shloss lastly notes a delay would risk witnesses’ ability to testify truthfully and accurately and the public has an interest in ensuring that injured parties who file lawsuits against tortfeasors will be guaranteed a fair and efficient forum in which they can either bring their case to trial.

 

It is unclear at this time what the status of Defendant Petros’ criminal proceeding is. In reply, Defendant Petros notes a pre-trial conference has been scheduled in Defendant Petros Gharibyan’s criminal matter for October 12, 2023. Regardless, Defendant Petros’ Fifth Amendment rights are implicated as of the pending criminal proceedings against him arise from the same set of facts as those in Plaintiff Shloss’ civil action. And while Defendant Petros could assert her Fifth Amendment rights in discovery, the court finds that, at this time, fairness weighs in favor of staying the action and discovery as to Defendant Petros.

 

As for Defendant Vazrik, Defendants contend discovery should be halted as well because it is already difficult and complicated to try to delineate certain discovery that may or may not impede Defendant Petros Gharibyan’s Fifth Amendment rights. If part of the case were to proceed while other parts are stayed, Defendants argue it is anticipated that there will be many motions or requests made to the court to clarify what discovery can be done for certain parts of the case. The court disagrees. Plaintiff has an interest in expeditious litigation and Defendant Petros does not provide an estimate for when his criminal proceedings will be disposed of. If Defendants object to certain discovery requests or deposition questions on Fifth Amendment grounds, the court will conduct a particularized inquiry with respect to each specific claim of privilege to determine whether the privilege applies. (See Siry Investment, L.P. v. Farkhondehpour (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 1098, 1124.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Stay the Civil Proceeding Due to the Pending Criminal Case, is GRANTED IN PART. The case is stayed as to Defendant Petros, but not as to Varik.  Petros may participate in depositions noticed by plaintiff or Varik and ask questions and may receive copies of discovery between those parties.  Petros, however, cannot issue discovery requests nor does he need to respond to any at this time. 

 

II.                  DEFENDANTS PETROS GHARIBYAN AND VAZRIK GHARIBYAN’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

 

Defendants Petros Garibyan and Vazrik Garibyan move the court for an order striking the following portions of Sophia Shloss’ complaint: Paragraph 14(a)(2) requesting punitive damages in the prayer for relief; and Exemplary Damages Attachment attached as page 9 to the complaint.

 

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code §3294(a).) 

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice ....” (Civ. Code §3294(a).)  “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code §3294(c)(1).) “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code §3294(c)(2).) “‘Fraud’ means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  (Civ. Code §3294(c)(3).) 

 

Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95.)  Malice is properly pleaded by alleging the wrongful motive, intent or purpose. A general allegation of that intent is sufficient. (Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal. App. 3d 306, 316–17.)  Specific facts must be pleaded in support of punitive damages. (See Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-92.) 

 

Defendants argue the complaint contains buzz words rather than sufficient allegations or any aggravating factors sufficient to support a recovery of punitive damages. Defendants contend Plaintiff simply has not and cannot allege sufficient facts to support a finding of malice, oppression or fraud, wherein this case amounts to a general negligence claim. Moreover, there are certainly insufficient allegations regarding the registered owner Defendant Vazrik Gharibyan to support the punitive damages claim. In support of their contention, Defendants primarily rely on two cases: Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890 and Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188.

 

Taylor held that, “[i]n order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis [conscious disregard of the safety of others], the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor, supra, 24 Cal.3d at 895-896.) Defendants argue this action is distinguishable from Taylor because there are no allegations of prior accidents of recklessness involving injury and in fact and this is not an alcohol related accident. Rather, this action is more similar to Lackner. In Lackner, the court found “the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Lackner, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at p. 1211.) Defendants contend like Lackner, although it is alleged that Defendant’s conduct may have been careless or reckless, it does not warrant punitive damages.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff Shloss argues Defendants' reliance on Lackner v. North is misplaced. Plaintiffs contend there is no evidence which would suggest that Defendant Petros Gharibyan's actions were unintentional or that he made any attempt to avoid the crash. Additionally, there is no evidence which would suggest that Defendant Petros was in control as he turned a sharp corner, going twice the posted speed limit, on a two-lane highway on that fateful night. Moreover, Lackner was decided at the summary judgment stage. As for Taylor, Plaintiffs argue Taylor does not stand for the proposition that punitive damages are only reserved for auto collisions where the defendant was intoxicated. In fact, the court in Taylor made it a point to avoid making such a broad ruling for all auto cases. Moreover, like Taylor, Defendant Petros’ history of reckless driving is alleged in Plaintiffs complaint. As for Defendant Vazrik, Plaintiff Shloss argues Plaintiff's complaint alleges that Defendant Vazrik knew of Defendant Petros’ history of reckless driving and still gave him permission to drive a sports car equipped with the ability to drive at an extremely high rate of speed. This is sufficient to show Defendant Vazrik acted with conscious disregard for the rights and safety of others.

 

Based upon the discussion set forth above, the court finds that the allegations contained in Plaintiff Shloss’ complaint regarding Defendant Petros’ erratic driving at a high-speed rate and Defendant Vazrik’s negligent entrustment of the vehicle to Defendant Petros are insufficient to support the imposition of punitive damages. Although Plaintiff Shloss does allege facts regarding Defendant Petros’ alleged reckless driving by driving at erratic high speeds, this behavior and Defendant Vazrik’s knowledge of Petros’ prior record for reckless driving – without more – has the character of ordinary negligent driving and does not show aggravating circumstances warranting punitive damages. 

 

However, the court believes Plaintiff’s proposed allegations of Defendant Petros driving more than twice the posted speed limit around a sharp corner, running a red light before the subject crash, a previous arrest for reckless driving, and Defendant Vazrik’s knowledge of Petros’ arrest for reckless driving would show that the risk of injury was probable and warrant a request for punitive damages. 

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

III.                DEFENDANTS PETROS GHARIBYAN AND VAZRIK GHARIBYAN’S MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE CASES

 

Defendants Petros Gharibyan and Vazrik Gharibyan move the court for an order to consolidate the following three actions for all purposes: Sophia Shloss v. Petros Gharibyan, et al. (23VECV00978); Lauren Elizabeth Bahorich et al. v. Petros Gharibyan, et al. (23SMCV01521); and Stacie Riley as successor in interest of Skylar Scripter (Decedent) v. Petros Gharibyan, et al. (23VECV02092). Defendants bring the motion on the grounds that all three actions involve common questions of law or fact and are pending in the same court.

 

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court … it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (CCP §1048(a).) The purpose of consolidation is to enhance trial court efficiency by avoiding unnecessary duplication of evidence and the danger of inconsistent adjudications. (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-979.)  There are two types of consolidation under section 1048: “a consolidation for purposes of trial only, where the two actions remain otherwise separate; and a complete consolidation or consolidation for all purposes, where the two actions are merged into a single proceeding under one case number and result in only one verdict or set of findings and one judgment.” (Hamilton v. Asbestos Corp. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1127, 1147.) 

 

“Cases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related into a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.” (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 3.3(g)(1).)  A Notice of Motion to consolidate cases must (1) include a list of all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; (2) include the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated; and (3) be filed in each case sought to be consolidated. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(1).)    

 

The court notes Defendants’ motion fails to comply with Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(1)(C). No notices were filed in Riley’s or Bahorich’s actions. Accordingly, Defendants’ motion is denied without prejudice. Defendant may seek consolidation again once properly complied with all applicable statutes and rules.


Case Number: 23VECV01866    Hearing Date: October 16, 2023    Dept: W

YANIRA GARCIA FIGUEROA V. JOSE CASTANEDA

 

SPECIALLY APPEARING DEFENDANT JOSE CASTANEDA’S  MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS BASED ON PERSONAL JURISDICTION

 

Date of Hearing:        October 16, 2023                              Trial Date:       None set   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01866

 

Moving Party:            Specially Appearing Defendant Jose Castaneda

Responding Party:     No opposition  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wrongful eviction case. On April 28, 2023, Plaintiff Yanira Garcia Figueroa filed a complaint against Defendant Jose Castaneda for (1) Negligent Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability; (2) Intentional Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability; (3) Nuisance (Negligence); (4) Nuisance (Intentional); (5) Negligence; (6) Intentional Infliction of emotional Distress; (7) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Quiet Use and Enjoyment; (8) Negligent Violation of Statutory Duty; (9) Intentional Violation of Statutory Duty; and (10) Constructive Eviction.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Specifically Appearing Defendant Jose Castaneda’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons Based on Personal Jurisdiction is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Specially Appearing Defendant Jose Castaneda, for the sole purpose of challenging jurisdiction, moves the court for an order pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10(a)(1) granting his Motion to Quash Service of Summons based on personal jurisdiction.

 

“A defendant . . . may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes:  (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her. . . .”  (CCP § 418.10(a).)  A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process. (See Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.) “[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.) “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper” but only if it “complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.” (Id. at 1441-1442.) When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.)

 

Castaneda argues Plaintiff failed to serve the complaint on Mr. Castaneda under any of the five methods enumerated under the Code of Civil Procedure: (1) personal service, (2) substituted service, (3) through a Notice of Acknowledgement and Receipt, (4) through publication, or (5) by posting. (See CCP § 417.10.) Specifically, Castaneda contends he was never personally served and only became aware of this litigation by receiving certified mail from Plaintiff containing a copy of the summons and the complaint on or about July 8, 2023. (Castaneda Decl. ¶¶2-3.) Moreover, there is no evidence Plaintiff adequately accomplished substituted service or accomplished service by Notice of Acknowledgment and Receipt, publication, or posting.

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition. Rather, Plaintiff filed a proof of service. However, the court finds the proof of service does not sufficiently establish the statutory requirements were satisfied. The proof of service states Castaneda was served by mail and acknowledgment of receipt of service. However, Castenada, under penalty of perjury, attests the envelope did not contain a Notice of Acknowledgment and Receipt. (Castaneda Decl. ¶2.) Plaintiff’s proof of service fails to include a signed acknowledgment of receipt per Code of Civil Procedure section 415.30(c).

 

Accordingly, Specifically Appearing Defendant Jose Castaneda’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons Based on Personal Jurisdiction is GRANTED.
