Case Number: 21STCV18145    Hearing Date: September 6, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 September 6, 2023

¿¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   21STCV18145

¿¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        David Zeil v. DOES 1-100

¿¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff, David Zeil  

¿¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant, Southwest Airlines Co.

 

TRIAL DATE:                        September 22, 2025

¿¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Order Compelling Defendant to Provide Further Responses to Inspection Demand No. 56, Set Two

                                                (2) Request for Monetary Sanctions 

                                               

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) Motion for Order Compelling Defendant to Provide Further Responses to Inspection Demand No. 56, Set Two is MOOTED.

                                                (2) Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED in the lowered amount of $1,068.40.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On May 14, 2021, Plaintiff, David Zeil (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants DOES 1 through 100. Plaintiff eventually learned of the identity of more Defendants including Southwest Airlines Co, and Ms. Manu. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Products Liability; and (2) General Negligence.

 

Because the Court addressed a similar discovery motion yesterday and posted a detailed tentative ruling, this tentative will be truncated. 

 

On May 1, 2024, Plaintiff contends that he served Requests for Admission, Set Two, Corresponding Form Interrogatory No. 17.1, and Inspection Demands, Set Two.   Plaintiff states that Defendant, Southwest Airlines Co.’s (“Defendant”) responses were due on or before June 5,  but no responses were provided by Defendant.  Instead, defense counsel asked for a two-week extension to respond to discovery.  A dispute arose as to conditions to be imposed on the requested extension because of the pendency at that time of the defense motion for summary judgment and Plaintiff’s desire to obtain the responses before the deadline to file opposition tot the MSJ.  The parties failed to resolve the dispute over condition so on June 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed motions to compel responses to the May 1 discovery.  The parties agree that belated responses were served on June 24, 2024, while the discovery motions were pending. 

 

            At the hearing on July 18, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s request for discovery sanctions but found the rest of the motion mooted by Defendant’s further discovery responses.  Plaintiff asserts that Defendant’s proposal regarding the further responses did not address the issues noted in Plaintiff’s June 24, 2024 meet and confer letter. Since that hearing, Plaintiff contends that Defendant has not provided the above promised further responses to Plaintiff.

 

Thus, Plaintiff has filed this Motion to Compel Defendant’s Further Responses to Inspection Demand No. 56.

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On August 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Compel Further Responses to Inspection Demand No. 56. On August 26, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition brief as to both motions. On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief. 

 

II. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

 A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

B.    Discussion

 

As noted in this Court’s previous minute order regarding Form Interrogatory 17.1 and Motion to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted, Defendant’s opposition brief confirms that prior to the filing of the opposition, Defendant submitted its unqualified admissions to each and every Request that is subject to the Motion, thereby negating the Inspection Demand and Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 corresponding to said Requests for Admission, and mooting all motions. The Court notes that this is true, but only as to the substantive portions of the motion. Because Defendant confirms that the responses only came after the filing of the motion, the costs of Plaintiff filing the motion were still taken on by Plaintiff in order to get said responses/admissions. As such, the Requests for Monetary Sanctions are not mooted.

 

C.    Sanctions

 

Because the untimely, but substantially Code-compliant admissions and denial were served prior to the hearing of this motion, the substantive motion to deem the RFAs admitted is mooted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) However, this does not moot Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

 

This Court again notes that a party should not be required to resort to a motion to compel in order to obtain discovery responses from an opposing party.  Since the parties argued monetary sanctions issues as to yesterday’s motions, the Court is well acquainted with the arguments on both sides.  Taking into account the monetary sanctions the Court ordered on September 5,  the Court orders Defendant and/or its counsel to pay monetary sanctions to Plaintiff’s counsel in the amount of $1,000, plus the $68.40 filing fee, for total monetary sanctions on this motion alone of $1,068.40, payable to Plaintiff’s counsel within thirty (30) days.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Inspection Demand No. 56 is substantively MOOTED. However, the Court awards Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,068.40.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

II. ANALYSIS ¿ 

¿ 

A.    Plaintiff’s Motion for Default Judgment

 

                                     i.          Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure Section 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant fails to timely answer following proper service of process. A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and provide: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (C.R.C., Rule 3.1800.) Typically, the Plaintiff will also file a statement of damages summarizing the categories of claimed harm and the amounts claimed as to each, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 425.11, using the mandatory judicial council form CIV-050. 

There was no allegation as to the dollar amount of Plaintiff’s claims in the operative complaint.  The Fourth Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges in the prayer the right to recover “general, specific, compensatory, incidental, and consequential damages according to proof,” but no dollar amount is specified.   Nor was there a statement of damages filed here, such as to given notice to the defendants of the amounts being sought for the different categories of alleged harm. A complaint’s allegation of general damages alone is insufficient. (Jones v. Interstate Recovery Service (1984) 160 Cal.App.3d 925, 929-930.) The amount of the default judgment “cannot exceed that demanded in the complaint, in the statement required by Section 425.11, or in the statement provided for by Section 425.115.” (Code of Civil Procedure § 580, subd. (a).)  With no amount alleged in the FAC, the defendants have no idea how much is being sought against them, and whether they should allow a default to be entered against them or to formally appear and contest the case.  This shortcoming is fatal to the requested money judgments by default.   Further, the Plaintiff’s testimony as to the number of units sold or the value of a “unit” lacks foundation, as the Cour GRANTS the evidentiary objections filed by Defendant Williams.  While many persons rely on Wikipedia or Internet sources for information, the rigors of litigation require more of a foundation than a number appearing on some website. 

  “A complaint must state the amount of money damages or other relief it seeks. (§ 425.10.) An exception applies where a complaint seeks damages for personal injury or wrongful death. (§ 425.11.) In these cases, the nature and amount of money damages must not be stated in the complaint, but must be stated in a separate statement described in section 425.11. (Ibid.; § 425.10.) The statement must be served in the same manner as a summons, before a default may be entered on the underlying complaint. (§ 425.11, subd. (d)(1).)  [¶]  Together, the statutes require that, before a default may be entered, the defendant must be served, in the same manner as a summons, with notice of the amount of money damages or other relief sought. (§§ 425.10, 425.11, 585.)”  (Schwab v. Southern California Gas Co. (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1320.)

These statutes preserve a defendant's right to contest an action and protect the defendant from unlimited liability. “Section 580, and related sections 585, 586, 425.10 and 425.11, aim to ensure that a defendant who declines to contest an action does not thereby subject himself to open-ended liability.” (Greenup v. Rodman (1986) 42 Cal.3d 822, 826.)   This is a fundamental fairness issue, concerning Due Process rights of persons being sued.  “It is precisely when there is no trial ... that formal notice, and therefore the requirement of section 425.10, become[s] critical. Notice is at the heart of the provision, as the Legislature underscored by adding section 425.11....” (Greenup, supra, 42 Cal.3d at pp. 826–827 (italics added.) “Section 580 constitutes a statutory expression of the mandates of due process, which require ‘formal notice of potential liability.’ ” (Parish v. Peters (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 202, 207.)

                                   ii.          Discussion

 

Proof of Service of Process: Proofs of Service via Personal and/or Substituted Service were filed as follows: (1) Estate of Richard Lawrence Grobl – Served on January 14, 2024 via Personal Service on Brett Ruben – Authorized Agent for Service; (2) Aaron Joseph Bolden a/k/a Imani Walker – Served on January 22, 2024 via substituted service on Giselle Bolden, his mother; (3)  Derek Bolden – Served on August 25, 2023 substituted service on Giselle Bolden, his mother; (4) Robert White – Served on November 23, 2022 via substituted service on Jack White, his father; (5) Pharrell Williams – Served on December 1, 2022 via personal service on Nathan Vedrani – Registered Agent for Service; and (6) Creative Works, LLC – Served on August 29, 2023 via Personal Service on Brett Rubin – Registered Agent for Service.

 

The Request for Court Judgment filed on July 9, 2024 notes that the CIV-100 was served by mail on June 4, 2024.

 

Request for Entry of Default: The Requests for Entry of Default as to each of the Defendants is as follows: (1) Estate of Richard Lawrence Grobl – Filed on June 10, 2024, was not entered, but subsequently filed on July 9, 2024, has not been entered; (2) Aaron Joseph Bolden a/k/a Imani Walker – Filed on June 10, 2024, has been entered; (3)  Derek Bolden – Filed on June 10, 2024, has been entered; (4) Robert White – Filed on June 10, 2024, has been entered; (5) Pharrell Williams – Filed on June 10, 2024, was not entered, but subsequently filed on July 9, 2024, has not been entered; (6) Creative Works, LLC – Filed on June 10, 2024, was not entered, but subsequently filed on July 9, 2024, has not been entered.

 

Prove Up: Plaintiff seeks entry of money judgments in the combined amount of over $1.6 Billion against multiple Defendants. The original through fourth amended complaint allege that Plaintiff has suffered: (1) general, specific, compensatory, incidental and consequential damages according to proof as a result of the fraud and misrepresentation sustained by Plaintiff in an amount according to proof; (2) for disgorgement and restitution of all profits and gains obtained by the unlawful and fraudulent acts and omissions alleged herein; according to proof; (3) an award to Plaintiff of the profits, gains and advantages derived by the Defendants as a result of the willful copyright infringement, in addition to punitive damages and compensation for the damages sustained by plaintiff in an amount according to proof; (4) an award to Plaintiff of the profits, gains, and advantages derived by the Defendants in an amount according to proof; and (5) The Defendants pay to Plaintiff all costs that may be incurred as attorney fees, and for such other and further relief as may be just and proper.

 

While the various pleadings allege the above, there is no dollar amount claimed in the Complaint and no Statement of Damages was filed.  Further, Plaintiff has failed to dismiss the  Doe defendants, and other defendants listed in the various complaints which are required before a court can enter a money judgment by default. 

 

The Court has reviewed the Default Package, and Plaintiff’s written testimony. Attached to those declarations are exhibits which contain purported song values based on units allegedly sold worldwide (which are incomplete), Defendants’ alleged net worths, earnings from music, and screenshots of Google searches. Although these exhibits may lend themselves to show how much profit was allegedly made from these songs, they lack foundation as discussed above.  Further, these exhibits certainly do not evidence the claimed fraud – intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, breach of contract, or “tort of another” causes of action – another requirement that must be met before a court can enter a money judgment. Does Plaintiff have a copy of the contract? Does Plaintiff have proof of fraud? Does Plaintiff have proof of his alleged writings and recordings that show his copyright? Without more, the proof attached does nothing to prove Plaintiff’s alleged claims against these various defendants.

 

However, even if Plaintiff’s evidence did prove each of the required elements of each of the alleged causes of action, such proof could not cure the failure to allege an amount of damages in the FAC. Because this is a Due Process requirement, the Court cannot enter a default judgment on the record before this Court. 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Default Judgment is DENIED.

 

B.    Specially-Appearing Defendant, Chris Brown’s Motion to Quash

 

                                     i.          Legal Standard

 

“When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.’” (Summers v. McClanahan¿(2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.) A proof of service containing a declaration from a registered process server invokes a presumption of valid service.  (See¿American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara¿(2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390; see also Evid. Code § 647.) This presumption is rebuttable. (See¿id.) The party seeking to defeat service of process must present sufficient evidence to show that the service did not take place as stated. (See Palm Property Investments, LLC v. Yadegar¿(2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 1419, 1428; cf.¿People v. Chavez¿(1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1471, 1483 [“If some fact be presumed, the opponent of that fact bears the burden of producing or going forward with evidence sufficient to overcome or rebut the presumed fact.”].) Merely denying service took place without more is insufficient to overcome the presumption.  (See¿Yadegar, supra, 194 Cal.App.4th at 1428.) 

 

                                   ii.          Discussion

 

Previously, this Court notes that on May 2, 2024, this Court GRANTED Specially-Appearing Defendant, Chris Brown’s (“Brown”) Motion to Quash, noting that the evidence before the Court, failed to show that Chris Brown had been properly served with the lawsuit papers. The Court further noted that delivery of suit papers to a lawyer who does transactional legal work for Mr. Brown is not sufficient on the record presented in the case at bar. However, since then, Brown notes that Plaintiff has ignored the deficiencies in his previous failed attempt to effect substituted service on Brown, on July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second Proof of Service, stating that he had effectuated service on Brown via substitute service by serving “Jaime Masuda – Agent for Process.” Despite this, Brown argues that Plaintiff’s July 15, 2024 Proof of Service again fails to effect personal jurisdiction over Brown as Plaintiff did not satisfy the stringent procedural requirements for substitute service which renders such service fatally defective. Accordingly, Brown has again requested that this Court quash the Proof of Service. Further, Brown has also requested this Court issue an Order to Show Cause regarding the misrepresentation in the Proof of Service, including the statement that Jaime Masuda – who per Brown’s Motion papers has no working relationship with Brown – is somehow Brown’s Registered Agent for Service.

 

This time, Brown argues that Plaintiff’s alleged substituted service is ineffective for at least two reasons: (1) First, on its face, the proof of service Plaintiff filed on behalf of Brown fails to demonstrate reasonable diligence such that Plaintiff was permitted to attempt to substitute service. Brown notes that Plaintiff did not attach a declaration of diligence stating the actions taken to first attempt personal service directly on Brown; and (2) Second, Plaintiff claims to have served Brown by delivering the summons and complaint to “Jamie Masuda – Agent for Process.” However, Brown asserts that Jaime Masuda is not Brown’s registered agent, has no working relationship with Brown and, thus, has no authority to accept service on behalf of Brown.

 

Brown has concurrently filed the Declaration of Jaime Masuda as well as Katy Rodriguez, executive assistant at the business management firm, David Weise & Associates. In Jaime Masuda’s declaration (“Masuda Decl.”), Masuda notes that he is a partner of the business management firm, David Weise & Associates. (Masuda Decl., ¶ 1.) Masuda also notes that he is not Brown’s Agent for Process, that he does not work with Brown in any capacity, and that he has no authority to accept service on Brown’s behalf. (Masuda Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Moreover, the declaration of Katy Rodriguez (“Rodriguez Decl.”) states that she is an executive assistant at the business management firm, David Weise & Associates. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 1.) Rodriguez also notes that Plaintiff’s Proof of Service filed on July 15, 2024, includes a “Due Diligence Declaration” which states that she “stated that [her] firm (NKSFB) is the agent of process for Chris Brown,” but that this conversation never occurred. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Rodriguez notes that she did not inform the unregistered process server that NKSFB was Chris Brown’s “agent of process,” as that would be a false statement. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 4.) Instead, Rodriguez notes that a female, who identified herself as a private investigator, requested to speak with Jaime Masuda, and when Rodriguez indicated that Jaime was not available, the woman left a couple of envelopes for Rodriguez to provide to Masuda. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff notes that he has attached the declaration of Karen White, his private investigator. In White’s declaration (“White Decl.”), she notes that on May 6, 2024, she was hired by Plaintiff to locate and serve defendants on this case, and that they were difficult to serve. In her research, White contends that she discovered information on the Defendant, Ahmad Balshe agent for service and located Jaime Masuda and served Katy Rodriguez. White asserts that she inquired about Chris Brown’ agent of service, and Katy Rodriguez stated Chris Brown is also their client for agent of service. White notes that she then served Katy Rodriguez and is shocked by Katy Rodriguez’s false claims alleging the conversation never occurred.

 

In Brown’s reply brief, he argues that both Plaintiff and White should be ordered to show cause as to why, given this Court’s previous instructions, that they should not be sanctioned. The Court will hear oral argument on this issue at the hearing. However, this Court tentatively GRANTS Brown’s motion to quash and denies the requested sanctions. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 415.20, subdivision (b), Plaintiff failed to attach a declaration of due diligence, demonstrating that after exercising reasonable diligence the summons and complaint could not be served personally upon Brown, thus necessitating substitute service. Moreover, even if this declaration were attached, the Court would still grant the motion as the individuals served with substituted service are shown by the moving papers to not be the registered agents or agents for process of Brown.

 

The Motion is GRANTED, and this Court will hear oral argument as to the issues of an OSC re sanctions.

                                     i.          Legal Standard

 

As noted above, “[w]hen a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.’” (Summers v. McClanahan¿(2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.)                                    ii.          Discussion

 

Here, Specially-Appearing Defendant, Peter Hernandez p/k/a Bruno Mars (“Mars”) also filed a motion to quash service of summons. The moving papers note that on June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a purported Proof of Service, stating that he had effectuated substitute service on Mars by serving “Deridra Moten c/o Matthew Ivanik Agent-for-Process.” However, Mars argues that Plaintiff’s attempted substitute service was ineffective for at least two reasons: (1) First, on its face, the proof of service Plaintiff filed on behalf of Mars fails to demonstrate reasonable diligence such that Plaintiff was permitted to attempt to substitute service. Proc., § 415.20, subd. (b).) However, Mars argues that the purported service on Ivanick failed to satisfy any of those requirements.

 

In his opposition, Plaintiff again cites to a declaration prepared by White, his private investigator. White’s declaration states that she completed a background searches to confirm Mars agent for service and located Ivanick. White states that she contacted Ivanick and identified herself as a private investigator and explained the reason for her call. White also notes that she told him that she had court documents to serve to Mars. White states that Ivanick told her he works from home and instructed her to drop off the documents to the receptionist at his office, where she left the service documents with Deirdra Moten, Ivanick’s receptionist.

 

For similar procedural reasons listed above, namely Code of Civil Procedure Section 415.20, subdivision (b), the Court notes that the motion can be granted as service was procedurally incorrect. However, further, Mars has included in his moving papers, the filing of the declaration of Matthew Ivanick (“Ivanick Decl.”) where he has confirmed that he is not Mars’ “Agent for Process,” and instead has performed accountant services on behalf of Mars. (Ivanick Decl., ¶ 4.) The Court notes that because Plaintiff has failed to show that reasonable diligence was exercised in attempting to personally serve Mars with the summons at a proper place, the motion can be GRANTED on these grounds alone. As such, the Court GRANTS Mars’ motion to quash service as it is procedurally defective and Ivanick has confirmed that he is not the agent for process for Mars.

¿II. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A.    Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set one

 

Legal Standard

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411

Further, where there has been no timely response to a Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300.) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion. Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required. 

 

Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff has requested this Court order Defendant’s verified, code-compliant responses without objection to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant Ralphs does not disagree that it failed to timely respond to Plaintiff’s propounded discovery. In the opposition brief, Ralph’s counsel asserts that on August 19, 2024, Defendant Ralphs served Plaintiff with full and complete responses, and subsequently provided verification on August 21, 2024. However, the responses nonetheless contain objections.

 

Regardless of the parties’ efforts to combine their arguments on original lack of any response with their arguments on the sufficiency of the belated responses (and the objections), and the asserted grounds for relief from waiver of the right to object, the constellation of arguments bearing on the August 19, 2024 written responses is premature.  This is a motion to compel INITIAL, not FURTHER responses.  The pending motions were filed before any responses were served, before any objections were asserted, and before additional meet-and-confer efforts to address the asserted inadequacy of the belated responses.  There is no separate statement required for a motion to compel initial responses, but one is required for FURTHER responses.  The Code has different provisions for motions to compel further responses, for an order addressing objections, and for argument as to whether objections are deemed waived or whether relief from the deemed waiver should be afforded.  Those latter issues are not properly before the Court at this time.

The Court determines that the motion to compel an initial response to the interrogatories and document demands has been MOOTED by Defendant’s service of belated written responses and even more belated verifications during the pendency of these motions.  The issue of waived objections or relief from any such waiver was not presented by the notice of motion and must thus be addressed in a future motion, unless the parties can resolve those issues by meeting and conferring as to the same.   The Court thus encourages the parties to stipulate to extend the time to bring a motion to compel FURTHER responses, to meet and confer on resolving the objections and claimed insufficient substantive responses, and to attempt to narrow the remaining issues if any requiring the Court’s assistance in resolving. 

Sanctions

           

Plaintiff has also requested monetary sanctions in bringing their motion. Sanctions are generally awarded in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel.¿¿(CCP §§ 2030.290(c),¿2030.300(d),¿2031.300(c),¿and 2031.310(h).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the Court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿¿(Id.)   The Court finds that, based on the fact that Plaintiff was forced to bring these motions after Defendant failed to timely provide any written responses, monetary sanctions are warranted. The Court will set a future, continued hearing on the sanctions aspect of these motions to allow the parties the opportunity to resolve the sanctions and objections issues themselves.  By way of tentative ruling, the Court would be inclined to grant monetary sanctions regardless of whether there was confusion in the transition of counsel for the defendant here.

 

 

B.    Motion to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted

 

Legal Standard

Additionally, Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290, subdivision (a), provides that “[o]n receipt of a particular response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete[;] (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.”  Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise the propounding party waives the right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (c).) The motions must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (b).)   Monetary sanctions are mandatory when a responding party serves a belated response to a set of RFAs after the propounding party has been required to file a motion for deemed admissions.  (CCP§§ 2030.280(c).)

 

The Court determines that the motion to compel an initial response to the set of RFAs has been mooted by Defendant’s service of belated admission and denials during the pendency of these motions.  The issue of waived objections or relief from any such waiver was not presented by the notice of motion and must thus be addressed in a future motion, unless the parties can resolve those issues by meeting and conferring as to the same. 

 

 

III. CONCLUSION¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production of Documents are MOOTED by Defendant’s service of written responses while these motions were pending.  The Court is inclined to award monetary sanctions, but will delay deciding the same or the amount of monetary sanctions pending an opportunity for counsel to meet and confer on the objections issues and amount of monetary sanctions given that Plaintiff acted with substantial justification in filing these motions after receiving no substantive responses despite written meet and confer efforts with counsel of record for Defendant.
