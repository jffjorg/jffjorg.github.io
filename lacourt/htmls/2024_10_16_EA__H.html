Case Number: 22PSCV00430    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: H

Abhari, et al. v. Gordes Hospitality Inc., et al., Case No. 22PSCV00430

ORDER ON DEFENDANTS’ MOTIONS TO COMPEL

4.       Defendant/Cross-Complainant Gordes Hospitality Inc.’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two from Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Amir Shamloo is DENIED as MOOT, except as to the issue of sanctions.           5.       Defendant/Cross-Complainant Gordes Hospitality Inc.’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two from Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Amir Shamloo is DENIED as MOOT, except as to the issue of sanctions. Sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $510.00 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

I.        Background

In this breach of contract case, Plaintiffs Zia Abhari and Amir Shamloo (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

On May 2, 2020, Gordes Hospitality, Inc. (“Gordes”), through Ganyu Huang aka Gary Huang, and Shamloo, with Abhari serving as a silent partner, entered into an “Asset Purchase Agreement” and a “Security Agreement” with Gordes for the transfer of ownership of Hamilton’s Steakhouse (“Hamilton”) from Gordes, as seller, to Plaintiffs, as buyers. On June 18, 2020, LVGEM Hotel Corporation (“LVGEM”), through Huang, and Shamloo, with Abhari serving as a silent partner of Shamloo, entered into a “Lease Agreement” for the lease of the property upon which Hamilton is located. II.      Legal Standard

Responses to requests for admission, interrogatories, and requests for production of documents are due 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.250, subd. (a), 2030.260, subd. (a), 2031.260, subd. (a).) If timely responses are not served, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses or deeming matters admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.280, subd. (b), 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).)

As to requests for admissions, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction . . . on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated [a] motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

As to interrogatories and requests for production of documents, “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories [or a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).)

 

III.     Discussion

Gordes has filed five discovery motions against either Abhari or Shamloo. In these motions, Gordes seeks: (1) an order that the truth of all specified matters and the genuineness of all specified documents in Gordes’ Requests for Admission, Set Two propounded to Abhari be deemed admitted (“Motion 1”); (2) an order compelling Abhari to respond to Gordes’ Form Interrogatories, Set Two (“Motion 2”); (3) an order that the truth of all specified matters and the genuineness of all specified documents in Gordes’ Requests for Admission, Set Two propounded to Shamloo be deemed admitted (“Motion 3”); (4) an order compelling Shamloo to respond to Gordes’ Form Interrogatories, Set Two (“Motion 4”); and (5) an order compelling Shamloo to respond to Gordes’ Request for Production of Documents, Set Two (“Motion 5”). 

LVGEM has filed three discovery motions against Shamloo, seeking: (1) an order that the truth of all specified matters and the genuineness of all specified documents in LVGEM’s Requests for Admission, Set One propounded to Shamloo be deemed admitted (“Motion 6”); (2) an order compelling Shamloo to respond to LVGEM’s Form Interrogatories, Set One (“Motion 7”); and (3) an order compelling Shamloo to respond to LVGEM’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One (“Motion 8”).

In support of the motions, Gordes and LVGEM’s counsel Caixing Ma (“Ma”) represents as follows:

On March 21, 2024, Gordes and LVGEM served Abhari and Shamloo with their discovery requests seeking the discovery at issue in the instant motions. (Ma Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) Written responses were due on April 23, 2024. (Id., ¶ 4.) Neither Abhari nor Shamloo served responses on April 23, 2024, or requested an extension of time to provide responses. (Id., ¶ 5.) On May 28, 2024, the parties attended an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) to address the discovery disputes. (Id., ¶ 6.) During the IDC, Abhari and Shamloo agreed to provide all responses by June 7, 2024. (Id.) From June 7-11, 2024, Gordes and LVGEM agreed to two extension requests made by Abhari and Shamloo, such that responses were due by June 17, 2024. (Id.) Neither Abhari nor Shamloo provided responses by June 17, 2024 or requested a further extension. (Id.)

Gordes and LVGEM filed the instant motions on June 21, 2024. After the motions were filed, the requested discovery was provided on either June 21, 2024 or June 25, 2024. (Vafaei Decl., ¶ 2; Reply Briefs, p. 1.)

Abhari and Shamloo oppose the motions as moot since the requested discovery has since been produced. While Gordes and LVGEM acknowledge having received the requested discovery, they contend that sanctions are still appropriate because the belated responses did not vitiate the need for the motions to be filed. The Court agrees. Accordingly, the motions are denied as moot, except as to sanctions.

Case Number: 23PSCV01959    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: H

Jiang v. Fu Bang Group Corp., USA, et al., Case No. 23PSCV01959


ORDER RE DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION

Background

Plaintiff Na Jiang (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

          In or around July 1, 2018, Plaintiff and Fu Bang Group Corp., USA (“Fu Corp.”) entered into a written contract, wherein Plaintiff agreed to loan Fu Corp. $1,320,000.00 at 5% per annum for a one-year term. Dos Lagos Center 2, LLC (“Dos Lagos”) signed as a guarantor for the contract. Bo Chen (“Chen”) later signed as a guarantor. Defendants have failed to pay the outstanding balance.

On June 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Fu Corp, Dos Lagos, Chen and Does 1-10 for (1) breach of contract and (2) breach of personal guarantee.

On February 14, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Chen.

A Case Management Conference and an Order to Show Cause Re: Default Judgment are set for October 16, 2024. For the reasons stated herein, the Court will defer ruling on the default judgment application as to Chen until the conclusion of the trial.

 

Discussion

“There are certain cases where default judgments were taken against defaulting defendants who were claimed to be jointly and severally liable with the answering defendants. There, however, they set up independent defenses not involving the defaulting defendants. Under such circumstances the courts held that a separate judgment against such defaulting defendants may be entered before the issue is tried as to the appearing defendants.” (Mirabile v. Smith (1953) 119 Cal.App.2d 685, 688.)

Co-Defendants Fu Corp. and Dos Lagos filed an answer on October 17, 2023 raising 26 affirmative defenses. Here, in light of the defenses raised in that answer, the Court will defer ruling on Plaintiff’s application for default judgment as to Chen until after trial of Co-Defendants Fu Corp. and Dos Lagos.     

Case Number: 23PSCV03378    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: H

Plaintiff University Promenade CRF3, LLC’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.


Background

In this breach of contract case, Plaintiff University Promenade GRF3, LLC (“Plaintiff”) alleges that its tenant, Defendant Check Solution, Inc., dba Tacos Super Gallito (“Check Solution”), and the guarantor, Defendant Sergio Barraza (“Barraza”), breached a lease agreement for the commercial property located at 962 E. Alosta Avenue in Azusa, when Check Solution failed to pay rent.

On January 17, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Check Solutions and Barraza.

An Order to Show Cause Re: Default Judgment is set for October 16, 2024.

Discussion 

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The following defects are noted: 

1.       Plaintiff has only dismissed Does 1-10 without prejudice. Plaintiff has failed to dismiss Does 11-20 or to submit “an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment,” pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(7). 

2.       Plaintiff has failed to submit “a brief summary of the case identifying the parties and the nature of plaintiff’s claim,” pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(1).

3.       Plaintiff seeks attorney’s fees of $8,947.90. It appears to the Court that attorney’s fees should be reduced to $4,182.06, pursuant to Local Rule 3.214(a).

4.       The lease agreement references a $7,139.94 security deposit. Plaintiff has failed to advise whether the security deposit was applied to the outstanding balance owed by Defendants or returned to them.

5.       Plaintiff is directed to provide the Court with a Statement of Account, to the extent one exists.

Accordingly, the Court denies Plaintiff’s Application without prejudice and directs Plaintiff to submit a new default judgment packet that addresses the aforementioned defects.
