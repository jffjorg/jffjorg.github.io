DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation. 



           


Case Number: 21STCV18241    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: 20

Tentative Ruling

Judge Kevin C. Brazile

Department 20

Hearing Date:                         Friday, October 11, 2024

Case Name:                             Brendan Schultz v. Pitzer College, et al.

Case No.:                                21STCV18241

Motion:                                   Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Brendan Schultz

Responding Party:                   Defendant Pitzer College

Notice:                                    OK

 

 

Ruling:                                    The Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 9, 2023, Plaintiff Brendan Schultz (“Plaintiff” or “Schultz”) served Defendant Pitzer College (“Defendant”) with Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions (set one) (“RFAs”). (Schultz Decl. ¶ 2.) Defendant served responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs on June 13, 2023. (Id., ¶ 3.)

            Finding these responses deficient, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses, without Objections, to RFAs on September 8, 2023. On June 12, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses and ordered Defendant to provide further responses within thirty days. (Minute Order, 6/12/2024.) By mutual agreement of the parties, the parties’ deadline to serve compliant responses to each party’s outstanding discovery was extended to August 15, 2024. (Schultz Decl., ¶ 5; Khachatryan Decl., ¶ 3.)

            On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to deem RFAs admitted.

            On September 30, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition.

            On __________, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            A party to whom a request for admissions has been made must respond within 30 days of service of the request absent a court order to the contrary. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.250(a).) “On receipt of a response to a party’s request for admission, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) [a]n answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete[,] [or] (2) [a]n objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.290(a).) “If a party then fails to obey an order compelling further response to requests for admission, the court may order that the matters involved in the requests be deemed admitted. In lieu of, or in addition to, this order, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 ….” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.290(e).)

According to well-established case law, there are two factual prerequisites to the imposition of non-monetary sanctions for violation of court ordered discovery, including an order deeming requests for admissions admitted: (1) a failure to comply with a court order, and (2) such failure must be willful. (See Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327; Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrators, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1102, citing Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545; Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 787-88 (“Lack of diligence may be deemed willful in the sense that the party understood his obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply. [Citation.] … A conscious or intentional failure to act, as distinguished from accidental or involuntary non-compliance, is sufficient to invoke a penalty.”) (internal citations omitted).)

Further, “[e]ven where nonmonetary sanctions are called for, they should be appropriate to the dereliction…. The sanctions the court may impose are such as are suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks, but the court may not impose sanctions which are designed not to accomplish the objects of discovery but to impose punishment.” (Biles, supra, at 1327 (cleaned up) (internal citations and quotations omitted); see also Do it Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 35, superseded by statute on another ground as stated in Union Bank v. Sup. Ct. (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 582-83; St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 783-84.)

The St. Mary Court forcefully explained the trial court’s duty in determining whether to impose non-monetary sanctions for lack of compliance with a Court order as follows:

“The RFA device is not intended to provide a windfall to litigants. Nor is the RFA procedure a ‘gotcha’ device in which an overly aggressive propounding party—who rejects facially reasonable requests for a short discovery extension and thereafter files [a] discovery motion after service of a slightly tardy proposed RFA response that is substantially code-compliant—may obtain a substantive victory in the case by having material issues deemed admitted. RFAs are not to be deemed admitted unless the party to whom RFAs are propounded … is recalcitrant and violates a court order compelling further responses that are deficient.”

(Id. at 783-84; see also Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979 (in fashioning remedies for violations of discovery requests, courts should seek to “eliminate[] … undeserved windfalls and the resulting subversion of the policy favoring the resolution of lawsuits on the merits.”))

 

Application to Facts

            Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s failure to provide timely and complete responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs is in direct contravention of this Court’s prior order and warrants the Court deeming Plaintiff’s RFAs admitted as to Defendant. Plaintiff cites to legal authority to support the proposition that failure to strictly comply with a Court’s order to comply with discovery obligations warrants the imposition of non-monetary sanctions, including deeming RFAs admitted, to preserve the integrity of the judicial process. (See Mot., p. 4:24-5:20.) However, Plaintiff improperly cites to numerous legal authority that either does not exist, or does not support the proposition he contends.

For example, Plaintiff cites to Wilcox v. Birtwhistle. (Supra, 21 Cal.4th 973.) Although the California Supreme Court recognized the feature of the discovery process Plaintiff cites, (Id. at 982) the primary crux of the Wilcox Court’s holding is that the primary scope of the Court’s holding was that Code of Civil Procedure § 2033(m), as it was then enacted, not only permitted a party to seek withdrawal of admissions contained in an actual response, but also an admission deemed admitted based on a failure to respond. (Id. at 979.) Further, as discussed above, the Court’s reasoning centers around avoiding gamesmanship and windfalls in the discovery process. (Id. at 983.) The rest of the cases Plaintiff cites are either more fully clarified in the legal standard section, above, or do not exist. (See Mot., p. 5:12-15.)

Here, the Court agrees with Defendant’s argument that deeming Plaintiff’s RFAs admitted is not warranted based on the trial court’s duty to limit the discretionary imposition of discovery sanctions only to remediate actual harm suffered by the propounding party. (See Kahn v. Kahn (1977) 68 Cal.App.3d 372, 381; see also Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992 (trial courts should “attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.”))

The facts in evidence that Defendant alleges are sufficient to warrant the finding that Defendant diligently, and in good faith, attempted to comply with the Court’s order, and did not violate the Court’s order voluntarily. (See Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at 787-88.) First, Defendant establishes that on August 15, 2024, Defendant attempted to call Plaintiff and advise him that he had been unable to work due to a family emergency related to his grandfather’s health. (Khachatryan Decl., ¶ 4.) Further, although Plaintiff was not available on the 15th, the Parties agreed to a meet and confer call on August 19, 2024, where Defendant would have supplied Plaintiff with fully compliant responses. (Id., ¶ 7.) However, instead, Plaintiff submitted the instant motion prior to business hours on August 19, 2024 and rejected Defendant’s calls. (Id., ¶¶ 8, 10, 11.) Within the same day, Defendant served on Plaintiff his fully verified and code compliant discovery responses, without any objections. (Id., ¶ 10.)

The facts evidenced by Defendant’s declaration, with penalty of perjury, establish that Defendant diligently and in good faith attempted to provide Plaintiff with compliant responses, despite substantial emotional distress caused by a family health emergency. (See Khachatryan Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5, 8, 10.) And, despite Plaintiff’s assumed knowledge of these facts, or blatant attempts to disregard such facts, Plaintiff instead chose to attempt to game the mutual discovery process in hopes of acquiring a windfall that he would not have otherwise received. (See St. Mary, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 783-84; Wilcox, supra, 21 Cal.4th at p. 983; Wilson v. Jefferson (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 952, 958 (The discretionary imposition of a sanction “should not operate in such a fashion as to put the prevailing party in a better position than he would have had if he had obtained the discovery sought and it had been completely favorable to his cause.”) (internal citations and quotations omitted).)

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Granted is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On November 17, 2021, Plaintiffs Keang Krouch, Tieng Uch, Bryan Tamoria-Bowen, Luzmila Villalobos, Jeffrey Marduce, Emad Sukkar, and Robert Gooch (“Plaintiffs”) filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant PR2020, LLC (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 to 20, inclusive for (1) Declaratory Relief; (2) Breach of Contract; and (3) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing. The FAC alleges Plaintiffs all had commercial leases for office spaces located in the building at 22015-22019 Vanowen St., Canoga Park, CA 91303 (“Property”), which Defendant acquired ownership of in or about 2020 and served 60-day notice of termination under Section 6.2(g) of the Leases on Plaintiffs regarding aa Hazardous Substance Condition. (FAC ¶¶13-29.) Plaintiffs allege Defendant knew about the Hazardous Substance Condition prior to taking ownership of the Property and such conditions existed prior to Plaintiffs entrance into their Leases, thus Defendant’s right to terminate under the Leases are not applicable to Plaintiffs. (Id. at ¶¶30-32.)

            On May 2, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) adding a cause of action for breach of Contract and removing the cause of action for declaratory relief.

            On June 20, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer and Cross-Complaint against Keang Krouch dba Royal Donuts, Tieng Uch dba Royal Donuts, Abdul Azin Salvoory, Abdul Saboory, Omar Mangal, Zehour Dourghalli dba Liquor Land, Emad Sukkar dba Liquor Land, Bryan Tamoria-Bowen dba Farmers Insurance Agency, Luzmila Villalobos dba Farmers Insurance Company, Jeffrey Alan Marduce, Robert Gooch dba Gooch Training Academy, Jeremy Warburton dba Gooch Training Academy (“Cross-Defendants”) and ROES 1 to 20 for six causes of action for Breach of Lease.

            On August 17, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a General Denial to Cross-Complaint.

            On December 1, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion for Sanctions related to Plaintiff Krouch’s failure to participate in discovery and comply with the Court’s discovery orders. The Court granted the motion and entered a terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Krouch by striking his SAC, his Answer to Cross-Complaint, and entering default.

            On January 10, 20204, default was entered against Cross-Defendant Zehour Dourghalli.

            On May 3, 2024, Cross-Defendant Emad Sukkar was dismissed from the Cross-Complaint.

            ON MAY 10, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion for Sanctions related to Plaintiff Robert Gooch’s failure to participate in discovery and comply with the Court’s discovery orders. The Court granted the motion and entered a terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Gooch by striking his SAC, his Answer to Cross-Complaint, and entering default.

            On August 12, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Enforce Settlement. The motion is unopposed.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            Pursuant the Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6: “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.”¿¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6 (emphasis added).)

“Because of its summary nature, strict compliance with the requirements of section 664.6 is prerequisite to invoking the power of the court to impose a settlement agreement.” (Sully-Miller Contracting Co. v. Gledson/Cashman Construction, Inc. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 30, 37;Critzer v. Enos (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1262.) In ruling on a motion under Section 664.6, the trial judge may receive oral testimony, or may determine the motion upon declarations alone. (Corkland v. Boscoe (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 989, 994.) Where the agreement was reached at a court hearing, the court can resolve the dispute on the basis of its own notes or recollection of what was agreed to (as well as any transcripts of the proceedings). (Richardson v. Richardson (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 91, 97.)¿¿

Generally, courts lose subject matter jurisdiction when an action is voluntarily dismissed. (Hagan Engineering, Inc. v. Mills (2003) 115 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1009.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6 provides the authority for the Court the retention of personal jurisdiction to enforce the settlement “if requested by the parties.” The stipulation as to jurisdiction must conform to the same requirements necessary for enforcement of the settlement agreement. (Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 440.) It is not enough simply to provide for a retention of jurisdiction in the settlement agreement. (Mesa RHF Partners, L.P. v. City of Los Angeles (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 913, 918.) Instead, the request to retain jurisdiction must be filed with the court: “the parties could have easily invoked section 664.6 by filing a stipulation and proposed order either attaching a copy of the settlement agreement and requesting that the trial court retain jurisdiction under section 664.6 or a stipulation and proposed order signed by the parties noting the settlement and requesting that the trial court retain jurisdiction under section 664.6. The process need not be complex. But strict compliance demands that the process be followed.” (Ibid.; see Sayta v. Chu (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 960, 966-968 [no subject matter jurisdiction to enforce settlement per CCP § 664.6 because parties failed to ask court to retain jurisdiction before case dismissed].)¿¿

 

Application to Facts

            Defendant PR2020, LLC (hereinafter “Defendant”) moves for an order enforcing a Settlement Agreement reached between Defendant, Plaintiff Bowen, and Plaintiff Villalobos on May 19, 2024. (Zakariaie Decl., ¶3, Ex. A.) Section 20 of the Settlement Agreement expressly retained the Court’s jurisdiction to enforce it pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6. (Id. at ¶4.)

            Pursuant to the terms of the Settlement Agreement, Paragraph 1 states “In exchange for the Parties entering into this Agreement, the Parties agree that within three (3) business days of full execution of this Agreement, BOWEN/VILLALOBOS and Defendant shall cause the filing of a Request for Dismissal, with prejudice, in the Action, wherein BOWEN/VILLALOBOS dismisses their Operative Complaint against Defendant and Defendant dismisses the Cross-Complaint against BOWEN/VILLALOBOS. Plaintiffs shall cause a conformed copy of the dismissal to be delivered to Defendant's counsel within five (5) business days after the full execution of this Agreement.” (Id. at ¶5.) Defendant contends Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos breached the Settlement Agreement because their counsel of record failed to file the Request for Dismissal. In fact, Defendant asserts its counsel sent the fully executed Agreement to Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos’ counsel Drew Sherman on May 30, 2024, and expressly stated for him to file the Request for Dismissal within 3 business days as required by the Settlement Agreement and then Defense counsel would file his Request for Dismissal as to the Cross-Complaint. (Zakariaie Decl., ¶6, Ex. B.) Mr. Zakariaie sent a follow-up email to Mr. Sherman on June 7, 2024 and June 10, 2024, reminding him of the obligation to file a Request for Dismissal of the Complaint to avoid the present motion. (Id. at ¶¶7-8.) Mr. Sherman informed Mr. Zakariaie he had a minor heart attack and needed more time to file the dismissal. Mr. Zakariaie sent another email on July 1, 2024 regarding the status of the dismissal and received no response. (Id. at ¶9.)

            The Court finds that enforcement is warranted under Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6. First, each of the parties subject to the Settlement Agreement signed it. (Id. at ¶3.) Next, there is no indication from the court records that a Request for Dismissal of the Complaint as to Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos has been filed in this action. Likewise, Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos have not filed an opposition to the instant motion indicating otherwise or attesting to any circumstances giving rise to their inability to effectuate such dismissal as required by the Settlement Agreement. Moreover, Mr. Sherman appears to have filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel set for hearing on October 14, 2024, however it is not in regards to being counsel for Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos.

In the event of breach, Defendant asserts Section 19 of the Settlement Agreement provides for the recovery of reasonable attorneys’ fees by the prevailing party in any proceeding brought to enforce the Settlement Agreement. (Zakariaie Decl., ¶3, Ex. A.) As such, Defendant requests $3,525,00 in attorneys’ fees for preparing the instant motion, reviewing the opposition, preparing the reply and attending the hearing on the motion to be paid by Plaintiffs Bowen and Villalobos. (Id. at ¶11-12.) The Court will reduce the reasonable attorneys’ fees awarded due to the motion being unopposed. Thus, Defendant will only be awarded 5.0 hours for preparing the motion and attending the hearing on the motion at an hourly rate of $495.00 plus filing fee of $60.00 for a total of $2,535.00.

           

           

CONCLUSION

            The Motion to Enforce Settlement is GRANTED. The SAC is dismissed with prejudice as to Plaintiffs Bryan Tamoria-Bowen and Luzmila Villalobos.

Ruling:                                    The Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant Alissa DeVogel to give notice.

 

If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly encouraged to appear in person.

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 23, 2023, Plaintiff Terrance Marshall (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants CTM Apartments Services Corporation and 5535 Carlton Way Partners LP (“Defendants”) for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

            On December 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), which added Los Angeles Sheriff’s Department, James Investment Partners LP, Tracie Lackey, Andrew Gonzalez, Charlene Escobar, Bradley Boleman, Annelore Lehouck a/k/a Anne Marie Morley a/k/a Anne Marie Morauy a/k/a Ann Lehouck a/k/a Bling Lehouck a/k/a Noam, Cody Allen Morley a/k/a Cody “Bling” Morley, Joshua Brigg, Vazrik Hartooni, Damon Burton, Jose Fernandez, Alissa DeVogel, Ryan Scott Nelson, and DOES 1 through 15, alleging fifteen causes of action.

            On June 24, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants Alissa DeVogel, James Investment Partners, 5535 Carlton Way Partners LP, CTM Apartment Services Corporation, Tracie Lackey, Andrew Gonzalez, and Bradley Boleman’s demurrers with leave to amend. The Court also granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions was denied.

            On July 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative SAC.

            On July 10, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) within 10 days. No TAC has been filed.

            On August 5, 2024, Defendant DeVogel filed the instant Motion to Strike the SAC. The motion is unopposed.

             

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof, but this time limitation shall not apply to motions specified in subdivision (e).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)  

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) 

 

Application to Facts

            Defendant Alissa DeVogel (hereinafter “Defendant”) moves for an order striking Paragraph 10, lines 22-26; Paragraph 18, lines 18-19; Lines 13-18 on page 13; and Lines 20-25, page 13 from the SAC. The motion is made is on the grounds that Plaintiff’s SAC allegations fails to amend the deficiencies of the FAC.

 

            Meet and Confer

Before filing a motion to strike, the moving party must meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.) “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).) A demurrer and motion to strike have similar meet and confer requirements. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.)

The declaration of Defendant’s counsel for record, Min Soo Ha, Esq., attests to meet and confer efforts made on July 30, 2024 via telephone call. (Ha Decl., ¶¶3-4, Ex. A.) The parties could not reach an informal resolution. (Id. at ¶4.) Defendant did agree to withdraw the instant motion upon Plaintiff filing a TAC. (Id.)

 

            Merits of Motion

Upon review of the FAC and SAC allegations, the Court finds that the allegations set forth in the SAC are essentially the same as those alleged in the FAC. Specifically, the SAC realleges that Defendant DeVogel conspired with other defendants and withheld evidence that was to be used in a previous court matter. Thus, the SAC allegations are not in conformity with the Court’s order sustaining the demurrers with leave to amend and should be stricken as to Defendant DeVogel.

DeVogel also cites to Cano v. Glover 143 Cal.App.4th 326, in support of her argument that the SAC should ultimately be dismissed with prejudice as to her. “Section 581, subdivision (f)(2) ‘... gives the defendant the right to obtain a court order dismissing the action with prejudice once the court sustains a demurrer with leave to amend and the plaintiff has not amended within the time given.’ ” (Cano v. Glover (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 326, 330.) Plaintiff has failed to make any showing that he can sufficiently amend the SAC as to Defendant DeVogel. In fact, Plaintiff has not filed the TAC despite prior leave to file such pleading being granted by the Court and agreed upon by Defendant DeVogel.

Therefore, the dismissal with prejudice is warranted in this instance.

 

           

CONCLUSION

            The Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend. The SAC is dismissed as to Defendant Alissa DeVogel with prejudice.

BACKGROUND

            On October 17, 2023, Plaintiff Ace Diversion Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant 1530 Date Street, LLC (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive for (1) Specific Performance and (2) Breach of Contract. The Complaint alleges Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a written Commercial Lease for real property located at 1530 Date Street, Montebello, CA 90640 (“Property”), wherein Plaintiff was granted an option to purchase the Property by delivering written notice to Defendant between June 1, 2023 through July 31, 2023, and a cashier’s check payable in the amount of $25,000.00. (Compl., ¶¶5-6.) Despite two payments of $25,000.00 made to Defendant, Plaintiff alleges Defendant refuses to open escrow and commence the purchase and sale of the Property, and made verbal representations regarding intent to sell the Property to a third party. (Id. at ¶¶10-15.)

            On August 12, 2024, Mark V. Asdourian, Esq. of Newport Harbor Law Group, P.C. filed the instant Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Defendant. The motion is unopposed.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (Code Civ. Proc., § 284(2).) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.)    

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion) (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)), MC-052 (Declaration) (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)). The proposed order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e).)    

Further, the requisite forms must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d).) The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e).) A motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)  

 

 

Application to Facts

            Counsel Asdourian has filed the MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053 forms pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362. Furthermore, Counsel Asdourian served Defendant by mail at its last known address, which Counsel Asdourian states was confirmed within the past 30 days as current through the Statement of Information filed with the Secretary of State on May 7, 2024 by the manager, Aron Petrosian on behalf of Defendant.

            Counsel Asdourian declares that Defendant failed to abide by the terms and conditions of the written retainer agreement, including but not limited to failing to make timely payment of statements for professional services rendered for a period of over five (5) months. Moreover, Counsel Asdourian declares he has been contacted by counsel who was considering taking over the case and requested pleadings and discovery, which were provided. However, Counsel Asdourian states the substitution of attorney form forwarded to Defendant on August 1, 2024 has not been executed and returned. The Court notes that no substitution of attorney has been filed in this matter.

            Nonetheless, Counsel Asdourian filed proof of service indicating that the requisite forms were served on Defendant and counsel for Plaintiff. Likewise, the proposed order on form MC-053 includes all the hearings scheduled in this matter as follows: (1) Final Status Conference set for 8/6/26; and (2) Jury Trial set for 8/10/26. Thus, Defendant has sufficient time to Finally, the fact that the corporate Defendant will be without representation is not a basis for denial of the motion to be relieved as counsel. As explained by the Court of Appeal in Thomas G. Ferruzzo, Inc. v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 501, 504:

 

There are two concepts, not one, which are here involved.  The first is the principle supported by [Merco Constr. Engineers, Inc. v. Municipal Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 724] that a corporation may not proceed in propria persona.  The second is that an attorney, given proper grounds, may be permitted by the court to withdraw as attorney of record.  These two concepts are not inconsistent in the case of a corporate client.  An attorney may be allowed to withdraw without offending the rule against corporate self-representation.  [¶] The effect of withdrawal is to leave the corporation without representation and without the ability to practice self-representation.  For the uncooperative corporate client who has not been willing to bring in new counsel, granting of the withdrawal motion will put extreme pressure on it to obtain new counsel of record for should it fail to do so it risks forfeiture of its rights through non-representation.

 

Therefore, the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

            The Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.
