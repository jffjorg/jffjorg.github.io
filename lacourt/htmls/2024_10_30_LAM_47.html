DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV10036    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 30, 2024                               TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Bruce Dwain Copeland v. Amy Pham

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV10036           

 

MOTION TO DEEM TRUTH OF MATTERS IN REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS (x2)

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Bruce Dwain Copeland, in propria persona

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Amy Pham

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         03/23/22: Complaint filed.

·         03/14/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         05/24/23: Plaintiff deemed vexatious litigant.

·         07/05/23: Notice of Appeal filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for quiet title and injunctive relief. Plaintiff contends that Defendant is fraudulently claiming ownership of the property in which Plaintiff resides and which he contends that he owns.

 

Plaintiff moves to deem the truth of matters stated in two sets of requests for admissions as admitted.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters in Requests for Admissions is DENIED.

 

Defendant Pham’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

//

 

//

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to deem the truth of matters stated in two sets of requests for admissions as admitted.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of (1) a deed to Defendant Pham from the now-dismissed Defendant US Bank; (2) an unlawful detainer judgment against Plaintiff in favor of Defendant Pham; and (3) the dismissal of Defendant US Bank.

 

These materials are not relevant to the Court’s ruling on this motion and Defendant’s request is therefore DENIED. (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 (“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Legal Standard

 

When a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to respond, the party propounding the requests may move for an order that the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) “The court shall make this order [deem the requests admitted], unless it finds that the party to whom the request for admissions have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff seeks an order deeming the truth of matters stated in two sets of requests for admissions which were purportedly served on Defendant Pham on September 19, 2024. Defendant claims that she did not receive a full copy of the discovery that is the subject of the motion. Further, Defendant contends that she was never served with a copy of the motion, and the included proof of service is undated. Even if service were properly made, the instant motion was filed on September 30, 2024, only 11 days after the requests for admissions were purportedly propounded. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.250 subdivision (a), a party has 30 days to respond to requests for admissions. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).) Plaintiff may not simply propound requests for admissions and then move to have those requests deemed admitted without first providing Defendant a fair opportunity to respond as provided by statute.

 

Plaintiff is therefore not entitled to an order deeming the truth of the matters stated in these requests for admissions.

 

//

 

Sanctions

 

            Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff for prematurely bringing this motion.

 

            The Court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in a misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by anyone because of that conduct. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Use of a discovery method in a manner that does not comply with its specified procedures is a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(b).) Persisting, over objection and without substantial justification, in an attempt to obtain information or materials that are outside the scope of permissible discovery is also a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(a).)

 

            Here, Defendant requests a total of $2,496 in sanctions against Plaintiff based on 3 hours of attorney time at $495 per hour, plus 2 additional anticipated hours, plus $21.00 in costs. (Declaration of Larry Rothman ISO Opp. ¶ 4.) The Court finds this request unreasonably inflated. It should not take five hours of attorney time to address a routine discovery motion. The Court therefore exercises its discretion to deny this request. (Chavez v. City of Los Angeles (2010) 47 Cal. 4th 970, 989-991.)

 

CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters in Requests for Admissions is DENIED.

 

Defendant Pham’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

HEARING DATE:     October 30, 2024                   TRIAL DATE: June 10, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Martin Melendez-Murillo et al. v. AKG Trading (USA), Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV08596           

 

MOTION FOR TRIAL PREFERENCE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Martin Melendez-Murillo and Rufina Barajas

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendants Home Depot, USA, Inc., et al. (24 Defendants)

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/18/23: Complaint filed.

·         12/05/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Caesarstone USA, Inc.

·         12/12/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Global Stone Trading, Inc.

·         12/28/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Lowe’s Home Centers, LLC

·         04/02/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Arizona Tile, LLC.

·         05/14/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         06/27/24: Cross-Complaint filed by WilsonArt LLC

·         07/03/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Marbolis, Inc.

·         08/12/24: Cross-Complaint filed by Caesarstone Ltd.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a toxic tort action alleging that Plaintiff Melendez-Murillo developed silicosis and consequential injuries by breathing in dust from Defendants’ stone products generated by his work as a fabricator and polisher of stone countertops.

 

Plaintiffs move for trial preference pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivisions (d) and (e).

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs move for trial preference pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 36 subdivisions (d) and (e).

Subdivision (d) of section 36 of the Code of Civil Procedure states that the Court, in its discretion, may grant a motion for preference “that is accompanied by clear and convincing medical documentation that concludes that one of the parties suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of survival of that party beyond six months, and that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 36(d).) If the motion is granted, the Court “shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 36(f).)

 

Plaintiff Martin Melendez-Murillo states he was diagnosed with silicosis and progressive massive fibrosis in December 2022. (Declaration of Martin Melendez-Murillo ISO Mot. ¶1.) Plaintiff’s treating physician, Dr. Jane Fazio, M.D., indicates she last examined Plaintiff on March 20, 2024, and, at that time, his CT scans and pulmonary function tests showed progression of his disease. (Declaration of Jane Fazio ISO Mot. ¶ 3.) Dr. Fazio states there is no known cure for Plaintiff’s condition, and the damage to his lungs is not reversible. (Id. ¶ 4.) Dr. Fazio further states that it is reasonably medically likely that Plaintiff will die within six months absent a lung transplant, but such a procedure is inherently risky, and there is no way of knowing if or when donor organs will become available. (Id. ¶ 5.) Dr. Fazio contends that, based on her knowledge of Plaintiff’s condition and her knowledge of typical outcomes for this type of illness, there is a substantial medical doubt of Plaintiff’s survival beyond six months. (Id. ¶ 9.)

 

Dr. Fazio’s declaration was not accompanied by any medical documentation. Moreover, while the Declaration of David R. Donadio in support of the motion purported to attach certain medical records for the Court’s review, it does not appear that those materials were ever presented to the Court. (See Declaration of David R. Donadio ISO Mot.) In light of the “clear and convincing” standard for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36(d), the Court considers those materials necessary, especially to the extent those records informed Dr. Fazio’s testimony. The Court therefore ordered Plaintiffs to lodge the medical documentation referenced in that Declaration, and directed the parties to meet and confer regarding the course of discovery and pre-trial motions if preference were granted and present a joint statement describing the results of those discussions.

 

Plaintiffs submitted the requested medical documentation with the Declaration of Jason M. Rose, filed with the Court on October 4, 2024. These records include office visits and emergency room records for Plaintiff Melendez-Murillo from mid-August through late September 2024, as well as additional records from March 20 through May 30, 2024. (Declaration of Jason M. Rose ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-6.) Although these records describe the development of Plaintiff’s condition since 2022 (see, e.g., Exh. A. pp.1-2 [History of Present Illness]), they do not, as Defendant C&C North America states in its supplemental opposition, tend to support the claim made by Dr. Fazio that Plaintiff’s survival beyond six months is in serious doubt. Several of these records explicitly comment on the stability of Plaintiff’s respiratory condition. For example, the records from a September 4, 2024 emergency room visit for a separate medical issue indicate that Plaintiff’s condition was “stable from a respiratory standpoint.” (Rose Decl. Exh. B. p.3.) Crucially, the records from an August 29, 2024 visit to the UCLA Lung Transplant Program notes “concerns at this time regarding the patient’s candidacy for lung transplantation” including that Plaintiff “[w]arrants evaluation but may be too well for listing at this time.” (Rose Decl. Exh. C. p.6.) This evidence substantially undercuts Dr. Fazio’s credibility regarding the severity of Plaintiff Melendez-Murillo’s condition and the probability of his survival. Given the heightened “clear and convincing” standard necessary to demonstrate entitlement to preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36(d), the Court is not persuaded that Plaintiffs have carried their burden under this subdivision.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Trial Preference is DENIED.

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 30, 2024                   TRIAL DATE: May 27, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Moayed Harb v. Ford Motor Company, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV18736           

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Ford Motor Company

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Moayed Harb

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on August 8, 2023. Plaintiff purchased a 2019 Ford F150 equipped with a 10R80 transmission system which he alleges has a series of known defects causing hesitation, shuddering, and shifting issues.

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the first four causes of action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and the seventh cause of action under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend as to the first four causes of action and otherwise DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the first four causes of action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and the seventh cause of action under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

 

Legal Standard

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings is the functional equivalent to a general demurrer.  (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198).  Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994). Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice.  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-22).  The parties’ ability to prove their respective claims is of no concern.  (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 99.)  Though the Court must accept the allegations of the complaint and answer as true (Gerawan Farming, Inc. v. Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515), it will not do so for “conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or [judicially noticed] facts…” (Stevenson Real Estate Servs., Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Servs., Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-20).

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to grant or deny a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Colin P. Cronin in support of the motion states that the parties met and conferred via online Teams conference on September 23, 2024 regarding the issues raised in this motion. (Declaration of Colin P. Cronin ISO Mot. ¶ 8; see also Exh. 4.) The parties were not able to reach a resolution and agreed that the Court’s determination was necessary. (Id.) Defendant has therefore satisfied its statutory meet and confer obligations.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of (1) Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Requests for Admission Nos. 16, 17, and 24; and (2) a Certificate of Approval for BBB National Programs, Inc’s BBB Auto Line to operate as a state-certified arbitration program, issued by the California Department of Consumer Affairs’ Arbitration Certification Program.

 

            Defendant’s Request No. 1 is GRANTED as a set of judicial admissions. (Barsegian v. Kessler & Kessler (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 446, 451-52 [responses to requests for admission constitute judicial admissions]; Alameda County Waste Management Auth. V. Waste Connections US, Inc. (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 1162, 1174-75 [judicial admissions subject to judicial notice].)

 

            Defendant’s Request No. 2 is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(c) (official acts).

 

//

First Cause of Action: Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(d)

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action on the grounds that Plaintiff has admitted that the subject vehicle was purchased outside of California and is therefore not subject to the refund-or-replace provision of section 1793.2(d) of the Civil Code.

 

            In Cummins v. Superior Court, the California Supreme Court expressly held that the entire Song-Beverly Act, with particular reference to section 1793.2(d) only applies to goods sold in the State of California. (Cummins, Inc. v. Superior Court (2005) 36 Cal.4th 478, 493-94.) Defendant states that Plaintiff admitted, in Request for Admission No. 16, that the vehicle was purchased at Camelback Ford Lincoln, 1330 E. Camelback Road, Phoenix AZ 85014. (Cronin Decl. Exh. 2 No. 16.) As admissions in response to requests for admissions are judicial admissions that are properly the subject of judicial notice, Defendant may rely on this admission in support of the motion.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that Cummins does not address the question of where a vehicle is purchased as a matter of law. This contention is irrelevant.  Plaintiff has admitted that the vehicle was purchased in Arizona, and so the issue is not in dispute. Second, Plaintiff argues that, under Civil Code section 1795.8, the Song-Beverly Act applies to members of the armed forces even if the vehicle is not purchased in California. (See Civ. Code § 1795.8.) This contention is also irrelevant because Plaintiff has not alleged that he is a member of the armed forces subject to this exception, and arguments of counsel in opposition that this “may or may not be the case here” (opposition p.3:7-8) does not cure the deficiency identified in the pleadings.

 

            In light of Plaintiff’s judicial admission that the vehicle was not purchased in California, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action: Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(b)

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the second cause of action for the same reasons stated with respect to the first cause of action above. As the parties agree that these claims survive or fail together, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the second cause of action.

 

Third Cause of Action: Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(A)(3)

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the third cause of action for the same reasons stated with respect to the first cause of action above. As the parties agree that these claims survive or fail together, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the third cause of action.

 

//

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Implied Warranty of Merchantability

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth cause of action for the same reasons stated with respect to the first cause of action above. As the parties agree that these claims survive or fail together, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth cause of action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Violation of Magnuson Moss Warranty-Federal Trade Commission Improvements Act

 

            Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the seventh cause of action on the grounds that Plaintiff did not engage in the informal dispute settlement procedure mandated under the Magnuson Moss Act before bringing this claim.

 

            Under the Magnuson Moss Act, a warrantor may establish an informal dispute settlement procedure and require in a written warranty that the consumer resort to that procedure before pursuing a legal remedy under the Magnuson Moss Act respecting the warranty. (15 U.S.C. § 2310(a)(3).) Provided the informal dispute settlement procedure in the warranty meets the requirements promulgated by the Federal Trade Commission, the consumer may not commence a civil action under the Magnuson Moss Act without first pursuing the informal dispute settlement procedure. (Id.) The Federal Trade Commission has codified these requirements in Title 16 of the Code of Federal Regulations Part 703. Section 703.2 imposes duties on the warrantor regarding the manner and contents of the warrantor’s disclosure of the informal dispute settlement mechanism. (16 C.F.R § 703.2.) Defendant claims that the disclosure of the dispute resolution procedure, contained at pages 5 through 8 of the Warranty Guide attached to the Complaint, satisfy these notification requirements. (See Complaint Exh. A. pp. 5-8.) However, section 703.2 subdivision (a) states that a warrantor “shall not incorporate into the terms of a written warranty a Mechanism that fails to comply with the requirements contained in §§ 703.3 through 703.8 of this part.” (16 C.F.R. § 703.2(a).) Sections 703.3 through 703.8 impose minimum requirements on dispute resolution mechanisms including, inter alia, qualification of members (§ 703.4), day-to-day operations (§ 703.5), and recordkeeping (§ 703.6). Defendant makes no effort to demonstrate through judicially noticeable material that the dispute resolution mechanism identified in the Warranty Guide complies with these regulatory requirements. As compliance with the regulatory standards is a predicate to the informal dispute settlement requirement, the failure to address this element is fatal to Defendant’s argument.

 

            Defendant is therefore not entitled to judgment on the pleadings as to the seventh cause of action.

 

Leave to Amend

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff has not demonstrated how the Complaint might be amended to cure the deficiencies identified in this motion. Moreover, as Defendant has prevailed on the first four causes of action on the grounds that Plaintiff conclusively admitted facts establishing that the Song Beverly Act does not apply to the vehicle, the Court concludes that amendment of the first four causes of action would be futile.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED without leave to amend as to the first four causes of action and otherwise DENIED.
