DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV11937    Hearing Date: December 4, 2023    Dept: 50

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

JAYESH KUMAR,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

THE CITY LOS ANGELES, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

22STCV11937 [c/w

22STCV19257]

Hearing Date:

December 4, 2023

Hearing Time:

 2:00 p.m.

TENTATIVE RULING RE:

 

DEFENDANT CITY OF LOS ANGELES’ JOINT DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFFS HALLER, LLC AND SHAUN TAN’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT AND PLAINTIFF JAYESH KUMAR’S THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT;

 

DEFENDANTS’ JOINT MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFFS’ HALLER LLC AND SHAUN TAN’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTIONS

 

            Background

On April 7, 2022, Plaintiff Jayesh Kumar (“Kumar”) filed the instant action against a number of defendants, including the City of Los Angeles (the “City”) (herein, the “Kumar Action”).

 On June 8, 2023, Kumar filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) in the Kumar Action against the City, Haller, LLC (“Haller”) and a number of other defendants. The TAC alleges causes of action for (1) breach of lease, (2) nuisance, (3) trespass, and (4) dangerous condition of public property.   

            On June 13, 2022, Haller and Shaun Tan (“Tan”) filed an action against a number of defendants in the matter Haller, LLC, et al. v. The City of Los Angeles, et al., Case No. 22STCV19257 (the “Haller Action”). On June 8, 2023, Haller and Tan filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) in the Haller Action against the City, Hollywood Arts Collective LP, Hollywood Arts Collective LLC, Thomas Safran and Associates Development, Inc., Thomas Safran and Associates, Inc., Morley Construction Company, and Benchmark Contractors Inc. The FAC alleges causes of action for (1) inverse condemnation, (2) nuisance, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) trespass, and (5) negligence.

On August 2, 2023, the Court issued an Order in the Kumar Action indicating that “IT IS HEREBY ORDERED that Case Nos. 22STCV11937 (Kumar Action) and 22STCV19257 (Haller Action) be consolidated for all pretrial, discovery, and motion purposes only. Case No. 22STCV11937 (Kumar Action) will be the Lead Case. The operative pleadings filed in the Kumar Action and the Haller Action will remain separate. Defendants may file one (1) Joint Demurrer and/or Motion to Strike as to the Third Amended Complaint in the Kumar Action and the First Amended Complaint in the Haller Action to be noticed pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 1005 for and heard by the Court on September 26, 2023. If the issue of Haller being both a plaintiff [and] a defendant is resolved, the parties may submit a further stipulation to consolidate the cases for trial…”[1] 

            The City now demurs to the second, third, and fourth causes of action of the TAC in the Kumar Action. Kumar opposes.

The City also demurs to each of the causes of action of the FAC in the Haller Action. Haller and Tan oppose.

            In addition, the City, Morley Construction Company, Hollywood Arts Collective LP, Hollywood Arts Collective LLC, Thomas Safran & Associates, Inc., Thomas Safran and Associates Development, Inc., and Benchmark Contractor’s Inc. (collectively, the “Moving Parties”) move to strike portions of the FAC in the Haller Action. Haller and Tan oppose.

Demurrer

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

B.     The City’s Demurrer to the TAC in the Kumar Action

The City demurs to the second, third, and fourth causes of action alleged in the TAC in the Kumar Action.

1.      Allegations of the TAC in the Kumar Action

In the TAC in the Kumar Action, Kumar alleges that at all relevant times he is and was a tenant operating a hotel at 1624 Schrader Blvd., Los Angeles, California 90028 known as the Cielo Hotel (the “Cielo Property”). (TAC, ¶ 11.) Kumar leased the Cielo Property from Haller on or about June 29, 2020 for five years, at a base rent of $32,500 per month and a deposit of $97,500. (TAC, ¶ 12.)

Beginning in or around late 2020 and/or early 2021, defendants (other than Haller) began excavating the ground adjacent to/north of the Cielo Property for the purpose of building a structure at 1630 Schrader Boulevard, Los Angeles, CA 90028, known as the Hollywood Arts Collective (the “HAC Property”). (TAC, ¶ 14.) The City owns the real property upon which the HAC Property is being built. (TAC, ¶ 15.) Several defendants have entered into and/or are subject to a Development Agreement with the City for the development of and construction at the HAC Property. (TAC, ¶ 16.)

On or about April 2, 2021, or soon thereafter, extensive damage began appearing at the Cielo Property, including but not limited to cracks in the walls and misalignment of doors that formerly were aligned. (TAC, ¶ 21.) Kumar hired experts to examine the Cielo Property and was informed that the construction activity of defendants at the HAC Property had caused the foundation of the Cielo Property to become compromised and had caused the wall cracking and structure shift that Kumar had noticed. (TAC, ¶ 22.) Due to the damage, Kumar has been unable to operate a hotel at the Cielo Property, losing business revenue on an ongoing basis. (TAC, ¶ 23.)

2.      Second Cause of Action for Nuisance and Third Cause of Action for Trespass

In the demurrer, the City asserts that “[b]ecause the TAC fails to allege sufficient facts to support the statutory basis for liability against the City for trespass and nuisance these claims are barred by the Tort Claims Act...” (Demurrer at p. 16:4-5.)

The City notes that pursuant to Government Code section 815, subdivision (a), “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute…[a] public entity is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person.” “In other words, direct tort liability of public entities must be based on a specific statute declaring them to be liable, or at least creating some specific duty of care…Otherwise, the general rule of immunity for public entities would be largely eroded by the routine application of general tort principles.” (Eastburn v. Regional Fire Protection Authority (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1183.) 

In the second cause of action for nuisance and the third cause of action for trespass, Kumar alleges, inter alia, that “under Government Code sections 815.2 and 815.4…the City may be found liable for the tortious acts of the Developer Defendants.” (TAC, ¶¶ 34, 46.) The City asserts that here, “Kumar’s reliance on Government Code Sections 815.2 and 815.4 to allege trespass and nuisance against the City is misplaced because there are no factual allegations in the TAC that any of the Developer Defendants are employees or independent contractors of the City that would give rise to liability against a public entity.” (Demurrer at p. 14:11-14.)

As noted by the City, Government Code section 815.2, subdivision (a) provides that “[a] public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee or his personal representative.” In addition, Government Code section 815.4 provides that “[a] public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by a tortious act or omission of an independent contractor of the public entity to the same extent that the public entity would be subject to such liability if it were a private person. Nothing in this section subjects a public entity to liability for the act or omission of an independent contractor if the public entity would not have been liable for the injury had the act or omission been that of an employee of the public entity.”

In the opposition, Kumar asserts that he “alleges several allegations earlier in the TAC under the Second Restatement of Torts regarding the independent contractor relationship between the Developer Defendants and the City, sufficient to allow Plaintiff to sue under Government Code sections 815.2 and 815.4.” (Opp’n at p. 8:22-25, citing TAC, ¶ 19, emphasis omitted.) But as noted by the City, Kumar does not appear to specifically allege that any of the defendants are independent contractor(s) of the City. Rather, Kumar alleges that “several Defendants herein have entered into and/or are subject to a Development Agreement with the City for the development of and construction at the HAC Property…” (TAC, ¶ 16.) This allegation does not specify whether any such defendants are independent contractor(s) of the City.

Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains the City’s demurrer to the second and third causes of action of Kumar’s TAC, with leave to amend.

3.      Fourth Cause of Action for Dangerous Condition of Public Property

The City also asserts that Kumar’s fourth cause of action for dangerous condition of public property fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

The City cites to Government Code section 835, which provides as follows:

“Except as provided by statute, a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and that either:

(a) A negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or

 

(b) The public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.”

The City notes that “[t]he limited and statutory nature of governmental liability mandates that claims against public entities be specifically pleaded. Accordingly, a claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.” (Brenner v. City of El Cajon (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 434, 439 [internal citations omitted].)

In the demurrer, the City asserts that “Kumar’s generalized claim for dangerous condition of public property lacks specificity of any alleged dangerous condition…or how any condition was dangerous.” (Demurrer at p. 17:6-7.) But as noted by Kumar, he alleges that “[a]s described in detail above, the design, construction, installation, operation, repair, inspection, servicing, supervision, management, and maintenance of the HAC constituted, and continues to constitute, a dangerous condition of public property.” (TAC, ¶ 54.) Kumar notes that he further alleges that “[b]eginning in or around late 2020 and/or early 2021, Defendants (other than HALLER) began excavating the ground adjacent to/north of the Cielo Property for the purpose of building a structure at 1630 Schrader Boulevard, Los Angeles, CA 90028, known as the Hollywood Arts Collective (‘HAC’ or ‘HAC Property’).” (TAC, ¶ 14.) Kumar notes that he alleges that “on or about April 2, 2021, or soon thereafter, extensive damage began appearing at the Cielo Property, including but not limited to cracks in the walls and misalignment of doors that formerly were aligned,” and that “Plaintiff hired experts to examine the Cielo Property and thereupon was informed and believes and thereon alleges that the construction activity of Defendants, and each of them, at the HAC Property had caused the foundation of the CIELO Property to become compromised and had caused the wall cracking and structure shift that Plaintiff had noticed. Plaintiff’s experts also determined that the Cielo Property constitutes a hazard and that it is unsafe to continue to conduct a hotel operation or any business at all therein.” (TAC, ¶¶ 21-22.)

In the demurrer, the City also argues that “Kumar also fails to allege how or what actual or constructive knowledge of any specific dangerous condition the City knew or should have known.” (Demurrer at p. 17:7-9.) Kumar counters that “[i]n order to recover under Government Code section 835, it is not necessary for plaintiff to prove a negligent act and notice; either negligence or notice will suffice.” (Curtis v. State of California ex rel. Dept. of Transportation (1982) 128 Cal.App.3d 668, 693 [emphasis in original].)  

The City also asserts that “the TAC lumps the City of LA with nine…other ‘Defendants’ but do [sic] not specify what acts and/or omissions by the City or what the City knew or should have known about the public property to give rise to this cause of action.” (Demurrer at p. 18:1-3.) Kumar does not appear to dispute this point in the opposition. As set forth above, Kumar notes that the TAC alleges that “[b]eginning in or around late 2020 and/or early 2021, Defendants (other than HALLER) began excavating the ground adjacent to/north of the Cielo Property for the purpose of building a structure at 1630 Schrader Boulevard, Los Angeles, CA 90028, known as the Hollywood Arts Collective…” (TAC, ¶ 14, emphasis added.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains the City’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action of Kumar’s TAC, with leave to amend.  

C.     The City’s Demurrer to the FAC in the Haller Action

The City also demurs to each of the causes of action alleged in the FAC in the Haller Action.

1.      Allegations of the FAC in the Haller Action 

In the FAC in the Haller Action, Haller and Tan allege that the Haller Action stems from damage to their two-story hotel building located at 1624 Schrader Blvd., Los Angeles, California 90028 (“the Building”). (FAC, ¶ 1.) Haller and Tan allege that at all relevant times, they were and are the owners of real, business, and personal property at 1624 Schrader Blvd., Los Angeles, California 90028. (FAC, ¶ 14.)

Haller and Tan allege that damage to the Building was caused by the negligent construction, development, inspection, and supervision of the Hollywood Arts Collective (“HAC”), which is located directly north of the Building at 1630 Schrader Blvd. in Los Angeles, California 90028. (FAC, ¶ 1.) The HAC is an income and rent restricted affordable housing complex set to open in Summer 2023. (FAC, ¶ 2.) The HAC is allegedly “owned, designed, constructed, installed, operated, repaired, inspected, serviced, supervised, managed and maintained” by various companies, including but not limited to the City. (FAC, ¶ 2.)

The construction of the HAC began in February 2021 and shortly thereafter, Haller and Tan discovered damage to the Building only feet away from where Defendants were performing subterranean construction. (FAC, ¶ 2.) Haller and Tan allege that “[a]s a result of Defendants’ negligent and improper design, construction, installation, operation, repair, inspection, servicing, supervision, management and maintenance of the HAC and the surrounding area, Plaintiffs suffered damages including but not limited to physical damaged to the Property, reduced value of the Property, reduced safety and marketability of the Property, out of pocket costs to mitigate the damage and repair the Property, costs of disposal, repair and/or replacement of damaged Property, loss of rent, costs for attorneys’ fees and such further costs and expenses according to proof.” (FAC, ¶ 17.)

2.      First Cause of Action for Inverse Condemnation

“Article I, section 19…of the California Constitution requires that just compensation be paid when private property is taken or damaged for public use. Therefore, a public entity may be liable in an inverse condemnation action for any physical injury to real property proximately caused by a public improvement as deliberately designed and constructed, whether or not that injury was foreseeable, and in the absence of fault by the public entity.” (Souza v. Silver Dev. Co. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 165, 170.) “To state a cause of action for inverse condemnation, the plaintiff must allege the defendant substantially participated in the planning, approval, construction, or operation of a public project or improvement which proximately caused injury to plaintiff’s property.” (Pacific Shores Property Owners Assn. v. Department of Fish & Wildlife (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 12, 43.) “The necessary finding is that the wrongful act be part of the deliberate design, construction, or maintenance of the public improvement.” (Arreola v. County of Monterey (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 722, 742.)  

The City asserts that the first cause of action for inverse condemnation fails because Haller and Tan fail to allege how the City substantially participated in the planning, approval, construction, or operation of a public project or improvement that caused injury to Haller and Tan’s property. But as noted by Haller and Tan, the first cause of action for inverse condemnation alleges that “as the owner of 1630 Schrader and the HAC, the City was directly and substantially involved, along with the other Defendants, and approved and participated in each and every step of the planning, design, construction, installation, operation, repair, inspection, servicing, supervision, management and maintenance of the HAC. The City and the Defendants were aware that their actions and inactions, including the plan, design, construction, and installation of the HAC would or could cause damage to Plaintiffs’ Building and proceeded with its HAC project for the benefit of the public.” (FAC, ¶ 21.)

The City also asserts that Haller and Tan “allege that all Defendants collectively damaged Plaintiffs without differentiating each parties’ individual, specific role in the construction and development of the HAC,” (Demurrer at p. 5:16-18), but Haller and Tan allege that “the City was the entity that was solely in charge of the inspection and approval of the plan, design, construction, and installation of the HAC.” (FAC, ¶ 23.)

In addition, the City argues that the FAC contains no allegations that the HAC is a “public project or improvement” as required to establish inverse condemnation. But as noted by Haller and Tan, they allege that “the City ignored those potential dangers to Plaintiffs’ Building and proceeded with its HAC project for the benefit of the public.” (FAC, ¶ 23, emphasis added.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules the City’s demurrer to the first cause of action of Haller and Tan’s FAC.

3.      Third Cause of Action for Dangerous Condition of Public Property

The City asserts that Haller and Tan’s third cause of action for dangerous condition of public property fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. As set forth above, Government Code section 835 provides as follows:

“Except as provided by statute, a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and that either:

(a) A negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or

 

(b) The public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.”

            In the demurrer, the City asserts that Haller and Tan’s “claim for dangerous condition of public property contains generalized claims of an alleged dangerous condition without any specificity as to what manner or how the condition constitutes a dangerous condition at the time of injury.” (Demurrer at p. 7:1-3.) But as acknowledged by the City, Haller and Tan allege that “[a]t all relevant times, Defendants’ design, construction, installation, operation, repair, inspection, servicing, supervision, management and maintenance of the HAC constituted, and continues to constitute, a dangerous condition of public property. More specifically, Defendants began digging substantially below the natural grade of 1630 Schrader. Plaintiffs are informed and believe, and based on that information and belief, that the substantial subterranean digging caused the surrounding areas to become unstable and shift and slide towards the substantially dug part of Defendants’ property creating a dangerous condition on the area surrounding the dig. Moreover, Defendants’ dig area was so close to the neighboring properties and was so deep that the dangerous condition extended well on to and under the neighboring properties, including Plaintiffs’ Property. That dangerous condition caused Plaintiffs’ Building to shift down and along with the land, towards to the dig area causing severe damage to Plaintiffs’ Building and rendered it unhabitable and likely a complete loss.” (FAC, ¶ 36.)

            The City also asserts in the demurrer that Haller and Tan “lump the City with the acts of all ‘Defendants’ without any specific facts related to the City’s conduct or as to…each parties’ respective conduct that is actionable.” (Demurrer at p. 7:3-5.) Haller and Tan do not appear to respond to or dispute this point in the opposition. As noted by the City, “[t]he limited and statutory nature of governmental liability mandates that claims against public entities be specifically pleaded. Accordingly, a claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.” (Brenner v. City of El Cajon, supra, 113 Cal.App.4th at p. 439 [internal citations omitted].) 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains the City’s demurrer to the third cause of action of Haller and Tan’s FAC, with leave to amend.  

 

4.      Second Cause of Action for Nuisance, Fourth Cause of Action for Trespass, and Fifth Cause of Action for Negligence

The City asserts that “the FAC fails to allege sufficient facts to support the statutory basis for liability against the City for trespass, nuisance, or negligence…” (Demurrer at p. 10:3-4.)

As set forth above, the City notes that pursuant to Government Code section 815, subdivision (a), “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute…[a] public entity is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person.” “In other words, direct tort liability of public entities must be based on a specific statute declaring them to be liable, or at least creating some specific duty of care…Otherwise, the general rule of immunity for public entities would be largely eroded by the routine application of general tort principles.” (Eastburn v. Regional Fire Protection Authority, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1183.) 

In the second cause of action for nuisance, Haller and Tan allege, inter alia, that “[t]he City is liable for the actions of its employees and its contractors pursuant to Gov. C. §§815.2, 815.4.” (FAC, ¶ 30.) In the fourth cause of action for trespass and the fifth cause of action for negligence, Haller and Tan similarly allege that “[t]he City is liable for the actions of its employees and its contractors pursuant to Gov. C. §§815.2, 815.4.” (FAC, ¶¶ 46, 51.)

The City asserts that “Plaintiffs fail to allege sufficient facts under Government Codes [sections] 815.2 and 815.4 to maintain a trespass, nuisance, or negligence cause of action against the City, a public entity.” (Demurrer at p. 8:7-9.)

As set forth above, Government Code section 815.2, subdivision (a) provides that “[a] public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee or his personal representative.” The City argues that here, “Government Code Section 815.2 does not apply because the FAC contains no allegations that any party, entity, or Defendants are employees or contractors of the City working within the scope of their employment giving rise to Plaintiffs’ claims for which the City would be liable.” (Demurrer at pp. 8:27-9:2.)

In addition, as discussed, Government Code section 815.4 provides that “[a] public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by a tortious act or omission of an independent contractor of the public entity to the same extent that the public entity would be subject to such liability if it were a private person. Nothing in this section subjects a public entity to liability for the act or omission of an independent contractor if the public entity would not have been liable for the injury had the act or omission been that of an employee of the public entity.” The City asserts that “[s]ection 815.4 also does not apply because there are no allegations in the FAC that any defendants are independent contractors of the City that caused Plaintiffs’ damages.” (Demurrer at p. 9:5-7)

In the opposition, Haller and Tan state that they “request leave to amend regarding the Nuisance, Trespass, and Negligence causes of action as the FAC was inadvertently missing necessary language.” (Opp’n at p. 8:24-26.) Thus, Haller and Tan appear to concede that these causes of action are insufficiently pled.

Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains the City’s demurrer to the second, fourth, and fifth causes of action of Haller and Tan’s FAC, with leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

A court may strike any “¿irrelevant, false, or improper matter¿inserted in any pleading¿” or any part of a pleading “¿not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 436¿.) “¿The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437¿.) 

The Moving Parties move to strike the request in Haller and Tan’s FAC “[f]or exemplary damages.” (FAC, Prayer for Damages, ¶ 2.)

 A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged do not rise to the level of “¿malice, oppression or fraud¿” required to support a punitive damages award. (¿Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 64¿.) As noted by the Moving Parties, “malice” means “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” means “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” means “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

The Moving Parties assert that the “FAC pleads no facts whatsoever alleging fraudulent, malicious, or oppressive conduct by any of the Defendants to support their prayer for exemplary damages.” (Mot. at p. 6:21-22, emphasis omitted.)

In the opposition, Haller and Tan assert that they have alleged “sufficient factual allegations of Defendants’ willful and conscious disregard for the safety of others, including [Haller and Tan].” (Opp’n at p. 3:1-2.) Haller and Tan point to, inter alia, their allegation that “the City and the Defendants were aware that their actions and inactions, including the plan, design, construction, and installation of the HAC would or could cause damage to Plaintiffs’ Building and that the City ignored those potential dangers to Plaintiffs’ Building and proceeded with its HAC project for the benefit of the public.” (FAC, ¶ 23.) But this allegation does not reference any impacts to the safety of Haller and Tan or others. Haller and Tan do not appear to point to any specific allegations of “conduct which is carried on by the [the Moving Parties] with a willful and conscious disregard of the…safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).)

The Moving Parties also note that pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b), “[a]n employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

The Moving Parties assert that there, Haller and Tan “do not allege any of Defendants’ employees’ malicious conduct, if any, was perpetrated, authorized, or knowingly ratified by a corporate officer, director, or managing agent.” (Mot. at p. 7:11-13.) In the opposition, the Moving Parties assert that “[t]he Court should exercise its discretion and grant leave to amend to include the additional language regarding the vicarious liability of the corporate entities.” (Opp’n at p. 6:1-3.) Thus, the Moving Parties appear to concede that the allegations of the FAC do not satisfy Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b). The Moving Parties indicate that “[i]f provided leave to amend the FAC, Plaintiffs can and will amend the FAC to add in the additional language regarding the officers’, directors’, and managing agents’ perpetration, authorization, and ratification of the alleged wrongful conduct.” (Fakhreddine Decl., ¶ 9.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the Moving Parties’ motion to strike, with leave to amend.

Conclusion 

Based on the foregoing, the City’s demurrer to the second, third, and fourth causes of action of Kumar’s TAC is sustained, with leave to amend.

The Court orders Kumar to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this Order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days of this Order, the City is ordered to file and serve its answer to Kumar’s TAC within 30 days of the date of this Order.¿¿ 

In addition, based on the foregoing, the City’s demurrer to the first cause of action of Haller and Tan’s FAC is overruled. The City’s demurrer to the second, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action of Haller and Tan’s FAC is sustained, with leave to amend. The Moving Parties’ motion to strike “[f]or exemplary damages” (FAC, p. 11:16) from Haller and Tan’s FAC is granted, with leave to amend.

The Court orders Haller and Tan to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this Order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days of this Order, the Moving Parties are ordered to file and serve their answer(s) to Haller and Tan’s FAC within 30 days of the date of this Order.¿¿ 

The City is ordered to provide notice of this Order.

TENTATIVE RULING RE:

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFFS’ FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

On June 13, 2022, Haller, LLC (“Haller”) and Shaun Tan (“Tan”) filed the instant action (the “Haller Action”) against a number of defendants.

Haller and Tan filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) in the instant action on June 8, 2023 against Defendants City of Los Angeles (the “City”), Hollywood Arts Collective LP, Hollywood Arts Collective LLC, Thomas Safran and Associates Development, Inc., Thomas Safran and Associates. Inc., Morley Construction Company, and Benchmark Contractors Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”). The FAC in the Haller Action alleges causes of action for (1) inverse condemnation, (2) nuisance, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) trespass, and (5) negligence.

On April 7, 2022, Plaintiff Jayesh Kumar (“Kumar”) filed an action against a number of defendants in the matter Jayesh Kumar v. The City of Los Angeles, et al., Case No. 22STCV11937 (the “Kumar Action”). On June 8, 2023, Kumar filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) in the Kumar Action against the City and a number of other defendants. The TAC alleges causes of action for (1) breach of lease, (2) nuisance, (3) trespass, and (4) dangerous condition of public property. 

The Court notes that on August 2, 2023, the Court issued an Order in the Kumar Action indicating that “IT IS HEREBY ORDERED that Case Nos. 22STCV11937 (Kumar Action) and

22STCV19257 (Haller Action) be consolidated for all pretrial, discovery, and motion purposes only. Case No. 22STCV11937 (Kumar Action) will be the Lead Case. The operative pleadings filed in the Kumar Action and the Haller Action will remain separate. Defendants may file one (1) Joint Demurrer and/or Motion to Strike as to the Third Amended Complaint in the Kumar Action and the First Amended Complaint in the Haller Action to be noticed pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 1005 for and heard by the Court on September 26, 2023. If the issue of Haller being both a plaintiff and a defendant is resolved, the parties may submit a further stipulation to consolidate the cases for trial…”

            After the Court issued the August 2, 2023 Order, Defendants filed a motion to strike portions of the FAC in the instant Haller Action on August 23, 2023. Defendants move to strike “Item 2 of Prayer on Page 11, Line 16, which reads: ‘2. Exemplary Damages…’” No opposition to this motion was filed.  

As an initial matter, it is unclear why Defendants filed a motion to strike portions of the FAC in both the instant action and the Kumar Action. As set forth above, the Court’s August 2, 2023 Order provides, inter alia, that “Defendants may file one (1) Joint Demurrer and/or Motion to Strike as to the Third Amended Complaint in the Kumar Action and the First Amended Complaint in the Haller Action…” In any event, in light of the Court’s tentative ruling on “Defendants’ Joint Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Haller LLC and Shaun Tan’s First Amended Complaint” filed in the Kumar Action, the instant motion is moot.

TENTATIVE RULING RE: 

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT

 

 

Background

            Plaintiff Shlomo Gottlieb (“Gottlieb”) filed this action on December 14, 2022 against Defendant 859 S. Bedford LLC (“859 S. Bedford”). The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) premises liability and (2) violation of Federal Fair Housing Act.

            On July 26, 2022, 859 S. Bedford filed a Complaint against Gottlieb in the matter 859 S. Bedford LLC v. Shlomo Gottlieb, et al., Case No. 22STCV24144. On January 6, 2023, the Court issued a minute order in the instant action providing, inter alia, that “[t]he Court finds that the following cases, 22STCV24144 and 22STCV38854, are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 22STCV24144 is the lead case. For good cause shown, said cases are assigned to Judge Teresa A. Beaudet in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse for all purposes.”

In Case No. 22STCV24144, 859 S. Bedford alleges that “[o]n or about January 6, 1997, [Gottlieb] entered into a written lease agreement with [859 S. Bedford’s] predecessor in interest for the subject premises located at 859 S. Bedford Street, No. 5, Los Angeles, CA 90035...” (Compl., ¶ 6.) 859 S. Bedford alleges that “as a result of [Gottlieb’s] inability to properly maintain the Subject Premises or to specially take care of his fish tank, the tank’s pipes providing fresh water into the tank and/or to recycle water in the tank, started leaking that was not attended to nor fixed by [Gottlieb]. As a result the entire apartment where [Gottlieb] resided in as well as the apartments below were flooded with water that had gathered in [Gottlieb’s] apartment and which had traveled down to the units below.” (Compl., ¶ 13.)

            859 S. Bedford now demurs to both causes of action of the Complaint in the instant action. Gottlieb opposes.

Discussion

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

B.     Allegations of the Complaint

In the Complaint, Gottlieb alleges that he “is and was at all relevant times herein an individual residing at 859 S. Bedford Ave., Apartment Number 5, Los Angeles, CA 90035” (herein, the “Premises”). (Compl., ¶ 1.) 859 S. Bedford is the owner of the Premises as well as six other apartments at 859 S. Bedford, Los Angeles, CA 90035. (Compl., ¶ 4.)

In or about late December 2021 and January 2022 Gottlieb slipped and fell at the Premises. (Compl., ¶ 9.) “At or about the same time [Gottlieb] experienced water intrusion in his apartment, including water that had leaked from the fish aquarium he kept at the Premises.” (Compl., ¶ 10.)

Gottlieb alleges that “[859 S. Bedford] had failed to keep the Premises in good repair and condition prior to December, 2021. [Gottlieb] slipped and fell as a result of such failure.” (Compl., ¶ 12.) Gottlieb alleges that “[859 S. Bedford] did not make appropriate repairs until after ordered to do so by the City [o]f Los Angeles in or about March, 2022.” (Compl., ¶ 12.) 

Gottlieb further alleges that he “was forced to vacate the Premises for about a month while [859 S. Bedford] repaired the Premises. [859 S. Bedford] promised to compensate [Gottlieb] to defray the cost of an alternative residence while the repairs were being made but [859 S. Bedford] has never compensated [Gottlieb] for the time he had to live at an alternative residence. [859 S. Bedford] nonetheless required [Gottlieb] to pay, and [Gottlieb] continued to pay rent to [859 S. Bedford] at all times.” (Compl., ¶ 13.)

In the first cause of action for “premises liability – negligence,” Gottlieb alleges that 859 S. Bedford “failed to maintain and repair the premises in violation of the law, including but not limited to city codes and ordinances, and his Lease for the Premises, and in that the structure, carpet and wooden floors of the premises were in poor and dangerous condition and repair.” (Compl., ¶ 16.) Gottlieb alleges that “[i]n or about late December 2021 or early January, 2022 [Gottlieb] fell two times inside the Premises and suffered and continues to suffer, physical, mental and emotional injury and suffering as a result of the unsafe conditions at the Premises and according to proof at the time of trial.” (Compl., ¶ 19.)

In the second cause of action for violation of the “Federal Fair Housing Act,” Gottlieb alleges that that 859 S. Bedford “discriminated and has continued discriminate against [Gottlieb] on account if [sic] his disability in that it provided inferior housing in comparison to the other apartment units at the Property and in violation of the law; harassed him; disparaged him to the public; threatened to evict him and failed to compensate him when forced to vacate the Premises so the premises could be made lawfully habitable and then initiated legal proceedings in bad faith.” (Compl., ¶ 23.)

C.     Demurrer

In the demurrer, 859 S. Bedford first asserts that “the two causes of action have been asserted regarding events and claims addressed in Defendant’s Action…Plaintiff was obligated and should have asserted his or her claims in that action. Plaintiff clearly could have filed a cross-complaint and elected not to do so.” (Demurrer at p. 9:2-5.) It appears 859 S. Bedford is referring to the matter 859 S. Bedford LLC v. Shlomo Gottlieb, et al., Case No. 22STCV24144. As set forth above, on January 6, 2023, the Court issued a minute order in the instant action providing, inter alia, that “[t]he Court finds that the following cases, 22STCV24144 and 22STCV38854, are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 22STCV24144 is the lead case…”

859 S. Bedford cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 426.30, subdivision (a), which provides that “[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute, if a party against whom a complaint has been filed and served fails to allege in a cross-complaint any related cause of action which (at the time of serving his answer to the complaint) he has against the plaintiff, such party may not thereafter in any other action assert against the plaintiff the related cause of action not pleaded.”

In the opposition, Gottlieb asserts that “the triggering action/transaction in Defendant’s Complaint is the leakage of Plaintiff’s fish tank. Plaintiffs Complaint for Premises Liability and Violation of the Federal Fair Housing Act, as explained above and is apparent from the face of the Complaint, involves different and distinct transactions/occurrences and alleged wrongful conduct of [859 S. Bedford] herein…” (Opp’n at p. 5:2-5.) The Court notes that 859 S. Bedford did not file any reply in support of the demurrer and thus does not dispute this point. 

The Court notes that Code of Civil Procedure section 426.10, subdivision (c) provides that “[r]elated cause of action” means “a cause of action which arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which the plaintiff alleges in his complaint.” 859 S. Bedford does not discuss this standard in the demurrer or explain how the causes of action alleged in the Complaint in the instant action purportedly “arise[] out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which [859 S. Bedford] alleges in [its] complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c).) The Court thus does not find that 859 S. Bedford has demonstrated in its demurrer that Gottlieb “fails to allege in a cross-complaint any related cause of action which (at the time of serving his answer to the complaint) he has against [859 S. Bedford]…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.30, subd. (a).)

            859 S. Bedford also asserts that “the complaint as asserted, fails to state a cause of action for the claims asserted in either the first or the second cause of action.” (Demurrer at p. 9:7-9.) In the “factual background” section of the demurrer, 859 S. Bedford argues that Gottlieb has “failed to state any material and relevant facts in support of his claims and has brought this action without any specific factual basis in support any of the two claims/causes of action asserted herein.” (Demurrer at p. 6:6-8.) However, the Court notes that 859 S. Bedford’s demurrer does not appear to contain any discussion of the elements of Gottlieb’s causes of action. Thus, it is unclear what facts 859 S. Bedford contends Gottlieb purportedly failed to allege in the Complaint.  

In the opposition, Plaintiff cites to CACI No. 1000, which provides that to establish a claim for premises liability, the plaintiff must prove all of the following: 1. That defendant [owned/leased/occupied/controlled] the property; 2. That defendant was negligent in the use or maintenance of the property; 3. That plaintiff was harmed; and 4. That defendant’s negligence was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.

Gottlieb asserts that here, he has alleged facts sufficient to state a cause of action for premises liability. Gottlieb notes that the Complaint alleges that 859 S. Bedford is the owner of the Premises, and that 859 S. Bedford “had failed to keep the Premises in good repair and condition prior to December, 2021. [Gottlieb] slipped and fell as a result of such failure.” (Compl., ¶¶ 4, 12.) Gottlieb alleges that “[i]n or about late December 2021 or early January, 2022 [Gottlieb] fell two times inside the Premises.” (Compl., ¶ 19.) Gottlieb further alleges that 859 S. Bedford “knew or should have known that the Premises were not being maintained in a safe condition and that the Premises were being operated by [859 S. Bedford] in violation of city codes and ordinances and the law and in breach of the Lease between [Gottlieb] and [859 S. Bedford].” (Compl., ¶ 17.) As set forth above, 859 S. Bedford did not file any reply in support of the demurrer and thus does not address the foregoing points.

As to the second cause of action for violation of the Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. section 3601 et seq.), Gottlieb cites to 42 U.S.C. section 3604, subdivision (f)(2), which provides that “[a]s made applicable by section 803 [42 USCS § 3603] and except as exempted by sections 803(b) and 807 [42 USCS §§ 3603(b), 3607], it shall be unlawful…To discriminate against any person in the terms, conditions, or privileges of sale or rental of a dwelling, or in the provision of services or facilities in connection with such dwelling, because of a handicap of—(A) that person; or (B) a person residing in or intending to reside in that dwelling after it is so sold, rented, or made available; or (C) any person associated with that person.”  Gottlieb notes that in the second cause of action, he alleges that “[a]t all times mentioned herein [859 S. Bedford] discriminated and has continued [sic] discriminate against Plaintiff on account if [sic] his disability in that it provided inferior housing in comparison to the other apartment units at the Property and in violation of the law; harassed him; disparaged him to the public; threatened to evict him and failed to compensate him when forced to vacate the Premises…” (Compl., ¶ 23.) As discussed, 859 S. Bedford did not file any reply and does not respond to this point.

Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules 859 S. Bedford’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the Complaint.

Conclusion  

Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules 859 S. Bedford’s demurrer to Gottlieb’s first and second causes of action.

The Court orders 859 S. Bedford to file and serve its answer to the Complaint within 10 days of the date of this order.¿
