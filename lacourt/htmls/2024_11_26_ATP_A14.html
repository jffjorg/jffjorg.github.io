Case Number: 21STCV10112    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a medical malpractice action. Plaintiff Saundra Putziger (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she was suffering from cervical pain as a result of a car collision. Beginning in or about March 2, 2020, Plaintiff became the patient of Brian Lai, M.D. (“Dr. Lai”) to treat that pain. On March 5, 2020, Dr. Lai administered an epidural injection on Plaintiff, which resulted in severe and permanent injuries to her, including paralysis, all of which are the subject of the Complaint. At all relevant times, Dr. Lai was an employee or contractor of Defendant Advanced Pain Medical Group, Inc. (“APMG”), and Defendant Shira Amor Bizaoui (“Bizaoui”) was an employee of Defendant Antelope Valley Specialty Surgical Center, LLC (“AVSSC”). Bizaoui acted as a California-certified registered nurse anesthetist for the epidural injection.

 

On March 15, 2021, Plaintiff filed her operative Complaint asserting one cause of action for medical malpractice.

 

On April 22, 2021, Bizaoui filed her Answer.

 

On May 20, 2021, Dr. Lai filed his Answer.

 

On June 24, 2021, APMG filed its Answer.

 

On November 23, 2022, Bizaoui filed her Motion for Summary Judgment, which Plaintiff opposed.

 

On February 8, 2023, the case was transferred and reassigned to this Court.

On July 18, 2023, the Court denied Bizaoui’s motion for summary judgment.

 

On October 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motions for terminating, issue, and/or evidentiary sanctions against AVSSC and Dr. Lai.

 

On November 13, 2024, Dr. Lai filed his opposition. AVSSC and APMG filed their opposition to the motion directed at AVSSC.

 

On November 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed her reply to the AVSSC-APMG opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed her reply to Dr. Lai’s opposition.

 

Jury trial is set for February 14, 2025.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Imposing Issue, Evidentiary, and Terminating Sanctions –  “Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010(a)(1).)

 

If a party engages in the misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b) [“The court may impose an issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses”]; subd. (c) [“The court may impose an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence”]; & subd. (d) [“The court may impose a terminating sanction by one of the following orders: ¶ (1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process. ¶ (2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed. ¶ (3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party. ¶ (4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party”].)

 

However, “absent exceptional circumstances, the court shall not impose sanctions on a party or any attorney of a party for failure to provide electronically stored information [“ESI”] that has been lost, damaged, altered, or overwritten as the result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(f) [“Section 2023.030(f)”]; Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1133 (“Victor Valley”) [referring to Section 2023.030(f) as a “safe-harbor provision”].)

 

In addition, “discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)

 

“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390 (“Los Defensores”).)

 

“‘But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].) “The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering [1] the totality of the circumstances: [2] [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; [3] the detriment to the propounding party; and [4] the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Ibid.)

Discussion

 

Application – As an initial matter, since the two motions make the same arguments, the Court will consider them together while keeping in mind that AVSSC and Dr. Lai are represented by different counsel and filed oppositions making slightly different arguments.

 

Plaintiff moves for an order imposing issue, evidentiary, or terminating sanctions against Defendants AVSSC and Dr. Lai, arguing that the defendants failed to save or preserve (1) fluoroscopic and (2) contralateral oblique images of Plaintiff.

 

According to Plaintiff, Dr. Lai testified during his deposition that he takes fluoroscopic images to help guide the placement of the epidural needle and catheter, and the contralateral oblique images to show the depth of the epidural needle and whether the needle is in the proper place. Dr. Lai also testified that he does not know where those photos are.

 

Plaintiff suggests that the images are relevant because (1) she is alleging that Dr. Lai committed malpractice when he injected the epidural directly into her spinal cord, and (2) the images show the epidural needle and catheter in her spinal cord.

 

According to Plaintiff, AVSSC and Dr. Lai must have intentionally destroyed, or negligently lost those images for the following reasons.

 

First, there was no doubt that the defendants knew something went wrong during Plaintiff’s epidural procedure. Specifically, Dr. Lai’s own notes showed that Plaintiff required ventilation support, she was paralyzed, and had a palsy. Dr. Lai even called an ambulance after the incident, and he expressed concern in text messages with Defendant Bizaoui, who was working with him that day.

 

Second, it is beyond doubt that the defendants had a record of those images because AVSSC’s PMQ, Rebecca Riedel, testified that she audited Plaintiff’s medical records the day after the epidural and found that the fluoroscopic images were already in the patient’s chart. Moreover, witness Tommy Nessan (“Nessan”) testified that he was instructed about six (6) months after the incident to look for more images. Nessan apparently found more images, and even instructed a medical assistant to print them out.

 

Third, on October 2, 2020, Plaintiff sent the defendants an intent to sue letter, which should have indicated to the defendants that they must save all potential evidence.

 

For those reasons, Plaintiff asks the Court to impose terminating sanctions on AVSSC and Dr. Lai by striking Defendants’ Answer and affirmative defenses. Alternatively, she asks the Court to impose issue sanctions (e.g., find as established the fact that the defendants breached their duty to Plaintiff resulting in Plaintiff’s injuries) or evidentiary sanctions (e.g., preclude the defendants from offering any evidence, argument, or examination stating that they were not negligent).

 

In its opposition, AVSSC argues that Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally defective because it is not based on any failure to obey a discovery court order and Plaintiff has failed to state the legal authority she is relying on to bring the motion. Dr. Lai also makes the latter procedural argument. (Dr. Lai’s Opposition, pp. 9:24-10:7.)

 

The Court disagrees the instant motions needed to be based on underlying discovery orders. As stated under the Legal Standard section of this ruling, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 allows a Court to impose issue, evidentiary, and terminating sanctions for misuse of the discovery process. According to the California Supreme Court, “intentional spoliation [of evidence] ‘[is] … a misuse of discovery within the meaning’ of [Code of Civil Procedure] section 2023.010.” (City of Los Angeles v. PricewaterhouseCoopers, LLP (2024) 17 Cal.5th 46, 71; see also Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1139 (“Victor Valley”) [“Section 2023.030 provides that a trial court may impose monetary and/or nonmonetary sanctions on a party or the party's attorney for ‘misuse of the discovery process.’ [Footnote omitted.] ‘Among other forms of sanctions, the court may “impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.” ([§ 2023.030], subd. (b).) The court may also prohibit the party from introducing designated matters in evidence. (Id., subd. (c).)’ [Citations.] And, in extreme cases, the trial court may issue terminating or contempt sanctions. (§ 2023.030, subds. (d), (e).) ¶ One serious form of discovery abuse is the spoliation of evidence, which is defined as the destruction or alteration of relevant evidence or the failure to preserve evidence for another party’s use in pending or future litigation” (emphasis added)].)

 

Given the above case law, the Court also finds AVSSC’s other procedural arguments (e.g., that Plaintiff’s motion omits the denial of their ex parte motion to compel) unpersuasive.

 

As for the defendants’ argument that Plaintiff failed to cite the applicable legal authority, the Court has not found (and the defendants have not cited) any law holding the motions must be denied because of that defect.

 

On the merits, AVSSC argues that sanctions are not warranted for the following reasons.

 

First, all of the images that ever existed were produced early in the case. Contrary to Plaintiff’s argument that Nessan “found more images” on the fluoroscopic machine and that he was instructed to “print more images,” Nessan testified that when he looked at the images on the machine, he found only two images. Those two images have already been produced in this litigation. Plaintiff has failed to prove there were other images and that the defendants willfully destroyed those images.

 

Second, the conduct at issue in the motion is not sanctionable because (1) the fluoroscopic machine has limited space and will automatically delete the oldest images as new images are taken, (2) because of this, only the saved images are accessible after the procedure, which are found in the patient’s electronic medical chart, and (3) the discovery of those unsaved ESI images falls under the safe-harbor statute, Section 2023.030(f) (see above), because they were overwritten as a result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system.

 

In his opposition, Dr. Lai argues that it would have been impossible for him to intentionally destroy evidence in light of this litigation because the litigation commenced after he had already stopped working for APMG. Dr. Lai had stopped working for APMG in May 2020, two months after the procedure and five months before Plaintiff sent the defendants her intent to sue notice. Therefore, without any indication of a pending lawsuit, Dr. Lai had no reason to destroy evidence. Moreover, once he left that employment, he did not have the means to either destroy or preserve evidence.

 

Like AVSSC, Dr. Lai also argues that there were only two images that were saved from Plaintiff’s procedure, and those images have already been produced.

 

Further, according to Dr. Lai, Nessan testified that the doctor “[took] photos during the procedure by tapping a foot petal and then [instructing] Mr. Nessan on what specific images to save [citations]. The images Mr. Nessan [was] instructed to save [were] then saved and printed by Mr. Nessan and put in the patient’s paper chart by either Mr. Nessan or a nurse [citations]. According to Advanced Pain’s Director of Nursing, Rebecca Riedel, once the paper chart, including the saved/printed images, is scanned into the patient’s electronic medical record, the paper chart, including the printed images, is destroyed, per the facility’s protocol [citation]. …. [Citation.] …. Not all fluoroscopic images ‘taken’ during a procedure are ‘saved’ during the procedure, as Mr. Nessan testified. Additionally, Dr. Lai testified that he ‘thought every picture that [he] asked to be saved would end up in the medical records.’ [Citation.]” (Dr. Lai’s Opposition, pp. 5:22-6:10.)

 

As stated above, “[s]poliation refers to the destruction or material alteration of evidence or to the failure to preserve property for another’s use as evidence in pending or reasonably foreseeable litigation.” (Victor Valley, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1143 [italics removed].)

 

“‘“The mere existence of a dispute does not necessarily mean that parties should reasonably anticipate litigation.” [Citations.] Instead, the duty seems to begin “somewhere between knowledge of the dispute and direct, specific threats of litigation.”’ [Citation.]” (Victor Valley, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1153.)

 

Stated differently, “‘a party’s duty to preserve arises when it has notice that the documents might be relevant to a reasonably-defined future litigation.’” (Victor Valley, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1153.)

 

“‘Ultimately, the court’s decision as to when a party was on notice must be guided by the particular facts of each case.’ [Citation.]” (Victor Valley, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1153.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has failed to prove that AVSSC and Dr. Lai failed to preserve some images despite pending or reasonably foreseeable litigation. Plaintiff argues in its reply that there is simply no good reason why Dr. Lai could not have asked Nessan to make sure all fluoroscopy images were saved after sending Plaintiff to the hospital. However, Plaintiff has not proven that at each point during the procedure and at each time Dr. Lai tapped the machine to take a photo, he had reason to believe that litigation was foreseeable. There is no evidence of direct, specific threats of litigation each time he took a photo and decided not to save it. (Victor, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1138 [“The duty to preserve evidence arises when the party in possession and/or control of the electronically stored information was objectively aware the evidence was relevant to reasonably foreseeable future litigation, meaning the future litigation was probable or likely to arise from an event, and not merely when litigation was a remote possibility].)

 

The Court also cannot impose sanctions based on AVSSC’s and Dr. Lai’s failure to preserve the unsaved fluoroscopic images especially when there is no evidence that the defendants had a pre-existing duty (statutory or otherwise) to preserve that ESI. (Victor, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1140 [“The safe-harbor provision of section 2023.030(f) specifically addresses when a trial court is authorized to impose sanctions for the spoliation of … [ESI]. ‘Notwithstanding subdivision (a), or any other section of this title, absent exceptional circumstances, the court shall not impose sanctions on a party or any attorney of a party for failure to provide electronically stored information that has been lost, damaged, altered, or overwritten as the result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system.’ (§ 2023.030(f)(1).) Finally, section 2023.030(f)(2) provides: ‘This subdivision shall not be construed to alter any obligation to preserve discoverable information’].)

 

Therefore, since Plaintiff has failed to prove spoliation, the Court denies both motions in their entirety.

Analysis

 

Standard for Compelling Further Responses to Requests for Production – “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.310(a).)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.310(b)(1).) In¿Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there¿is¿“a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v.¿Sup. Ct¿(2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ.¿Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See¿West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County¿(1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (See¿Obregon v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Trial courts are vested with “wide discretion” to allow or prohibit discovery. (Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 540.)¿¿¿¿¿

Analysis

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c), a trial judge is required “to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each of Plaintiff’s causes of action on the following bases: (1) each of Plaintiff’s causes of action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act (Causes of Action I – IV) are foreclosed by the California Supreme Court’s recent decision in Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC (Oct. 31, 2024) 557 P.3d 735, 2024 WL 4631069 (“Rodriguez”); (2) Plaintiff’s Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment claim (Cause of Action V) fails as a matter of law because (a) Plaintiff did not plead the claim with the requisite specificity, and (b) Plaintiff has not alleged a transactional relationship between GM and Plaintiff giving rise to a duty to disclose; (4) Plaintiff claim for violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (Cause of Action VI) fails as a matter of law because there is no viable state law warranty claim on which it can stand; and (5) even if Plaintiff’s claims withstand a judgment for GM, Plaintiff cannot claim as “damages” the optional, non-GM warranty Plaintiff purchased concurrently with the Subject Vehicle.

 

Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act Claims for Breach of Express and Implied Warranty (Causes of Action I through IV)

 

Plaintiff brings four causes of action for Defendant’s alleged violation of the Song-Beverly Act Consumer Warranty Act. (Cal. Civ. Code, §§ 1791 et seq.) (the “Song-Beverly Act” or the “Act.”) Plaintiff’s first through third causes of action relate to violations of the Act’s express warranty provisions, and the fourth cause of action relates to violations of the Act’s implied warranty provisions.

 

Plaintiff’s first cause of action claims that Defendant violated the refund-or-replace provision of the Act, Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.2(d)(2), which “requires manufacturers to ‘promptly replace’ a defective new motor vehicle or ‘promptly make restitution’ to the buyer when the manufacturer is ‘unable to service or repair a new motor vehicle, as that term is defined in paragraph (2) of subdivision (e) of Section 1793.22, to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of attempts.’” (Rodriguez, supra, 557 P.3d at p. 737, quoting Cal. Civ. Code § 1793(d)(2).)

 

 

Plaintiff’s second cause of action claims that Defendant violated the 30-day requirement of the Act, Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.2(b), which provides that a “manufacturer of consumer goods” shall service or repair “goods . . . that do not conform with the applicable express warranties” “so as to conform to the applicable warranties within 30 days” “[u]nless the buyer agrees in writing to the contrary.” (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff’s third cause of action claims that Defendant violated the Act’s requirement that manufacturers of consumer goods “make available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.2(a)(3).) Failure to provide the required facilities, or failure to make available the required literature and replacement parts, allows the buyer to return non-conforming goods to the retail seller or a retail seller of like goods, or to take the goods to an independent facility for service or repair at the manufacturer’s expense. (Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.3; see Cal. Civ. Code § 1793.5 (liability of manufacturer to retailer).)

 

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action claims that Defendant violated the Act’s implied warranty provision, which “requires consumer goods to be accompanied by the manufacturer’s and retail seller’s implied warranty of merchantability (§ 1792) and by the manufacturer’s implied warranty of fitness where the manufacturer has reason to know the buyer is relying on its skill or judgment in furnishing a suitable good for a particular purpose (§ 1792.1) (See § 1791.1) A consumer may sue the manufacturer for breach of these warranties. (§ 1791.1, subd. (d); cf. § 1792 [retail seller has ‘a right of indemnity’ against the manufacturer for breach of implied warranty of merchantability].)” (Rodriguez, supra, 557 P.3d at p. 741.)

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Causes of Action I – IV on the basis that the California Supreme Court’s recent decision in Rodriguez forecloses all of these causes of action. This Court finds that Defendant’s assertion is correct.

 

The Rodriguez Court’s central holding was that “a motor vehicle purchased with an unexpired manufacturer’s new car warranty does not qualify as a ‘motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty’ under section 1793.22, subdivision (e)(2) ’s definition of ‘new motor vehicle’ unless the new car warranty was issued with the sale.” (Rodriguez, supra, 557 P.3d at p. 737.) Therefore, the Supreme Court held, a buyer of a used car that has some balance remaining from “an unexpired manufacturer’s new car warranty” cannot sue for recovery under the Song-Beverly Act’s refund-or-replace provision. (Ibid.)

 

This holding directly disposes of Plaintiff’s first cause of action, for violation of the Act’s refund-or-replace provision. The undisputed facts in evidence establish that Plaintiff entered various warranty contracts with Defendant GM after purchasing the Subject Vehicle used from a CarMax location. (GM Separate Statement, ¶ 2; Farmer Separate Statement, ¶ 1; Farmer Decl., ¶ 1, Exh. 1 (indicating as “Buyer” “Andre L. Farmer” and indicating as “Seller” “CarMax Auto Superstores California, LLC”).) The undisputed facts further establish that Plaintiff was not the original owner of the car, which was originally sold in April 20, 2016 (GM Separate Statement, ¶ 7; Kay Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. 2 (GM’s “View Vehicle Delivery Information” report,), and that GM did not issue any new warranty coverage to Plaintiff or the Subject Vehicle when he purchased it from CarMax (GM Separate Statement, ¶ 10; Farmer Separate Statement 2; FAC, ¶ 6, Exh. A (attaching a copy of the Warranty that GM issued when the vehicle was delivered to its original owner(s).) Further, although Plaintiff purchased a separate extended service contract concurrently with his purchase of the Subject Vehicle, such warranty was connected with a non-GM entity, “CNA National Warranty Corporation.” (GM Separate Statement, ¶ 5; Farmer Decl., ¶ 1, Exh. 1, Itemization of Amount Financed, Line A 10.)

 

Plaintiff seems to concede as much, given that Plaintiff repeatedly states, both in his moving papers and Separate Statement, that “Section 1793.22, subdivision (e), defines ‘new motor vehicle’ and specifically limits its use of the definition to Section 1793.2, subdivision (d). That limitation does not carry over to other Song-Beverly claims contained in Plaintiff’s Complaint, namely claims under Section 1793.2, subdivision (b), Section 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3), 1791.1, Section 1794, and Section 1795.5.” (Opp., p. 14:6–10, Farmer Separate Statement ¶¶ 7, 8, 10; see Opp., p. 15:2–4.) Thus, Plaintiff argues that, although his first cause of action may be foreclosed by Rodriguez, his second, third, and fourth causes of action still state valid claims under the Song-Beverly Act. This is not true.

 

A major aspect of Rodriguez Court’s ruling was the “overall framework of the Song-Beverly Act, which distinguishes between new and used products and calibrates manufacturers’ and sellers’ obligations accordingly.” (Rodriguez, supra, 557 P.3d at p. 740.) Although the other statutory provisions that Plaintiff cites to do not incorporate Section 1793.22(e)(2)’s definition of “new motor vehicle,” they instead incorporate references to “manufacturers of consumer goods.” (See Cal. Civ. Code §§ 1793.2(a)(3), 1793.2(b), 1791.1, 1794.) “ ‘ “Consumer goods” means any new product or part thereof that is used, bought, or leased for use primarily for personal, family, or household purposes, except for clothing and consumables.’ (§ 1791, subd. (a).)” (Rodriguez, supra, at p. 740 (emphasis added).) Accordingly, the fact that Plaintiff purchased the Subject Vehicle used from CarMax instead of new from GM similarly forecloses Plaintiff’s recovery under his second, third, and fourth causes of action, whose applicable statutory language, as discussed above, all explicitly refer to “manufacturers of consumer goods.”

 

The Rodriguez Court also notes, however, that the Song-Beverly Act provides separate protections for purchasers of used cars. (Cal. Civ. Code § 1795.5.) The Court ruled on the Song-Beverly Act’s protections available for used vehicles as follows:

 

Section 1795.5 says: “Notwithstanding the provision of subdivision (a) of Section 1791 defining consumer goods to mean ‘new’ goods, the obligation of a distributor or retail seller of used consumer goods in a sale in which an express warranty is given shall be the same as that imposed on manufacturers under this chapter,” with certain exceptions. (Italics added.) In other words, “[s]ection 1795.5 provides express warranty protections for used goods only where the entity selling the used product issues an express warranty at the time of sale.” [Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 209, 218.] In addition, the obligation to maintain adequate repair facilities lies with “the distributor or  retail seller making express warranties with respect to used consumer goods (and not the original manufacturer, distributor, or retail seller making express warranties with respect to such goods when new).” (§ 1795.5, subd. (a).) Further, subdivision (c) of Section 1795.5 provides for implied warranties of merchantability and fitness by the distributor or retail seller, with those warranties having a shorter duration than the manufacturer’s implied warranties for new products. (See Ruiz Nunez v. FCA US LLC (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 385, 399, 275 Cal.Rptr.3d 618 (Ruiz Nunez) [under section 1795.5, “only distributors or sellers of used goods – not manufacturers of new goods – have implied warranty obligations in the sale of used goods”].)

 

Thus, the basic framework of the Song-Beverly Act distinguishes between new and used products and “provides similar remedies in the context of the sale of used goods, except that the manufacturer is generally off the hook.” (Kiluk v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 334, 339, 256 Cal.Rptr.3d (Kiluk).)

 

. . .

 

From this statutory context and history, we discern two points. First, in enacting and amending the Song-Beverly Act, the Legislature has maintained a distinction between “new” and “used” products and has specified the warranty protections applicable to each category.

 

. . .

 

Second, section 1795.5, which governs used goods, makes clear that the same warranty protections that consumers of new products have against manufacturers apply to consumers of used products against distributors or retail sellers “in a sale in which an express warranty is given.” In other words, just as with new products, the Act’s remedies for failure to repair a used product in conformity with an express warranty have applicability where the express warranty accompanies the sale of the product. For new products, liability extends to the manufacturer; for used products, liability extends to the distributor or retail seller and not to the manufacturer, at least where the manufacturer has not issued a new warranty or played a substantial role in the sale of a used good. (See Rodriguez, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at p. 218, 292 Cal.Rptr.3d 382; Ruiz Nunez, supra, 61 Cal.App.5th at p. 399, 275 Cal.Rptr.3d 618; Kiluk, supra, 43 Cal.App.5th at pp. 339–340, 256 Cal.Rptr.3d 484.)

 

. . .

 

(Rodriguez, supra, 557 P.3d at p. 740–741.)

 

Thus, Defendant has satisfied his burden of proof to negate an essential element of Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act claims by presenting evidence that Plaintiff cannot claim either of the following facts: (1) that the Subject Vehicle is new, (2) that GM entered any new warranty agreements with Plaintiff or the Subject Vehicle when Plaintiff bought it. (See State v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1008, 1017 (moving party must show that plaintiff cannot establish one or more elements of his causes of action).) As the Court’s decision in Rodriguez implies, although Plaintiff may have valid claims under the Song-Beverly Act, such claims would need to be levied against the “distributor or retail seller” of his used car, in this case CarMax. (See Rodriguez, supra, at p. 741.) Further, Plaintiff has not, and cannot, present any evidence sufficient to show a triable issue of material fact of whether the Subject Vehicle was new or used because the undisputed evidence shows that it was used.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to Plaintiff’s Causes of Action I – IV.

 

Plaintiff’s Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment Claim (Cause of Action V)

 

Plaintiff Has Pled Fraud with the Required Specificity

 

As in Defendant’s Demurrer to the FAC, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s Fifth Cause of Action is not pled with the requisite specificity, and therefore Plaintiff’s Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment claim must fail as a matter of law.

 

In order to prove a fraudulent concealment claim, a plaintiff must present facts sufficient to show: (1) defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) defendant was under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) defendant intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff; (4) plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted in the same way knowing of the concealed or suppressed fact; (5) causation; and (6) the plaintiff sustained damage. (Blickman v. Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 868.)

 

Although fraud claims must generally be pled with specificity, this Court previously noted in its Order ruling on Defendant’s Demurrer, that “[l]ess specificity is required when it ‘appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess ful information concerning the facts of the controversy.’ ” (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216 [quoting Bradley v. Hartford Acc. & Indem. Co. (1973) 30 Cal.App.3d 818, 825]; see also Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384 (rule of specifically pleading how, when, where, to whom, and by what means, misrepresentations were communicated, applies to affirmative misrepresentations, not to concealment) Jones v. ConocoPhillips (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1200 (concealment is sufficiently pled when the complaint as a whole provides sufficient notice of the particular claims against defendants).)

 

As with his prior objection to Plaintiff’s pleadings, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s fraudulent concealment claim fails as a matter of law because he failed to allege (i) the identity of the individuals at GM who purportedly concealed material facts or made untrue representations about the Subject Vehicle, (ii) their authority to speak and act on behalf of GM, (iii) Defendant’s knowledge about alleged defects in the Subject Vehicle at the time of purchase, (iv) any interactions with Defendant before or during the purchase of the Volt, or (v) Defendant’s intent to induce reliance by Plaintiff to purchase the Subject Vehicle as is. Essentially, Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to demonstrate GM’s intent to defraud Plaintiff or demonstrate that the Volt was unsuitable for its intended use at the time of purchase.

 

However, this Court directly addressed such contentions in its previous Order overruling Defendant’s Demurrer, and found that the following allegations were sufficient to sustain Plaintiff’s fraudulent concealment claim: (1) “[Defendant] knew since 2016 that the model year 2017 or newer Chevrolet Volt EV vehicles, including 2017 Chevrolet Volt vehicles such as the Subject Vehicle . . ., contained one or more design and/or manufacturing defects in the battery . . .” (FAC, ¶ 21); (2) “on November 13, 2020, GM issued NHTSA Recall No. 20V-701/GM Recall No. N202311730, as an interim remedy. The repair procedure calls for dealers to program the hybrid propulsion control module 2 (HPCM2) to limit full charge of the battery to 90%. The revised software is intended to limit the vehicle’s full charge to 90% of the battery capacity” (FAC, ¶ 36); and (3) “[o]n July 14, 2021, even for those that had the Recall performed, GM instructed owners and lessees of these Chevy Vehicles to ‘park their vehicles outside away from homes and other structures immediately after charging and . . . not leave their vehicles charging overnight” (FAC ¶ 37).

 

This Court upholds its prior ruling and finds, as previously, that Plaintiff has pled sufficient facts to plead a claim for fraudulent concealment.

 

Plaintiff Alleges a Transactional Relationship Between GM and Plaintiff Giving Rise to a Duty to Disclose

 

Defendant also argues, as with his Demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC, that Plaintiff’s fraudulent concealment claim fails as a matter of law because he does not allege a transactional relationship between the parties which gives rise to a duty to disclose.

 

In Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276 (“Bigler-Engler”), the court analyzed its own ruling in LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326 (“LiMandri”) to determine when a duty to disclose arises:

 

In that case [LiMandri], we explained, “There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’” (Id. at p. 336.) Where, as here, a fiduciary relationship does not exist between the parties, only the latter three circumstances may apply. These three circumstances, however, “presuppose[] the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise.” (Id. at pp. 336-337.) “A duty to disclose facts arises only when the parties are in a relationship that gives rise to the duty, such as “‘seller and buyer, employer and prospective employee, doctor and patient, or parties entering into any kind of contractual arrangement.”’” (Shin v. Kong (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 498, 509 [95 Cal.Rptr.2d 304].)

 

(Bigler-Engler, supra, 7 Cal.App.5th at 311.)

 

As in his Demurrer, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot establish that any of these three latter circumstances apply. Defendant cites to the Bigler-Engler court’s emphasis that such transactional relationships “must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large.” (Id., at p. 311.) Defendant argues that because Plaintiff did not purchase his Volt directly from GM, any alleged concealment by GM did not arise in a direct transaction between Plaintiff and GM.

 

However, as this Court previously noted, Defendant failed to address that this statement includes parties entering into a contractual agreement, which necessarily includes warranty contracts such as the one in this action. (See also Bigler-Engler, supra, at p. 314 (to satisfy the requisite transactional relationship that gives rise to the duty to disclose, a plaintiff may present evidence of the defendant’s advertising products to consumers such as the plaintiff, or defendant’s deriving a monetary benefit from the plaintiff); Shapiro v. Sutherland (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1534, 1549 (“the principle of indirect deception has been recognized . . .” as reflected in BAJI No. 12.50, relating to concealment and deceit with respect to persons not in privity with the defendant).)

 

Further, the undisputed facts in evidence demonstrate that, even if the Bumper-to-Bumper warranty between GM and Plaintiff and the Subject Vehicle had expired at the time Plaintiff purchased the Subject Vehicle from CarMax, the Powertrain Warranty was still effective because it only expired sometime between June 15, 2020, and October 2, 2020, when Plaintiff brought the Subject Vehicle in for recall repair. (GM Separate Statement ¶¶ 9, 11.) These facts show that at the time Plaintiff purchased the Subject Vehicle, at least one of the contractual warranty agreements levied by GM to the Subject Vehicle, and purchased by Plaintiff as a result of his purchase of the Subject Vehicle, was still in effect.

 

Thus, Defendant has not met its burden of establishing facts to negate an essential element of Plaintiff’s fraudulent concealment claim.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to Plaintiff’s Cause of Action V.

 

Plaintiff’s Magnusson-Moss Warranty Act Claim (Cause of Action VI)

 

Plaintiff’s Cause of Action IV alleges that Defendant violated the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (15 U.S.C. §§ 2301, et seq.) (“Magnuson-Moss”).

 

“Magnuson-Moss governs warranties for consumer products distributed in interstate commerce. It requires disclosures in connection with written warranties, regulates the substantive content of warranties, and establishes a federal cause of action for breach of a written or an implied warranty (15 U.S.C. § 2310(d)), among other provisions. Magnuson-Moss does not substitute federal law for state law of consumer product warranties, but instead supplements state law.” (Orichian v. BMW of N. Am., LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1322, 1330.) “Magnusson-Moss ‘calls for the application of state written and implied warranty law, not the creation of additional federal law,’ except in specific instances in which it expressly prescribes a regulating rule.” (Daugherty v. Am. Honda Motor Co. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 833.) Accordingly, the Daugherty court held that the “failure to state a warranty claim under state law necessarily constitute[s] a failure to state a claim under Magnuson-Moss.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendant cites to Daugherty, supra, for the contention that a failure to state a viable breach of warranty claim under state law, e.g., the Song-Beverly Act, constitutes a failure to state a claim under Magnuson-Moss. Defendant further cites to Jose Javier Arana v. Tesla Motors, Inc., et al., (C.D. Cal. Feb. 7, 2023) No. 2:22-CV-08664-RGK-JC, 2023 WL 1796956 (“Arana”). In Arana, the plaintiff purchased a used Tesla from a third-party dealership, which had 11,107 miles at the time of purchase, and which the plaintiff alleged was sold with the remaining balance of Tesla's new-vehicle express warranties for bumper-to-bumper coverage and powertrain coverage. (Id., at p. *1.) The Arana court held that, pursuant to the Fourth District Court’s ruling in Rodriguez, the plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act claims were foreclosed because plaintiff purchased the car used, not new. The court further held that “Plaintiff’s MMWA [claim] must fail because his SBA claim fails.” (Id. at p. *3.) The Arana court also addressed the plaintiff’s contention that, based on Dagher v. Ford Motor Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 905 (“Dagher”), his Magnuson-Moss claim could survive defendant’s motion to dismiss based on application of the California Uniform Commercial Code. The Arana court rejected the plaintiff’s argument because, unlike in Dagher, plaintiff did not bring any causes of action under the California Uniform Commercial Code in his complaint, or move to amend his complaint to include such causes of action while the motion was processing. (Arana, supra, at p. *3.)

 

In Dagher, which Plaintiff cites in support of his contention that his Magnuson-Moss claim can survive independent of his Song-Beverly Act claims, the court addressed factual circumstances like the circumstances here, and as in Arana. The Dagher plaintiff purchased a used Ford F-350 from private parties, not a Ford dealership. (Dagher, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at p. 912.) In Ford’s motion for summary judgment, the Dagher court ruled in favor of Ford because plaintiff did not purchase the vehicle new and therefore lacked standing under the Song-Beverly Act. (Id., at p. 924.)

 

However, the Dagher court did not grant summary judgment against plaintiff’s Magnuson-Moss claim. During the pendency of the defendant’s motion for summary judgment, plaintiff moved to amend his complaint to assert claims under Magnuson-Moss based on Ford’s breach of the express warranty and implied warranty provisions of the UCC. (Id., at p. 913.) Accordingly, the court found that the plaintiff’s Magnuson-Moss claim survived summary judgment because the plaintiff had valid claims under the UCC by seeking repairs for apparent defects during the unexpired transferred warranty period. (Id., at p. 928.)

 

Here, this Court notes that, because Arana is an unpublished opinion of the United States District Court for the Central District of California, Defendant may only cite it as persuasive authority on this Court. However, Dagher, as a published opinion of the Court of Appeal for the Fourth District in California, is binding precedent upon this Court.

 

This Court further notes that, like in Dagher, the undisputed facts in the record demonstrate that Plaintiff sought repairs from Defendant for apparent defects in the Subject Vehicle during the unexpired transferred warranty period under the Powertrain Warranty period at least one time. (GM’s Separate Statement, ¶ 12.) Despite seeking this repair, the undisputed facts in the record further demonstrate that the issues with the Subject Vehicle persisted, and Plaintiff sought further repairs for apparent defects to the Subject Vehicle after this initial repair attempt. (GM’s Separate Statement, ¶¶ 12, 13.) Thus, Defendant has failed to present sufficient evidence to sustain its initial burden of disproving an essential element of Plaintiff’s Magnuson-Moss claim, and that like, in Dagher, Plaintiff’s Magnuson-Moss claim may survive independent of Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act claims based on breach of warranty claims under the California Uniform Commercial Code.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to Plaintiff’s Cause of Action VI.

 

 

Conclusion

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

And the Court GRANTS in part and DENIES in part Defendant’s Motion in the alternative for Summary Adjudication.

 

The Court GRANTS Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Causes of Action I – IV. The Court DENIES Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Cause of Action V and VI.

Case Number: 23AVCV00654    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a negligence and premises liability action. On June 16, 2023, Plaintiff Juan Alvarez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants EPIC Auto Dismantling, Inc. (“EPIC”), TVBD Real Estate LLC (“TVBD”), and Does 1 to 20. This action arises out of an alleged accident that occurred on June 19, 2021 on private property owned by Defendants located at 37855 90th Street East, Littlerock, CA 93543 (“Junkyard”). The Complaint alleges Defendants ran a business at the Junkyard in which goods for sale were made available to the public. Plaintiff walked into the Junkyard and went inside one of the vehicles. As Plaintiff was partially inside the vehicle, a forklift operated by an EPIC employee allegedly listed the vehicle up into the air and abruptly lowered it back down, causing Plaintiff to sustain serious injuries. The Complaint alleges two causes of action for negligence and premises liability.

 

On October 28, 2024, TVBD filed this Demurrer without Motion to Strike.

 

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, TVBD filed the reply.

 

Analysis

 

Meet and Confer

           

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), and section 435.5, subdivision (a), require meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” On October 8, 2024, TVBD’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter regarding the issues in the Complaint subject to demurrer. (Allen-Blaine Decl., ¶ 2.) On October 16 and 18, 2024, the parties met and conferred via telephone regarding the Complaint. (Id. at ¶ 5.) Plaintiff opposed amending the Complaint, and this Demurrer followed. (Id. at ¶ 6.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met.

 

Standard for Ruling on Demurrer

 

            As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245) 

 

Second Cause of Action for Premises Liability

 

TVBD demurs to the second cause of action for premises liability on the grounds that it does not plead facts sufficient to state a claim. First, TVBD argues that the Complaint pleads no facts identifying a dangerous condition—it is unclear whether the dangerous condition is the forklift itself or the risk of being inside a salvaged vehicle.  TVBD further argues Plaintiff was sitting inside a scrap vehicle, which is not a public walkway, and thus it had no duty to warn Plaintiff of any dangerous condition. Second, the Complaint also pleads no facts to identify how TVBD, as the owner of property rented to EPIC, had any knowledge of the unidentified dangerous condition. Defendant also argues that, because the Complaint does not specify the “dangerous condition,” that there is no way to determine whether TVBD had constructive knowledge of the condition. Lastly, the Complaint pleads no facts to show how TVBD, as property owner that rents to a vehicle salvage yard company, breached a duty regarding this condition that then caused Plaintiff’s injury.

 

Plaintiff argues that the Complaint sufficiently alleges that the dangerous condition is unambiguous—Defendant did not take steps to maintain precautionary measures while operating heavy machinery. Plaintiff argues he was walking around the scrap vehicle but that a portion of his body was in the vehicle when it was lifted. Plaintiff argues that the public could not appreciate the risk of the forklift’s diameter and that Defendant, with its superior knowledge, should have had precautionary measures in place. Plaintiff further argues TVBD had constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition and controlled the property through Defendant Torgom Abramian (named as Doe 2). Plaintiff suggests Abramian is the owner of TVBD. In support, Plaintiff references a claim filed with the California Labor Commission in which it is established via the agency’s findings that Defendant Abramian is the sole operator of TVBD. (Opp., Exh. 4.) Lastly, Plaintiff argues that the Complaint alleges facts to establish breach and causation.

 

In reply, TVBD argues that Plaintiff cannot incorporate by reference exhibits in its opposition to establish allegations outside of the Complaint. TVBD argues that the Complaint does not plead facts establishing that TVBD had control over the Junkyard and its operations. Lastly, TVBD argues that it is still unclear from the Complaint and Opposition how Plaintiff was both inside the scrapped vehicle and in a public pathway.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) The Court address each element of premises liability in turn.

 

Damages

 

            The parties do not dispute that the Complaint sufficiently alleges damages. The Court finds damages are sufficiently alleged in paragraphs 37 to 42 of the Complaint.

 

Duty

 

            The Court finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges Defendant’s duty of care. The Complaint alleges the following:

 

“34.     Defendants, as the owner and possessor of the Private Property and the Junkyard located thereon, were under a duty to exercise ordinary care in the use or maintenance of the premises in order to avoid exposing patrons of the Junkyard to unreasonable risk of harm.”

 

(Compl., ¶ 34.)

 

            The parties dispute and conflate two issues in relation to TVBD’s duty of care: 1) TVBD’s knowledge of the dangerous condition and 2) TVBD’s control and management over the property. The Court finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges both.

 

i.                    Knowledge

 

TVBD argues that the Complaint fails to allege that it had actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition because it is unclear what the condition is. TVBD argues the Complaint does not allege that TVBD knew “1) that another Defendant’s employee was operating a forklift by a car that Plaintiff was either ambulating toward or also inside of or 2) that TVBD failed to tell their lessee that the lessee’s employee forklift driver was creating a danger to Plaintiff.” (Mot., p. 8, lines 7-9.) TVBD cites requirement that plaintiffs have the burden of showing an owner had actual or constructive knowledge of a dangerous condition in sufficient time to correct it, where failure to correct a dangerous condition is the theory of liability. (See Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206-07.) The Court notes that Plaintiff’s response speaks to whether TVBD had control of the premises, rather than to TVBD’s knowledge.

 

The Court finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges actual or constructive knowledge on behalf of TVBD. TVBD is the owner of the property and admits it rents to EPIC, a vehicle salvage yard business. TVBD is thus constructively on notice of potentially dangerous conditions that may occur at a vehicle salvage yard business. Further, the Complaint alleges the following as to TVBD’s knowledge:

 

“35.     . . .Defendants knew or should have known in the exercise of reasonable care that forklift operations would not permit a person to safely walk through the property. Defendants further knew or should have known in the exercise of reasonable care that the forklift operations were hazardous at the moment of Plaintiff’s injury due to the lack of precautionary measures while the forklift was in operation. Defendants also knew or should have known in the exercise of reasonable care that the state of the forklift operations on the property represented a dangerous condition and an unreasonable risk of harm to patrons. . .”

 

(Compl., ¶ 35.)

 

            The Court thus finds the Complaint sufficiently alleges TVBD’s knowledge of the dangerous condition.

 

ii.                  Control and Possession

 

There is a related issue of control and possession that bears on TVBD’s duty to Plaintiff. “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.)

 

TVBD argues the Complaint fails to allege that it controlled the property as a lessor. Plaintiff argues that TVBD controlled the property through its sole managing-member Defendant Torgom Abramian (named as Doe 2). In support, Plaintiff references a claim filed with the California Labor Commission in which it is established via the agency’s findings that Defendant Abramian is the sole operator of TVBD. (Opp., Exh. 4.) TVBD argues in reply that Plaintiff cannot incorporate by reference exhibits in its opposition to establish allegations outside of the Complaint.

 

The Court initially agrees with Defendant that Plaintiff cannot introduce allegations outside of the Complaint to oppose the Demurrer. Even if the Court takes judicial notice of the documents attached as exhibits, the Court would not be able to recognize the truth of the statements contained in them. Judicial notice is limited to matters that are indisputably true. (Evid. Code, section 452(d); Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 482.) The truth of the matters asserted in [court and other official] records is not subject to judicial notice. (Arce, 181 Cal.App.4th at p.483.) Further, it is unclear how Defendant Torgom Abramian’s alleged management of TVBD establishes that TVBD controlled the property. The Complaint nor the opposition alleges that Defendant Torgom Abramian controlled or managed the property.

 

Nevertheless, the Court finds the Complaint sufficiently alleges TVBD’s control over the property. The Complaint alleges the following as to TVBD and EPIC’s ownership and control:

 

“17.     At all times mentioned herein, Defendants owned, maintained, controlled, managed, and operated the subject Private Property where the incident in question transpired. Defendants owned, operated, maintained, managed, and supervised the Junkyard on the Private Property in which various goods for sale were and are made available to patrons and members of the public.”

 

(Compl., ¶ 17.)

 

            Such allegation, taken as true, is all that is required of Plaintiff to meet his burden at this stage. The Court finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges TVBD’s knowledge and control of the property. The Complaint, therefore, alleges TVBD’s duty to Plaintiff.

 

Breach and Causation

 

The Court initially finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges the dangerous condition that is the subject of TVBD’s breach. The Complaint alleges the premises were dangerous where TVBD “permitted forklift operations to exist on the property where customers and patrons, including Plaintiff, were invited to pass. . .the state of the forklift operations on the property represented a dangerous condition and an unreasonable risk of harm to patrons. . .” (Compl., ¶ 35.)

           

As to the issue of causation, Defendant and Plaintiff dispute whether Plaintiff was in a public walkway when he was injured. Defendant argues that Plaintiff was inside a junkyard vehicle, which is not a pathway designated for public use. Defendant implies that the cause of Plaintiff’s injury is Plaintiff himself.  Plaintiff, in response, argues that the forklift operations and lack of precautions were the cause of his injury. Plaintiff also argues that Defendant assumes Plaintiff was sitting in the scrap vehicle, but the Complaint alleges Plaintiff was “ambulating.” (Compl., ¶¶ 25, 35.) Plaintiff argues that it is possible he was walking around the vehicle while his body was partially inside the vehicle, as the Complaint suggests. Defendant replies that the Complaint is uncertain as to whether Plaintiff was walking in public pathways or inside the vehicle.

 

            The Complaint states that “As Plaintiff was in one of the Junkyard vehicles, a forklift operated by an EPIC employee, DOE 1, lifted the vehicle up into the air and abruptly lowered it back into the ground, causing Plaintiff to sustain serious injuries.” (Compl., ¶ 19.) But the Complaint later states that Plaintiff was “ambulating” in the area operating heaving machinery. (Compl., ¶¶ 25, 35.) Reading the Complaint in light most favorable to the Plaintiff, the Court finds it is possible that Plaintiff’s body was partially in the car while he was walking near the scrapped vehicles. Although the allegations are tenuous, the Court finds that such uncertainty is insufficient to prove a lack of causation. TVBD has thus failed to meet its burden. The Complaint alleges the elements of breach and causation clearly:

 

“36.     At the aforementioned time and place, Defendants breached its duty of care to Plaintiff when they failed to take steps to either make the area where Plaintiff was ambulating safe, or warn Plaintiff of the dangerous condition, all of which caused Plaintiff to suffer injuries and damages as hereinafter described. No “caution” tape or safety cones were in place near the point where Plaintiff was injured, even though the area was operating heavy machinery. No effort was made by any of the Defendants to decrease the hazard in the area.

 

“37.     Defendants’ failure to use ordinary care with respect to the Junkyard caused Plaintiff to be lifted into the air and fall on the above-mentioned date. Plaintiff suffered several injuries as a result of these falls.”

 

(Compl., ¶¶ 36-37.)

           

            The Court finds that the Complaint pleads sufficient facts to state a cause of action for premises liability. The Demurrer is thus overruled.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court OVERRULES the Demurrer to the second cause of action for premises liability.

Case Number: 24AVCV00243    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

On February 29, 2024, Plaintiffs Genea Knight and Jernea Moody (“collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed the Complaint against Defendants General Motors LLC and Does 1 through 10 alleging (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, (2) violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act, and (3) violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.

 

On October 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion.

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendant General Motors LLC filed its opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

 

Trial has been set on May 2, 2025.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Plaintiffs Genea Knight and Jernea Moody submit evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Matthew Dobson.

 

The Court rules as follows:

 

OVERRULED: 2-3

SUSTAINED: 1 (as to only “without making any substantial attempts to address GM’s objections and limit the scope of the requested testimony”), 4

 

 

Analysis

 

Standard for Motion to Compel Deposition (PMQ)

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. The person deposed may be a natural person . . . [or] an organization such as a public or private corporation.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.010.) “The service of a deposition notice . . . is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document . . . [or] electronically stored information.” (Id. § 2025.280(a).)

 

“It is established that a litigant has the right to take a proper deposition, and to receive responsive answers to proper questions . . . for the purposes of discovery or for use as evidence, or for both purposes.” (Beverly Hills Nat’l Bank & Tr. Co. v. Super. Ct. (1961) 195 Cal.App.2d 861, 864–65 (citing I.E.S. Corp. v. Super. Ct. (1955) 44 Cal.2d 559 and Tatkin v. Super. Ct. (1958) 160 Cal.App.2d 745).) “The statute authorizing the taking of depositions to be used in pending trials should be liberally construed to the end that a litigant in a pending action may be afforded a reasonable opportunity to procure available testimony in support of his cause.” (Moran v. Super. Ct. (1940) 38 Cal.App.2d 328, 334.) In keeping with this liberal tradition, Plaintiffs are entitled to testimony and documents that are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence about all of the issues in this case. Indeed, “[f]or discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. . . .These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery, and (contrary to popular belief), fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.” (Stewart v. Colonial W. Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1013.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiffs argue that the deposition testimony of GM’s PMQ is fundamental to Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly Act claims. Plaintiffs further argue that they have good cause to seek the discovery information because the seven categories of testimony and ten document requests go to the essential elements of Plaintiffs’ express warranty and damages claims. Plaintiffs further request sanctions in the amount of $3,000.00.

 

In opposition, GM argues that Plaintiffs’ motion should be denied for failure to meet and confer in good faith. Moreover, GM argues that Plaintiffs seek irrelevant testimony because Plaintiffs failed to sufficiently explain how or why the requested testimony is discoverable, let alone so essential to their claims or GM’s defenses that GM should be compelled to provide it. GM also argues that it agreed to produce a witness to testify about repairs made to Plaintiffs’ vehicle, communications, and the reasons that GM did not repurchase Plaintiffs’ vehicle and thus the request for sanctions is groundless. 

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that they attempted to meet and confer on six separate occasions, to no avail. Moreover, they met and conferred during an IDC. Additionally, Plaintiffs set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying their discovery requests. For instance, matter nos. 3 and 7, refer to the subject vehicle’s defects, warranty nonconformities, and repair history which are relevant to Song Beverly claims. Also, questions related to GM’s procedure to determine whether Plaintiffs’ vehicle qualified for a repurchase prior to filing suit (Matter Nos. 1, 2, and 5) are relevant to Plaintiffs’ civil penalty claims. Plaintiffs properly seek discovery regarding GM’s lemon law policies and procedures. GM’s policies and procedures are relevant to the issues related to GM’s good faith compliance with the Act, including a breach of the express warranty and implied warranty of merchantability. Plaintiffs further argue that GM’s trade secret and confidentiality claims are meritless because it failed to file a privilege log or protective order. Plaintiffs also argue that sanctions are warranted because GM continues to ignore its discovery obligations and is substantially prejudicing Plaintiffs’ ability to obtain necessary discovery in order to prepare for trial and failed to show that it acted with substantial justification or that sanctions are unjust. Plaintiffs further argue that GM conflates claiming to agree to produce a witness to actually producing a witness. Plaintiffs first noticed GM’s deposition in April of 2024. In the seven months since that first notice, GM has not offered a single date for its deposition.

 

The Court grants the Motion because in accordance with CCP § 2025.450 and CCP § 2025.230, a deposition notice was properly served, the deposition was for June 11, 2024, and Defendant did not appear. After meet and confer efforts followed and proved unfruitful, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion.  

 

On April 2, 2024, Plaintiffs served written discovery and a unilaterally scheduled Notice of Deposition of GM’s Person Most Qualified (“PMQ”). (Sanchez Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. 1.) GM served its responses on April 18, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 5, Exh. 2.) On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs served their First Amended Notice, which no longer contained the contested category and document request regarding GM’s lemon law buybacks of other similar vehicles. (Id. at ¶ 8, Exh. 4.) On May 14, 2024, GM served its responses. (Id. at ¶ 9, Exh. 5.) On May 21, 2024, Plaintiffs served their Second Amended Notice. (Id. at ¶ 11, Exh. 7.) On May 14, 2024, GM served its responses. (Id. at ¶ 12, Exh. 8.) On June 26, 2024, Plaintiffs and GM participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) in this Court. At the IDC, GM still had not provided any dates. GM also explained that it planned to file a dispositive motion. (Id. at ¶ 14.)

 

Meet and Confer Efforts were Sufficient 

 

Defendant correctly contends that under both CCP §§ 2025.450 and 2025.480 meet and confer is required. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were insufficient. The Court disagrees.  

 

Plaintiff details their efforts in the Declaration of Scott A. Sanchez (Sanchez Decl.) which notes that after Defendant’s objections were served, Plaintiff sent a detailed meet and confer letter addressing deficiencies in Defendant’s objections. (Sanchez Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs requested deposition dates and offered to withdraw a contested category of other buybacks and the related document request. (Id.) Plaintiffs also proposed a protective order to cover the production of GM’s lemon law policy and procedure manual. (Id.) On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs served their 1st Amended Notice, which no longer contained the contested category and document request regarding GM’s lemon law buybacks of other similar vehicles. (Id. at ¶ 8.) On May 16, 2024, Plaintiffs called GM to meet and confer to no avail. (Id. at ¶ 10.) On May 21, 2024, Plaintiffs served their Second Amended Notice. On June 7, 2024, Plaintiffs called GM to meet and confer. GM did not answer, so Plaintiffs left a voicemail message. Plaintiffs then sent GM a detailed correspondence requesting deposition dates, reminding it of their concession in withdrawing the contested category, proposing a protective order, and proposing a Zoom meeting to discuss any other issues. Again, GM did not respond. (Id. at ¶ 13.) On June 26, 2024, Plaintiffs and GM participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) in this Court. (Id. at ¶ 14.) Defendant fails to demonstrate why these meet and confer efforts were insufficient.  

 

The Testimony Sought is Relevant 

 

Defendant’s next contention is that the testimony sought via the Deposition Notice is irrelevant. The Deposition Notice requests testimony on the following four categories, and requests that documents be produced on six more: 

 

Here, the categories of testimony requested include: (1) why GM did not repurchase the vehicle pursuant to the lemon law pre-suit; (2) questions about the service, repair and communication history related to the vehicle and Plaintiff; (3) questions relating to GM’s investigation of the service history; and (4) questions about the warranty nonconformities of which Plaintiffs complain. The document requests seek: (1) documents evidencing repairs to the vehicle; (2) photographs or videos GM may have of the vehicle; (3) communications not involving attorneys about the vehicle or Plaintiffs; (4) GM’s customer service file; (5) the recalls and technical bulletins that apply to Plaintiffs’ vehicle; (6) GM’s and its dealers’/customer service representatives’ lemon law policies and procedures; and (7) copies of the documents GM relied on when it chose not to repurchase the vehicle pre-litigation.

 

As to the matters for examination, Defendant objects to each matter with similar objections, arguing that either the terms are vague and ambiguous, or that the category is overbroad, unduly burdensome, or oppressive. First, for all matters of examination, the objections are unclear as in the concurrently filed Separate Statement states “GM already offer[ed] to produce a PMQ on the Categories for Examination and producing a plethora of responsive documents) (Separate Statement, 5:6-7.)  

 

Defendant notes that the dispute lies in Defendant’s internal operations, policies, and procedures. Defendant argues that these materials will provide little benefit to Plaintiff and that attempting to discover these internal operations, policies, and procedures is bad faith discovery tactics and an attempt at discovering privileged or proprietary information. The Court disagrees for two main reasons.  

 

First, although discovery is not limitless, it is broad. As it pertains to relevance, “…any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010). “A trial court must be mindful of the Legislature's preference for discovery over trial by surprise, must construe the facts before it liberally in favor of discovery…” (Williams v. Sup.Ct. (Marshalls of CA, LLC) (2017) 220 Cal.Rptr.3d 472, 479-480).      

 

Here, Plaintiff is requesting the PMK to speak on whether the Subject Vehicle should have been repurchased. This is relevant because one of the elements of a prima facie case under Song-Beverly is that the Defendant did not promptly repair or repurchase the vehicle. (Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101; Also see CA CIVIL § 1793.2.)  Plaintiff’s intent here is clear, to find out why that did not happen. The same principle can be applied to the second matter for examination, a history of service and repair attempts will show what repairs if any were attempted. Finally, both (1) policies and procedures for determining whether a vehicle qualifies for a repurchase or replacement and (2) training for evaluating a repurchase request will assist in Plaintiff understanding what violations, if any, occurred during the process.   

 

Plaintiff Does Not Seek Trade Secret Material 

 

The second reason the Court disagrees with the contention that Plaintiff is engaging in bad faith discovery is that a protective order is an effective tool well within Defendant’s reach. Defendant argues extensively that if discovery is permitted into the categories of examination, that trade secret material will have to be produced. The Court finds this assertion without merit. Plaintiff is not seeking information about how the Subject Vehicle is conceived, created, or even manufactured. Plaintiff is seeking policies related to processes surrounding the situation when a vehicle does not conform to the warranty. Additionally, if Defendant fears trade secrets may be revealed, it is their burden to show good cause in seeking a protective order. (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 318.)  

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified is GRANTED.

 

Request for Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs seek sanctions in the amount of $3,000.00 for Defendant’s misuse of discovery. Defendant argues that sanctions are meritless because they already agreed to providing the Person Most Qualified and the objections were appropriate. The Court disagrees with Defendant.

 

Sanctions are warranted as the Motion is granted and GM failed to show substantial justification in not providing the Person Most Qualified.

 

 Plaintiffs’ counsel provides the following calculations: 

 

·         Counsel’s hourly rate is $375.00 

·         Counsel spent 5 hours drafting this Motion and related documents and meeting and conferring about this issue.

·         Counsel anticipated spending 3 hours reviewing opposition papers and drafting reply papers, and attending the hearing on this matter 

 

Although sanctions are warranted, the amount sought is excessive as this is a simple discovery motion. Thus, sanctions are imposed on Defendant and awarded to Plaintiff in the amount of $937.50 for two and half hours drafting the motion, preparing a reply, and attending the hearing. 

 

Conclusion

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. Sanctions are imposed on Defendant and awarded to Plaintiff in the amount of $937.50.

Background

 

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff Sagnik Mahata (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Jaime Allen Weitz (“Defendant”) alleging intentional infliction of emotional distress and intentional tort. Plaintiff now alleges that Defendant is evading service and therefore has been unable to serve Defendant with the Complaint and Summons. On the same day, Plaintiff filed a request to waive Court fees and the Court granted the fee waiver.

 

On October 3, 2024, Plaintiff in pro per filed the Motion for Alternate Service.

 

On June 11, 2024, the Court denied the initial Motion for Alternate Service.

 

On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the declaration of process server.

 

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a due diligence declaration.

 

As of November 20, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

 

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff requests that the Court issue an order directing service of the documents by posting at the location listed below Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles 42011 4th Street West Lancaster California 93534.

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes Plaintiff fails to cite to any legal authority. “Pro per litigants are held to the same standards as attorneys.” (Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.) “[M]ere self-representation is not a ground for exceptionally lenient treatment. Except when a particular rule provides otherwise, the rules of civil procedure must apply equally to parties represented by counsel and those who forgo attorney representation. … A doctrine generally requiring or permitting exceptional treatment of parties who represent themselves would lead to a quagmire in the trial courts, and would be unfair to the other parties to litigation.” (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 984–985, citation omitted.) 

 

Service of summons is generally effected via personal service, substituted service including first-class mailing, mail service with acknowledgment of receipt, and publication, among others methods. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 415.10-415.95.) 

 

Where no provision is made in this chapter or other law for the service of summons, the court in which the action is pending may direct that summons be served in a manner which is reasonably calculated to give actual notice to the party to be served and that proof of such service be made as prescribed by the court. (Code of Civil Procedure § 413.30.)

 

Here, the Court granted Plaintiff’s fee waiver on October 22, 2024. Plaintiff further provided the declaration of the registered process server who declares, “I have previously served this defendant, so she is aware of who I am. She has evaded me in the past, and even driven away from me as I was walking up to her car to serve her . . . she is once again evading service, refusing to open the door when she is home. Her car is there, as is another vehicle. I can hear talking, tv’s, etc. . .” (Gelt Decl. at p.1.) Plaintiff also provided the process server’s declaration regarding diligence where she listed four other attempts from August to September, three of which were at Defendant’s home and a fourth in Defendant’s workplace. (Decl. of Diligence at p. 1.) The process server declares under perjury that although nobody answered the door, there were noises and lights on inside home while Defendant’s vehicle was present. Moreover, the process server was not allowed inside Defendant’s workplace. Therefore, the evidence suggests that Defendant is evading service.

 

The Court notes Plaintiff has provided sufficient evidence showing due diligence because service has been attempted about seven times on different dates and times.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS the Motion for Alternate Service.


Case Number: 24AVCV00476    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. On April 22, 2024, Plaintiff Paul Thalman (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against U-Haul Co. of California (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 25. This action arises from an alleged accident (“Subject Accident”) that occurred on April 30, 2023 at a U-Haul storage center located at 42925 Sierra Highway, Lancaster, CA 93534. Plaintiff was loading a marble table using a moving dolly into a truck rented from Defendant (“Rental Truck”). When Plaintiff reached the top of the truck ramp, he took a couple of steps backward and stepped on a loose metal plate in the truck bed. The metal plate gave way, and Plaintiff fell to the ground with the table and dolly falling on top of him. Plaintiff sustained injuries. The Complaint alleges two causes of action for general negligence and premises liability.

 

On June 17, 2024, Defendant filed the Answer.

 

On October 29, 2024, Defendant filed this Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay.

 

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, Defendant filed the reply.

 

 

Analysis

 

Standard for Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

“If a court of competent jurisdiction. . .has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (CCP § 1281.4.)¿¿

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, Arbitration Clauses are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218, 105 Cal.Rptr.2d 597; see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.)

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff entered into a Self-Storage Rental Agreement (“Storage Agreement”) on March 27, 2023, in which Plaintiff rented a self-storage unit from Defendant on a monthly basis (Lapidus Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) The Storage Agreement contained an arbitration clause. (Ibid.) Defendant incorporates the arbitration clause into the Motion and attached a copy of the agreement. (Ibid.) Defendant’s theory is that the Storage Agreement binds Plaintiff to arbitration because the Subject Accident happened while Plaintiff was unloading his property from the storage unit onto the Rental Truck. Defendant acknowledges that the Rental Truck that caused Plaintiff’s injury was rented by Jacquilyn Crabtree (“Ms. Crabtree”), not Plaintiff, from an independent U-Haul dealer located in Granada Hills, CA on April 30, 2023. (Hill Decl., ¶¶ 2-3, Exh. A; Hatanian Decl., ¶ 3.) Ms. Crabtree signed a truck rental agreement with the independent U-Haul dealer (“Rental Truck Agreement”) that contained an arbitration clause. (Hill Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.)

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the Subject Accident was solely caused by the condition of the Rental Truck, and Plaintiff was not party to the Rental Truck Agreement. Plaintiff argues that this action is outside of the scope of the Storage Agreement. Plaintiff further argues that, even assuming the Storage Agreement covers the Subject Accident, the Agreement had ended by that date. The Storage Agreement was a month-to-month lease, and Plaintiff had only made one and final payment for the period between March 27, 2023 and April 27, 2023, when the next payment was due. (Hatanian Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. 2; Fish Decl., ¶2, Ex. A.) Plaintiff argues he was removing his property from the storage unit on April 30, 2023, on the date the accident occurred, although Plaintiff provides no support for this contention. Plaintiff argues his actions—failing to pay on the next monthly due date and removing his property from the unit—indicated his intent to terminate the agreement, and any arbitration clause would be ineffective to cover claims past April 27, 2023 (i.e., his claim regarding the Subject Accident that occurred on April 30, 2023). Defendant argues in reply that Plaintiff admitted he was in possession of the storage unit past April 27, 2023 and provided no evidence suggesting otherwise.

 

            The Court finds that Defendant has failed to meet its burden to establish a valid arbitration agreement. The Storage Agreement does not bind Plaintiff to arbitrate claims unrelated to the storage unit or the Storage Agreement. The fact that the Subject Accident occurred at the site of the storage unit does not place Plaintiff’s claims regarding the incident under the Storage Agreement. Nowhere in the Complaint does Plaintiff mention the storage unit.

 

The Court finds that the Storage Agreement explicitly states that it only covers claims related to Plaintiff’s storage unit rental transaction. The Storage Agreement states the following:

 

“By engaging in a ‘Transaction,’ ‘You’ and ‘U-Haul’ voluntarily and knowingly enter into this Arbitration Agreement which waives your right to sue and bring claims in court, other than as stated below, or have a jury resolve any dispute.”

 

“a. “Claims” is broadly interpreted to include any dispute, complaint, controversy, or cause of action related to your Transaction and to U-Haul. Claims, including assigned claims, brought under any legal theory, whether at law or in equity, are covered by this Agreement and shall include, but not be limited to, all statutory and tort claims, that may be asserted.”

 

“d. “Transaction” means the commencement, completion, or fulfillment of: A) a request or reservation to rent or use a Storage Unit or to receive services; and/or B) the use or review of the content of any U-Haul website.”

 

(Hill Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A, p. 15.) (Emphasis added.)

 

            The Court finds that the Storage Agreement, containing the aforementioned arbitration clause, applies to claims related to the rental of the storage unit, not the Rental Truck. Further, Ms. Crabtree, not Plaintiff, signed the Rental Truck Agreement. (Hill Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A; Hatanian Decl., ¶ 3.) There is no indication that Ms. Crabtree was signing on behalf of Plaintiff or as Plaintiff’s agent. Plaintiff is not a party to the Rental Truck Agreement and did not agree to arbitration claims relating to the Rental Truck. Defendant has thus failed to establish that there is a binding arbitration agreement between itself and Plaintiff for the claim arising from the Rental Truck.

 

            The Court acknowledges, but disagrees, with Plaintiff’s argument that Storage Agreement had been terminated by Plaintiff before the Accident happened on April 30, 2024. The Storage Agreement was a month-to-month lease, and Plaintiff had made his one and final payment for the period between March 27, 2023 and April 27, 2023. (Hatanian Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 2; Fish Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Yet Defendant’s Contract Overview indicates that Plaintiff maintained possession of the storage unit until July 3, 2023. (Fish Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. B.) Plaintiff also admits in the opposition that he continued to possess the storage unit until April 30, 2023, thus extending the lease. (See Schmitt v. Felix (1958) 157 Cal.App.2d 642, 646 (“When a tenant under a lease remains in possession of the leased premises with the permission of the lessor from month [to month] after the term expires, a new tenancy is not created but the original tenancy is deemed to have been extended.” (citations omitted.).) Plaintiff was still bound by the Storage Agreement as long as Plaintiff possessed storage unit. Regardless, as discussed earlier, the Storage Agreement does not cover Plaintiff’s claim where it arises solely out of the Rental Truck’s condition. That the Storage Agreement was effective on the day of the Subject Accident does not undo the fact that Plaintiff and Defendant had no agreement to arbitrate claims outside of the scope of the storage unit transaction.

 

Enforceability

           

Because the Storage Agreement does not apply to Plaintiff’s claim, the Court need not assess the parties’ arguments regarding the potential unconscionability of the Storage Agreement and arbitration clause.

           

Waiver

 

“[T]he court shall [compel arbitration]. . .unless it determines. . .[t]he right to compel arbitration has been waived.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) A party seeking to prove a waiver may do so by showing “(1) knowledge of an existing right to compel arbitration; (2) acts inconsistent with that existing right; and (3) prejudice to the party opposing arbitration resulting from such inconsistent acts.” (Richards v. Ernst & Young, LLP (9th Cir. 2013) 744 F3d 1072, 1074 (internal quotes omitted).) In determining waiver, courts consider: whether the party demanding arbitration has taken action inconsistent with the right to arbitrate, whether litigation machinery “has been substantially invoked” and the parties “were well into preparation of a lawsuit” before the party demanding arbitration notified the opposing party of an intent to arbitrate, whether the party demanded arbitration close to the trial date or delayed for a long period before seeking a stay, whether a defendant seeking arbitration filed a counterclaim without asking for a stay of the proceedings, whether important intervening steps have taken place (e.g., taking advantage of judicial discovery procedures not available in arbitration), and whether the delay “affected, misled, or prejudiced the opposing party.” (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.App.4th 19, 31 (internal quotes omitted).)

 

Defendant argues that it did not waive its right to enforce arbitration. Defendant argues that it found out that the Subject Accident was connected to Plaintiff’s storage rental on September 26, 2024. (Fish Decl., ¶ 7.) Once Defendant knew about the connection, it demanded Plaintiff to arbitrate pursuant to the Storage Agreement four days later. (Lapidus Decl., ¶ 2-3.) Defendant argues it thus did not unequivocally waive its right to arbitrate.

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant waived its right to arbitrate because Defendant has provided no support explaining why it did not find out about the connection between the Subject Accident and Plaintiff’s storage unit. Plaintiff argues that Ms. Crabtree notified a U-Haul employee of the Subject Accident on the date that it occurred, although there is no verified declaration supporting this contention. Plaintiff further argues Defendant filed an answer that did not raise arbitration. (Hatanian Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 3.) Plaintiff also argues Defendant propounded a written discovery request on Plaintiff, subpoenaed Plaintiff’s medical records, and scheduled, but subsequently cancelled, a deposition for Ms. Crabtree. (Hatanian Decl., ¶¶ 7-10, Exhs. 4-7.) Plaintiff has also served written discovery requests on Defendant and Defendant has served objections. (Hatanian Decl., ¶ 10, Exh. 7.) The parties have also agreed to an informal discovery conference scheduled for November 27, 2024. (Hatanian Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. 8)

 

            The Court finds that Defendant has not waived its right to arbitrate, had the Storage Agreement applied to Plaintiff’s claim. Plaintiff has not proven that Defendant’s delay in seeking arbitration would result in prejudice against him. Although Defendant has engaged in some discovery, a trial date has not yet been set and the parties were not “well into preparation of a lawsuit” before Defendant notified Plaintiff of its intent to arbitrate. (See Wagner, supra, 41 Cal.App.4th at p. 31.) The Court thus finds there is no waiver at this juncture.

 

            Nevertheless, Plaintiff’s waiver defense is of no issue where there is no valid arbitration agreement to waive. The lack of a valid agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant to arbitrate claims relating to the Rental Truck precludes Defendant from compelling Plaintiff to arbitrate.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court DENIES the Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay.

Background

 

On May 14, 2024, Plaintiff Michael Moran filed the Complaint against Defendants General Motors LLC and Does 1 through 10 alleging violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

On October 11, 2024, the parties filed a Stipulation and Protective Order.

 

On October 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion.

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendant General Motors LLC filed its opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed his reply.

 

Trial has been set for July 18, 2025.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Plaintiff Michael Moran submitted evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Matthew Dobson.

 

The Court rules as follows:

 

OVERRULED: 2-3

SUSTAINED: 1 (as to only “without making any substantial attempts to address GM’s objections and limit the scope of the requested testimony”), 4

 

 

Analysis

 

Standard for Motion to Compel Deposition (PMQ)

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. The person deposed may be a natural person . . . [or] an organization such as a public or private corporation.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.010.) “The service of a deposition notice . . . is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document . . . [or] electronically stored information.” (Id. § 2025.280(a).)

 

“It is established that a litigant has the right to take a proper deposition, and to receive responsive answers to proper questions . . . for the purposes of discovery or for use as evidence, or for both purposes.” (Beverly Hills Nat’l Bank & Tr. Co. v. Super. Ct. (1961) 195 Cal.App.2d 861, 864–65 (citing I.E.S. Corp. v. Super. Ct. (1955) 44 Cal.2d 559 and Tatkin v. Super. Ct. (1958) 160 Cal.App.2d 745).) “The statute authorizing the taking of depositions to be used in pending trials should be liberally construed to the end that a litigant in a pending action may be afforded a reasonable opportunity to procure available testimony in support of his cause.” (Moran v. Super. Ct. (1940) 38 Cal.App.2d 328, 334.) In keeping with this liberal tradition, Plaintiffs are entitled to testimony and documents that are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence about all of the issues in this case. Indeed, “[f]or discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. . . .These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery, and (contrary to popular belief), fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.” (Stewart v. Colonial W. Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1013.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff argues that the deposition testimony of GM’s PMQ is fundamental to Plaintiff’s Song-Beverly Act claim. Plaintiff further argues that he has good cause to seek the discovery information because the seven categories of testimony and ten document requests go to the essential elements of Plaintiff’s express warranty and damages claims. Plaintiff further requests sanctions in the amount of $2,750.00.

 

In opposition, GM argues that Plaintiff’s motion should be denied for failure to meet and confer in good faith. Moreover, GM argues that Plaintiff seeks irrelevant testimony because he failed to sufficiently explain how or why the requested testimony is discoverable, let alone so essential to their claims or GM’s defenses that GM should be compelled to provide it. GM also argues that it agreed to produce a witness to testify about repairs made to Plaintiff’s vehicle, communications, and the reasons that GM did not repurchase Plaintiff’s vehicle and thus the request for sanctions is groundless. 

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues that they attempted to meet and confer multiple times, to no avail. Moreover, they met and conferred during an IDC on October 4, 2024. Additionally, Plaintiff set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying their discovery requests. For instance, matter nos. 3 and 7, refer to the subject vehicle’s defects, warranty nonconformities, and repair history which are relevant to Song Beverly claims. Also, questions related to GM’s procedure to determine whether Plaintiff’s vehicle qualified for a repurchase prior to filing suit (Matter Nos. 1, 2, and 5) are relevant to Plaintiff’s civil penalty claims. Plaintiff properly seeks discovery regarding GM’s lemon law policies and procedures. GM’s policies and procedures are relevant to the issues related to GM’s good faith compliance with the Act, including a breach of the express warranty and implied warranty of merchantability. Plaintiff further argues that GM’s trade secret and confidentiality claims are meritless because there is a protective order and no privilege log. Plaintiff also argues that sanctions are warranted because GM continues to ignore its discovery obligations and is substantially prejudicing Plaintiff’s ability to obtain necessary discovery in order to prepare for trial and failed to show that it acted with substantial justification or that sanctions are unjust. Plaintiff further argues that GM conflates claiming to agree to produce a witness to actually producing a witness. Plaintiff first noticed GM’s deposition on July 1, 2024. To date, GM has not offered a single date for its deposition.

 

The Court grants the Motion because in accordance with CCP § 2025.450 and CCP § 2025.230, a deposition notice was properly served, the deposition was for July 29, 2024, and Defendant did not appear. After meet and confer efforts followed and proved unfruitful, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion.  

 

Meet and Confer Efforts were Sufficient 

 

Defendant correctly contends that under both CCP §§ 2025.450 and 2025.480 meet and confer is required. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were insufficient. The Court disagrees.  

 

Plaintiff details his efforts in the Declaration of Scott A. Sanchez (Sanchez Decl.) which notes that after Defendant’s objections were served, Plaintiff sent a detailed meet and confer letter addressing deficiencies in Defendant’s objections. (Sanchez Decl. ¶ 7.) On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff served his Notice of Deposition for GM’s PMQ. (Sanchez Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. 1.) GM served its responses on June 24, 2024. (Id. at ¶ 6, Exh. 2.) July 2, 2024, Plaintiffs served their First Amended Notice. (Id. at ¶ 8, Exh. 4.) On May 14, 2024, GM served its responses. (Id. at ¶ 9, Exh. 5.) On July 22, 2024, GM served its responses. On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff called GM to meet and confer. Receiving no answer, Plaintiff left a meet and confer voicemail message. Plaintiff also sent a detailed meet and confer correspondence describing his voicemail message. (Sanchez Decl., ¿ 10, Ex. 6.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff served his Second Amended Notice of Deposition. (Sanchez Decl. ¿ 11, Ex. 7.) On August 20, 2024, GM served its objections to the Second Amended Notice. (Sanchez Decl. ¿ 12, Ex. 8.) On August 20, 2024, Plaintiff called GM to meet and confer. GM did not answer, so Plaintiff left a voicemail message. Plaintiff then sent GM a detailed correspondence requesting deposition dates, reminding it of his concession in withdrawing the contested category, proposing a protective order, and proposing a Zoom meeting to discuss any other issues. (Sanchez Decl. ¿ 13, Ex. 9.) On October 4, 2024, based on Plaintiff’s unsuccessful meet and confer efforts, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held. (Sanchez Decl. ¿ 14.) Defendant fails to demonstrate why these meet and confer efforts were insufficient.  

 

The Testimony Sought is Relevant 

 

Defendant’s next contention is that the testimony sought via the Deposition Notice is irrelevant. The Deposition Notice requests testimony on the following seven categories, and requests that documents be produced on ten more: 

 

The categories of testimony include: (1) why GM did not repurchase the vehicle pre-suit; (2) questions about the service, repair and communication history related to the vehicle and Plaintiff; (3) questions related to GM’s investigation of the service history; and (4) questions about the complained of warranty nonconformities. The document requests seek: (1) documents evidencing repairs to the vehicle; (2) photographs or videos GM may have of the vehicle; (3) communications, not involving attorneys, about the vehicle or Plaintiff; (4) the recalls and technical bulletins that apply to Plaintiff’s vehicle; (5) GM’s and its dealers’/customer service representatives’ lemon law policies and procedures; and (6) copies of the documents GM relied on when it chose not to repurchase the vehicle pre-litigation. The categories of testimony and document requests are plainly relevant to the claims in the case, are not overbroad in scope, and are not privileged or otherwise protected in any way.

 

As to the matters for examination, Defendant objects to each matter with similar objections, arguing that either the terms are vague and ambiguous, or that the category is overbroad, unduly burdensome, or oppressive. First, for all matters of examination, the objections are unclear as in the concurrently filed Separate Statement states “GM already offer[ed] to produce a PMQ on the Categories for Examination and producing a plethora of responsive documents.” (Separate Statement, 6:6-7.)  

 

Defendant notes that the dispute lies in Defendant’s internal operations, policies, and procedures. Defendant argues that these materials will provide little benefit to Plaintiff and that attempting to discover these internal operations, policies, and procedures is bad faith discovery tactics and an attempt at discovering privileged or proprietary information. The Court disagrees for two main reasons.  

 

First, although discovery is not limitless, it is broad. As it pertains to relevance, “…any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010). “A trial court must be mindful of the Legislature's preference for discovery over trial by surprise, must construe the facts before it liberally in favor of discovery…” (Williams v. Sup.Ct. (Marshalls of CA, LLC) (2017) 220 Cal.Rptr.3d 472, 479-480).      

 

Here, Plaintiff is requesting the PMQ to speak on whether the Subject Vehicle should have been repurchased. This is relevant because one of the elements of a prima facie case under Song-Beverly is that the Defendant did not promptly repair or repurchase the vehicle. (Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101; Also see CA CIVIL § 1793.2.)  Plaintiff’s intent here is clear, to find out why that did not happen. The same principle can be applied to the second matter for examination, a history of service and repair attempts will show what repairs if any were attempted. Finally, both (1) policies and procedures for determining whether a vehicle qualifies for a repurchase or replacement and (2) training for evaluating a repurchase request will assist in Plaintiff understanding what violations, if any, occurred during the process.   

 

Plaintiff Does Not Seek Trade Secret Material 

 

The second reason the Court disagrees with the contention that Plaintiff is engaging in bad faith discovery is that a protective order is an effective tool well within Defendant’s reach. Defendant argues extensively that if discovery is permitted into the categories of examination, that trade secret material will have to be produced. The Court finds this assertion without merit. Plaintiff is not seeking information about how the Subject Vehicle is conceived, created, or even manufactured. Plaintiff is seeking policies related to processes surrounding the situation when a vehicle does not conform to the warranty. Additionally, if Defendant fears trade secrets may be revealed, it is their burden to show good cause in seeking a protective order. (Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 318.)  

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified is GRANTED.

 

Request for Sanctions

 

Plaintiffs seek sanctions in the amount of $2,750.00 for Defendant’s misuse of discovery. Defendant argues that sanctions are meritless because they already agreed to providing the Person Most Qualified and the objections were appropriate. The Court disagrees with Defendant.

 

Sanctions are warranted as the Motion is granted and GM failed to show substantial justification in not providing the Person Most Qualified.

·         Senior Counsel’s (J. Brian Lynn) hourly rate is $475.00 

·         Counsel’s (Scott Sanchez) hourly rate is $375.00

·         Senior Counsel spent 5 hours drafting this Motion and related documents and meeting and conferring about this issue.

·         Counsel anticipates 1 hour attending the hearing on this matter 

 

Although sanctions are warranted, the amount sought is excessive as this is a simple discovery motion. Thus, sanctions are imposed on Defendant and awarded to Plaintiff in the amount of $937.50 for two and half hours drafting the motion, preparing a reply, and attending the hearing. 

 

Conclusion

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED. Sanctions are imposed on Defendant and awarded to Plaintiff in the amount of $937.50.

Case Number: 24AVCV00765    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a wrongful death action. On June 21, 2024, Plaintiffs Cataleya Inez Lopez, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem Andrea Galvez, Nia Violet Lopez, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem Andrea Galvez, and Noah Alexander Gomez, a minor by and through his guardian ad litem Jacqueline Gomez (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants City of Lancaster, Monique Coreen Molina, Gary L. Carrera, Brenda Carrera, and Does 1 to 50. On August 20, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC). Plaintiffs bring the action on behalf of decedent Joseph Oscar Lopez’s estate (“Decedent”) as his children. The FAC alleges Decedent died due to a collision with a vehicle driven by Defendant Molina and owned by Defendants Gary L. Carrera and Brenda Carrera. The FAC further alleges Defendant City of Lancaster negligently maintained the intersection where the collision occurred—specifically, the intersection had an unprotected left-turn signal such that drivers could not perceive approaching vehicles. Defendant Molina allegedly made a left turn and collided into Decedent, who was riding a motorcycle. The FAC alleges two causes of action: 1) Dangerous Condition of Public Property and 2) Negligence – Wrongful Death and Survivor Action.

 

On October 21, 2024, Defendants Monique C. Molina, Brenda Carrera, and Gary L. Carrera (“Defendants”) filed this Motion to Strike the First Amended Complaint.

 

On November 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the opposition.

 

On November 19, 2024, Defendants filed the reply.

 

Analysis

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendants request judicial notice be taken of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint. The Court notes that it need not take judicial notice of the Complaint and First Amended Complaint.

 

Standard for Ruling on Motion to Strike

 

Code Civil Procedure section 436 permits trial courts to strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in a pleading:

 

 “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

 (a)¿Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

 (b)¿Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 436.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5, subdivision (a) requires the parties to meet and confer “in person or by telephone” regarding the proposed motion to strike. Defendants’ counsel has provided evidence that the parties met and conferred via email since September 24, 2024, yet there is no indication that they met in person or by telephone. (See Minasyan Decl., ¶ 2; Appendix of Exhs., Exh. C.) Defendants’ counsel made efforts to call Plaintiffs’ counsel but counsel did not respond due to an outage with her service provider. The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met. In the interest of judicial efficiency, the Court will assess the merits of the Motion.

 

Motion to Strike

 

            Defendants seek to strike portions of the FAC suggesting Defendants acted intentionally, recklessly, or carelessly. (FAC ¶¶ 32-33, 37, 40, 42-43, 47, 48(a), (b).) Defendants also seek to strike requests for punitive damages. (FAC ¶ 47, Prayer ¶ 8.)

 

            Defendants argues that Plaintiff cannot recover punitive damages under either the wrongful death or survival action claim because 1) there is no felony homicide allowing for an exception under Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (d), and 2) the FAC fails to allege sufficient facts under the survival action claim to meet the threshold for punitive damages.

 

            Plaintiffs argue that there is a less strict pleading standard for a motion to strike punitive damages. Plaintiffs argue that the FAC sufficiently alleges Defendant Molina acted with the requisite recklessness to warrant punitive damages. Plaintiffs argue that they are able to plead inconsistent causes of action. Lastly, Plaintiffs argue a homicide conviction is not a prerequisite to an award of punitive damages for wrongful death claimants.

 

            In reply, Defendants note that Plaintiffs filed the opposition a day late. Defendants argue that Plaintiffs cite the same pleading standard as Defendants—that Plaintiffs must allege facts proving malice, oppression, or fraud under Civil Code section 3294. Plaintiffs cite cases that are unrelated to punitive damages or have to do with the pleading standard in fraud cases. Plaintiffs fail to identify any allegation that would support a claim for punitive damages. Plaintiffs’ argument about inconsistent theories was not raised by Defendant and is therefore, irrelevant. Lastly, Plaintiffs are incorrect that neither Civil Code Section 3294(d) nor Shore v. Gurnette, 122 Cal.App.4th 166 (2004) requires a felony homicide conviction as a prerequisite to punitive damages in a wrongful death claim.

 

            The Court will address the merits of the punitive damages claim under wrongful death claim and the survival action claim.

 

Wrongful Death Claim

 

            Generally, wrongful death claimants cannot recover punitive damages, unless the death resulted from a felony homicide claim for which defendant was convicted. (See Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (d); Punitive Damages, Cal. Prac. Guide Pers. Inj. Ch. 3-E.)

 

            The Court thus finds punitive damages are unavailable for Plaintiffs’ wrongful death claim where the death did not result from a felony homicide claim for which defendant was convicted.

 

Survival Action Claim

 

            In survival actions, “Punitive damages are awardable to the decedent's estate in an action by the estate representative based on the cause of action the decedent would have had if he or she had survived. Relatively minor compensatory damages. . .can be the springboard for substantial punitive damages.” (Rufo v. Simpson (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 573, 616; see also Code Civ. Proc. § 377.34.) The Court notes that while punitive damages are allowable in survival action, Plaintiffs must meet the pleading standard required for the underlying negligence action.

 

“‘In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.’ [Citation.]” (Today IV’s Inc. v. Los Angeles County MTA (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) Civil Code section 3294 provides that on a non-contract cause of action, a plaintiff may recover punitive damages when they prove “by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).) In a negligence action, plaintiffs must allege facts in the complaint demonstrating malice—conclusory allegations that simply characterize defendant's conduct as “malicious” are insufficient and subject to motion to strike. (See Smith v. Sup.Ct. (Bucher) (1992) 10 CA4th 1033, 1036, 1042 (finding, in a legal malpractice claim, plaintiff’s conclusory allegations that attorneys' conduct was “intentional, knowing, malicious, fraudulent, false and deceitful” and action in “conscious and knowing disregard of the interest and rights of plaintiff” were deficient as a matter of law to show entitlement to punitive damages).)

           

            The Court finds the FAC contains conclusory allegations of malice, which is insufficient to support a punitive damages award. A complaint must plead facts, not conclusory allegations, showing entitlement to punitive damages. Here, the FAC fails to set forth allegations suggesting that Defendants acted intentionally, recklessly, or with conscious disregard to Decedent’s rights or safety when Defendant Molina collided with Decedent. Thus, the punitive damages claim sought under the survival action is unsupported.

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the Motion to Strike portions of the FAC suggesting Defendants acted intentionally, recklessly, or carelessly and portions of the FAC in reference to punitive damages.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS the Motion to Strike.

Case Number: 24AVCV00806    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a breach of contract case. On July 3, 2024, Plaintiff Coates Construction Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against Defendants RTMS LEASING- California II, LLC (RTMS), and Antelope Valley Hospital (AVH; collectively Defendants). Plaintiff alleged that it entered into an agreement with Defendants regarding building an extension to AVH’s emergency treatment facility. Plaintiff was to provide labor and materials necessary for the site including construction of the foundations so that RTMS could install the modular building components. RTMS’s design issues caused unreasonable delays which it foisted upon Plaintiff, eventually leading RTMS to wrongfully terminate the Agreement. Plaintiff sued.

 

Now RMTS moves to compel the parties to arbitration. AVH opposes the motion.

 

Evidentiary Issues

 

RTMS request for judicial notice of the fact that Antelope Valley Healthcare District is a public entity, and that Edward Mirzabegian is Chief Executive Officer of the Antelope Valley Healthcare District is GRANTED. (Evid. Code, §452, subd. (h).)

 

AVH’s request for judicial notice of the Complaint, and Plaintiff’s request for dismissal of two causes of action in that Complaint are denied as unnecessary. The court may refer to its own docket absent judicial notice.

 

AVH’s objections to the declaration of Zachariah Tomli and Michael Kosinski are overruled.

 

 

 

Analysis

 

Legal  Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, a court may order arbitration of a controversy if it finds that the parties have agreed to arbitrate that dispute. Because the obligation to arbitrate arises from contract, the court may compel arbitration only if the dispute in question is one in which the parties have agreed to arbitrate. (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 352.) Since arbitration is a favored method of dispute resolution, arbitration agreements should be liberally interpreted, and arbitration should be ordered unless the agreement clearly does not apply to the dispute in question. (Id. at p. 353; Segal v. Silberstein (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 627, 633.)

 

The moving party bears the initial burden “of producing prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy.” (Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 165-66 (Gamboa).) The moving party must establish “the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.” (Engalla v. Permanente Med. Grp., Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951,  972 (Engalla).)

 

The moving party can meet this burden “by attaching to the motion or petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the opposing party’s signature.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at pp. 165-66.) Alternatively, the moving party can meet its burden by setting forth the agreement’s provisions in the motion. (Ibid; see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) “If the moving party meets it initial prima facie burden and the opposing party does not dispute the existence of the arbitration agreement, then nothing more is required for the moving party to meet its burden of persuasion.” (Gamboa, supra, 72 Cal.App.5th at pp. 165-66.)

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

            In ruling on a petition to compel arbitration, a court must determine two threshold matters: first, whether a valid agreement to arbitrate exists; and second, whether that agreement encompasses the dispute at issue. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)  

 

By way of background, on November 11th, 2020, Plaintiff and RTMS entered into an agreement (“Agreement”). (Declaration of Michael Kosinski [“Kosinski Decl.”], ¶11, Ex A [“Agreement”].) The Agreement states that “This Agreement has been approved by Antelope Valley Hospital (‘AVH’ or ‘Owner’) which is the owner of the site on which the Project will be built. . .” (Agreement, ¶Preamble.) In moving for arbitration, RTMS request a court order compelling the parties to arbitrate the claims pursuant to this Agreement.

 

            Acknowledging that the Agreement do not identify AVH as one of the parties entering into it, RTMS argues AVH is nonetheless bound to the agreement after having been referred to throughout and having signed as acknowledging the terms and conditions of the Agreement.

 

            “A contract must be so interpreted as to give effect to the mutual intention of the parties as it existed at the time of contracting, so far as the same is ascertainable and lawful.” (Civ. Code, §1636.) “The words of a contract are to be understood in their ordinary and popular sense. . .; unless used by the parties in a technical sense, or unless a special meaning is given to them by usage, in which case the latter must be followed.” (Civ. Code, §1644.)

 

            Here, the terms of the Agreement are definite and clear that while Plaintiff is a “Party” bound to the terms of the Agreement, AVH is not.

 

The Agreement defines Plaintiff as “Contractor” and RTMS as “Purchaser.” (Agreement, ¶Preamble.) In the “Project Participants” section it states “ ‘Party’ means either of Contract or Purchaser and ‘Parties’ means both Contractor and Purchaser.” (Agreement, ¶2) AVH is expressly excluded from the definition of “Parties”. The arbitration provision states:

 

The Parties agree to first seek to resolve any dispute by good faith negotiations. If a dispute is not promptly resolved through negotiation, and if not resolved in any event within five (5) business days, the dispute will be submitted to the AOR for decision. In reaching a decision the AOR may involve Contractor, Purchaser, AVH, and any relevant third parties for resolution and decision, and the AOR shall attempt to issue a decision within ten (10) business days after the AOR notified of the dispute.

 

(Agreement, ¶16 [emphasis added].) The plain language of the Agreement defines the terms “Parties” and then uses that defined terms to state who agrees to arbitration. The arbitration provision also differentiates “Parties” from AVH when it states that the arbitrator may involve AVH in reaching a decision. Were AVH bound to arbitrate there would be no need to identify it as an entity the arbitrator could consult with or “involve” in reaching a decision.  

 

            The signature blocks also indicate that AVH is not a party to the Agreement. Above RTMS and Plaintiff’s signatures—which are side-by-side in a separated box—is the final paragraph of the contract which states that “The Parties intend for this writing to be the complete and final expression of their agreement.” (Agreement, ¶25 [emphasis added].) Above Edward Mirzabegian’s signature block identifying him as the CEO of AVH, on the other hand, it states:

 

This Agreement is acknowledged by AVH, as the Owner of the project Site and the intended occupant and tenant of the Modular Building to be constructed as part of the Project. AVH acknowledges that the terms and conditions of this Agreement may affect or result in Additional Charges to AVH under the Installation Services and Warranty Agreement entered into by and between AVH and RAD Technology Medical Systems, LLC (the parent company of Purchaser).

 

(Agreement, at p. 15.) Mirzabegian’s signature is separate from the signatures of the previously defined Parties and the language directly prior to it demonstrates the Parties and AVH intended to differentiate AVH.

 

            In the reply RTMS continues to argue that as a signatory AVH is bound to the entire Agreement. RTMS argues that in signing the agreement AVH manifested assent to all the terms and that signatories are bound to the provisions of what they sign. (See Meyer v. Benko (1976) 55 Cal.App.3d 937, 942-43.) However, contrary to RTMS’s position, the court is following the rule the signatories are bound to what they sign in finding AVH is not a party. The Agreement explicitly excludes AVH from the definition of party, and uses a defined term in describing who agrees to arbitration. It also includes specific language to ensure it is clear that AVH’s signature serves a limited purpose. Had the parties wanted to AVH to be bound by the arbitration provision they could have drafted the Agreement to do so. In asking the court to compel AVH to arbitration, RTMS is asking it to disregard the express contractual terms; it cannot.

 

            Furthermore, there is no consideration binding AVH to the agreement or any manifestation of mutual assent via a promise to begin or render some performance.  (See Rest.2d Contract, §18 [“Manifestation of mutual assent to an exchange requires that each party either make a promise or begin or render a performance.”].) A review of the Agreement confirms that AVH made no promises to perform. While AVH is referenced regularly in the Agreement, none of the references compel AVH to act. For example, the Parties state that changes can be made to the Agreement in several ways including “under a separate written agreement signed by Contractor, Purchase, AVH and the AOR. . .” (Agreement, ¶3.) This language does not obligate AVH to enter into a separate written agreement, it simply prohibits the Parties from doing so without AVH’s permission.  

 

As another example, the Agreement states that if the contractor fails to prosecute the work and fourteen days have passed without adequate assurances, then “Purchaser may, with AOR and AJH and AVH approval, and without further notice to Contractor terminate this Agreement, in whole or in part. . .” (Agreement, ¶4.) In this provision AVH is referenced, but has no obligation. AVH has not responsible for reviewing Purchaser’s notice, giving approval, or taking any specific action. The provision simply limits the Purchaser’s ability to take certain action without AVH’s approval. AHS’s obligation to take action may exist elsewhere, such as in a contract between RTMS and AVH, but it does not exist in this provision.

           

            RTMS also argues that it may enforce the arbitration against AVH because AVH is a third-party beneficiary.

 

“Under California law, a nonsignatory can be compelled to arbitrate under two sets of circumstances: (1) where the nonsignatory is a third party beneficiary of the contract containing the arbitration agreement; and [(]2) where a preexisting relationship existed between the nonsignatory and one of the parties to the arbitration agreement, making it equitable to compel the nonsignatory to also be bound to arbitrate his or her claim.” (Crowley Mar. Corp. v. Bos. Old Colony Ins. Co., (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1061, 1069–70.)

 

In considering the first prong the court looks to the definition of a third party beneficiary. “A third party beneficiary is someone who may enforce a contract because the contract is made expressly for his benefit.  [Citation.]  The test for determining whether a contract was made for the benefit of a third person is whether an intent to benefit a third person appears from the terms of the contract. [Citation.]  The mere fact that a contract results in benefits to a third party does not render that party a third party beneficiary. [Citation]”  (Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 973 [internal quotation marks and brackets omitted].)

 

RTMS relies on the reasoning Loduca v. Polyzos (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 334, 343 (Loduca) to support that it can compel AVH to arbitration as a third party beneficiary. In Loduca, “a property owner, as a third party beneficiary, sued a subcontractor in contract and tort for breach of the contract between the subcontractor and the general contractor.” (Id. at p. 337.) The court granted the owner’s motion for attorney fees pursuant to an attorney fee clause in the contract between the subcontractor and general contractor. In affirming the trial court’s decision to award attorneys’ fees to the owner, the Court of Appeal explained that the owner was an “intended third party beneficiary.” (Id. at p. 343.)

 

The court reasoned that the contract expressly named the owner in the reference line, the attorneys’ fees provision did not limit recovery only to the parties, the contract did not impose any limitation on third party rights, and the relationship between the owner and the subcontractor was sufficiently close. (Id. at pp. 343-344.)

 

However, Session Payroll Management Inc., v. Noble Const. Co. Inc (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 671 (Session Payroll) is the more persuasive authority because the facts are more analogous to the present suit. In Session Payroll, the Court of Appeal reversed an award of attorneys’ fees to a defendant signatory against a losing plaintiff third-party.

 

The issue was whether, pursuant the principles of reciprocity imposed by Civil Code section 1717, the plaintiff would have been entitled to attorney’s fees as a third-party beneficiary on a contract between a general contractor and subcontractor. He was not. (Id. at p. 680.) The court reasoned that the underlying contract gave no indication that the parties “agreed or intended to include [plaintiff] within the attorney fee provision” and the agreement expressly disclaimed that it created any rights or conferred any benefits on their parties “ ‘[e]xcept as specifically prescribed. . .’ ” (Ibid.)

 

Here, similar to Session Payroll, the parties explicitly limit the application of the third-party beneficiaries doctrine. The Agreement states “[i]t is not intended by any of the provisions of this Agreement to create any third-party beneficiary relationship. . . except to the extent that specific provisions (such as the warranty and indemnity provisions) identify third parties and state that they are entitled to benefits hereunder.” (Agreement, ¶19.) Also, similar to Session Payroll, the parties explicitly identified who was bound by the arbitration provisions, i.e., “Parties”. (Agreement, ¶16.)

 

Yet, even assuming that AVH was a third-party beneficiary, RTMS still fails to demonstrate a sufficiently close relationship between AVH and RTMS as required under the Crowley test. In Crawley, the court stated “[e]xamples of the preexisting relationship include agency, spousal relationship, parent-child relationship and the relationship of a general partner to a limited partnership.” (Crowley, supra, 158 Cal.App.4th at p. 1070.) As another example, in Loduca, the court found the relationship close because the subcontractor accepted payment directly from the owner and the subcontractor, who authored the contract, and the general contractor “both understood [the owner] to be the only intended beneficiary who could enforce the contract.” (Loduca, supra, 84 Cal.App.4th at p. 343.)

 

Here, the court lacks evidence of any sufficiently close preexisting relationship between RTMS and AVH such that compelling arbitration is appropriate.

 

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED as to AVH.

 

Plaintiff does not oppose the motion and as discussed above, is a party to the Agreement and its arbitration provision. The motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitration is GRANTED.

 

Conclusion

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED in part. The motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitration is GRANTED. The motion to compel AVH to arbitration is DENIED.

 

The case is stayed pending arbitration.
