Case Number: 21AVCV00905    Hearing Date: January 23, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Cristina Mondy (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on or about May 21, 2020, at approximately 6:00 pm, she was lawfully visiting a friend who was renting a home owned by Defendants Tom Deghionno (“Tom”)[1], Audrey Deghionno (“Audrey”), and Laura Deghionno (“Laura” and collectively “Defendants”) when Defendants’ two large pit bull mix dogs got loose and bit Plaintiff on both of her arms. Plaintiff further alleges that sustained severe injuries.

 

On November 09, 2021, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging two causes of action for Negligence and Premises Liability.

 

On October 28, 2022, Tom and Audrey (collectively “Moving Defendants”) filed their Answer.

 

The Court notes that Laura was served on January 23, 2023 and, at this time, has neither answered nor been placed in default.

 

On November 07, 2023, Moving Defendants filed this Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication.

 

On January 09, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition.

 

On January 18, 2024, Moving Defendants filed the Reply.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Summary Judgment – The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc.¿§437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67 (“Juge”), citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Cal.¿Code Civ.¿Proc.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v.¿Paetkau¿(1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿¿¿¿

Plaintiff’s Request for Continuance

 

Plaintiff places this request at the very end of her 25-page Opposition filing. The Court address it first as the request would impact the Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

Plaintiff requests a continuance to conduct Laura’s deposition. However, Laura has not answered the Complaint.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(h) reads:

 

If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.

 

While Plaintiff has stated she served Laura and attempted to depose Laura (see Decl. Desman ¶¶ 6-9), Plaintiff has stated that her Opposition is “is based upon the attached Memorandum of Points and Authorities, the Declaration of Andrew M. Jacobson, Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, Written Objections to Defendant’s Evidence, Plaintiff’s Response to Defendants’ Separate Statement, and all pleadings and papers on file herein as well as any oral and/or documentary evidence to be presented at the time of the hearing of this Motion.” (Opp. 2:8-12.) Plaintiff has provided a complete Opposition, including a 26-page Opposition filing, a 51-page filing for Requests for Judicial Notice, a 25-page Separate Statement filing, a 15-page evidentiary objection filing, and a 190-page declaration by Desman which includes approx. 184 pages of exhibits. It is not clear that any additional facts that are essential to justify the opposition may exist, but cannot be presented as Plaintiff has provided a fully formulated Opposition.

 

Further, “a party who seeks a continuance under section 437c, subdivision (h), must show why the discovery necessary to oppose the motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication could not have been completed sooner, and accordingly requires the court to grant the continuance.” (Braganza v. Albertson’s LLC (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 144, 156 [citing Cooksey v. Alexakis (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 246].)  Here, Laura has been named a defendant since the inception of the action, November 09, 2021; Plaintiff did not serve Laura with the Summons and Complaint until January 16, 2023; subsequently, Plaintiff took no action against Laura in the case until January 11, 2024, after the filing of this Opposition, wherein Plaintiff requested default of Laura. Outside of the case filings, the subpoena filed by Desman shows that the deposition of Laura was not noticed until January 05, 2024. (See Pl.’s Exh. 8.) Plaintiff has been aware of the Motion for Summary Judgment  since at least November 07, 2023, yet waited until 19 days prior to the hearing on the Motion for Summary Judgment to notice Laura’s deposition. Desman has not attested to any lack of diligence on Plaintiff’s part despite the record and Exhibit 8 showing otherwise.

 

Accordingly, the Court denies the request for continuance to depose Laura, a defaulted party.

 

For clarity regarding how the default impacts this Motion for Summary Judgment, the Court directs the parties to Inzunza v. Naranjo (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 736 (“Inzunza”) which summarizes the effects of defaulting defendants on codefendants which reads, in relevant part:

 

. . .[C]ourts have held that admissions implied from the default of one defendant are not binding on a codefendant who answered, and expressly denied, the allegations in the complaint.

 

For example, in Taylor v. Socony Mobil Oil Co. (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 832, 833 [51 Cal. Rptr. 764] (Taylor), the plaintiff sued Socony Mobil and its employee for malicious prosecution. The employee failed to answer the complaint and his default was entered, but Socony Mobil answered, “denying all material allegations of the complaint.” (Ibid.) The action went to trial against Socony Mobil and at the conclusion of plaintiff's case, the trial court granted Socony Mobil's motion for nonsuit. (Ibid.) On appeal, the plaintiff did “not seriously argue that he presented testimony constituting a prima facie showing of lack of probable cause or of malice” but argued “proof of these issues is supplied by the pleadings” because the employee admitted the allegations in the pleadings by failing to answer the complaint. (Id. at pp. 833–834.) The Court of Appeal affirmed the judgment, explaining: “The validity of plaintiff's argument rests upon his major premise that an admission implied from the default of one defendant is binding upon an answering codefendant who has denied the relevant allegations of the complaint. His position is untenable.” (Id. at p. 834.) Rather, “the correct rule … [is] ‘… that admissions implied from the default of one defendant ordinarily are not binding upon a codefendant who, by answering, expressly denies and places in issue the truth of the allegations thus admitted by the absent party.’” (Ibid.)

 

Similarly, in Western Heritage Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1196, 1199–1200 [132 Cal. Rptr. 3d 209] (Western Heritage), the court held an intervening insurer was permitted to litigate the issue of liability notwithstanding its insured's default. The court explained: “A party's default does not bind nondefaulting codefendants, even when the basis for the action against the codefendants is vicarious liability arising from the acts of the defaulting defendant.” (Id. at p. 1211.)

 

Accordingly, any admission implied from Laura’s default does not impact this Motion for Summary Judgment.

The Court has addressed the issue of the causes of actions pled, ante. (See Issues Framed by the Pleading).

 

“ ‘The elements of a cause of action for negligence are well established. They are “(a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c)the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” ’ ” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. Premises liability ‘ “is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises” ’; accordingly, ‘ “mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act.” ’ But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases. In determining whether a premises owner owes a duty to persons on its property, we apply the Rowland [Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 [70 Cal.Rptr. 97, 443 P.2d 561]] factors. Indeed, Rowland itself involved premises liability.’ ” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.)

 

Case law provides a standard for a landlord’s duty in relation to dogs on the premises:

 

·       Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1369-70: “. . .[A] landlord does not owe a duty of care to protect a third party from his or her tenant's dog unless the landlord has actual knowledge of the dog's dangerous propensities, and the ability to control or prevent the harm. [Citations.] . . . ‘ “[A] duty of care may not be imposed on a landlord without proof that he knew of the dog and its dangerous propensities. Because the harboring of pets is such an important part of our way of life and because the exclusive possession of rented premises normally is vested in the tenant, … actual knowledge and not mere constructive knowledge is required. . .” ’ ” 

·       Yuzon v. Collins (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 149, 152: “Under California law, a landlord owes a duty of care to his tenant’s invitees to prevent injury from the tenant’s vicious dog when the landlord has ‘actual knowledge’ of the dog’s vicious nature in time to protect against the dangerous condition on his property. [Citation.]” 

·       Donchin v. Guerrero (1995) 34 Cal.App.4th 1832, 1838 (“Donchin”): “Under California law, a landlord who does not have actual knowledge of a tenant's dog's vicious nature cannot be held liable when the dog attacks a third person. In other words, where a third person is bitten or attacked by a tenant's dog, the landlord's duty of reasonable care to the injured third person depends on whether the dog's vicious behavior was reasonably foreseeable. Without knowledge of a dog's propensities a landlord will not be able to foresee the animal poses a danger and thus will not have a duty to take measures to prevent the attack.” 

·       Uccello v. Laudenslayer (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 504, 514 (“Uccello”): “It should be emphasized that a duty of care may not be imposed on a landlord without proof that he knew of the dog and its dangerous propensities. Because the harboring of pets is such an important part of our way of life and because the exclusive possession of rented premises normally is vested in the tenant, we believe that actual knowledge and not mere constructive knowledge is required. For this reason we hold that a landlord is under no duty to inspect the premises for the purpose of discovering the existence of a tenant's dangerous animal; only when the landlord has actual knowledge of the animal, coupled with the right to have it removed from the premises, does a duty of care arise.” 

 

Thus, duty of care may not be imposed on a landlord without proof that he or she knew of the dog and its dangerous propensities or, in this case, both dogs and their dangerous propensities.

 

Audrey has stated that she had no knowledge that any dogs resided at the subject residence. (See Decl. Audrey ¶ 6.) However, Tom has conceded that he knew of the presence of the dogs, but not their dangerous propensities. (See Decl. Tom ¶ 11.)

 

Moving Defendants have provided discovery responses showing that Plaintiff lacks evidence of the dogs’ dangerous propensities.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory 17.1 states in various sectors that the dogs have a “previous history” in the incident report created. (See Decl. John G. Wilcoxson, Exh. E.) However, the “previous history” is not expanded upon and Plaintiff states in her Requests for Admission (“RFAs”) that “[a]t this time, she does not have sufficient information at this time to either admit or deny” as to whether Plaintiff had evidence that the two dogs had bitten or attacked any person prior to the incident. (See id. at Exh. C RFA No. 16.) RFA No. 17 is similar to RFA No. 16, but asks about the dogs’ propensity. (See id. at Exh. C RFA No. 17.) Plaintiff denies this RFA but no information is provided. (See ibid.) As to Plaintiff’s discussion of the dogs breed as evidence of dangerous propensities, Yuzon holds that judicial notice is improper where the request is that all dogs of a certain breed are dangerous. (See id. at Exh. E Form Interrogatory 17.1 [“Defendants knew that the dogs were large in size Defendants knew that the dogs were a mix Pit Bull breed, a mix breed that includes Pit Bull, which has known dangerous propensities. . . which has known dangerous propensities. Based on the nature, demeanor, size and breed of the subject Dogs, the subject dogs had a propensity to bite or attack people, which was known by Defendants and each of them.”]; see also Yuzon, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at 168 [“Dr. Polsky’s statement that adult male pit bulls ‘are often unpredictable in nature and frequently possess an inherent propensity for aggressive responding towards unfamiliar people who come near their territory’ has no possible relevance given the absence of any evidence that Collins knew or must have known that Kemo was an adult male pit bull. The question is not whether Collins should have known or should have determined that Kemo was an aggressive adult male pit bull, but whether Collins must have known or had actual knowledge of that fact. (Uccello v. Laudenslayer, supra, 44 Cal. App. 3d at p. 507.) Moreover, just as it is improper to take judicial notice that all German shepherds are dangerous (Lundy v. California Realty (1985) 170 Cal. App. 3d 813, 822 [216 Cal. Rptr. 575]), it would be improper to take judicial notice that all adult male pit bulls are dangerous.”].) Based on precedent, what must be shown is not that Moving Defendants knew that the dogs were pit bull mixes, but that that Moving Defendants knew that the dogs were pit bull mixes with dangerous propensities. Moving Defendants have shown that Plaintiff does not have information concerning this element.

 

The Court need not address the argument that Moving Defendants had no duty to perform inspections of the rented dwelling as an essential element has already been negated.

 

As to the argument regarding Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(o)(2), addressed in the Table of Contents as “California of Civil Section 473(o)(2)” and in the moving papers as only “section 473(o)(2) (see Motion for Summary Judgment 12:27, 13:23), the Court believes this is a scrivener’s error as Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 concerns amendments of pleadings and does not have a subdivision (o). The Court believes Moving Defendants are using Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437(o)(2).

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437(o)(2) provides:

 

A cause of action has no merit if either of the following exists:

(1) One or more of the elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded.

(2) A defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.

 

Moving Defendants have met their initial burden by establishing there is no evidence that they did not own the dogs and did not know of the dogs’ dangerous propensities.

 

The burden then shifts to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.

(See Opp. 23:5-23.) However, Tom’s knowledge of the dogs is a non-issue as Tom concedes knowledge of them in his declaration. (See decl. Tom ¶ 11.)

 

Regarding requisite knowledge of dangerous propensities, Plaintiff relies on five things: (1) the Request for Judicial Notice; (2) Plaintiff’s statements; (3) Laura’s statements; (4) the fence erected by Cervantes and Emily; (5) Tom visited the premises before the subject incident and was aware that Laura kept dogs; and (5) Tom recognized that Laura needed dogs for protection. As to Laura’s statements, Plaintiff would like Laura’s knowledge imputed on Tom. Plaintiff presents that Laura is Tom’s agent. However, this goes against the pleadings. (See Complaint ¶¶ 1, 2, 12.) FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367 (“FPI Development”) discusses the role of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment: 

 

“The purpose of a summary judgment proceeding is to permit a party to show that material factual claims arising from the pleadings need not be tried because they are not in dispute.” (Andalon v. Superior Court, supra, 162 Cal.App.3d at pp. 604-605.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues: the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Orange County Air Pollution Control Dist. v. Superior Court (1972) 27 Cal.App.3d 109, 113 [103 Cal.Rptr. 410]; see also, e.g., Pultz v. Holgerson (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 1110, 1114 [229 Cal.Rptr. 531].) The complaint measures the materiality of the facts tendered in a defendant's challenge to the plaintiff's cause of action. (C. L. Smith Co. v. Roger Ducharme, Inc. (1977) 65 Cal.App.3d 735, 745 [135 Cal.Rptr. 483].) The answer supplements that measure where the plaintiff is the moving party and the defendant relies upon an affirmative defense. It is incumbent upon a moving party plaintiff not only to show there is no material factual dispute with respect to its cause of action but also to show that there is no material factual dispute with respect to the defenses proffered by the defendant. (See Sadlier v. Superior Court (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 1050, 1055-1056 [229 Cal.Rptr. 374].) 

 

[. . .] 

 

The procedure for resolving a summary judgment motion presupposes that the pleadings are adequate to put in issue a cause of action or defense thereto. (See Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262 [223 P.2d 244].) However a pleading may be defective in failing to allege an element of a cause of action or in failing to intelligibly identify a defense thereto. In such a case, the moving party need not address a missing element or, obviously, respond to assertions which are unintelligible or make out no recognizable legal claim. The summary judgment proceeding is thereby necessarily transmuted into a test of the pleadings and the summary judgment motion into a motion for judgment on the pleadings. In these circumstances it has been said that a defendant's “motion for summary judgment necessarily includes a test of the sufficiency of the complaint and as such is in legal effect a motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Barnett v. Delta Lines, Inc. (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 674, 682 [187 Cal.Rptr. 219], citations omitted.) Similarly, a plaintiff's motion for summary judgment necessarily includes a test of the sufficiency of the answer. (See Sadlier, supra, 184 Cal.App.3d at p. 1055.) 

 

(FPI Development, Inc., supra, 231 Cal.App.3d at 381-32.) 

 

That is, since a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication is limited to the issues raised by the pleadings (see Lewis v. Chevron (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 690, 694), all evidence submitted in support of or in opposition to the motion must be addressed to the claims and defenses specifically raised in the pleadings; an issue that is “within the general area of issues framed by the pleadings” is properly before the court on a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion (see Lennar Northeast Partners v. Buice (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1576,1582-1583). The Court cannot consider an unpleaded issue in the context of its ruling on motion for summary judgment or adjudication. (Roth v. Rhodes (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 530, 541.) As such, the Court cannot consider Plaintiff’s agency argument due to the pleadings. The pleadings allege that a friend is renting the room from Tom and that Doe Defendants are agents of Tom. (See Complaint ¶¶ 1, 2, 5[5], 12.) Because the Complaint is vague as to who the friend is, a liberal reading of the Complaint as a whole, including Paragraph 4 which discusses Laura’s address, would allow the inference that the friend is Laura. However, there is no such leeway for the agency argument as Plaintiff has distinguished Laura from Doe Defendants 1 through 20.

 

The Court addresses the Rowland factors for clarity. Application of the Rowland factors is a two-step inquiry: (1) first, the court must determine whether there exists a special relationship between the parties or some other set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty to protect; and (2) if so, the court must consult the factors described in Rowland to determine whether relevant policy considerations counsel limiting that duty. (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 209 (“Brown”).) The California Supreme Court clarified that (1) Cal. Civ. Code § 1714 creates a general rule of duty; (2) Cal. Civ. Code §1714 has limits – “the law imposes a general duty of care on a defendant only when it is the defendant who has ‘ “created a risk” ’ of harm to the plaintiff, including when ‘ “the defendant is responsible for making the plaintiff's position worse[;]” ’ (3) the no-duty-to-protect rule is not absolute and, under some circumstances, a defendant may have an affirmative duty to protect the plaintiff from harm at the hands of a third party, even though the risk of harm is not of the defendant's own making such as if a person choosing to undertake to come to the aid of another or when a special relationship exists. (Id. at 213-16.) In Brown, the plaintiff argues that a duty may arise without a special relationship or where there are no circumstances that would give rise to an affirmative duty to protect; the Brown court summarized the Rowland ruling and its place in California jurisprudence; and held that Rowland “was not designed as a freestanding means of establishing duty, but instead as a means for deciding whether to limit a duty derived from other sources.” (Id. at 217; see id. [generally].) Cal. Civ. Code § 1714 reads, in relevant part:

Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself. The design, distribution, or marketing of firearms and ammunition is not exempt from the duty to use ordinary care and skill that is required by this section. The extent of liability in these cases is defined by the Title on Compensatory Relief.

 

A dangerous condition includes dogs, if the landlord knew, or in the exercise of reasonable care would have known that the dog was regularly present on the property and was dangerous. (See Portillo v. Aiassa (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133-34 (“Portillo”).) Portillo reads, in relevant part:

 

A landlord owes a duty of care to a tenant to provide and maintain safe conditions on the leased premises. ( Becker v. IRM Corp. (1985) 38 Cal.3d 454, 467 [213 Cal.Rptr. 213, 698 P.2d 116, 48 A.L.R.4th 601].) This duty of care also extends to the general public. "A lessor who leases property for a purpose involving the admission of the public is under a duty to see that it is safe for the purposes intended, and to exercise reasonable care to inspect and repair the premises before possession is transferred so as to prevent any unreasonable risk of harm to the public who may enter. [Citations.] An agreement to renew a lease or relet the premises … cannot relieve the lessor of his duty to see that the premises are reasonably safe at that time." ( Burroughs v. Ben's Auto Park, Inc. (1945) 27 Cal.2d 449, 453-454 [164 P.2d 897].)

 

Where there is a duty to exercise reasonable care in the inspection of premises for dangerous conditions, the lack of awareness of the dangerous condition does not generally preclude liability. ( Becker v. IRM Corp., supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 469.) "Although liability might easily be found where the landowner has actual knowledge of the dangerous condition '[t]he landowner's lack of knowledge of the dangerous condition is not a defense. He has an affirmative duty to exercise ordinary care to keep the premises in a reasonably safe condition, and therefore must inspect them or take other proper means to ascertain their condition. And if, by the exercise of reasonable care, he would have discovered the dangerous condition, he is liable.' " ( Swanberg v. O'Mectin (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 325, 330 [203 Cal.Rptr. 701].)

 

(Id. at 1134.)

 

Swanberg v. O’Mectin 1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 325 (“Swanberg”) provides, in relevant part:

 

In California, it has long been the law that a person may be liable for injuries resulting from his failure to use ordinary care in the management of his property. ( Civ. Code, § 1714; Lipson v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 362, 372 [182 Cal.Rptr. 629, 644 P.2d 822].) It makes no difference whether the danger posed by said lack of care manifests itself in the form of a natural as opposed to an artificial condition on the property. ( Sprecher v. Adamson Companies (1981) 30 Cal.3d 358 [178 Cal.Rptr. 783, 636 P.2d 1121].) CA(4) (4) Further, a landowner may face liability for injury to another, incurred outside of the former's property (on an adjacent street), if the injury is found to be caused by a traffic obstruction in the form of shrubbery growing from the property. ( Wisher v. Fowler (1970) 7 Cal.App.3d 225 [86 Cal.Rptr. 582].)

 

Although liability might easily be found where the landowner has actual knowledge of the dangerous condition, "[the] landowner's lack of knowledge of the dangerous condition is not a defense. He has an affirmative duty to exercise ordinary care to keep the premises in a reasonably safe condition, and therefore must inspect them or take other proper means to ascertain their condition. And if, by the exercise of reasonable care, he would have discovered the dangerous condition, he is liable." (4 Witkin, Summary of Cal. Law (8th ed. 1974) Torts, § 592, p. 2860; italics in original.) Accordingly, it would appear that actual knowledge is not an absolute requirement for finding liability here.

 

Unfortunately, it is not clear whether the above authority applies merely to the owner of property under all circumstances, or solely to an owner in possession. In the matter sub judice, the trial court's disallowance of jury instructions embodying the above tenets of law, was undoubtedly grounded upon a belief that a landowner can exempt himself from said affirmative duty, by leasing his property and therein relinquishing his right to control same. Although we are in accord with the general principle that a party should not be held liable for conditions beyond his control, in this case the evidence strongly suggests that the landowner could have easily controlled his property, and further, that said duty to control is nondelegable.

 

 The authority underlying the court's rejection of the proffered jury instructions is the case of Uccello v. Laudenslayer (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 504 [118 Cal.Rptr. 741, 81 A.L.R.3d 628]. Uccello involved an action against a landlord brought by an invitee who received injuries from a vicious dog kept on the leased premises. The Uccello court emphasized that actual, as opposed to constructive, knowledge was a requisite for finding the landlord liable. The reason for the distinction being rooted to the historical doctrine that a landlord who leases his property, surrenders virtual control of his land to the lessee, absent an agreement to the contrary. ( Uccello, supra, at pp. 510-511; see also com. a to Rest., Torts, § 355.) Because said loss of control includes the right of a landlord to reenter the property without the lessee's permission, short of abridging the latter's right of quiet enjoyment, amongst other privacy law considerations, the landlord is in no position to reliably determine whether or not a tenant/lessee is harboring a dangerous animal.

 

 In contrast, this case does not involve a "transitory" and "easily-concealed" danger such as an animal with vicious propensities. On the contrary, it involves a portion of the property easily viewed from the outside of same. In fact, it was so easily viewed, as alleged, that it may have constituted a menace to persons off the land on the adjacent street. For this reason, we find Uccello v. Laudenslayer, supra, 44 Cal.App.3d 504, inapplicable to the instant case.

 

Aside from the inapplicability of Uccello, it would appear that the O'Mectins' duty to care for their property, so as to reasonably avoid injury to others, outside the perimeters of their land, was nondelegable.

 

(Swanberg, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 325, 330-31.)

 

Unlike Swanberg, Uccello is directly applicable to this action. As such, actual knowledge of the dogs and their dangerous propensities is necessary for finding the landlord liable in this action. Plaintiff has not shown that Moving Defendant knew of the dogs’ dangerous propensities.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Reconsideration – Cal. Civ. Code Proc. § 1008(a) states:¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.¿ The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances or law.” (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1499.)¿Motions for reconsideration are restricted to circumstances where a party offers the Court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier.¿ (Id.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

Moreover, there is a strict requirement of diligence, which means the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence or different facts earlier.¿ (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.)¿ The burden under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at trial.¿ (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213; Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338 [“ ‘[T]he party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.’ ”]; Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441 [“The party seeking reconsideration must provide not just new evidence or different facts, but a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce it at an earlier time.”].)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

New circumstances can be shown by evidence that the court failed to consider a timely-filed memorandum of points and authorities in its prior ruling.¿ (Johnston v. Corrigan (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 553, 556.)¿¿¿¿¿

However, that does not detract from counsel’s declaration and screenshot, evidencing that a technical error occurred on his end.

Defendant presents that the Court did not rule on the wrong motion, highlighting that the Court refers to the first motion for terminating sanctions, the Court’s Minute Order states that the matter called for hearing is the Motion for Sanctions and that it is clear from a complete reading that the motion ruled upon is the Motion for Sanctions. Thus, Defendant believes that this is not enough for a Motion for Reconsideration. Next Defendant argues that Halpern’s microphone issue did not violate Plaintiff’s rights as Plaintiff fails to explain why no other attempts were made to appear at the hearing or no earlier attempts were made to notify the Court of her attempt to appear, emphasizing that each party receives from LACC prior to any remote hearing a back up phone number and the confirmation form advises: “If you have audio or video problems, or do not have access to join by computer or smart phone: Backup conference phone number: (213) 793-4728.” (Decl. John T. Hill ¶ 31, Exh. F). Thus, Defendant presents that Plaintiff had the opportunity to present an Opposition and an argument and counsel’s inability to make the Court aware of his appearance during and/or immediately following the hearing reflect the same disregard which necessitated not one but two Motions to Terminate and the failure rests on Defendant, not the Court.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff argues that her motion did demonstrate new and different facts and circumstances and the Court ruled upon the wrong motion. Plaintiff states that her counsel did not have enough time to call the backup phone line but could only take a screenshot of his computer to prove he was in fact at the hearing but could not be heard. It appears that Plaintiff is attempting to make her Reply a declaration by Halpern as the final page includes the statement, “I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the above is true and correct,” and a signature line signed by Halpern. Plaintiff’s Reply goes further to discuss that she did not abuse discovery, that she is willing to submit to a physical examination, and arguments related to the previously noticed physical examination and her surgery.

 

The Court notes that, though Plaintiff presents that she was not served properly with the Demand for Physical Examination, this appears to be an argument presented for consideration upon the granting of reconsideration. Further, Plaintiff had also presented this argument in her late-filed Opposition to the Motion for Sanctions, which the Court considered, and as such, this contention cannot be a basis for the Motion for Reconsideration as it is not new or different facts, circumstances, or law. (See legal standard, ante.)

 

The Court accepts Plaintiff’s presentation of the technical error as a new circumstance as it led to an oral argument failing to be heard on Plaintiff’s behalf.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Reconsideration is GRANTED.

 

The Court turns to the presented arguments by the parties filed with the Motion for Sanctions. The Court disregards new argument presented in the Motion for Reconsideration, its Opposition, and the Reply that are focused on sanctions and unrelated to reconsideration.

 

Defendant requested terminating or issue/evidentiary sanctions as Plaintiff failed to appear for a physical examination and conducted surgery on parts of her body that were at issue in this action. Defendant presents Plaintiff’s neck is altered and/or destroyed forever and it cannot investigate Plaintiff’s physical condition. (See Mtn. for Sanctions Spoilation of Evidence section.) Because of the aforementioned facts, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has abused the discovery process and destroyed evidence, leading the requested sanctions (i.e., terminating or issue/evidentiary sanctions) to be warranted as there is no remedy available for Defendant. (Id. 8:3-11:3.) Defendant also requests $800.00 in the cancellation fee for the scheduled physical examination. (Id. 11:4-12.)

 

Plaintiff presents that the Demand for Physical Examination was served to Jesse Halpern, not Halpern who was handling the case and, as such, Halpern was not aware of the demand for physical examination until July 31, 2023, when defense counsel’s email confirming the examination was forwarded to Hapern’s secretary. Plaintiff argues that she did not spoil any evidence because the medical procedure was needed to alleviate the intense paid and suffering from the incident and Defendant can still examine Plaintiff after her surgery. Plaintiff further argues that her body is not property and, as such, is not subject to the spoilation argument. Plaintiff next presents that she has a constitutionally protected right to receive medical treatment under England v. La. State Bd. of Med. Examiners (5th Cir. 1958) 259 15 F.2d 626, 627 (“England”). Plaintiff’s next argument concedes that she received a surgical fusion surgery. Plaintiff presents that a cervical fusion is not elective as she had pain and that elective surgeries are cosmetic surgeries.

 

Defendant did not file a Reply as Plaintiff’s Opposition was filed on the day the Reply was due.

 

The Court has discussed the issue with Halpern’s electronic address, ante.

 

A movant seeking discovery sanctions based on the alleged spoliation of evidence proceeds by showing a prima facie case that the respondent destroyed evidence which had a substantial probability to establish an element of the movant’s defense. (Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1226.) The burden then shifts to the respondent to show otherwise. (Ibid.)

 

The terminating sanctions or, in the alternative, issue and/or evidentiary sanctions are based on Plaintiff’s treatment on her neck.

 

Defendant has presented that (1) Plaintiff’s physical condition is at issue in this action, (2) that the surgery has prevented Defendant from being able to investigate Plaintiff’s claims, (3) Plaintiff knew of the physical examination, but continued with the surgery prior to the physical examination, and (4) any evidence related to Plaintiff’s neck which could provide a defense has been altered. The Court believes a prima facie case has been shown that as any evidence of the impact the incident had on Plaintiff’s neck has been altered permanently.

 

The burden then shifts to respondent, Plaintiff, to show otherwise.

 

Plaintiff has conceded that she received a surgical fusion surgery on her neck. Plaintiff’s argument regarding fusion surgeries being non-elective is not well taken. It is common knowledge that elective surgeries may extend to more serious conditions and Plaintiff has not presented any evidence that the surgery was necessary. There has also been no showing that Plaintiff’s procedure could not have waited for two days in order to conduct the physical examination. Plaintiff’s constitutional argument fails as well as there has been no showing that the persuasive authority, England, applies in the context of this action. That is, Plaintiff underwent a surgery on a body part at issue in the action that has not been evidenced to be necessary prior to a physical examination. Plaintiff’s arguments regarding spoilation of evidence does not include any citation to a legal standard or statute. Plaintiff states only that the surgery was to relieve pain and that her body is not subject to spoilation. Thus, Plaintiff has not met her burden.

 

As to monetary sanctions, Defendant seeks the $800.00 cancellation fee due to Plaintiff’s lack of respect for the Civil Discovery Act. In light of the error in service of the Demand for Physical Examination, the Court declines to impose monetary sanctions.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Motion for Sanctions in part as to terminating sanctions, and DENIES the Motion for Sanctions as to Monetary Sanctions.

Defendant Walmart, Inc.’s Motion for Sanctions is GRANTED in part as to terminating sanctions and DENIED in part as to monetary sanctions.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Arbitration – California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-72.) Cal. Code¿Civ.¿Proc.¿§ 1281.2 permits a party to file a motion to request that the Court order the parties to arbitrate a controversy. Under Cal. Code¿Civ.¿Proc.¿section 1281.2, the Court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that:¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or¿¿¿ 

(b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.¿¿¿ 

(c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. For purposes of this section, a pending court action or special proceeding includes an action or proceeding initiated by the party refusing to arbitrate after the petition to compel arbitration has been filed, but on or before the date of the hearing on the petition. This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(Cal. Code¿Civ.¿Proc.¿§ 1281.2.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration.¿(See United Transportation Union v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 804, 808-809; Engineers & Architects Assn. v. Community Development Dept. (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 644, 652; California Correctional Peace Officers Assn. v. State of California (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205; San Francisco Police Officers' Assn. v. San Francisco Police Com. (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 676, 683.)¿The Court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1281.2 [“. . .unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement”].)¿Unless the parties clearly and unmistakably provide otherwise, the question of whether the parties agreed to arbitrate is to be decided by the court, not the arbitrator.¿(Id.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.¿(Id.)¿There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes which the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.¿(Id.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

The party seeking to enforce the arbitration agreement bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence.¿(Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc.¿(2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.)¿The trial court first decides whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties and then¿determine whether the plaintiff’s claims are covered by the agreement.¿(Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co.¿(2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

The party opposing the petition to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.¿(Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc.,¿supra,¿at¿1284.)¿In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.¿(Id.)¿

Discussion

 

Application – Defendant presents that there is an arbitration agreement included with the Retail Installment Sales Contract (“RISC”), titled “Retail Installment Sales Contract–Simple Finance Charge (With Arbitration Provision)” and that the entire action is based upon transactions covered by the Arbitration Agreement. Defendants believe no grounds exist for revocation of the arbitration agreement as it is neither procedurally nor substantively unconscionable. Defendant emphasizes that the matter must be stayed while the Motion to Arbitrate is pending and that Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324 (commonly referred to as Ochoa v. Ford Motor Co.) is not binding as review has been granted.

 

Plaintiffs argue that Defendant does not meet its initial burden to establish that it is a party to the arbitration agreement as Felisilda has been rejected in Ford Motor Warranty Cases; Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958 (“Montemayor”); Kielar v. Superior Court (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614 (“Kielar”); and Yeh v. Superior Court (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 264 (“Yeh”). Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d. 450, 456, to choose between the sides of any such conflict.” (Ochoa v. Ford Motor Co. (In re Ford Motor Warranty Cases) (2023) 2023 Cal. LEXIS 4235/ 2023 WL 4630837.) Defendant reiterates that the arbitration agreement is independently enforceable by Defendant due to equitable estoppel, citing Felisilda and other cases, the arbitration agreement is not unconscionable, and Felisilda is the better reasoned decision and the Court should follow Felisilda and not Ford Motor Warranty Cases.

 

As an initial matter, Defendant cites to various superior court decisions, including tentative decisions, in its Reply brief and attaches them as exhibits. The Court declines to consider these arguments as other superior court decisions are not binding on this Court. (See People v. Sons (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 90, 100 [“Trial court judges are required to follow the United States and California Constitutions, California statutory law, the law as declared by the appellate courts of this state (Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 455 [20 Cal. Rptr. 321, 369 P.2d 937]) as well as any “law of the case” as declared by the appellate court. . . Trial court judges are independent judicial officers. They have both the right and the duty, consistent with their oaths of office, to exercise their best judgment, not to abandon it to previous trial court rulings.”].)

 

The Court addresses the timeliness of this motion as it must determine whether Defendant waived its right to arbitrate.

 

The standard for waiver is as follows:

 

When an arbitration agreement does not specify the time within which arbitration must be demanded, a reasonable time is allowed; a party who does not demand arbitration within a reasonable time is deemed to have waived the right to arbitration. (Sawday v. Vista Irrigation Dist. (1966) 64 Cal.2d 833, 836 [52 Cal.Rptr. 1, 415 P.2d 816] (Sawday); see also Davis v. Blue Cross of Northern California (1979) 25 Cal.3d 418, 425-426 [158 Cal.Rptr. 828, 600 P.2d 1060]; A. D. Hoppe Co. v. Fred Katz Constr. Co. (1967) 249 Cal.App.2d 154, 161-162 [57 Cal.Rptr. 95, 25 A.L.R.3d 1162] (A. D. Hoppe Co.); Meyer v. Carnow, supra, 185 Cal.App.3d at pp. 174-175 & fn. 2.) "[W]hat constitutes a reasonable time is a question of fact, depending on the situation of the parties, the nature of the transaction, and the facts of the particular case.  [Citations.]" (Sawday, supra, 64 Cal.2d at p. 836.) Among the facts a court may consider is any prejudice the opposing party suffered because of the delay. (See A. D. Hoppe Co., supra, 249 Cal.App.2d at pp. 161-162.)

 

(Spear v. California State Auto. Assn. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1035, 1043.)

 

The Court takes judicial notice of its records pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code § 452(d) as it is pertinent to this pending motion. The Court provides the information and its sources, infra, to be made part of the record and this tentative to afford each party reasonable opportunity to meet such information before judicial notice of the matter may be taken. (See Cal. Evid. Code § 455.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint on February 23, 2023. Defendant filed its Answer shortly after on April 06, 2023. The Court conducted a Case Management Conference on July 10, 2023 in which counsels for both parties appeared and scheduled the non-jury trial for May 31, 2024. (See 07/10/2023 Minute Order.) Only on December 18, 2023 did Defendant file this motion. Leading up to this hearing, the an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was noticed and a joint statement was filed by the parties. The Court held an IDC on January 02, 2024; however, the parties stipulated to a continuation. The Join Statement filed by the parties indicates that the discovery process had begun. Defendant indicates that it responded to Plaintiff’s discovery requests and included objections as to preserve its right to compel arbitration, conceding that participating in discovery is seen as a party acting inconsistent with its right to arbitrate. (See Joint IDC Statement 8:5-18.) Despite this, Defendant has served Requests for Productions upon Plaintiffs. (See Decl. James S. Kostas, Exh. D.)

 

The Court highlights the standard that Defendant has presented in the Joint IDC Statement:

 

See e.g., Burton v. Cruise (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 939, 945-946 (noting that a defendant can waive arbitration by for example, acting “inconsistent with the right to arbitrate.”); see also, St. Agnes Med. Ctr. v. PacifiCare of Ca. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187, 1204 (noting that waiver can occur by the party’s participation in the judicial discovery process).

 

(Joint IDC Statement 8:9-12.)

 

Defendant has conceded that a legal standard exists where a party waives its right to arbitration by participating in discovery and, despite this, Defendant has participated in the discovery process.

 

Further Defendant has been aware of the upcoming trial date of May 31, 2024 since July 10, 2023 and did not bring this motion until December 18, 2023. 

 

Accordingly, based on the facts of this particular case, Defendant has waived its right to arbitration.

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

Defendant’s presentation of what the FAC says and what the FAC actually says are different. The FAC reads, in relevant part:

 

Defendant is aware or should have been aware as a property owner, or landlord, of its obligation to provide relocation assistance to Plaintiff in accordance with the Los Angeles County Rent Stabilization Ordinance, but instead of providing any relocation assistance, Defendant intentionally or with conscious disregard, disregarded the following pertaining to Plaintiff:

 

• What lead to the Defendant giving Plaintiff the 30 day-notice was due to a fire that essentially made the living unit uninhabitable. . .

 

[. . .]

 

Immediately after the fire, Defendant informed Plaintiff in writing that she would be locked out of her apartment and was prevented from entering her unit because of the Defendants insurance underwriting reasons several times. At that time, Plaintiff was told by employees of Palmdale City and the Gas company that the apartment had not been tagged as "uninhabitable" and they informed Plaintiff that she had a legal right to allow city inspectors into her apartment.

 

(FAC ¶¶ 12-13 [emphasis added].)

 

The FAC concedes that (1) a fire occurred; (2) that the fire damaged the Property making it “essentially” uninhabitable; and (3) it was not red tagged as uninhabitable by the City of Palmdale and the gas company. Thus, there is an issue of whether Plaintiff should have been allowed access to the Property.

 

Cal. Civ. Code § 789.3 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

(a) A landlord shall not with intent to terminate the occupancy under any lease or other tenancy or estate at will, however created, of property used by a tenant as his residence willfully cause, directly or indirectly, the interruption or termination of any utility service furnished the tenant, including, but not limited to, water, heat, light, electricity, gas, telephone, elevator, or refrigeration, whether or not the utility service is under the control of the landlord.

 

(b) In addition, a landlord shall not, with intent to terminate the occupancy under any lease or other tenancy or estate at will, however created, of property used by a tenant as his or her residence, willfully:

 

(1) Prevent the tenant from gaining reasonable access to the property by changing the locks or using a bootlock or by any other similar method or device;

 

(2) Remove outside doors or windows; or

 

(3) Remove from the premises the tenant's personal property, the furnishings, or any other items without the prior written consent of the tenant, except when done pursuant to the procedure set forth in Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 1980) of Title 5 of Part 4 of Division 3.

 

(Cal. Civ. Code § 789.3.)

 

Plaintiff has clearly alleged that Defendant locked her out. (See FAC ¶ 20 [“. . .Defendant intentionally ignored Plaintiff’s right and locked her out and denied her reasonable access to her unit. . .”].)

 

Plaintiff has adequately alleged a violation of Cal. Civ. Code § 789.3.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the First Cause of Action (Illegal Lockout) is OVERRULED.

 

b.     Second Cause of Action (Trespass)

 

Defendant presents that trespass requires five elements. However, landlords are entitled to enter the premises in cases of emergency without advance notice, citing to Civil Code § 1954 (a)(1) and (e)(1), and Miller & Starr, 10 Cal. Real Est. § 34:57 (4th ed.). Defendant presents that the FAC lacks sufficient evidence to show intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property and lack of permission for entry as the FAC is vague as to when the entry occurred and, if entry occurred after the fire, it cannot be deemed intentional, reckless, or negligent.

 

Plaintiff argues that an inference can be made that there was no emergency as, if there was an emergency, the 30-Day Notice would serve no purpose. That is, Defendant never treated this as an emergency and since it was not treated as an emergency, Defendant was required to five 24-hour notice before entering the unit. Plaintiff further argues that Defendant cannot self-certify the circumstances as an emergency.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not address Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action (Trespass) is uncertain and that Plaintiff fails to plead facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for trespass, citing that the Property was uninhabitable as alleged in paragraph 12 of the FAC.

 

“ ‘Trespass is an unlawful interference with possession of property.’ The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant’s intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm. (See CACI No. 2000.)” (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 261-62.)

 

As discussed, ante, there is an issue of whether Plaintiff should have had access to the property as it was not red tagged as uninhabitable by the City of Palmdale and the gas company. (See FAC ¶ 13.) Further, from the Complaint, a reasonable inference can be made that the alleged entry and lockout occurred after the fire. The use of a 30-Day Notice, as alleged, supports Plaintiff’s position that the emergency had passed and Defendant does not deny entry onto the Property. However, the Court believes that whether the timing of the entry constituted an emergency is outside the scope of this Demurrer and is better suited for a different motion.

 

Plaintiff has pled: (1) Plaintiff leased/had control of the Property (FAC ¶ 5); (2) Defendant entered the Property (id. at ¶¶ 9 25); (3) Plaintiff did not give Defendant permission to enter the property/no proper notice was given (ibid.); (4) Plaintiff was harmed as she currently has no access to the Property (id. ¶ 25). Thus, the elements for trespass have been met.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action (Trespass) is OVERRULED.

As to whether the allegations are sufficient, to succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.) Civil Code section 3294, subsection (a) authorizes punitive damages “where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civil Code §3294(a).) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth. In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.” (Clauson v. Superior Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) 

 

Cal. Civ. Code § 3924(c) provides the following definitions: 

 

·        “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. 

·        “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. 

·        “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. 

 

Further willful misconduct may be inferred from an actor’s conduct: 

 

Three essential elements must be present to raise a negligent act to the level of wilful [sic] misconduct: (1) actual or constructive knowledge of the peril to be apprehended, (2) actual or constructive knowledge that injury is a probable, as opposed to a possible, result of the danger, and (3) conscious failure to act to avoid the peril. 

 

[. . .] 

 

If conduct is sufficiently lacking in consideration for the rights of others, reckless, heedless to an extreme, and indifferent to the consequences it may impose, then, regardless of the actual state of mind of the actor and his actual concern for the rights of others, we call it wilful [sic] misconduct, and apply to it the consequences and legal rules which we use in the field of intended torts. 

 

(New v. Consolidated Rock Products Co. (1985) 171 Cal.App.3d 681, 689-90 (“New”).) 

 

The Court notes that it has addressed the emergency argument, ante, and directs the parties to the discussion on page seven.

 

Plaintiff has alleged (1) abuse from Defendant’s employee Josue Garcia (FAC ¶ 14); (2) that she had to cancel medical appointments and undergo a surgery without medications and medical records (id. at ¶ 15); (3) that she was unable to meet with autoimmune specialists as she did not have her work up records and has become very sick (id. at ¶ 16); and (4) that she lacks access to the Property (see id. [generally]).

 

As Defendant correctly points out, the allegation of abuse is vague and conclusory. However, the Court need not take action as there has been no demurrer or request to strike as to this allegation. 

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike has not addressed various portions of the FAC which discuss malice and conscious disregard, including the Fourth Cause of Action (Negligence) and the Fifth Cause of Action (Unfair Business Practice Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.).

 

The Court cannot strike punitive damages as no argument is given to relevant portions of the FAC which allege malice.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. a. First Cause of Action (Violation of Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5)

 

Defendants present that Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5 requires a servicer to attempt to contact a

borrower, to assess their financial situation and explore options to avoid foreclosure, at least 30 days before a notice of default is recorded, and, if the servicer is unable to with the borrower, it may satisfy the outreach requirements by sending and making three telephone calls. Defendants argue that the Complaint only alleges that Plaintiffs did not receive letters, but this does not show that a violation occurred as the statutory requirement is to send out letters, not confirm that the letters are received. Defendants present that the factual allegations for this cause of action were not changed from the Complaint and the deficiencies remain. Defendants further argue that (1) Plaintiffs contradict themselves as they allege that they submitted loan modifications and were denied at least once and so the First Cause of Action (Violation of Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5 is disingenuous; (2) Plaintiff has not shown that the violation is material; and (3) there are no allegations that Plaintiff changed their actions due to the purported Homeowner’s Bill of Rights (“HBOR”) violations or that, absent the alleged violations, the Property would not have gone into foreclosure.

 

Plaintiffs emphasize that HBOR requirements mandate that a servicer may not record a notice of default until 30 days after contacting the borrower to discuss alternatives to foreclosure and that, here, Plaintiff was available by phone and mail and never received either a phone call from Rushmore or any written notifications and, despite this, Rushmore recording a Notice of Default and notice of Sale on December 02, 2022.

 

Defendants argue, in reply, that the claim under Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5 fails due to the wording of the FAC which was not changed from the Complaint. Defendants reiterate that (1) alleging a violation under Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5 with the basis that they were never notified of options to explore avoiding for closure is disingenuous as Plaintiffs have admitted that they applied numerous times for a loan modification and were denied at least once; (2) Plaintiff has not alleged that the statutory violation was material; and (3) there are no allegations that Plaintiffs changed their actions due to the purported HBOR violations or that, absent the alleged violations, the Property would not have gone into foreclosure.

 

The Court first addresses the argument regarding conflicting allegations as it has the potential to affect the FAC in its entirety. Plaintiff provides no argument. The Court notes that the allegations at issue were present in the Complaint. (Compare Complaint ¶ 20 and First Cause of Action to FAC ¶ 20 and First Cause of Action.) However, this argument was not raised previously and, as such, was unaddressed until this Demurrer. The allegation at issue reads:

 

PLAINTIFFS submitted loan modifications over and over again, only to be told that they were missing documents every time. This occurred over several years. During the time that New Rez, LLC, Select Portfolio Services, Inc. and RUSHMORE, PLAINTIFFS were only denied at least once. The file was closed out several times, and as he recalls it was during the transfer period when PLAINTIFFS were not notified until they got mortgage statements from the new servicer.

 

(FAC ¶ 20.)

 

The Court believes this is contradictory to the allegations in the FAC that allege that (1) Plaintiffs were never contacted, and (2) Plaintiffs were not offered foreclosure alternatives.

 

It is undisputed that the code requirement is to state the facts, not theories:  a statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 426; Manti v. Gunari (1970) 5 Cal. App. 3d 442, 449-50 (“Manti”).) While inconsistent theories of recovery are permitted, a pleader cannot blow hot and cold as to the facts positively stated, regardless of whether the pleading is verified. (Manti, supra, 5 Cal. App. 3d 442, 449, 450.) In reference to the antagonistic allegations in the single cause of action the court is required to take as true the averments in the complaint which bear most strongly against the pleader. (Manti, supra, 5 Cal. App. 3d at 450.) As paragraph 20 bears most strongly against the pleader, the Court must take it as true. Thus, this appears to be an issue on the face of the pleadings.

 

With this addressed, the Court need not delve into the remaining arguments as Plaintiff has conceded that they did attempt loan modifications with Rushmore, alleged to be the current loan servicer, and were denied at least once. (See FAC ¶¶ 2, 20.)

Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿¿¿ 

 

Here, the issue with the causes of actions in Plaintiff’s FAC is due to the contradictory allegation in paragraph 20. Rectification would mean an omission of the allegation, which would go against the sham pleading doctrine. (See ante.) Accordingly, an amendment would not rectify the deficiencies within the FAC.

Background

 

This is a defamation action. Plaintiffs Craig Van Dam (“Craig”)[1]; Marta Van Dam (“Marta”); and Craig Van Dam Farms, LLC (“Van Dam Farms” and collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that on October 03, 2023, Defendant Lucy Faddoul (“Defendant”) published a comment on Facebook wherein she made a number of false statements and injurious allegations, including:

 

·       “To all of my Antelope Valley followers if you have anything to do with the Vandam [sic] family, I would be extremely careful…Evil, evil, evil who hide behind the word of God;”

·       “They make fun of special needs adults calling them retards…;”

Plaintiffs present that Defendant has a social media presence due to her status in the community and her time as a reality television actress. Plaintiffs further allege that Defendants’ post acts as a deterrent to retain current customers and to attract prospective customers, thereby causing damages in the form of lost consumers.

 

On October 10, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint alleging five causes of action for: (1) Defamation Per Se; (2) Defamation; (3) Trade Libel; (4) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”); and (4) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (“NIED”).

 

On December 04, 2023, Defendant filed this Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike.

On January 09, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition.

 

On January 12, 2024 Defendant filed her Reply, including objections.

 

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed amended declarations and Defendant filed objections to the amended declarations.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Special Motion to Strike under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 – Under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16, a cause of action asserted against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of his/her right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution, in connection with a public issue, is subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(1); See also Wilbanks v. Wolk (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 883, 892.) 

 

In determining whether to grant or deny a special motion to strike, the Court engages in a two-step process.¿ (See, e.g.,¿Shekhter¿v. Fin.¿Indemn. Co.¿(2001), 89 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.)¿ This process is essentially a “summary-judgment-like procedure at any early stage of the litigation.” (Varian Med. Sys., Inc. v.¿Delfino¿(2005), 35 Cal.4th 180, 192.)¿ “First, the court must determine whether defendant has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action ‘arises from’ a protected activity.¿ Second, if the defendant makes this showing, the trial court must determine whether the plaintiff has established a probability of prevailing on the claim.”¿ (Optional Capital, Inc. v. Das Corp.¿(2014), 222 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1398.)¿¿¿ 

 

The motion requires the moving party to bear the initial burden of establishing a prima facie showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s free speech or petition activity. (Wilbanks, supra, 121 Cal.App.4th at 894.) The defendant may meet this burden by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff's cause of action was a written or oral statement made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding; or such a statement in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body; or such a statement was made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest.  (Wilcox v. Superior Court (1994) 27 Cal. App. 4th 809, 820 [overruled in part as to showing of intent of party that filed the complaint].) 

 

Once a defendant meets their burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to establish a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim, i.e., make a prima facie showing of facts which would, if proved at trial, support a judgment in plaintiff’s favor. (Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1497-78.) Specifically, the plaintiff carries their shifting burden by making an evidentiary showing like that required under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c. (Id.; See also Ludwig v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 8, 15.) 

 

If the special motion to strike is granted, the court must enter judgment for the defendant.¿ (DuPont Merck Pharmaceutical Co. v. Superior Court¿(2000), 78 Cal.App.4th 562, 564.)¿ No leave to amend may be given.¿ (Schaffer v. City and County of San Francisco¿(2008), 168 Cal.App.4th 992, 1005.)¿

Discussion

 

Application – The post at issue reads:

 

Dealing with things gracefully....no more!!!

 

To all of my Antelope Valley followers if you have anything to do with the Vandam family, I would be EXTREMELY careful...It's one thing to go through a divorce but the amount of lies one family can make up....it's unbelievable. Evil, evil, evil who hide behind the word of God.

 

They make fun of special needs adults calling them retards, yelling are you going to **** your brother to have another retarded child, and will do things to break up marriages and relationships. A family of 7 kids who are adults, that live solely off their "godly" father, thriving on harassment, lies, gossip, and drama.

 

I would not want ANYONE else to experience what my family has. Enough is enough!

 

(Complaint, Exh. A.)

 

Defendant presents that the post is protected under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e), highlighting the following enumerated examples from the statute: (1) Any written or oral statement or writing made before a judicial proceeding or any other official proceeding authorized by law; (2) Any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a judicial body or any other official proceeding authorized by law; and (3) Any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.

 

Defendant first argues that litigation privilege (i.e., enumerated list numbers (1) and (2)) applies as the post stems from what happened in court during a divorce and custody proceeding between Lacey Van Dam, Craig and Marta’s daughter, and Levi Faddoul, Defendant’s brother.

 

Defendant provides the following cases:

 

·       Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1 – Homeowner’s association Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (“Tuscany Hills”) initiated suit against property owner Gloria Healy (“Healy”) alleging that she denied Tuscany Hills access through her property to an adjacent slope over which Tuscany Hills had maintenance obligations. Subsequently, Tuscany Hills sent a letter to its residents which stated that Healy’s prohibition of ingress and egress through her property resulted in increased costs to members of Tuscany Hills. Healy filed a cross-complaint due to the letter and Tuscany Hills brought an Anti-SLAPP motion to strike. The appellate court found that the letter was unquestionably made in connection with judicial proceedings and bears some relation to judicial proceedings as it (1) expressly referred to the litigation arising from Healy’s prohibition on ingress and egress for weed abatement purposes and refers to an enclosed disclosure letter and (2) one purpose of the letter was to inform members of Tuscany Hills of pending litigation involving the association.

·       Anette F. v. Sharon S. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1146 – Annette and Sharon got married and the wedding had public coverage in mainstream media. Later, they published a book that was quoted in discussions on the internet; they appeared on television with Diane Sawyer on a broadcast on gay and lesbian marriage as one of the four featured couples; the Turning Point covered Annette and Sharon’s wedding and their son’s birth which was featured in national news media; and Annette filed a motion to adopt their second child which received widespread coverage on the media and the internet. During the litigation Sharon wrote a letter to the board of directors of The Center, a gay and lesbian organization in San Diego and sent the letter to the Gay and Lesbian Times of San Diego who had covered a story about the decision in the family court litigation. The letter expressed Sharon’s belief that a forum The Center had held on the court’s decision had been one-sided and explained why Sharon agreed with the court’s initial ruling. It included the following sentence: “Annette, a convicted perpetrator of domestic violence against me, has made repeated false accusations of child abuse and neglect against me while actively litigating for sole custody of both children, ages 5 and 2.” Annette filed a libel action against Sharon. The appellate court held that the statements (1) were made in connection with an issue under review by a judicial body as Sharon’s allegations of domestic violence against Annette were directly at issue in the underlying proceedings because Sharon claimed that her consent to their second child’s adoption had been obtained by fraud or undue influence arising from Annette's acts of domestic violence against her and Annette’s allegations of abuse and neglect were related to competing claims as to the second child’s best interest; (2) were made in a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest as a news publication is a public forum within the meaning of the Anti-SLAPP statute and the letter affected a large number of children and adoptive parents beyond direct participants; and (3) Sharon’s statements constitute conduct in furtherance of the exercise of free speech in connection with an issue of public interest as the litigation received widespread public attention because of its potential impact on those who had adopted children through second-parent adoptions, particularly in the gay and lesbian community.

·       Zucchet v Galardi (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1466 – Galardi owned several strip clubs in Las Vegas and one in San Diego and hired lobbyist to try to repeal a “no-touch” ordinance in San Diego that affected interactions between exotic dancers and patrons in strip clubs. The lobbyist’s activities included meeting and making campaign contributions, including money that was traceable to Galardi, to members of the San Diego City Council, including Zucchet, who was elected. A breakfast meeting occurred between the lobbyist and Zucchet where the lobbyist discussed how he had contributed money during Zucchet’s campaign and Zucchet agreed to “do the lifting at the committee level” of a plan that the lobbyist had formulated as to how to get the repeal of the no-touch ordinance on the city council agenda. Concerned citizens appeared before city council to request an expansion of the distance requirements between adult businesses and referral of Zucchet’s participation in the lobbyist’s plans. As a result, a grand jury indictment was filed naming Zucchet and Galardi. Galardi entered a plea deal and a superseding indictment was filed against Zucchet and the lobbyist. Subsequently, Zucchet filed suit against Galardi for malicious prosecution, alleging that Galardi falsely told the United States Attorney’s Office in January 2005 that he had given $10,000 in cash to Malone to bribe three city council members, including Zucchet, and that Galardi falsely testified to that cash bribery during the criminal trial against Zucchet. Galardi then filed an anti-SLAPP motion to strike. The appellate court held that Galardi’s actions were activities protected by the Anti-SLAPP statute as his testimony was an oral statements made before a judicial proceeding and his statement to federal prosecutors was an oral statement made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body. The appellate court made notations that the record contained no evidence that Galardi made false statements and Zucchet conceded that he engaged in illegal activity.

·       Haight Ashbury Free Clinics, Inc. v Happening House Ventures (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1539 – Several suits underly this action: (1) a partnership case where Haight Ashbury Free Clinics, Inc. (“HAFCI”) filed suit against Happening House Ventures (“HHV”), David E. Smith (“Smith”), and HHV’s administrator; and (2) a lease case where HHV filed suit against HAFCI for breach of its lease. HAFCI filed this lawsuit against HHV and Smith in which HAFCI alleged that smith breached his fiduciary duties. HHV filed an anti-SLAPP motion to strike based on allegations made in paragraphs 31(o) and 31(p) of the pleadings which alleged false testimony in the partnership case and misrepresenting facts in a letter to the San Francisco Bay Guardian. The appellate court found that the acts alleged in paragraphs 31(o) and 31(p) were protected under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e), enumerated numbers (1) [any written or oral statement or writing made before a … judicial proceeding … ], (2) [any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law], (3) [any written or oral statement or writing made in … a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest] and (4) [or any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest].

 

Defendant’s cases are substantially different from the action at hand. Whereas the cases cited concern speech directly related to actions such as a letter informing homeowner association members of a lawsuit and its effect, false testimony, or commentary of a court’s ruling, the act at hand does not. Rather, the post in this action does not include any discussion of a case or any statement made in connection to a judicial body. The post alludes to a divorce but focuses the Van Dam family’s conduct as a whole. While it may have stemmed from a judicial proceeding, the post was not made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings by litigants or other participants authorized by law such as the testimony discussed in Zucchet v Galardi (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1466 and Haight Ashbury Free Clinics, Inc. v Happening House Ventures (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1539. There has been no showing that the post was made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a judicial body or any other official proceeding authorized by law (i.e., such as in Anette F. v. Sharon S. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1146 where the commentary affected influence on a child and custody). The post is also differentiated from the letter in Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1 as it does not expressly refer to a court proceeding and is not meant to inform anyone of litigation that may affect their rights. As such, Defendant does not show that litigation applies nor does the Court believe, based on Defendant’s arguments, that litigation privilege applies.

 

The Court turns next to the argument that the post is conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.

 

Defendant presents her post “involve[s] the public interest in the integrity of divorce and/or custody proceedings and to be able to attend court proceedings or testify as a witness free of intimidation.”

 

Hilton v. Hallmark Cards (9th Cir. 2010) 599 F.3d 894 (“Hilton”), summarizing California law, provides the following tests for determining whether a matter involves the public interest:

 

The California intermediate appellate courts have developed multiple tests to determine whether a defendant's activity is in connection with a public issue. One commonly cited test comes from Rivero v. American Federation of State, County, & Municipal Employees, 105 Cal. App. 4th 913, 130 Cal. Rptr. 2d 81, 89-90 (Ct. App. 2003) (concluding that the plaintiff's supervision of eight individuals was not a matter of public interest, where people involved had "received no public attention or media coverage"). There, the Court of Appeal for the First District surveyed the appellate cases and divined from them three categories of public issues: (1) statements "concern[ing] a person or entity in the public eye"; (2) "conduct that could directly affect a large number of people beyond the direct participants"; (3) "or a topic of widespread, public interest." Id. at 89. The Fourth District has followed this approach. See, e.g., Commonwealth Energy Corp., 1 Cal. Rptr. 3d at 394-95 (describing Rivero as the first systematic treatment of the "public issue-public interest aspect of the anti-SLAPP statute"). 

 

By contrast, Weinberg v. Feisel, a case from the Third District, articulated a somewhat more restrictive test, designed to distinguish between issues of "public, rather than merely private, interest." 110 Cal. App. 4th 1122, 2 Cal. Rptr. 3d 385, 392 (Ct. App. 2003). 

 

First, "public interest" does not equate with mere curiosity. Second, a matter of public interest should be something of concern to a substantial number of people. Thus, a matter of concern to the speaker and a relatively small, specific audience is not a matter of public interest. Third, there should be some degree of closeness between the challenged statements and the asserted public interest; the assertion of a broad and amorphous public interest is not sufficient. Fourth, the focus of the speaker's conduct should be the public interest rather than a mere effort to gather ammunition for another round of private controversy. Finally, . . . [a] person cannot turn otherwise private information into a matter of public interest simply by communicating it to a large number of people. 

 

Id. at 392-93 (internal quotation marks, citations, and alteration omitted). 

 

(Hilton, supra, 599 F.3d 894, 906-07.)

 

The action at hand fails to satisfy both tests. Defendant fails to show that her post concerns a person or entity in the public eye nor a topic of widespread, public interest. Further, under both tests, the matter needs to concern either “large number of people beyond the direct participants” or a “substantial number of people.” (Ibid.) The post appears to concern the parties directly and Defendant has made no showing or argument that that a large number of people have been affected. Defendant has also made no showing that her either her post or the divorce proceeding are matters that the public would take an interest in. (See Nygard, Inc. v. Uusi-Kerttula (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 1027.)

 

Defendant has failed to meet her initial burden of establishing a prima facie showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s free speech or petition activity. (See Legal Standard, ante.) As such, the burden does not shift and the Court need not move to the second-prong of the two-step process of determining whether to grant or deny a special motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

Reply

 

Defendant’s Reply reiterates that the post was made as a commentary on statements made by Plaintiff’s during her brother’s divorce proceeding and is protected because of this. Defendant presents that Plaintiffs’ cited case, Paul v. Friedman (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 853 (“Paul”), supports her argument. Defendant believes that she has met the first prong of the two-step process. Defendant highlights that the Opposition should be disregarded as there are issues surrounding the filed declarations.

 

Paul is inapposite as it focuses on a determination whether the alleged acts in Paul’s complaint, acts related to a securities arbitration proceeding, was encompassed by the anti-SLAPP statute. (See Paul, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th 835 [generally].) As to Defendant’s statement that Paul “reiterates that speech is protected if undertaken in connection with a judicial proceeding, as here,” Paul reiterates the relevant statute[2] by citing: “Subdivision (e) reads in full: ‘As used in this section, “act in furtherance of a person's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest; (4) or any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.’ (§ 425.16, subd. (e).)” (Id. at 863 fn. 17.) The Court has discussed the application of the current statute and relevant case law to this action, ante.

 

As such, the Court’s opinion remains unchanged.

Attorney’s Fees and Costs

 

Defendant requests attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $7,500.00 [retainer amount] under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(c).

 

Plaintiffs oppose as they believe that this motion is without merit and directs the Court and the parties to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(c)(1), presenting that they are entitled to attorney’s fees.

 

Defendant presents that Plaintiffs are not entitled to attorney’s fees as the only way attorney’s fees would be provided to Plaintiffs is if the motion was found to be frivolous. Defendant argues that whether an anti-SLAPP motion is “totally and completely without merit” requires a finding that “ ‘any reasonable attorney would agree such motion is totally devoid of merit.’ ” (Reply 5:1-12.) Defendant further presents that Plaintiffs’ counsel’s declaration is also defective as the declaration fails to state it is based on her personal knowledge or affirmatively states that she is competent to testify.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(c) provides:

 

(1) Except as provided in paragraph (2), in any action subject to subdivision (b), a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover that defendant’s attorney’s fees and costs. If the court finds that a special motion to strike is frivolous or is solely intended to cause unnecessary delay, the court shall award costs and reasonable attorney’s fees to a plaintiff prevailing on the motion, pursuant to Section 128.5.

(2) A defendant who prevails on a special motion to strike in an action subject to paragraph (1) shall not be entitled to attorney’s fees and costs if that cause of action is brought pursuant to Section 11130, 11130.3, 54960, or 54960.1 of the Government Code, or pursuant to Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 7923.100) of Part 4 of Division 10 of Title 1 of the Government Code. Nothing in this paragraph shall be construed to prevent a prevailing defendant from recovering attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Section 7923.115, 11130.5, or 54960.5 of the Government Code.

 

While Defendant may have not succeeded on her Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike, there has been no showing that this motion was frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. Further, defense counsel may have been acting in what he believed was the best interests of his client, Defendant.

 

The Court declines to find the anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay and, as such, does not award costs and attorney’s fees.
