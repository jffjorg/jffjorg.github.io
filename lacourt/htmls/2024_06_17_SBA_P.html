DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00594    Hearing Date: June 17, 2024    Dept: P

MOTION: Demurrer

Tentative Rulings: Cross-Defendant’s Demurrer is overruled

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Robert Bass (“Bass”) and Jeannette Buchanan aka Jeannette Bass (“Buchanan”) (“Plaintiffs”) commenced this action on August 12, 2021. The operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”), filed on June 22, 2023, alleges causes of action for: (1) Breach of Oral Contract; and (2) Fraudulent Misrepresentation against defendant Tim Roth (“Roth”) and DOES. Plaintiffs allege that Roth breached an oral settlement agreement the parties entered into with regard to Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. YC072026 (the “Original Complaint”) (the “Settlement”). Plaintiffs allege that, in reliance on Roth’s promise to pay them $115,000 pursuant to the Settlement, Plaintiffs submitted a Request for Dismissal of the Original Complaint with the court on January 3, 2018.

On February 20, 2024, Roth filed his First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) against Plaintiffs and ROES alleging causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud; (3) Promissory Fraud; (4) Indemnity; (5) Breach of Contract; and (6) Promissory Fraud. The fourth COA is asserted against Buchanan only. The fifth and sixth COA are asserted against Bass only. The allegations common to all COAs are as follows:

Bass and Roth were childhood friends. (FACC ¶ 5) In 2000, Bass started renting space from Roth for his business operations. (Id., ¶ 6.) Roth is a licensed real estate broker and general contractor. (Id., ¶ 15.) Roth met Buchanan after her marriage with Bass in 1993. (Id., ¶ 7.) In 2006, Bass and Buchanan got divorced. (Id., ¶ 8.) In 2007, Buchanan purchased real property located at 2410 Grant Avenue, Hermosa Beach, CA (the “Property”) for the total price of $800,000. (Id., ¶ 9; Exhibit A.) Buchanan borrowed about $640,000 for the purchase (the “Loan”), leaving $160,000 to be placed into escrow from other sources. (Ibid.) No real estate agents were involved in this transaction. (Ibid.) The Loan had a variable rate of interest which began in excess of 7.5% per annum with payments to be applied solely against interest for 10

years. (Id., ¶ 10; Exhibit B.) Buchanan granted Bass, now her ex-husband, some form of ownership in the Property which was not recorded. (Id., ¶ 11.)

When the real estate market crashed in 2008, Plaintiffs pleaded with Roth to help them avoid foreclosure. (Id., ¶ 15.) In March 2010, the parties entered into a purchase agreement, whereby Roth agreed to purchase 99% of the title to the Property, subject to the loans relating to the Property, for $50,000 (the “Purchase Agreement”). (Id., ¶¶17-19; Exhibits C and F.) While Bass never had any ownership of record and was already divorced from Buchanan prior to her purchase of the Property in her own name, Plaintiffs executed a Grant Deed purporting to convey Bass’ interest in the Property to Buchanan to induce Roth to enter the Purchase Agreement. (Id., ¶ 20; Exhibit D.) In 2016, the Property was sold without losses to Plaintiffs or damage to Buchanan’s credit score. (Id., ¶ 23.) Roth was not aware of the Original Complaint (filed in 2017 and voluntarily dismissed in 2018) or any purported settlement agreement between him and Plaintiffs until after this action was commenced. (Id., ¶ 24.)

Plaintiffs now demur to the FACC.

II. MEET AND CONFER

Plaintiffs’ counsel’s two-paragraph declaration states that the parties met and conferred but were unable to reach an agreement. (See Ruhle Decl.) Roth’s counsel also maintains that the parties met and conferred, but there is no discussion or evidence of the substance of the parties’ efforts to meet and confer. Roth’s counsel also states that he encouraged Plaintiffs to file a declaration triggering an automatic 30-day extension of the time to respond to the FACC. (Johnson Decl., ¶ 3.)

Nevertheless, Plaintiffs’ demurrer was untimely filed, even under the extended deadline. Roth moves the Court to strike the demurrer based on untimeliness. The court exercises its discretion to consider the demurrer. (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)

III. MOVING PARTY’S GROUNDS

Plaintiffs demur to all six COAs for uncertainty and insufficient facts (CCP § 430.10(e) and (f).)

IV. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court may take judicial notice of “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States” pursuant to Evid. Code § 452(d). The Court may take judicial notice of the existence of the records, but not the truth of matters asserted in such records. (Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565).

Roth requests judicial notice of various filings and rulings in this action and Case no. YC072026. Plaintiffs request judicial notice of the Court’s May 16, 2024 Minute Order re: Status Conference in this action.

The parties’ requests for judicial notice are GRANTED as to the existence of the records, but not the truth of the matters contained in the records.

V. ANALYSIS

A. Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

1st and 5th COAs: Breach of Contract

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are: (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to plaintiff.” (Coles v. Glaser (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 384, 391(internal quotations omitted).) An action for breach of a written contract must be commenced within 4 years of the breach of that contract. (CCP § 337(a).)

Plaintiffs contend that the FACC fails to allege the third element of breach. The FACC alleges that Plaintiffs breached the Purchase Agreement “by demanding Roth pay to Cross-Defendants sums that could not be owing to them in relation to the Property or for any other reason as a consequence of which Roth . . . has been deprived of the benefit of funds previously expended to fulfill his obligations under the agreements”. (FACC ¶ 29.) Plaintiffs argue that this is insufficient because Plaintiffs’ demand, which Roth alleges constitutes a breach of the Purchase Agreement, arises from Roth’s alleged breach of the oral agreement to settle the Original Complaint, not from the agreement to purchase the Property. However, the Purchase Agreement and the Settlement are inextricable from one another. Plaintiffs themselves admitted that the Original Complaint “arose from a real estate investment in a property located at 2410 Grant Avenue”, “in furtherance of” which Plaintiffs “provided a capital contribution to Roth . . . pursuant to an oral agreement.” (TAC at lines 18-24.) The purported Settlement arises from the Purchase Agreement. Roth properly pled each element of a claim for breach of contract.

Regardless, the cross-complaint alleges a breach of written contract to purchase property; it need not and does not cite the factual allegations in plaintiff’s pleading.

Plaintiffs also contend that the claim is time-barred because the Agreement was entered into in 2010. This argument fails because the limitations period only begins to run with Plaintiffs’ discovery of an alleged breach, not when a contract is formed.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ demurrer to the 1st and 5th COAs is OVERRULED.

2nd, 3rd, and 6th COAs: Fraud and Promissory Fraud

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

Plaintiffs argue that the FACC fails to state how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered to comply with the heightened pleading standards of a fraud cause of action. Plaintiffs did not specifically identify any missing links in the FACC. The Court finds that the FACC sufficiently alleged a cause of action for fraud against both plaintiffs. Roth specifically identified misrepresentations made by Bass (FACC ¶ 32), misrepresentations made by Buchanan (FACC ¶ 33), and information that both plaintiffs hid from him (FACC ¶ 34). Roth believed and relied on those misrepresentations and omissions. (FACC ¶ 35.) The misrepresentations were false and the omissions were material. (FACC ¶ 36.) Roth was injured as a consequence. (FACC ¶ 38.)

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ demurrer to the 2nd, 3rd, and 6th COAs is OVERRULED.

4th COA: Indemnity

“To state a claim for equitable indemnity, a defendant must allege the same harm for which he may be held liable is properly attributable—at least in part—to the cross-defendant.” (Platt v. Coldwell Banker Residential Real Estate Services (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 1439, 1445, fn. 7.)

The FACC alleges indemnity against Buchanan and ROES only. Bass seeks damages against Roth in the TAC. The FACC alleges that if Roth is held liable to Bass on the TAC, such liability is not the result of any wrongdoing on the part of Roth, but solely on the acts and omissions of Buchanan by reason of her failure to provide all material facts to Roth and her

failure to honor the promises and representations set forth in the general allegations and other undisclosed agreements. (FACC ¶ 47.)

Plaintiffs demur to this cause of action on the grounds that Roth’s other causes of action in the FACC are invalid. As discussed above, Roth sufficiently pled all of the other causes of action. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ demurrer to the 4th COA must be OVERRULED.

VI. CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.


Case Number: 24TRCV00674    Hearing Date: June 17, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Strike

The court considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

RULING

Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED with 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff Client Dennis filed a complaint against Defendant El Dorado LF, LLC, alleging causes of action for (1) assault; (2) battery; (3) negligence; (4) negligent hiring, supervision and training; (5) Violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act; and (6) premises liability.

The complaint alleges that on June 7, 2023, Plaintiff Dennis, along with his spouse were on the premises at the Hustler Casino located at 1000 W Redondo Beach Boulevard, in the city of Gardena. (Complaint ¶ 11.)

Plaintiff alleges that a dispute arose between another patron and Plaintiff, and escalated when the other patron made gestures with his hands indicating he was going to shoot Plaintiff.

(Complaint ¶ 12.) The complaint further alleges that Hustler Casino’s security guards proceeded to confront Plaintiff about the incident but not the patron who caused the initial disruption, showing clear preference to the other patron, who was allowed to leave the Premises. (Id.)

Thereafter, Hustler Casino’s security refused both requests and called over at least three large security guards who circled Plaintiff, and swiftly moved in without warning and proceeded to forcefully handcuff him, escorted him to a separate room, falsely imprisoned him, and left him sitting in an uncomfortable handcuffed position for approximately 20 minutes. (Id.) Plaintiff was forced to sit in a contorted position that he was unable to correct on his own because of his current and prior injuries. (Id.) When he vocalized his pain and pleaded with security for assistance, they ignored his pleas for help. (Id.)

On March 22, 2024, Defendant El Dorado LF, LLC filed a motion to strike the demand for punitive damages. On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On June 10, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(1).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

DISCUSSION

i. Meet and Confer Requirements

Defendant submits the Declaration of Greg Ryan, which adequately shows Counsel met and conferred prior to bringing this motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5.)

ii. Merits

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.) The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Id.) A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.)

“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.)

To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.) Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.” (Id. at 90). Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that Defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.)

“An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages ..., based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification, or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civil Code § 3294.) The California Supreme Court interpreted the “latter statement as requiring the officer, director, or managing agent to be

someone who ‘exercise[s] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.’” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 577.) Furthermore, there must be allegations that the managing agent was acting as a corporate representative or in a professional capacity. (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 724.)

Here, the complaint does not set forth any allegations that the pit boss exercised substantial control over Hustler’s corporate policy such that the store manager was a managing agent of Hustler. (See, e.g., Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168 (finding that a supervisor who did not manage numerous stores, supervised only a few employees, and had authority over only one narrow area of the single store’s multifaceted operations did not exercise authority over corporate principles such that he was a managing agent for purposes of section 3294.) Further, Plaintiff does not allege that the pit boss was acting as a corporate representative or in a professional capacity. (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 724.)

Moreover, the court finds there are no allegations in the complaint to hold a corporate defendant liable for punitive damages. Plaintiff does not allege personal act of malice on the part of a managing agent. Plaintiff alleges “security guards” detained him. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Those are not managing agents. Managing agents must “exercise substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 573.)

Plaintiff also alleges Defendant “authorized or ratified their security guard’s wrongful acts.” (Complaint, ¶ 57.) This allegation contains no facts and is conclusory. The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Smith, supra, 10 Cal. App. 4th at 1042.) In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that the title “Pit Boss” confers sufficient authority to bind the corporate entity, but that assertion is absent from the pleading. Indeed, as Defendant points out, the Complaint states that the pit boss asked why the security guards handcuffed plaintiff, but they “provided no answer.” (Complaint ¶ 13.) This act is wholly inconsistent with either ratification of conduct or the act of a managing agent. Even if the pit boss was aware of the altercation and acting as a managing agent, her instruction was that Plaintiff be removed from the premises. Simply removing a patron from the premises when they are involved in an altercation can hardly be considered malicious conduct.

Plaintiff also alleges that the pit boss was aware of the altercation, and she wrongfully ordered Plaintiff’s removal from the premises based on his race, color, or ancestry, failed to monitor the actions of the security guards, disregarding a clear risk of safety by permitting the security guards to detain Plaintiff, and their failure to remove him from the premises safely and without incident. (Complaint ¶¶ 12-13, 35-36.) However, again, Plaintiff alleges in a conclusory fashion that Hustler and its agents, employees, officers denied him full and equal accommodations or services based on his race. (Id., ¶ 36.) There are no facts to support this allegation. Moreover, the allegations relating to failing to monitor can only amount to negligence, which is not sufficient for punitive damages.

Based on the foregoing, the motion to strike punitive damages is granted. As this is the first motion to strike, the court will allow 20 days leave to amend.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
