DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21BBCV01044    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES


I.        INTRODUCTION

           In this breach of contract action, Plaintiffs David Myers and Sunny Park (collectively Plaintiffs) allege that the contractor who agreed to remodel their home breached the remodeling contract. Defendants are the owners and principals of the contractor, Davis Brothers Construction (collectively Davis Defendants). Defendant Morning Star is a separate entity formed by Tim Davis in Idaho after Davis Brothers Construction, Inc. shut down.

This Court found it instructive to examine the history of this case.  On January 6, 2023, Defendants jointly filed a motion to compel arbitration, which Plaintiffs opposed. On February 28, 2023, the Court issued an 8-page order granting Defendants’ motion and ordering the parties to arbitrate their dispute.  The parties subsequently paid their arbitration fees ($29,250 ÷ 3 or $9,750), except for Morning Star, which was either unable or unwilling to pay the $9,750 price of admission.  This effectively ended a process that Defendants had initiated. On March 13, 2024, the Court ruled that Morning Star was a drafting party to the arbitration agreement and had materially breached the arbitration agreement.  The Court granted Plaintiffs’ motion to vacate the order compelling arbitration and imposed sanctions on Morning Star in the amount of $7,687.50.

          Plaintiffs now ask the Court to impose attorneys fees, jointly and severally, on all Defendants.  This motion was filed February 6, 2024. Davis Defendants filed an opposition on February 28, 2024. Defendant Morning Star filed an opposition on February 28, 2024. Plaintiffs filed a reply on March 4, 2024. Plaintiffs also submitted a supplemental declaration of their attorney, Lawrence Szabo, in reply.

          The Court previously continued the hearing on this motion for further clarification into certain billing records. Plaintiffs’ attorney submitted a supplemental declaration on April 4, 2024.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          “[I]n an employment or consumer arbitration that requires, either expressly or through application of state or federal law or the rules of the arbitration administrator, the drafting party to pay certain fees and costs before the arbitration can proceed, if the fees or costs to initiate an arbitration proceeding are not paid within 30 days after the due date, the drafting party is in material breach of the arbitration agreement, is in default of the arbitration, and waives its right to compel arbitration...” (CCP § 1281.97(a).)

          CCP Section 1281.99(a) provides that “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction against a drafting party that materially breaches an arbitration agreement pursuant to subdivision (a) of Section 1281.97 or subdivision (a) of 1281.98, by ordering the drafting party to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and costs, incurred by the employee or consumer as a result of the material breach.” (CCP § 1281.99(a).)

III.     ANALYSIS

A. Consumer Arbitration

 The arbitration between the parties is a consumer arbitration, as Plaintiffs were the consumers/recipients of home improvements by Defendants. (Motion, p.8: 1-8.)

B. Reasonableness of Fees

The analysis of reasonableness of attorney fee awards considers “the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required and sjill employed in handling the litigation, the attention given, the success of the attorney’s efforts, his learning, his age, and his experience in the particular type of work demanded…” (Berry v. Chaplin (1946) 74 Cal.App.2d 669, 678-679, citations omitted.) There is no requirement that billing records be provided to a trial court in connection with a motion for fees pursuant to contract. The declaration of counsel as to the hours spent on this litigation and the hourly rates charged for such time is all that is required. “[T]here is no legal requirement that an attorney supply billing statements to support a claim for attorney fees.” (Mardirossian & Associates, Inc. v. Ersoff (2007) 135 Cal.App.4th 257, 269.) “[T]here is no legal requirement that such statements be offered in evidence. An attorney’s testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.” (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293.)

Here, the declaration of Plaintiffs’ attorney, Lawrence Szabo, supports the request for attorney fees. He declares that he has been licensed in California since 1974 and has practiced construction/real estate law for over 30 years. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 21.) His hourly rate is $375. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 21.) He declares that his work stipulating to the motion to compel arbitration totaled 4.1 hours or $1,537.50. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 24.) He declares he spent 16.60 hours initially opposing the motion to compel arbitration but reduced his fee amount to $3,806.25 or 10.15 hours. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 25.) He declares that in connection with the arbitration, his firm spent 95.6 hours. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 26.) He also declares that plaintiffs paid interest of 10% per year on the amount owed, for a total of $1,957.30. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 29.) He includes a description of the total fees incurred. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 32.) He declares all fees incurred were necessary. (Szabo Decl. ¶ 33.) He attaches copies of the papers submitted by both sides on the motion to compel arbitration, the arbitrator’s letters, his response to the letters and communications with other counsel regarding the arbitrator’s fees. (Exhs. 1-15.) He also attaches a timesheet printout of the fees Plaintiffs have incurred in prosecuting this case. (Exh. 16.) The timesheet printout provides a brief description of attorney activity and is sectioned by motion/proceeding. (Id.) He also attaches a schedule of interest at Exh. 17.

With respect to this motion, he declares he spent 14.1 hours and expects another 4.5 hours to respond to opposition and appear at the hearing. (Szabo Decl. ¶¶ 28, 30-31.)

Plaintiff David Myers’ declaration also supports the attorney fee request. He declares that Plaintiffs retained Mr. Lawrence Szabo as their attorney in this matter for $375/ hour. (Myers Decl. ¶ 3.) He declares that plaintiffs have paid their attorney up to January 2024, for which they have not received a bill. (Myers Decl. ¶ 4.) He provides the amount they have paid so far: $50,548.00. (Myers Decl. ¶ 5.)

In its opposition, Morning Star urges that it was not a drafting party to the arbitration agreement. (Morning Star Opposition p. 4: 22.) However, per CCP Section 1280(e), Morning Star is a drafting party, as it moved to compel arbitration and in doing so, was a third party relying on the arbitration agreement. (CCP § 1280(e).)

Secondly, Morning Star urges that the fees sought by Plaintiffs in this motion are “patently unreasonable.” (Morning Star Opposition p. 7: 3-4.) Morning Star highlights several things: (1) Exh. 12 attached to Plaintiff’s motion is not very descriptive, (2) the amount claimed in attorney fees for this motion is high, (3) the motion to compel arbitration was made necessary by the conduct of Plaintiffs’ attorney, (4) the entry of December 4, 2023 is unreasonable (23.2 hours), (5) an entry in January of 2024 purports that 32.1 hours were spent on this case in one day and that overall, the amount requested is unreasonably high. (Morning Star Opposition p. 7: 1- p. 10: 28.) Morning Star also attaches the declaration of its attorney, David M. Grey, who declares that the fees sought  are “both disproportionate and outrageously excessive. I make this observation based on my 43 years of experience as a civil litigator in Southern California.” (Grey Decl. ¶ 9.)

In their opposition, the remaining defendants, individual Davises and entity Davis Brothers Construction, urge that they are not responsible for any fees as they did not materially breach the arbitration agreement, which is the impetus for this motion. (See Davis Opposition p. 7: 1-28.) Indeed, per Exh. H of their attorney’s declaration, Davis defendants were found to have paid their arbitration fees. (Bodie Decl. Exh. H.)

Additionally, the Davis Defendants urge that the billing records submitted in support of this motion demonstrate block-billing, bill-padding and excessive hours claimed. (Davis Opposition p. 12:2- p. 13: 27.) Specifically, the Davis Defendants point out that on January 2, 2024, Plaintiffs’ attorney claimed to perform 34 hours of work on the file, despite there only being 24 hours in a day. (Id.) They also urge that the descriptions provided often trail off, and that this prevents them from analyzing whether the fees are reasonable.

In their reply, Plaintiffs reduce the amount of fees requested as “Plaintiffs received a refund in the amount of $15,855.82 from the American Arbitration Association, thereby reducing the amount Plaintiffs have paid to the American Arbitration Association from $25,480.00 to $9,624.18.” (Reply p. 2: 8-10.) Plaintiffs are adjusting their request for fees by the refund amount of $15,855.82.

Plaintiffs’ attorney, Lawrence Szabo, provided a supplemental declaration on April 4, 2024. He declares that “The January 2, 2024 date that Defendants have stated reflects a total of 34+ hours of time billed on that date is the date of the January invoice, and not a date that the services described on that invoice were provided.” (Supplemental Szabo Decl. ¶ 22.) He further declares that the services billed on December 4, 2023, were charges for the entire month of December. (Id.) He attaches individual invoices to reflect this. (Supplemental Szabo Decl. Exhs. 3-5.) However, Exh. 3 includes an invoice dated December 1, 2022, one dated January 3, 2023 and one dated March 8, 2023. It is unclear to the Court how these three much-earlier invoices related to the charges of January 2, 2024  or December 4, 2023. Exh. 4 contains the same invoices as Exh. 3. Exh. 5 does provide the specific invoice for December 4, 2023, with dates the services were rendered (Supplemental Szabo Decl. Exh. 5 p. 39-41) and the invoice for January 2, 2024. (Supplemental Szabo Decl. Exh. 5 p. 42-44.)

          The Court has examined the evidence presented by Plaintiffs and makes the following findings: the rate requested is reasonable. 112 total hours requested is reasonable, given that this matter proceeded to the eve of arbitration. The interest incurred is reasonable. As to the current and anticipated fees spent on this motion, the Court finds 14.1 hours plus 4.5 hours excessive.  The Court awards a total of 10 hours at $375 per hour or $3,750. 

          C. Additional Sanctions

          The Court declines to award sanctions beyond monetary sanctions.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiffs’ motion for attorney fees is granted. Defendant Morning Star is ordered to pay $43,282.90 in attorney fees to Plaintiffs.  However, Plaintiffs ask this Court to impose this order on all Defendants jointly and severally.  The Court asks the parties to address this question at the hearing and provide legal authority supporting their respective positions. The question is whether the Court can order attorneys fees as to a non-breaching drafting party. Stated differently, is there derivative liability for Morning Star’s breach?   As noted above, CCP Section 1281.99(a) provides that “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction against a drafting party that materially breaches an arbitration agreement….”  The statute does not refer to all drafting parties. It is worthwhile to note that the sanctions previously awarded by Judge Oldendorf were imposed only on Morning Star.

Case Number: 23AHCV01836    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA SERVED ON KAISER PERMANENTE

and

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT RALPH GROCERY COMPANY’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION 


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is an action for premises liability. Plaintiff Bryce Harris (Harris) alleges that on April 23, 2023, he slipped and fell while checking out at Defendant Ralph’s Grocery Company in Pasadena. Before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion to quash subpoenas served on his medical care provider, Kaiser Permanente.

Plaintiff filed the motion to quash on February 28, 2024. Ralph’s filed an opposition on April 8, 2024. Plaintiff filed a reply on April 12, 2024.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code Civ. Proc. §1987.1:

“(a) If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

          Subd. (b)(1) permits a party to seek relief.

          Code Civ. Proc. §1987.2 provides for an award of sanctions in the court’s discretion. “[I]n making an order pursuant to motion made under. . . Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”

          Code Civ. Proc. §1985.3:

“(g) Any consumer whose personal records are sought by a subpoena duces tecum and who is a party to the civil action in which this subpoena duces tecum is served may, prior to the date for production, bring a motion under Section 1987.1 to quash or modify the subpoena duces tecum. Notice of the bringing of that motion shall be given to the witness and deposition officer at least five days prior to production. The failure to provide notice to the deposition officer shall not invalidate the motion to quash or modify the subpoena duces tecum but may be raised by the deposition officer as an affirmative defense in any action for liability for improper release of records.

Any other consumer or nonparty whose personal records are sought by a subpoena duces tecum may, prior to the date of production, serve on the subpoenaing party, the witness, and the deposition officer, a written objection that cites the specific grounds on which production of the personal records should be prohibited.

No witness or deposition officer shall be required to produce personal records after receipt of notice that the motion has been brought by a consumer, or after receipt of a written objection from a nonparty consumer, except upon order of the court in which the action is pending or by agreement of the parties, witnesses, and consumers affected.” (CCP § 1985.3(g).)

III.     ANALYSIS

A. Motion

The Complaint alleges that plaintiff was injured when he slipped and fell at Ralph’s in Pasadena. (Complaint ¶¶ 9-11.) Defendant seeks evidence as to these injuries. The deposition subpoenas seek “Any and all documents and records pertaining to the care, treatment, and examination of BRYCE HARRIS AKA BRYCE ALPHONCE HARRIS SR., born October 24, 1940, with SS# UNK, including, but not limited to all office, emergency room, inpatient and outpatient charts and records, pathology slides of specimens, hard copy of EEG’s, ECG’s, & EKG’s, color photographs and/or color surgical photographs and electronical files and/or electronic charts from any and all dates”, “Any and all records of payment and/or discount regarding any medical billing as well as the bill themselves, billing information, including but not limited to procedure, service and diagnosis codes, statements, computer printouts, itemized breakdown of all charges, payments, adjustments/write-offs or balance due” and “Any and all x-ray films, to include MRI films, CAT scans, myelograms, radiological images, ultrasounds and any other films specific to BRYCE HARRIS AKA BRYCE ALPHONCE HARRIS SR., born October 24, 1940, with SS# UNK[.]”(Burkes Decl. Exh. 1 [three separate subpoenas to Kaiser were served- one seeking general medical records, one seeking billing records and one seeking radiology records.])

Plaintiff Harris seeks to quash the subpoena on three grounds: (1) the records sought are not relevant; (2) the records sought are overbroad and (3) disclosure would violate privacy rights.

i. Relevancy

The Court notes that Harris is seeking damages for the injuries he sustained from the accident. Plaintiff urges that his entire medical record is not relevant, as the only relevant records are those pertaining to the incident. (Motion p. 6: 25-28.)

Plaintiff urges he did not open discovery into his entire medical record by way of asserting damages from a slip and fall in 2023. Plaintiff cites Rancho Publications v. Superior Court (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1538, 1549 and  Heda v. Superior Court (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 525, 528 for the proposition that due to his privacy interests in not disclosing his medical records, the standard for relevancy is higher. Plaintiff further cites Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal. App. 4th 1379, 1387-1388 for the proposition that “the patient thus is not obligated to sacrifice all privacy to seek redress for a specific mental or emotional injury; the scope of the inquiry permitted depends upon the nature of the injuries which the patient-litigant himself has brought before the court.” (Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal. App. 4th 1379, 1387-1388, internal citations omitted.)

In opposition, Ralph’s urges that the records sought are relevant, as this is a personal injury action where Plaintiff is asserting back and neck pain arising from underlying incident and Plaintiff has a prior history of back issues. (See Opposition p. 7: 18-19.) The Court agrees; the scope of discovery is broad by nature and medical records are relevant to evaluate Plaintiff’s claims and/or Defendant’s defenses.

The Court declines to quash the deposition subpoena for irrelevancy.

ii. Privacy

The subpoena seeks medical records. Plaintiff argues that these records are protected by his right to privacy. In support, Harris cites case law for the proposition that his claim of injury does not invite discovery into his entire medical record, but just the records relevant to the car accident injuries. (See, e.g., Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 849; see also Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1379; Hallendorf v. Superior Court (1978) 85 Cal. App. 3d 553, 556-557.) When a party seeks information sought to be protected by a privacy interest, that party must demonstrate a compelling need for the information’s disclosure and then balance the interest in privacy versus the need for discovery. (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1017; Lantz v. Superior Court (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1839, 1853.)

Harris urges that even if Defendant has shown that the information sought is relevant, Defendants would then need to (1) demonstrate a compelling need for the records, and then (2) balance the privacy interest against the need for discovery. Plaintiff urges that this has not been done.

In its opposition, Ralph’s urges that though medical records are private information, Plaintiff has put his medical condition at issue in this case, as he seeks redress for back injuries allegedly sustained when he slipped and fell at Ralph’s. Citing Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 841-842, Ralph’s urges that there is an implicit waiver of discovery directly relevant to Plaintiff’s claim. Ralph’s further urges “[t]he discovery responses provided by Plaintiff confirm he has had an ongoing history of chronic back pain for at least 20-30 years.” (Opposition p. 7: 18-19.)

The Court declines to quash the deposition subpoena on privacy grounds.

          iii. Overbreadth

          Here, the subpoena seeks records without a time limit. The incident occurred in 2023. Any inquiry into them must be narrowly tailored to limit impingement on privacy. (Wood vs. Superior Court (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 1138, 1149.) Specifically, the party seeking the subpoena must “identify the particular records that contain pertinent information and, unless it is obvious to a lay person, show why that information must be obtained to resolve the investigation.” (Id. at 1150.) The deposition and its associated request for production of records is not narrowly tailored, as it simply seeks all records involving Harris.

           In its opposition, Ralph’s points out that “Plaintiff's own discovery responses and medical records confirm that his history of back problems extends back to at least the 1990s[.]” In fact, Plaintiff’s medical records indicate that his back problems go back to the 1980s. Exhibit B of Ralph’s opposition includes pages from these records. Bates stamped page PLTF000340 indicates “Thoraco/lumbar revision 2015,” “Lumbar fusion-2002,” and “Cervical fusion- 1980s….” While the parties admirably attempted to limit the document production request by body part, Kaiser was unable to comply because it can only produce documents based on date.  Given Plaintiff’s lengthy history of back problems, Ralph’s should be allowed to obtain medical records going back to 1980.

          The Court declines to quash the subpoena for overbreadth.  However, the Court modifies the request, limiting it to documents from 1980 to the present.

B. Sanctions

          The Court declines to award monetary sanctions, as the Court does not find the motion was made nor opposed in bad faith.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiff Bryce Harris’s motion to quash the subpoenas is denied. His request for monetary sanctions is denied.

          Counsel for Defendant Ralph’s Grocery Company to give notice of this ruling.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT RALPH GROCERY COMPANY’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION 


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is an action for premises liability. Plaintiff Bryce Harris (Harris) alleges that he slipped and fell on April 23, 2023, while checking out at Defendant Ralph’s Grocery Company in Pasadena. Plaintiff is now 82 years old.

Plaintiff Harris filed a motion for trial preference on November 3, 2023. The Court granted the motion for trial preference on January 30, 2024.

Before the Court is Defendant’s motion for reconsideration filed on February 16, 2024. Ralph’s argues that upon a review of Mr. Harris’ complete medical record, trial preference is not warranted. Plaintiff filed an opposition on April 8, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on April 12, 2024.

The Court DENIES the motion for reconsideration.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008 provides, in pertinent part: 

“(a) When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make an application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. 

 

(b) A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or in part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on an ex parte motion. 

… 

(e) This section specifies the court’s jurisdiction with regard to applications for reconsideration of its orders and renewals of previous motions, and applies to all applications to reconsider any order of a judge or court, or for the renewal of a previous motion, whether the order deciding the previous matter or motion is interim or final. No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.” (CCP § 1008(a), (b), (e).) 

A motion for reconsideration under § 1008 requires that the moving party present new or different facts that were not previously considered by the Court.  (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13.)  However, the burden under CCP Section 1008 “is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”  (Ibid.; Even Zohar Construction & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 833 [finding that § 1008 imposes the special requirement of having to not only show new or different facts, circumstances, or law, but also to “show diligence with a satisfactory explanation for not presenting the new or different information earlier…”].)  Reconsideration cannot be granted based on claims that the court misinterpreted the law in its initial ruling because this is not a "new" or "different" matter.  (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.) 

III.     ANALYSIS

A. Timeliness of the Motion for Reconsideration

Per CCP Section 1008, a motion for reconsideration must be brought within 10 days of notice of the ruling the party wishes to be reconsidered. (CCP § 1008(a).) Here, the notice of ruling of the 1/30/24 Order granting trial preference was e-served 2/6/24 giving 10 days from that date in which to file this motion. This motion was filed on February 16, 2024.

The motion is timely.

B. Discussion of Plaintiff’s Medical Records

Defendant urges that the January 30 order granting the motion for trial preference should be reconsidered. In support, Defendant cites a medical record dated November 1, 2023 deeming Plaintiff cleared for surgery. (McIntosh Decl. Exh. B.) Defendant also cites a medical record dated November 9, 2023 that Plaintiff was cleared for the rotator cuff surgery. (McIntosh Decl. Exh. C.) In considering these records attached to the motion, it is unclear to the Court how these records were obtained and if they are properly authenticated.

Secondly, Defendant urges that Plaintiff has failed to provide medical documentation for his heart attack in September of 2023. However, as the Court stated in its order on trial preference, there is no such requirement. (Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal. App. 5th 529, 534 [“The standard under subdivision (a), unlike under subdivision (d), which is more specific and more rigorous, includes no requirement of a doctor's declaration.”], referencing CCP § 36(a).)

          Lastly, Defendant urges that certain discovery disputes between the parties show gamesmanship and unfair tactics by Plaintiff and his counsel. It is unclear to the Court how this is relevant to a motion for reconsideration or indeed, the standard for trial preference.

          In his opposition, Plaintiff urges that the two medical records raised by this motion were provided to Defendant in discovery served November 17, 2023. (See Opposition p. 4: 25- p. 5:7, Paterson Decl. Exhs 1-2.) To prevail on a motion for reconsideration, Defendant must demonstrate that these new or different facts were not available earlier, despite diligence. (Even Zohar Construction & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 833.) Plaintiff urges that these medical records, to the extent that they are new or different facts, were available to Defendant before its opposition to the Motion for Trial Preference was due. Defendant’s opposition was due on January 18, 2024. These records were available to defendant two months before that date. The Court holds that these medical records were in fact available earlier and that Defendant did not demonstrate otherwise. (Paterson Decl. Exhs. 1, 2.)

          The Court denies the motion for reconsideration for failure to demonstrate new or different facts not considered by the Court previously were not available earlier, despite diligence.

         

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

Plaintiff Bryce Harris is ordered to give notice.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civ. Proc. §526 (a) provides grounds on which an injunction may issue:

(1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually.

(2) When it appears by the complaint or affidavits that the commission or continuance of some act during the litigation would produce waste, or great or irreparable injury, to a party to the action.

(3) When it appears, during the litigation, that a party to the action is doing, or threatens, or is about to do, or is procuring or suffering to be done, some act in violation of the rights of another party to the action respecting the subject of the action, and tending to render the judgment ineffectual.

(4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief.

(5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief.

(6) Where the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of judicial proceedings.

(7) Where the obligation arises from a trust.

          V.       DISCUSSION

          A. Prohibitory Injunction versus Mandatory Injunction

          A prohibitory injunction requests the Court intervene to maintain the status quo. Here, Maxspeed is asking for a prohibitory injunction preventing Defendants from using Maxspeed’s credit cards, bank account information and business records.

          The burden is higher for a mandatory injunction because the moving party is asking the Court to disturb the status quo. (See Shoemaker v. County of Los Angeles (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 618, 625.) Here, Maxspeed is also requesting a mandatory injunction ordering the handing over of business-related documents, including but not limited to, bookkeeping documents and software accounts, to Maxspeed’s shareholders, directors, and officers.

B.  Balancing the Equities

“ ‘[A] preliminary injunction is an order that is sought by a plaintiff prior to a full adjudication of the merits of its claim.’ (White v. Davis (2003) 30 Cal.4th 528, 554, 133 Cal.Rptr.2d 648, 68 P.3d 74, italics omitted.) ‘To obtain a preliminary injunction, a plaintiff ordinarily is required to present evidence of the irreparable injury or interim harm that it will suffer if an injunction is not issued pending an adjudication of the merits.’ (Ibid.)

“Trial courts ‘ “ ‘evaluate two interrelated factors when deciding whether or not to issue a preliminary injunction. The first is the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits at trial. The second is the interim harm that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction were denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the preliminary injunction were issued.’ ” ’ (ITV Gurney Holding Inc. v. Gurney (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 22, 28–29.)” Amgen, Inc. v. Health Care Services (2020) 47 Cal.App.5th 716, 731.)

C. Likelihood of Prevailing on the Merits

1. Conversion

Maxspeed urges that it is likely to prevail on the merits of its conversion cause of action against all defendants because it has demonstrated stealing of business information. Citing Lee v. Harley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240, Maxspeed urges that the elements of conversion are: (1) plaintiff’s ownership of the property at issue, (2) defendant’s conversion by wrongful act and (3) plaintiff was harmed as a result. Preliminary injunctions are possible remedies to conversion. (Gladstone v. Hillel (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 977, 989-990.)

Maxspeed urges that it has adequately demonstrated conversion has taken place, as the individual Defendants, Huang and You, were fired November 13, 2023 (Ma Decl. ¶ 3) and the suspect bank account and emails to Maxspeed customers directing them to order from Defendant’s newly formed business and deposit into the suspect ban account occurred after Huang and You were fired. (Ma Decl. ¶¶ 30-32.)

In their opposition, Defendants urge that they have presented sufficient evidence that the ownership of Maxspeed is in question. (See Opposition p. 9: 13-14.) The Court agrees, as Defendants have presented 21 separate declarations stating that they are the owners and operators of Maxspeed. As the ownership of Maxspeed is in question, it is unclear from the evidence presented what actions constitute conversion from Maxspeed. The second element of conversion is not met. (Lee v. Harley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)

Defendants also urge that they have presented sufficient evidence to demonstrate that Ma’s role in Maxspeed is in dispute. The Court at this time does not conclude who owns Maxspeed or that Ma was or was not the CEO/CFO.

In its reply, Maxspeed argues that Ma and Maxspeed are distinguishable, and Ma’s actions cannot be imputed to the business. (Reply p. 3: 10-23.) Maxspeed further argues about the corporate structure of Maxspeed, and in doing so, clearly demonstrates that the corporate structure is in dispute.

Maxspeed has not presented sufficient evidence of a likelihood of prevailing on the merits of its claims. Due to the overwhelming amount of evidence presented in opposition, the Court concludes that the ownership of Maxspeed and the various roles parties had with respect to Maxspeed is in dispute. As both sides allege and support with evidence that the other side is stealing from Maxspeed, the Court concludes the first prong of a preliminary injunction, likelihood of prevailing on the merits of the conversion cause of action, has not been met. For that reason, the preliminary injunction is denied.

VI.     ORDER

           Maxspeed’s motion for a preliminary injunction is denied.
