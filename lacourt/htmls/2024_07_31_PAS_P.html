DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00462    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT SAN GABRIEL VALLEY MEDICAL CENTER LP’S MOTION TO HAVE PLAINTIFF DECLARED A VEXATIOUS LITIGANT

I.        INTRODUCTION

           This is an action arising from alleged medical malpractice. On March 6, 2023, Plaintiff Hong Gan (“Plaintiff”), who is self-represented, filed a Complaint against Defendant San Gabriel Valley Medical Center.  

           On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant San Gabriel Valley Medical Center alleging causes of action for: (1) Intentional Tort, Sexual Abuse; (2) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (3) Negligence; (4) False Involuntary Detention in a Mental Hospital; and (5) Patient Rights Violation.  

           On November 14, 2023, Defendant AHMC San Gabriel Valley Medical Center LP filed a demurrer to the entire FAC.   

           On April 30, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court sustained Defendant AHMC San Gabriel Valley Medical Center LP’s demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend on the grounds that this action is barred by the doctrine of res judicata. (04/30/24 Minute Order.)

           On March 27, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.

           On June 3, 2024, Defendant San Gabriel Valley Medical Center LP (“Defendant”), who indicates that it was erroneously sued as San Gabriel Valley Medical Center, filed and served the instant Motion to Have Plaintiff Declared a Vexatious Litigant.

           As of July 26, 2024, the motion to have Plaintiff declared a vexatious litigant is unopposed. Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

II.       LEGAL STANDARD      

The vexatious litigant statute “provides a procedure in pending litigation for declaring a person a vexatious litigant, and establishes procedure strictures that can be imposed on vexatious litigants.” (In re Bittaker (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1008.) A court may “issue a prefiling order that prohibits the vexatious litigant from filing any new litigation without first obtaining permission of the presiding judge of the court where the litigation is proposed to be filed.” (Ibid.) “The vexatious litigant statute . . . was enacted . . . to curb misuse of the court system by those acting in propria persona who repeatedly litigate the same issues.” (Ibid.) “Their abuse of the system not only wastes court time and resources but also prejudices other parties waiting their turn before the courts.” (Ibid.) “The prefiling order component of the vexatious litigant statute is a necessary method of curbing those for whom litigation has become a game.” (In re Natural Gas Antitrust Cases (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 387, 394.) “It does not deny the vexatious litigant access to the courts, but operates to preclude meritless litigation and the attendant expenditure of resources.” (Ibid.)  

“A court may declare a person to be a vexatious litigant who, in the immediately preceding seven-year period has commenced, prosecuted, or maintained in propria persona at least five litigations other than in a small claims court that have been . . . finally determined adversely to the person . . . .” (Garcia v. Lacey (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 402, 406, quoting CCP § 391(b)(1).) “The term [l]itigation is defined broadly as any civil action or proceeding, commenced, maintained or pending in any state or federal court.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.) “A litigation is finally determined adversely to a plaintiff if he does not win the action or proceeding he began, including cases that are voluntarily dismissed by a plaintiff.” (Ibid.)

A plaintiff may also be declared a vexatious litigant under Code Civ. Proc., § 391, subd. (b)(2). A vexatious litigant is a person who “[a]fter litigation has been finally determined against the person, repeatedly relitigates or attempts to relitigate, in propria persona, either (i) the validity of the determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined or (ii) the cause of action, claim, controversy, or any of the issues of fact or law, determined or concluded by the final determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391, subd. (b)(2).)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          

A.   Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452,

453.)

B.    Appropriateness of Declaring Plaintiff a Vexatious Litigant

Defendant contends that Plaintiff is a vexatious litigant under the terms of Code of Civil Procedure section 391(b)(1) and 391(b)(2). The Court agrees with Defendant.

In the last seven years, Plaintiff has filed six separate actions in propria persona—two of which were against Defendant—that were all determined adversely to Plaintiff. (Defendant’s RJN at Exs. A, D, E, H, I, K, L, M, N, P, Q, and R.) Moreover, in the instant action, the demurrer of Defendant to the FAC has been sustained without leave to amend. (Defendant’s RJN at Exs. T and U.) Thus, Plaintiff is a vexatious litigant under CCP § 391(b)(1) as she has commenced, prosecuted, or maintained at least five litigations, in propria persona, that have all been determined adversely to Plaintiff.

Even under CCP § 391(b)(2), a finding is appropriate that Plaintiff is a vexatious litigant. Including the instant action, Plaintiff has brought three separate cases against Defendant. (Defendant’s RJN, Exhibit U at p. 1.) In ruling on the demurrer to the FAC in the instant action, the Court noted that “[t]his is Plaintiff’s third lawsuit against Defendant concerning these same allegations. In Los Angeles Superior Court case numbers 19STLC08450 and 20STLC07641, demurrers were sustained without leave to amend and the actions were dismissed.” (Id.) Plaintiff has therefore repeatedly attempted to litigate the same claims against Defendant even “[a]fter a litigation has been finally determined against [Plaintiff]” and such claims were “determined or concluded by the final determination against the same defendant . . . as whom the litigation was finally determined.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391, subd. (b)(2).)

Defendant requests that, along with being declared a vexatious litigant, a prefiling order be entered. “[T]he court may, on its own motion or the motion of any party, enter a prefiling order which prohibits a vexatious litigant from filing any new litigation in the courts of this state in propria person without first obtaining leave of the presiding justice or presiding judge of the court where the litigation is proposed to be filed. Disobedience of the order by a vexatious litigant may be punished as a contempt of court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.7, subd. (a).) “The clerk of the court shall provide the Judicial Council a copy of any prefiling orders issued pursuant to subdivision (a).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.7, subd. (f).)

The Court finds it appropriate to grant Defendant’s request to enter a prefiling order.

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, Defendant San Gabriel Valley Medical Center LP’s Motion to Have Plaintiff Declared a Vexatious Litigant is GRANTED.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is an action arising from alleged negligence in providing healthcare services. On August 21, 2023, Plaintiff Marcelline Benois (“Plaintiff”), by and through her Power of Attorney, Sherlyn Benois-Green, filed a Complaint against Defendants Kaiser Foundation Hospitals, The Southern California Permanente Medical Group, Inc., Riverside Healthcare System, L.P., Community Convalescent Center of Montclair, Inc., and Does 1 through 200, inclusive (collectively, the “Defendants”), alleging causes of action for: (1) Elder Abuse; (2) Negligence; and (3) Violation of Residents’ Rights.

          On September 21, 2023, Defendant Riverside Healthcare System, L.P. (“Riverside”) filed an Answer to the Complaint.

          On November 9, 2023, Defendant Community Convalescent Center of Montclair, Inc. (“CCCM”) filed its Answer to the Complaint.

          On December 18, 2023, Defendants Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (“KFH”) and Southern California Permanente Medical Group (“SCPMG”) (collectively, “Kaiser”) filed the instant Petition to Compel Arbitration (the “Petition”). Kaiser moves for an order compelling arbitration and staying this action pending completion of arbitration.

          On March 27, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.

          On April 24, 2024, Defendant Riverside filed a Motion for an Order to Stay the Civil Action Pending Arbitration (the “Motion”). The Motion is made on the grounds that Kaiser has petitioned the Court to compel arbitration with Plaintiff and Defendant Riverside moves to stay this civil action as to Defendant Riverside pending the conclusion of the arbitration between Plaintiff and Kaiser.

          On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Motion.

          On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Petition.

          On July 24, 2024, Defendant Riverside filed a reply brief as to the Motion.

Also, on July 24, 2024, Kaiser filed a reply brief pertaining to the Petition.

          The Court will address the respective filings by Kaiser and Defendant Riverside in this one ruling. The Court will first address the petition to compel arbitration, which was filed by Kaiser, followed by the motion to stay the civil action filed by Defendant Riverside.

 

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD     

Code Civ. Proc. §1281 provides: “A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter existing is valid, enforceable, and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” 

Code Civ. Proc. §1281.2 provides that upon petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate and a party’s refusal to submit to arbitration, the court shall order the parties to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement exists, unless it determines that the right to arbitrate has been waived, that grounds exist for revocation, or that a party to the agreement is also party to a pending litigation arising out of the same facts and there exists a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of fact or law.  In such a situation, the court may (1) refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement and order intervention or joinder of all parties in a single action, (2) order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues, (3) order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the action pending outcome of arbitration, or (4) stay arbitration pending outcome of the litigation. 

A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)¿¿ ¿ 

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (CCP § 1281.4.)¿ 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 provides, “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” 

 

III.     ANALYSIS AS TO THE PETITION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

         

A.   The Existence of a Written Agreement to Arbitrate is Established

Kaiser contends that where the parties have a written agreement to arbitrate,

California law requires that they be ordered to arbitrate their disputes. Plaintiff argues that the arbitration agreement is legally invalid as her purported electronic signature has not been legally authenticated. Plaintiff also contends her medical records indicate that she was diagnosed with dementia and Alzheimer’s and therefore was incompetent to sign any documents.

          “A voluntary acceptance of the benefit of a transaction is equivalent to a consent to all the obligations arising from it, so far as the facts are known, or ought to be known, to the person accepting.” (Civ. Code § 1589.) “All persons are capable of contracting, except minors, persons of unsound mind, and persons deprived of civil rights.” (Civ. Code § 1556.) “A person who has a mental or physical disorder may still be capable of contracting . . . .” (Prob. Code § 810(b).) “A judicial determination that a person is totally without understanding, or is of unsound mind, or suffers from one or more mental deficits so substantial that, under the circumstances, the person should be deemed to lack legal capacity to perform a specific act, should be based on evidence of a deficit in one or more of the person’s mental functions rather than on a diagnosis of a person’s mental or physical disorder.” (Prob. Code § 810(c).)

“[A]n electronic signature has the same legal effect as a handwritten signature.” (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1061.) “The act of the person may be shown in any manner, including a showing of the efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.) In showing the authenticity of a signature on an electronic document, a party seeking to compel arbitration must “explain how, or upon what basis, [an inference is made] that the electronic signature” is that of the non-moving party. (Ibid.) Where there is a contention that a plaintiff did not electronically sign an arbitration agreement, the moving defendant has “the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the electronic signature was authentic.” (Id. at p. 1059.)

          Kaiser’s Evidence in Support of the Petition

          In support of the Petition, Kaiser presents the declaration of Danny Covarrubio (“Covarrubio”), who is the Operations Manager, Medicare Membership Administration and is employed by Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (“Health Plan”). (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 1.) Health Plan’s membership records show that Plaintiff was enrolled as an individual Kaiser Permanente Senior Advantage Member from November 1, 2014, through March 1, 2023 and then from April 1, 2023 through the present time. (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. B.) According to Mr. Covarrubio, “KPSA” stands for Kaiser Permanente Senior Advantage, and “SCR” stands for Southern California Region. (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 4.) The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (“CMS”) permits Medicare beneficiaries to enroll electronically, by telephone, or in writing. (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff became Medicare-eligible on September 1, 2014, her KPSA enrollment was submitted on October 26, 2014, and she became enrolled under the KPSA effective November 1, 2014 through March 1, 2023. (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. C.) On March 9, 2023, Plaintiff re-enrolled telephonically as a Senior Advantage member effective April 1, 2023, and remains so enrolled through the present time. (Covarrubio Decl., ¶ 4.)

          In support of the Petition, Nancy Garcia (“Garcia”) declares that Health Plan’s records indicate that Plaintiff enrolled with an effective date of November 1, 2014, as an individual Senior Advantage Member, which is a plan available to Medicare-eligible enrollees in the appropriate region. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 5.) Ms. Garcia attaches a copy of the 2022 Kaiser Permanente Senior Advantage (HMO) Evidence of Coverage (the “Senior Advantage EOC”) applicable to Plaintiff. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. A.) The arbitration provision begins on page 253 therein. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 5.) The Senior Advantage EOC remains in effect for a single contract year and has always contained an arbitration provision. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 6.) As of January 1, 2008, the arbitration provision was amended to permit enrollees in the individual Kaiser Permanente Senior Advantage plan to opt-out of the arbitration provision by submitting a written opt-out notice within a specified time period. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff was mailed a copy of the Senior Advantage EOC upon enrollment. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 8.)

Kaiser presents evidence that Plaintiff did not opt-out of the arbitration provision in the Senior Advantage EOC. (McWilliams Decl., ¶¶ 5-6; Arams Decl., ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff’s Evidence in Opposition to the Petition

In opposition to the Petition, Plaintiff presents the declaration of Sherlyn Benois-Green (“Green”) who states that she is acting as the Power of Attorney for Plaintiff. (Green Decl., p. 1:26-28.) Ms. Green states that she has reviewed the arbitration agreement attached to the Petition and she has never received any arbitration agreement to sign, and she did not sign it electronically on or behalf of Plaintiff. (Green Decl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff has been diagnosed with Alzheimer’s since 2012 and she would not have been unable to sign the arbitration agreement and would not have had the mental ability to understand or electronically sign the agreement. (Green Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff is completely unable to take, receive telephone calls, or to make any telephone calls as a result of the above-referenced diagnosis. (Green Decl., ¶ 3.) The signature on the arbitration agreement is not the signature of Plaintiff and Ms. Green states that “any purported signature on the arbitration agreement was done without [her] consent, knowledge and/or authorization as her power of attorney, and her documented lack of mental capacity to be able to do so.” (Green Decl., ¶ 5.)

Plaintiff also presents medical records showing that she has been diagnosed with dementia as well as Alzheimer’s. (Peck Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. 3.)

 

Analysis

The Court has reviewed the declarations of Timothy Everett, Danny Covarrubio, Nancy Garcia, Alicia McWilliams, Judy Tome, and Aura Armas, which were all submitted in support of the Petition. The Court notes that none of the aforementioned declarations set forth any facts showing “the efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.” (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group, supra, 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1061.) The purported signature of Plaintiff, which Kaiser alleges is a representation of Plaintiff’s agreement to the arbitration provision in the Senior Advantage EOC, is a mere typed signature. (Petition at Ex. C.)

Kaiser has “met its initial burden to show an agreement to arbitrate by attaching a copy of the [enrollment form], which purportedly bears [Plaintiff’s] electronic . . . signature.” (Fabian v. Renovate America, Inc. (2019) 42 Cal.Appp.5th 1062, 1067.) Due to the argument raised in the opposition that Plaintiff did not sign the enrolment form which purportedly bound her to the Senior Advantage EOC, Kaiser has the burden of establishing the authenticity of Plaintiff’s signature on the enrollment form. (Id. at p. 1068.) None of the declarations offered in support of the Petition states “how [Plaintiff’s signature was placed on the [enrollment form], who received the [enrollment form], how the signed [enrollment form] was returned . . . and how [Plaintiff’s] identification was verified as the person who actually signed the [enrollment form].” (Id. at p. 1069.) Moreover, Kaiser did not provide any declarations in support of the reply, which does not allow Kaiser to show “how, or on what basis” the purported signature was the act of Plaintiff and could have only been placed on the enrollment form by Plaintiff. (Ibid.) The declaration of Mr. Covarrubio, who Kaiser uses to indicate that Plaintiff enrolled as a KPSA member and therefore is bound by the Senior Advantage EOC, does not articulate the procedures utilized to determine the veracity of the signature on the enrollment form.

While Kaiser did not meet its burden to authenticate the electronic signature of Plaintiff, the Court finds that irrespective of such fact an agreement to arbitrate exists between the parties. Plaintiff is bound by the Senior Advantage EOC as she has accepted the benefits of such contract. Plaintiff does not argue that she did not receive healthcare services from Kaiser pursuant to the Senior Advantage EOC. Where there is a policy “containing a clear and conspicuous arbitration provision” a court will find that a moving defendant has met its burden to establish the existence of an arbitration agreement. (Mission Viejo Emergency Medical Associates v. Beta Healthcare Group (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1146, 1156.) Moreover, Plaintiff does not deny that she accepted policy benefits under the Senior Advantage EOC and therefore she cannot deny its existence. (Ibid.) Plaintiff was given notice of the provisions of the Senior Advantage EOC, and accepted benefits thereunder and therefore can be bound by such an agreement. (Craig v. Brown & Root, Inc. (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 416, 420.) The Court reminds Plaintiff that an arbitration agreement need not be signed to be enforceable. (Serafin v. Balco Properties Ltd., LLC (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 165, 176.)

As to Plaintiff’s argument that she lacked capacity to sign the enrollment form or re-enroll herself in the Senior Advantage EOC via telephone, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not submitted substantial evidence to support a finding of incapacity. (Algo-Heyres v. Oxnard Manor LP (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 1064, 1072.) Plaintiff submitted no expert declaration or declaration from a medical professional as to her purported incapacity. (Ibid.)

Thus, the Court finds that there is a valid written agreement to arbitrate between Plaintiff and Kaiser.

 

B.    The Scope of the Arbitration Provision

The arbitration provision in the Senior Advantage EOC provides that it applies to “[a]ll members enrolled in a Kaiser Permanente Senior Advantage Individual Plan with an effective date of January 1, 2008 or after who have not expressly opted out of the binding arbitration process within 60 calendar days of his or her Senior Advantage effective date.” (Petition, Ex. A at p. 253.) The arbitration provision covers “any claim for medical or hospital malpractice . . . or relating to the coverage for, or delivery of, services or items, irrespective of the legal theories upon which the claim is asserted.” (Petition, Ex. A at p. 254.) KFH and SCPMG are entities that are bound to the arbitration provision. (Petition, Ex. A at p. 254.)

Here, the Complaint arises from Kaiser’s care and treatment of Plaintiff from April 2022 through August 2022, where she alleges that she developed infections and Stage IV pressure injuries to her sacrum/coccyx. (Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 80.) Thus, the causes of action asserted in the Complaint are covered by the arbitration provision in the Senior Advantage EOC.

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s claims fall within the scope of the arbitration provision.

 

C.   Health and Safety Code Section 1363.1

Plaintiff contends that Health and Safety Code Section 1363.1 precludes enforcement of the arbitration provision in this case. The Court disagrees.

“As part of its regulation of health care service plans, California imposes certain disclosure requirements as a predicate to the enforcement of arbitration clauses contained in plan subscriber agreements.” (Smith v. PacifiCare Behavioral Health of California, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 139, 143.) “Health and Safety Code section 1363.1 . . . provides that a binding arbitration clause in a health care service plan must incorporate various disclosures, including a clear statement of whether the subscriber or enrollee is waiving his or her right to a jury trial.” (Ibid.) “The waiver language . . . must appear immediately before the signature line for the individual enrolling in the plan.” (Ibid.)

However, [t]he Medicare Act preempts all state regulation of Medicare Advantage plans not related to licensing or plan solvency . . . .” (Clay v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (2007) 540 F.Supp.2d 1101, 1108.) An “EOC is considered marketing material and must be approved by the CMS.” (Id. at p. 1109.) The regulations promulgated by the CMS “supersede any state law or regulation with respect to Medicare Advantage plans . . . .” (Ibid.) “To the extent that California Health and Safety Code section 1363.1 purports to regulate the adequacy of any disclosures in [an] EOC, it is superseded by federal law, and its application . . . is preempted.” (Ibid.)

Here, Kaiser presents evidence that is uses the Medicare-approved arbitration opt-out procedure. (Tome Decl., ¶¶ 2-3; Exs. E and F.) Plaintiff is a Medicare member of Kaiser and its plan documents, including the Senior Advantage EOC, are issued pursuant to regulations promulgated by Medicare. (Garcia Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, 7-10; Tome Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)

Thus, under Clay, supra, 540 F.Supp.2d 1101, Health and Safety Code section 1363.1 is preempted and has no bearing on the enforceability of the arbitration provision.

 

D.   Applicability of CCP § 1281.2(c)

Plaintiff contends that the Court should use its absolute discretion under CCP §

1281.2(c) to deny the Petition to compel arbitration in this matter to avoid conflicting rulings. Kaiser contends that CCP § 1281.2(c) does not apply to the arbitration agreement at issue.

          Initially, the Court notes that the third cause of action is only asserted against Defendant CCCM. A court may deny a petition to compel arbitration if it determines that “[a] party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2(c).) “This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.” (Id.)

While the Court notes that all defendants are parties to the negligence and elder abuse causes of action, the Court finds that CCP § 1281.2(c) is inapplicable to the instant action. The Senior Advantage EOC is not within the scope of CCP § 1281.2(c) as this action concerns the professional negligence of a healthcare provider. The Petition states that the Senior Advantage EOC is subject to CCP § 1295(f). (Petition, ¶ 12.)

Moreover, the Court notes that the Senior Advantage EOC explicitly states that it is governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”). The FAA “does not authorize courts to stay arbitration pending resolution of litigation, or to refuse to enforce a valid arbitration provision to avoid duplicative proceedings or conflicting rulings.” (Mastick v. TD Ameritrade, Inc. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1263.)

The Court therefore DENIES Plaintiff’s request to deny the Petition under CCP § 1281.2(c).

The Court GRANTS the Petition and orders this action STAYED pending the conclusion of arbitration between Kaiser and Plaintiff pursuant to the Petition. (CCP § 1281.4.)

 

IV.     ANALYSIS AS TO THE MOTION TO STAY THE CIVIL ACTION PENDING ARBITRATION

Defendant Riverside requests that pending the Court’s order compelling arbitration between Kaiser and Plaintiff, the Court stays this civil action as to Defendant Riverside until after the conclusion of arbitration.

“Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4 requires a court to stay an action submitted to arbitration pursuant to an agreement of the parties.” (Titan/Value Equities Group., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 482, 487.)

The Court, pursuant to its discussion above, has granted the Petition filed by Kaiser. As such, all proceedings in this Court are stayed pending the completion of arbitration.

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Motion for an Order to Stay the Civil Action Pending Arbitration filed by Defendant Riverside.

 

V.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, the Petition to Compel Arbitration and Stay Action filed by Defendants Kaiser Foundation Hospitals and Southern California Permanente Medical Group is GRANTED.

The Court GRANTS the Motion for an Order to Stay the Civil Action Pending Arbitration filed by Defendant Riverside Healthcare System, L.P.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          In this action, Plaintiffs The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency (“TOP”), Dandan Song aka Alex Song, Peiran Li aka Alice Li, Feng Tang aka Jenny Tang, and Annie Yuan On (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege that their competitors in the Asian--American insurance and wealth management industry, Defendants Pom Insurance Agency Inc. dba Peace of Mind Wealth Management (“POM”), Alan Wen (“Wen”), and Angel Wenjun Tang (“Tang”), caused many of Plaintiffs’ contracts (valued in millions and billions) to be terminated, Plaintiffs’ clients to cease doing business with TOP, and Plaintiffs to suffer substantial losses.

The complaint asserts 15 causes of action, including breach of contract, intentional interference with contractual relations, misappropriation of trade secrets, defamation, assault, and battery.

          Before the Court are motions by POM, Wen, and Tang (collectively “Defendants”) seeking orders compelling nonparties Life Insurance Company of the Southwest (“LSW”) and Lovel Firm, P.C. (“Lovel Firm”) to comply with the Defendants’ subpoenas for the production of business records. The nonparties oppose the motions. Because the motions fail to comply with the relevant Code of Civil Procedure sections, the motions are denied.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court …, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

“For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it ‘might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.…’ [Citation.] Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.” (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)

 

“[D]iscovery from a nonparty may be obtained only by ‘deposition subpoena’ (§ 2020.010, subd. (b)).” (Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 130 (italics removed) (“Unzipped”).) “A business records subpoena directs the nonparty’s custodian of records (or other qualified person) to deliver the requested documents (in person, by messenger, or by mail) to the ‘deposition officer’ specified in the subpoena. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 131.)

“A deposition subpoena that commands only the production of business records for copying shall designate the business records to be produced either by specifically describing each individual item or by reasonably particularizing each category of item, and shall specify the form in which any electronically stored information is to be produced, if a particular form is desired.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.410, subd. (a).)

“If a subpoena requires … the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court …, the court, upon motion … may make an order … directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.” (Civ. Code Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

 

          III.     SUBPOENA DIRECTED AT LOVEL FIRM

“Any person may serve the subpoena by personal delivery of a copy of it as follows: ¶ (1) If the deponent is a natural person, to that person. ¶ (2) If the deponent is an organization, to any officer, director, custodian of records, or to any agent or employee authorized by the organization to accept service of a subpoena.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.220, subd. (b).)

“Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require all of the following of any deponent who is a resident of California at the time of service: ¶ (1) Personal attendance and testimony, if the subpoena so specifies. ¶ (2) Any specified production, inspection, testing, and sampling.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.220, subd. (c).)

“[A] deposition subpoena that has not been personally served in compliance with section 2020.220 imposes no obligations on a nonparty deponent.” (In re Marriage of Moore (2024) 322 Cal.Rptr.3d 249, 261.)

Here, Lovel Firm argues that Plaintiffs did not personally serve the deposition subpoena on the firm. Plaintiffs do not address that issue in their reply papers. Plaintiffs only address the other service issue that Lovel Firm raised (i.e., Plaintiffs failed to serve the affected consumers with the required notices).

          Therefore, because Plaintiffs failed to personally serve the Lovel Firm with the subpoena, the subpoena does not impose any obligation on the nonparty and

the motion to compel is denied.

 

IV.     SUBPOENA DIRECTED AT LIC

          LSW opposes Plaintiffs’ motion, arguing (among other things) that Plaintiffs failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 1985.3 (“Section 1985.3”) with regard to serving the affected consumer with a statutory notice and, therefore, the motion should be denied.

“A deposition subpoena that seeks ‘personal records pertaining to a consumer’ must be accompanied by proof that the consumer was served with notice of the subpoena or by the consumer’s written authorization to release his or her personal records. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.410, subd. (d).) The scope and nature of this notice is described by statute. (Id., § 1985.3.)” Board of Registered Nursing v. Superior Court of Orange County (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1035 (“Board of Registered Nursing”) [emphasis added].)

“A ‘consumer,’ … means any natural person.” (Board of Registered Nursing, supra, 59 Cal.App.5th at p. 1036.)

“A subpoena that requires the production of records, documents, or other things is known as a subpoena duces tecum.” (City of Woodlake v. Tulare County Grand Jury (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1293, 1299.)

“Prior to the date called for in the subpoena duces tecum for the production of personal records, the subpoenaing party shall serve or cause to be served on the consumer whose records are being sought a copy of the subpoena duces tecum, of the affidavit supporting the issuance of the subpoena, if any, and of the notice described in subdivision (e), and proof of service as indicated in paragraph (1) of subdivision (c).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (b) [emphasis added].)

Service of the documents mentioned above (including a copy of the subpoena) shall be made to the consumer: “Not less than 10 days prior to the date for production specified in the subpoena duces tecum, plus the additional time provided by Section 1013 if service is by mail [i.e., five (5) calendar days if service is made by mail to an address in California under Section 1013, subdivision (a)].)” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (b)(2) [emphasis added].)

Service must also be made: “At least five days prior to service upon the custodian of the records, plus the additional time provided by Section 1013 if service is by mail.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (b)(3).)

The subpoena attached to defense counsel’s declaration as Exhibit A asked LSW to produce the following records by April 22, 2024.

1.     All DOCUMENTS related to customer complaints against The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency.

2.     All COMMUNICATIONS related to customer complaints against The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency.

3.     All DOCUMENTS related to customer complaints against DANDAN SONG.

4.     All COMMUNICATIONS related to customer complaints against DANDAN SONG.

5.     All DOCUMENTS related to customer complaints against PEIRAN LI.

6.     All COMMUNICATIONS related to customer complaints against PEIRAN LI.

7.     All DOCUMENT'S related to investigations against The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency.

8.     All COMMUNICATIONS related to investigations against The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency.

9.     All DOCUMENTS related to investigations against Pom Insurance Agency Inc. dba Peace of Mind Wealth Management.

10. All COMMUNICATIONS related to investigations against Pom Insurance Agency Inc. dba Peace of Mind Wealth Management.

LSW argues that because the subpoena seeks consumer records of Dandan Song and Peiran Li, Defendants were required to first serve the statutory notices on those consumers but failed to do so.

In response to that argument, Defendants argue in their reply brief that the subpoena directed at LSW also seeks records relating to entities TOP and POM and that since those entities are not “consumers” within the meaning of Section 1985.3, no consumer notice was required, and, therefore, LSW should have produced records pertaining to those entities.

However, Defendants did not raise that argument in their moving papers.

Therefore, in its opposition, LSW did not have the opportunity to address Defendants’ suggestion that the relevant statutes should be construed to only apply to the portion of a subpoena that seeks consumer records and not the entire subpoena.

          But even if the Court were to consider that argument on its merits, the Court finds the argument unpersuasive because it is not clear that the subpoena can be interpreted the way Defendants have suggested. “The term ‘personal information,’ as adopted by the consumer notice statutes, means ‘any information that is maintained by an agency that identifies or describes an individual, including, but not limited to, his or her name, social security number, physical description, home address, home telephone number, education, financial matters, and medical or employment history. It includes statements made by, or attributed to, the individual.’ (Civ. Code, § 1798.3, subd. (a), adopted by Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.4.)” (Board of Registered Nursing, supra, 59 Cal.App.5th at p. 1036 [emphasis added].) Here, the complaint alleges that Dandan Song and Peiran Li founded TOP. (Compl., 2.) TOP and those two individuals are plaintiffs in this case. Request Nos. 1, 7, and 8 above seek records concerning investigations and complaints against TOP. However, those requests do not contain language excluding records that contain statements by or attributed to consumers. In other words, there is no evidence or language in the subpoena that allows the Court to find (as Defendants appear to suggest) that LSW could have produced records in response to Request Nos. 1, 7, and 8 that were exclusive of the documents sought by Nos. 3 to 6. To the extent Request Nos. 1, 7, and 8 encompass records that contain statements by or attributed to Dandan Song and Peiran Li, Defendants were required to comply with the consumer notice requirements.

          Since Defendants failed to comply with the consumer notice requirements, the motion to compel LSW to comply with their subpoena is denied. 

         

V.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Motions by Defendants Pom Insurance Agency Inc. dba Peace of Mind Wealth Management (“POM”), Alan Wen (“Wen”), and Angel Wenjun Tang (“Tang”) to compel nonparties Lovel Firm, P.C., and Life Insurance Company of the Southwest to comply with their deposition subpoenas is denied. Any request for sanctions by the moving defendants or opposing nonparties is denied.
