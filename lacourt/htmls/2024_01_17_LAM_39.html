DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV28189    Hearing Date: January 17, 2024    Dept: 39

SoCal Lien Solutions, LLC v. BDB Properties, Inc.

Case No. 19STCV28189

Motion to Set Aside Default

Motion for a Turnover Order

 

Plaintiff SoCal Lien Solutions, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this breach of contract action against BDB Properties (“Defendant”), based upon having been assigned the claim, per Civil Code section 954.  The Court incorporates its minute orders of March 23, 2021, and September 29, 2023, by reference and includes the relevant details in this order. 

 

Plaintiff filed this action on August 8, 2019.  The summons and original complaint were served on  “Vanik Vergovian of Shalili & Shalili—Agent for Service” at “1200 S. Hope Street, Ste. 1000, Los Angeles, CA 90015.”  Plaintiff obtained a default judgment, following which Defendant filed an ex parte application to set aside that default judgment.  The Court gave Defendant the benefit of the doubt and granted the ex parte application on March 23, 2021.   

 

Following that hearing, on March 29, 2021, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint.  Plaintiff filed a proof of service on August 23, 2021, which states that “BDB Properties, Inc.” was served personally on April 21, 2021, at 3:14 p.m. by serving “Daniel Khalili-agent of Service” at the following address: “925 N Croft Ave Los Angeles CA 90069.”  Plaintiff filed a request for entry of default, which the Clerk’s Office rejected because: (1) The proof of service stated that the first amended complaint was served, but not the summons; (2) The request for entry of default stated that the plaintiff’s name was “SoCal Lien Solutions, LLC” but the first amended complaint listed the plaintiff as “SoCal Lien Solutions;” and (3) The request for entry of default stated that the defendant’s name was “BDB Properties,” but the first amended complaint listed the defendant’s name as “BDB Properties, Inc.”    

 

Plaintiff corrected these issues and made two attempts to serve Defendant at its address-of-record.  Plaintiff filed an application to serve through the Secretary of State which has a declaration stating that the process server attempted service on December 1, 2021, but could not locate Defendant at its address-of-record.  Plaintiff filed a second application to serve through the Secretary of State which has a declaration stating that the process server attempted service at Defendant’s address-of-record on March 11, 2022.  The declaration states: “I walked every floor and found no body or entity with that name.”  The declaration also states that he made efforts to locate Defendant at the property through the property manager, to no avail.  The Court granted the application, following which Plaintiff served the summons and operative complaint through the Secretary of State.

 

Since that time, Plaintiff’s counsel mailed requests for entry of default to Defendant’s address of record: “1200 S Hope St Los Angeles CA 90015.”  These requests for entry of default were mailed on August 2, 2022; August 4, 2022; August 18, 2022; and August 23, 2022.  The Court entered a default judgment in the amount of $44,153.36 on September 2, 2022. 

 

Now, Defendant (again) seeks to set aside default judgment, per Code of Civil Procedure section 473.  As an initial matter, Defendant’s counsel argues that Plaintiff should have served Defendant through counsel.  In fact, at the hearing on March 23, 2021, the Court asked Defendant’s counsel whether he would accept service, and he indicated that he had no authority to do so.  Defendant’s counsel also argues that Plaintiff was not eligible to serve Defendant through the Secretary of State.  Per California Corporations Code section 1702, a plaintiff may serve a corporate defendant through the Secretary of State if, among other things, the agent for service cannot be found.  Plaintiff appears to have satisfied this requirement, as two different process servers went to Defendant’s purported address and could not locate Defendant’s office at that address.    

 

            In support of Defendant’s ex parte application, Daniel Khalili submitted a declaration stating that he is the sole owner of BDB Properties.  Mr. Khalili states that he is the agent for service of process and that his address is: “1200 S. Hope Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012.”  He claims never to have been served with the summons or complaint, either personally or through his address.  He also states: “I carefully receive and review all mail addressed to BDB Properties at 1200 S. Hope Street in Los Angeles, CA 90012, however I never received the Summons, the Complaint or any other papers filed in this action.”

 

            The Court is skeptical of Mr. Khalili’s representations.  Plaintiff’s counsel declared under penalty of perjury that he mailed four different requests for entry of default to Defendant’s address-of-record.  The summons and original complaint were purportedly served at Defendant’s address-of-record by Mr. Pradip Kissoondyal, a registered process server.  Two different process servers—Mr. Babak Ghorbanian and Mr. Brian Weller, a registered process server—attempted to serve the summons and the first amended complaint to Defendant at its address-of-record and could not locate Defendant at its purported business address.  A fourth process server—Mr. Eric Gamble, a registered process server—personally served “Daniel Khalili.”  It strains credulity for Mr. Khalili to maintain that his business address is correct and that he was never served with any pleading concerning this case.

 

            Nevertheless, the Court must grant the motion.  The Court entered the default judgment on September 2, 2022, based upon Plaintiff’s purported service of Defendant through the Secretary of State.  The proof of service was filed on August 17, 2022, and states that the documents were served on the Secretary of State on June 20, 2022.  Inexplicably, the Secretary of State did not actually serve the documents on Defendant until October 24, 2022, after the default judgment had already been issued.  (See Notice of Lodging, filed on December 28, 2023.)  Therefore, the default judgment is void.    

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

            1.         The Court grants Defendant’s motion to set aside default and default judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d).

 

            2.         The Court finds that Defendant was served through the Secretary of State on October 24, 2022.  The Court’s clerk shall update the docket accordingly.    

 

            3.         The Court orders Defendant to file an answer or responsive pleading on or before February 22, 2024.

 

            4.         The Court shall hold a case management conference on March 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

            5.         The Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for a turnover order.

 

            6.         Plaintiff’s counsel shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.    

 


Case Number: 21STCV46583    Hearing Date: January 17, 2024    Dept: 39

Mandana Nazar v. Shawn Yadidi, et al.

Case No. 21STCV46583

Ex Parte Application

 

            Plaintiffs Mandana Nazar and Fole, Inc. dba as “Suncare Medical Supplies” (“Suncare”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Shawn Yadidi, Promed Medical Supplies (“Promed”), and Navid Zad (collectively, “Defendants”) asserting 22 causes of action for employment-based claims, discrimination and sexual harassment, defamation and trade libel, intentional infliction of emotional distress, interference with existing and prospective contractual relationships, and unfair competition.  Shawn Yadidi and Promed filed a first amended cross-complaint against Plaintiffs asserting causes of action for civil conspiracy for fraud, misappropriation of trade secrets, and unfair business practices.  The complaint was filed on December 21, 2021, and the first amended cross-complaint was filed on September 28, 2022.

 

            This case has been rife with gamesmanship and a lack of civility and professionalism which has necessitated unnecessary litigation.  Counsel for Defendants has twice refused to extend common courtesies in extending filing deadlines, necessitating ex parte applications, both of which were granted.  (See Court’s Minute Orders, dated January 4, 2023 and February 23, 2023.)  In one order, the Court noted that “[t]he Court should not have to resolve disputes of this nature” and ordered the parties to review the Los Angeles County Superior Court’s Guidelines for Civility in Litigation.  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated February 23, 2023.)  In another order, the Court found that one attorney for Defendants sent “correspondence that was argumentative and disrespectful, which is counterproductive to resolving discovery disputes.”  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated July 5, 2023.)  Moreover, there have been unsubstantiated allegations.  One attorney for Defendants told a witness’s attorney that Plaintiff “has been trying to hire people to purchase guns and harm our client” and suggested that Plaintiff’s counsel should “place her client on notice to cease and desist her actions to hire hitmen and attempt to harm civilians.”  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated September 6, 2023, at p. 2.)  Plaintiff’s counsel accused the friend of a witness of threatening harm to Plaintiff.  (Ibid.)  Based upon the foregoing, the Court ordered a stay as a “cooling off” period.  “The Court is concerned by the parties’ conduct, and this case has taken on a level of acrimony that is counterproductive to the litigation and resolution.”  (Id., p. 1.)  The Court ordered all depositions to be conducted remotely for the safety of the parties unless the parties stipulate otherwise.  (Id., p. 3.)   

 

            The Court also has concerns that Plaintiff was engaged in gamesmanship in not sitting for her deposition before the Court’s order of September 6, 2023.  Defendants served a notice of deposition on October 27, 2022, and noticed the deposition for November 22, 2022.  At the hearing on February 14, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel “conceded that her client must appear for a deposition and suggested that the Court order the deposition and set a 45-day deadline.”  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated February 14, 2023.)  The Court did so.  Yet, Plaintiff’s deposition still has not occurred.  Plaintiff has cited ongoing health issues and frequent international travel, but the record does not support a delay of this length.  The Court also notes that Defendants’ unclean hands in the conduct of this litigation may have contributed to this delay as well.      

 

            On September 6, 2023, the Court ordered that Plaintiff’s deposition would occur between December 6, 2023, and January 5, 2024.  The Court ordered: “If Plaintiff will be unable to comply with this order, the Court orders Plaintiff’s counsel to file declarations and supporting medical documentation on or before November 6, 2023, explaining why she cannot sit for her deposition.”  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated September 6, 2023, p. 3.)  No such documentation was filed.  The Court ordered that the depositions of Defendants Shawn Yadidi, Navid Zad, and Promed Medical Supplies shall occur after Plaintiff’s deposition.  (Id., pp. 2-3.)  The Court set a deposition scheduling conference on December 6, 2023, at 8:30 a.m.  The hearing was continued due to a death in the judge’s family.  (See Court’s Minute Order, dated November 29, 2023.)

 

            Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to schedule the deposition.  (See Declaration of Heather L. McCloskey, Exh. A.)        On December 5, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendants’ counsel to inquire whether he was available to conduct Plaintiff’s deposition on December 18, 19, or 20, 2023.  Receiving no reply, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed again on December 8, 2023, to inquire whether those dates would work.  Defendants’ counsel asked for dates in “mid to late January,” even though those dates were after the deadline set by the Court (January 5, 2024).  On December 10, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel responded that her client was available between December 11 and 21, 2023.  Again, Defendants’ counsel did not respond.  On December 15, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel again emailed to inquire when he would like to take Plaintiff’s deposition.  Again, Defendants’ counsel asked for dates in “mid to late January,” even though those dates were after the deadline set by the Court (January 5, 2024).  On December 20, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed and offered January 4 or January 5, 2024.  Defendants’ counsel did not respond.  Then, on January 1, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed to inform him of a conflict on the morning of January 5, 2024, but offered to begin at 1:00 p.m. and then seek an extension of the deadline to finish the deposition.  Again, Defendants’ counsel asked for dates in “mid to late January” even though those dates were after the deadline set by the Court (January 5, 2024). 

 

            Based upon the correspondence, it is clear that Defendants’ counsel is responsible for the failure to take Plaintiff’s deposition.  The Court ordered the deposition to occur between December 6, 2023, and January 5, 2024.  Plaintiff’s counsel offered him numerous dates between December 11, 2023, and January 5, 2024.  Plaintiff’s counsel was proactive in emailing Defendants’ counsel, and he twice failed to respond: (1) Defendants’ counsel failed to respond to the email of December 8, 2023, necessitating a follow-up email on December 10, 2023; and (2) Defendants’ counsel failed to respond to the email of December 20, 2023, until January 1, 2024. 

 

            The Court is baffled by Defendants’ counsel’s approach to this issue.  Defendants’ counsel—who purportedly wants to take Plaintiff’s deposition—made no apparent effort to schedule the deposition.  Instead, Plaintiff’s counsel took the initiative.  The Court set a deadline for Plaintiff’s deposition, which was January 5, 2024.  Yet, Defendants’ counsel repeatedly asked for dates after the Court-ordered deadline and never sought to extend this deadline.  Even in his opposition, he does not acknowledge or address this deadline.  The Court gave some consideration to finding that Defendants have waived the right to take Plaintiff’s deposition, but declines to do so.  Plaintiff is not seeking that remedy and still is willing to sit for her deposition.  Also, the Court’s unexpected absence on December 6, 2023, prevented the Court from setting the deposition date, which would have circumvented this dispute.  Therefore, the Court will give Defendants’ counsel the benefit of these issues. 

 

Based upon the foregoing, the Court orders as follows:

 

1.         Plaintiff’s ex parte application is granted. 

 

2.         The Court orders the deposition of Plaintiff to occur remotely on Friday, March 29, 2024, starting at 9:00 a.m.  The parties may not change the date/time/location of this deposition unless they file a stipulation and proposed order.  The Court provides notice: If Plaintiff’s deposition does not go forward based upon the conduct of Defendants or their counsel, the Court will find that they have waived the right to take Plaintiff’s deposition.   

 

3.         The Court advances and continues the hearing on Defendants’ motion for terminating sanctions from February 14, 2024, to May 2, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. 

 

4.         The Court orders the parties to file a joint statement on or before April 2, 2024, informing the Court: (a) Whether the deposition was completed and the motion for terminating sanctions is off-calendar; or (b) Whether the deposition was not completed and the Court must decide the motion for terminating sanctions.

 

5.         If the Court must decide the motion for terminating sanctions, Defendants may file a supplement to the motion to reference any additional grounds for terminating sanctions that occurred after the motion was first filed (e.g., any failure to comply with this order, etc.)  Plaintiff may file a supplemental opposition, and Defendants may file a supplemental reply brief.  All supplemental briefing shall be due based upon statutory deadlines for the hearing date of May 2, 2024. 

 

6.         The Court orders the depositions of Shawn Yadidi, Navid Zad, and Promed Medical Supplies to be conducted on April 8, 9, and 10, 2024.  The depositions shall be conducted in the following order:

 

            a.         Shawn Yadidi on April __, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.

 

            b.         Navid Zad on April __, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.

 

            c.         Promed Medical Supplies on April __, 2024, at 9:00 a.m.

 

These depositions shall be conducted remotely.  The parties may not change the date/time/location of the depositions unless they file a stipulation and proposed order.  The Court provides notice: If these depositions do not go forward based upon the conduct of Plaintiff or her counsel, the Court will find that they have waived the right to take Defendants’ depositions.

 

            7.         Given the conduct of the parties, the Court issues an Order to Show Cause why it should not appoint a discovery referee to preside over the depositions under Code of Civil Procedure section 639.  The OSC hearing shall be held on February 14, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  The parties may file a response to this OSC at least nine (9) court days in advance of the hearing.

 

            8.         Plaintiff’s counsel shall provide notice and file proof of such with the Court.
