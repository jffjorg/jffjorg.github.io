[REVISED TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING AMENDED PETITION TO APPROVE COMPROMISE OF DISPUTED CLAIM WITHOUT PREJUDICE AND CONTINUING HEARING

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

November 5, 2024

 

On August 13, 2024, counsel for Plaintiffs filed a Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim.  On August 22, 2024, the Court issued an order that detailed the Petition’s many deficiencies and continued the hearing so an amended petition could be filed no later than September 3, 2024.  Plaintiffs did not timely file an amended petition.  Plaintiffs did file a late amended petition on September 5, 2024.  The amended petition remained deficient for many of the same reasons as the original petition.  The Court posted a tentative ruling before the September 10, 2024 hearing that again detailed the Petition’s many deficiencies.  In light of that tentative ruling, Plaintiffs’ counsel represented to the Court that an amended petition would be filed to cure the defects of the previous Petitions.  The Court therefore did not adopt the tentative and continued the hearing.

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed a new Amended Petition.  This Amended Petition continues to have some of the same deficiencies.  Counsel’s inability to complete the necessary amendments is unduly delaying approval of the settlement.  The Court will again detail the ongoing deficiencies in the Amended Petition.

COMPROMISE OF CLAIM

Claimant Isaias Cervantes, a person with disabilities (by and through his parent, guardian ad litem, and conservator Rosa Padilla), has agreed to settle his claims against Defendant County of Los Angeles in exchange for $25 million.  Of this amount, $105,573.75 is allocated for a Medi-Cal lien, $9.25 million is allocated for attorney fees, and $228,601.70 is allocated for costs and expenses, leaving a balance of $15,415,824.55 for Claimant.  (This is not the same as the $14,855,824.60 balance calculated in Item 15 of the Petition.)  If approved, a portion of the funds will be paid to the guardian or conservator of the estate and a portion of the funds will be transferred to the trustee of a special needs trust.

Court approval is required for all settlements of the claims of people with disabilities.  (Probate Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.)  The Court has reviewed the proposed settlement and cannot approve it at this time.

Item 15 does not correctly state the balance of proceeds for the Claimant.

Item 11(b)(5) states that Plaintiff Rosa Padilla will receive $500,000 and Plaintiff Yajaira Cervantes will receive $60,000.  Item 11(b)(1) does state that the total amount offered by all defendants to others is $560,000.  The settlement agreement states that Defendant will pay $25 million “in full and final settlement of any and all claims by the Plaintiffs against the Defendants.”  It therefore appears that Plaintiff Rosa Padilla and Plaintiff Yajaira Cervantes will receive a portion of the $25 million settlement, and $25 million is not the amount of Claimant’s own settlement for approval.  (See Item 10(a).)  This is not what the Petition states in Items 10 and 11.

Counsel’s declarations in support of the reasonableness of attorney fees do not adequately support the request for $9.25 million (37%) in attorney fees.  Although Claimant’s parent, guardian ad litem, and conservator contracted with counsel for attorney fees of forty-percent of the settlement recovery, $9.25 million is excessive in this case.

Counsel Dove provides a spreadsheet summarizing billing records for his 4,250 hours.  (Dove Decl. ¶ 5 & Ex. 12.)  He calculates his fees based on an hourly rate of $1,275 and a lodestar multiplier of 1.7 or 1.8.  (Dove Decl. ¶ 58.)  A significant amount time billed (321 hours) was for the criminal action in Case No. BA499677, not this case.  (Dove Decl., Ex. 12.)

Counsel Doyle provides a spreadsheet summarizing billing records for his 169.72 hours.  (Doyle Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. 1; see Dove Decl. ¶ 58.)  Counsel Dove calculates his fees based on an hourly rate of $1,275 and a lodestar multiplier of 1.8.  (Dove Decl. ¶ 58.)

Counsel Contreras provides sufficiently detailed information for this Court to determine whether his 393.95 hours were reasonably spent.  (Contreras Decl. ¶¶ 3-47.)  However, the Court cannot determine whether his fees are reasonable.  Counsel declares that “the amount of the fee is xxxxx.”  (Contreras Decl. ¶ 49.)  Counsel Dove calculates his fees based on an hourly rate of $650 and a lodestar multiplier of 1.75.  (Dove Decl. ¶ 58.)

Despite the detailed declarations from counsel, as well as declarations from non-parties who testify to the quality of counsel’s work, the Court finds that both the hourly rates and lodestar multipliers are excessive.  The total amount of time spent by all counsel (without deducting the 321 hours for the criminal action in Case No. BA499677) is 4,813.67 hours.  Based on the requested fees of $9.25 million, this equals an exorbitant hourly rate of approximately $1,921.61.  Applying counsel’s highest hourly rate ($1,275) to all hours results in a total award of a little less than $6.14 million.

Based on the evidence presented (including counsel’s declarations addressing the factors listed in California Rules of Court, rule 7.955), the Court finds that a reasonable amount of attorneys fees is $2,246,335.  The Court calculated this total based on a reasonable hourly rate of $500 for 4,492.67 hours (4,813.67 minus 321 for the criminal case).

Additionally, the Proposed Order is incomplete except for the hearing information and identity of the parties.  The Proposed Order must include the settlement figures, fees, liens, and other information, including language approving the funding of the special needs trust.

On December 19, 2022, The Greenspan Co., aka The Greenspan Co./Adjustors International Inc. (“Greenspan”) filed this action against Jennifer McCullum.  The Complaint alleges that McCullum failed to pay for Greenspan’s services in obtaining insurance proceeds for her.

On December 6, 2023, McCullum filed a Cross-Complaint against Greenspan, Greenspan Adjusters International Inc. (“Greenspan Adjusters”), Adjusters International/California Inc. (“Adjusters California”), Matthew Blumkin, Matthew Goldstein, Sydney Greenspan, Etehad Law APC, and Simon Etehad.  The Cross-Complaint alleges (1) breach of fiduciary duty; (2) fraud, deceit, and concealment; (3) constructive fraud; (4) conversion; (5) civil theft; (6) accounting; (7) money had and received; (8) declaratory relief (against Greenspan only); (9) negligence; and (10) restitution.

On February 23, 2024, Greenspan Adjusters filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

On March 1, 2024, Etehad Law APC and Simon Etehad (collectively, “Etehad”) filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

LEGAL STANDARD FOR DEMURRER

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce ex rel. Arce v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 556.)

GREENSPAN ADJUSTERS’ DEMURRER

Greenspan Adjusters’ request for judicial notice of three filings in In The Matter Of Greenspan Adjusters International, Inc., NPN: 7812070, is granted in part.  In doing so, the Court takes judicial notice of the documents’ existence and the issues presented to the New Mexico Office of Superintendent of Insurance (“OSI”), and not the truth of the contents as fact.  “While courts take judicial notice of public records, they do not take notice of the truth of matters stated therein.  [Citation.]  ‘When judicial notice is taken of a document, . . . the truthfulness and proper interpretation of the document are disputable.’  [Citation.]”  (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.)

A.        The Cross-Complaint Does Not Sufficiently Allege Agency.

Greenspan Adjusters demurs to all causes of action because McCullum fails to allege duty or agency.  (Demurrer at pp. 8-10.)

Greenspan Adjusters relies on the OSI ruling that established “(1) that no contract existed between GAII and McCullum, but rather McCullum’s public adjuster contract was with The Greenspan Company, (2) McCullum never has done business with GAII, and (3) the public adjusters that McCullum alleged worked on her claim, Matthew Blumpkin, and Sydney Greenspan, were never employed by GAII.”  (Demurrer at p. 9.)  This requires the Court to accept the truth of the contents of the RJN exhibits, not just their existence.  At most, the Court can consider the that the OSI’s ruling dismissed the Order to Show Cause, struck all hearing dates, and cancelled all appointments.  (RJN, Ex. C. at p. 3.)  Greenspan Adjusters’ argument is outside the scope of demurrer.

Greenspan Adjusters also argues that the allegations about its involvement are entirely predicated on the allegation that Greenspan Adjusters “was the agent, employee, partner, and representative of each of the other Cross-Defendants.”  (Demurrer at p. 10; see Cross-Complaint ¶ 11.)  “An allegation of agency constitutes an averment of ultimate fact, which we accept as true on a demurrer.  [Citations.]  But where factual allegations are based on information and belief, the plaintiff must allege ‘information that “lead[s] [the plaintiff] to believe that the allegations are true.”’  [Citation.]”  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1106.)  The Cross-Complaint alleges that “Cross-Complainant is informed and believes, and thereon alleges, that, at all times herein mentioned, each of the Cross-Defendants was the agent, employee, partner, and representative of each of the other Cross-Defendants, and in doing the things herein alleged, was acting within the authorized course and scope of this agency, employment, partnership, and representation with the full knowledge and consent of the remaining Cross-Defendants.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 11.)  There are no facts about information that leads McCullum to believe that the allegations are true.

The demurrer is sustained on this ground.

B.        The Cross-Complaint Does Not Sufficiently Allege Breach of Fiduciary Duty (First Cause of Action).

A cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty requires (1) a fiduciary duty, (2) breach of the duty, and (3) damage caused by the breach.  (Charnay v. Cobert¿(2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.)  “A fiduciary relationship is ‘ “ ‘any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is in duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.  Such a relation ordinarily arises where a confidence is reposed by one person in the integrity of another, and in such a relation the party in whom the confidence is reposed, if he voluntarily accepts or assumes to accept the confidence, can take no advantage from his acts relating to the interest of the other party without the latter’s knowledge or consent . . . . ’”’”  (Wolf v. Superior Court (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 25, 29.)

McCullum alleges that the cross-defendants breached their fiduciary duties by concealing material facts concerning the insurance policy, refusing to act in McCullum’s interests, engaging in covert deals amongst themselves and with others to perpetrate illicit acts and transactions, and forging documents, among other things.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 40.)  However, there are no specific allegations about Greenspan Adjusters’ fiduciary relationship with McCullum, aside from the general agency allegations.  (See Demurrer at pp. 10-11.)

The demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained.

C.        The Cross-Complaint Does Not Allege Fraud with Specificity (Second and Third Causes of Action).

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.  [Citations.]  The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.  [Citations.]”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231.)

Fraud based on concealment requires that “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.”  (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310-311.)

Fraud must be pleaded with specificity.  (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.)  “‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’  [Citation.]  A plaintiff’s burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater.  In such a case, the plaintiff must ‘allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.’  [Citation.]”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

McCullum alleges that the cross-defendants “knew that none of them had the licenses required under applicable law to undertake the Engagement,” but they expressly represented that they did during a January 28, 2021 phone call.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 16.)  “On repeat occasions, orally and in writing,” the cross-defendants continued to misrepresent that they were and had been properly licensed to undertake the Engagement.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 17.)  The cross-defendants also concealed conflicts of interests, concealed and evaded McCullum’s inquiries to them concerning the facts that they were unlicensed, and concealed denials of policy benefits.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 23-25.)  However, the Cross-Complaint does not contain factual allegations about who made the statements or omissions, their authority to speak, or Greenspan Adjusters’ duty to disclose the facts.  (See Demurrer at pp. 11-12.)

The demurrer to the second cause of action is sustained.

Similarly, the third cause of action for constructive fraud also fails.  “The elements of the cause of action for constructive fraud are: (1) fiduciary relationship; (2) nondisclosure (breach of fiduciary duty); (3) intent to deceive, and (4) reliance and resulting injury (causation).”  (Younan v. Equifax Inc. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 498, 516, fn. 14.)  The Cross-Complaint does not allege specific facts to support these elements.

The demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained.

D.        Conclusion

Greenspan Adjusters’ demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

GREENSPAN ADJUSTERS’ MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

Greenspan Adjusters moves to strike portions of paragraphs 43, 48, 61, and 68, as well as the prayer for punitive damages.

A plaintiff can recover punitive damages in tort cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort.  (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-895.)  A corporate employer can be liable for punitive damages only when an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b).)  The Cross-Complaint’s conclusory allegations are insufficient.

The motion to strike is GRANTED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

ETEHAD’S DEMURRER

A.        The Cross-Complaint Sufficiently Alleges a Fiduciary Relationship (First Cause of Action).

Etehad argues that there are no allegations of a fiduciary relationship.  (Demurrer at pp. 3-4.)

“When an attorney receives money on behalf of a third party who is not his client, he nevertheless is a fiduciary as to such third party.  Thus the funds in his possession are impressed with a trust, and his conversion of such funds is a breach of the trust.”  (Johnstone v. State Bar (1966) 64 Cal.2d 153, 155-156.)  That is what is alleged here.  The Cross-Complaint alleges that the Greenspan Cross-Defendants received a check from State Farm (for the benefit of McCullum), they delivered it to Etehad for deposit into their bank account, all cross-defendants fraudulently endorsed the check, and they concealed the receipt of the check.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 30-34.)

The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

B.        The Cross-Complaint Does Not Allege Fraud with Specificity (Second and Third Causes of Action).

Etehad argues that the fraud-based claims do not allege fraud with specificity.  (Demurrer at p. 4.)

McCullum alleges that the cross-defendants “knew that none of them had the licenses required under applicable law to undertake the Engagement,” but they expressly represented that they did during a January 28, 2021 phone call.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 16.)  “On repeat occasions, orally and in writing,” the cross-defendants continued to misrepresent that they were and had been properly licensed to undertake the Engagement.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 17.)  The cross-defendants also concealed conflicts of interests, concealed and evaded McCullum’s inquiries to them concerning the facts that they were unlicensed, and concealed denials of policy benefits.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 23-25.)  However, the Cross-Complaint does not contain factual allegations about who made the statements or omissions and their authority to speak.

The demurrer to the second and third causes of action is sustained.

C.        Civil Theft Does Not Allege Sufficient Facts, But Conversion and Unjust Enrichment are Sufficient (Fourth, Fifth, and Tenth Causes of Action).

Etehad argues that the property-related causes of action allege only conclusions of law.  (Demurrer at pp. 4-5.)

Conversion requires (1) plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property, (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages.  (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119)  McCullum argues that the cross-defendants “wrongfully received and retained monies that were owed to and owned by Cross-Complainant” and “exerted wrongful dominion over the property of the Cross-Complainant.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 64-65.)  Specifically, Etehad deposited the check into their own account, fraudulently endorsed it, and concealed it from McCullum.  This sufficiently alleges facts about Etehad’s disposition of McCullum’s property.  The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

Civil theft requires that “(i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property.”  (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)  The Cross-Complaint alleges that Etehad obtained McCullum’s property “by theft, on false pretenses, through embezzlement, through forgery, and through larceny, and knowingly withholding the property from the Plaintiff and aiding and abetting in obtaining, concealing, and knowingly withholding said property.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 70.)  This is conclusory and does not allege knowledge of stolen property.  The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained.

“The elements of an unjust enrichment claim are the ‘receipt of a benefit and [the] unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.’  [Citation.]”  (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593.)  Unjust enrichment is not a cause of action, but a court may construe a cause of action for unjust enrichment as a cause of action for quasi-contract seeking restitution.  (Rutherford Holdings, LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 231.)  Like with the conversion claim, there are sufficient allegations of Etehad’s receipt and retention of McCullum’s funds.  The demurrer to the tenth cause of action is overruled.

D.        The Cross-Complaint Does Not Sufficiently Allege Accounting (Sixth Cause of Action).

“A cause of action for accounting requires a showing of a relationship between the plaintiff and the defendant . . . that requires an accounting or a showing that the accounts are so complicated they cannot be determined through an ordinary action at law.”  (Fleet v. Bank of America N.A. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1413.)

The Cross-Complaint alleges that “[t]he accounts relating to the Policy, Policy benefits, and related funds, including the $500k Check, are so complicated that an ordinary legal action demanding a fixed sum is insufficient,” and “Cross–Defendants are in possession of certain books, records, and statements for and relating to these matters.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 75-76.)  However, McCullum also alleges that Etehad received and deposited the $499,770.00 check from State Farm.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 30-32.)  There are no facts, instead of a mere conclusion, about the matter being so complicated that an accounting is necessary.  (See Demurrer at p. 6.)

The demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained.

E.        The Cross-Complaint Sufficiently Alleges Money Had and Received (Seventh Cause of Action).

“A cause of action for money had and received is stated if it is alleged the defendant ‘is indebted to the plaintiff in a certain sum “for money had and received by the defendant for the use of the plaintiff.”’  [Citation.]”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)

The Cross-Complaint alleges that Etehad received and deposited the $499,770.00 check from State Farm.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 30-32.)  The cross-defendants “received money that was intended to be used for the benefit of Cross-Complainant,” the money was not used for her benefit, and the cross-defendants owe the money.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 79-82.)  This sufficiently alleges money had and received.

The demurrer to the seventh cause of action is overruled.

F.         The Cross-Complaint Does Not Sufficiently Allege Negligence (Ninth Cause of Action).

A cause of action for negligence requires (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) causation, and (4) damage to the plaintiff.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)

The Cross-Complaint alleges that Etehad owes a duty to McCullum under Civil Code section 1714, which provides that “[e]veryone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 91.)  The cross-defendants “engaged in negligence in that they failed to exercise ordinary care under the circumstances in their dealings with Cross-Complainant, to Cross-Complainant’s detriment.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 92.)  This is conclusory, reciting some elements of negligence without alleging facts.  As Etehad argues, “There is no way, from the face of the Cross-Complaint, for the Etehad Cross-Defendants to have any idea what they allegedly did that falls within the ambit of Civil Code §1714(a). . . . There is no answer to be found in the pleading.”  (Demurrer at pp. 6-7.)

The demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained.

G.        Conclusion

The demurrer to the second, third, fifth, sixth, and ninth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

The demurrer is otherwise OVERRULED.

ETEHAD’S MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

Etehad moves to strike paragraphs 39-43, 63-68, 70-73, 75-76, 83, 95-96, and 97, as well as the second and third causes of action on the grounds that “: the matter is irrelevant, false or improper, as no facts supporting this allegation have been alleged as to Etehad Law, APC or Simon Etehad.”  This is duplicative of demurrer and not proper for a motion to strike.

The motion to strike is denied.

CONCLUSION

Greenspan Adjusters’ demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.  Greenspan Adjusters’ motion to strike is GRANTED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

Etehad’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED IN PART as set forth above, with 30 days’ leave to amend.  Etehad’s motion to strike is DENIED.

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff Isabel Kilroe served Requests For Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories, and Requests for Admission on Defendant FCA US LLC.  On May 3 and 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed motions to compel.  Defendant served untimely responses on May 27, 2024, shortly before the May 30, 2024 hearing.  Although the motions were then moot, the Court ordered Defendant to pay monetary sanctions to Plaintiff.

On June 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendant’s compliance with its responses to RFP Nos. 1-8.  The Court’s July 16, 2024 order noted that Defendant’s responses declaring that it would comply in full were made on May 27, 2024, but Defendant still did not produce any documents.  The Court granted the unopposed motion and awarded monetary sanctions to Plaintiff.

Plaintiff followed up with Defendant about document production on July 25, July 31, August 12, and August 20, 2024.  (Inscore Decl. ¶ 8.)  On August 21, 2024, Defendant sent an empty link and claimed that it was unaware of the Court’s order, which Plaintiff’s counsel re-supplied that day. (Inscore Decl. ¶ 9.)  Defendant then produced documents responsive to RFP Nos. 2, 4, and 5.  (Inscore Decl. ¶ 9.)  On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff followed up again to request supplementation.  (Inscore Decl. ¶ 10.)

At Defendant’s September 9, 2024 deposition, Plaintiff discovered that Defendant failed to produce the correct warranty, the correct owner’s manual, diagnostic materials, and communications logs.  (Inscore Decl. ¶¶ 14-17.)  Plaintiff followed up by email on September 9 and 17, 2024, but Defendant failed to supplement its production.  (Inscore Decl. ¶ 19.)

The parties were not ready for trial at the September 16, 2024 Final Status Conference, so the Court continued the Final Status Conference and trial dates.  The Court did not reset any discovery and motion deadlines.

On October 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to reopen discovery and for sanctions against Defendant.  Plaintiff seeks issue, evidentiary, and monetary sanctions.  Defendant did not file any opposition.

DISCUSSION

A court may impose an issue sanction ordering that designated facts must be taken as established in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).)  A court may also impose an issue sanction prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.  (Ibid.)

Defendant has still failed to produce (1) the warranty; (2) the owner’s manual; (3) vehicle diagnostic evidence; (4) call logs that identify witnesses; (5) policies for dealers; (6) lemon law policies; and (7) call center policies.  (Inscore Decl. ¶ 4.)

Plaintiff argues that she cannot prevail without proving that Defendant provided a warranty that was breached.  (Motion at p. 8.)  Plaintiff also argues that she is entitled to the owner’s manual defining the proper use and care of the high voltage battery and the diagnostic evidence, if Defendant claims it is defective.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff therefore seeks an order that it be taken as established that “Plaintiff’s vehicle had a defect covered by the warranty that substantially impaired the vehicle’s use, value, or safety to a reasonable buyer in her situation.”  (Id. at pp. ii, 10.)  This issue sanction would affirmatively answer CACI verdict form at question 3: “Did the vehicle have a defect covered by the warranty that substantially impaired the vehicle’s use, value, or safety to a reasonable [buyer/lessee] in [name of plaintiff]’s situation?”  (Id. at pp. 8-9.)

Plaintiff also argues that Defendant’s withholding of the persons at its call center, witnesses, and policies that it imposes on call center workers and its dealerships prevents her from being able to prove Defendant’s willfulness.  (Motion at p. 9.)  Plaintiff therefore seeks an order that it be taken as established that “Defendant willfully failed to repurchase or replace Plaintiff’s vehicle.”  (Id. at pp. ii, 10.)  This issue sanction would affirmatively answer CACI verdict form at question 7: “Did [name of defendant] willfully fail to repurchase or replace the [new motor vehicle]?”  (Id. at p. 9.)

Additionally, Plaintiff seeks an order prohibiting Defendant from attempting to introduce evidence or argument to contradict the findings of the issue sanctions.  (Motion at p. 9.)

Defendant still has not produced necessary documents in response to Plaintiff’s March 22, 2024, despite the Court’s July 16, 2024 order.  This continued through the September 16, 2024 Final Status Conference, when the parties were supposed to be prepared for the September 30, 2024 trial.  Now, with the trial scheduled for January 27, 2025, Defendant is still obstructing Plaintiff from being able to present her case.  Accordingly, the Court will impose sanctions on Defendant.

CONCLUSION

The motion is GRANTED IN PART.

The Court ORDERS that it must be taken as established that (1) Plaintiff’s vehicle had a defect covered by the warranty that substantially impaired the vehicle’s use, value, or safety to a reasonable buyer in her situation; and (2) Defendant willfully failed to repurchase or replace Plaintiff’s vehicle.  Defendant may not introduce evidence or argument to contradict the findings of these issue sanctions.

The request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED.  Defendant is ordered to pay sanctions of $6,560.00 to Plaintiff within 30 days.

The request for an order reopening the discovery motion cutoff is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.  (See Motion at pp. 4-5.)

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND COMPLAINT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

November 5, 2024

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff Commercial Scaffolding of California Inc. filed this action against Defendants Second Baptist Church of Los Angeles and U.S. Small Business Administration.  Plaintiff later dismissed U.S. Small Business Administration.  On August 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for leave to file a first amended complaint (“FAC”).

The Court may, in its discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow an amendment to any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  A motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments and must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the allegations are located.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)  The motion shall also be accompanied by a declaration attesting to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)  The Court does not ordinarily consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading when determining whether to grant leave to amend.  (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.)

Plaintiff identifies the amendments and provides a copy of the proposed FAC.  Plaintiff seeks to correct the address for the mechanic’s lien and remove now-dismissed U.S. Small Business Administration as a defendant.  Plaintiff discovered the incorrect address on July 11, 2024 and has recorded a corrected lien.  The amendment is necessary to ensure the correct property address and the current parties.  (See Amended Boruszewski Decl.)

Because there is no showing of prejudice, the unopposed motion for leave to amend the complaint is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve the FAC within 10 days.
