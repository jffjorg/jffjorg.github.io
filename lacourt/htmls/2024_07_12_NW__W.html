DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20VECV00347    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: W

PEARLIE KATE MILLER, JR. V. JANE DOE, ET AL.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO CHANGE JURISDICTION FOR PURPOSES OF COLLECTION OF JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        July 12, 2024                                                  Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        20VECV00347

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Pearlie Kate Miller

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Change Jurisdiction for Purposes of Collection of Judgment is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff requests for a change in jurisdiction for the purposes of satisfying the judgment entered for Plaintiff Pearlie Kate Miller and against Defendant Tiffany Warren on the grounds Ms. Warren is a resident of Fairfield County, Ohio.

 

This is not a basis for a motion to change jurisdictions or transfer venue. The court has already entered judgment in Plaintiff’s favor. If Plaintiff wishes to enforce the California judgment in Ohio State (i.e., domesticate the California or “foreign state” judgment in Ohio), Plaintiff will need to petition the Ohio State Court. The relevant statutes in Ohio are outlined in Ohio Revised Code 2329.022 and 2329.023.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Change Jurisdiction for Purposes of Collection of Judgment is DENIED.

 


Case Number: 22VECV01536    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: W

ALEN COHEN, et al. v. MOSHE ZEMACH, et al.

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE FURTHER DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT LIVINIT UP, LLC’S PERSON(S) MOST QUALIFIED AND PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

Date of Hearing:        July 12, 2024                                                  Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        22VECV01536

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Alen Cohen

Responding Party:     Defendant Livinit Up, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 11, 2022, Plaintiffs Alen Cohen and Nastran Cohen filed a complaint against Defendants Moshe Zemach, David Zemach, Maya Librush, Oded Zemach, Livinit Up, LLC, UrbinoConstruction Services LLC, IO Lifetime Builders, Inc., Chai Group, LLC, Serrano Demolition, Inc., Expert Decking and Waterproofing Experts, Inc. and The Ohio Casualty Insurance Company, asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Contract; (3) Negligence; (4) Negligence; (5) Negligence; (6) Breach of Express Warranty; (7) Breach of Implied Warranty; (8) Violations of Civil Code Sections 895, et seq.; (9) Intentional Misrepresentation; (10) Negligent Misrepresentation; (11) Promissory Fraud; (12) Breach of Contract; (13) Breach of Contract; (14) Recovery on Contractor’s Bond; (15) Declaratory Relief; and (16) Rescission. Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint on March 21, 2023. On June 20, 2023, Plaintiffs dismissed their ninth and tenth causes of action against Oded Zemach, Urbinoconstruction Services, LLC, IO Lifetime Builders, Inc., and Chai Group LLC.

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants LivinIt Up, LLC, Moshe Zemach, David Zemach, Maya Librush, Oded Zemach, UrbinoConstruction Services, LLC, IO Lifetime Builders, Inc., and Chai Group, LLC (“Zemach Defendants”) misrepresented the condition of certain real property sold to Plaintiff. The Zemach Defendants purchased the real property in 2015 and had substantially remodeled the property. Plaintiffs further allege Serrano Demolition was not a licensed contractor nor otherwise qualified to perform the required work and caused damaged to the property. Plaintiffs further allege Defendant Expert Decking and Waterproofing not only delayed substantial completion of its scope of work, Defendant Expert Decking also defectively and negligently performed its work causing Plaintiffs to sustain even further damages.

 

On January 3, 2023, Expert Decking and Watering Proofing, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Roes 1 -100 for (1) equitable indemnity; (2) contribution; (3) comparative fault; and (4) declaratory relief.

 

The Ohio Casualty Insurance Company filed a cross-complaint on February 24, 2023 against Expert Decking, Alen Cohen, and Nastran Cohen for interpleader.

 

Moshe Zemach, David Zemach, and Livinit Up filed a cross-complaint against Serrano Demolition, Inc., Expert Decking and Water Proofing, Inc. and James West on April 21, 2023 for equitable indemnity, contribution, declaratory relief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel the Further Deposition of Defendant Livinit Up, LLC’s Person(s) Most Qualified and Production of Documents is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Alen Cohen moves the court for an order compelling Defendant Livinit Up, LLC to produce for oral examination its person(s) most qualified, who are adequately prepared to testify on all 93 topics of examination; conduct a diligent search and reasonable inquiry for all documents responsive to the Notice’s Requests for Production of Documents No’s. 1-37 and produce all responsive documents by July 19, 2024; agree to produce Moshe Zemach for his continued oral examination either July 24, 2024 or July 26, 2034 (after the production of all responsive documents); serve complete, verified code-compliant responses to the Notice, including each of the Requests, without any objections; and require Defendant’s counsel to affirm, under oath and on July 19, 2024, that no documents have been withheld from production and attesting to his and his client’s efforts to locate and produce all documents responsive to the Requests. Plaintiff also seeks an order imposing monetary sanctions in the amount of $18,725.00.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480 provides in relevant part that “[i]f a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document…under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (CCP §2025.480(a).) The motion must be made “no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (CCP §2025.480(b); see also Weinstein v. Blumberg (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 316, 321 [the 60-day deadline is mandatory].) Not less than five days prior to the hearing on the motion, the moving party “shall lodge with the court a certified copy of any parts of the stenographic transcript of the deposition that are relevant to the motion.” (CCP §2025.480(h).)¿ 

 

The court notes Defendant’s initial argument in opposition to the motion to compel further deposition. Defendant avers the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ, Moshe Zemach, took place on April 1, 2024 and was signed by the reporter on April 8, 2024. (Hanigan Decl., Exh. 1.) Accordingly, Defendant contends the deposition record was completed on April 8, 2024 and the mandatory provision of Section 2025.480 would have required the motion be made no later than 60 days after April 8, 2024 – June 7, 2024. Plaintiff, however, filed the motion June 14, 2024.

 

Plaintiff argues that the deposition was complete, at the earliest, on April 15, 2024, when the court reporter first sent written notice to the parties that the deposition transcript was available for review. (See CCP §2025.520; 15. [8:787] Motion to Compel Answers: Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 8E-15.) The court agrees. Section 2025.480(b) states that the motion “shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  The deposition was held on April 1, 2024. The court reporter sent notice under CCP section 2025.520 that the transcript was ready for review on April 15, 2024. The thirty days for making changes to the transcript under Section 2025.520 would have expired on May 15, 2024. That being the latest date for completion of the record of the deposition, sixty days later would have been July 15, 2024. Defendant filed its motion on June 14, 2024. Accordingly, the motion is TIMELY.

 

Going to the merits of the motion, Plaintiff argues Mr. Zemach was not properly qualified to testify on Defendant’s behalf on the 63 Topics. Mr. Zemach testified that he was the PMQ for only 16 out of the 93 topics and to date, Defendant has not designated or produced for deposition any other person qualified to testify on its behalf as to the outstanding topics. Moreover, Mr. Zemach was not adequately prepared to testify at the deposition. Mr. Zemach’s testimony demonstrates that he failed to review documents and other information reasonably available to him in advance of his deposition. Plaintiff lastly argues Defendant did not object to the Notice, including any of the Requests, and did not produce a single document at the April 1 deposition. Moreover, because Defendant did not serve any responses to the Notice, Plaintiff has no way of knowing whether Defendant conducted a diligent search and reasonable inquiry for each of the documents asked for in the Requests, or whether any of the requested documents ever existed, or are no longer in Defendant’s possession, custody or control because they were destroyed, lost, misplaced, or stolen.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues in May 2023, Defendant produced document nos. 1-934. On April 17, 2024, two weeks after the deposition, Defendant supplemented its document production by producing documents nos. 935-1311. Accordingly, there are no other responsive documents to produce. As for witnesses, Defendant contends at the end of the deposition, Mr. Hanigan (counsel for Defendant) stated that Defendant would produce David Zemach, Moshe’s son, to testify as to any matters which were unknown by his father. However, due to calendar conflicts on all sides, picking a mutually convenient date has been difficult.  Because Defendant has produced all responsive documents in its possession, and has always been willing to produce David Zemach for deposition, on a mutually convenient day for all parties and counsel, Defendant contends the instant motion should be denied.

 

Based on the record before the court, the court compels the further deposition of Defendant’s PMQ. It is not surprising an expansive PMQ request of 63 Topics could not be answered by one individual. However, Defendant should have been aware of this and conferred with Plaintiff that Moshe could not answer all 63 Topics. It seems the parties dispute whether the April 29, 2024 date was actually confirmed for David Zemach’s deposition. Regardless, Defendant has still failed to produce David Zemach for deposition. Moreover, Mr. Zemach admitted to only reviewing the interrogatories answered by him in preparation for the deposition.

 

The parties are to work together to confirm what documents have already been produced and what needs further production.  Defendant must confirm whether a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been done for all responsive documents. Moreover, the court orders Mr. Moshe to be produced for any remaining topics that he failed to answer that were in his purview and that Zemach be produced on the remaining issues.

 

Sanctions

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480(j), “[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction…against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel an answer or production, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $18,725.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record, jointly and severally, for having to bring the instant motion for Defendant’s failure to provide a PMQ who was knowledgeable as to all of the Topics or adequately prepared to testify.

 

In opposition, Defendant also seeks sanctions in the amount of $5,750.00 in connection with opposing the motion.

 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $3,000.00 (5 hours preparing the motion, reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply at Geylikman’s hourly rate of $600.). Mr. Geylikman does not describe what Ms. Benyamin did on this motion. Moreover, Mr. Geylikman has not demonstrated Mr. Foster’s work is not duplicative of Mr. Geylikman’s work.

 

The court denies Defendant’s request. Defendant has not demonstrated substantial justification in opposing this motion. 

 

 

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV05696    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: W

ANA MARIA SERNA CHAVEZ, ET AL. VS CITY OF LOS ANGELES, ET AL.

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES’ MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT  

 

Date of Hearing:        July 12, 2024                                                  Trial Date:       N/A 

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        23VECV05696

 

Moving Party:            Defendant City of Los Angeles

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 26, 1023, Plaintiffs Ana Maria Serna Chavez and Estate of Noe Antonio Lopez filed a complaint against Defendant City of Los Angeles and Alyazeed Moawya Dobi. Plaintiff alleges Defendant Dobi collided with his vehicle into Decedent Noe Antonio Lopez, a pedestrian. After the collision, Defendant Dobi and other witnesses called for an ambulance. Plaintiff alleges LAPD and paramedics assumed the duty of providing reasonable assistance to the injured party, and also represented that they were investigating and looking for the injured party. Unfortunately, no investigation was done, no report was made, and no search was conducted by Defendant City of Los Angeles personnel. The following day, Decedent Noe Antonio Lopez was found dead near the location of the subject incident.

Request for entry of default against Defendants Alyazeed Moawya Dobi and the City of Los Angeles was entered April 5, 2024. The court set aside the default against Alyazeed Moawya Dobi on May 22, 2024.

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

City of Los Angeles’ Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant City of Los Angeles moves the court for an order requesting that the default against the City be set aside on the grounds of mistake and/or excusable negligence under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).

 

Case law instructs that so long as counsel is willing to fall on their sword, relief is mandatory. (See Abers v. Rohrs (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1199, 1210.) “Section 473’s provision for mandatory relief from a dismissal based upon a declaration of attorney error does not require a determination the error was excusable. It applies even when the attorney has no excuse.” (Ibid.) “Relief is mandatory when a complying affidavit is filed, even if the attorney's neglect was inexcusable.” (SJP Limited Partnership v. City of Los Angeles (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 511, 516-517.) 

 

Counsel for the City avers over the past several years, the number of civil case filings against the City have skyrocketed and the personnel on hand to address new filings have been stretched thin for years. (Quinones Decl. ¶2.) Counsel contends though the Complaint was received on or about February 20, 2024, the deadline to file an Answer was not properly calendared. (Quinones Decl. ¶2.) Counsel was not assigned the matter until April 24, 2024 and by the time he received the matter, the deadline to file an answer already passed. (Quinones Decl. ¶3.) The City attempted to file an answer but the answer was rejected and default was entered. (Quinones Decl. ¶¶6, 7.) Plaintiff has refused to voluntarily set aside the default. (Quinones Decl. ¶8.)

 

The court finds relief mandatory. The motion has been filed within six months after entry of default. Moreover, the deadline to file an answer was missed due to a calendaring error at the City’s Counsel’s office. Accordingly, Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) mandates that the default be set aside.

 

City of Los Angeles’ Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED. The City is ordered to file an answer within 20 days.
