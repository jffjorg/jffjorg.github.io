DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00808    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: H

Murillo v. Dr. Privitera’s Holistic Clinic, et al., Case No. 21PSCV00808


ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

Defendant Anakin Mark Privitera’s Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED.

 

Background

In this employment discrimination case, Plaintiff Malissa Murillo (“Plaintiff”) alleges that while working for Defendants Dr. Privitera’s Holistic Clinic (the “Clinic”), Alternative Endeavors, Inc. (“Endeavors”), and Anakin Mark Privitera (“Privitera”) (collectively, “Defendants”) as an office assistant at the Clinic, Privitera made lewd and vulgar comments in her presence on a repeated basis and made sexual advances to her.

On October 5, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendants and Does 1-40 for (1) discrimination based on sexual harassment in violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) and (2) failure to timely pay wages owed.

On December 23, 2021, the Clerk entered default against Endeavors. On April 18, 2022, the Clerk entered default against the Clinic.

On April 24, 2023, the Court granted Endeavors’ motion to set aside default and motion to quash service of summons.

The Final Status Conference is set for October 30, 2024. A Court Trial is set for November 8, 2024.

Pending before the Court is Privitera’s motion for reconsideration. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is denied.

 

Legal Standard

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (b).)

“A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on ex parte motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (b).)

“A violation of this section may be punished as a contempt and with sanctions as allowed by Section 128.7. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (d).)

 

Discussion

Privitera moves the Court to reconsider its September 23, 2024 order denying Privitera’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings.

At the outset, it appears that the motion was not timely served as it should have been electronically served no later than September 26, 2024.[FN] Even so, the Court will overlook this deficiency because Plaintiff has opposed the motion on the merits: “It is well settled that the appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on its merits is a waiver of any defects or irregularities in the notice of the motion.” (Tate v. Superior Court (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 925, 930.) 

Turning to the merits, Privitera’s motion fails to articulate any “new or different facts, circumstances or law” warranting reconsideration of the aforesaid ruling. The motion is denied. 

The Court declines to entertain Plaintiff’s request for relief pursuant to subdivision (d), inasmuch as it is the subject for a separate motion.

The foregoing reflects insufficient notice pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1005(b) (i.e., 16 court days) and 1010.6(a)(3)(B) (an additional two court days for electronic service). Humberto G. v. Doe 1, Case No. 22PSCV01942

ORDER ON MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

Defendant City of El Monte’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.

 

I.        Background

Plaintiff Humberto G. (“Plaintiff”), who is now over 40 years old, alleges that in 1981, he was the victim of childhood sexual assault, abuse, and other misconduct by a youth baseball league coach, Michael Stewart Mullicane.

On March 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting a public entity liability cause of action (Government Code §§ 815.2 and 815.4) against the City of El Monte (“the City”) and Does 2 through 50. 

The Final Status Conference is set for April 14, 2025. Trial is set for April 22, 2025. 

Pending before the Court is the City’s motion for judgment on the pleadings, where the City contends that Plaintiff’s claim is barred because Plaintiff did not submit a government tort claim in 1981-1982 and that any subsequent legislation purporting to remove the claim requirement would constitute an unconstitutional gift of public funds. For the reasons stated herein, and light of the recent decision in West Contra Costa Unified School District v. The Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 1243, the City’s motion is denied. 

II.      Legal Standard

The rules governing demurrers are generally applicable to a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999; Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (d) [“The grounds for motion. . . shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. Where the motion is based on a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. . ., the matter shall be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court may otherwise permit”].)

 

A motion by a plaintiff may only be made on the grounds “that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(A).) A motion by a defendant may only be made on the grounds that (1) “[t]he court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint” or (2) “[t]he complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c).)

 

Although a nonstatutory motion “may be made at any time either prior to the trial or at the trial itself” (Stoops v. Abbassi (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 644, 650 [quotation marks and citation omitted]), a statutory motion cannot be made after entry of a pretrial conference order or 30 days before the initial trial date, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (e).)

 

III.     Requests for Judicial Notice 

Turning to the parties’ requests for judicial notice, a court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1-8 (i.e., legislative history of Assembly Bill No. 218).

Because “a written trial court ruling has no precedential value,” the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 9 and 10 (i.e., of two superior court orders in other cases) and the City’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits A-C (i.e., of three superior court orders in other cases). (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshe (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831; Drummond v. Desmarais (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 439, 448, fn. 4 [“(I)n the absence of some additional showing—such as the conditions for claim or issue preclusion—the actions of other judges are simply irrelevant”].)

The City’s request that the Court take judicial notice of the Declaration of Rigoberto Gutierrez is denied as irrelevant. Plaintiff concedes that the Operative Complaint does not allege compliance with the claim presentation requirement of the Tort Claims Act. (Opp., 1:23.) 

IV.     Discussion

The City moves for judgment on the pleadings under Code of Civil Procedure § 438, on the basis that the Operative Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Specifically, the City contends that notwithstanding Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”), which revives certain childhood sexual abuse claims that would otherwise be barred under statutes of limitation or claim presentation requirements, Plaintiff’s claim against the City cannot proceed because Plaintiff failed to comply with the claim presentation requirement of the Tort Claims Act and AB 218’s attempt to retroactively waive that requirement amounts to an unconstitutional gift of public funds.

On March 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Operative Complaint, alleging that he suffered childhood sexual abuse in or about 1981 when he participated in a youth baseball league at age 14. (FAC, ¶¶ 11-21.)[FN1] AB 218 amended Code of Civil Procedure § 340.1 to extend the time for filing claims for childhood sexual assault and create a three-year revival window for lapsed claims. AB 218 also amended Government Code § 905 “by deleting from subdivision (m) the language that previously limited [an] exception to the government claim presentation requirement to claims arising out of conduct occurring on or after January 1, 2009, and adding subdivision (p), which made this change retroactive.” (Coats v. New Haven Unified School Dist. (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 415, 424.)[FN2]

The City argues that AB 218, in purporting to retroactively apply the claim presentation waiver, violates Article XVI, Section VI of the California Constitution (the “gift clause”), which prohibits gifts of public funds: “The Legislature shall have no power to give or to lend, or to authorize the giving or lending, of the credit of the State, or of any county, city and county, city, township or other political corporation or subdivision of the State now existing, or that may be hereafter established, in aid or to any person, association, or corporation, whether municipal or otherwise, or to pledge the credit thereof, in any manner whatever, for the payment of the liabilities of any individual, association, municipal or other corporation whatever.”

The Court disagrees. At the outset, the Court observes that, after the parties submitted their briefs in this matter, the First District Court of Appeal issued its decision in West Contra Costa Unified School District v. The Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 1243, where it expressly determined that “AB 218 did not create new ‘substantive liability’ for the underlying alleged wrongful conduct[, but] [i]nstead, . . . simply waived a condition the state had imposed on its consent to suit.” (Id. at 1257.) In so holding, the West Contra Costa court looked to Chapman v. State (1894) 104 Cal. 690, which involved the collapse of a state-operated wharf, allegedly due to the state’s negligence, and consequent loss of coal stored on the wharf. At the time of the loss, the plaintiff could only present a claim to the state board of examiners, which was done and rejected. (Id. at 692.) The plaintiff subsequently filed suit under a statute enacted after the loss that expressly authorized such lawsuits: “All persons who have, or shall hereafter have, claims on contract or for negligence against the state, not allowed by the state board of examiners, are hereby authorized, on the terms and conditions herein contained, to bring suit thereon against the state in any of the courts of this state of competent jurisdiction, and prosecute the same to final judgment.” (Id. at 693.)

The trial court in Chapman sustained the state’s demurrer to the complaint. On appeal, the state argued that “at the time when the coal belonging to the assignors of the plaintiff was lost, the state was not liable for the damage occasioned by said loss . . . and that the act should not be construed as intended to create any liability against the state for such past negligence,” relying on the principle that “in the absence of a statute voluntarily assuming such liability, the state is not liable in damages for the negligent acts of its officers while engaged in discharging ordinary official duties pertaining to the administration of the government of the state.” (Id. at 693). The state contended that no such statute existed at the time of the loss. The Chapman court explained that, while it would violate the gift clause for the legislature to retroactively authorize any action for negligence, the plaintiff’s action was actually founded upon breach of contract because the state was contractually obligated, in consideration of wharfage paid, to preserve the property entrusted to it. The Chapman court explained that “the liability of the state accrued at the time of the breach—that is, when the coal was lost through the negligence of the officers in charge of the state’s wharf—although there was then no law giving to the plaintiff’s assignors the right to sue state thereof.” (Id. at 696 (Emphasis added).) While the “right to sue the state has since been given by the act of February 28, 1893 . . . the legislature did not create any liability or cause of action against the state where none existed before” as “[t]he state was always liability upon its contracts.” (Id.) In other words, the fact that there was no remedy prior to the legislative enactment does not mean there was “no liability and no claim.” (Id.)

The West Contra Costa court concluded that “under Chapman the crucial consideration is whether the Legislature has created new substantive liability, or, instead, has simply removed an obstacle to recovery imposed by the state as a condition of its consent to suit that is not an aspect of the underlying substantive liability.” (Id. at 1263.)

As relevant here, the West Contra Costa court observed that the Law Revision Commission comments to Government Code section 905.8 “ma[ke] clear that the claims presentation provisions [do] not impose substantive liability,” that liability for the alleged misconduct at issue in AB 218 cases is found in sections 820 and 815.2, and that “[b]ecause a statute imposing liability on the District existed at the time of the sexual assaults, AB 218 imposes no new substantive liability under Chapman’s gift clause analysis.” (Id. at 1260-1261). The Court agrees.

The Court is unpersuaded by the City’s citations to Bourn v. Hart (1892) 93 Cal. 321[FN3], Conlin v. Board of Supervisors (1893) 99 Cal. 17[FN4], and Powell v. Phelan (1903) 138 Cal. 271[FN5], in which violations of the gift clause were found.[FN6] In each of the foregoing cases, the legislature created liability after the fact. By way of contrast, there was liability at the time of the alleged sexual assaults against Plaintiff. This fact is not disputed by City.

Moreover, it is well settled that if the appropriated public funds “are to be used for a public purpose, they are not a gift within the meaning of” the gift clause. (Page v. MiraCosta Community College Dist. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 471, 495.) Here, even if “AB 218 authorizes expenditures within the scope of the gift clause, the expenditures are not ‘gifts’ because they serve a public purpose.” (West Contra Costa, supra, 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 1265.) In contrast to laws permitting “discrete payments to private parties with no larger public goal,” AB 218 “provide[s] relief to a disadvantaged group of persons,” namely, childhood sex assault victims, which is “a sufficient public purpose under the gift clause.” (Id. at 1267-68; cf. Page, supra, 180 Cal.App.4th at p. 495 [“The settlement of a good faith dispute between the state and a private party is an appropriate use of public funds and not a gift because the relinquishment of a colorable legal claim in return for settlement funds is good consideration and establishes a valid public purpose.”].) 

The motion is denied.

A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1-8 (i.e., legislative history of Assembly Bill No. 218).

 

IV.     Discussion 

The District moves for judgment on the pleadings under Code of Civil Procedure section 438 on the basis that the Operative Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Specifically, the District contends that notwithstanding Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”), which revives certain childhood sexual abuse claims that would otherwise be barred under statutes of limitation or claim presentation requirements, Plaintiff’s claim against the District cannot proceed because Plaintiff failed to comply with the claim presentation requirement of the Tort Claims Act and AB 218’s attempt to retroactively waive that requirement amounts to an unconstitutional gift of public funds.

On May 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Operative Complaint, alleging that she suffered childhood sexual abuse beginning in 1994 when she was a 16-year-old student at Pomona High School. (FAC, ¶¶ 4, 5 and 11-24).[FN1] Plaintiff has directed her third through seventh causes of action (i.e., for Sexual Harassment, Negligent Hiring, Supervision & Retention of an Unfit Employee, Breach of Mandatory Duty: Failure to Report Suspected Child Abuse, Negligent Failure to Warn, Train or Educate and Negligent Supervision of a Minor, Respectively) against the District.[FN2]

Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”) amended Code of Civil Procedure § 340.1 to, inter alia, extend the time for filing claims for childhood sexual assault and create a three-year revival window for lapsed claims. AB 218 also amended Government Code § 905 “by deleting from subdivision (m) the language that previously limited [an] exception to the government claim presentation requirement to claims arising out of conduct occurring on or after January 1, 2009, and adding subdivision (p), which made this change retroactive.” (Coats v. New Haven Unified School Dist. (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 415, 424.)[FN3]

The District argues that AB 218, in purporting to retroactively apply the claim presentation waiver, violates Article XVI, Section VI of the California Constitution (the “gift clause”), which prohibits gifts of public funds: “The Legislature shall have no power to give or to lend, or to authorize the giving or lending, of the credit of the State, or of any county, city and county, city, township or other political corporation or subdivision of the State now existing, or that may be hereafter established, in aid or to any person, association, or corporation, whether municipal or otherwise, or to pledge the credit thereof, in any manner whatever, for the payment of the liabilities of any individual, association, municipal or other corporation whatever.”

The Court disagrees. At the outset, the Court observes that, after the parties submitted their briefs in this matter, the First District Court of Appeal issued its decision in West Contra Costa Unified School District v. The Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 1243, where it expressly determined that “AB 218 did not create new ‘substantive liability’ for the underlying alleged wrongful conduct[, but] [i]nstead, . . . simply waived a condition the state had imposed on its consent to suit.” (Id. at 1257.) In so holding, the West Contra Costa court looked to Chapman v. State (1894) 104 Cal. 690, which involved the collapse of a state-operated wharf, allegedly due to the state’s negligence, and consequent loss of coal stored on the wharf. At the time of the loss, the plaintiff could only present a claim to the state board of examiners, which was done and rejected. (Id. at 692.) The plaintiff subsequently filed suit under a statute enacted after the loss that expressly authorized such lawsuits: “All persons who have, or shall hereafter have, claims on contract or for negligence against the state, not allowed by the state board of examiners, are hereby authorized, on the terms and conditions herein contained, to bring suit thereon against the state in any of the courts of this state of competent jurisdiction, and prosecute the same to final judgment.” (Id. at 693.)

The trial court in Chapman sustained the state’s demurrer to the complaint. On appeal, the state argued that “at the time when the coal belonging to the assignors of the plaintiff was lost, the state was not liable for the damage occasioned by said loss . . . and that the act should not be construed as intended to create any liability against the state for such past negligence,” relying on the principle that “in the absence of a statute voluntarily assuming such liability, the state is not liable in damages for the negligent acts of its officers while engaged in discharging ordinary official duties pertaining to the administration of the government of the state.” (Id. at 693). The state contended that no such statute existed at the time of the loss. The Chapman court explained that, while it would violate the gift clause for the legislature to retroactively authorize any action for negligence, the plaintiff’s action was actually founded upon breach of contract because the state was contractually obligated, in consideration of wharfage paid, to preserve the property entrusted to it. The Chapman court explained that “the liability of the state accrued at the time of the breach—that is, when the coal was lost through the negligence of the officers in charge of the state’s wharf—although there was then no law giving to the plaintiff’s assignors the right to sue state thereof.” (Id. at 696 (Emphasis added).) While the “right to sue the state has since been given by the act of February 28, 1893 . . . the legislature did not create any liability or cause of action against the state where none existed before” as “[t]he state was always liability upon its contracts.” (Id.) In other words, the fact that there was no remedy prior to the legislative enactment does not mean there was “no liability and no claim.” (Id.)

The West Contra Costa court concluded that “under Chapman the crucial consideration is whether the Legislature has created new substantive liability, or, instead, has simply removed an obstacle to recovery imposed by the state as a condition of its consent to suit that is not an aspect of the underlying substantive liability.” (Id. at 1263.)

As relevant here, the West Contra Costa court observed that the Law Revision Commission comments to Government Code section 905.8 “ma[ke] clear that the claims presentation provisions [do] not impose substantive liability,” that liability for the alleged misconduct at issue in AB 218 cases is found in sections 820 and 815.2, and that “[b]ecause a statute imposing liability on the District existed at the time of the sexual assaults, AB 218 imposes no new substantive liability under Chapman’s gift clause analysis.” (Id. at 1260-1261). The Court agrees.

The Court is unpersuaded by the District’s citations to Bourn v. Hart (1892) 93 Cal. 321 [FN4], Conlin v. Board of Supervisors (1893) 99 Cal. 17 [FN5], and Powell v. Phelan (1903) 138 Cal. 271 [FN6], in which violations of the gift clause were found.[FN7] In each of the foregoing cases, the legislature created liability after the fact. By way of contrast, there was liability at the time of the alleged sexual assaults against Plaintiff.

Moreover, it is well settled that if the appropriated public funds “are to be used for a public purpose, they are not a gift within the meaning of” the gift clause. (Page v. MiraCosta Community College Dist. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 471, 495.) Here, even if “AB 218 authorizes expenditures within the scope of the gift clause, the expenditures are not ‘gifts’ because they serve a public purpose.” (West Contra Costa, supra, 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 1265.) In contrast to laws permitting “discrete payments to private parties with no larger public goal,” AB 218 “provide[s] relief to a disadvantaged group of persons,” namely, childhood sex assault victims, which is “a sufficient public purpose under the gift clause.” (Id. at 1267-68; cf. Page, supra, 180 Cal.App.4th at p. 495 [“The settlement of a good faith dispute between the state and a private party is an appropriate use of public funds and not a gift because the relinquishment of a colorable legal claim in return for settlement funds is good consideration and establishes a valid public purpose.”].)

The motion is denied.

II.      Legal Standards 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.) 

 

III.     Request for Judicial Notice 

A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshe (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831; Drummond v. B.       The District’s Demurrer to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action

The District next argues that Plaintiff’s fourth and fifth causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action. The Court agrees.

A duty to educate, train, and warn of the risks of sexual abuse was recognized by the First District Court of Appeal in Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000), which held that the Boy Scouts of America (and that entity only) had a duty to educate, train, or warn youth or their parents or adult leaders about the risk of sexual abuse. Juarez, however, was expressly limited to that case. (Id. at 409 [“[W]e wish to make special note that that the reach of this opinion is only intended to extend as far as the record before us today.”].) Plaintiff has not provided the Court with any authority wherein such a duty was imposed as against a school district. The Court sustains the fourth cause of action on this basis.

Additionally, “[a] cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.) “It is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903.) Plaintiff has not alleged any conduct directed at her. The Court sustains the District’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action on this basis.

IV.     Discussion

The District demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10, subdivision (e), to Plaintiffs’ entire complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Specifically, the District first contends that notwithstanding Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”), which revives certain childhood sexual abuse claims that would otherwise be barred under statutes of limitation or claim presentation requirements, Plaintiffs’ claims against the District cannot proceed because Plaintiffs failed to comply with the claim presentation requirements of the Tort Claims Act and AB 218’s attempt to retroactively waive that requirement amounts to an unconstitutional gift of public funds. The District separately demurs on the basis of misjoinder.

 

A.       The District’s Demurrer Based on AB 218 

Plaintiffs filed their complaint on May 6, 2024, alleging therein that they were the victims of childhood sexual abuse, harassment, and molestation while they were students at Rosemead High School. Plaintiff Jane C.H. Doe identifies her perpetrator as teacher Alex Rai (“Rai”) and asserts that the alleged misconduct occurred between 2008-2009. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff Jane D.C. Doe identifies her perpetrators as Rai and coach/aide Eduardo Escobar and asserts that the alleged misconduct occurred between 2007-2011. (Id., ¶¶ 13 and 19.) Plaintiff Jane J.L. Doe identifies her perpetrator as coach Wing Chan and asserts that the alleged misconduct occurred between 2005-2009. (Complaint, ¶ 12.)

AB 218 amended Code of Civil Procedure § 340.1 to extend the time for filing claims for childhood sexual assault and create a three-year revival window for lapsed claims. AB 218 also amended Government Code § 905 “by deleting from subdivision (m) the language that previously limited [an] exception to the government claim presentation requirement to claims arising out of conduct occurring on or after January 1, 2009, and adding subdivision (p), which made this change retroactive.” (Coats v. New Haven Unified School Dist. (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 415, 424.)[FN1] 

The District argues that AB 218, in purporting to retroactively apply the claim presentation waiver, violates Article XVI, Section VI of the California Constitution (the “gift clause”), which prohibits gifts of public funds: “The Legislature shall have no power to give or to lend, or to authorize the giving or lending, of the credit of the State, or of any county, city and county, city, township or other political corporation or subdivision of the State now existing, or that may be hereafter established, in aid or to any person, association, or corporation, whether municipal or otherwise, or to pledge the credit thereof, in any manner whatever, for the payment of the liabilities of any individual, association, municipal or other corporation whatever.”

The Court disagrees. At the outset, the Court observes that, days after the District filed the instant demurrer, the First District Court of Appeal issued its decision in West Contra Costa, where it expressly determined that “AB 218 did not create new ‘substantive liability’ for the underlying alleged wrongful conduct[, but] [i]nstead, . . . simply waived a condition the state had imposed on its consent to suit.” (Id. at 1257.) In so holding, the West Contra Costa court looked to Chapman v. State (1894) 104 Cal. 690, which involved the collapse of a state-operated wharf, allegedly due to the state’s negligence, and consequent loss of coal stored on the wharf. At the time of the loss, the plaintiff could only present a claim to the state board of examiners, which was done and rejected. (Id. at 692.) The plaintiff subsequently filed suit under a statute enacted after the loss that expressly authorized such lawsuits: “All persons who have, or shall hereafter have, claims on contract or for negligence against the state, not allowed by the state board of examiners, are hereby authorized, on the terms and conditions herein contained, to bring suit thereon against the state in any of the courts of this state of competent jurisdiction, and prosecute the same to final judgment.” (Id. at 693.)

The trial court in Chapman sustained the state’s demurrer to the complaint. On appeal, the state argued that “at the time when the coal belonging to the assignors of the plaintiff was lost, the state was not liable for the damage occasioned by said loss . . . and that the act should not be construed as intended to create any liability against the state for such past negligence,” relying on the principle that “in the absence of a statute voluntarily assuming such liability, the state is not liable in damages for the negligent acts of its officers while engaged in discharging ordinary official duties pertaining to the administration of the government of the state.” (Id. at 693). The state contended that no such statute existed at the time of the loss. The Chapman court explained that, while it would violate the gift clause for the legislature to retroactively authorize any action for negligence, the plaintiff’s action was actually founded upon breach of contract because the state was contractually obligated, in consideration of wharfage paid, to preserve the property entrusted to it. The Chapman court explained that “the liability of the state accrued at the time of the breach—that is, when the coal was lost through the negligence of the officers in charge of the state’s wharf—although there was then no law giving to the plaintiff’s assignors the right to sue state thereof.” (Id. at 696 (Emphasis added).) While the “right to sue the state has since been given by the act of February 28, 1893 . . . the legislature did not create any liability or cause of action against the state where none existed before” as “[t]he state was always liability upon its contracts.” (Id.) In other words, the fact that there was no remedy prior to the legislative enactment does not mean there was “no liability and no claim.” (Id.)

The West Contra Costa court concluded that “under Chapman the crucial consideration is whether the Legislature has created new substantive liability, or, instead, has simply removed an obstacle to recovery imposed by the state as a condition of its consent to suit that is not an aspect of the underlying substantive liability.” (Id. at 1263.) 

As relevant here, the West Contra Costa court observed that the Law Revision Commission comments to Government Code section 905.8 “ma[ke] clear that the claims presentation provisions [do] not impose substantive liability,” that liability for the alleged misconduct at issue in AB 218 cases is found in sections 820 and 815.2, and that “[b]ecause a statute imposing liability on the District existed at the time of the sexual assaults, AB 218 imposes no new substantive liability under Chapman’s gift clause analysis.” (Id. at 1260-1261). The Court agrees.

The Court is unpersuaded by District’s citations to Bourn v. Hart (1892) 93 Cal. 321 [FN2], Conlin v. Board of Supervisors (1893) 99 Cal. 17 [FN3], and Powell v. Phelan (1903) 138 Cal. 271 [FN4], in which violations of the gift clause were found.[FN5] In each of the foregoing cases, the legislature created liability after the fact. By way of contrast, there was liability at the time of the alleged sexual assaults against Plaintiffs.

Moreover, it is well settled that if the appropriated public funds “are to be used for a public purpose, they are not a gift within the meaning of” the gift clause. (Page v. MiraCosta Community College Dist. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 471, 495.) Here, the Court agrees that even if “AB 218 authorizes expenditures within the scope of the gift clause, the expenditures are not ‘gifts’ because they serve a public purpose.” (West Contra Costa, supra, 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 1265.)

In contrast to laws permitting “discrete payments to private parties with no larger public goal,” AB 218 “provide[s] relief to a disadvantaged group of persons,” namely, childhood sex assault victims, which is “a sufficient public purpose under the gift clause.” (Id. at 1267-68; cf. Page, supra, 180 Cal.App.4th at p. 495 [“The settlement of a good faith dispute between the state and a private party is an appropriate use of public funds and not a gift because the relinquishment of a colorable legal claim in return for settlement funds is good consideration and establishes a valid public purpose.”].)

 

B.       The District’s Demurrer Based on Misjoinder

The District asserts that Plaintiffs have violated Code of Civil Procedure section 378 and that the complaint is subject to demurrer on the basis of misjoinder.[FN6] Section 378 provides, in relevant part, that “[a]ll persons may join in one action as plaintiffs if: (1) They assert any right to relief jointly, severally, or in the alternative, in respect of or arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences and if any question of law or fact common to all these persons will arise in the action; or (2) They have a claim, right, or interest adverse to the defendant in the property or controversy which is the subject of the action.”

Here, Plaintiffs’ alleged sexual abuse, harassment, and molestation occurred during different time periods in different locations and involve three different perpetrators; accordingly, their claims do not arise out of the same transaction, occurrence, or related series of occurrences. The Court sustains the District’s demurrer on this basis.

 

          C.       The District’s Motion to Strike

In light of the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, the District’s motion to strike is denied as moot.
