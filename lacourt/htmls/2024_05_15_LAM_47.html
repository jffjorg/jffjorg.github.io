DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV39278    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     May 15, 2024                         TRIAL DATE: September 17, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Loretta L. Tobin v. Ernst & Haas Management Co., Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV39278

 

           

 

DEMURRER TO COMPLAINT; MOTION TO STRIKE COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Solimar Homeowner’s Association

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Loretta L. Tobin

 

CASE HISTORY:

  • 10/05/21: Complaint filed.
  • 12/29/21: Linda J. Vitz substituted in for Doe Defendant #1
  • 08/17/23: Solimar Homeowners Association substituted in for Doe Defendant #2.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a habitability defect action. Plaintiff leased an apartment from Defendants that she alleges contained toxic mold.

 

Defendant Solimar Homeowner’s Association demurs to the Complaint and moves to strike the Complaint.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant Solimar Homeowner’s Association’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            Defendant Solimar Homeowner’s Association demurs to the Complaint in its entirety.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet-and-confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Attorney Andrew Scoble states that the parties met and conferred telephonically on November 6, 2023, and that Plaintiff’s counsel stated he intended to amend the Complaint but required leave of court to do so. (Declaration of Andrew D. Scoble ISO Demurrer ¶ 3.) The declaration thereafter states that the parties were unable to reach an agreement resolving these objections but provides no explanation for how that conclusion was reached. (Id.) The Court therefore does not find this declaration sufficient to satisfy Defendant’s statutory meet-and-confer obligations. Nevertheless, the Court will address the demurrer on its merits.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of (1) a Grant Deed conveying ownership of Lot 6 of Tract No. 13628, commonly known as 7683 Yorktown Avenue, Huntington Beach, the Subject Property, to Linda J. Vitz recorded on May 8, 2000 in the Orange County Recorder’s Office as Instrument No. 20000239290; (2) a Grant Deed conveying Lot 10 of the same parcel to Solimar recorded on August 8, 1990 as Instrument No. 90-418891; (3) the Tract Map for Tract No. 13628 recorded on June 28, 1989 as Instrument No. 89-342013; and (4) the Grant Deed conveying Lot 6 from Linda J. Vitz to Thomas A. Brown recorded on November 15, 2021 as Instrument No. 2021000700361.

            Defendant’s requests are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c) (official acts) and (h) (matters not reasonably subject to dispute and capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy).

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

            Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing on the grounds that Plaintiff did not adequately allege the details of the underlying contract. Defendant further argues that it is not the owner of the subject property as a matter of law, and therefore cannot be liable for claims arising out of the rental agreement.

 

            The Court addressed the sufficiency of Plaintiff’s contract allegations in its May 11, 2022 ruling on the demurrer brought by the other Defendants in this action. In that ruling, the Court found that the Complaint did not adequately set forth the terms of the underlying lease agreement, and therefore could not support a claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing. (May 11, 2022 Minute Order p.5.) As the Complaint has not been amended, that decision stands as the ruling of the Court. Moreover, Plaintiff’s attempt to introduce the putative lease agreement as an exhibit in her opposition is unavailing. Even if the Court considered this extrinsic evidence, the document does not contain a single reference to a homeowner’s association in general or to Defendant Solimar in particular. (See Plaintiff’s Exh. A.) Plaintiff therefore cannot maintain a claim for breach of a lease agreement or any of the consequential implied covenants against this Defendant.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Second Cause of Action: Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

 

            Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment on the grounds that Plaintiff did not state facts sufficient to support a claim.

 

            “[E]very lease includes a covenant of quiet possession and enjoyment.” (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1299 (citing Civ. Code, § 1927).) “Minor inconveniences and annoyances are not actionable breaches of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.” (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates¿(2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 589.) “To be actionable, the [landlord’s] act or omission must substantially interfere with a [tenant’s] right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy.” (Id.) “The tenant need not vacate the property before bringing an action for breach of the¿covenant of quiet enjoyment.” (Marchese v. Standard Realty & Dev.¿Co. (1977) 74 Cal.App.3d 142, 148.)

 

            Defendant argues that it cannot be liable for a breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment because it did not own the subject property and therefore was not a party to the lease agreement as a matter of law. Defendant offers a set of publicly recorded grant deeds, which are subject to judicial notice, to demonstrate that although Solimar owns a neighboring parcel, it does not own the subject property, which belonged first to Linda J. Vitz, and then to Thomas A. Brown. (RJN Exhs. 1, 2, 4.) Further, as stated above, the putative lease agreement offered by Plaintiff shows that Defendant Solimar is not a party to that agreement.

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

 

            Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. For the reasons stated above, Defendant’s Demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Negligent Premises Liability

 

            Defendant demurs to the fourth cause of action on the grounds that Plaintiff cannot state facts constituting a cause of action against this Defendant.

 

            To state a claim for premises liability, a plaintiff must plead a duty arising from the defendant’s ownership or control of the premises, a breach of that duty, and resultant injury. (See Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.)

 

            Defendant argues that the Complaint is silent as to any duties owed by Defendant as a homeowner’s association in general, and, further, that Defendant did not own the subject property and had no duties arising from ownership or management of the property. (See RJN Exhs. 1-2, 4.) Plaintiff offers no response beyond a conclusory assertion that Defendant Solimar owed a duty of care to Plaintiff. The Court is not persuaded. The judicially noticeable records establish that Defendant Solimar did not own the subject property, and the Complaint pleads no facts which would establish that the homeowner’s association had any control over the property, as opposed to the owner or the property manager, who are separately named.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Failure to Disclose

 

            Defendant demurs to this cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. The parties agree that this cause of action survives or falls with the fourth cause of action. Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Dangerous Conditions

 

            Defendant demurs to this cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. The parties agree that this cause of action survives or falls with the fourth cause of action. Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

            Defendant demurs to this cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are:  

‘“(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress;  (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. …” Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ [Citation.]” (Citation omitted.) “It is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” 

 

(Catsouras v. Department of California Highway Patrol (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 856, 874-875.) The defendant’s conduct must be “directed primarily” at the plaintiffs, “calculated to cause them severe emotional distress,” or “done with knowledge of their presence and of a substantial certainty that they would suffer severe emotional injury.” (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 906.) 

 

Defendant contends that this cause of action fails because it is premised on the concealment of and failure to remediate toxic mold on the subject property, which, as discussed above, Defendant Solimar did not own or control. Thus, Defendant argues, even if true, its conduct does not rise to the level of “extreme and outrageous” contemplated by this cause of action.  Plaintiff asserts that this cause of action is adequately pled, but merely restates the general allegations of the Complaint without referencing any specific conduct by Solimar. As the judicially noticed matter establishes that Defendant Solimar did not own the subject property, the Court concurs that this cause of action is fatally deficient.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Eighth Cause of Action: Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

            Defendant demurs to this cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. The parties agree that this cause of action survives or falls with the fourth and seventh causes of action. Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Ninth Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

            Defendant demurs to the ninth cause of action for breach of contract for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            For the reasons stated above with respect to the first cause of action, Defendant’s demurrer to this cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Tenth Cause of Action: Fraud

 

            Defendant demurs to the tenth cause of action for fraud for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

            “The elements of fraud that will lead to a tort action are: (a) misrepresentation; (b) knowledge of falsity; (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant[s] to understand fully the nature of the charge made. (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Ibid.) “[G]eneral and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

            As the Court stated in its May 11, 2022 ruling:

 

Here, the Complaint does not identify specific statements made by Defendants, who made them, how they were made, when they were made, or by what means they were tendered. Instead, the Complaint makes general allegations that the Defendants made repeated and constant misrepresentations concerning the source of mold, its effects, and their remediation efforts. (Complaint ¶ 185.) The Complaint provides no specifics or examples of any misrepresentations, or any of the information required by Stansfield. Under the particularity requirement for fraud actions, these allegations are not sufficient. Plaintiff has failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

(May 11, 2022 Minute Order p.8.) Because the Complaint has not been amended, the allegations remain deficient.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the tenth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Eleventh Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation

 

            Defendant demurs to the eleventh cause of action for negligent misrepresentation for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. As the parties agree this cause of action survives or fails with the tenth cause of action, Defendant’s Demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Uncertainty

 

            Defendant demurs to the Complaint in its entirety on the grounds that it is uncertain.

 

Demurrers¿for uncertainty are disfavored, because discovery can be used for clarification, and they apply only where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated.¿(Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822; Khoury v. Maly's of Cal., Inc.¿(1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) If the complaint is sufficiently comprehensible that Defendant can reasonably respond, the complaint is not uncertain. (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Ins. Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848 n.3.)  

 

            Defendant argues that the Complaint is uncertain because it contains no allegations specific to Solimar. This is an argument for the failure to allege sufficient facts, not uncertainty.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Plaintiff does not demonstrate how the Complaint might be amended to cure the deficiencies identified by Solimar’s demurrer. Moreover, with respect to the contract claims and premises liability claims, the judicially noticed facts establish that Solimar cannot be liable on Plaintiff’s contract claims or premises claims because it did not own or control the subject property. Further, what few facts are pled as to the remaining tort claims demonstrate that those claims are premised on the ownership, control, and management of the property. The Court therefore finds that leave to amend is not appropriate in this instance.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike Complaint

 

            As the Court has sustained Defendant’s demurrer in its entirety without leave to amend, Defendant’s corresponding Motion to Strike is MOOT.

           

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendant Solimar Homeowner’s Association’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant’s Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

Moving party to give notice, unless waived.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants Daniel Wang and Grace Lee move for evidentiary and issue sanctions against Defendants and Cross-Complainant 360 Capital Ventures for failure to comply with the Court’s July 24, 2023 Order requiring further production of documents in response to certain requests for production.

The purpose of a separate statement in the context of a discovery motion is to “provide[ ] all the information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses to it that are at issue,” and why the relief sought is appropriate. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 191, disapproved of on other grounds in Presbyterian Camp & Conference Centers, Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 12 Cal.5th 493.) The absence of a separate statement hinders the Court’s ability to match the remedy to the harm caused by the misuse of the discovery process. For this reason, Plaintiffs’ request for nonmonetary sanctions cannot be granted.

Case Number: 23STCV21465    Hearing Date: May 15, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     May 15, 2024             TRIAL DATE: NOT SET

                                                          

CASE:                         Omar Penagos Lozada v. Miguel Victoria, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV21465           

 

DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Miguel Victoria and Alexandria Mujica

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 5/10/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         09/07/23: Complaint filed.

·         09/14/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a quiet title action. Plaintiff seeks to rescind a trustee sale that he alleges was improperly executed against his home.

 

Defendants Miguel Victoria and Alexandria Mujica demur to the first amended complaint.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendants Miguel Victoria and Alexandria Mujica’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants Miguel Victoria and Alexandria Mujica demur to the first amended complaint.

 

Legal Standard

 

A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged. (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true”].) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet-and-confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

Defendants filed a Declaration on Optional Judicial Council Form CIV-140 stating that Plaintiff failed to respond to the meet-and-confer request as of October 19, 2023. (Declaration of Demurring Party ¶ 2b.) Defendants have therefore satisfied their statutory meet-and-confer obligation.

 

Misjoinder of Parties

 

            Defendants first demur to the Complaint under Code of Civil Procedure section 410.30(d) for failure to join an indispensable party. An “indispensable party” is a party who “is subject to service of process and whose joinder will not deprive the court of jurisdiction over the subject matter,” and whom “(1) in his absence complete relief cannot be accorded among those already parties or (2) he claims an interest relating to the subject of the action and is so situated that the disposition of the action in his absence may (i) as a practical matter impair or impede his ability to protect that interest or (ii) leave any of the persons already parties subject to a substantial risk of incurring double, multiple, or otherwise inconsistent obligations by reason of his claimed interest.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 389.)

 

            As Defendants correctly state, each of the four causes of action asserted in the First Amended Complaint seeks to set aside the trustee sale and restore title to Plaintiff. Any challenge to the ownership of the property must name as a defendant the current owner whose interest is challenged. (See Hardie v. Chew Fish Yuen (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 301, 305-307.) The First Amended Complaint freely states that the person who purchased the subject property is an individual named Benjamin Dao, who is not a named party. (Complaint ¶¶ 35-37.) The Court therefore concurs with Defendants that Plaintiff has failed to join an indispensable party. For this reason alone, the First Amended Complaint is deficient.

 

First Cause of Action: Set Aside Trustee Sale

 

            Defendants demur to the first cause of action to set aside the trustee sale for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            The first cause of action claims that the trustee sale must be set aside for “procedural irregularities,” specifically for violation of Civil Code section 2923.5. (FAC ¶¶ 42-43, 50.) Section 2923.5 establishes the procedure under which a mortgage servicer, mortgagee, trustee, beneficiary, or authorized agent may record a notice of default, and specifically requires that the borrower be contacted before recording the notice to “assess the household’s financial situations and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure.” (Civ. Code § 2923.5(a)(2).) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to do so. (FAC ¶ 43.)

 

            Defendants argue that this cause of action is deficient because Plaintiff cannot challenge the validity of a sale in favor of a bona fide purchaser. This argument draws the improper inference that the purchaser was, in fact, a bona fide purchaser. However, the Court must draw the opposite inference in the context of a demurrer since that inference favors the non-demurring party.

 

            Defendants also argue that the first cause of action is deficient because Plaintiff does not allege that he tendered full payment of the debt. As stated by the Court of Appeal in Arnolds Management Corp. v. Eischen, “[i]t is settled that an action to set aside a trustee’s sale for irregularities in sale notice or procedure should be accompanied by an offer to pay the full amount of the debt for which the property was security.” (Arnolds Mgmt. Corp. v. Eischen (1984) 158 Cal.App.3d 575, 578.) However, an offer of tender is not required for an action under section 2923.5 based on an improper notice of default. (Mabry v. Superior Court (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 208, 225-226.) This argument is therefore without merit.

 

            The Court therefore finds that the first cause of action is not deficient for failure to allege sufficient facts, notwithstanding the misjoinder of parties.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED for misjoinder of parties only.

 

//

 

//

Second Cause of Action: Quiet Title

 

            Defendants demur to the second cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim. A claim for quiet title must name all persons, known or unknown, claiming an interest in the subject property. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 762.010; 762.020.) As discussed above, Plaintiff has not named the owner of the property. In addition, Defendants assert without explanation or citation to the pleadings that Plaintiff cannot allege that any of the named Defendants have adverse claims to title. Conclusory assertions are not a basis to challenge a cause of action, notwithstanding the other deficiencies in the First Amended Complaint. However, the Court agrees that the second cause of action fails to name one of the parties with an ownership interest in the property and is therefore defective.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Third Cause of Action: Cancellation of Instrument

 

            Defendants demur to the third cause of action for cancellation of instrument for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            Civil Code 3412 provides that “[a] written instrument, in respect to which there is a reasonable apprehension that if left outstanding it may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable, may, upon his application, be so adjudged, and ordered to be delivered up or canceled.” (Civ. Code § 3412.)

 

            Defendants contend that this cause of action is inadequately pled because the First Amended Complaint does not set forth any basis for why the Deed of Trust, Notice of Default, or Notice of Trustee’s Sale are void or voidable, and does not allege a reasonable apprehension of serious injury. To the contrary, Plaintiff contends that the Notice of Default did not comply with Civil Code section 2923.5. (See FAC ¶ 43.) Moreover, the contention that Plaintiff does not allege a reasonable apprehension of serious injury is specious. Plaintiff alleges that his home was foreclosed upon. (FAC ¶¶ 12-14.) The Court therefore declines to find that this cause of action is inadequately pled in this respect.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED for misjoinder of parties only.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Recission

 

            Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for recission for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            The fourth cause of action for recission is based on the claim that Defendants were unlicensed lenders. (FAC ¶ 64.) However, as Defendants argue, a loan by an unlicensed lender is not void but is merely subject to usury laws. (See Creative Ventures LLC v. Jim Ward & Associates (2010) 195 Cal.App.4th 1430, 1441-42.) The remedy for a usurious loan is the recovery of the usurious interest, plus statutory treble damages. (Civ. Code § 1916-3(a); Stock v. Meek (1950) 35 Cal.2d 809, 817.) The Court is therefore persuaded that the fourth cause of action is defective because it does not state a legal basis for recission of the trustee sale.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff has not responded to the Demurrer and has not shown how the Complaint might be amended. However, as the deficiencies arise from the failure to name indispensable parties and the absence of detail, the Court finds good cause to permit leave to amend in this instance.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendants Miguel Victoria and Alexandria Mujica’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with 30 days’ leave to amend.

 

            Moving Parties to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

By submitting on the tentative you have, in essence, waived your right to be present at the hearing, and you should be aware that the court may not adopt the tentative, and may issue an order which modifies the tentative ruling in whole or in part.
