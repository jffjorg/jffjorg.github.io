DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 18VECV00128    Hearing Date: January 2, 2025    Dept: O

 

                                            

 

 

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES – NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

JUANA MENDOZA; FABIAN VARGAS; AND EDMUNDO VARGAS (SUING BY HIS GUARDIAN AD LITEM, JUANA MENDOZA),

 

Plaintiffs,

vs.

NORTH HILLS WILLS APTS, INC.,

 

Defendant.

Case No.: 18VECV00128

ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFFS’ Motion for ATTORNEY’S FEES AND COSTS

 

I.       INTRODUCTION

Plaintiffs Juana Mendoza, Fabian Vargas, and Jose Edmundo Vargas (collectively, Plaintiffs) brought this action against their former landlord, Defendant North Hills Wills Apts., Inc. (“Defendant”) alleging three causes of action: (1) Breach of the Warranty of Habitability; (2) Negligent Maintenance; and (3) Nuisance.

Following a jury trial that spanned several days, the jury returned a verdict in favor of Plaintiffs as to all three of their causes of action and awarded damages in the amount of $14,400 to Juana Mendoza, $8,100 to Fabian Vargas, and $7,500 to Jose Edmundo Vargas.

On July 5, 2024, the Court awarded Plaintiffs’ attorney’s fees (“Order”). Defendant did not file an opposition.

On December 3, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Vacate the Order on the basis of Defendant’s counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect and ordered Defendant to file its opposition to Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs (“Motion”).

On December 5, 2024, Defendant filed its Opposition to the Motion.

Plaintiffs filed their Reply on December 16, 2024.

II.     PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 26, 2018, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint against Defendant.

On December 6, 2018, Defendant filed its Answer to the Complaint.

On February 6, 2024, the jury trial commenced.

On February 20, 2024, the jury returned with a verdict in favor of all three Plaintiffs as to all three of their causes of action in the amounts listed above.

On May 6, 2024, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiffs.

On July 5, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ Motion in part.

On December 3, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Vacate the Order.

On December 5, 2024, Defendant filed its Opposition.

On December 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Reply.

III.    LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.420 provides:

“(a) If a party fails to admit the genuineness of any document or the truth of any matter when requested to do so under this chapter, and if the party requesting that admission thereafter proves the genuineness of that document or the truth of that matter, the party requesting the admission may move the court for an order requiring the party to whom the request was directed to pay the reasonable expenses incurred in making that proof, including reasonable attorney's fees.

(b) The court shall make this order unless it finds any of the following:

(1) An objection to the request was sustained or a response to it was waived under Section 2033.290.

(2) The admission sought was of no substantial importance.

(3) The party failing to make the admission had reasonable ground to believe that that party would prevail on the matter.

(4) There was other good reason for the failure to admit.”

Any fees awarded pursuant to this section “can be awarded only for the period after [the losing party] denied the requests for admission.” (Yoon v. Cam IX Trust (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 388, 395 (emphasis added) (citing Garcia v. Hyster Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 724, 736).)

A party seeking to avail itself of the exceptions set forth in subdivision (b) has the burden of proving such exception(s) apply because, among other reasons, “as a practical matter, the denying party is in the best position to explain the reasons for the denial.” (Samsky v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 517, 524.)

“An RFA has ‘substantial importance’ if it is ‘central to disposition of the case.’… In evaluating whether a ‘good reason’ exists for denying a request to admit, ‘a court may properly consider whether at the time the denial was made the party making the denial held a reasonably entertained good faith belief that the party would prevail on the issue at trial.’” (Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Fam. Servs. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 690 (quoting Brooks v. American Broadcasting Co. (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 500, 509 & 511).) “Where it becomes clear from evidence introduced by either party at trial that the party who denied for lack of information or belief had access to the information at the time requests for admissions were propounded, sanctions are justified because that party has a duty to investigate.” (Bloxham v. Saldinger (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 729, 752 (cleaned up).)

Statutory attorney fees are ordinarily determined by the court pursuant to the “lodestar” or “touchstone” method. Under this approach, a base amount is calculated from a compilation of time reasonably spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney. (See, e.g., Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 639.) The base amount is then adjusted in light of various factors. The Supreme Court has not mandated “a blanket ‘lodestar only’ approach; every fee-shifting statute must be construed on its own merits.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1136.)

Normally, a “reasonable” hourly rate is the prevailing rate charged by attorneys of similar skill and experience in the relevant community. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) However, the court may consider various other factors when determining a reasonable hourly rate—e.g., the attorney's skill and experience, the nature of the work performed, the relevant area of expertise and the attorney's customary billing rates. (See Flannery v. California Highway Patrol (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 629, 632-33.)

The purpose of a lodestar multiplier is “primarily to compensate the attorney for the prevailing party at a rate reflecting the risk of nonpayment in contingency cases as a class.” (Ketchum, 24 Cal.4th at 1138.) The party seeking the fee enhancement bears the burden of proof. (See id.) The lodestar amount may be adjusted based on various factors specific to the case in order to fix the attorney fees at fair market value for the services provided. (See PLCM Group, 22 Cal.4th at 1095-96; see also Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1329.) The factors include “‘(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, and (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.’” (Holguin, 229 Cal.App.4th at 1332-33 (quoting Ketchum, 24 Cal.4th at 1132).) However, the multiplier may not be imposed merely as a sanction to punish the losing party nor may the court consider factors it already considered when arriving at the lodestar amount. (See Ketchum, 24 Cal.4th at 1139.)

IV.    ANALYSIS

A.      Defendant Has Proven Section 2033.420(b)(1) Applies.

First, the Court must examine whether Defendant’s responses to the Requests for Admission (“RFA’s”) the instant motion relies on Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.420(a)’s fee trigger provision. Plaintiffs rely on Defendant’s responses to RFA’s Nos. 1-7, 9-11, and 21. (See Motion, pp. 3-4.) Defendant’s response to each RFA was the following: “Objection. The request is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Calls for information which is completely irrelevant to whether untenantable conditions existed, and is harassing, and unduly burdensome, and grossly overbroad. The request is also vague, ambiguous and overbroad as to time. The request is also overbroad with respect to the term ‘subject property’. Plaintiffs’ lawsuit is based on the habitability of one unit and not the entire apartment complex..[sic]” (Declaration of Christian Oronsaye dated May 15, 2024, ¶¶ 4-5, Exs. 1-2.)

In Opposition, Defendant argues Plaintiffs forfeited the right to attorney’s fees by failing to compel further Defendant’s further responses to their RFA’s, as Defendant asserted only objections to all eleven of Plaintiffs’ RFA’s. (See Opposition, pp. 3-4.) Plaintiffs therefore waived the right to further responses and an award of costs for proving the genuineness of the document or truth of the matter requested is barred pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.420(b)(1). (See id.)

In Reply, Plaintiffs argue Defendant objected to the RFA’s but that “there was nothing left to compel in the admissions responses because the answers of Defendants[sic] following the admissions were complete, i.e. they left no part of the admission unanswered (as in the case of an assertion of an inability to admit or deny, for example).” (Reply, pp. 3-4.) In support of their argument, Plaintiffs rely on American Federation of State, County & Municipal Employees v. Metropolitan Water Dist. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 247, 268 (“American Federation”).

The Court finds Plaintiffs are not entitled to an award of attorney’s fees because Defendant has met its burden of proving the exception contained in Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.420(b)(1) applies. Defendant provided only objections to Plaintiffs’ RFA’s Nos. 1-7, 9-11, and 21. Defendant did not provide admissions or denials to any of the RFA’s at issue. As a result, Plaintiffs should have moved to compel Defendant’s further responses to their RFA’s. (See Wimberly v. Derby Cycle Corp. (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 618, 636.) Plaintiffs did not do so, thereby waiving their right to further responses pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.290(c) and barring their instant fee request. (See American Federation, 126 Cal.App.4th at 268.)

Plaintiffs’ reliance on American Federation is misplaced. In American Federation, “the Local did not simply object to the nine requests or answer only part of those requests. As noted, after posing objections, the Local responded by admitting in part and/or denying the entire request, and so no response was needed.” (American Federation, 126 Cal.App.4th at 268 (emphasis in original).) For example, “[t]he Local responded that it ‘objects to this request on the ground that it seeks information beyond the scope of discovery and it is compound. Without waiving these objections, Local 1902 responds as follows: Deny.’” (American Federation, 126 Cal.App.4th at 267.) “[T]he Local’s admissions and/or denials provided complete responses to the requests, thus leaving nothing to address in a motion to compel.” (American Federation, 126 Cal.App.4th at 269.)

Here, by contrast, Defendant did not object and then issue a denial or partial denial—Defendant provided only objections. Plaintiffs should have filed a motion to compel further, and their failure to do so prohibits them from recovering under Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.420(a) pursuant to Code Civil Proc. § 2033.420(b)(1).

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ Motion.

ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTION FOR DISMISSAL OF MOORE’S COMPLAINT  

 

 

 

I.              BACKGROUND 

This case arises out of a motor vehicle accident between Plaintiffs Miguel Alanis (“Alanis”) and Shane Moore (“Moore”)(collectively, “Plaintiffs”) and Defendants Callan Buechsenschuetz (“Callan”) and Richard Buechsenschuetz (collectively, “Defendants”).

Defendants now seek terminating, issue, or discovery sanctions against Moore.

///  

II.           PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On March 16, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging (1) Negligence.

On January 16, 2024, Defendants filed their Answer.

On August 23, 2024, this Court granted Moore’s counsel’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel.

On September 10, 2024, this Court granted Callan’s Motion to Compel Moore’s Answers to Demand for Production of Documents, Motion to Compel Moore’s Answers to Form Interrogatories, and Motion to Compel Moore’s Answers to Special Interrogatories (“Order”).

On November 27, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Terminating Sanction for Dismissal of Moore’s Complaint (“Motion”).

On December 24, 2024, Defendants filed Notice of Non-Opposition.

III.        LEGAL STANDARD

A.            Terminating, Issue, Evidentiary, and/or Sanctions

On motion, if a party “fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction” as provided in Section 2030.010, et seq. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(h).)[1] The “court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after an opportunity for hearing, may impose” monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions “against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process[.]” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a)-(d).) Failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are misuses of the discovery process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)   

///

IV.         ANALYSIS

Defendants request this Court dismiss Moore’s Complaint because Moore has not complied with the Court’s September 10, 2024, Order. (See Motion, pp. 4-5.) At the end of their Motion, Defendants also request alternative “issue and evidentiary sanctions” to preclude “Plaintiff from presenting any evidence, including testimony on any of the matters addressed by the unanswered discovery, and imposing monetary sanctions in a sum determined by the court.” (Id. at pp. 5-6.)

Moore did not oppose.

The Court declines to issue Defendants’ requested terminating, issue, or evidentiary sanctions. As an initial matter, Defendants did not submit a separate statement to support their alternative request for issue or evidentiary sanctions, as required by Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a). Even if the Court deems Defendants excused from this requirement pursuant to rule 3.1345(b) as a result of Moore’s failure to comply, the Court cannot grant the Motion as to issue or evidence sanctions because Defendants did not include either of those sanctions in their Notice of Motion and did not identify the specific issues or evidence they would like established or excluded. Defendants’ general statement in their conclusion asking the Court “preclude Plaintiff from presenting any evidence, including testimony on any of the matters addressed by the unanswered discovery” is simply insufficient.

As for Defendants’ requested terminating sanctions by striking the Complaint in its entirety, the Court finds such sanctions to be too harsh and procedural overkill given that there are two plaintiffs but only one is in violation of this Court’s order. While the Court expects its orders to be followed, Moore has failed to respond to only one of the Court’s orders and the Court does not find this singular failure sufficient to justify dismissal of his complaint.

In light of the substantive shortcomings outlined above combined with the fact Defendants are equally unspecific about the amount of monetary sanctions they are seeking for Moore’s failure to comply with the Order, as opposed to the fees associated with bringing the instant Motion, the Court finds that Defendants have not prevailed and are therefore not entitled to monetary sanctions either.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion.

V.            CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendants’ Motion for Terminating Sanction for Dismissal of Moore’s Complaint.
