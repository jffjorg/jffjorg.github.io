DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.   

Case Number: 22STCV37262    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ERICA WOHLWEND, 

 

         vs.

 

VEGAMOUR, INC.

 Case No.:  22STCV37262

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 1, 2024

 

Plaintiff Erica Wohlwend’s motion to compel further responses from Defendant Vegamour, Inc. to her Request for Production of Documents (Set Two) is granted.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is granted against Vegamour, Inc., and its counsel of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $3,660.00, payable within 20 days of this ruling.

Plaintiff Erica Wohlwend’s motion to compel further responses from Defendant Vegamour, Inc. to her Request for Production of Documents (Set Three) is granted.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is granted against Vegamour, Inc., and its counsel of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $3,360.00, payable within 20 days of this ruling.

Plaintiff Erica Wohlwend’s motion to compel further responses from Defendant Vegamour, Inc. to her Special Interrogatories (Set Two) is granted.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is granted against Vegamour, Inc., and its counsel of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $3,360.00, payable within 20 days of this ruling.

Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is denied.

 

Plaintiff Erica Wohlwend (“Wohlwend”) (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant Vegamour, Inc. (“Vegamour”) (“Defendant”) to her Request for Production of Documents (Set Two) (“RFP 2”) Nos. 35 and 36 and requests sanctions against Vegamour and its attorney of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, in the amount of $8,400.00.  (Notice Motion RFP 2, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2017.010, 2030.010(b), 2030.220(a)-(b), 2030.300(a), 2023.030(a), 2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), 2030.300(d).)

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses from Vegamour to her Request for Production (Set Three) (“RFP 3”) and requests sanctions against Vegamour and its attorney of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, in the amount of $8,460.00.  (Notice Motion RFP 3, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2017.010, 2030.010(b), 2030.220(a)-(b), 2030.300(a), 2023.030(a), 2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), 2030.300(d).)

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses from Vegamour to her Special Interrogatories (Set Two) (“SROG”) and requests sanctions against Vegamour and its attorney of record, Fisher & Phillips LLP, in the amount of $8,460.00.  (Notice Motion SROG, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2017.010, 2030.010(b), 2030.220(a) and (b), 2030.300(a), 2023.030(a), 2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), 2030.300(d).)

 

Meet and Confer

On November 2, 2023, the parties engaged in an IDC before this Court, regarding Plaintiff’s RFP (Set 3 and 4) and SROG (Set 2) and the issues were deemed unresolved.  (11/2/23 Minute Order; 10/27/23 Defendant’s Statement for IDC.)

 

1.     Motion to Compel Further RFP 2

Having reviewed Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Vegamour to Plaintiff’s RFP 2, the Court rules as follows.

          On February 24, 2023, Plaintiff served RFP 2 on Vegamour.  (Decl. of Levy ¶3, Exh. 2.)  On June 22, 2023, Vegamour served its response.  (Decl. of Axelrad ¶4.)  On January 23, 2024, Vegamour served its supplementary responses to RFP 2 with boilerplate objections.  (Decl. of Levy ¶4, Exh. 3.)  On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Vegamour’s counsel a meet and confer letter.  (Decl. of Levy ¶5, Exh. 4.)  Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses. 

          RFP 2, No. 35, requests, “All COMMUNICATIONS sent or received by plaintiff between August 2021 and April 2022 that refer or relate to defendant’s ‘GRO AGELESS’ product(s).”  (Motion RFP 2 SS, pg. 3.)

          C.C.P. §2031.240(c)(1) provides, “[i]f an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”  (C.C.P. §2031.240(c)(1).)  “The information in the privilege log must be sufficiently specific to allow a determination of whether each withheld document is or is not in fact privileged.”  (Wellpoint Health Networks, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 110, 130.)

Here, Defendant’s counsel argues that the documents in question are privileged and thus not subject to discovery.  However, despite this assertion, Defendant’s counsel has failed to produce a privilege log to demonstrate this.

          Defendant’s opposition states, “Defendant and Plaintiff have been working to put in place a protective order with an attorneys-eyes only provision with regard to Defendant’s confidential, proprietary, and trade secret information. (See Declaration of Jacob M. Axelrad, ¶ 8.) Once the protective order is in place, Defendant has agreed to produce responsive documents to Plaintiff’s Request No. 35.”  Defendant has willful delayed producing the discovery requested in RFP 2 No. 35 by failing to submit to an authorized discovery method and refusing to respond on the basis of evasive objections meant only to prevent the discovery of evidence that is discoverable under the applicable standard. 

Defendant is ordered to produce Code-compliant, non-evasive responses to RFP 2, No. 35, subject to a privilege log, within 20 days of this Court’s ruling.

RFP 2, No. 36, requests, “All COMMUNICATIONS sent or received by plaintiff between January and April 2022 that refer or relate to ‘GRO Biotin Gummies.’”  (Motion RFP 2 SS, pg. 5.)

For the same reasons stated with regard to RFP 2, No. 35, this Court orders Defendant to produce Code-compliant, non-evasive responses to RFP 2, No. 36, subject to a privilege log, within 20 days of this Court’s ruling.

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $8,460.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record.  Plaintiff requests attorneys’ fees for time spent meeting and conferring with Defendant, which is required by statute and therefore not compensable on the instant motion.  Therefore, the Court reduces the number of hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent working on the instant motion from the requested 14 hours on the moving papers alone.

          The Court awards sanctions against Defendant and its counsel pursuant to C.C.P. §§2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), and 2031.310(h) in the reduced amount of $3,660.00, calculated as follows:

([4.5 hours to prepare instant motion + 1 hour to review the opposition and draft a reply + 0.5 hour to attend hearing on motion] x $600.00) + $60.00 filing fee = $3,660.00

The sanctions are payable, jointly and severally, by Vegamour and its attorneys from Fisher & Phillips LLP, within 20 days of this order.

Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is denied.

Moving Party is to give notice of this ruling.

 

2.     Motion to Compel Further RFP 3

Having reviewed Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Vegamour to Plaintiff’s RFP 3, the Court rules as follows.

          On August 11, 2023, Plaintiff served RFP 3 on Vegamour.  (Decl. of Levy ¶3, Exh. 2.)[1]  On September 12, 2023, Vegamour served its response.  (Decl. of Axelrad ¶4.)  On January 23, 2024, Vegamour served its supplementary responses to RFP 3 with boilerplate objections.  (See Decl. of Levy ¶7, Exh. 4.)  On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Vegamour’s counsel a meet and confer letter.  (Decl. of Levy ¶8, Exh. 5.)  Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses. 

          RFP 3, Nos. 41-50, 52, 53, 57, and 58 ask Defendant to produce: (41) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Dan Hodgdon”; (42) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Mallory

Yohannan”; (43) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Darren Summer”; (44) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Lily Shao”; (45) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Jacob Hernandez”; (46) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Carla Durango”; (47) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Candice Macias”; (48) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and Erin McGilvra”; (49) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and any of YOUR

former employees”; (50) “Any and all Slack COMMUNICATIONS between Plaintiff and any of YOUR current employees”; (52) “Any and all COMMUNICATIONS between YOUR current employees and the National Advertising Division of the Better Business Bureau on behalf of YOU”; (53) “Any and all COMMUNICATIONS between YOUR former employees and the National Advertising Division of the Better Business Bureau on behalf of YOU”; (54) “Any and all articles ever posted on longhairtrends.com relating to the GRO Ageless Anti*-*Grey Hair Serum”; (55) “Any and all articles ever posted on hairhealthsecrets.com relating to the GRO Ageless Anti-Grey Hair Serum”; (56) “Any and all reviews ever posted on longhairtrends.com relating to the GRO

Ageless Anti-Grey Hair Serum”; (57) “Any and all reviews ever posted on hairhealthsecrets.com relating to the GRO Ageless Anti-Grey Hair Serum”; and (58) “Any and all Sephora Marketing Decks created by Plaintiff”.  (See RFP 3, SS.)

C.C.P. §2031.240(c)(1) provides, “[i]f an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”  (C.C.P. §2031.240(c)(1).)  “The information in the privilege log must be sufficiently specific to allow a determination of whether each withheld document is or is not in fact privileged.”  (Wellpoint Health Networks, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 110, 130.)

Here, Defendant’s counsel argues that the documents in question are overly broad and privileged and thus not subject to discovery.  However, despite this assertion, Defendant’s counsel has failed to produce a privilege log to demonstrate this.

          Plaintiff’s discovery requests are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  First, the messages from the company’s communication platform, Slack, are likely to lead to discovery of admissible evidence in the form of complaints or communications by Plaintiff to others or by others to Plaintiff regarding the allegations made against Defendant.  Second, Plaintiff’s causes of action surround allegations of false advertising and the CEO Dan Hodgdon instructing Plaintiff to make false and illegal claims involving the product in question.  The documents requested are relevant because relate to the truth and transparency of Defendant’s advertising, especially since the advertisements in question were about the same product Plaintiff was allegedly instructed to make false claim(s) about.  Third, with regard to RFP 3, Nos. 54-57, Plaintiff requests any and all articles concerning the product in question, which is relevant because they were determined by the National Advertising Division to be advertising and marketing for Defendant’s product in question. These requests would include all articles posted before during and after the complaints and Plaintiff’s termination. The articles from after Plaintiff’s termination are relevant and reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence because they potentially give insight into the patterns and practices of Defendant’s advertising and whether CEO Dan Hodgdon responded to or ignored Plaintiff’s alleged complaints surrounding his instruction to falsely advertise.  Finally, with regard to RFP 3, No. 58, Defendant has failed to produce the documents pursuant to a protective order; failure to produce the documents does not moot Plaintiff’s request and is in violation of C.C.P. §2031.240(c)(1).

Defendant is ordered to produce Code-compliant, non-evasive responses to RFP 3, 41-50, 52, 53, 57, and 58, subject to a privilege log, within 20 days of this Court’s ruling.

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $8,460.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record.  Plaintiff requests attorneys’ fees for time spent meeting and conferring with Defendant, which is required by statute and therefore not compensable on the instant motion.  Therefore, the Court reduces the number of hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent working on the instant motion from the requested 14 hours on the moving papers alone.

          The Court awards sanctions against Defendant and its counsel pursuant to C.C.P. §§2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), and 2031.310(h) in the reduced amount of $3,360.00, calculated as follows:

([4.5 hours to prepare instant motion + 1 hour to review the opposition and draft a reply] x $600.00) + $60.00 filing fee = $3,360.00

The sanctions are payable, jointly and severally, by Vegamour and its attorneys from Fisher & Phillips LLP, within 20 days of this order.

Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is denied.

Moving Party is to give notice of this ruling.

 

3.     Motion to Compel Further SROG

Having reviewed Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Vegamour to Plaintiff’s SROG, the Court rules as follows.

          On August 11, 2023, Plaintiff served SROG (Set Two) on Vegamour.  (Decl. of Levy ¶3, Exh. 2.)  On September 12, 2024, Vegamour served incomplete and non-Code compliant responses to SROG.  (Decl. of Levy ¶4.)  On January 23, 2024, Vegamour served supplementary responses to SROG with boilerplate objections.  (Decl. of Levy ¶7, Exh. 4.)   Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses. 

          SROG Nos. 9, 16-22 asks Defendant to (9) “State any and all reasons that Dan Hodgdon is no longer YOUR CEO”; (16) “State the name and dates of employment of all copywriters YOU employed from 2019 until the present”; (17) “Provide the contact information (names, address, phone number, of all copywriters YOU employed from 2019 until the present”; (18) “State all website domains that YOU currently own”; (19) “State all review websites that YOU currently own”; (20) “State all website domains that YOU have owned from 2013 until the present”; (21) “State all review websites that YOU owned from 2013 until the present”; and (22) “State all review websites that YOU have a material connection or relationship with”. (See SROG SS.)

          Defendant’s responses assert boilerplate objections including relevance, proprietary and confidential information, attorney-client privilege, attorney work product doctrine, third-party privacy rights, and “vague and overbroad as to

the term ‘review websites.’”  Further, the information sought is not within Plaintiff’s possession nor does it involve confidential or trade secret information.  Defendant’s position that the documents in question are privileged and thus not subject to discovery is unavailing because Defendant’s counsel has failed to produce a privilege log to demonstrate such privilege.

Defendant is ordered to produce Code-compliant, non-evasive responses to SROG Nos. 9, 16-22, subject to a privilege log, within 20 days of this Court’s ruling.

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $8,460.00 against Defendant and its counsel of record.  Plaintiff requests attorneys’ fees for time spent meeting and conferring with Defendant, which is required by statute and therefore not compensable on the instant motion.  Therefore, the Court reduces the number of hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent working on the instant motion from the requested 14 hours on the moving papers alone.

          The Court awards sanctions against Defendant and its counsel pursuant to C.C.P. §§2023.010(d), 2023.010(e), and 2030.300(d) in the reduced amount of $3,360.00, calculated as follows:

([4.5 hours to prepare instant motion + 1 hour to review the opposition and draft a reply] x $600.00) + $60.00 filing fee = $3,360.00

The sanctions are payable, jointly and severally, by Vegamour and its attorneys from Fisher & Phillips LLP, within 20 days of this order.

Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is denied.

Moving Party is to give notice of this ruling.
