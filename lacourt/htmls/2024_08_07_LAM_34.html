Case Number: 20STCV13849    Hearing Date: August 7, 2024    Dept: 34

Plaintiff Raul Maldonado, individually, and on behalf of all other Aggrieved Employees’ Motion for Preliminary Approval of Class Action and PAGA Settlement is GRANTED.

Background[1]  

This action was instituted on April 9, 2020. On January 24, 2024, Plaintiff Raul Maldonado, individually and on behalf of all other aggrieved employees, filed a Third Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against FS Hotels LA, Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10 for:

1.               Failure to provide meal periods and pay meal period premiums at correct rates (Labor §§ 226.7, 512, 558)

2.               Failure to provide rest periods and pay rest period premiums at correct rates (Labor Code §§ 226.7, 516)

3.               Failure to pay all wages due during employment, including minimum wage and straight time (Labor Code §§ 1194, 1194.2, 1197)

4.               Failure to Pay Overtime (Labor Code §§ 204, 510, 558, 1194, 1198)

5.               Failure to pay all wages due upon separation from employment (Labor Code §§ 201-203)

6.               Failure to provide accurate wage statements and maintain accurate payroll records (Labor Code § 226 et seq.)

7.               Failure to Reimburse Necessary Business Expenses (Labor Code §§ 2800 and 2802)

8.               Unfair and unlawful business practices (California Business & Professions Code, § 17200 et seq.)

9.               Civil Penalties pursuant to the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004 (Cal. Labor Code, § 2698 et seq.)

A Hearing on the Motion for Preliminary Approval of Class Action Settlement and Order to Show Cause Re: Complex Determination are set for August 7, 2024.

Discussion

Plaintiff moves for an order granting preliminary approval of the “Joint Stipulation of Class Action and PAGA Settlement” (“SA”).[2]

Preliminary Approval Standard

California Rules of Court, rule 3.769 sets forth the procedures for settlement of class actions in California. Approval occurs in two steps: (1) an early “preliminary” review by the trial court; and (2) a subsequent “final” review after notice of the settlement has been distributed to class members for their comments and objections. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(c)-(g); Cellphone Termination Fee Cases (2010) 180 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1118.)

“Any party to a settlement agreement may serve and file a written notice of motion for preliminary approval of the settlement. The settlement agreement and proposed notice to class members must be filed with the motion, and the proposed order must be lodged with the motion.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(c).)

If the court grants preliminary approval of a settlement, “its order must include the time, date, and place of the final approval hearing; the notice to be given to the class; and any other matters deemed necessary for the proper conduct of a settlement hearing. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(e).) “If the court has certified the action as a class action, notice of the final approval hearing must be given to the class members in the manner specified by the court. The notice must contain an explanation of the proposed settlement and procedures for class members to follow in filing written objections to it and in arranging to appear at the settlement hearing and state any objections to the proposed settlement.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(f).)

The Settlement

Plaintiff filed the SA, which attaches proposed notice documents as Exhibits A-C thereto. Plaintiff has also submitted a proposed order.

The “Class” is defined as “all non-exempt employees of Defendant at any time from April 9, 2016, through September 25, 2023.” (SA, ¶ 1.4). “Class Member” is defined as “an individual who falls within the definition of the Class.” (Id., ¶ 1.5). The “Class Period” is defined as “the period from April 9, 2016 through September 25, 2023.” (Id., ¶ 1.8).

The terms of the Settlement Agreement provide for “Settlement Fund—Maximum” (“SF”) of $1,300,000.00 (not including the employer’s share of payroll taxes). (Id., ¶ 1.34). The parties have agreed to the following allocations to be paid from the SF:

1.               A Net Settlement Payment (“NSP”), which is the SF, less the Service Payment,

Attorneys’ Fees, Litigation Expenses, PAGA Payment and Settlement Administration Costs (Id., ¶ 1.18). The estimated NSP is approximately $750,000.00. (Perlman Decl., ¶ 21).

2.               A Service Payment of $10,000.00 to Raul Maldonado (“Maldonado”) (SA, ¶¶ 1.31, 6.4);

3.               Attorneys’ Fees of up to 35% of the SF (i.e., $455,000.00) (Id., ¶¶ 1.3, 7.1);

4.               Litigation Expenses of up to $30,000.00 (Id., ¶¶ 1.17, 7.1);

5.               A PAGA payment of $50,000.00 (75% of which will be paid to the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency [“LWDA”] and 25% of which will be paid to the “PAGA Group Members” [defined below][3]) (Id., ¶¶ 1.25, 7.1);

6.               Settlement Administration Costs of up to $15,000.00 (Id., ¶ 1.32).

The SA calls for automatic payment to class members (i.e., no claim forms used, no reversion to Defendant). Each Class Member’s settlement payment is calculated by dividing the NSP by the total number of Workweeks[4] worked by all Participating Class Members (i.e., Class Members who do not opt out) during the Class Period and multiplying the result by each Participating Class Member’s Workweeks. (SA, ¶ 6.2(a).) The payment to each Class Member will be apportioned as 33 1/3% wages for which each Class Member will be issued an IRS Form W-2 and 66 2/3% penalties, interest and other non-wage payments for which each Class Member will be issued an IRS Form 1099. (Id., ¶ 6.2(b).) Phoenix Settlement Administrators (“Phoenix”) will deduct applicable taxes from the wage portion of the settlement payment. (Id.)

Defendant has represented that, as of September 25, 2023, there were 1,305 potential Class Members having a total of 76,919 Workweeks. (Id., ¶ 8.12). If the final figures exceed 1,305 and/or 76,919 by more than 5%, Defendant must either proportionately increase the SF or amend the class period end date so as to align with the SF of $1,300,000.00. (Id.)

If 5% or more of the Class Members opt out of the settlement, Defendant has the sole right to withdraw from and void this settlement. (Id., ¶ 8.11).

Any Class Member who does not opt out is deemed to have released Defendant of any and all federal and state law claims, whether known or unknown, arising during the Class Period based on the allegations that were asserted or could have been asserted in the operative complaint based on the factual allegations contained therein. (Id., ¶ 10.1.) Maldonado makes an additional general release. (Id., ¶ 10.2).

Class Members who wish to exclude themselves from the class have the right to opt out from the settlement by mailing, faxing, or emailing a written exclusion (containing specified information) from the settlement to Phoenix no later than 45 calendar days after Phoenix mails the Notice [see below] or by completing and timely returning the Request for Exclusion form provided with the Class Notice. (Id., ¶¶ 8.4 and 8.6). Class Members who do not opt out of the settlement may object to the settlement by mailing a signed and dated written objection containing specified information to Phoenix postmarked no later than 45 calendar days after Phoenix mails the Notice. (Id., ¶ 8.4). Class Members may also appear at the Final Approval Hearing, either in person or through a lawyer they retain at their own expense. (Id.) Any checks issued will remain negotiable for 180 days from the date of mailing. (Id., ¶ 9.4). If any checks are not cashed within 180 days they will be void and a stop payment order may be placed on the check. (Id.) The funds represented by all uncashed checks will be disbursed to the Legal Aid Foundation of Los Angeles. (Id.)

Fairness, Adequacy and Reasonableness

To prevent fraud, collusion or unfairness, a settlement or dismissal of a class action, or of a cause of action in a class action, or as to a party, requires court approval after hearing. (Cal. Rules of Court, rules 3.769(a), 3.770(a).)

A trial court must determine that the settlement is fair, adequate and reasonable. (In re Microsoft I-V Cases (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 706, 723.) “It is vested with a broad discretion in making this determination. . . [t]he trial court operates under a presumption of fairness when the settlement is the result of arm’s-length negotiation, investigation and discovery that are sufficient to permit counsel and the court to act intelligently, counsel are experienced in similar litigation, and the percentage of objectors is small.” (Id.)

Here, the presumption of fairness applies. Plaintiff filed the original complaint on April 9, 2020, the First Amended Complaint on July 16, 2020 and the Second Amended Complaint on January 25, 2021. (Perlman Decl., ¶ 2)[5].

On July 14, 2021, following an exchange of employee data and analysis thereof by Plaintiff’s expert, the parties participated in a mediation session with Retired Judge Richard Stone (“Judge Stone”). (Id.). On January 12, 2022, the parties participated in a second mediation session with Judge Stone. (Id.). Judge Stone thereafter continued working with the parties and eventually an agreement was reached, resulting in the SA. (Id.) The SA was executed in March 2024. (Id.)

The terms of the SA also appear to be reasonable. The settlement terms are set forth above.

Plaintiff’s Third Amended Complaint filed on January 24, 2024 contains causes of action for:

(1)       Failure to provide meal periods and pay meal period premiums at correct rates (Labor §§ 226.7, 512, 558);

(2)       Failure to provide rest periods and pay rest period premiums at correct rates (Labor Code §§ 226.7, 516);

(3)       Failure to pay all wages due during employment, including minimum wage and straight time (Labor Code §§ 1194, 1194.2, 1197);

(4)       Failure to Pay Overtime (Labor Code §§ 204, 510, 558, 1194, 1198);

(5)       Failure to pay all wages due upon separation from employment (Labor Code §§ 201-203);

(6)       Failure to provide accurate wage statements and maintain accurate payroll records (Labor Code § 226 et seq.);

(7)       Failure to Reimburse Necessary Business Expenses (Labor Code §§ 2800 and 2802);

(8)       Unfair and unlawful business practices (California Business & Professions Code, § 17200 et seq.); and,

(9)       Civil Penalties pursuant to the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004 (Cal. Labor Code, § 2698 et seq.).

Plaintiff asserts that since at least April 9, 2016, Defendant failed to (1) provide meal and rest periods, (2) pay all wages due during employment, including minimum, straight time, and overtime wages, (3) pay all wages due at separation from employment, (4) provide accurate wage statements and maintain accurate payroll records and to (5) reimburse necessary business expenses and committed unfair and unlawful business practices.

Defendant produced a 15% sample of class member time and payroll data, as well as relevant policy documents for the class. (Id., ¶ 8). Raosoft Sample Size Calculator (www.raosoft.com) indicates that a 15% sample would provide a confidence level of 86% with a 5.43% margin of error. (Id.) Based on the above data of 206,016 violations x $18.00 (average hourly rate), Plaintiff’s counsel Douglas Perlman (“Perlman”) represents that Plaintiff’s maximum potential damages on the meal period claims is $3,708,288.00. (Id., ¶ 9). Perlman represents that Plaintiff’s maximum potential damages on the rest period claims is $4,636,278.00. (Id., ¶ 10).

Perlman represents that Plaintiff’s Labor Code § 226 claim is derivative of the other claims and that, based on the 76,919 workweeks during the class period, which equates to approximately 38,459 paychecks, the value would be approximately $3,845,900.00 (i.e., 38,459 x $100.00/paycheck), subject to a reduction based upon the section 226 limitation to $4,000.00 per employee. (Id., ¶ 13).

Perlman represents that Plaintiff’s Labor Code § 203 claim is valued at $3,875,040.00 (i.e., 897 former employees[6] x 8 hours x $18.00/hour x 30 days). (Id, ¶ 14). He calculates Plaintiff’s claim for failure to reimburse for necessary business expenses at $316,975.00 (i.e., 409 x $100.00 x 7.75 years). (Id., ¶ 15). He calculates maximum potential PAGA penalties at $4,662,600.00, which figures assumes one violation per pay period (i.e., 57 months in PAGA Period x 818 employee pay periods per month x $100.00). (Id., ¶ 17).

Perlman represents that he has discounted the above maximum estimated potential damages and penalties significantly, however, because of Defendant’s defenses, the significant risks of proceeding and the discretionary nature of the penalties. (Id., ¶ 17). Perlman represents that Defendant has an enforceable arbitration agreement, which “mak[es] class certification impossible without Defendant’s waiver of the agreement,” and that if the case did not resolve as a class action but went to trial on the PAGA claims alone, there would be numerous substantive issues present regarding the likelihood of proving the merits of the various claims, including that Defendant does not have a non-compliant meal and rest break policy (Id., ¶¶ 22-23).

Perlman represents that Defendant will argue that under Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1004 (“Brinker”), a Plaintiff must show that Defendant impeded, discouraged, or prohibited employees from taking a proper break or otherwise failed to release them of all control and that this involves an individualized inquiry. (Id., ¶ 23). He also represents that there are substantial risks with Plaintiff’s rest break claims, because while an employer must “authorize and permit” rest breaks, it need not ensure they are taken, that there is no requirement that such breaks be taken and that this likewise involves an individualized inquiry and reliance on anecdotal evidence. (Id., ¶ 24).

Perlman represents that the “off the clock” claim is partially based on the “donning and doffing” of uniforms, as well as rounding and encouragement to work off the clock, and is based almost entirely on anecdoctal evidence. (Id., ¶ 11). Perlman represents that the maximum potential recovery for same would be essentially identical to the rest break claim; however, he ascribes negligible value to both, based on obstacles of certification and proof. (Id.) Perlman represents that damages analysis for Plaintiff’s overtime claims is “extremely speculative” since such work is, by definition, unrecorded, and that liability and damages would depend on anecdotal evidence (and the possibility of expert analysis based on a survey). (Id., ¶ 12). He ascribes minimal value to these claims. (Id.) Perlman represents that Defendant will likely argue that off the clock and business expense claims are problematic in the context of a class action or PAGA trial. (Id., ¶¶ 25 and 26).

Perlman points out that Plaintiffs sections 203 and 226 claims are derivative of the meal and rest break claims and will thus fail if the underlying claims fail. (Id., ¶ 27). He notes that, even if Plaintiff is successful on the underlying claims, the derivative claims have additional elements (i.e., “knowing and intentional” failure to comply for section 226 and “willful” failure for section 203). (Id., ¶ 27). He notes that Plaintiff’s PAGA claims are also derivative of the underlying claims and present the above risks, plus the risk that a court has discretion to award lesser amounts than the maximum penalty per section 2699. (Id., ¶ 28).

The gross settlement amount is approximately $995.00 per class member, regardless of duration of employment (i.e., $1,300,000.00 divided by 1,306 class members); the estimated net settlement amount is approximately $574.00 per class member (i.e., $750,000.00 divided by 1,306 class members). (Id., ¶ 21).

Notice

“If the court has certified the action as a class action, notice of the final approval hearing must be given to the class members in the manner specified by the court. The notice must contain an explanation of the proposed settlement and procedures for class members to follow in filing written objections to it and in arranging to appear at the settlement hearing and state any objections to the proposed settlement.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(f); Litwin v. iRenew Bio Energy Solutions, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 877, 883-884 [notice may not require objectors to attend final approval hearing in order to have their objections considered].) The form and content of the notice and the method used to notify the class are within the trial court’s discretion. (Wershba v. Apple Computer, Inc. (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 224, disapproved of on other grounds by Hernandez v. Restoration Hardware, Inc. (2018) 4 Cal.5th 260.)

Class members are entitled to the best practical notice under the circumstances. (Kass v. Young (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 100, 106.) The purpose of the notice is to give class members sufficient information to decide whether they should accept the benefits offered, opt out and pursue their own remedies, or object to the settlement. (Trotsky v. Los Angeles Fed. Savings & Loan Assn. (1975) 48 Cal.App.3d 143, 151-152, disapproved of on other grounds by Hernandez, supra, 4 Cal.5th 260.) The notice must advise class members “that they may be excluded from the class if they so request and that they will be bound by the judgment, whether favorable or not, if they do not request exclusion.” (Kass, supra, 67 Cal.App.3d at 106.) In a case such as this involving a significant number of class members, California authorities generally require service of the class notice by mail or similar reliable means. (Chance v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 275, 290; Cartt v. Superior Court (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 960, 972.)

The class notice is attached to the SA as Exhibit A (“Class Notice”). The Class Notice contains a definition of the class as “All individuals who were employed by FS Hotels LA, Inc., as non-exempt employees in California at any time during the period from April 9, 2016, through _________.” This definition must be amended to include the September 25, 2023 end date identified previously. (See SA, ¶ 1.4). The Class Notice must also be amended to reflect Judge Peter A. Hernandez as the judicial officer presiding over this case. The Class Notice contains all necessary information, including a discussion of the litigation and the terms of the settlement, the different options for responding to the notice, and a space containing information about when and where the fairness hearing will be held.[7] Class members are also informed about the attorneys’ fees and other deductions that will be requested from the fund, and provided with the names and contact information for Plaintiff’s counsel.

Defendants will provide Phoenix, within thirty (30) calendar days following Preliminary Approval, all of the following information about each Class Member: name, last known address (and email address, if available), social security number and the number of workweeks worked during the Class Period. (SA, ¶ 8.1).

Not later than fifteen (15) calendar days after Defendant provides Phoenix with this information, Phoenix will send each Class Member the Notice via U.S. Mail to the last known address of each Class Member and via email to those Class Members for whom Defendant has an email address. (SA, ¶ 8.2). Prior to mailing the Notice, Phoenix will run the last known addresses and social security numbers of Class Members through the United States Postal Service’s (“USPS”) National Change of Address database to verify a Class Member’s address. (SA, ¶¶ 1.29 and 8.3). Phoenix will re-mail any Notices returned by the USPS to Phoenix with a forwarding address for the recipient to that updated address no later than the last day of the Notice Period. (Id., ¶ 8.10). Phoenix will conduct a “skip trace” search for any Notice returned by the USPS to Phoenix without a forwarding address; if a more current address is found, Phoenix will promptly re-send the notice no later than the last day of the Notice Period. (Id.) For those Class Members who are re-sent the Notice, the Notice Period will be extended another twenty (20) calendar days beyond the initial Notice Period. (Id.) Within five (5) business days following the end of the extended twenty (20) calendar day period, Phoenix will provide a final report of the number of Class Members who have objected to, or requested to Opt Out of, the Settlement. (Id.).[8]

The court finds that both the method of disseminating the notice packet and the content of the Class Notice are sufficient.

PAGA Settlement

Labor Code section 2699(l)(2) states, “[t]he superior court shall review and approve any settlement of any civil action filed pursuant to this part. The proposed settlement shall be submitted to the agency at the same time that it is submitted to the court.” The court has discretion to approve any penalty amount, even $0 (See Nordstrom Commission Cases (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 576, 589.)

A PAGA claim is a form of a qui tam action. (Iskanian v. CLS Transportation Los Angeles LLC (2014) 59 Cal.4th 348, 382.) As such, the court looks to the standards in evaluating a qui tam settlement in evaluating this settlement, i.e., that the settlement is “fair, adequate, and reasonable.” (See Gov’t Code § 12652€(2)(B) [In a qui tam action “[t]he state or political subdivision may settle the action with the defendant notwithstanding the objections of the qui tam plaintiff if the court determines, after a hearing providing the qui tam plaintiff an opportunity to present evidence, that the proposed settlement is fair, adequate, and reasonable under all of the circumstances”].) While PAGA actions are distinct from class actions, the class action standard (fair, reasonable and adequate settlement) is adopted for these reasons.

“[C]ivil penalties recovered by aggrieved employees shall be distributed as follows: 75 percent to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency for enforcement of labor laws, including the administration of this part, and for education of employers and employees about their rights and responsibilities under this code, to be continuously appropriated to supplement and not supplant the funding to the agency for those purposes; and 25 percent to the aggrieved employees.” (Lab. Code § 2699, subd. (i).)

The SA defines the term “PAGA Payment” as meaning:

            [T]he payment in settlement of claims for civil penalties under the

Private Attorneys General Act (‘PAGA’, Lab. Code,§ 2698, et seq.) in the

amount of $50,000 from the Settlement Fund - Maximum, subject to the

approval. Pursuant to the PAGA, 75 percent of the PAGA payment will be

paid to the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency, and 25

percent of the PAGA payment will be paid to the PAGA Group Members.

 

(SA, ¶ 1.25.)

As previously noted, this number was reached after good-faith negotiation. (Perlman Decl., ¶ 2.)

The amount to be paid to the LWDA here, i.e., $37,500.00, is 2.8% of the gross settlement amount. (Id., ¶ 29).

The court finds this settlement amount for PAGA is substantial and fair. Further, the PAGA penalties as a percentage of the settlement range is consistent with other hybrid class/PAGA settlements approved by California courts. (Nordstrom Commission Cases, supra, 186 Cal.App.4th at 579 [“trial court did not abuse its discretion in approving a settlement which does not allocate any damages to the PAGA claims”].)

Enhancements

Enhancement payments “are fairly typical in class action cases.” (Cellphone Termination Fee Cases, supra, 186 Cal.App.4th at 1393 [affirming incentive awards of $10,000]; Rodriguez v. West Publishing Corp. (9th Cir. 2009) 563 F.3d 948, 958.) Enhancement payments “are intended to compensate class representatives for work done on behalf of the class, to make up for financial or reputational risk undertaken in bringing the action, and, sometimes, to recognize their willingness to act as a private attorney general.” (Id. at 958-959.)

“[T]he rationale for making enhancement or incentive awards to named plaintiffs is that he or she should be compensated for the expense or risk he has incurred in conferring a benefit on other members of the class.” (Clark v. American Residential Services LLC (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 785, 806.)

Here, Plaintiff requests $10,000.00 for his service in the action and in exchange for his general release of all claims. (Perlman Decl., ¶¶ 32-33; SA, ¶ 6.4; see Maldonado Decl.) Maldonado represents that he has spent in excess of 100 hours participating in this case, including speaking with and meeting with his attorneys, searching for and reviewing documents, preparing for and appearing at two sessions of his deposition, helping his attorneys prepare for mediation, making himself available for both sessions of mediation, and discussing the settlement with his attorneys. (Maldonado Decl., ¶ 9). Maldonado also believes he took on a significant professional risk by agreeing to be the Named Plaintiff in this case, as knowledge learned by subsequent employers of the litigation could jeopardize his prospective employment. (Id., ¶ 10).

The court believes that this payment is fair and reasonable compensation for Maldonado’s efforts.

Attorneys’ Fees

California Rules of Court, rule 3.769(b) provides: “Any agreement, express or implied, that has been entered into with respect to the payment of attorney’s fees or the submission of an application for the approval of attorney’s fees must be set forth in full in any application for approval of the dismissal or settlement of an action that has been certified as a class action.” An award of attorney’s fees is made by the court at the fairness hearing. (Lafitte v. Robert Half Intern., Inc. (2016) 1 Cal.5th 480.) Despite any agreement by the parties to the contrary, “the court ha[s] an independent right and responsibility to review the attorney fee provision of the settlement agreement and award only so much as it determine[s] reasonable.” (Garabedian v. Los Angeles Cellular Telephone Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 123, 128.)

Accordingly, the question of whether class counsel is entitled to fees will be addressed at the fairness hearing when class counsel brings a noticed motion for attorney fees. With the motion, counsel must provide an overall summary of the time spent by each attorney or paralegal who worked on this matter. (Lafitte, supra, 1 Cal.5th at 505 [“[t]he trial court in the present case exercised its discretion in this matter, performing the cross-check using counsel declarations summarizing overall time spent, rather than demanding and scrutinizing daily time sheets in which the work performed was broken down by individual task”].)

Furthermore, any agreement about how attorney fees will be paid, including fee splitting and whether the client has given written approval, should be provided. (Mark v. Spencer (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 219; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769.)

Counsel should also be prepared to justify any costs sought by detailing how such costs were incurred.

Certification

A precertification settlement may stipulate that a defined class be conditionally certified for settlement purposes. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.769(d); Weil & Brown, CAL. PRAC. GUIDE: CIV. PRO. BEFORE TRIAL (The Rutter Group 2024) ¶ 14:139.1.)

Before the court may approve the settlement, the settlement class must satisfy the normal prerequisites for a class action. (Amchem Products, Inc. v. Windsor (1997) 521 U.S. 591, 625-627.) “The party advocating class treatment must demonstrate the existence of an ascertainable and sufficiently numerous class, a well-defined community of interest, and substantial benefits from certification that render proceeding as a class superior to the alternatives.” (Brinker Restaurant Corp., supra, 53 Cal.4th at 1021; see Code Civ. Proc. § 382.)

A class is ascertainable when members “may be readily identified without unreasonable expense or time by reference to official [or business] records.” (Sevidal v. Target Corp. (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 905, 919.) Based on this proposed settlement class definition, the proposed class can be ascertained from Defendant’s personnel and employment records, which reveal a class size of approximately 1,306 members. (Perlman Decl., ¶ 19). Thus, the ascertainable and numerosity requirements are satisfied.

“[T]he community of interest requirement embodies three factors: (1) predominant common questions of law or fact; (2) class representatives with claims or defenses typical of the class; and (3) class representatives who can adequately represent the class.” (Brinker Restaurant Corp., supra, 53 Cal.4th at 1021.) Where the defendant systematically engages in policies and practices that allegedly violate the Labor Code and thereby subjects all of its employees to the same unlawful conduct, common questions of law and fact will predominate over individualized issues. (Id. at 1032-1034 [“[t]he theory of liability—that Brinker has a uniform policy, and that that policy, measured against wage order requirements, allegedly violates the law—is by its nature a common question eminently suited for class treatment”].)

This case is a typical wage and hour violation suit with a clearly identified class through Defendant’s records. The statutes relating to Maldonado’s claims apply with equal force and effect to all Class Members. Maldonado contends that Defendants’ policies and practices apply class-wide Defendant’s liability can be determined by facts common to all members of the class. Maldonado is a former employee of Defendant and was employed in California as a non-exempt employee during part of the relevant time period. (Maldonado Decl., ¶ 2). Maldonado has the same claims as those that the members of the Settlement Class would reasonably be expected to bring based on the course of conduct alleged herein. (Id., ¶¶ 5-7). Accordingly, certification for the class as defined in the settlement agreement is appropriate.
