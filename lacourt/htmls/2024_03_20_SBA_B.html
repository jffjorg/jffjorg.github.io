Hearing Date:                          March 20, 24

 

Moving Parties:                      Cross-complainant Kiewit Infrastructure West Co. f/k/a Kiewit Pacific Co.

Responding Party:                  Cross-defendant CST Industries, Inc.

Motion for Order Granting Leave to Amend Cross-Complaint

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  Cross-complainant Kiewit is ordered to file its FACC within five days.

BACKGROUND

            On January 28, 2022, plaintiffs Emily Perez and Joe Perez filed a wrongful death and survival action complaint against City of Los Angeles, CST Industries, Inc., CST Covers Industries, Inc., Kiewit Construction, Inc. for (1) dangerous condition of public property, (2) strict products liability, and (3) negligence.  Plaintiffs allege that decedent Jose Perez was an employee of Murray Company, which was contracted with the City.  Decedent was brought in to assist the City with an acid wash procedure and construction involving the primary sedimentation tanks at Hyperion.  On March 8, 2021, while manually adjusting or lifting with a co-worker a weakened and corroded aluminum tank cover, using the tank cover’s folding hinged flap handles, the handle on his co-worker’s end broke, or the co-worker lost control of his end, causing the tank cover to drop into the tank.  When the co-worker’s end dropped, decedent’s folding hinged flap handle trapped decedent’s hand and decedent was thrown 20-plus feet into the tank.

On April 13, 2022, plaintiffs filed an amendment designating Kiewit Infrastructure West Co. f/k/a Kiewit Pacific Co. as Doe 11.

On April 20, 2022, CST Industries, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Kiewit.

On April 21, 2022, CST Industries filed a FACC.

On May 9, 2022, plaintiffs filed a FAC.

On June 8, 2022, Kiewit filed a cross-complaint against CST Industries.

On September 2, 2022, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

On October 18, 2022, the court overruled CST’s demurrer.

On April 11, 2023, CST Industries, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against CST Covers Industries, Inc. for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

On January 11, 2024, the court granted Kiewit Infrastructure West Co. f/k/a Kiewit Pacific Co.’s motion for determination of good faith settlement.  The court also granted City of Los Angeles’ motion for determination of good faith settlement.

Trial is set for August 5, 2024.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Kiewit Infrastructure West Co. f/k/a Kiewit Pacific Co. requests an order granting leave to amend its cross-complaint to add a claim for express indemnity against cross-cross-defendant CST Industries, Inc.

            Defendant Kiewit contends that plaintiffs’ claim centers on alleged defects in the design of the “folding hinge flap handles” on the aluminum tanks covers designed and manufactured by Conservatek.  Kiewit explains that it installed the tank covers between 2005 and 2007 pursuant to a contract with the City of Los Angeles that owns the Hyperion Water Reclamation Plant property.  Kiewit asserts that the contract with the City required Kiewit to purchase the particular Conservatek aluminum tank covers that Kiewit ultimately installed.  Kiewit contends that it did not design, select, or manufacture the tank covers.  It also asserts that on September 9, 2005 it entered into a “Material Contract” with Conservatek, and that “[t]hrough the discovery process, it has come to [Kiewit’s] attention” that the contract “contains explicit language stating that Conservatek shall indemnify buyers from all claims arising out of bodily injury or death.”  Kiewit tendered defense and indemnity to CST Industries as the legal successor to Conservatek.  On January 30, 2024, Kiewit renewed its tender.  See Mary Fersch decl. 

            Kiewit argues that it has acted in good faith and is justified in seeking an amendment at this time.  Kiewit contends that its cross-complaint for indemnity may be brought now or upon defendant incurring damages associated with a settlement or jury verdict at trial.  Kiewit entered into a settlement agreement with plaintiffs for $2.5 million in January 2024.  Kiewit asserts that “[i]t makes sense to bring the action in this case because Plaintiffs are continuing their action against Cross-Defendant,” Kiewit already has a pending cross-complaint against CST for equitable indemnity, and it will be more expeditious to try both actions in the same case.  Kiewit further argues that no party will be prejudiced.

            In opposition, cross-defendant CST Industries, Inc. contends that the motion should be denied because of Kiewit’s undue delay and that the claim for express indemnity should have been brought against CST in June 2022 when Kiewit filed its cross-complaint.  CST argues that the amendment will prejudice it and impose additional costs and necessitate additional discovery and that it could have defended this case differently had it accepted tender and defense from Kiewit in June 2022.  Lastly, CST contends that the motion is premature because the successor liability issue has not yet been resolved and that it may not have any contractual obligations under that contract.

            In reply, Kiewit argues that the motion is not untimely because circumstances changed after the parties attended mediation and CST refused to contribute funds to the settlement and, following the issuance of a mediator’s proposal on November 11, 2023, Kiewit immediately notified CST upon discovering the contract and tendered defense and indemnity to CST, and Kiewit has incurred $2.5 million in damages.  Kiewit argues that the amendment would not prejudice CST because it arises out of the same set of facts as the complaint and cross-complaint.

The court finds that cross-complainant Kiewit complied with CRC Rule 3.1324.  “Leave to amend is in general required to be liberally granted.”  Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal. App. 4th 402, 412 (citation omitted).  Further, the court finds that Kiewit did not unduly delay in filing the motion and that cross-defendant CST will not be prejudiced.

The Court appreciates that the issue of a contractual indemnity provision is new and raises issues that may involve additional discovery regarding that basis for imposing liability.  By the same token, the underlying personal injury claim of Plaintiff that gives rise to the claim of contractual indemnity has been the subject of this litigation throughout, and the damages claimed were the subject of discovery.  Of course, the issue of the damages that Kiewit may claim by contractual indemnity are now quantified by its settlement.

In short, while there are some new issues, it is in the interest of justice to have the disputes that arose from the unfortunate event fully resolved in one case rather than foster yet another suit where contractual indemnity must be addressed.

The motion is thus GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            Cross-complainant Kiewit is ordered to file its FACC within five days.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
