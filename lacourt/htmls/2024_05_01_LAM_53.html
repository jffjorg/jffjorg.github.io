Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”) filed, on January 25, 2024, a motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication against plaintiff Eric Jones (“Plaintiff”), with a hearing date of May 1, 2024.  At the time that Defendant’s motion was filed, trial was scheduled for February 28, 2024.

On January 29, 2024, the court issued a minute order granting in part and denying in part Defendant’s ex parte application to continue trial, and (1) denied Defendant’s request to continue trial from February 28, 2024 to a date after the May 1, 2024 hearing date on Defendant’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication on the ground that Defendant filed the motion only 34 days before the February 28, 2024 trial date and therefore did not comply with the notice requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, but (2) granted Defendant’s request to continue trial to accommodate Defendant’s counsel’s vacation scheduled for mid-February 2024.  (Jan. 29, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Thus, the court continued trial for the express purpose of accommodating Defendant’s counsel’s vacation to March 27, 2024.  (Ibid.)

Thereafter, on March 6, 2024, the court issued an order denying Defendant’s motion to continue trial to June 5, 2024, so that its motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication could be heard based on the court’s finding that Defendant had not filed its motion within the time limits of Code of Civil Procedure section 437c by failing to file and serve the motion with 75 days’ notice (plus two court days’ notice for electronic service) and with enough time to be heard at least 30 days before the then-scheduled trial date of February 28, 2024 or the then-current March 27, 2024 trial date.  (Mar. 6, 2024 Order, p. 2:1-17.)

Also on March 6, 2024, Plaintiff and Defendant lodged with the court a “Joint Stipulation and [Proposed] Order to Continue Trial to Accommodate April 16, 2024 Mediation,” in which the parties “stipulated to a continuance of trial to accommodate the April 16, 2024 mediation[.]”  (Joint Stip. & Order, p. 2:16-18.)  The court entered an order on the parties’ stipulation on March 8, 2024, and continued trial to June 5, 2024.  (Joint Stip. & Order, p. 4.)  The parties did not stipulate to extend the trial date to allow Defendant’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication to be heard.

Based on the court’s finding in its March 6, 2024 order that Defendant did not have a right to have its motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication heard because it was not filed within the time required by Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, the court orders, nunc pro tunc, that Defendant’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication, filed on January 25, 2024, is stricken for that reason as of March 6, 2024.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.)

Thus, the court takes the May 1, 2024 hearing on defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s motion for summary judgment or, alternatively, summary adjudication off calendar.

The court directs the clerk to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:  May 1, 2024

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Vision Construction Group, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Unopposed

Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories and for Sanctions

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Vision Construction Group, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling plaintiff Morillo Construction, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) to serve responses to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories, and (2) awarding sanctions in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff in the amount of $3,611.65.

The court notes a possible defect with service.

Defendant served the pending motion on counsel for Plaintiff, Howard S. Blum (“Counsel”) by electronic service at the email address “howblum@gmail.com.”  (Mot., p. 34:10-13, 34:20-23.)  However, this appears to be the incorrect email address for Counsel.  First, on August 5, 2022, Plaintiff filed a “Proof of Service of Summons” on Judicial Council for POS-010, which listed Counsel’s email address to be “hblum@albrightblum.com.”  (POS-010 filed Aug. 5, 2022, p. 1.)  Second, the emails submitted by Defendant in support of its motion indicate that they were sent from Counsel at the email address “hblum@albrightblum.com.”  (Rothstein Decl., Ex. 4, p. 1.)  Thus, it appears that Defendant has not properly served Plaintiff with the pending motion at its Counsel’s correct email address.

The court also notes that Defendant served the subject Special Interrogatories on Plaintiff at the email address “howblum@gmail.com.”  (Rothstein Decl., Ex. 1, pp. 14:8-12, 15:1-4.)  Thus, although the court does not reach the merits of this motion since Plaintiff has not been properly served, the court notes that Defendant may not have served Plaintiff with the Special Interrogatories at Counsel’s correct email address.

The court therefore exercises its discretion to continue the hearing on Defendant’s motion to give Defendant an opportunity to serve Plaintiff at Counsel’s correct email address.

ORDER

            The court orders that the hearing on defendant Vision Construction Group, Inc.’s motion to compel responses to special interrogatories and for sanctions is continued to June 14, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., in Department 53.

            The court orders defendant Vision Construction Group, Inc. (1) to serve a notice of this ruling on all parties that have appeared in this action and to file a proof of service of the notice of ruling, and (2) to serve plaintiff Morillo Construction, Inc. with the moving papers filed in connection with this motion at the proper email address (or mailing address) for counsel no later than May 14, 2024, and to file a proof of service of that service with the court.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

order to show cause re why the court should not, on its own motion, reconsider, vacate, and issue new order on plaintiff/ relator’s code of civil procedure § 170.6 challenge to judge cochran

 

 

Order to Show Cause re why the court should not, on its own motion, (1) reconsider and vacate Judge Cochran’s March 5, 2024 order accepting plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s March 4, 2024 Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge against Judge Cochran and reassigning this matter to Judge Robert B. Broadbelt in Department 53 for all further proceedings, and (2) issue a new order denying plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge against Judge Cochran

On April 18, 2024, the court held a Case Management Conference in this action.  On that date, the court issued an Order to Show Cause why the court should not, on its own motion, (1) reconsider and vacate Judge Cochran’s March 5, 2024 order accepting plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s March 4, 2024 Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge against Judge Cochran and reassigning this matter to Judge Robert B. Broadbelt in Department 53 for all further proceedings, and (2) issue a new order denying plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s March 4, 2024 Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge against Judge Cochran.  (April 18, 2024 Order, p. 2:3-9.)  The court (1) set a hearing on the Order to Show Cause for the parties to be heard on May 1, 2024, and (2) ordered that any response to the Order to Show Cause shall be filed no later than April 26, 2024.  (April 18, 2024 Order, p. 2:9-11.)

First, the court finds that it may reconsider the March 5, 2024 order issued by Judge Cochran accepting plaintiff/relator Newton Kellam’s Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge because Judge Cochran is unavailable to reconsider the order since he has been disqualified pursuant to the March 5, 2024 order.  (Geddes v. Superior Court (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 417, 425-426 [although one trial court may not review the ruling of another trial court judge, that principle “does not apply where the original judge is ‘unavailable’”].)

Second, the court grants its own motion for reconsideration.

On August 28, 2018, plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam filed a Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 peremptory challenge to Judge Nieto, which was accepted and granted on August 31, 2018.  (Aug. 28, 2018 Peremptory Challenge.)  Thereafter, on March 4, 2024, plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam filed a Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 peremptory challenge to Judge Cochran, which was accepted and granted on March 5, 2024.[1]  (Mar. 4, 2024 Decl. of Erik McLain Pursuant to CCP § 170.6; Mar. 5, 2024 minute order, p. 1.)

Thus, plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam filed two peremptory challenges pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6.  However, except as otherwise provided, “no party or attorney shall be permitted to make more than one such motion in any one action or special proceeding pursuant to this section.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 170.6, subd. (a)(4).)  Thus, the court finds that plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s second Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 peremptory challenge to Judge Cochran was improper.  (Ibid.)  The court further notes that plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam does not dispute this finding.  (McLain Decl., p. 1:19-21 [“Plaintiff agrees with the Court’s tentative issued April 18, 2024 that its Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge to Judge Cochran was improper and should be withdrawn”].)

The court therefore (1) vacates the March 5, 2024 order granting plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge to Judge Cochran and reassigning this matter to Judge Robert B. Broadbelt in Department 53, and (2) issues a new order denying plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam’s Code of Civil Procedure section 170.6 challenge, filed on March 4, 2024, to Judge Cochran.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 170.6, subd. (a)(4).)

The court orders that this action is reassigned at the direction of the Assistant Supervising Judge to Judge Steve Cochran in Department 16 at the Stanley Mosk Courthouse.

            The court orders plaintiff/relator Newton W. Kellam to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendants’ motion to compel plaintiff’s deposition and request for monetary sanctions

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Emilio Reyes

Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition and Request for Monetary Sanctions

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas (“Defendants”) move the court for an order (1) compelling plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”) to appear for and testify at a videotaped, in-person deposition, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Defendants and against Plaintiff in the amount of $2,070.

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action . . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination . . . , the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).) 

The court notes that, as a threshold matter, Plaintiff contends that (1) this motion is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020, and (2) Defendants did not file a meet and confer declaration that complies with section 2016.040.  The court disagrees.

First, the court finds that (1) Defendants timely noticed the deposition of Plaintiff for February 26, 2024, more than 30 days before the May 15, 2024 trial date, and (2) this motion was scheduled for hearing more than 15 days before the May 15, 2024 trial date, and the court issued an order on April 29, 2024, continuing the hearing on this motion and ordering that the discovery motion cutoff date is extended for the limited purpose of allowing this motion to be heard on May 1, 2024.  (Lopez Decl., Ex. C [Notice of Deposition]; Mar. 6, 2023 Minute Order, p. 1 [setting trial in this action for May 15, 2024]; Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a) [“any party shall be entitled as matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action”]; Apr. 29, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Thus, the court finds that Defendants’ motion is timely.

Second, the court finds that Defendants have submitted a meet and confer declaration that satisfies Code of Civil Procedure sections 2025.450, subdivision (b)(2), and 2016.040 by submitting a declaration establishing that their counsel (1) met and conferred with Plaintiff regarding his availability for deposition in November 2023, December 2023, and January 2024, and (2) attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff telephonically on March 6, 2024 to schedule his deposition.  (Lopez Decl., ¶¶ 15, 16, 18, 28, 29; Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.450, subd. (b), 2016.040.)

Thus, the court rules on the merits of Defendants’ motion.

The court grants Defendants’ motion to compel the deposition of Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  The court notes that Plaintiff appears to be seeking accommodations necessitated by a medical condition that would allow him to participate in the deposition.  The court acknowledges that Plaintiff has submitted a letter from his nurse practitioner, Katherine Perry, in which Perry states that Plaintiff “[w]ould benefit from a small pillow while in session,” “may need lighting adjusted,” and “needs to be able to stand up as needed due to medical conditions.”  (Reyes Decl., Ex. B to Ex. 3.) 

To the extent that Plaintiff’s opposition – and specifically, his attached meet and confer letter dated February 20, 2024 – appears to request accommodations that would facilitate his participation in the deposition, the court considers each request pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 1.100 as follows:

·       Plaintiff requests that Defendants, nonmoving defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, and attorney Michael Sisson not be present at the deposition.  (Reyes Decl., Ex. 1, p. 2.)  The court denies this request pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 1.100, subdivision (f)(3) because it would fundamentally change the nature of the proceeding. 

·       Plaintiff requests that the deposition be taken in person, in writing only, and not recorded by video.  (Reyes Decl., Ex. 1, p. 2.)  The court denies this request pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 1.100, subdivision (f)(3) because it would fundamentally change the nature of the proceeding. 

·       Plaintiff requests a small pillow and ability to stand.  (Reyes Decl., Ex. 1, p. 2.)  The court grants the request for Plaintiff to bring a small pillow and to take reasonable, short breaks to stand and move around, so long as the breaks are not taken while a question is pending.

·       Plaintiff requests low light in the room where the deposition will occur.  (Reyes Decl., Ex. 1, p. 2.)  The court grants this request to the extent reasonably possible to allow parties to conduct the deposition.

The court finds that the circumstances presented would make the imposition of sanctions unjust and therefore denies Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) 

ORDER

The court grants defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas’s motion to compel deposition of plaintiff as follows.

The court orders plaintiff Emilio Reyes to attend and testify in person at a deposition to be taken and recorded by counsel for defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas on ___________________, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., at Diamond View, 350 10th Avenue, Suite 1000, San Diego, California, 92101. 

The court orders that, at the deposition of plaintiff Emilio Reyes, (1) plaintiff Emilio Reyes may bring a small pillow, (2) the lighting may be adjusted in a reasonable manner, and (3) plaintiff Emilio Reyes may stand up and take reasonable breaks as needed.

The court denies defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas’s request for monetary sanctions.

            The court orders defendants Los Indios de San Gabriel, Inc., and Andrew Salas to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
