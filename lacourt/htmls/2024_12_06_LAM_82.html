DEPARTMENT 82 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCP02901    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: 82

Jessica M., et al.                                                        Case No. 24STCP02901

                                                            

v.                                                                     Hearing: December 6, 2024

                                                                        Location: Stanley Mosk Courthouse

California Department of                                        Department: 82                                       

Corrections and Rehabilitation, et al.                     Judge: Stephen I. Goorvitch

           

[Tentative] Order Denying Petition for Writ of Mandate and/or Prohibition

 

[Tentative] Order Overruling Respondents’ Demurrer as Moot

 

           

INTRODUCTION

 

In 2006, the voters passed Proposition 83, commonly known as “Jessica’s Law,” which amended certain provisions of Penal Code section 667.6, the provision that governs sentencing of certain sex offenses.  Two years later, on July 26, 2008, “Petitioner Jessica M. was kidnapped at knifepoint, forced to orally copulate her attacker, forcibly raped, forcibly sodomized, and forcibly digitally penetrated” by Sergio Linares, who was 25 years and nine months old at the time.  (Petition for Writ of Mandate (“Pet.”) ¶¶ 1, 14 & Exh. F; Declaration of Jessica M. ¶ 3.)  Linares pleaded no contest to five forcible sex offenses and admitted a weapons enhancement.  Pursuant to section 667.6(c), Linares was sentenced to a total of 50 years’ imprisonment based upon a series of consecutive sentences.  At the time Linares was sentenced, he was not eligible for a “youth offender parole hearing” under Penal Code section 3051.  However, between 2015 and 2019, the Legislature amended section 3051 and raised the relevant age from 23 years old to 25 years old, which now entitles Sergio Linares to regular parole hearings, notwithstanding his “full, separate, and consecutive sentences.” 

 

Petitioners Jessica M. and Crime Survivors, Inc. (collectively, “Petitioners”) seek a writ of traditional mandate or prohibition under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1085 and/or 1103 against California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation (the “Department” or the “CDCR”) and the Board of Parole Hearings (the “Board”) (collectively, “Respondents”).  Petitioners seek a writ preventing Respondents from holding youthful offender parole hearings for, and granting parole to, any inmate sentenced to consecutive terms under section 667.6.  Respondents argue that Petitioners do not present a justiciable controversy because they do not have standing and the issue is not ripe since they identify no inmate who has been paroled.  Respondents also argue that there was no unconstitutional amendment of section 667.6.

 

The court questions whether this issue is ripe.  On March 29, 2023, the Board denied Linares parole under section 3051, and on October 2, 2024, Linares stipulated to a three-year period of unsuitability for parole.  Because Linares has only served about 16 years of a 50-year sentence for violent sexual offenses he committed when he was almost 26 years old, it is unlikely he will be paroled under section 3051 for the foreseeable future, if at all.  Nevertheless, the court assumes without deciding that Petitioners present a justiciable controversy with respect to Linares and similarly-situated inmates.  The court denies the petition on the merits, finding that there has been no unconstitutional amendment of section 667.6. 

BACKGROUND

 

A.        Factual Background

 

“On July 26, 2008, Petitioner Jessica M. was kidnapped at knifepoint, forced to orally copulate her attacker, forcibly raped, forcibly sodomized, and forcibly digitally penetrated by a stranger,” who was later identified as Sergio Linares.  (Petition for Writ of Mandate (“Pet.”) ¶ 1 and Exh. F; Declaration of Jessica M. ¶ 3.)  Linares was 25 years and nine months old when he committed the crime.  (Pet. ¶ 14 & Exh. F.)  Linares was identified, apprehended, and charged with multiple criminal counts and faced a maximum sentence of 136 years-to-life in state prison.  (Pet. ¶ 1.) 

 

On December 12, 2008, Linares pleaded no contest to five forcible sexual offenses and admitted a weapons enhancement: (1) Two counts of forcible oral copulation under Penal Code section 288a(c)(2); (2) One count of forcible rape under Penal Code section 261(a)(2); (3) One count of forcible sodomy under Penal Code section 286(c)(2); (4) One count of forcible digital penetration under Penal Code section 289(a)(1); and (5) A weapons enhancement for use of a knife.  (Ibid.)  Linares was sentenced to eight years’ imprisonment on each sex offense and ten years’ imprisonment for the weapons enhancement, to run consecutively, for a total term of imprisonment of 50 years.  (Pet. ¶¶ 1, 14 and Exh. E and F.)

 

B.        Penal Code section 667.6(c) and Proposition 83

 

Two years before the crime, in 2006, California voters passed, by a wide margin, Proposition 83, tilted: “The Sexual Predator Punishment and Control Act: Jessica’s Law.”  (Pet Exh. G and H; Resp. RJN Exh. 5.)  Proposition 83 increased the penalties for certain sex offenses, prohibited probation in lieu of prison for some sex offenses, and extended parole for habitual sex offenders.  Proposition 83 also enacted lifetime monitoring using global positioning satellite technology; felony status for possession of child pornography; and indeterminate, involuntary civil commitment for sexually violent predators.  (Resp. RJN Exh. 5 at 43-44.)[1]  Proposition 83 was focused, in substantial part, on predators who victimize children. (Id. at 44 [“Proposition 83—Jessica’s Law—will protect our children by keeping child molesters in prison longer; keeping them away from schools and parks; and monitoring their movements after they are released.”]

 

Article IV, section 9 of the California Constitution mandates that “[a] section of a statute may not be amended unless the section is re-enacted as amended.”  Accordingly, to comply with Article IV, section 9, section 667.6 was reenacted along with certain changes in Proposition 83.  (See Resp. RJN Exh. 5 at 49.)  However, Proposition 83 did not amend the judge’s discretion to impose consecutive sentences for forcible sex crimes against the same victim on the same occasion.  (See ibid.)  Proposition 83 made the following amendments to section 667.6(c) with the new changes noted in bold italics: 

           

In lieu of the term provided in Section 1170.1, a full, separate, and consecutive term may be imposed for each violation of Section 220, other than an assault with intent to commit mayhem, provided that the person has been convicted previously of violating Section 220 for an offense other than an assault with intent to commit mayhem, paragraph (2), (3), (6), or (7) of subdivision (a) of Section 261, paragraph (1), (4), or (5) of subdivision (a) of Section 262, Section 264.1, subdivision (b) of Section 288, Section 288.5 or subdivision (a) of Section 289, of committing sodomy in violation of subdivision (k) of Section 286, of committing oral copulation in violation of subdivision (k) of Section 288a, or of committing sodomy or oral copulation in violation of Section 286 or 288a by force, violence, duress, menace, or fear of immediate and unlawful bodily injury on the victim or another person whether or not the crimes were committed during a single transaction an offense specified in subdivision (e) if the crimes involve the same victim on the same occasion. A term may be imposed consecutively pursuant to this subdivision if a person is convicted of at least one offense specified in subdivision (e). If the term is imposed consecutively pursuant to this subdivision, it shall be served consecutively to any other term of imprisonment, and shall commence from the time the person otherwise would have been released from imprisonment. The term shall not be included in any determination pursuant to Section 1170.1. Any other term imposed subsequent to that term shall not be merged therein but shall commence at the time the person otherwise would have been released from prison.

 

(RJN Exh. 5 at 49.)  In other words, as relevant to this petition, Proposition 83 expanded the list of qualifying offenses and moved them from subsection (c) to subsection (e).  The new list contained the same offenses as well as additional one:

 

(e) This section shall apply to the following offenses:

 

(1) Rape, in violation of paragraph (2), (3), (6), or (7) of subdivision (a) of Section 261.

 

(2) Spousal rape, in violation of paragraph (1), (4), or (5) of subdivision (a) of Section 262.

 

(3) Rape, spousal rape, or sexual penetration, in concert, in violation of Section 264.1.

 

(4) Sodomy, in violation of paragraph (2) or (3) of subdivision (c), or subdivision (d) or (k), of Section 286.

 

(5) Lewd or lascivious act, in violation of subdivision (b) of Section 288.

 

(6) Continuous sexual abuse of a child, in violation of Section 288.5.

 

(7) Oral copulation, in violation of paragraph (2) or (3) of subdivision (c), or subdivision (d) or (k), of Section 288a.

 

(8) Sexual penetration, in violation of subdivision (a) or (g) of Section 289.

 

(9) As a present offense under subdivision (c) or (d), assault with intent to commit a specified sexual offense, in violation of Section 220.

 

(10) As a prior conviction under subdivision (a) or (b), an offense committed in another jurisdiction that includes all of the elements of an offense specified in this subdivision.

 

(RJN Exh. 5 at 49.)  In other words, Proposition 83 removed the list of qualifying offenses from subsection (c) and created a subsection (e) with largely the same offenses.  Each of the crimes for which Linares was convicted was included in the original language of section 667.6(c).  (Cf. Pet. Exh. E & RJN Exh. 5 at 49.)       

 

            C.        Penal Code section 3051

 

“The Legislature first created this system of youth offender parole hearings in 2013, following a series of court decisions identifying Eighth Amendment limits on the sentencing of juvenile offenders.”  (People v. Hardin (2024) 15 Cal.5th 834, 843.)[2]  In 2013, the Legislature passed, and the Governor signed, Senate Bill No. 260 (SB 260) which added section 3051 to the Penal Code, titled: “Youth Offender Parole Hearings.”  Originally, section 3051 authorized youth offender parole hearings after 15, 20, or 25 years of imprisonment for persons who were convicted of committing a “controlling offense” before they turned 18 years old.  “Controlling offense” is defined as “the offense or enhancement for which any sentencing court imposed the longest term of imprisonment.”  (Pen. Code § 3051(a)(2)(B).)   

 

Subsequently, in 2015, 2017, and 2019, the Legislature passed, and the Governor signed, Senate Bills Numbers 261 and 394 and Assembly Bill Number 965 (SB 261, SB 394, and AB 965), which amended section 3051 by raising the age for of those eligible for youthful parole hearings to include inmates who committed their crimes when they were 25 years old or younger.  “In expanding section 3051 beyond the constitutional minimum age of 18 set out in Graham …, the Legislature considered scientific evidence that neurological development, particularly in areas of the brain relevant to judgment and decisionmaking, continues beyond adolescence and into the mid-20’s.”  (Hardin, supra, 15 Cal.5th at 846.)  As relevant to this petition, section 3051 states as follows:

 

A person who was convicted of a controlling offense that was committed when the person was 25 years of age or younger and for which the sentence is a determinate sentence shall be eligible for release on parole at a youth offense parole hearing during the person’s 15th year of incarceration. 

 

(Pen. Code § 3051(b)(1).)  The hearing must “provide for a meaningful opportunity to obtain release” (Pen. Code § 3051(e)), and includes an assessment of the offender’s “growth and maturity” that “take[s] into consideration the diminished culpability of youth as compared to that of adults [and] the hallmark features of youth.” ( Pen. Code § 3051(f)(1).)

 

            D.        Procedural History

 

Pursuant to section 3051, Linares qualified for and received a youth offender parole hearing on March 29, 2023.  Jessica M. attended the parole hearing and provided the following victim impact statement:

 

As I say here, resurfacing through trauma that I had thought was behind me in 2008, his sentence to 50 years with no parole, provided me with some sense of closure.  Even though it did not change how I was violated, a crime was [] committed against me, and I have little right, rights outside of this statement.  The system promised me that he would serve 50 years without parole, and now the system has broken that promise.  There needs to be a change.

 

(Jessica M. Decl. ¶ 6.)  On March 29, 2023, the Board denied Linares parole for three years.  (Pet. ¶ 20 and Exh. O.)

 

On June 29, 2024, after an administrative review, the Board notified Jessica M. that Linares’ parole hearing had been advanced to October 2, 2024.  At the hearing on October 2, 2024, Linares stipulated to a three-year period of unsuitability for parole.  When questioned by the presiding Commissioner about the stipulation, Linares’ attorney explained that Linares “wanted to participate in more programming.”  (Jessica M. Decl. ¶¶ 8-11.)

 

In her declaration, Jessica M. states that Linares’ “stipulation of being unsuitable for 3 years does not give me 3 years of peace because his parole hearing can be advanced as it was last time.”  (Id. ¶ 12.)  She declares that “[t]his instability is overwhelming and prevents me from returning to a level of peace and healing that I’ve worked on for such a long time.”  (Id. ¶ 13.)  Petitioners filed the instant petition for writ of mandate on September 10, 2024. 

 

E.         Summary of the Parties’ Positions   

 

            Petitioners allege that the subsequent amendments to Penal Code section 3051 “nullify” Proposition 83 as it relates to inmates sentenced to Penal Code section 667.6(c) and (d).  (Pet. ¶ 23.)  In other words, Petitioners argues that affording sex offenders a youth offender parole hearing after 15 years “indirectly amends” the language of “full, separate, and consecutive” sentences for forcible sex crimes under Penal Code section 667.6(c) and (d).  (Pet. ¶¶ 23, 24.) 

 

Pursuant to Article II, section 10(c) of the California Constitution, “[t]he Legislature may not amend an initiative statute without subsequent voter approval unless the initiative permits such amendment, and then only upon whatever conditions the voters attached to the Legislature's amendatory powers.”  (People v. Rojas (2023) 15 Cal.5th 561, 568.)  The “Amendment Clause” of Proposition 83 provides as follows:

 

The provisions of this act shall not be amended by the Legislature except by a statute passed in each house by rollcall vote entered in the journal, two-thirds of the membership of each house concurring, or by a statute that becomes effective only when approved by the voters.  However, the Legislature may amend the provisions of this act to expand the scope of their application or to increase the punishments or penalties provided herein by a statute passed by majority vote of each house thereof.

 

(Resp. RJN Exh. 5 at 57, § 33.)  It is undisputed that SB 260, SB 261, SB 394, and AB 965 were passed with simple majorities and not with two-thirds majorities of both houses of the Legislature.  (Pet. ¶¶ 15-18, Exh. K-N and Respondents’ Answering Brief (“Ans. Br.”) 20:13-14.)  Therefore, Petitioners contend that SB 260, SB 261, SB 394, and AB 965 “are void” to the extent they conflict with section 667.6.  (Pet. ¶ 41.)

STANDARD OF REVIEW

 

“There are two essential requirements to the issuance of a traditional writ of mandate:

(1) a clear, present and usually ministerial duty on the part of the respondent, and (2) a clear, present and beneficial right on the part of the petitioner to the performance of that duty.” (California Ass’n for Health Services at Home v. Department of Health Services (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 696, 704.)  “A writ of mandate is granted only where necessary to protect a substantial right and only when it is shown that some substantial damage will be suffered by the petitioner if said writ is denied.”  (Schmier v. Supreme Court (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 703, 707-708.).  

 

“Mandamus is … appropriate for challenging the constitutionality or validity of statutes or official acts.”  (Wenke v. Hitchcock (1972) 6 Cal.3d 746, 751.)  The court exercises its independent judgment on questions of law arising in mandate proceedings.  (Christensen v. Lightbourne (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1251.)  “Interpretation of a statute or regulation is a question of law subject to independent review.”  (Ibid.)  However, the court affords a presumption of constitutionality to legislative acts.  “In considering the constitutionality of a legislative act [the court] presume[s] its validity, resolving all doubts in favor of the Act. Unless conflict with a provision of the state or federal Constitution is clear and unquestionable, [the court] must uphold the Act.”  (Amwest Surety Ins. Co. v. Wilson (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1243, 1252, citations omitted.)

DISCUSSION

 

            A.        Justiciability

 

Petitioners pray for a statewide writ of mandate or prohibition directing Respondents “to … refrain from enforcing Penal Code § 3051, subdivision (b) for inmates sentenced to full, separate and consecutive terms under Penal Code § 667.6” and “from releasing any inmate granted parole under Penal Code§ 3051 prior to completion of a full, separate and consecutive sentences lawfully imposed pursuant to Penal Code § 667.6.”  (Pet. Prayer ¶¶ 3-4.)  As an initial matter, Respondents argue that Petitioners do not raise a justiciable controversy. 

 

California courts will decide only justiciable controversies.  The concept of justiciability is a tenet of common law jurisprudence and embodies the principle that courts will not entertain an action which is not founded on an actual controversy.  Justiciability thus involves the intertwined criteria of ripeness and standing. 

 

(Wilson & Wilson v. City Council of Redwood City (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 1559, 1573 [citations, internal quotations, and internal alterations omitted].)  Respondents argue that Petitioners do not have standing to initiate new litigation to enjoin post-judgment proceedings related to the execution of a defendant’s sentence.  Respondents also argue that the petition is not ripe for judicial review because Petitioners do not identify any individual whose release is pending because of a youth offender parole hearing. 

 

                        1.         Standing

 

            To have standing to seek a writ of mandate, a party must be “beneficially interested.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 1086.)  “A petitioner is beneficially interested if he or she has some special interest to be served or some particular right to be preserved or protected over and above the interest held in common with the public at large.”  (Rialto Citizens for Responsible Growth v. City of Rialto (2012) 208 Cal. App. 4th 899, 913.)  “One who is in fact adversely affected by governmental action should have standing to challenge that action if it is judicially reviewable.”  (Carsten v. Psychology Examining Com. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 793, 796-97.)

 

Nevertheless, a petitioner who is not beneficially interested in a writ may have “citizen standing” or “public interest standing” to file a petition under the “public interest exception” to the beneficial interest requirement.  (Rialto, supra, 208 Cal.App.4th at 913-914.)  “The public interest exception applies where the question is one of public right and the object of the action is to enforce a public duty—in which case it is sufficient that the plaintiff be interested as a citizen in having the laws executed and the public duty enforced.”  (Ibid. [citations and internal quotations omitted].) 

 

            2.         Ripeness

 

By contrast, the ripeness doctrine requires that “[t]he controversy must be definite and concrete, touching the legal relations of parties having adverse legal interests. It must be a real and substantial controversy admitting of specific relief through a decree of a conclusive character, as distinguished from an opinion advising what the law would be upon a hypothetical state of facts.”  (Santa Teresa Citizen Action Group v. City of San Jose (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 689, 708.)  In deciding whether a claim is ripe, courts “evaluate both the fitness of the issues for judicial decision and the hardship to the parties of withholding court consideration.”  (Id. at 708; see also Pacific Legal Foundation v. California Coastal Com. (1982) 33 Cal.3d 158, 171-172.)

 

3.         Analysis

 

The court assumes without deciding that Jessica M. has standing as a crime victim to challenge the constitutionality of section 3051 as applied to youth offender parole hearings for Sergio Linares.  This assumption is based, in part, on Article I, section 28 of the California Constitution, the Victims’ Bill of Rights (the “VBOR”), which provides that a crime victim has a right “to participate in the parole process” and to “a prompt and final conclusion of the case and any related post-judgment proceedings.”  (Cal. Const. Art. I, § 28(b)(9) and (15); see also id.

§ 28(a)(4)-(6) [discussing the rights of victims to “sufficient” punishment and “finality in their criminal cases”].)  The VBOR “clearly demands a broad interpretation protective of victims’ rights.”  (Santos v. Brown (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 398, 418.)  Moreover, because this petition raises a purely legal question, the court assumes without deciding that the petition is ripe as it relates to Sergio Linares.  Based upon these assumptions, the court will decide the constitutionality of section 3051 as it relates to inmates sentenced under section 667.6(c).

 

However, the court finds that Petitioners do not have a beneficial interest in the writ beyond Sergio Linares.  As a crime victim, Jessica M. understandably has concerns about her safety and emotional wellbeing with respect to parole proceedings of Linares and his potential release from prison.  However, she does not identify any special interest or right in the parole proceedings of other incarcerated persons.  (See Jessica M. Decl. ¶¶ 6-14.)  Nor does Petitioner Crime Survivors, Inc. demonstrate that it has standing.  Particia Wenskunas, the founder/CEO of Petitioner Crime Survivors, Inc., declares that “CSI provides services to victims including services to victims who oppose parole for the perpetrators of the crimes against them” and “[t]he increased number of parole hearings puts a strain on CSI’s already limited resources.”  (Wenskunas Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.)  This evidence is too conclusory, speculative, and insufficient to establish, as a factual matter, that CSI has any special interest or “organizational standing” with respect to the requested statewide writ as it relates to inmates sentenced under section 667.6(d).  (See Opening Brief (“OB”) 10-11, citing Pacific Legal Foundation v. Unemployment Ins. Appeals Bd. (1977) 74 Cal.App.3d 150, 154-155.) 

 

Petitioners also do not prove that their petition is ripe with respect to the request for a statewide writ.  The Board heard approximately 4,063 youth offender parole hearings in 2023.  (See Dem. 9, citing Board of Parole Hearings 2023 Report of Significant Events, p. 2.)[3]  For those inmates who were sentenced pursuant to section 667.6, a judicial determination that section 3051 “amended” section 667.6 as applied to those inmates depends, in part, on the enumerated sex crimes upon which each inmate’s sentence was based.  (See discussion of changes to list of enumerated offenses in Proposition 83, infra.)  The constitutionality of section 3051 may also depend on whether each inmate was sentenced pursuant to the discretionary subdivision (c) or the mandatory subdivision (d) of section 667.6.  Further, information about each inmate’s age, underlying crimes, and sentence is necessary to evaluate all relevant constitutional issues, including the Eighth Amendment’s prohibition against cruel and unusual punishment.  (See OB 19-20.)  Because more factual context about inmates other than Linares is necessary to resolve Petitioners’ request for a statewide writ, the first prong of the ripeness test is not met.  (See Pacific Legal Foundation, supra, 33 Cal.3d at 171-172.)

 

Nor do Petitioners demonstrate that they will suffer hardship if court does not consider their request for a statewide writ.  As discussed, neither Jessica M. nor CSI have shown, with evidence, that they will suffer any concrete or irreparable harm if the court declines to issue a statewide writ.  Petitioners also have not identified any individual whose release is pending or imminent because of a youth offender parole hearing.  Thus, any alleged harm to the public is speculative and remote.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Petitioners have not shown a beneficial interest in the statewide writ that they request or that the matter is ripe for judicial review as it relates to inmates other than Sergio Linares, in particular those sentenced under section 667.6(d).  Therefore, the court will consider the petition for writ of mandate only as it relates to Linares and the legal issues necessarily implicated by that analysis, viz., the constitutionality of section 3051 as it relates to inmates sentenced under section 667.6(c).      

 

            B.        Constitutionality  

 

            1.         Summary of Petitioners’ Argument

 

Petitioners contend that section 3051 amends section 667.6 because it “supersed[es] the statutorily mandated sentences of most prisoners who committed their controlling offense before the age of 26.”  (OB 7-8, quoting People v. Williams (2024) 17 Cal.5th 99, 115.)  Petitioners contend that this amendment violated article II, section 10(c) of the California Constitution because section 667.6 was “rewritten” by Proposition 83 and the Legislature did not enact section 3051 with two-third majorities of the California Assembly and Senate, as required by the  Amendment Clause in Proposition 83.  (OB 8.) 

 

2.         Applicable Law

 

In general, “[t]he Legislature may not amend an initiative statute without subsequent voter approval unless the initiative permits such amendment, and then only upon whatever conditions the voters attached to the Legislature’s amendatory powers.”  (People v. Rojas (2023) 15 Cal.5th 561, 568; see Cal. Const., art. II, § 10(c).)  The purpose of this limitation is to “protect the people’s initiative powers by precluding the Legislature from undoing what the people have done, without the electorate’s consent.”  (People v. Kelly (2010) 47 Cal.4th 1008, 1025.) 

 

Special rules apply when voters make changes to an existing statute in an initiative measure.  When an existing statute is amended, the state Constitution requires the entire section to be reenacted as amended.  (Cal. Const., art. IV, § 9.) 

 

The rationale for compelling reenactment of an entire statutory section when only a part is being amended is to avoid the enactment of statutes in terms so blind that legislators themselves were sometimes deceived in regard to their effect and the risk that the public, from the difficulty of making the necessary examination and comparison, failed to become apprised of the changes made in the laws. 

 

(County of San Diego v. Commission on State Mandates (2018) 6 Cal.5th 196, 208 [internal citations, quotations, and alterations omitted].)  “Consequently, a substantial part of almost any statutory initiative will include a restatement of existing provisions with only minor, nonsubstantive changes — or no changes at all.”  (Ibid.)

 

The California Supreme Court has adopted the following test for determining whether the state constitutional limitation on legislative amendment applies to an initiative statute:

 

When technical reenactments are required under article IV, section 9 of the Constitution — yet involve no substantive change in a given statutory provision — the Legislature in most cases retains the power to amend the restated provision through the ordinary legislative process.  This conclusion applies unless the provision is integral to accomplishing the electorate’s goals in enacting the initiative or other indicia support the conclusion that voters reasonably intended to limit the Legislature’s ability to amend that part of the statute.  

 

(San Diego, supra, 6 Cal.5th at 214.) 

 

3.         Proposition 83 Did Not Make Substantive Changes to Section 667.6(c)

 

            Contrary to Petitioners’ argument, Proposition 83 did not make substantive changes to Penal Code section 667.6(c) as it relates to Linares’s sentencing.  The prior version of section 667.6(c) listed the qualifying offenses in that subdivision.  Proposition 83 merely moved the list to a separate subdivision.  While there were some changes to the list of qualifying offenses, the specific offenses for which Linares was convicted were included in both the prior and current versions of the statute.  Therefore, Proposition 83 was merely a technical reenactment of the sentencing authority applicable to Linares.  Contrary to Petitioners’ argument, this was not “integral to accomplishing the electorate’s goals in enacting [Proposition 83].”  Nor is there any indicia to “support the conclusion that voters reasonably intended to limit the Legislature’s ability to amend that part of the statute.”  Indeed, Proposition 83 did not make any substantive changes to the court’s sentencing authority in section 667.6(c) that are relevant to Linares.  Both before and after Proposition 83, section 667.6(c) authorized—but did not require—the court to impose consecutive sentences on defendants who commit multiple qualifying offenses against the same victim on the same occasion.  (See Rep. RJN Exh. 5 at 49.) 

 

            Petitioners acknowledge that subdivision (e) contains nearly all the same criminal offenses that were enumerated in section 667.6(c) prior to Proposition 83.  Instead, Petitioners argue that “there are substantive changes in the removal of the prior conviction requirement for assault with intent to commit a sex crime (§ 667.6, subd. (e)(9)) and the addition of subdivision (g) of section 289. (§ 667.6, subd. (e)(8).)”  (OB 18:8-10.)  Linares was not convicted of assault with intent to commit a sex crime (Penal Code § 220) or sexual penetration in violation of subdivision (g) of section 289.   (See Pet. ¶¶ 1, 14 and Exh. E and F.)  Similarly, Petitioners argue that two additional crimes were added to subdivision (e).  Again, Linares was not convicted of either of these two new qualifying offenses.  Accordingly, the court need not decide whether these changes to the list of enumerated crimes in section 667.6 could only be amended by two-third majorities of the Legislature.

 

Petitioners suggest that the question of whether Proposition 83 made “technical reenactments” to section 667.6 cannot be decided with respect to only those parts of section 667.6 that apply to Linares.  (See Reply 14:13-14 and 14:20-24.)  Petitioners do not explain why that would be the case.  To the contrary, San Diego instructs that, if technical reenactments were required and did not involve “substantive change in a given statutory provision,” the Legislature generally “retains the power to amend the restated provision through the ordinary legislative process.”  (San Diego, supra, 6 Cal.5th at 214 [emphasis added].)  San Diego did not hold or suggest that this analysis applies only to an entire codified statute, as opposed to specific provisions within the statute.[4] 

 

Petitioners also argue that the scope of subdivision (c) was changed from “on the victim or another person whether or not the crimes were committed during a single transaction” to “if the crimes involve the same victim on the same occasion.”  This is not dispositive.  The Court of Appeal has instructed that “[t]he substance, not the form, is what matters.”  (People v. Superior Court (Pearson) (2010) 48 Cal.4th 564, 573.) 

 

In sum, Proposition 83 did not make substantive changes to Penal Code section 667.6(c) with respect to any of the qualifying offenses for which Linares was convicted or the trial court’s authority to impose consecutive sentences.  Instead, as applied to Linares’s conviction and sentence, Proposition 83 made only non-substantive changes and “technical reenactments” to section 667.6(c).  As a result, the Amendment Clause of Proposition 83 does not apply and the Legislature could amend those parts of section 667.6(c) through the ordinary legislative process.

 

                        4.         Section 3051 Did Not Amend Section 667.6 as Applied to Linares

 

In the alternative, even if Proposition 83 made substantive changes to Penal Code section 667.6(c) as it relates to Linares’s convictions and sentencing, section 3051 did not amend the sentencing statute.  Instead, section 3051 “create[d] an earlier postjudgment opportunity for incarcerated individuals to demonstrate growth, rehabilitation, and readiness to reintegrate into society.”  (Ans. Br. 21:22-24.) 

 

In this context, the California Supreme Court has described an amendment as “a legislative act designed to change an existing initiative statute by adding or taking from it some particular provision.”  (Pearson, supra, 48 Cal.4th at 571.)  “The Legislature remains free to address a ‘related but distinct area’ or a matter that an initiative measure ‘does not specifically authorize or prohibit.’”  (Kelly, supra, 47 Cal.4th at 1025-26 [internal citations and quotation marks omitted].)  Here, section 3051 does not take away or prohibit anything that was “authorized” by Proposition 83.  Rather, the issue is whether Proposition 83 “prohibits” the youth offender parole program created under section 3051.  (Pearson, supra, 48 Cal.4th at 571.) 

 

            The principles of statutory interpretation are well-known and apply to the court’s interpretation of an initiative like Proposition 83. 

 

We first consider the initiative’s language, giving the words their ordinary meaning and construing this language in the context of the statute and initiative as a whole. If the language is not ambiguous, we presume the voters intended the meaning apparent from that language, and we may not add to the statute or rewrite it to conform to some assumed intent not apparent from that language. If the language is ambiguous, courts may consider ballot summaries and arguments in determining the voters’ intent and understanding of a ballot measure.

 

(Pearson, supra, 48 Cal.4th at 571.)  The court first considers the plain language of Proposition 83.  Section 667.6(c) states in relevant part: “In lieu of the term provided in Section 1170.1, a full, separate, and consecutive term may be imposed for each violation of an offense specified in subdivision (e) if the crimes involve the same victim on the same occasion.”  Thus, section 667.6(c) authorizes—but does not require—consecutive sentences for each of the crimes specified in subdivision (e).  Significantly, under section 3051, the incarcerated individual remains subject to the original sentence, and will forfeit the right to youth offender parole consideration if “subsequent to attaining 23 years of age” the inmate “commits an additional crime for which malice aforethought is a necessary element … or for which the individual is sentenced to life in prison.” (§ 3051(h); see generally People v. Franklin (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 261, 278-279.)  Proposition 83 and section 667.6 do not expressly prohibit this type of parole eligibility for youthful offenders in which the original sentence remains operative.   

 

            The court also considers whether Proposition 83 and section 667.6 should be read to implicitly prohibit a program creating parole eligibility for youthful offenders.  The court answers this question in the negative.  “[S]tatutes covering the same subject matter are to be construed together and harmonized, if possible, to maintain the integrity of both statutes.”  (Id. at 797.)  “[C]ourts should construe all legislative enactments to give them some meaning.”  (Rosado v. Wyman, 397 U.S. 397, 415 (1970).)  Parole eligibility for youthful offenders represents “a related but distinct area” than the sentencing scheme established by section 667.6(c).  (Kelly, supra, 47 Cal.4th at 1025-26.)  Specifically, although a form of punishment, “parole constitutes a distinct phase from the underlying prison sentence.”  (People v. Nuckles (2013) 56 Cal.4th 601, 609.)  Section 667.6 is a sentencing statute, not a parole statute, and section 3051 does not amend the sentencing authority set forth in section 667.6.  Further, as summarized above, “[t]he Legislature first created this system of youth offender parole hearings in 2013, following a series of court decisions identifying Eighth Amendment limits on the sentencing of juvenile offenders.”  (Hardin, supra, 15 Cal.5th at 843, 846.)  In fact, in a 2012 decision, the California Supreme Court urged the Legislature to “enact legislation enabling a parole eligibility mechanism” that would provide “an opportunity to obtain release on a showing of rehabilitation and maturity” for youthful offenders serving lengthy sentences. (People v. Caballero (2012) 55 Cal.4th 262, 269, fn. 5.)  The plain language of Proposition 83 does not show an intent by the electorate to foreclose legislation that responds to developments in the law with respect to Eighth Amendment limits on the sentencing of juvenile offenders.  (See Resp. RJN Exh. 5.) 

 

Petitioners’ remaining arguments are not persuasive.  In the opening brief, Petitioners cite the following statement from the legislative findings for Proposition 83: “Existing laws that punish aggravated sexual assault, habitual sexual offenders, and child molesters must be strengthened and improved.”  (OB 18:21-23; see Resp. RJN Exh. 5 at 46, Sec. 2(h).)  In reply, Petitioners cite the following statement from the Argument in Favor of Proposition 83: “Proposition 83 will: … Require dangerous sex offenders to serve their entire sentence and not be released early for any reason.”  (Reply 12:26-28, citing Resp. RJN Exh. 5 at 44.)  These generic statements from the ballot materials do not mention “parole” and do not show that the voters intended in Proposition 83 to prohibit parole eligibility for youthful offenders. 

 

Finally, Petitioners argue that there “can be no doubt” that section 3051 amended section 667.6 in light of the following paragraph from a recent California Supreme Court decision:

 

Section 3051 “reflects the Legislature's judgment that 25 years is the maximum amount of time that [most youth] offender[s] may serve before becoming eligible for parole.” (Franklin, supra, 63 Cal.4th at p. 278, 202 Cal.Rptr.3d 496, 370 P.3d 1053; see id. at p. 281, 202 Cal.Rptr.3d 496, 370 P.3d 1053 [§ 3051 “effectively reforms the parole eligibility date of a [youth] offender's original sentence”].) By superseding the statutorily mandated sentences of most prisoners who committed their controlling offense before the age of 26, section 3051 “changed the manner in which the [youth] offender's original sentence operates by capping the number of years that he or she may be imprisoned before becoming eligible for release on parole.”

 

(People v. Williams (2024) 17 Cal.5th 99, 115 [modifications to Franklin in original].)  In this discussion from Williams, and also in the decision cited therein, People v. Franklin (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 261, the California Supreme Court did not consider whether section 3051 “amended” section 667.6 within the meaning of the constitutional limits on legislative amendments established by Article II, section 10(c) of the California Constitution.  Indeed, while other parts of Proposition 83 were discussed in Williams and Franklin, section 667.6 was not even mentioned.  Furthermore, although Williams and Franklin refer to section 3051 as “changing” the original sentences of certain juvenile offenders, the Supreme Court did not opine that the voters intended to “prohibit” such changes when they enacted Proposition 83.  “‘It is axiomatic that language in a judicial opinion is to be understood in accordance with the facts and issues before the court. An opinion is not authority for propositions not considered.’”  (People v. Knoller (2007) 41 Cal.4th 139, 154-55.)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Based upon the foregoing, the court orders as follows:

 

            1.         The court denies Petitioners’ petition for a writ of traditional mandate or prohibition under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1085 and/or 1103.

 

            2.         The court overrules Respondents’ demurrer as moot.

 

            3.         The parties shall meet-and-confer and lodge a proposed judgment forthwith.

 

            4.         The court’s clerk shall provide notice.
