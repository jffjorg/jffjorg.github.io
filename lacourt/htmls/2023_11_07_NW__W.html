DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03586    Hearing Date: November 7, 2023    Dept: W

JESSICA ALVARADO, et al. v. FORD MOTOR COMPANY, et al.

 

Demurrer to plaintiffs’ complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        November 7, 2023                            Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03586

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Ford Motor Company and Sunrise Ford

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Jessica Alvarado and Brian Mendes  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Chen Fei Liu Decl. ¶3, Exh. D.)   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action which arises from the purchase of a 2021 Ford Explorer. On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Ford Motor Company and Sunrise Ford for Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section; Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment; and Negligent Repair.

 

Plaintiff alleges their vehicle had a sensing defect, which Defendant knew about and concealed.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Ford Motor Company and Sunrise Ford Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Ford Motor Company and Sunrise Ford to the Plaintiffs’ complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs cannot state their fifth cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment against Ford  as a matter of law and Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action fails to plead facts sufficient to state a cause of action.

 

Fifth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment against Defendant Ford on the grounds Plaintiffs fail to plead the defect Ford allegedly concealed as well as a duty to disclose and Plaintiffs’ Fraud Claim is barred by the Economic Loss Rule.

 

The elements of a claim for fraudulent concealment are: “(1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose that fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damages as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc.¿(2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.) As it is a species of fraud and deceit, such claim must be plead with specificity. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 (specificity means pleading the who, where, when, what, and how).) But this rule for fraud by concealment, unlike fraud by an affirmative misrepresentation, must be applied differently in the case of non-disclosure because it is difficult to allege “who” or “how” or “by what means” something was not disclosed or “when” or “where” a statement was not made. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Imp. System & Planning Ass’n, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  Because of the nature of such fraud and the fact such facts supporting concealment are more likely to be known by defendant, less particularity is required.  (Id.)

 

Defendants first argue Plaintiffs’ fraudulent concealment claim fails to allege with particularity the defect Ford allegedly concealed. Defendants contend the complaint merely describes a list of ways in which Ford F-150s transmissions may be defective—e.g. “hesitation and/or delayed acceleration, harsh and/or hard shifting, jerking, shuddering, and/or juddering ("Transmission Defect")” (Compl. ¶25) and hazardous driving conditions relating generally to the purported transmission defect.

 

The court agrees. The complaint simply alleges the vehicle suffered from transmission defects, electrical defects, body defects and Defendants were allegedly aware such defects can cause the vehicles and their 10-speed transmissions to experience hesitation and/or delayed acceleration; harsh and/or hard shifting; jerking, shuddering, and/or juddering. There are no allegations Plaintiffs’ vehicle suffered any of these issues as a result of the Transmissions Defect.

 

Next, Defendants argue even if Plaintiffs had pled an identifiable defect, Plaintiffs fail to allege that Ford had a legal duty to disclose those facts. “There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336 (citation omitted).) Defendants contend Plaintiffs do not allege any kind of fiduciary relationship with Ford, Ford had exclusive knowledge, or actively concealed the defect. The court agrees, as alleged, it is unclear that Ford had exclusive knowledge of the particular defect in Plaintiffs’ vehicle. The court notes, however, if Plaintiffs’ were to sufficiently allege defects in their vehicle, the allegations of Ford’s exclusive knowledge may be sufficient to establish Ford’s knowledge of hesitation and/or delayed acceleration; harsh and/or hard shifting; jerking, shuddering, and/or juddering in the subject vehicle.

 

Lastly, Defendants argue the fifth cause of action is barred by the Economic Loss Rule. The economic loss rule prevents “a party alleging fraud or deceit in connection with a contract” from recovering in tort unless she can “establish tortious conduct independent of a breach of the contract itself, that is, violation of ‘some independent duty arising from tort law.’”  (Food Safety Net Services v. Eco Safe Systems USA, Inc. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1118, 1130, citing Robinson Helicopter Co. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 990.)  This exception is extremely “narrow in scope and limited to a defendant’s affirmative misrepresentations on which a plaintiff relies and which expose a plaintiff to liability for personal damages independent of the plaintiff’s economic loss.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., 34 Cal.4th at pg. 993.)  Courts require a showing that the alleged product defect opens Plaintiff up to third party liability in order to overcome the economic loss rule, and a separate tort independent of the breach of contract itself must be alleged.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 326-329; Food Safety Net Services, Cal.App.4th at pg. 1130.) 

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs’ fraud claim is based on an alleged fraudulent omission rather than affirmative misrepresentation, seeks purely economic losses, and derives from Ford’s alleged breach of warranty. Moreover, there are no allegations of physical damage to Plaintiffs’ property—beyond the defective vehicle itself—or that personal injury accompanied such losses.

 

The Court of Appeal expressly ruled late last year that the economic loss rule does not apply to fraudulent concealment claims in Song-Beverly actions. (Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 843 [review granted February 1, 2023].)[1] However, even under Dhital, the court finds the allegations insufficient. Unlike Dhital, Plaintiff has not alleged an actual defect in their vehicle’s transmission. Plaintiffs’ general allegations are insufficient to support a fraudulent concealment claim.

 

Accordingly, the court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Sixth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action for negligent repair on the grounds the Economic Loss Rule bars Plaintiffs’ claims and the complaint fails to plead the essential element of damages.

 

In a negligent performance case, a “plaintiff will be entitled to recover economic loss damages without the need to allege and prove personal injury or property damage” based on a consideration of the following factors: “(1) the extent to which the transaction was intended to affect the plaintiff, (2) the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, (3) the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, (4) the closeness of the connection between the defendant’s conduct and the injury suffered, (5) the moral blame attached to the defendant’s conduct, and (6) the policy of preventing future harm.”¿ (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 782, 786.)¿ “[T]he foreseeability of the economic harm to the plaintiff from the defendant’s negligent conduct [is] the critical factor.”¿ (Id. at 782.)¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiffs delivered their car for repair—a professional service—and reasonably expected that Sunrise would perform the repair with care and skill.¿ The transaction was clearly intended to affect the Plaintiffs because the transaction was for repair of their car.¿ The harm to Plaintiffs were foreseeable in the event Sunrise negligently repaired Plaintiffs’ car.¿ Plaintiffs allege they suffered injury due to the negligent repair, and public policy supports imposing liability for negligent repair where the car may result in injury to the owner or others on the road.¿ In sum, even if Plaintiffs’ damages are solely economic, they may still seek recovery because the economic loss rule does not apply in cases involving the negligent performance of services that results in foreseeable injury.¿ Moreover, the holding in North American that the economic loss rule does not apply to contracts for services was recently reaffirmed by the Court of Appeal in Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905.

 

As for Defendants’ contention regarding lack of damages, the court agrees. The elements of a negligence cause of action are duty, breach, causation and damages.¿ (Burgess v. Super. Ct. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064, 1072.)¿ The complaint does not allege that Plaintiffs paid any out of pocket expenses for any repairs performed by Sunrise, or otherwise explain how Plaintiff was damaged by the negligent repair.¿ Therefore, the court finds Plaintiffs have not adequately alleged the essential element of damages to state their negligent repair claim. 

 

Accordingly, the court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action with leave to amend.
