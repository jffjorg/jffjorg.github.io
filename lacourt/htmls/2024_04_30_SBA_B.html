Hearing Date:                          April 30, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Attorney Richard C. Giller for defendant Urban Commons Queensway, LLC

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

The court considered the moving papers.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED. The court orders that the attorney is relieved as counsel of record for defendant Urban Commons Queensway, LLC, effective upon the filing of the proofs of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” (Judicial Council form MC-053) upon the client.

BACKGROUND

On January 5, 2021, plaintiff Radiant Services Corporation filed a complaint against Urban Commons Queensway, LLC for common counts.

            On February 5, 2021, both plaintiff and defendant filed a notice of stay of proceedings as to the bankruptcy filing of defendant.

            A status conference re bankruptcy is set for January 21, 2025.

LEGAL STANDARD

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.  See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal. App. 2d 398.

CRC Rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant’s attorney Richard C. Giller at Greenspoon Marder LLP seeks to be relieved as counsel of defendant Urban Commons Queensway, LLC.

            Counsel states in the declaration that the attorney originally in charge of this matter has since left Greenspoon Marder LLP and that “at this time, there is no one currently at the Firm with the time, resources, or client contact to take over the matter and effectively represent the best interests of the client.”

The court finds that the attorney submitted a declaration establishing that the service requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, have been satisfied.  The court also finds that the attorney has shown sufficient reason why the motion to be relieved as counsel should be granted.

            The motion is GRANTED.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED. The court orders that the attorney is relieved as counsel of record for defendant Urban Commons Queensway, LLC, effective upon the filing of the proofs of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” (Judicial Council form MC-053) upon the client.

Moving counsel is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Hearing Date:                          April 30, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Atlee George Pollock, Estate of Mary Pollock, deceased, Paul Carpenter

Responding Party:                  None

(1)   Motion for an Order Imposing a Terminating Sanction, or in the alternative, Evidentiary Sanctions, and Awarding a Monetary Sanction (as to Jorge Chilel)

(2)   Motion for an Order Imposing a Terminating Sanction, or in the alternative, Evidentiary Sanctions, and Awarding a Monetary Sanction (as to Everlido Chilel)

 

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed. 

RULING

            The motions for terminating sanctions are GRANTED.  The First Amended Complaint is DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE.

BACKGROUND

On October 24, 2022, plaintiffs Jorge Chilel and Everlido Chilel filed a complaint against Atlee George Pollock, Mary Pollock, and Paul Carpenter for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence based on an incident that occurred on October 30, 2020, at Vallon Dr. and Hawthorne Blvd., Rancho Palos Verdes.

On March 29, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC, identifying Estate of Mary Pollock, deceased as a defendant.

On September 29, 2023, the court granted plaintiffs’ counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

On January 26, 2024, the court granted defendants’ motions to compel discovery responses.

Trial is set for January 22, 2025.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions.  CCP § 2025.450(h) (depositions); § 2030.290(c) (interrogatories); § 2031.300(c) (demands for production of documents).  CCP § 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .  [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .”  CCP § 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . . .” 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances:  [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’”  Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390 (quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 1225, 1246).  “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’”  Los Defensores, 223 Cal. App. 4th at 390 (citation omitted).

“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.”  Los Defensores, 223 Cal. App. 4th at 390 (citing Lang, 77 Cal. App. 4th at 1244-1246 (discussing cases)); see, e.g., Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 1611, 1617-1622 (terminating sanctions imposed after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery); Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 481, 491, disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal. 4th 469, 478, n. 4 (terminating sanctions imposed against plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes).

DISCUSSION

Defendants Atlee Pollock, Estate of Mary Pollock, deceased, and Paul Carpenter request an order imposing terminating sanctions against plaintiffs Jorge Chilel and Everlido Chilel or, in the alternative, imposing evidentiary sanctions.

On January 26, 2024, the court granted defendants’ motions to compel responses to defendants’ form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and demand for inspection and production of documents, within twenty days.  The court also ordered plaintiffs to pay sanctions in the amount of $380 each, within 30 days.  Plaintiffs did not file an opposition.

Defense counsel states that to date, defense counsel has not received responses and that plaintiffs failed to pay sanctions.  See Catherine Walsh decl.

The court notes that a court may not issue a terminating sanction for failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction.  Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 608, 610, 615.

The court rules as follows:  The court finds that plaintiffs have engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by disobeying the court’s January 26, 2024 order to serve responses without objections and to produce responsive documents as directed by the court.  CCP §§2023.010(g), 2023.030.  The court thus finds that it is appropriate, and exercises its discretion, to impose terminating sanctions.  CCP §2023.030(d)(1).

Defendants also request monetary sanctions in the amount of $560 against each plaintiff.  The court declines to impose additional monetary sanctions as terminating sanctions are sufficient.

The motions are GRANTED. 

ORDER

The motions are GRANTED.

The First Amended Complaint is DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE.

Defendants are ordered to give notice of the ruling.

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Providence Health System – Southern California

(1)   Demurrer to Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The demurrer to the first cause of action in the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first cause of action and prayer at no. 1 for attorney’s fees and prayer at no. 3 for punitive and exemplary damages. 

BACKGROUND

On May 11, 2022, plaintiff Paula Renee Pruitt filed a complaint against Providence Health System – Southern California dba Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center Torrance, Knd Development 53, LLC dba Kindred Hospital South Bay, Kindred Healthcare Operating, LLC, and Hosayn Khaleeli for (1) dependent adult abuse/neglect and (2) medical negligence.

On November 16, 2023, plaintiff dismissed Knd Development 53, LLC dba Kindred Hospital South Bay, Kindred Healthcare Operating LLC, and Hosayn Khaleeli.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendant Providence demurs to the first cause of action for dependent adult abuse/neglect on the grounds that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

The complaint alleges that plaintiff was a patient at Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center Torrance and its associated Transitional Care Center at various times between February 6, 2021 and April 7, 2021.  Plaintiff was also a patient at Kindred Hospital South Bay between April 7, 2021 and January 22, 2022.  While a patient at defendants’ institutions, she was a dependent adult as defined in Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.23.  Hosayn Khaleeli is a physician who had the responsibility for the care and treatment of plaintiff.  Complaint, ¶1.  While plaintiff was a patient of defendants’ healthcare institutions, she was bedridden, helpless, and totally reliant on the hospital care providers for all of her needs including nutrition and repositioning.  As a result of improper withholding of care and reckless neglect, she developed several decubitus ulcers including at least one Stage IV decubitus ulcer.  These defendants and their agents and employees were either plaintiff’s “care custodians” as defined by Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.17, or her medical providers who owed a professional duty of care.  Id., ¶2.

            First cause of action for elder abuse

In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789.   There must be a showing of recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression.  Id. at 783.

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.”  Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).  “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.”  Id. at 410 (citation omitted).  “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.”  Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).  Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code §15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.  “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.”  Id. at 159.

The complaint alleges that as care custodians of plaintiff, defendants owed duties of care to her but recklessly neglected her health needs and violated applicable federal, state, and local laws, regulations, and codes that apply to those providing services to persons such as plaintiff. Among defendants’ acts and omissions constituting a withholding of care and reckless neglect were the failure to monitor and assess the condition of plaintiff’s skin, the failure to properly reposition her while confined to bed, and the failure to adequately monitor plaintiff’s nutrition all of which was necessary to prevent the formation of decubitus ulcers.  Complaint, ¶11.  The conduct of defendants constituted statutory neglect of plaintiff as defined by Welf. & Inst. Code §15610.57.  Said defendants and/or agents and employees under their supervision, recklessly neglected plaintiff and withheld essential care and services from her and allowed her skin integrity to deteriorate to such a degree that a Stage IV decubitus ulcer was permitted to form.  Id., ¶12.  At all times, defendants authorized, ratified, assisted and encouraged the acts of all nurses by failing to establish and implement skin care policies and procedures and by failing to sufficiently train/retrain their employees, and by failing to take other reasonable actions to ensure that the dependent patients in their care and custody, including plaintiff, were not neglected or abused in violation of the Elder/Dependent Adult Abuse/Neglect Act.  Id., ¶13. Defendants knew that if they did not comply with respective duties of care, and if they did not provide adequate care, supervision, reassessment, appropriate care planning, and provision of care consistent with the plan of care, plaintiff would probably sustain serious skin injury.  In particular, and without

limiting the generality of the foregoing, defendants knew that because plaintiff was in a totally dependent state, she was unable to voice complaints of pain or demand that she be fed or repositioned.  These defendants also knew that their failure to provide such care as appropriate would pose the peril that plaintiff would suffer serious pain and suffering as a result of the formation of decubitus ulcers.  Id., ¶16.

Defendant argues that allegations relative to the provision of substandard medical services by a hospital “have the gravamen of a theory of medical negligence, not elder abuse” and that the patient-medical provider relationship is not the sort of relationship the Legislature was addressing in the Elder Abuse Act.  Defendant also asserts that theories of professional negligence and elder abuse are mutually exclusive.  Defendant further contends that Providence’s hospital services do not implicate elder abuse because the services were not for the basic needs that “an able-bodied adult would ordinarily manage without assistance.”  Defendant argues that plaintiff’s allegations that Providence failed to properly assess, reposition, and provide adequate nutrition to plaintiff to prevent skin breakdown and the formation of pressure injuries does not transform the hospital from its capacity as provider of medical services to just a care custodian.  Moreover, defendant argues, plaintiff has failed to plead with particularity.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient, and that alternative pleading is not subject to demurrer.  Plaintiff also contends that hospital nurses can be liable for elder neglect.  Plaintiff further asserts that she has alleged with sufficient particularity.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to meet the elements.  The allegations do not show oppression, fraud, or malice or a conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (decubitus ulcers, including Stage IV).  Plaintiff has also not alleged with sufficient factual particularity.  As to moving defendant, it appears that plaintiff is alleging that she was a patient at both the hospital and transitional care facility over a period of two months at various times.  Plaintiff has only generally alleged moving defendant’s conduct that purportedly caused plaintiff’s decubitus ulcers, including a Stage IV one.  Plaintiff also lumps defendants together and does not allege the specific conduct of moving defendant, whether it be at the hospital or transitional care facility.  Further, the allegations are insufficient as to ratification as they are conclusory.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Motion to Strike

            Defendant Providence requests that the court strike the first cause of action in its entirety, prayer at no. 1 for attorney’s fees and at no. 3 for punitive and exemplary damages.

In light of the ruling on the first cause of action, the motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.       

ORDER

The demurrer to the first cause of action in the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first cause of action and prayer at no. 1 for attorney’s fees and prayer at no. 3 for punitive and exemplary damages.  

Moving defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

LAX PHYSICAL MEDICINE, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          April 30, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants LAX Physical Medicine and Matthew Araghi

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Mahin Golchin

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action in the FAC.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer within 20 days.

BACKGROUND

            On December 6, 2023, plaintiff Mahin Golchin filed a complaint against LAX Physical Medicine, LAX Physical Therapy, and Matthew Araghi for general negligence; and against DJO Global, Inc. for product liability. 

            On January 26, 2024, plaintiff filed an amendment designating Doe 1 as Matthew Szkalak.

            On March 14, 2024, the court sustained defendants LAX Physical Medicine and Matthew Araghi’s demurrer with leave to amend based on uncertainty because there were no allegations against LAX Physical Medicine.

            On March 14, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendants LAX Physical Medicine and Matthew Araghi demur to the first cause of action for general negligence in the complaint on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

Under the first cause of action for negligence, the FAC alleges that on September 5, 2023, plaintiff was a patient of LAX Physical Therapy and LAX Physical Medication, which are subsidiaries and DBA businesses owned, operated, controlled, and supervised by owner defendant Matthew Araghi.  Defendants, including LAX Physical Therapy and LAX Physical Medicine, both working at the control, order, and supervision of Araghi, agreed to perform and undertook to perform physical therapy services for plaintiff, including heat pads for her legs.  Defendant Araghi, treating plaintiff at LAX Physical Therapy and LAX Physical Medicine, owed a reasonable duty of observation, attention, examination, evaluation, diagnosis, proper and safe treatment of plaintiff while she was under their care for treating her leg pain at their therapy centers.  Defendants negligently failed to conform to the standard of care and duty owed with respect to the care and treatment rendered to plaintiff when she was prescribed heat pads at defendants, under the control of Araghi, without proper care, supervision, administration, that burned plaintiff’s legs, causing her permanent injuries.

Defendants argue that although plaintiff is asserting a cause of action for “general negligence,” the allegations sound in medical malpractice, and that plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for medical malpractice.  Defendants argue that the two causes of action, while both rooted in negligence, “are two distinct and separate theories with different standards and difference recoveries.”  Demurrer, at 5.  Yet, defendants cite to Flowers v. Torrance Memorial Hospital Medical Center (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 992, 997-98, which determined that there are no “separate and independent” distinctions between a claim for ordinary negligence and professional negligence as to the elements of duty, breach, causation, and damages.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that she has properly pled the elements for negligence.

The court rules as follows:  Courts must overrule a demurrer if the complaint states a cause of action under any theory, regardless of the title of the cause of action.  See Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38 (“If the complaint states a cause of action under any theory, regardless of the title under which the factual basis for relief is stated, that aspect of the¿complaint¿is good against a demurrer”).  At the pleading stage, the court need not, and should not, decide whether this case is a general negligence case or a medical negligence case.  As defendant’s own citation to Flowers, supra, indicates, the difference between a general negligence case and a medical negligence case is if the wrongdoer is a professional, the factfinder considers additional circumstances in determining what constitutes ordinary prudence in the situation.  See also Donnelly v. Southern Pacific Co. (1941) 18 Cal.2d 863, 871 (“There are no ‘degrees’ of care, as a matter of law; there are only different amounts of care, as a matter of fact . . . .”).  So long as the complaint states a cause of action under any theory, the factual distinctions between the “amounts of care” that may have been owed by moving defendants are not relevant on demurrer, and such factual issues cannot be resolved on demurrer.
            The court finds that the allegations are sufficient as to the elements to constitute a cause of action against defendants, regardless of the title of the cause of action.

            The demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

ORDER

            The demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer within 20 days.

            Defendant’s request that the Court issue an order under CCP § 116.1 is DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion for Reconsideration

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            Judgment was entered on November 3, 1987 in favor of plaintiffs Frank Wilson, Thelma Wilson, Johnnie Wilson, and Lonnie Wilson against defendants Delta Homes, Inc., Delta Omni Corporation, Corporate Development, Brad Handy, Joe Polifroni, Kevin Williams, Chris Kalageris, and Theadore Kalogeris.  The judgment was renewed on October 15, 1997 and on September 20, 2007 for $427,670.

On September 20, 2007, judgment creditor filed a full satisfaction of judgment as to judgment debtor Joe Polifroni’s pro-rata liability on the judgment. 

 

On February 25, 2015, the court denied plaintiff Lonnie B. Wilson’s motion to revoke the acknowledgment of assignment of judgment of the assignee of record, Attorney Collection Service, Inc., and to revert all rights, title, and interest to the judgment to Lonnie Wilson.

On November 10, 2016, Lonnie Wilson filed an application for and renewal of judgment for $721,519.  The judgment was renewed.

In March 2017, the court denied a similar motion as the one that was denied on February 25, 2015.

On November 16, 2018, the court denied a similar motion as the one filed in March 2017, except as to Acclaim Credit Technologies. 

On November 25, 2019, plaintiff Lonnie Wilson filed an acknowledgement of assignment of judgment, all rights and interest to Rickie Joel Crocket, power of attorney for judgment creditor.

On January 16, 2020, the court denied Lonnie Wilson’s order for an affidavit of identity as to Theodore Kalogaris.

On July 24, 2020, the court denied Lonnie Wilson’s motions for an affidavit of identity as to Theodore Kalogeris and Chris Kalogeris.

On April 6, 2023, Lonnie Wilson filed a notice of renewal of judgment.

On August 28, 2023, Lonnie Wilson filed a notice of renewal of judgment.

On January 16, 2024, the court granted in part Chris Kalogeris’ motion to vacate the judgment or, in the alternative, to vacate and strike the renewals of judgment.  The court vacated and set aside the notices of renewal of judgment filed on April 6, 2023 and August 28, 2023.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP §1008(a), “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.  The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”

As stated by the court in Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal. App. 4th 1494, CCP §1008 governs reconsideration of court orders whether initiated by a party or the court itself.  “It is the exclusive means for modifying, amending or revoking an order.  That limitation is expressly jurisdictional.”  Id. at 1499.   

CCP §128(a) states, “Every court shall have the power to do all of the following: . . . (8) To amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice. . . .”

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Frank Wilson requests that the court reconsider its January 16, 2024 order striking Wilson’s renewal of judgment. 

            Plaintiff argues that the assignment to Attorney Collection Services, Inc. on August 8, 2005 was invalid.  Plaintiff contends that it was not signed by Lonnie Wilson but purportedly by Cynthia Manning through a power of attorney.  Manning states in her declaration that she does not recall signing such a document and believes that “any assignment through me as an assignee was a forgery or a lifting of my signature from somewhere else.”  Plaintiff argues that if this assignment never occurred, Lonnie Wilson would be entitled to renew on his own.

            In opposition, defendant argues that none of the “facts” offered by plaintiff are new or warrant reconsideration by the court as he has assigned his rights to the judgment three times to three different assignees.  Plaintiff’s attempts to revoke the assignments were rejected by the court three times. 

            In reply, plaintiff argue that equity weighs in favor of plaintiff.

            The Court rules as follows:  Plaintiff has not met its burden of showing new or different facts, circumstances, or law that could not have been discovered or presented earlier.  Further, plaintiff appears to be requesting that the Court reconsider its rulings in 2015, 2017, and 2018 denying plaintiff’s requests to revoke the assignments.  Plaintiff’s request is untimely. 

            The motion is DENIED.

ORDER

            The motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
