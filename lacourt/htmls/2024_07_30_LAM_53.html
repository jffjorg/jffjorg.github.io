County of Los Angeles – Central District

July 30, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff David Maijala         

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:     Defendant and cross-complainant Mulgrew Aircraft Components, Inc., and defendant Mohammad Houshiar            

Motion for Terminating Sanctions

The court considered the moving, opposition, reply, and supplemental reply papers filed in connection with this motion.[1]

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff David Maijala (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) imposing terminating sanctions against defendant Mulgrew Aircraft Components, Inc. (“Defendant”), and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $18,100.  Plaintiff moves for this relief on the ground that Defendant violated the court’s September 8, 2023, and March 4, 2024 orders.

On September 8, 2023, the court issued an order (1) granting in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel the production of documents in response to Plaintiff’s First Amended Notice of Deposition, and (2) ordering Defendant to produce, at the deposition of its person(s) most qualified, documents responsive to Plaintiff’s requests for production of documents (i) numbers 1, 5-8, 10, 13, 16, 22, 54-59, 61-62, 69, and 71, and (ii) numbers 14-15, 28, 30-44, 64, and 117-124, limited to the period of time from May 1, 2016 through October 10, 2019, as set forth in Plaintiff’s First Amended Notice of Deposition.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:7-15.)  Thereafter, on March 4, 2024, the court issued an order (1) denying Plaintiff’s motion for terminating and monetary sanctions, but (2) ordering Defendant to produce the documents specified in the court’s September 8, 2023 order at a deposition of its person(s) most qualified, to be taken by Plaintiff’s counsel “on a date no later than March 29, 2024[.]”  (Mar. 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:16-26.)

Plaintiff contends that Defendant did not produce the following documents at the deposition of its person most qualified: (1) approximately 150 Best Deburring Services Certificates of Conformance (Request for Production No. 39); (2) all subcontracted forming-related documents (Requests for Production Nos. 32, 38, 69); (3) a list of Defendant’s employees and consultants (Request for Production No. 37); (4) all communications to and from Plaintiff’s email addresses at Mulgrew (Requests for Production Nos. 7 and 13); (5) all of Defendant’s policies and procedures for manufacturing rainbow fittings (Request for Production No. 14);    (6) all of Defendant’s policies and procedures regrading employee protections against retaliation (Request for Production No. 15); (7) all contracts and purchase orders between Defendant and Lockheed (Request for Production No. 33); (8) completed traveler documents for Lockheed rainbow fittings (Request for Production No. 35); (9) AS9100 system audit documentation (Request for Production No. 41); (10) all Lockheed annual surveys or audits of Defendant (Request for Production No. 43); and (11) all penetrant inspection results for Lockheed wing joint fitting (rainbow fittings) (Requests for Production Nos. 120 and 124).

In its opposition to this motion, Defendant (1) submits evidence establishing that, on May 20, 2024, Defendant produced approximately 150 documents in response to Request for Production of Documents, number 39, and (2) states, in its opposition brief, that it produced various other documents on that date and on May 24, 2024.  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 22; Opp., pp. 7:10-12, 14:5-7.)  Defendant’s counsel further states that Defendant is “not aware of any existing, responsive documents which have not been produced in this matter.”  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 24.)  Plaintiff asserts, in his supplemental reply papers, that Defendant still has not produced all responsive documents.

The court finds that Plaintiff has met his burden to show that Defendant (1) has documents in its possession, custody, or control that are responsive to Requests for Production of Documents numbers 7, 13, 32, 35, 38-39, and 69, but (2) did not produce those documents at the deposition of those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on Defendant’s behalf no later than March 29, 2024.  (March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26.)  

1.     Meet and Confer Requirement

As a threshold matter, the court notes that Defendant contends that Plaintiff did not meet and confer before filing the pending motion.  However, Plaintiff was not required to meet and confer with Defendant before filing this motion for terminating and monetary sanctions for failure to obey the court’s orders directing Defendant to produce documents.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.030, 2025.450, subd. (h).)  The court therefore evaluates Plaintiff’s motion on its merits.

2.     Best Deburring Services Certificates of Conformance (Request for Production No. 39)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control Best Deburring Services (“BDS”) Certificates of Conformance that were not produced within the time ordered by the court.  (March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Depo., p. 15:24-27 [Request No. 39].)

Plaintiff states, in his declaration, that (1) Defendant produced only a single BDS Certificate of Conformance (the same certificate produced by Plaintiff), and (2) Plaintiff knows that nearly 150 certificates were not produced since (i) defendant Mohammad Houshiar testified that a Certificate of Conformance accompanies every order of parts processed by BDS and delivered to Defendant, and (ii) Defendant produced over 150 invoices and purchase orders from BDS, such that there exists at least 150 corresponding certificates that Defendant did not produce.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 5, pp. 4:3-4, 4:15-16, 5:18-21.)  In opposition, Defendant submits the declaration of its counsel, in which counsel states that, after reviewing Plaintiff’s moving papers, Defendant “determined there were indeed some outstanding Certificates of Compliance from [BDS], a vendor, to [Defendant], responsive to Demand No. 39, which had not yet been produced[,]” and that Defendant “produced these approximately 150 documents on May 20, 2024.”  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 22.)  Plaintiff does not dispute, in his supplemental reply papers, that Defendant has produced all documents responsive to this demand.  (Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 4.)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has now complied with the court’s orders to produce all documents responsive to Request for Production of Documents, number 39.  The court further finds, based on the evidence presented, that Plaintiff has also shown that Defendant did not comply with the court’s March 4, 2024 order to produce those documents to Plaintiff at the deposition of those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf to be taken “on a date no later than March 29, 2024 . . . .”  (March 4, 2024 Order, p. 3:18-26.)  As set forth above, Defendant did not make the ordered production of documents until May 20, 2024—i.e., approximately two months after the date by which the court ordered the documents to be produced.  The court also notes that Defendant did not present evidence (1) explaining why Defendant did not discover the responsive documents until after counsel reviewed the pending motion for terminating sanctions, or (2) establishing that the documents could not have been produced at the deposition of its person most qualified had Defendant acted diligently.  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 22.)

Thus, the court finds that (1) Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to comply with the court’s order to produce those documents at a deposition of its person most qualified no later than March 29, 2024, but (2) Defendant has now complied with the court’s order, such that Plaintiff is no longer prejudiced by Defendant’s failure to produce those documents by March 29, 2024 as ordered by the court.  (March 4, 2024 Order, p. 3:18-26; Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).)

 

3.     Subcontracting of Forming Work (Requests for Production Nos. 32, 38, and 69)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control documents regarding subcontracting for the performing of “forming” work that were not produced as ordered by the court in its September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Depo., pp. 14:23-27 [Request No. 32], 15:21-24 [Request No. 38 demanding documents for invoices, purchase orders, and payments for BDS], 21:5-8 [Request No. 69 demanding documents that Defendant gave BDS requiring BDS to perform forming].)

Plaintiff has stated, in his declaration, that (1) Defendant produced 18 BDS invoices;     (2) five responsive documents exist but were not produced; and (3) Plaintiff knows that responsive documents exist but were not produced because (i) the BDS invoices contain unique identification numbers (e.g., job numbers, Mulgrew PO numbers) and (ii) when searching the unique identification numbers, no associated documents were found in the production.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 6, pp. 5:2-3, 6:12-21, 7:4-9.)  In his supplemental reply papers, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant did not produce additional documents responsive to these document demands on May 20, 2024 and May 24, 2024.  (Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 6.)  Moreover, the court finds that Defendant has not presented evidence establishing that it has since produced responsive documents or that the documents described by Plaintiff do not exist.  Although counsel for Defendants states, in his declaration, that counsel and Defendants “are not aware of any existing, responsive documents which have not been produced in this matter[,]” the court (1) notes that counsel did not present evidence establishing his personal knowledge of this fact, and (2) finds this assertion to be insufficient to show that these documents do not exist.  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 24.) 

Thus, the court finds that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to comply with the court’s order to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 32, 38, and 69.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).)  However, Plaintiff has not presented evidence or argument establishing that Defendant’s failure to produce the specified documents has prejudiced him.  Specifically, Plaintiff states only that the failure to produce is important because it may indicate that there are 42 to 72 missing associated documents.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 6, p. 6:24-28.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not identified prejudice that he has suffered as a result of Defendant’s conduct regarding the failure to produce this discovery.

4.     List of Mulgrew Employees and Consultants (Request for Production No. 37)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that documents responsive to Request for Production number 37, which demands the production of a list of all employees and consultants working as quality department and engineering department personnel or Quality Management System consultants for Defendant, exist but were not produced.  (Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Depo., p. 15:14-20 [Request No. 37].)  Plaintiff has not presented evidence establishing that such a list exists, and instead has stated generally that he knows this information was withheld because Defendant’s production does not include this information.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 7, p. 8:8-10.)  The court finds that this showing is insufficient to establish that Defendant has such a list in its possession, custody, or control.  Moreover, Defendant did not have an obligation to create a list including this information to produce at the deposition of its person(s) most qualified.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by not producing a list of its employees and consultants in certain positions.

5.     Communications to and from Plaintiff’s Email Account at Mulgrew (Requests for Production Nos. 7 and 13)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control documents showing communications sent to and from the email account that Plaintiff used while working for Defendant that were not produced as ordered by the court in its September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-25; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., pp. 11:11-12 [Request No. 7 demanding all communications to and from Plaintiff’s email], 12:4-6 [Request No. 13 demanding all documents and communications regarding Plaintiff’s work duties].)  

Plaintiff, who has knowledge of the emails sent to and from the email address he used while working for Defendant, has identified specific emails that were not included in Defendant’s production, and has also stated that portions of emails and continued communications were not produced.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 8, pp. 9:8-10:2.)  Moreover, Defendant did not produce sufficient evidence, independent of its counsel’s conclusory assertion that there exist no other responsive documents that have not been produced, establishing that these documents do not exist.  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 24.)  In his supplemental reply declaration, Plaintiff asserts that there remain outstanding emails from Defendant’s production.  (Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 7, p. 7:14-27.)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to comply with the court’s order to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 7 and 13.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-25; Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).)  The court further finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated a low level of prejudice because he has shown that the documents that have not been produced may refute Defendant’s claims that it was not informed by Plaintiff of violations of certain requirements.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 8, p. 10:4-9.)   

6.     Mulgrew’s Policies and Procedures for Manufacturing Rainbow Fittings (Request for Production No. 14)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that documents responsive to Request for Production number 14, which demands the production of all documents regarding Defendant’s policies and procedures with regard to the manufacturing of rainbow fittings for Lockheed Martin, exist but were not produced.  (Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., p. 12:7-11 [Request No. 14].)

Plaintiff asserts that documents regarding one specific procedure (QP-7.5, Quality Traveler Authority) remain outstanding.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 9, p. 11:14.)  However, Plaintiff did not present evidence establishing how he knows that documents evidencing such a procedure exists (e.g., if Plaintiff, while working for Defendant, had knowledge of this procedure) and was therefore withheld by Defendant.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by not producing documents regarding the QP-7.5 Quality Traveler Authority procedure.

7.     Mulgrew’s Policies and Procedures Regarding Employee Protections Against Retaliation (Request No. 15)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that documents responsive to Request for Production of Documents, number 15, which demands the production of all documents regarding Defendant’s policies and procedures regarding employee protections against retaliation for reporting illegal activity to Defendant, exist but were not produced.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., p. 12:12-15 [Request No. 15].)  Plaintiff has not presented evidence establishing knowledge that documents evidencing policies and procedures for employee protections against retaliation exist, and instead has generally asserted that such documents were not produced.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 10.)  Moreover, Plaintiff’s evidence appears to indicate that these documents may not exist.  Specifically, Plaintiff stated that, “[i]n his deposition, [Mohammad] Houshiar was uncertain whether [Defendant] provided such protections.”  (Ibid.)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by not producing documents evidencing policies and procedures regarding employee protections against retaliation.

8.     Contracts and Purchase Orders Between Mulgrew and Lockheed (Request for Production No. 33)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control documents regarding contracts and/or purchase orders between Defendant and Lockheed Martin for rainbow fittings that were not produced as ordered by the court in its September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., pp. 14:28-15:2 [Request No. 33].)

Plaintiff asserts that (1) Defendant has produced a number of contracts between Defendant and Lockheed Martin; (2) those contracts “contain references to [Department of Defense] contracts or other Lockheed documents containing further requirements, such as Federal Acquisition Regulations (FARs), which are Federal requirements applicable to contractors and subcontractors working under Federal contracts[;]” but (3) the documents that were referenced in the contracts have not been produced.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 11, p. 13:1-6, 13:20-28.)  However, Plaintiff has not shown that the documents that he contends were not produced are responsive to demand number 33.  Specifically, demand number 33 requests all documents regarding “contracts and/or purchase orders between [Defendant] and Lockheed for the Lockheed ‘Rainbow Fittings[.]’”  (Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., pp. 14:28-13:2.)  It appears to the court that, based on Plaintiff’s declaration, Defendant produced those contracts.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 11, p. 13:1, 13:9-11 [Defendant produced “[a] number of Lockheed contracts with Mulgrew”].)  Plaintiff has not shown that the Department of Defense contracts or other documents referenced therein should have been produced.  Plaintiff did not, for example, establish that the documents referenced in the contracts between Defendant and Lockheed Martin are part of the contracts themselves.  Instead, Plaintiff has identified only documents that were referenced in those contracts.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by not producing documents referenced in the contracts between Defendant and Lockheed Martin.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).)

9.     Completed Traveler Documents for Lockheed Rainbow Fittings (Request for Production No. 35)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control documents regarding completed traveler documents for each production run of the Lockheed rainbow fittings that were not produced as ordered by the court in its September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-25; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., p. 15:6-9 [Request No. 35].)

Plaintiff asserts that responsive documents exist but were not produced in connection with this demand.  Specifically, Plaintiff states that Traveler J/N 21271 and Traveler Job No. “TEST1” have not been produced.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 12, p. 14:14-16.)  Plaintiff has explained that TEST1 “was a Traveler created in response to [Plaintiff’s] proposed corrective action to fix several of the violations [Defendant] repeatedly conducted on Rainbow Fittings[,]” and that this traveler “was created to process Rainbow Fitting in accordance with the corrective action and determine its validity.”  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 12, p. 15:8-11.)  The court finds that Plaintiff’s assertions, made in his declaration, that the traveler was created in response to Plaintiff’s own proposed corrective actions establish that Plaintiff has knowledge that this document exists.  Moreover, Plaintiff’s supplemental declaration also establishes the existence of this document based on Plaintiff’s assertion that the TEST1 traveler “is referenced in Mulgrew’s own document with Bates No. MACI007675.”  (Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 11, p. 12:24-26.)  Defendant did not produce sufficient evidence, independent of its counsel’s conclusory assertion that there exist no other responsive documents that have not been produced, establishing that these documents do not exist.  (Nenni Decl., ¶ 24.)  The court further finds that Plaintiff has shown that this document was not produced.[2]  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 12, p. 15:18-20; Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 11, p. 12:23-25 [“As of the May 20, 2024 and the May 24, 2024 document productions, , an important Traveler for Job. No. ‘TEST1’ is still not produced”].)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to comply with the court’s order to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production of Documents, number 35 no later than March 29, 2024.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-25; Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).)  The court further finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that he has suffered a low level of prejudice as a result of this conduct because he has stated that the TEST1 documents will show whether Defendant completed the corrective action experiment.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 12, p. 15:12-13.)

 

10.  AS9100 System Audit Documentation (Request for Production No. 41)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that documents responsive to Request for Production number 41, which demands the production of all documents regarding the AS9100 system audit documentation conducted by International Standards Authority, Inc., exist but were not produced.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., p. 16:4-6 [Request No. 41].)   

Plaintiff asserts that Defendant “produced only partial audit reports for the years 2016, 2017, 2018, and 2019[,]” and that the audit nonconformities documentation issued during each audit is missing.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 13, p. 15:25-27.)  Specifically, Plaintiff states that five nonconformities are missing from 2016, five nonconformities are missing from 2017, and unknown number are missing from 2018, and 10 nonconformities are missing from 2019.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 13, p. 15:27-28.)  However, while Plaintiff appears to identify a certain number of missing nonconformities, Plaintiff did not produce sufficient evidence establishing that that he has knowledge that such nonconformities exist, such that the court can make a determination that Defendant failed to comply with the court’s orders to produce documents responsive to demand number 41.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 13, p. 16:28-17:2 [stating that Plaintiff knows the documents were withheld because they were not found in the produced documents].)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to produce nonconformities documents in response to Request for Production of Documents, number 41.  

11.  Lockheed Annual Surveys or Audits of Mulgrew (Request for Production No. 43)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that documents responsive to Request for Production number 43, which demands the production of all documents regarding completed Lockheed Martin annual surveys or audits of Defendant for approval under Lockheed specification STP51-302, exist but were not produced.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15 [limiting production to period of time between May 1, 2016 through October 10, 2019]; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., p. 16:12-16 [Request No. 43].)

Defendant produced to Plaintiff one survey dated 2017 and one undated survey, both of which omit the surveyor.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 14, p. 17:7-9.)  The court acknowledges that Plaintiff contends that the survey documents will “demonstrate that [Defendant] was subjected to periodic assessments to maintain the approval status to form Lockheed parts they received from Lockheed.”  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 14, p. 17:10-12.)  However, Plaintiff has not presented evidence establishing that Lockheed Martin completed annual surveys or audits of Defendant for the years 2016 and 2018-2019.  Plaintiff did not, for example, state that he had knowledge that Lockheed Martin performed surveys or audits of Defendant for those years, or that Defendant asserted that Lockheed Martin completed those surveys in response to discovery (e.g., interrogatories or requests for admission). 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process by failing to produce completed Lockheed Martin surveys or audits for the years 2016 and 2018-2019.

12.  Penetrant Inspection Results for Lockheed Wing Joint Fitting a/k/a Rainbow Fittings (Requests for Production Nos. 120 and 124)

The court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant has in its possession, custody, or control documents responsive to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 120 and 124, which demand, respectively, the production of all documents regarding the Penetrant Inspection Results provided to Defendant by its subcontractors for the Lockheed Martin wing joint fitting, a/k/a rainbow fittings, that were not produced as ordered by the court in its September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:9-15 [limiting production to period of time between May 1, 2016 through October 10, 2019]; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 5:18-26; Kakoian Decl., Ex. C, Pl. First Amended Notice of Dep., pp. 29:7-11 [Request No. 120 as to part number 3339709-2], 30:1-5 [Request No. 124 as to part number 3339709-2].)

Plaintiff has presented his declaration, in which he states that (1) although Defendant produced some penetrant inspection results, not all of the traveler documents were accompanied by the associated penetrant inspection results, and (2) specifically, the penetrant inspection results for Traveler J/N 21271 and Traveler Job. No. TEST1 – as referenced in MACI007675 – were not produced.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 15, pp. 18:25-26, 19:7-11.)  However, Plaintiff has not submitted evidence establishing the penetrant inspection results for those traveler documents exist.  Plaintiff did not, for example, present evidence establishing that all traveler documents are accompanied by such results and that the results associated with the two travelers identified above were omitted from Defendant’s production.   

The court therefore finds that that Plaintiff has not shown that the penetrant inspection results associated with the specified travelers exist but were not produced in Defendant’s initial production and the May 20, 2024 and May 24, 2024 productions.  (Maijala Decl., ¶ 15; Maijala Supp. Decl., ¶ 14, pp. 16:24-17:20.)

13.  Terminating Sanctions Are Not Appropriate

As set forth above, the court finds that Plaintiff has met his burden to show that Defendant violated the court’s September 8, 2023 and March 4, 2024 orders by failing to produce, within the time required by the court (i.e., at a deposition to be taken no later than March 29, 2024), all documents responsive to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 7, 13, 32, 35, 38-39, and 69 set forth in Plaintiff’s First Amended Notice of Deposition.

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’”¿ (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal. App. 4th 1225, 1246.)¿ “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.¿ But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’”¿ (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal. App. 4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].)¿¿¿¿¿ 

After considering the totality of the circumstances, the court finds that terminating sanctions against Defendant are not warranted.  First, the evidence establishes that Defendant partially complied with the court’s orders to produce documents at the deposition of its person(s) most qualified. 

Second, as to the documents that Plaintiff has established exist but were not produced, Plaintiff has either (1) had the resulting prejudice remedied by Defendant’s subsequent production (Request No. 39), (2) not identified prejudice suffered as a result of Defendant’s conduct (Request Nos. 33, 38, and 69), or (3) identified only a low level of prejudice suffered as a result of Defendant’s conduct (Request Nos. 7, 13, and 35).  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that terminating sanctions are required to protect Plaintiff’s interests.  (Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1158 [discovery sanctions “should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery”] [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

Third, Plaintiff has not shown that Defendant’s violation of the court’s orders is preceded by a history of abuse.  (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal. App. 4th at p. 390.)  The court found, in its March 4, 2024 order denying Plaintiff’s first motion for terminating sanctions, that Defendant did not willfully disobey the court’s September 8, 2023 order because (1) Plaintiff did not show that he noticed a deposition for Defendant’s person(s) most qualified for which Defendant did not appear or produce documents, and (2) the court did not set a date by which Defendant was required to comply with that order.  (March 4, 2024 Order, pp. 3:19-4:2.)  

Fourth, Plaintiff has not shown that less severe sanctions—such as evidence or issue sanctions—would not remedy the prejudice suffered as a result of Defendant’s failure to produce the remaining responsive documents and would not produce compliance with the court’s orders and the discovery process.  (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal. App. 4th at p. 390.)  Moreover, Plaintiff did not request that the court impose issue or evidence sanctions as an alternative to his request for terminating sanctions. 

Finally, the court has not yet imposed less severe sanctions against Defendant in connection with the production of these documents.  (Sep. 8, 2023 Order, p. 3:25 [“The court denies all requests for sanctions”]; March 4, 2024 Order, p. 4:5-6 [“The court denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions”]; Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158 [“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination”] [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s request that the court impose terminating sanctions against Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).)  

14.  Monetary Sanctions Are Appropriate

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown that monetary sanctions against Defendant are appropriate.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).)  The court finds that $6,225 ((11 hours x Kakoian’s $350 hourly rate) + (5 hours x Amkraut’s $475 hourly rate)) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Defendant.  (Kakoian Decl., ¶ 14; Amkraut Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)

The court denies Defendant’s request that the court award monetary sanctions in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff in the amount of $5,425.  (Opp., p. 19:7.)

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff David Maijala’s motion for terminating sanctions.

            The court grants plaintiff David Maijala’s motion for monetary sanctions.

            The court orders defendant Mulgrew Aircraft Components, Inc. to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,225 to plaintiff David Maijala within 30 days of the date of this order.

            The court orders plaintiff David Maijala to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Angela Brown (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Smart Resources Nursing Registry (“Defendant”) to serve further supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, numbers 2, 5, 6, 10, 11, and 13, and       (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $2,160.

As a threshold matter, the court notes that, on December 5, 2023, the court issued an order granting the motion to be relieved as Defendant’s counsel filed by attorney W. Dan Lee.  The court ordered that W. Dan Lee will be relieved as counsel of record for Defendant effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” on Defendant.  (Dec. 5, 2023 Order, MC-053, ¶ 5, subd. (a).)  On December 13, 2023, Defendant’s counsel filed a proof of service purporting to establish service of the signed order on Defendant.  However, the proof of service does not state that the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” was served on Defendant; instead, it describes the document that was served to be the “Proof of Service re Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel of Defendant Smart Resources Nursing Registry.”  (Dec. 13, 2023 Proof of Service, p. 2:5-7 [emphasis added].)  Thus, the proof of service establishes only that the proof of service itself was served on Defendant, and not the “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil.”  Moreover, the “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” was not attached to the proof of service, such that service of the proof of service established service of the court’s order on Defendant.  (Dec. 13, 2023 Proof of Service.)

Thus, because Defendant’s counsel has not filed a valid proof of service of the signed “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” on Defendant, the court finds that counsel (i.e., attorney W. Dan Lee) has not been relieved as counsel for Defendant.  The court will therefore order Plaintiff to serve a notice of this ruling on both (1) Defendant directly, at its address of record, and (2) attorney W. Dan Lee.

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 2 and 5-6 because (1) Defendant’s answers (made by exercising the option to produce documents under section 2030.230) to those interrogatories are evasive and incomplete; (2) the exercise of the option to produce documents under section 2030.230 is unwarranted and the required specification of those documents is inadequate, and (3) the objections to those interrogatories are without merit.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2), and (a)(3); Shakouri Decl., ¶ 6 and Ex. 2.)

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further response to Special Interrogatories, number 10 because Defendant’s answer to that interrogatory is not evasive or incomplete.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(1).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to Special Interrogatories, numbers 11 and 13 because Defendant’s answers to those interrogatories are evasive and incomplete.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a)(1).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)  The court finds that $1,810 ((2.5 hours x $700 hourly rate) + $60 filing fee) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Defendant in connection with this motion.  (Shakouri Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff Angela Brown’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories as follows.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, the court orders defendant Smart Resources Nursing Registry to serve further, full and complete answers to plaintiff Angela Brown’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, numbers 2, 5, 6, 11, and 13, which comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.210-2030.250, within 20 days of the date of service of this order.

            The court orders defendant Smart Resources Nursing Registry to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,810 to plaintiff Angela Brown within 30 days of the date of service of this order.

The court orders plaintiff Angela Brown to give notice of this ruling (1) to defendant Smart Resources Nursing Registry at its address of record, as set forth in section 6 of the “Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel – Civil” filed on December 5, 2023, and (2) to attorney W. Dan Lee.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim for Minor Claimant Janelle Kimbella Oliveros

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this petition.  No opposition papers were filed.  

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff and petitioner Jeanette Oliveros (“Petitioner”) seeks court approval of the settlement made on behalf of minor claimant Janelle Kimbella Oliveros (“Minor Claimant”) in this action.

The compromise of a minor’s disputed claim for damages is valid only after it has been approved, upon the filing of a petition, by the court.¿ (Prob. Code, § 3500.)¿ The petition must be verified by the petitioner, must contain a full disclosure of all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise, and must be prepared on Judicial Council form MC-350.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.)¿

Defendants Joseph Heffesse, as trustee of the Coldwater Canyon Trust, Sandra B. Sternberg Heffesse, and LA Properties Heffesse LLC have agreed to pay a total of $175,000 to settle this action, of which $5,000 will be allocated to Minor Claimant.  (MC-350, ¶¶ 10-11.)  Of the $5,000 allocated to Minor Claimant, $1,250 will be paid to counsel for attorney’s fees and $134.35 will be paid to counsel for litigation costs.  (MC-350, ¶¶ 13, 15-16.)  The remaining $3,615.65 will be paid or delivered to Petitioner, Minor Claimant’s parent, without bond, on the terms and under the conditions specified in Probate Code sections 3401-3402.  (MC-350, ¶ 18, subd. (b)(5) and Attachment 18b(5), Oliveros Decl., ¶¶ 1, 3, 6; Prob. Code, §§ 3401, 3402.)

The court has reviewed the petition and finds the settlement to be fair and reasonable, and in the best interest of Minor Claimant.  The court further finds that the declaration of Rachel Fishenfeld is sufficient to support the request for attorney’s fees in the amount of $1,250 (representing 25 percent of the $5,000 settlement).  (MC-350, Attachment 13(a), Fishenfeld Decl., ¶¶ 2-3, 6-11; Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 7.955.)  The court therefore grants Petitioner’s petition.

ORDER

            The court grants petitioner Jeanette Oliveros’s petition for approval of compromise of claim on behalf of minor claimant Janelle Kimbella Oliveros.

            The court orders that the $3,615.65 settlement on behalf of minor claimant Janelle Kimbella Oliveros may be paid to plaintiff and petitioner Jeanette Oliveros pursuant to Probate Code sections 3401 and 3402.

            The court orders petitioner Jeanette Oliveros to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
