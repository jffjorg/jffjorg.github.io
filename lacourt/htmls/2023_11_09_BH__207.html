DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV00991    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

November 9, 2023

CASE NUMBER

21SMCV00991

MOTIONS

Motions to Quash Third Party Subpoenas

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff 1059Corning, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Marmar Corning, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

            This lawsuit arises from a dispute between the owners of adjoining residential properties.  Plaintiff 1059Corning, LLC (“Plaintiff”) alleges that construction defects in Defendant Marmar Corning, LLC’s (“Defendant”) adjoining building caused damage to Plaintiff’s building. 

 

Prior to initiating litigation, Plaintiff hired third party consultants Soil Pacific, Inc. and PSFEG, Inc. to evaluate the damage.  Also prior to initiating litigation, Plaintiff sent Defendant copies of the consultant reports.  Defendants have sent third party subpoenas to both Soil Pacific and PSFEG, seeking their respective records pertaining to Plaintiff’s retention of each of them and records pertaining to their prelitigation analyses of the subject property.   

 

Plaintiff has moved to quash the subpoenas, on the basis that neither Soil Pacific nor PSFEG has been designated as an expert witness, and therefore, the requested documents are privileged.  Defendant has opposed the motions to quash and Plaintiff has replied.

 

PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

1.      Meet and Confer

 

            Defendant contends that Plaintiff did not sufficiently meet and confer prior to filing the Motions to Quash.  (Opp. at pp. 4-5.)  On September 6, 2023, Plaintiff sent Defendant’s counsel an email asking Defendant to withdraw its subpoenas by September 7, 2023, or else it would file a motion to quash.  (Coppola Decl. ¶ 11 and Ex. F thereto.)  Plaintiff’s stated basis in the email was that the third parties were prelitigation consultants that Plaintiff did not intend to designate as an expert or otherwise rely upon their opinions at trial.  (Ibid.) 

 

Consistent with the email, Plaintiff filed the instant motions on September 8, 2023.  (Coppola Decl. ¶ 13.)  The parties attended a status conference on September 13, 2023.  Subsequently, on September 22, 2023, Defendant sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter, asking for further information to support their position that the requested documents were privileged.  (Coppola Decl. ¶ 14 and Ex. H thereto.) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff and Defendant met and conferred over the phone on September 29, 2023.  (Coppola Decl. ¶ 15.) 

 

Plaintiff argues in reply that motions to quash third party subpoenas are not part of the Discovery Act, and therefore, there is no meet and confer requirement. 

 

Under the circumstances presented, the Court does not find that further meet and confer efforts would be fruitful.

 

2.      Separate Statement

 

            California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 requires that any motion involving the content of discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for why an order compelling further responses is warranted. 

 

            Here, Plaintiff has filed separate statements related to the motions to quash, which complies with Rule 3.1345.

 

ANALYSIS

 

If a subpoena requires the production of documents, the court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

 

            Plaintiff makes two arguments in support of the motions: (1) the documents Defendant seeks are all privileged because the third party companies have not been designated as expert witnesses; and (2) the information sought is not relevant because the third party companies are not percipient witnesses.

 

1.      Privilege

 

In support of its argument that the documents are privileged, Plaintiff relies on Shadow Traffic Network v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1067, 1079 (hereafter Shadow Traffic), citing Williamson v. Superior Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 829, 834-35.) 

 

In Shadow Traffic, the Court of Appeal held substantial evidence supported the trial court’s finding that plaintiff’s attorneys had imparted confidential information subject to protection from subsequent disclosure to an accounting firm that plaintiff ultimately did not select as an expert witness.

 

The court noted that confidential communications between an attorney and a prospective expert could potentially implicate the attorney-client privilege and the attorney work product doctrine.

 

With respect to attorney-client privilege, the court noted that communications between an attorney and a prospective expert witness would be protected if such disclosure is reasonably necessary to further the attorney’s representation of the client’s interest, but that “privilege is lost upon designation of the expert as a witness because the decision to use the expert as a witness manifests the client’s consent to disclosure of the information.”  (Shadow Traffic, supra, 24 Cal.App.4th at p. 1079.)  Moreover, to the extent an expert’s reports “embrace counsel’s impressions and conclusions, the work-product doctrine gives absolute protection to that information.”  (Ibid.)

 

            Here, the Court notes that while Plaintiff contends the documents are “privileged,” Plaintiff does not explicitly raise either the attorney-client privilege or the attorney work product doctrine.  In any event, neither the attorney-client privilege nor the attorney work product doctrine apply, because it was Plaintiff, not Plaintiff’s counsel, who hired both third party companies.  (Krol Reply Decl. ¶ 3.)  By contrast, in both Shadow Traffic and Williamson, counsel hired the consultants, not the client-party. 

 

 

            Nor has Plaintiff demonstrated that it disclosed any confidential information to the consultants.  Critical to the Shadow Traffic decision was a declaration that counsel “had explicitly told [the accounting firm] personnel that confidential information was being imparted to them and that said personnel acknowledged same.”  (Id. at 1082.)  “Metro’s counsel then extensively discussed Metro’s litigation and trial strategies, including an explanation of Metro’s potential damages theories” and the accounting firm’s personnel “fully participated in a discussion of these points, offering suggestions and comments on Metro’s damages strategy.”  (Ibid.)  By contrast, here, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that it disclosed confidential information to the third party companies.

 

            Moreover, Plaintiff has not demonstrated that it disclosed any confidential information to the consultants.  Critical to the Shadow Traffic decision was a declaration that counsel “had explicitly told [the accounting firm] personnel that confidential information was being imparted to them and that said personnel acknowledged same.”  (Shadow Traffic, supra, 24 Cal.App.4th at p. 1082.)  “Metro’s counsel then extensively discussed Metro’s litigation and trial strategies, including an explanation of Metro’s potential damages theories” and the accounting firm’s personnel “fully participated in a discussion of these points, offering suggestions and comments on Metro’s damages strategy.”  (Ibid.)  By contrast, here, Plaintiff has not provided any evidence that it disclosed confidential information to the third party consultants.

 

In the Reply brief, Plaintiff argued for the first time that it is not asserting the attorney-client privilege or the attorney work product doctrine.  Instead, Plaintiff contends that the requested documents and information are privileged under Code of Civ. Procedure section 411.35, subdivision (b), which applies whenever filing a negligence complaint against an engineer or similar professional.  Despite the fact that Plaintiff’s motions to quash cited only to case law involving the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine, and Plaintiff did not raise the issue of section 411.35 until the reply brief, in an abundance of caution, the Court continued the hearing and permitted the parties to provide additional briefing as to whether section 411.35 creates a privilege here.

 

Section 411.35 provides, in relevant part:

 

(a) In every action, including a cross-complaint for damages or indemnity, arising out of the professional negligence of a person holding a valid architect's certificate issued pursuant to Chapter 3 (commencing with Section 5500) of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code, or of a person holding a valid registration as a professional engineer issued pursuant to Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 6700) of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code, or a person holding a valid land surveyor's license issued pursuant to Chapter 15 (commencing with Section 8700) of Division 3 of the Business and Professions Code on or before the date of service of the complaint or cross-complaint on any defendant or cross-defendant, the attorney for the plaintiff or cross-complainant shall file and serve the certificate specified by subdivision (b).

 

(b) A certificate shall be executed by the attorney for the plaintiff or cross-complainant declaring one of the following:

 

(1) That the attorney has reviewed the facts of the case, that the attorney has consulted with and received an opinion from at least one architect, professional engineer, or land surveyor who is licensed to practice and practices in this state or any other state, or who teaches at an accredited college or university and is licensed to practice in this state or any other state, in the same discipline as the defendant or cross-defendant and who the attorney reasonably believes is knowledgeable in the relevant issues involved in the particular action, and that the attorney has concluded on the basis of this review and consultation that there is reasonable and meritorious cause for the filing of this action. The person consulted may not be a party to the litigation. The person consulted shall render his or her opinion that the named defendant or cross-defendant was negligent or was not negligent in the performance of the applicable professional services.

 

                                                […]

 

(e) For purposes of this section, and subject to Section 912 of the Evidence Code, an attorney who submits a certificate as required by paragraph (1) or (2) of subdivision (b) has a privilege to refuse to disclose the identity of the architect, professional engineer, or land surveyor consulted and the contents of the consultation. The privilege shall also be held by the architect, professional engineer, or land surveyor so consulted. . . .

 

“The purpose of the statute is to “discourag[e] frivolous professional negligence suits against registered civil engineers” by imposing a sanction on the noncomplying plaintiff or cross-complainant.”  (UDC-Universal Development, L.P. v. CH2M Hill (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 10, 28.)

 

Defendant argues in supplemental opposition that Plaintiff’s principals hired PSFEG and Soil Pacific to do analyses and prepare reports about the damage before Plaintiff’s counsel became involved.  These reports were also shared, in part, with Defendant prior to litigation.  Therefore, Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot use the fact that Plaintiff’s counsel subsequently relied upon these otherwise nonprivileged and discoverable reports in preparing the certificate of merit to shield the reports from discovery.  (Supplemental Opposition Brief at pp. 4-5.) 

 

Defendant points out that Section 411.35 protects only the identity of consultants and the contents of the consultation between attorney and consultant, not the non-privileged investigation reports generated long before any attorney consultation.  Moreover, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not identified whether counsel subsequently met with anyone from PSFEG and/or Soil Pacific or whether any new information was exchanged, and has therefore not demonstrated that there is any privileged information at issue.  Finally, Defendant contends that because the reports were shared in part with Defendant prior to the litigation, any privileged was waived in any event.

 

In supplemental reply, Plaintiff contends (1) the privilege conferred by Section 411.35 precludes Plaintiff from disclosing “what was disclosed during the consultation”; (2) because Defendant does not dispute that Plaintiff’s counsel consulted with PSFEG and Soil Pacific or that “the contents of that consultation are not privileged” the only way Defendant can defeat the motions to quash is by demonstrating the privilege was waived; but (3) it must be waived by both the attorney and the consultant, which it was not.[1]

 

Plaintiff, as the moving party, bears the burden to establish a privilege applies.  Plaintiff has not done so here.  Plaintiff vaguely references Section 411.35 as a reason why PSFEG and Soil Pacific should be precluded from having to produce any relevant documents or information, including reports regarding the property damage at issue, commissioned at the request of Plaintiff’s principals long before counsel got involved, which were also disclosed in part to Defendant before litigation commenced. 

 

It is unclear from the record whether Plaintiff’s counsel merely reviewed these pre-existing reports in doing the due diligence required to prepare the Section 411.35 certification, or whether counsel separately and subsequently consulted with PSFEG and/or Soil Pacific.  To the extent Plaintiff attempts to shield these pre-litigation reports commissioned by Plaintiff’s principals, on the basis that counsel may or may not have subsequently reviewed them in connection with the due diligence requirements of Section 411.35, such argument is unpersuasive. 

 

Plaintiff and its counsel may not shield otherwise discoverable documents and information by obliquely referencing Section 411.35.  Even if Plaintiff had concretely established that its counsel reviewed the reports as part of the Section 411.35 due diligence, Plaintiff’s counsel may not shield otherwise discoverable third-party documents merely by reviewing them.  Of course, Plaintiff’s counsel’s impressions and analyses of the documents would be protected by the attorney work product doctrine, but Plaintiff has indicated that no attorney-client or attorney work product privilege applies to the subpoenas at issue.  (See original Reply at p. 3, fn. 2.) 

 

To the extent Plaintiff’s counsel had a subsequent consultation with PSFEG and/or Soil Pacific, in furtherance of counsel’s duties under Section 411.35, such information would presumably be privileged under Section 411.35, as well as by the attorney work product doctrine, and also likely the attorney-client privilege, as communications that were reasonably necessary to further the attorney’s representation of the client’s interest.  But it is Plaintiff’s burden to establish that such a privilege exists.  Plaintiff has not provided evidence, or even argued, that Plaintiff’s counsel had any such subsequent consultation.[2]  Therefore, Plaintiff has not identified any documents or information that Defendants seek from PSFEG and/or Soil Pacific are privileged, by Section 411.35 or otherwise.[3]

 

As such, Plaintiff has not met its burden to establish that the requested documents are protected from disclosure.

 

2.      Relevance

 

Plaintiff next argues that the requests are not relevant because the third party consultants are not percipient witnesses and the requests are too broad.

 

In support of its second argument, Plaintiff cites to Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224 (hereafter Calcor).)  In Calcor, the requests were so broad, they amounted to a demand to “produce everything in its possession having anything to do with gun mounts.”  (Ibid.)  By contrast, here, Defendant seeks documents pertaining to the third parties’ retention and prelitigation examinations, and reports pertaining to the subject property.  Thus, the records would necessarily be relevant to the litigation.

 

Having found that Plaintiff has failed its burden to establish the requested documents are privileged or irrelevant, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motions to quash.

 

3.      Monetary Sanctions

Both parties seek monetary sanctions.  Because the Court denies Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash, Defendant acted with substantial justification in opposing it.  Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is denied.

 

Defendant requests $2,902.50 in monetary sanctions, representing 1.5 hours meeting and conferring with Plaintiff, 4 hours reviewing the Motions and drafting the Oppositions, 1.5 hours analyzing the reply and 3 hours attending the hearing, and 3.5 hours preparing the supplemental briefing, for a total of 13.5 hours at a rate of $215.00. 

 

Plaintiff argues, “Under the circumstances, the Motions to Quash were reasonably made, and the content of both subpoenas were oppressive.”  (Supp. Reply at p. 2.)  Because the Court rejects all of Plaintiff’s arguments made in the motion, on reply, and in the supplemental briefing, the Court finds that Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification in bringing the Motions to Quash.

 

Therefore, the Court awards Defendant monetary sanctions in the amount of $2042.50, representing 1.5 hours to meet and confer, 2 hours to analyze the motions and draft the Oppositions, 1 hour analyzing the reply, 3 hours to prepare the supplemental papers and 2 hours attending the hearings, for a total of 9.5 hours at the rate of $215.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Having found Plaintiff did not meet its burden to establish that the requested information is privileged or irrelevant, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motions to quash. 

 

The Court further grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $2042.50.  Plaintiff’s counsel[4] shall pay Defendant $2042.50 in monetary sanctions, by and through counsel for Defendant, on or before November 30, 2023.  

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

HEARING DATE

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01121

MOTIONS

Motions to be Relieved as Counsel

MOVING PARTIES

Ellyn S. Garofalo and Amir Kaltgrad

OPPOSING PARTY

(none)

 

MOTION

 

            Ellyn S. Garofalo and Amir Kaltgrad of Carlton Fields, LLP (“Counsel”), Counsel for Defendants Pali Cap Management 9; Palisades Capital Fund 4; Palisades Capital Fund 8; Palisades Capital Fund 7; Palisades Development Co.; Leslie Gornik; Palisades Capital Management LLC; Palisades Funding; and Patrick McKenna  move to be relieved as counsel for each Defendant. 

 

            No opposition has been filed, however, Jay R. Stein, Esq., counsel for Plaintiffs, has filed a declaration requesting that the Court impose conditions on the requests to be relieved as Defendants’ counsel.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 provides “[t]he attorney in an action or special proceeding may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination as follows: 1. Upon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered in the minutes; 2. Upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

 

Procedural Requirements

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, requires:

 

(1) the motion must be made on form MC-051; (subd. (a));

 

(2) it must be accompanied by a declaration on form MC-052 stating why the motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) instead of a consent brought under section 284(1); (subd. (c));

 

(3) a proposed order on form MC-053 must be lodged with the court, specifying all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known; (subd. (e)); and

 

(4) The documents must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. (subd. (d).)

 

If the notice is served by mail or electronic service, it must be accompanied by a declaration indicating that the address served is the current address, or in the case of service by mail, that it was served on the last known address and a more current address could not be located after reasonable efforts within 30 days before filing the motion.  (Ibid.)  The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Substantive Requirements

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(a) outlines the reasons a lawyer must withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client is bringing an action, conducting a defense, asserting a position in litigation, or taking an appeal, without probable cause and for the purpose of harassing or maliciously injuring any person;

 

(2)   the representation will result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the State Bar Act;

 

(3)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it unreasonably difficult to carry out the representation effectively; or

 

(4)   the client discharges the lawyer.

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(b) outlines the reasons a lawyer may withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client insists upon presenting a claim or defense in litigation, or asserting a position or making a demand in a non-litigation matter, that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law;

 

(2)   the client either seeks to pursue a criminal or fraudulent course of conduct or has used the lawyer’s services to advance a course of conduct that the lawyer reasonably believes was a crime or fraud;

 

(3)   the client insists that the lawyer pursue a course of conduct that is criminal or fraudulent;

 

(4)   the client by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(5)   the client breaches a material term of an agreement with, or obligation, to the lawyer relating to the representation, and the lawyer has given the client a reasonable warning after the breach that the lawyer will withdraw unless the client fulfills the agreement or performs the obligation;

 

(6)   the client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the representation;

 

(7)   the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal;

 

(8)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(9)   a continuation of the representation is likely to result in a violation of these rules or the State Bar Act; or

 

(10)           the lawyer believes in good faith in a proceeding pending before a tribunal that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Counsel have filed forms MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053 and proofs of service as to each defendant.  The attorney declarations (MC-052) indicate that the motions were filed instead of filing consents because of “a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship.” 

 

Rule 1.16(b)(4) permits withdrawal where “the client by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively[.]”  As such, the motions are substantively proper.

 

The proofs of service indicate that the clients were served by mail, and the other parties were served both electronically and by mail, in compliance with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d) [“The notice may be by personal service, electronic service, or mail”].  Paragraph 3(b) of the attorney declaration (MC-052) confirms that the clients were each served by mail at the last known address, as confirmed by telephone within the past 30 days, in conformance with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1)(A).  Therefore, the motions are procedurally proper.

 

Plaintiffs do not deny that the motions are procedurally and substantively proper.  However, Plaintiffs’ counsel has filed a declaration, indicating that certain discovery productions are still outstanding, and requests the Court place the following conditions on Defendants’ counsel’s withdrawal:

 

1.      Any and all Accountings and/or accounting related documents in the actual possession of the Defendants attorneys of the above properties identified in Paragraphs 2 and 3 from the time of acquisition, sale, and distribution of proceeds should be turned over to Plaintiffs prior to being relieved as counsel of record as those are not privileged documents. Carlton Fields is not required to produce any documents it does not have in its possession, however it must make that representation in writing to the Court prior to any order issued by the Court takes effect.

 

2.      Since Carlton Fields will no longer be representing Defendants, no IDC will be required prior to the Plaintiffs filing any Motion to Compel Further Documents and for Sanctions if in fact Defendants or their new counsel fail to provide the Accountings documents in their possession and control after relief is granted by this Court.

 

Plaintiffs have not provided the Court any authority for conditioning the withdrawal of counsel on the production of discovery, especially where, as here, there has been a breakdown in the relationship between Defendants and Counsel, and the terms of any such production by counsel are likely to be inherently fraught with conflict.  Moreover, the Courtroom rules for Department 207 require that the parties schedule an IDC prior to bringing any discovery motion.  The Court sees no reason to create an exemption from this requirement on the basis of Counsel’s withdrawal.  If anything, the Court finds an IDC would be particularly helpful, since new counsel may be involved.

 

Therefore, the Court declines to impose the requested conditions on Counsels’ withdrawals.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motions to be Relieved as Counsel.

 

Counsel must serve the signed orders (forms MC-053), which shall include information about all future hearings and proceedings noticed by any party, or ordered by the Court, on all Defendants and all other parties who have appeared in the action, within 10 days of the date of this Order, and file proofs of service of such.  Counsel will remain the attorneys of record for Defendants until Counsel files the requisite proofs of service.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

MOTION

MOVING PARTY

MOTION

 

This case stems from a dispute over the remodel of Plaintiff Glenn Vallecillos, M.D., Inc.’s (“Plaintiff”) surgical center.  Defendant Michael Keyes DBA Global Ocean Land Construction (“Defendant” or “Keyes”) demurs to the third and fourth causes of action of the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) for failure to state a cause of action under Code of Civil Procedure, section 430.10, subd. (e), as well as to the entire complaint for misjoinder of parties, pursuant to subdivision (d) and uncertainty pursuant to subdivision (f)[1].  Defendant also objects to the FAC as procedurally defective for adding new parties and causes of action without leave of court. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the demurrer and Defendant replies.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

            Defendant objects to the Declaration of David Glaubiger and the proposed Second Amended Complaint filed in opposition to the demurrer on the basis that it is improperly filed for the purpose of harassment, is frivolous, and wastes the Courts’ and the parties’ resources.   

 

            Extrinsic evidence is generally improper, and the Court will not consider the extrinsic evidence with respect to its determination of the demurrers.  However, to the extent Plaintiff has presented the Court with additional facts it could add to cure any deficiencies identified in the operative complaint, the Court may properly consider such additional facts in determining whether granting Plaintiff leave to amend is proper.

 

            Therefore, the Court overrules Defendant’s objections to the Glaubiger Declaration and proposed Second Amended Complaint, in accordance with these principles.

 

OBJECTION TO DEMURRER

 

            Plaintiff objects to Defendant’s demurrer for failure to distinctly specify the grounds upon which each objection to the complaint is based pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.60.  Defendant’s notice of demurrer indicates it is brought to the entire FAC on the grounds of uncertainty and misjoinder of parties, and the third and fourth cause of action for failure to state a cause of action.  Defendant’s notice also indicates Defendant objects to the FAC as procedurally improper for exceeding the scope of the Court’s leave.  To the extent Defendant’s briefing contains other arguments, the Court will disregard them as not properly noticed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations of the complaint “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule is that if one consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the cause of action shown, or although the plaintiff may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

A.    UNCERTAINTY

 

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that the responding party cannot reasonably respond - i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Where a demurrer is made upon the ground of uncertainty, the demurrer must distinctly specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain, and where such uncertainty appears by reference to page and line numbers.  (See Fenton v. Groveland Comm. Services Dist. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 797, 809.) 

 

Although Defendant characterizes the FAC as “confusing, inconsistent, and uncertain” as well as “baseless” and “shotgun blasts,” the Court finds the FAC clearly delineates among the parties, the role each party played, and the allegations being made and causes of action being brought as to each party.  Defendant does not demonstrate that any portions of the FAC are so bad that Defendant cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against Defendant.  The Court thus declines to sustain Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC on the basis of uncertainty. 

 

B.     FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

                                                                    i.            Third Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384, as modified on denial of reh'g (Mar. 18, 2009).)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendant appears to contest the truth of the allegations themselves.  (Demurrer at p. 6 [“Again, plaintiff falsely claims he hired defendant to do the remodel, when the facts prove differently.”])  But for purposes of a demurrer, the Court must accept as true all allegations in the operative complaint.

 

            Here, Plaintiff alleges the following:

 

     11.       In the Spring of 2021, plaintiff’s principals met with defendant Keyes to discuss hiring defendant Keyes to remodel a surgical center located on the Subject Property.  Prior to hiring defendant Keyes, defendant Keyes tendered the following representations:

 

                 1.         Defendant was a properly licensed contractor.

                 2.         Defendant had fully reviewed the remodel plans and specifications.

                 3.         Defendant was fully capable of and would make all repairs set forth in the

plans and specifications.

                 4.         Defendant and his employees/workers were covered by policy of worker’s

compensation insurance.

 

     12.       Based on defendant Keyes’ representations, plaintiff agreed to hire defendant Keyes to remodel the surgical center.  As a pre-condition thereto, defendant Keyes was required to have in place certain insurance coverage, i.e. (1) general commercial liability insurance and (2) worker’s compensation insurance.  As a further precondition, Defendant Keyes was required to add plaintiff and plaintiff’s landlord as additional insured’s on these policies.

 

     13.       Pursuant thereto, in order to show compliance with the above pre-conditions regarding worker’s compensation insurance, etc., plaintiff is informed and believes defendants Keyes, Pacific and Turner entered into an agreement whereby defendants Pacific and Turner would create false insurance documentation (stating that defendant Keyes was ‘in fact’ covered by a policy of worker’s compensation insurance).  Pursuant to this agreement, defendants Pacific and Turner “created” a Certificate of Insurance bearing the date 4-1-22.  This Certificate of Insurance identifies the insured as “Global Ocean and Land Construction” (defendant Keyes’ fictitious business name) and falsely states that defendant Keyes is insured under a policy of worker’s compensation insurance, Policy No. 9217479-2021.  This Certificate of Insurance also includes language that defendant Keyes maintained a general commercial liability policy of insurance and that plaintiff was added as an additional insured on this policy.  A true and correct copy of the Certificate of Insurance is attached hereto as Exhibit A and is incorporated herein by this reference.

 

14.            In light thereof, Plaintiff agreed to hire defendant Keyes to remodel the surgical center.  Plaintiff agreed to and made payment on defendant Keyes’ construction contract, as well as a series of change orders.[2]  True and correct copies of all such agreements are collectively attached hereto as Exhibit B and are incorporated herein by this reference. 

 

15.            In response to defendant Keyes' demands for payment that were directed to plaintiff, during the period of June 2021 through January 2022, plaintiff made payments to defendant Keyes totaling $258,020.00. All of these funds were paid by plaintiff within the three 11 years preceding the filing of this suit. Proof of such payment is attached hereto as Exhibit C.

 

16.            During the period of June 2021 through October 2021, defendant Keyes provided construction workers/employees and materials and commenced the remodel. Notwithstanding the cost of the construction and applicable building code, defendant Keyes failed to obtain any building permits, performed work without worker's compensation insurance for all workers/employees (discussed below), installed electrical wiring in a faulty manner, incorrectly installed drywall, among many other negligent acts and omissions. Further, defendant Keyes deviated from plans and specifications and sold defective products to plaintiff, e.g. defective nurse call station and defective vacuum pump.

 

17.            In December 2021, defendant abandoned the job prior to completion. As a result, plaintiff was forced to locate and hire alternate contractors to complete the construction. Hiring 22 alternate contractors to complete construction increased plaintiff’s construction costs by a 23 minimum of $1 00,000.00.

 

18. After hiring alternate contractors, plaintiff learned that much of defendant's construction was defective and required remediation/repair. Such remediation/repair costs were in excess of $100,000.00.

 

19. Shortly after defendant abandoned the construction, plaintiff discovered that defendant Keyes removed materials from the job site without plaintiff’s consent or approval, e.g.  2 scrub sinks and associated plumbing, etc. Despite demand for return of such items, defendant Keyes refused and failed to return these items.

 

20. Recently, plaintiff learned that defendant Keyes has not had a policy of worker's compensation insurance since July 2003. A true and correct copy of defendant's worker's compensation history as registered with the CSLB is attached hereto as Exhibit D and is incorporated by herein by this reference. 

 

21. In August 2022, after plaintiff had paid defendant Keyes upwards of $250,000, defendants Pacific and Turner provided plaintiff with a revised Certificate of Insurance. The revised Certificate of Insurance removed all statements regarding coverage for worker's compensation insurance, at any time during the course of the construction. A true and correct copy of the revised Insurance Certificate is attached hereto as Exhibit E and is incorporated by herein by this reference.

 

                 […]

 

31.            Defendant Keyes intentionally failed to disclose to Plaintiff, among other things, 21 the following material facts:

 

A.             Neither Defendant Keyes, nor his employees/workers were covered under 23 a policy of worker's compensation insurance at any time during the course 24 of the above-described construction.

 

B. The portion of the provided Certificate of Insurance representing to plaintiff that defendant Keyes was covered by a policy of worker's compensation insurance was false.

 

32.            Further, defendant Keyes intentionally tendered to plaintiff the following false statements:

 

A.    Defendant and his workers were covered by a Policy of worker's compensation insurance that was active during the period of September 2020 through September 2021.

 

33.            The information described above was material to any reasonable person's decision to take the actions herein alleged, e.g. agree to retain Keyes to remodel the surgical center and make substantial payments to defendant Keyes.

 

34.            Defendants Keyes intentionally did not advise Plaintiff of the truth of the above described material facts.

 

35.            Defendants Keyes tendered the above identified false statements knowing they were false and with the intent to induce plaintiffs reliance thereon, e.g. agree to retain Keyes to remodel of the surgical center, make payments to Keyes, etc. Knowing the true fact that he did not carry worker's compensation insurance, defendant Keyes failed to disclose the above stated material facts with the intent to lull plaintiff into the false belief that defendant Keyes had a policy of worker's compensation insurance that provided coverage for Keyes and his workers/employees during the course of construction.

 

36.            Plaintiff, in reasonable reliance thereon, took the actions herein alleged.

 

37.            At all times pertinent hereto, the tendered representations were in fact false and were known to be false by defendant Keyes. Plaintiff was ignorant of the falsity of these representations. Had Plaintiff known the truth, Plaintiff would not have taken the actions herein alleged, e.g. entered into the agreements and change orders referenced herein, made payment thereon, etc.

 

38.            As a consequence of the above described material omissions and misrepresentations, Plaintiff has been generally damaged in a sum to be proven at the time of trial, but in excess of $250,000, plus interest, costs, attorney's fees (Bus and Prof Code §7160), and expenses.

 

            Thus, Plaintiff has alleged all the elements of intentional misrepresentation with requisite specificity.

 

                                                                  ii.            Fourth Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

“The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.”  (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231 (hereafter Chapman).)  Like intentional misrepresentation, causes of action for negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud, and must also, therefore, be pleaded with particularity.  (Ibid.) 

 

As to the Fourth Cause of Action, Plaintiff additionally alleges:

 

40.            At the times Defendant Keyes tendered the above-described misrepresentations defendant Keyes had no reasonable factual basis for believing them to be true.

 

41.            Defendant Keyes tendered the above described representations to plaintiff with the intent to induce plaintiff's reliance thereon, i.e. agreement to hire Keyes to remodel of the surgical center, make payment to defendant Keyes, etc. Defendants Keyes' representations lulled plaintiff into the false belief that Keyes and his workers/employees were covered by a worker's compensation insurance policy during the entire course of construction and that the provided insurance certificate purporting to provide worker's compensation insurance coverage contained accurate information - which it did not.

 

42.            Plaintiff, in reasonable reliance thereon, took the actions herein alleged.

 

43.            At all times pertinent hereto, Plaintiff was ignorant of the falsity of the representations described above, and believing them to be true, reasonably relied thereon in doing the things herein alleged.

 

44.            As a result thereof, Plaintiff has been damaged in an amount to be proven at the 19 time of trial, but in excess of $250,000.00.

 

Thus, Plaintiff alleges that at the time Keyes misrepresented that he had insurance, he either knew that he did not have worker’s compensation insurance, or had no reasonable basis to think that he did.  As such, Plaintiff has pleaded a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation with sufficient particularity.

 

C.    MISJOINDER OF PARTIES/PROCEDURAL DEFECTS

 

Anyone who has a “claim, right, or interest adverse to them in the property or controversy which is the subject of the action” may be joined as defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 379, subd. (a)(2).) A demurrer for misjoinder will lie only where it appears from the face of the complaint or matters judicially noticeable that either plaintiffs lack sufficient unity of interest (Code Civ. Proc., § 378) or there is no common question of law or fact as to the defendants (Code Civ. Proc., § 379). (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 7:78 et seq., p. 7(I)-42 et seq.)

 

Defendant’s misjoinder and procedural defect arguments are the same – that “plaintiff has unlawfully added causes of action and additional parties without the required leave of court, also lacking service of a new summons.”  (Demurrer at pp. 7-8.)  However, Defendant does not specifically identify which additional parties or causes of action were added without Court authorization, or how or why this constitutes misjoinder of parties. 

 

Notwithstanding, a review of the record demonstrates that on April 13, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the intentional and negligent misrepresentation causes of with leave to amend.  (See Minute Order of April 13, 2023.)  Yet, to the extent that Defendant claims that Plaintiff’s FAC was not drawn in conformance with the Court’s April 13, 2023 order, Defendant should have filed a motion to strike as opposed to a demurrer which is procedurally improper in this context.  (See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 7:174, p. 7(I)-78 [“CCP § 436(b) is commonly invoked to challenge pleadings filed in violation of a deadline or court order or requirement of prior leave of court. [Ferraro v. Camarlinghi, supra, 161 CA4th at 528, 75 CR3d at 34] . . . Amended complaint alleging facts beyond permissible scope of amendment”].)   

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Defendant’s Demurrer in its entirety and orders Defendant to file and serve an Answer to the FAC on or before November 30, 2023.

 

Defendant shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.
