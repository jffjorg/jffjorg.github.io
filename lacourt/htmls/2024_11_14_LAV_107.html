DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01576     Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Hamid Kohan Darvish,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

                       

Khelika Bell, et al.

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV01576                   107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion to Dismiss

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:  Denied.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On November 6, 2022, Hamid Kohan Darvish was crossing the street when he was struck by a vehicle driven by Defendant Khelika Bell. (Complaint p. 5.)  Hamid Kohan Darvish sustained injuries ultimately leading to his death. (First Amended Complaint, “FAC” ¶ 15.) On April 11, 2023, Hamid Kohan Darvish filed a complaint against Defendants Khelika Bell, El Camino Real Charter High School, Friends of ECR, a Nonprofit Public Benefit Corporation, the City of Los Angeles, and Does 1 to 20 (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

On May 23, 2024, the Estate of Hamid Kohan Darvish and Sara Kohan Darvish (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a first amended against Defendants.

 

On August 1, 2024, Defendant City of Los Angeles filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings because Plaintiffs failed to comply with the claims presentation requirement of the California Tort Claims Act. (Notice of Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings p. 2.)

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion for leave to file a second amended complaint.

 

On August 29, 2024, this court denied Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to file a second amended complaint.

 

On September 12, 2024, this court granted the motion for judgment on the pleadings and granted Plaintiffs thirty days to amend. (09/12/24 Minute Order.)

 

On October 29, 2024, Defendant Friends of ECR, a Nonprofit Public Benefit Corporation (“Friends of ECR”) filed an ex parte application for order of dismissal because Plaintiffs failed to amend the complaint within thirty days’ notice of the court’s order (the “Motion”). (Ex Parte Application p. 5.)  That same day, Plaintiffs filed a request for dismissal of El Camino Real Charter High School and the City of Los Angeles. (Request for Dismissal filed 10/29/24.) The City of Los Angeles and El Camino Real Charter High School were subsequently dismissed from this action. (Request for Dismissal filed 10/29/24.)

 

On October 30, 2024, this court held a hearing on Friends of ECR’s ex parte application and advanced the hearing to November 14, 2024. (10/30/24 Minute Order.)

 

On November 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. Friend’s of ECR filed a reply on November 8, 2024.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same purpose and effect as a general demurrer. (Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586.) As such, the same rules that apply to a general demurrer apply to a judgment on the pleadings. (Id.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 581, subdivision (f), provides that “[t]he court may dismiss the complaint as to that defendant when . . . [e]xcept where Section 597 applies, after a demurrer to the complaint is sustained with leave to amend, the plaintiff fails to amend it within the time allowed by the court and either party moves for dismissal.” Dismissal is discretionary. (Contreras v. Blue Cross of California (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 945, 948.) 

 

III.           THE MOTION, OPPOSITION, AND REPLY

 

Friends of ECR argues this court should dismiss Plaintiffs’ complaint because they failed to amend the complaint within thirty days’ notice of this court’s order granting the City of Los Angeles’ motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Memorandum p. 5.) Friends of ECR states that Plaintiffs were present when the court granted the motion for judgment on the pleadings, but the court served a copy of the Minute Order to all parties via USPS mail on September 14, 2024. (Memorandum p. 5.) Thus, Plaintiffs’ amended complaint was due by October 15, 2024, or October 17, 2024, at the latest. (Memorandum p. 5.) Plaintiffs failed to file or serve an amended complaint. (Memorandum p. 5.) Friends of ECR also notes that “[t]his is the second time Defendant has filed an ex parte application for an order of dismissal due to plaintiff’s failure to amend the complaint as ordered by the court [and p]reviously, plaintiff failed to communicate with Defendant regarding the delay and did not amend the complaint after the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer for fifty-one (51) days[.]” (Memorandum p. 3.)

 

Plaintiffs state that the City of Los Angeles filed the motion for judgment on the pleadings and, “upon extensive discussions” Plaintiffs agreed to dismiss the City of Los Angeles and El Camino Real Charter High School. (Opposition p. 5.) Plaintiffs state that Friends of ECR did not join the City of Los Angeles’ motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Opposition p. 6.) Plaintiffs argue that, because Friends of ECR did not join the City of Los Angeles’ motion for judgment on the pleadings, and the City of Los Angeles and El Camino Real Charter High School were subsequently dismissed, “Plaintiff would not have to amend the Complaint as . . . the other Defendants had already filed an Answer to Plaintiff’s complaint.” (Opposition p. 5.)

 

Plaintiffs further argue that Friends of ECR cannot request dismissal of this cause of action because Friends of ECR did not move for judgment on the pleadings. (Opposition p. 7.) Plaintiffs cite the language in Code of Civil Procedure section 438, subdivision (h)(4)(c), which states: “ Except where subparagraphs (A) and (B) apply, if the motion is granted with respect to the entire complaint or answer with leave to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be, but an amended complaint or answer is not filed, then after the time to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be, has expired, judgment shall be entered forthwith in favor of the moving party.” Plaintiffs also cite Code of Civil Procedure section 438, subdivision (i)(1)(B), which states “If no amended pleading is filed, then the party shall move for entry of judgment in its favor.”

 

Plaintiffs argue that “[a]ccording to the above code section, only the moving party is entitled to a Judgment” and “[n]o other Defendant is entitled to a Judgment as the basis of the Judgment does not apply to any other Defendant.” (Opposition p. 7.) Plaintiffs state that the Friends of ECR’s answer did not allege Plaintiffs failed to file a statutory claim with Friends of ECR. (Opposition p. 7.) And “the Complaint cannot be dismissed unless the Motion to dismiss is filed by the party filing for the Judgment on the Pleading which is the City of Los Angeles and not Defendant Friends of ECR.” (Opposition p. 7.)

 

In its reply, Friends of ECR argues this court should dismiss the action under Code of Civil Procedure section 583.410, which provides that “The court may in its discretion dismiss an action for delay in prosecution pursuant to this article on its own motion or on motion of the defendant if to do so appears to the court appropriate under the circumstances of the case.” (Reply pp. 3-4.) Although Friends of ECR argued in its Motion that Plaintiff has repeatedly delayed the litigation, Friends of ECR did not specifically argue in its Motion that the court should dismiss the action under section 583.410 because Plaintiff failed to prosecute the case.  (Memorandum p. 6.) “The salutory rule is that points raised in a reply brief for the first time will not be considered unless good cause is shown for the failure to present them before.” (Balboa Ins. Co. v. Aguirre (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 1002, 1010.)  Accordingly, this court does not consider Friends of ECR’s argument it should dismiss the action under section 583.410.

 

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 581, subdivision (f), grants the trial court authority to “dismiss

the complaint as to that defendant when . . . [e]xcept where Section 597 applies, after a demurrer to the complaint is sustained with leave to amend, the plaintiff fails to amend it within the time allowed by the court and either party moves for dismissal.” (Emphasis added.)

 

This section, along with Code of Civil Procedure section 438 that is cited by Plaintiffs above,

appear to only give this court authority to dismiss the complaint and enter judgment in favor of

the defendant who moved for judgment on the pleadings.

 

Moreover, even if this court has authority to dismiss the action under Code of Civil Procedure

section 581, subdivision (f), dismissal is discretionary. (See Contreras v. Blue Cross of

California, supra, 199 Cal.App.3d at p. 948.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel submits a declaration stating that, after this court granted the City of Los

Angeles’ motion for judgment on the pleadings, Plaintiffs’ counsel contacted the City of Los

Angeles’ and El Camino Real Charter High School’s counsel and later agreed to dismiss those

Defendants from the lawsuit. (Nehoray Declaration ¶¶ 2-4.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states that, while

they were waiting to receive authorization to dismiss the City of Los Angeles and El Camino Real

Charter High School, Friends of ECR filed this Motion. (Nehoray Declaration ¶ 6.) Further, 

Plaintiffs’ counsel appears to believe it did not need to file an amended complaint because the

motion for judgment on the pleadings only pertained to the City of Los Angeles and El Camino

Real, and Plaintiffs’ counsel was negotiating dismissing those defendants. (Nehoray Declaration

¶¶ 2-7; Opposition p. 5.)

 

Accordingly, this court exercises its discretion and denies Friends of ECR’s Motion.

 

V.              CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Friends of ECR’s Motion is denied

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

Defendant Marc. M. Asheghian individually and as trustee of the Marc. M. Asheghian Declaration of Trust dated August 18, 2004 (“Defendant”) demurs to Plaintiff Daniel Zuckerman’s first amended complaint.

 

This court overrules the demurrer.

 

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

Plaintiff owns real property located at 3437 Castlewoods Place, Sherman Oaks, California (the “Castlewoods Property”). (Complaint ¶ 1.) Defendant owns real property located at 3430 Alana Dr., Sherman Oaks, California (the “Alana Property”). The two properties are adjoining. (Complaint ¶ 1.) 

 

Sometime between October 2012 and December 2013, Defendant constructed a deck on the Alana Property. (Complaint ¶ 3.) Plaintiff acquired the Castlewoods Property in 2016. (Complaint ¶ 5.) In or around June 2023, Plaintiff removed foliage from the Castlewoods Property to extend the height of the fence between the properties. (Complaint ¶ 6.) Plaintiff discovered the encroaching portion of Defendant’s deck and discovered Defendant was in the process of expanding the deck. (Complaint ¶ 6.)

 

On February 16, 2024, Plaintiff, as an individual and as trustee of the Daniel L. Zuckerman Living Trust Dated April 8, 2013, filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 25, alleging causes of action for quiet title, nuisance, trespass, declaratory relief, and injunctive relief.

 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. (06/20/24 Minute Order.)

 

On August 13, 2024, Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint. On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

 

II.             JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of a grant deed for the Castlewoods Property, recorded with the Office of the Los Angeles County on September 4, 1963. (Ex. A.)

 

“A recorded deed is an official act of the executive branch, of which this court may take judicial notice.” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 194.)

 

Accordingly, this court grants Defendant’s request and takes judicial notice of the grant deed.

 

III.           PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

  1. Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40(a).) When service is made electronically, “[a]ny period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response” is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc, § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

 

Plaintiff served Defendant with the first amended complaint electronically on June 6, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 07/30/24.) On July 17, 2024, Defendant filed a declaration of demurring party in support of automatic extension, which extended Defendant’s deadline to file the demurrer by thirty days. Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint on August 13, 2024.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is timely.

  1. Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating they met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel before filing the demurrer, but “Plaintiff’s counsel determined that they would not be filing an amended pleading.” (Solberg Declaration ¶ 3.)

 

This court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

IV.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).) A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

 

V.              DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s causes of action are time-barred. (Demurrer at p. 8.) Defendant further argues that if Plaintiff’s causes of action are based on the alleged 2023 discovery of the expansion, they are unclear. (Demurrer at p. 10.) Finally, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s “attempt to plead around the statute of limitations problem has caused him to submit sham pleadings.” (Demurrer at p. 10.)

 

  1. Statute of Limitations

 

Plaintiff’s first amended complaint alleges causes of action for quiet title, nuisance, trespass, declaratory relief, and injunctive relief.

 

“The legislature has not established a specific statute of limitations for actions to quiet title.” (Salazar v. Thomas (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 467, 476.) “[C]ourts refer to the underlying theory of relief to determine the applicable period of limitations” which requires “the court to identify the nature (i.e., the ‘gravamen’) of the cause of action.” (Id.) However, “[a]lthough the applicable limitations period is determined by looking at the gravamen of the quiet title cause of action, the general principles about when that limitations period commences do not necessarily apply because quiet title actions have special rules for when the limitations period begins to run.” (Id. at p. 477.) “[A]s a general rule, the statute of limitations [for a quiet title action] does not run against one in possession of land.” (Id.)

 

Actions for trespass upon or injury to real property are governed by a three-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338(b).)  The “’discovery rule’ . . . postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.” (Ventura29 LLC v. City of San Buenaventura (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 1028, 1044.) “A plaintiff has reason to discover a cause of action when he or she ‘has reason at least to suspect a factual basis for its elements.” (Id.) “Under the discovery rule, suspicion of one or more of the elements of a cause of action, coupled with knowledge of any remaining elements, will generally trigger the statute of limitations period.” (Id.)

 

“A plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his or her claim would be barred by the applicable orthodox statute of limitations, and who intends to rely on the discovery rule to toll the orthodox limitation period, ‘must specifically plead facts which show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence. [Citations.] Mere conclusory assertions that delay in discovery was reasonable are insufficient and will not enable the complaint to withstand general demurrer. [Citation.]’ [Citations.] Arguments that discovery-rule issues are necessarily factual and cannot be resolved on demurrer have been rejected.” (Ventura29 LLC v. City of San Buenaventura, supra, 87 Cal.App.5th at p. 1044.)

“The duration of the limitations period applicable to a declaratory relief action is determined by the nature of the underlying obligation sought to be adjudicated. [Citation] Thus, a declaratory judgment action . . . to enforce a statutory liability must be brought within the same . . . period after accrual of the cause of action . . . as an action for damages or injunction on the same liability.” (Snyder v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1196, 1208.)

“A demurrer based on a statute of limitations will not lie where the action may be, but is not necessarily, barred. [Citation] In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.” (Snyder v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn., supra, 229 Cal.App.4th at p. 1208.)

  1. Application

Plaintiff alleges he acquired the Castlewoods Property in 2016. (Complaint ¶ 4.) Plaintiff further alleges that, “[b]ased on publicly available satellite imagery reviewed after this dispute arose, Plaintiff is informed and believes, and based thereon alleges, that Defendant constructed a deck on the Alana Property sometime between October 2012 and December 2013.” (Complaint ¶¶ 3, 5.) Because Plaintiff alleges the standard statute of limitations began sometime between October 2012 and December 2013, Plaintiff must allege facts showing the discovery rule postponed the accrual of his causes of action. (Ventura29 LLC v. City of San Buenaventura, supra, 87 Cal.App.5th at p. 1044.) Specifically, Plaintiff must allege (1) “the time and manner of discovery” and (2) “the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.” (Id.)

Plaintiff alleges he “discovered that Defendant was in the process of expanding the existing deck on the Alana Property”, and “discovered the encroaching portion of the deck” “[i]n or around June 2023” while removing overgrown foliage to extend the height of the original fence between the properties. (Complaint ¶¶ 6, 18.) Thus, Plaintiff has alleged the time and manner of discovery.

Plaintiff also alleges that “[b]ecause of the existing foliage, at the time the deck appears to have been constructed on the Alana Property, Plaintiff had no way of knowing whether, or even if, the deck extended onto the Castlewoods Property.” (Complaint ¶ 5.)  Thus, Plaintiff has alleged he was unable to make an earlier discovery.

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s “cannot and does not plausibly allege that a ‘reasonable investigation’ would not have led him to discover this large ‘elevated’ deck in his backyard.” (Reply p. 2.)  “Once belated discovery is pleaded, the issue of whether plaintiff exercised reasonable diligence in discovering the negligent cause of the injury is a question of fact.” (Bastian v. County of San Luis Obispo (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 520, 527.) “When a plaintiff reasonably should have discovered facts for purposes of the accrual of a case of action or application of the delayed discovery rule is generally a question of fact, properly decided as a matter of law only if the evidence (or, in this case, the allegations in the complaint and facts properly subject to judicial notice) can support only one reasonable conclusion.” (Broberg v. The Guardian Life Ins. Co. of America (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 912, 921.)

Plaintiff alleged he did not discover that the deck extended onto the Castlewoods Property until June 2023, when he removed the overgrown foliage. (Complaint ¶¶ 5-6.) Accordingly, Plaintiff has alleged belated discovery, and this court cannot, as a matter of law, determine that Plaintiff’s failure to discover the deck until that time was unreasonable.

Defendant further argues Plaintiff’s causes of action are time-barred because Plaintiff “admits in his FAC that a portion of the deck was erected sometime between October 2013 and December 2013.” (Demurrer p. 9.) However, Plaintiff’s first amended complaint clearly alleges that he believes the deck was constructed sometime between October 2012 and December 2013 because he reviewed satellite imagery “after this dispute arose.” (Complaint ¶ 3 [Emphasis added].) Accordingly, although Plaintiff admits the deck was constructed in 2012 or 2013, Plaintiff does not allege he discovered the deck encroachment in 2012 or 2013.

Plaintiff filed his original complaint on February 16, 2024. February 16, 2024, is within three years from June 2023. (Complaint ¶¶ 6, 18.) Thus, Plaintiff’s causes of action are not barred by the statute of limitations.

  1. Unclear

Defendant argues that “[o]nly the causes of action that are based on the alleged deck expansion in 2023 are not time barred by the three-year statute of limitations” and because the first amended complaint “fails to distinguish between the encroachment caused by the original deck versus any encroachment by the expansion of the original deck” Defendant is left “to guess what part of the deck is actually at issue in this action.” (Demurrer p. 10.)

 

This court has found that Plaintiff’s causes of action based on the encroachment of the original deck are not time barred. Accordingly, Defendant’s argument is meritless.

 

  1. Sham Pleadings

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s “attempt to plead around a statute of limitations problem has caused him to submit sham pleadings.” (Demurrer p. 10.) Defendant states that in Plaintiff’s original complaint, he alleged that “In or around October 2023, Plaintiff removed the overgrown foliage to permit the construction of a new fence between the properties” and discovered the deck. (Demurrer p. 10.) But in Plaintiff’s first amended complaint, Plaintiff alleges he discovered the deck in June 2023. (Demurrer p. 10.) Defendant also argues that the survey attached to Plaintiff’s complaint was dated “August 27, 2022, almost a year before October 2023, the date [Plaintiff] now says he first discovered the encroachment by the deck.” (Demurrer p. 10.) Defendant states that “[t]hese inconsistent and arbitrary dates for discovery of the encroachment render all of [Plaintiff’s] claims subject to the sham pleading doctrine.” (Demurrer pp. 10-11.)

 

In his opposition, Plaintiff states the original complaint’s allegation he discovered the deck in October 2023 was a “scrivener’s error in the original complaint and the four months has no bearing on the timeliness of the claims.” (Opposition p. 8.) Plaintiff also states that the survey attached to the first amended complaint is dated July 17, 2023, and Defendant’s “argument is based on his interpretation of the file path at the bottom of the survey, which only indicates the location of the survey on that user’s computer.” (Opposition p. 7.) Plaintiff argues that Defendant “assumes, with no factual basis whatsoever, that ‘1_LS_Survey-Projects-08-27-2022’ in the file path location must refer to the actual date of the survey.” (Opposition p. 8.) Finally, Plaintiff states that Defendant “completely ignores the fact that the same path includes 07-15-2023 (the indicated date of the field survey was completed) and 7/17/2023 8:11 PM (the date the survey was signed).” (Opposition p. 8.)

 

“Under the sham-pleading doctrine, admissions in an original complaint that has been superseded by an amended pleading remain within the court’s cognizance and the alteration of such statements by amendment designed to conceal fundamental vulnerabilities in a plaintiff’s case will not be accepted.” (Lockton v. O’Rourke (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1051, 1061.) “[I]f a party files an amended pleading and attempts to avoid defects of original complaint by either omitting facts that rendered prior complaint defective or adding facts inconsistent with prior allegations, [the] court may take judicial notice of prior pleadings and disregard inconsistent allegations or read into amended complaint the allegations of the superseded complaint.” (Id.)

 

This court agrees that whether the deck expansion was discovered in June 2023 or October 2023 has no bearing on the relevant statute of limitations. Plaintiff’s original complaint was filed on February 16, 2024, which is within the three-year of limitations, regardless of whether the time began to accrue in June 2023 or October 2023. Further, Plaintiff stated that the inconsistent dates were due to a scrivener’s error. (Opposition p. 8.) This court finds that Plaintiff’s explanation is persuasive.

 

Further, Plaintiff’s first amended complaint alleges he commissioned a survey in July 2023. (Complaint ¶ 22.) The survey attached to the first amended complaint is signed and dated July 17, 2023. (Ex. 1.) Accordingly, Plaintiff alleges the survey was created in July 2023.  Based on the face of the pleading, this court finds no basis to conclude the file name shows the survey was created in August 2022. Moreover, this court agrees with Plaintiff that any dispute over when this survey was created is better suited for discovery.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court overrules Defendant’s demurrer.
