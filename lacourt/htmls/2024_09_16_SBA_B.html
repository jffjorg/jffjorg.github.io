DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV01305    Hearing Date: September 16, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

DIANA ALEXANDER,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV01305

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

WONDERGREEN, INC. DBA GREEN FARM MARKET GARDENA,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          September 16, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Diana Alexander

Responding Party:                  Defendant Wondergreen, Inc. dba Green Farm Market Gardena

(1)   Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint

(2)   Motion to Reopen Discovery

 

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

The motion for leave to file a FAC is DENIED.

The motion to reopen discovery is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On November 21, 2022, plaintiff Diana Alexander filed a complaint against defendant Wondergreen, Inc. dba Green Farm Market Gardena for negligence – premises liability.

            On February 24, 2023, defendant filed an answer.

            On March 11, 2024, the parties stipulated and the Court entered an order to continue trial from March 27, 2024 to July 15, 2025.

            On July 5, 2024, the parties stipulated and Court entered an order to continue trial to October 14, 2024.  The stipulation and order do not include a discovery extension.

            Trial is set for October 14, 2024

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Amend

CCP §473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal. App. 3d 1045, 1047.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are:  (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal. App. 3d 486, 490.  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal. App. 4th 471, 486-488.

Reopen discovery

CCP §2024.050 states: “(a) On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set.  This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.  

            (b) In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following:  (1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery.  (2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.  (3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party.  (4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action. 

            (c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to extend or to reopen discovery, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

DISCUSSION

            Amend

Plaintiff requests leave to amend the complaint to add a claim for punitive damages.

The complaint alleges that on December 1, 2020, plaintiff was lawfully on the premises known to the public as Green Farm Market Gardena, when she was walking with due care through a common area designated for pedestrian foot traffic to meet her husband in the fish market, her left foot got caught on a lifted part of a black carpet/mat, which was slippery and wet with fish-smelling water, causing her to slip, trip, and fall onto her knees and brace herself with her hands, resulting in serious injuries to her knee and wrist, as well as other injuries to plaintiff’s person.  Complaint, ¶8.  There were no customer signs or other notifications advising customers that the black carpet/mat was slippery or wet or that any hazardous or dangerous conditions existed at or around the location that plaintiff fell.  At no point prior to plaintiff’s fall did she observe the lifted portion of the black carpet/mat or that it was slippery and wet.  Id., ¶9.

Plaintiff argues that defendant has intentionally allowed the condition of the floors within its store to deteriorate resulting in significant hazards that defendant is well aware are certain to result in slip, trip, and falls incidents with harm to others.  Plaintiff asserts that the condition of the flooring in defendant’s store is not the result of mere negligence.  Rather, plaintiff contends, defendant has known that its floors are hazardous but has taken no measures to remedy or reduce the hazards.  Plaintiff asserts that defendant’s director of operations testified that he had been deposed in at least two other cases related to slip, trip, and fall incidents within the store; an employee testified that she has completed at least ten incident reports; and defendant has been sued at least three other times in the past ten years for slip, trip, and fall injuries.  Plaintiff also contends that a few months prior to plaintiff’s fall, defendant had been ordered by the County to discontinue operation at the facility “at once” due to unsafe flooring.  Defendant’s deponents testified that either they would fix the rolled-up corners of the mats or saw mats with the corners rolled-up.  Further, they testified as to plastic produce bags on the ground.  Plaintiff also argues that there is evidence of defendant’s lack of concern for safety because there was no safety training or any written policies and procedures for inspecting rugs/runners.  Plaintiff further contends that there was a failure to place caution cones and that plaintiff found multiple hazardous conditions on the floor at the site inspection on August 13, 2024.

In opposition, defendant argues that the motion is procedurally and substantively defective.  Defendant points out that the motion was not served in compliance with the statutory service requirements under CCP §§1005(b) and 1010.6 as the motion was served electronically on August 23 for a hearing date of September 16; thus, the motion should have been served no later than August 20.  Further, defendant asserts, counsel’s declaration failed to comply with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(b) by omitting the mandatory attestations.  Defendant also argues that an amendment would be futile because punitive damages are not recoverable in connection with plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence and that plaintiff’s proposed allegations do not support any malicious conduct by defendant.

 

 

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that plaintiff has not complied with CRC Rule 3.1324(b) as there is no declaration from plaintiff’s counsel stating (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  Further, plaintiff has been dilatory.  Although the case was filed in November 2022, written discovery was not conducted until early in 2024, the depositions were taken in April 2024, and the store inspection was not conducted until August 2024.  Trial is set for October 14, 2024.

Moreover, amendment would be futile.  Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code §3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud.  Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.  The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient.  Id.   

The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”   “’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1). As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

“[I]t is not enough that the defendant has acted with an intent which is tortious or even criminal, or that he has intended to inflict emotional distress, or even that his conduct has been characterized by ‘malice,’ or a degree of aggravation which would entitle the plaintiff to punitive damages for another tort.”  Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal. App. 4th 488, 496.  “Liability has been found only where the conduct has been so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community.”  Id. 

“Inasmuch as Civil Code section 3294 requires as a prerequisite to the recovery of punitive damages that the defendant ‘has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,’ the cases have uniformly recognized that proof of negligence, even gross negligence, or recklessness is insufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.”  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87 (citations omitted).

Thus, punitive damages are not recoverable under plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence.  Also, plaintiff’s proposed allegations do not rise to the level of malice.

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.

 

Reopen discovery

Plaintiff requests that the Court reopen discovery to obtain discovery of a surveillance video from plaintiff’s inspection on August 13, 2024 and if her motion for leave to amend to add punitive damages is granted, to also conduct discovery as to the condition of defendant’s flooring prior to the subject fall, defendant’s safety policies and procedures, and conduct taken by defendant to make the condition of its floors safer.

Plaintiff contends that new facts have recently been discovered by plaintiff during a site inspection of defendant’s store on August 13, 2024 that are “directly relevant and highly probative to” plaintiff’s alleged injuries.  Plaintiff asserts that defendant disputes that there was a fold in the mat that caused plaintiff to trip (on December 1, 2020) and that during the site inspection (on August 13, 2024, over three and a half years later) plaintiff’s counsel observed two customers trip over a hazardous mat in the same area that plaintiff previously fell; thus, plaintiff seeks the security video of the subject area and the customers tripping.  Plaintiff acknowledges that if the Court grants leave to allege punitive damages and discovery is reopened, trial would have to be continued and suggests a continuance to October 2025.

In opposition, defendant contends that the motion was not filed with statutory notice.  Defendant argues that plaintiff has not shown good cause to reopen discovery or for discovery of the surveillance video of other customers who allegedly tripped (but did not fall or were injured) on a different floor mat and almost four years after the alleged incident.  Defendant argues that such evidence is entirely irrelevant and inadmissible at trial.  Defendant also requests sanctions in the amount of $1330 under CCP §2024.050.

The Court rules as follows:  Plaintiff has not met her burden of showing good cause.  In considering the factors under CCP §2024.050(b), plaintiff has not shown the necessity and reason for the discovery of the video on the date of the inspection.  Further, plaintiff lacked diligence.  Plaintiff only provides the excuse of “scheduling conflicts.”  The trial has already been continued twice and the parties are well aware that the discovery cut-off was not extended and was cut-off on June 14, 2024 and that the parties have had ample opportunity to complete discovery.  Further, the Court denies plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend to add a claim for punitive damages; thus, there is no basis to reopen discovery.

The Court denies defendant’s request for sanctions.

The motion is DENIED.

 

ORDER

The motion for leave to file a FAC is DENIED.

The motion to reopen discovery is DENIED.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

Motion to Vacate and Set Aside Dismissal

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

The dismissal of the entire action without prejudice entered on July 17, 2024 is set aside and vacated.  Plaintiff is ordered to file the Doe amendments within five days.  Defendant Performance Contracting, Inc. is dismissed without prejudice.

BACKGROUND

On January 24, 2024, plaintiff Steven Hall filed a complaint against Performance Contracting, Inc. for (1) negligence and (2) premises liability. 

On February 28, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC alleging that the incident occurred on January 26, 2024 around 7:30 a.m. at terminal 3 at the Los Angeles International Airport when heavy metal plates fell and struck plaintiff when he was working on a project at the premises causing serious injury.

On May 2, 2024, defendant filed a notice of removal to federal court.

On July 17, 2024, the parties stipulated to dismissing defendant Performance Contracting Inc. and the Court entered an order dismissing the entire case without prejudice.  The stipulation states in part that the parties have agreed to remand the case for all further proceedings to allow plaintiff to add any unknown parties that may destroy federal jurisdiction.  It also states that the parties filed a joint stipulation to dismiss without prejudice in federal court on July 12, 2024.

On July 25, 2024, the Court received a remand from the U.S. District Court.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a . . . dismissal . . . taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief . . . shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the . . . dismissal . . . . was taken. . . .  Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. . . .”

“[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Under CCP §473(b), plaintiff requests that the Court set aside and vacate the order dismissing the entire action without prejudice.

            Plaintiff asserts that the dismissal was due to a mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect on behalf of plaintiff’s attorneys.  Plaintiff explains that on July 17, 2024, the parties filed a joint stipulation to dismiss defendant without prejudice, but that the parties did not agree to dismiss the entire action.  The case was dismissed without prejudice.  Plaintiff’s counsel was not aware that the dismissal had occurred until August 8, 2024, after plaintiff had attempted to file four Doe amendments to add the parties he believes are responsible for the injuries caused by the incident.  See Mathew Russell decl.

The Court finds that plaintiff has met his burden of showing that the dismissal was the result of counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and/or neglect.  The stipulation filed on July 17, 2024 indicated that the parties had agreed to dismiss without prejudice defendant Performance Contracting Inc.  As defendant was the only defendant named, the entire action was dismissed although plaintiff had intended to dismiss only Performance Contracting Inc. and to file four Doe amendments.

The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED. 

            The dismissal of the entire action without prejudice entered on July 17, 2024 is set aside and vacated.  Plaintiff is ordered to file the Doe amendments within five days.  Defendant Performance Contracting Inc. is dismissed without prejudice.

            Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of ruling.

BACKGROUND

            On March 7, 2024, Janet Dayani and Parviz Dayani filed a complaint against Delta Air Lines, Inc. and Los Angeles World Airport for (1) premises liability, (2) negligence, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, (5) strict products liability, and (6) loss of consortium.

            On June 10, 2024, plaintiffs filed a SAC for (1) premises liability, (2) negligence, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, (5) strict products liability, (6) accident in international transportation, and (7) loss of consortium.

            On August 5, 2024, the parties stipulated and an order entered for a protective order-confidential designation only.

            On August 21, 2024, the hearing on plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents, set one was continued to September 16, 2024 to allow counsel to further meet and confer and/or to engage in an IDC.

            On September 12, 2024, defendant filed a a joint status report regarding the motion to compel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP § 1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  CCP § 2031.310(a).  A statement of compliance shall state that the production, inspection, and related activity demanded will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.  CCP § 2031.220.  “A representation of inability to comply with [a] particular demand for inspection . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.  This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.  This statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”  CCP § 2031.230.

A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).  “To establish ‘good cause,’ the burden is on the moving party to show both:  [1] Relevance to the subject matter (e.g., how the information in the documents would tend to prove or disprove some issue in the case); and [2] Specific facts justifying discovery (e.g., why such information is necessary for trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial).  The fact that there is no alternative source for the information sought is an important factor in establishing ‘good cause’ for inspection.  But it is not essential in every case.”  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1495.6 (citations omitted).  “Declarations are generally used to show the requisite ‘good cause’ for an order to compel inspection.  The declarations must contain ‘specific facts’ rather than mere conclusions.”  Id. at 8:1495.7 (citation omitted).  “The declarations may be on information and belief, if necessary.  However, in such cases, the ‘specific facts’ supporting such information and belief (the sources of the information) must also be alleged.”  Id. at 8:1495.8 (citation omitted).  “Most declarations are made by the attorney for the moving party, who is usually more familiar with the relevancy and ‘specific facts’ constituting ‘good cause’ for inspection.”  Id. at 8:1495.9.

“If ‘good cause’ is shown by the moving party, the burden is then on the responding party to justify any objections made to document disclosure (the same as on motions to compel answers to interrogatories or deposition questions. . . ).”  Id. at 8:1496 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Janet Dayani requests that the Court order defendant Delta Air Lines, Inc. to serve further responses and to produce responsive documents to plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 11 and 13.

            The Court had ruled at the hearing on the motion on August 21, 2024 as follows:  The Court finds that the requests are overbroad to a certain extent in terms of “reflecting” and “present” as the time period is not specified, and the location is not identified.  The Court also finds that defendant is interpreting the requests too broadly despite plaintiff’s counsel providing information during the meet and confer as to what plaintiff is seeking.  The Court notes that defendant’s privacy objection lacks merit, especially in light of the protective order.  The Court denied plaintiff’s request for sanctions. 

The Court also continued the hearing to allow counsel to further meet and confer and/or to engage in an IDC and to file a joint status report by September 12, 2024 as to the status of the meet and confer.

            On September 12, 2024, defendant filed a joint report stating that “[t]he parties are pleased to report that the remaining issues raised by the pending motion to compel have been informally resolved and as to this motion there are no matters that require the Court’s involvement.”

            Accordingly, the motion is MOOT.

 

ORDER

            The motion is MOOT.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.
