Case Number: 22STCV15941    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Sara Simms’s Motion for Protective Order and Motion to Compel Deposition of Defendant Simon Ourian

Protective Order

Plaintiff Sara Simms moves for a protective order regarding her deposition.  A party or deponent may move for a protective order “that justice requires to protect” him or her “from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).)  Generally, “the burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought.”  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

Plaintiff moves for two orders regarding her deposition. 

1. Date of Deposition

Plaintiff seeks an order precluding defendants from taking her deposition on the date previously scheduled, September 11, 2024.  Protective orders may include one “[t]hat the deposition be taken at a different time.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b)(2).)  September 11, 2024, was almost two months ago.  The motion is moot as to this order.  Moreover, though it is a professional courtesy to mutually schedule a deposition, the Civil Discovery Act does not require that.  “An oral deposition shall be scheduled for a date at least 10 days after service of the deposition notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.270, subd. (a).)  “The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action… to attend and to testify.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).) 

2. Remote Testimony

Plaintiff moves for an order permitting her to testify at deposition remotely.  Unless the court issues a protective order, “any party or attorney of record may … be physically present at the deposition at the location of the deponent.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.310, subd. (b).)  Plaintiff does not meet her burden of showing good cause to prohibit defendants’ counsel from being physically present at her location during the deposition.

Plaintiff argues the court should issue such an order for three reasons.  First, she argues defendants originally planned to take the deposition remotely in 2022.  Plaintiff gives no reason why defendants should be bound by their original notice of deposition two years later.  Second, plaintiff argues the deposition may be delayed if someone contracts COVID-19.  This reason is speculative.  And if it occurs, defendants may prefer to delay the deposition rather than take it remotely.  Finally, plaintiff argues the arbitrator in the former arbitration proceeding ordered that her deposition be taken remotely.  Plaintiff chose to withdraw this entire case from arbitration.  The arbitrator’s order no longer applies.  This court does not find good cause to prohibit defendants from taking plaintiff’s deposition in person. 

Order Compelling Simon Ourian’s Deposition

            Plaintiff moves to compel the deposition of defendant Simon Ourian.  Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a) provides, “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action … without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination,” the deposing party “may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony.” 

            Defendants argue Ourian never failed to appear for examination after service of a deposition notice.  Defendants present evidence plaintiff took his deposition set for May 31, 2024, off calendar (Kroll Decl., Exs. 3 & 4) and has not served a new notice of deposition since then (Kroll Decl., ¶ 19).  Plaintiff, however, initially noticed Ourian’s deposition for April 29, 2024.  (Gutierrez Decl., Ex. D.)  When defendants’ counsel stated he and Ourian were “not available for deposition on April 29, 2024” and offered to have the deposition between June 17 and July 19, plaintiff’s counsel agreed to reschedule and proposed several dates within that range.  (Id., Ex. I.)

            Despite several letters from plaintiff’s counsel asking defendants to propose a date for the deposition (Gutierrez Decl., Exs. J, K, L, O), defendants did not do so (id., ¶ 17).  Plaintiff’s counsel extended the courtesy of rescheduling the deposition instead of taking it on April 29 so that the parties could cooperate in picking a date.  Filing this motion after taking the May 31 deposition off calendar but without serving a new deposition notice was reasonable in the circumstances.  Plaintiff shows good cause for an order compelling Ourian’s deposition.

Sanctions

            Plaintiff and defendants move for monetary sanctions against one another.  All parties were partially successful on this combined motion for protective order and to compel Ourian’s deposition.  All parties acted with substantial justification and made reasonable arguments in support of their positions.  The court will not impose sanctions.

Disposition

Plaintiff Sara Simms’s motion for protective order is denied.  Plaintiff Sara Simms’s motion to compel the deposition of defendant Simon Ourian is granted.  The court hereby orders defendant Simon Ourian to testify at deposition no later than December 8.  By November 8, defendant shall propose three dates between November 11 and December 8 for the deposition.  Plaintiff shall choose one of the three dates defendant proposes.


Case Number: 24STCP01133    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 52

Respondent Theo Fram’s Motion to Dismiss Appellant’s Appeal and Request for Attorney’s Fees

Dismissal of Appeal

            Respondent Theo Fram moves to dismiss appellant Memory Buss’s appeal of the Labor Commissioner’s award in matter No. WC-CM-804011.

            Fram is entitled to dismissal of this appeal.  Labor Code section 98.2, subdivision (b) provides, “As a condition to filing an appeal pursuant to this section, an employer shall first post an undertaking with the reviewing court in the amount of the order, decision, or award.”  If the employer fails to do so, the “consequence of not posting the undertaking is that a notice of appeal cannot be filed, the court never obtains jurisdiction, and the Commissioner’s order becomes final.”  (Palagin v. Paniagua Construction, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 124, 138.)  Buss was the employer in the proceeding before the Labor Commissioner.  Buss did not post an undertaking with this court.  (King Decl., ¶ 3.)  The court therefore has no jurisdiction to consider Buss’s appeal.

            Fram is also entitled to attorney fees.  Labor Code section 98.2, subdivision (c) provides, “If the party seeking review by filing an appeal to the superior court is unsuccessful in the appeal, the court shall determine the costs and reasonable attorney’s fees incurred by the other parties to the appeal, and assess that amount as a cost upon the party filing the appeal.”  Buss filed an unsuccessful appeal.  Fram reasonably incurred $2,200 in expenses as a result of the appeal.  (King Decl., ¶ 6.)

            Respondent Theo Fram’s motion to dismiss appellant Memory Buss’s appeal of the Labor Commissioner’s award is granted.  The court hereby dismisses Buss’s appeal with prejudice.  Fram shall recover $2,200 in expenses from Buss.

Case Number: BC680213    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 52

Plaintiff Andrew Rodriguez’s Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint and Motion for Leave to Amend to Conform to Proof

Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint

Plaintiff Andrew Rodriguez moves for leave to file a second amended complaint under Code of Civil Procedure sections 473(a) and 576.  Plaintiff seeks to add causes of action for “violation of CFRA [the California Family Rights Act] rights” and for “CFRA – retaliation.” 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a)(1) provides, “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  Courts exercise their discretion “liberally to permit amendment,” and “[t]he policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)  A motion for leave to amend will normally be granted unless (a) the party seeking to amend has delayed bringing the proposed amendment; and (b) the delay in seeking leave to amend will cause prejudice to an opposing party. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)

Plaintiff unreasonably delayed bringing this proposed amendment.  Plaintiff filed this action seven years ago.  After reversal of the judgment on appeal, the Court of Appeal issued a remittitur almost two years ago.  Plaintiff makes no attempt to explain why he waited until one month before trial to bring this motion.  His counsel’s declaration in support of this motion does not state “[w]hen the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b)(3).)  To the contrary, plaintiff argues, “These two additional causes of action relate back to and share the same factual and legal nexus” as the existing claims.  (Motion, p. 1.)  Plaintiff did not discover any new facts giving rise to the amendment. 

Defendant County of Los Angeles, however, shows no prejudice from allowing plaintiff to add the proposed fourth cause of action for CFRA retaliation.  In its unpublished opinion reversing the judgment, the Court of Appeal expressly considered such a cause of action.  “On appeal, the County contends that taking medical leave is not a protected activity under FEHA.  While taking medical leave may not be an activity protected under section 12940, subdivision (h), retaliation for taking medical leave constitutes a violation of section 12945.2, subdivision (k) of the California Family Rights Act (CFRA).  Further, CFRA is a part of FEHA.”  (Rodriguez v. County of Los Angeles (Cal. Ct. App., Sept. 20, 2022, No. B304818) 2022 WL 4354328, at *7 (Rodriguez).)  The court noted, “The County also argues that plaintiff failed to properly proceed on a violation of CFRA.  The record, however, demonstrates that the County acquiesced to permitting plaintiff to proceed on a claim of retaliation for taking medical leave.”  (Id. at p. *7, fn. 3.)  The court further stated, “The County does not dispute on appeal that: it is an employer covered by the CFRA; plaintiff was an employee eligible to take medical leave; and plaintiff took medical leave for a qualifying purpose.  Thus, substantial evidence supports a finding that plaintiff engaged in ‘protected activity’ by taking medical leave.”  (Id. at p. *7.)

Defendant does not and cannot show any prejudice resulting from an amendment to add a claim for CFRA retaliation after it “acquiesced” to permitting that cause of action at the first trial and actually litigated that cause of action on appeal. 

Defendant relies in part on authority that courts may deny leave to amend solely due to unwarranted delay.  “ ‘[E]ven if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, unwarranted delay in presenting it may—of itself—be a valid reason for denial.’ ”  (Record v. Reason (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 472, 486.)  But nearly all the cases restating that authority found prejudice or other reasons to deny leave to amend.  (Id. at p. 487 [“we do not see how appellant could have been prejudiced by the rejection of the proposed amendment” because he “put forth nearly identical evidence in opposition to summary judgment as he did in support of the motion to amend”]; Tariwala v. Mack (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 807, 812 [trial court “found allowing the amendment would prejudice respondents”]; Duchrow v. Forrest (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1359, 1381 [“The amendment of the complaint prejudiced Forrest in several ways”]; P&D Consultants, Inc. v. City of Carlsbad (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 1332, 1345 (P&D Consultants) [trial court found the “amendment would require additional discovery and perhaps result in a demurrer or other pretrial motion”]; Huff v. Wilkins (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 732, 746 [“Leave to amend is properly denied when the facts are undisputed and as a substantive matter no liability exists under the plaintiff’s new theory”].)

Permitting plaintiff to bring his proposed third cause of action for violation of CFRA would substantially prejudice defendant.  As discussed above, on appeal the record showed it was undisputed that “plaintiff took medical leave for a qualifying purpose.”  (Rodriguez, supra, 2022 WL 4354328, at *7.)  Plaintiff now seeks to allege that, despite allowing plaintiff to take the medical leave required by CFRA, defendant violated his CFRA rights, including by “refus[ing] to grant Plaintiff’s request for family care or medical leave.”  (Prop. SAC, ¶ 73.)  That is a materially different legal theory than any alleged in plaintiff’s first amended complaint. 

As in P&D Consultants, permitting plaintiff to bring this new cause of action on the day before trial would deprive defendant of the opportunity to bring a pretrial motion based on defenses including failure to timely exhaust administrative remedies, would require allowing defendant to conduct additional discovery, or would require significantly delaying the trial.  Plaintiff’s lengthy and unexplained delay in moving for leave to bring a cause of action for violating CFRA has substantially prejudiced defendant. 

The court therefore will permit plaintiff to file a second amended complaint but will strike all portions that only apply to the proposed third cause of action for violation of CFRA rights.

Motion for Leave to Amend to Conform to Proof

Plaintiff also moves for leave to file a second amended complaint to conform to proof.  Code of Civil Procedure section 469 provides, “Variance between the allegation in a pleading and the proof shall not be deemed material, unless it has actually misled the adverse party to his or her prejudice in maintaining his or her action or defense upon the merits.  If it appears that a party has been so misled, the court may order the pleading to be amended, upon such terms as may be just.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 469.)

For the reasons discussed above, permitting plaintiff to amend the complaint to add a new cause of action for violation of CFRA rights would prejudice defendant.  That variation would be material and would mislead defendant in maintaining its defense upon the merits.

This motion is also premature.  “Section 469 specifically governs motions to amend at trial to conform to proof.”  (Garcia v. Roberts (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 900, 909.)  The new trial has not begun.  There is no proof before the court yet.  Section 469 does not apply.

Disposition

            Plaintiff Andrew Rodriguez’s motion for leave to file a second amended complaint is granted in part as to the proposed fourth cause of action and denied as to the proposed third cause of action.  Plaintiff is ordered to file his proposed second amended complaint forthwith.  The court hereby strikes the following portions of plaintiff’s second amended complaint without leave to amend: the entire third cause of action, paragraphs 35, and 67-75 in their entirety, and paragraph 3 of the prayer for relief. 

            Plaintiff Andrew Rodriguez’s motion for leave to amend to conform to proof is denied.
