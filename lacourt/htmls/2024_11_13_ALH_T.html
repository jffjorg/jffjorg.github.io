DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV48317    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Bifurcate

Case Name: Toni Dee Strimple v. City of Los Angeles, et al.
Moving Party: Defendant City of Los Angeles
Responding Party: N/A
Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

This is a personal injury case arising from injuries incurred when Plaintiff was struck in the head by a hose being used to fight an active house fire.  Plaintiff’s operative complaint alleges three causes of action against Defendant: (1) Dangerous Condition of Public Property; (2) Negligent Act or Omission of Govt. Employee; and (3) General Negligence. (declaration of Patty Thammalaiviroj, Exhibit A.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues the interests of economy, efficiency, and “the ends of justice” would be promoted by bifurcating this trial — particularly arguing Defendant’s immunity defense should be tried separately and first. (Motion to Bifurcate, p. 3-4.)
 
NON-OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff agrees with Defendant that the first phase of the trial be held as a bench trial. (Non-opposition, p. 1.)

ANALYSIS

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1048(b) provides that “[t]he court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action . . . of any separate issue or any number of causes of action or issues. . .” The Court also has the power to “provide for the orderly conduct of proceedings before it,” and to “amend and control its processes and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice.” (Civ. Proc. Code § 128(a)(3), (8).) Intrinsic to the Court’s power to regulate the order of trial is its power to bifurcate at any point during proceedings, including even during the trial itself. (See McLellan v. McLellan (1972) 23 Cal.App.3d 343, 353.) Under the Code of Civil Procedure section 1048(b), the court can bifurcate trial to address specific issues “to avoid prejudice.” 

One objective of Code of Civil Procedure section 597 is “avoidance of waste of time and money caused by the trial of issues which may be rendered moot.” (Bedolla v. Logan & Frazer (1975) 52 Cal.App.3d 118, 135.) Code of Civil Procedure section 1048(b) similarly provides courts authority to order a separate trial of any issue for furtherance of convenience, or to avoid prejudice or where separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy. Moreover, courts have discretion under Evidence Code section 320 to regulate the order of proof. 

There is good cause to bifurcate the trial, as doing so serves the interests of economy, efficiency, and the ends of justice. Namely, bifurcating the trial to first address Defendant’s argument that it is immune to liability under Government Code section 850.4. Whether Defendant is immune in this case is highly impactful on the length and expense of a full trial adjudicating the remaining issues, as prevailing would dispose of the case against Defendant completely. Bifurcation would permit the issue of liability to be tried prior to the issue of damages — which is highly desirable in this instance "to avoid wasting court time in cases where the plaintiff loses on the liability issue” in addition to promoting settlement Plaintiff prevails on the liability issue. (Id. at fn. 8.) Thus, bifurcation would allow the parties to resolve the pivotal issue of liability with the expectation the parties will resolve remaining disputed issues by agreement. (Sagi Plumbing v. Chartered Construction Corp (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 443, 449.) As a result, the Court finds it is appropriate to grant Defendant’s motion. 

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Bifurcate is GRANTED.

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

Case Name: Christina Aragon v. Orlando Garcia, et al.

This is a car accident case wherein Plaintiff alleges the vehicle she was a passenger in was negligently owned, operated, entrusted, and maintained, causing a collision with another vehicle, injuring her. (Complaint, p. 4.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Attorney submits this request to be relieved as counsel, citing a significant breakdown in the relationship between counsel and client. (Declaration in Support of Motion, p. 1.)

OPPOSITION

No opposition was filed in response to this motion. 

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 states that an attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment: (1) upon the consent of both client and attorney; or (2) upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Code Civ. Proc. § 284; Rules of Ct., Rule 3.1362.)  The court has power to permit withdrawal of an attorney within its sound discretion.¿(Jones v. Green¿(1946) 74 Cal.App.2d 223, 230.) An attorney may withdraw without cause as long as the withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interest – i.e., counsel cannot withdraw at a critical point, but if the case is not at a critical point withdrawal is permitted because the client will not be prejudiced.¿(Ramirez v. Sturdevant¿(1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.) The court has discretion to deny the withdrawal request when withdrawal would work an injustice or cause undue delay in proceeding; but the court’s discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably.¿(See Mandell v. Superior¿(1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.)  

Counsel must make a motion to be¿relieved¿as attorney of record. The form, content and service of such motion are governed by the Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362. The motion must be made using mandatory forms: Notice of Motion and Motion to be¿Relieved¿as Counsel – Civil (MC-051), Declaration (MC-052), and Proposed Order (MC-053).

Attorney here has properly filed and served Plaintiff with the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel, the Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel, and the proposed Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. Attorney’s declaration asserts the underlying reason for this Motion is that “the attorney-client relationship has completely broken down and cannot be re-established.” (Declaration in Support of Motion, p. 1-2) (additionally noting Attorney made every reasonable effort to establish contact with Plaintiff but has not received any responses.) Plaintiff has not raised any formal objections to this withdrawal or alleged that significant prejudice would result from the withdrawal. Because no prejudice to Plaintiff is anticipated due to this withdrawal, the Court finds granting the motion is appropriate. 

RULING

This Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED. Attorney is ordered to provide notice to Plaintiff and the parties.
 

Case Number: 24NNCV02798    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Stay

Case Name: Ashley Sanchez v. Josh Adam Klinghoffer
Moving Party: Defendant Josh Adam Klinghoffer
Responding Party: Plaintiff Ashley Sanchez, in her individual capacity and as Successor in Interest to Israel Sanchez
Tentative Ruling: GRANT IN PART

BACKGROUND

This is a wrongful death case in Plaintiff alleges a car driven by Defendant struck and killed Decedent Israel Sanchez (“Decedent”). Following the accident criminal charges were brought against Defendant for Vehicular Manslaughter Without Gross Negligence and are still pending in the Alhambra Courthouse under case number 24AHCM01336-01. (Motion to Stay, p. 3.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues that the interests of justice require that this Court stay the civil proceedings pending the final determination of the criminal action. Defendant argues a stay will not prejudice Plaintiff, that Defendant will be unable to defend the civil action while the criminal proceedings are pending, and that there are no additional persons with an interest in the lawsuit who are not parties. Defendant also argues it is in the interest of the public to stay proceedings to prioritize the government's allocation of resources to prosecute its criminal action rather. (Motion to Stay, p. 5.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues the interests of justice would be undermined by imposing a blanket stay on this matter, as it would bar Plaintiff from conducting basic discovery into non-privileged matters. (Opposition, p. 4.)

REPLY

Defendant argues that it is in the interest of justice that this Court stay the civil proceedings pending the final determination of the criminal action because Plaintiff will not be prejudiced by a stay of the civil proceeding or a stay of discovery directed at Defendant, whereas Defendant will be prejudiced by having to defend a civil matter and criminal matter simultaneously. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS

I. Motion to Stay Proceedings

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.) In deciding whether to stay proceedings when a civil defendant faces parallel criminal charges, courts consider: “(1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation.” (Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal. App. 4th 876, 885.) “[T]he privilege is properly invoked whenever the witness’s answers ‘would furnish a link in the chain of evidence needed to prosecute’ the witness for a criminal offense.” (People v. Cudjo (1993) 6 Cal.4th 585, 617.) “[I]t need only be evident from the implications of the question, in the setting in which it is asked, that a responsive answer to the question or an explanation of why it cannot be answered might be dangerous because injurious disclosure could result.” (Id.) A court may stay civil proceedings pending the outcome of parallel criminal proceedings in its own discretion based on the particular circumstances and competing interests involved. (Klein v. Sup. Ct. (Thomas) (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 894, 905); (see Avant! Corp. v. Sup.Ct. (Nequist) (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885 (enumerating various factors to be considered).) The court may also stay discovery until disposition of any pending criminal proceedings so that defendant can no longer claim a Fifth Amendment privilege. (Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 686, 689.) 

The Avant! case provides factors to consider on a motion to stay, including: (l) the interest of the opposing party to proceed expeditiously with litigation or potential prejudice to them caused by delay; (2) the burden any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on moving party; (3) the convenience of the court and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation. (Avant! Corp. v. Sup.Ct. (Nequist) (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885.) Applying the Avant! factors, the Court finds that a stay of discovery directed to Defendant is appropriate to safeguard Defendant’s Fifth Amendment interest.

The first Avant! factor disfavors staying proceedings, as doing so imposes risks that witnesses’ memories fade, or they may relocate or become unavailable during the intervening time. (Opposition, p. 4.) Plaintiff here identifies her foremost concern is in establishing insurance coverage and the potential civil liability of Doe defendants, “which will [be] [sic] the major liability fight in this case.” (Id. at 4-5.) Plaintiff cites the need to conduct discovery into “any and all avenues of potential direct and vicarious liability theories that do not bear on Klinghoffer’s individual criminal liability,” suggesting that ongoing investigation and discovery is necessary in order to timely establish the basis for additional liability theories and to identify additional defendants.  This information could indeed become more difficult to obtain with the passage of time.  The Court thus concludes that Plaintiff would be prejudiced by a complete stay of the case.

The second Avant! factor strongly weighs in favor of granting a stay, at least with regard as to discovery directed to Defendant. Courts have held that civil defendants facing possible criminal prosecution involving the same facts as the civil action have no obligation to disclose to the Plaintiffs information they reasonably believe might be used against them in the criminal proceeding. (Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 686). Notwithstanding that this case considers an actual criminal prosecution, the discovery requests Plaintiff has served Defendant undoubtedly implicates his Fifth Amendment. Notable here is that many of Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions relate to Defendant’s fault, negligence, causation of injuries, alleged violations of various vehicle codes, or other facts relating to the accident that can potentially implicate Defendant criminally. (See Reply, p. 24, Decl. Kendall Craver Para. 2, Ex. “A.”) The Special Interrogatories include questions of what drugs Defendant was took at the time, alongside other questions with clear implications on his criminal liability. (See Reply, p. 20, Decl. Kendall Craver Para. 2, Ex. “A”) The Form Interrogatories seek information concerning Defendant’s investigation of the incident, factual contentions, and proof of affirmative defenses. (See Decl. Kendall Craver ¿2, Ex. “A.”) The Requests for Production seek all statements given about the incident, all investigation reports in connection with the incident, the event data recorder and all documents concerning the incident. (See Decl. Kendall Craver Para. 2, Ex. “A.”) 

Plaintiff’s discovery forces Defendant into a predicament wherein fully engaging with Plaintiff’s discovery would risk implicating Defendant criminally, as engaging with discovery requests can waive Fifth amendment privilege. (Brown v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701) (holding that civil defendants waived their Fifth Amendment privilege against self-incrimination by failing to raise it as an objection to interrogatories within the prescribed 30-day period.) As a result, not granting a stay would burden Defendant significantly — requiring him to choose between waiving his Fifth Amendment privilege or accepting the civil consequences of invoking it and remaining silent. (See Blackburn v. Superior Court, 21 Cal.App.4th 414.) 

The remaining Avant! factors — the efficient use of judicial resources, the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation, and the public interest in the pending civil and criminal litigation — are less significant here. The judicial economy is not served by a stay, as the case will ultimately need to be litigated and it will not necessarily be more efficient to manage the case at a later date.  The interests of people who are not parties to the civil action, namely the family members Plaintiff, does weigh against granting a stay, but not so strongly to overcome the prejudice to Defendant a stay would cause. The public interest here both favors granting a stay in proceedings to allow Defendant to first litigate his criminal case and denying the stay in order to allow Plaintiff to prosecute the civil matter. 

While the Avant! factors favor granting a stay, a full stay of proceedings is not appropriate. Rather, staying discovery directed to Defendant pending the final determination of the criminal action provides a tailored remedy that balances the interests at stake. Doing so safeguards Defendant’s Fifth Amendment interests in the pending the criminal action without preventing Plaintiff from engaging in discovery directed at other parties before memories fade and collecting evidence before it can be lost to time.  The Court thus finds staying discovery as to Defendant until the conclusion of the criminal action is appropriate.

Defendant’s alternative proposed interests do not convince the Court otherwise. Defendant’s argument that defending two cases simultaneously would divide Defendant’s resources is not convincing, as Defendant’s concern is largely ameliorated by the fact that it is his attorneys, not Defendant himself, who will be primarily responsible for defending the civil suit.  Though Defendant may occasionally be involved in assisting counsel is not so substantial a burden on Defendant’s time or resources to prevent him from defending the criminal action. The Court therefore declines to stay all proceedings on this basis. 

The Court accordingly finds that staying discovery directed toward Defendant pending the final determination of the criminal action is appropriate. 

RULING

Defendant’s motion to stay is GRANTED IN PART, as to discovery directed at Defendant pending the final determination of the criminal action.
