DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00259    Hearing Date: April 16, 2024    Dept: W

4467 WOODLEY PARTNERS, LLC vs ALLIED INSULATION, INC., et al.

 

motion for determination of good faith settlement  

 

Date of Hearing:        April 16, 2024                                     Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00259

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a habitability action. On July 26, 2023, Plaintiffs Melvy Cardona; Felipe Argueta; Ever Carbajal; Maymarie Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; Melanie Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; Michael Carbajal, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona, Bella Rodriguez, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona, Chanel Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; and Mason Cardona, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona filed a complaint against SFV Portfolio – 14732 Blythe Owner, LP and 14732 Blythe Associates, L.L.C. for 1. Failure to Provide Habitable Dwelling; 2. Breach of Covenant and Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property; 3. Nuisance 4. Negligence 5. Violation of Civil Code Section 1942.4. Plaintiffs allege the property suffered from several nuisances as well as untenantable and substandard conditions including cockroaches, mice, inoperable heating, mold, deteriorated closet, and so on.

 

Plaintiff dismissed Jeff Warling D/B/A Warling and Associates and Byer Geotechnical Inc.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.      Boc Construction, Inc.’s Motion for Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

2.      Premier Pool Plastering’s Motion for Good Faith Settlement Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6 is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

1.      BOC CONSTRUCTION, INC.’S MOTION FOR GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT

 

Boc Construction, Inc. (“BOC”) moves this court for determination of a good faith settlement made between them and Plaintiff Seth Lichtenstein.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6(a)(1) provides that a “party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors…” Under subdivision (a)(2), a settling party may give notice of settlement to the parties and court, together with an application for determination of good faith settlement and a proposed order. The application “shall indicate the settling parties, and the basis, terms, and amount of the settlement” and must include a proof of service. (CCP §877.6(a)(2).)

 

In determining the good faith of a settlement, the court may consider the affidavits served with the notice of hearing, and any counter-affidavits filed in response, or other evidence in the court’s discretion. (CCP §877.6(b).) The California Supreme Court, in Tech-Bilt Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, provided factors to be considered in determining the good faith of a particular settlement. The court explained that the policies and intent underlying section 877.6 “require that a number of factors be taken into account,” such as: a rough approximation of a plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability; the amount paid in settlement; the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs; a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. (Tech-Bilt at p. 499.) Other relevant factors include financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, and the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of non-settling defendants. (Id.). This evaluation must be made “on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.” (Id.) The court further explained that settlement by a defendant “must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability to be.” (Id., citations omitted.) 

 

The non-settling tortfeasors or obligors bear the burden of demonstrating the absence of good faith in the settlement.  (CCP §877.6(d).) In order to demonstrate a lack of good faith, the non-settling party must show that the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of Section 877.6.  (Nutrition Now, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 209, 213.)

 

BOC has filed an application for good faith settlement, which provides BOC is to pay Plaintiff $75,000 in exchange for a dismissal of BOC Construction with prejudice. BOC contends there are no defects with the work it performed at the Subject Property and that its proportional liability is zero; however, in the interest of good faith and to avoid additional costly legal fees moving forward with further litigation, discovery, and trial preparation, BOC has agreed to settle with Plaintiff. BOC goes on to address each of the Tech-Bilt factors. (Mot. 7:9 – 10:16.)

 

United Excavation Group Inc. opposes the motion on the grounds BOC has failed to provide any substantial evidence that its settlement is in good faith and $75,000 is not a fair representation of BOC’s proportionate share of liability. United Excavation argues BOC acknowledges the total recovery in this matter could be more than $6,000,000.00 and a settlement demand of $1,000,000.00 was made yet maintains its proportional liability is zero and its $75,000.00 settlement is within the reasonable range of its potential liability.

 

According to United Excavation, BOC has attempted to justify its disproportionate settlement by placing blame on other defendants in this matter without providing any substantial evidence in the form of documentation to be considered in evaluating the validity of its alleged good faith settlement.

 

The court finds BOC has presented sufficient evidence and the settlement represents BOC’s proportional share of liability. As noted above, the party contesting the settlement bears the burden of proving that it is in bad faith. (CCP §877.6(d).) If the party contesting the settlement can show, with admissible evidence, that the settlement is “so far ‘out of the ballpark’ in relation to [the above-referenced factors] as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute,” then the court should find the settlement to be lacking in good faith. (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 499-500.) If no such showing is made, the settlement should be deemed to be in good faith and the settlor is entitled to an order barring any further claims by any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor for “equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity” and/or an order dismissing any such claims. (CCP § 877.6(c).)

 

BOC has submitted declarations of BOC's President and CEO, BOC's retained expert, and BOC's counsel in support of their application. BOC also argues a settlement demand from another party is not a factor for the court to consider in determining whether or not a settlement is in good faith. As presented, BOC has demonstrated the settlement is reasonable. Moreover, the instant settlement was reached through multiple mediations with the assistance of mediator, Ross Hart, Esq.

 

Accordingly, BOC Construction, Inc.’s Motion for Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

 

2.      PREMIER POOL PLASTERING’S MOTION FOR GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 877.6

 

Premier Pool Plastering moves the court for an order determining that its settlement between Premier and Plaintiff Seth Lichenstein.

 

Premier Pool Plastering has filed an application for good faith settlement, which provides BOC is to pay Plaintiff $200,000 in exchange for a dismissal of Premier Pool Plastering with prejudice. Premier Pool Plastering contends notwithstanding the lack of evidence demonstrating liability on the part of Premier, Premier’s settlement sum of $200,000 constitutes an economic decision to avoid the expensive and uncertainty associated with a long and protracted litigation. Premier also addresses each of the Tech-Bilt factors. (Mot. 7:10 – 11:16.)

 

United Excavation Group Inc. opposes the motion on the grounds Premier has failed to provide substantial evidence the settlement represents its proportional share of liability. Specifically, Premier Pool acknowledges the pool tilting and cracking to be caused by improper construction and the estimated cost to replace the pool to be approximately $1,800,000.00 yet contends that $200,000.00 is a reasonable settlement amount to represent its proportional share of liability. According to United Excavation, Premier has attempted to justify its disproportionate settlement by placing blame on other defendants in this matter without providing any substantial evidence in the form of affidavits or declarations to be considered in evaluating the validity of its alleged good faith settlement.

 

The court finds Premier has presented sufficient evidence and the settlement represents Premier’s proportional share of liability. As noted above, the party contesting the settlement bears the burden of proving that it is in bad faith. (CCP §877.6(d).) If the party contesting the settlement can show, with admissible evidence, that the settlement is “so far ‘out of the ballpark’ in relation to [the above-referenced factors] as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute,” then the court should find the settlement to be lacking in good faith. (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 499-500.) If no such showing is made, the settlement should be deemed to be in good faith and the settlor is entitled to an order barring any further claims by any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor for “equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity” and/or an order dismissing any such claims. (CCP § 877.6(c).)

 

Premier has submitted a report by Helfrich Associates and Grover-Hollingsworth which provides that Premier’s possible contribution to the alleged problem was small (roughly 10% to 20% liability) and confirmed that the bulk of the pool related damages arise from construction and design deficiencies caused by other defendants. As presented, Premier has demonstrated the settlement is reasonable.

 

Accordingly, Premier Pool Plastering’s Motion for Good Faith Settlement Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6 is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23VECP00613    Hearing Date: April 16, 2024    Dept: W

PRICE V. CITY OF LOS ANGELES

 

RULING ON PETITION TO PRESENT LATE TORT CLAIM

 

Date of Hearing:        April 16, 2024                                    Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECP00613

 

Moving Party:            Damien Price

Responding Party:     City of Los Angeles

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Price brings this petition seeking permission to present a late tort claim against the City of Los Angeles pursuant to Gov’t Code Section 912.  This petition arises out of a motor vehicle accident which occurred on October 22, 2022. Gov’t Code Section 911.2 provides that “[a] claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property or growing crops shall be presented as provided in Article 2 (commencing with Section 915) not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.”  Petitioner did not file a tort claim until May 5, 2023, more than 180 days after the accident.  Petitioner argues that he should be granted leave to file a late tort claim for two reasons: first, the cause of action did not accrue until March of 2023, when the extent of plaintiff’s injuries was understood; and second, that plaintiff’s error in not filing within the six month period was the result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.

 

The City counters that the petition for relief from the claims presentation requirements set forth in Government Code § 945.4 must be denied because petitioner did not first present an application for leave to present a late

claim to the City, as required by Section 946.6.   That section provides that a petitions for relief from the claims presentation requirements imposed by Section 945.4 can only be filed after “an application for leave to present a claim is denied or deemed to be denied.” (Gov. Code, § 946.6, subd. (a).) The petition can only be

granted upon a determination by the court that, among other things, “the application [for leave to present a late claim] … was made within a reasonable time not to exceed that specified in subdivision (b) of Section 911.4 and was denied or deemed denied.” (Gov. Code, § 946.6, subd. (c).)   The City asserts that no such application was set forth in the petition or, in fact, ever presented.  Any such application would have to have been made no later than October 22, 2023, one year after the accrual of the cause of action. (Gov’t Code Section 911.4.)  Here, there can be no question but that the cause of action accrued at the time of the collision with the City’s fire truck and not at some later point when the petitioner understood the full extent of his injuries.  

 

The court agrees that the petitioner must be denied unless the petitioner can establish that it first presented the application to the City prior to October 22, 2023, and that such application was denied or deemed denied.   There is no evidence before the court that any such application was made.   “[R]elief provided by section 946.6 is available only if an application for leave to present a late claim is filed with the public entity involved and is denied or deemed denied.” (Williams v. Mariposa County Unified Sch. Dist. (1978) 82 Cal.App.3d 843, 848.)

 

The Petition to File a Late Claim is DENIED.
