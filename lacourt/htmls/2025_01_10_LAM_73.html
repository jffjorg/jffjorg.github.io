DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STC24177    Hearing Date: January 10, 2025    Dept: 73

January 10, 2025

Dept. 73

Rolf M. Treu, Judge presiding

 

Janice Deutsch v. Braswell’s Colonial Care (24STCV24177)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Pro Per

Counsel for Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care/moving party: Wilson Getty LLP

 

[motion TO DECLARE VEXATIOUS LITIGANT] (filed November 27, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care’s motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant is GRANTED in its entirety. The Court will discuss briefing schedule for security amount at the time of the hearing.

 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care alleged lack of medical care to Plaintiff Janice Deutsch’s mother (Decedent) prior to her death.

 

On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff Janice Deutsch (Plaintiff) filed a complaint in pro per against Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care (Defendant) alleging causes of action for: (1) Intentional Tort, (2) Fraudulent Concealment, (3) Deception, and (4) Intentional Misrepresentation.

 

On November 27, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant. No opposition was filed.  

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 391 subdivision (b) defines a vexatious litigant as a person engaged with the court and conducting any of the following activities:  

 

“(1) In the immediately preceding seven-year period has commenced, prosecuted, or maintained in propria persona at least five litigations other than in a small claims court that have been (i) finally determined adversely to the person or (ii) unjustifiably permitted to remain pending at least two years without having been brought to trial or hearing. 

(2) After a litigation has been finally determined against the person, repeatedly relitigates or attempts to relitigate, in propria persona, either (i) the validity of the determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined or (ii) the cause of action, claim, controversy, or any of the issues of fact or law, determined or concluded by the final determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined. 

(3) In any litigation while acting in propria persona, repeatedly filed unmeritorious motions, pleads, or other papers, conducts unnecessary discovery, or engages in other tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. 

(4) Has previously been declared to be a vexatious litigant by any state or federal court of record in any action or proceeding based upon the same or substantially similar facts, transaction, or occurrence.”

 

“The trial court exercises its discretion in determining whether a person is a vexatious litigant. Review of the order is accordingly limited and the Court of Appeal will uphold the ruling if it is supported by substantial evidence.  Likewise, a court's decision that a vexatious litigant does not have a reasonable chance of success in the action is based on an evaluative judgment in which the court weighs the evidence. If there is any substantial evidence to support the court's determination, it will be upheld.”  (Singh v. Lipworth (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 813, 828.)

 

“Evidence that a litigant is a frequent plaintiff or defendant alone is insufficient to support a vexatious litigant designation.”  (Morton v. Wagner (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 963, 969.)  Where a pro per plaintiff repeatedly files motions revisiting issues that have already been determined, procedurally defective motions, constantly revisits issues and files voluminous supplemental and amended briefing, the Court may deem him a vexatious litigant under Code of Civil Procedure, section 391 subdivision (b)(3).  (See Singh, supra, 227 Cal.App.4th at 828.; see also Golin v. Allenby (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 616, 638-639.)

 

B.      Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court to take judicial notice of the following pursuant to Evidence Code Sections 452 and 453:

1.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, Case No. 21BBCV00309, including its October 8, 2021 order granting NBC’s anti-SLAPP motion.

14.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. DLI Properties, et al., Case No. 24STCV24180, filed on September 18, 2024.

Although a court may take judicial notice of court records and files, judicial notice is limited to matters that are indisputably true. (Evid. Code, section 452(d); Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 482.) The truth of the matters asserted in these records is not subject to judicial notice. (Arce, supra, 181 Cal.App.4th at p. 483.) However, the court may take judicial notice of a document’s legal effect. (Julian Volunteer Fire Co. Assn. v. Julian-Cuyamaca Fire Protection Dist. (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 583, 600 [“In taking judicial notice of an official document, a court may take notice not only of the fact of the document but also facts that can be deduced, and/or clearly derived from, its legal effect, such as the names and dates contained in the document, and the legal consequences of the document. This is different from taking judicial notice of the truth of specific factual representations within a document.”].)

Thus, the Court takes judicial notice of above as to their existence and legal effect but not as to the truth of the matters asserted in them.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves for (1) an order declaring Plaintiff a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b); (2) Requiring Plaintiff to provide security to assure payment of Defendant’s litigation costs, including attorney’s fees, incurred defending this frivolous action; (3) Staying this action until such time as Plaintiff has provided the security; and (4) issuing a prefiling order prohibiting Plaintiff from filing any new litigation in California state courts in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the Presiding Judge of the court in which the litigation is proposed to be filed pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391.7. 

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff should be declared as a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(1) because in the past seven years, Plaintiff has commenced and prosecuted at least five civil cases no in small claims court that were all adjudicated against her and dismissed.

 

Further, Defendant argues that Plaintiff should be declared as a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(2) because Plaintiff has file and litigated an action in 2021 against Defendant for alleged false imprisonment and mistreatment of Decedent at Defendant’s skilled nursing facility from October 2012 to August 2015. (See Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Braswell’s Colonial Care, LASC Case No. 21STCV36154 (hereinafter “2021 action”), filed on October 1, 2021 at Request for Judicial Notice ¶ 8.) The complaint in the 2021 actionalleged the following causes of action: General negligence, violation of due process, unlawful seizure of person, violation of 6th Amendment, obstruction of justice – frustration of a subpoena and violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, Title 22 Section 72527. (Motion, Exhibit A.) After a sustained demurrer with leave to amend and Plaintiff filing a first amended complaint, the Court on July 28, 2023 sustained Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend as all the causes of action in the first amended complaint were barred by the applicable statutes of limitations. Defendant now contends that the instant action and alleges identical causes of action and contemplates the same underlying facts as the 2021 matter, given that the instant action deals with Defendant’s alleged unlawful confinement of Plaintiff’s mother, Patricia Deutsch, in its care facility from October 2012 until her death on July 21, 2015.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(1), Plaintiff is considered a vexatious litigant. In the last seven years, Plaintiff has filed five pro per civil lawsuits that were all adjudicated against her:

 

(1)   Janice Deutsch v. Rick Hill, Case No. ED CV 19-824-JLS (KS) (C.D. Cal.) filed on May 2, 2019, which resulted in a judgment of dismissal. (Request for Judicial Notice (JN), ¶¶ 6-7 and Exhibits 3 and 4.)

(2)   Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, LASC Case No. 21BBCV00309, filed on March 30, 2021, which resulted in the granting of an anti-SLAPP motion filed by NBC. (JN ¶ 1.) 

(3)   Janice Deutsch v. CBS News Los Angeles, LASC Case No. 21BBCV00312, filed on April 1, 2021, which resulted in the granting of an anti-SLAPP motion filed by CBS. (JN ¶ 3.)

(4)   Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Mission Hospital, LASC Case No. 21STCV36156, filed on October 1, 2021, which resulted in a sustained demurrer to the first amended complaint without leave to amend. (JN ¶ 5)

(5)   Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Braswell’s Colonial Care, LASC Case No. 21STCV36154, filed on October 1, 2021, which resulted in a sustained demurrer to the first amended complaint without leave to amend based on the applicable statutes of limitations barring the alleged causes of action. (JN ¶ 8.)

 

Further, in addition to the instant action, Plaintiff has filed six other pro per actions in Los Angeles Superior Court. (JN ¶¶ 9-14.)

 

Plaintiff is also considered a vexatious litigant under Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(2).

 

“What constitutes ‘repeatedly’ and ‘unmeritorious’ under subdivision (b)(3), in any given case, is left to the sound discretion of the trial court.”  (Morton, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at 971 [three motions over three years involving two separate judgments did not qualify as repeated filing of unmeritorious motions].)  “While there is no bright-line rule as to what constitutes ‘repeatedly,’ most cases affirming the vexatious litigant designation involve situations where litigants have filed dozens of motions either during the pendency of an action or relating to the same judgment.”  (Id. at p. 972 [citing Bravo v. Ismaj (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 211, 225 [court found that approximately 20 motions constituted “repeated” because they all arose during the same action and many of the motions were identical to motions previously brought and denied]].) In the event a plaintiff’s conduct satisfies the above test, the court may require them to furnish security and/or dismiss the litigation. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 391, 391.1).

 

The 2021 action against Defendant alleged similar causes of action and was based on identical facts as the instant action: Defendant’s alleged false imprisonment and mistreatment of her mother at its skilled nursing facility from October 2012 to August 2015. The ultimate outcome of the 2021 action was a sustained demurrer without leave to amend, due a statutes of limitations defense, which qualifies as a “final determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 391(b)(2).)

 

Additionally, Plaintiff has no reasonable probability of prevailing against Defendant in the instant action for the same reasons that the 2021 action failed. The instant action asserts identical causes of action as the 2021 action, arising from conduct that occurred prior to Decedent’s death on July 21, 2015 and is filed an additional 3 years after the 2021 action and more than 9 years after the alleged offending acts. Because the 2021 action was fully barred by the statute of limitations, the instant action, arising from the same conduct as the 2021 action, would also reasonably be barred by the applicable statutes of limitations, given that the instant action is filed three years after the 2021 action. Further, as Defendant contends, the doctrine of res judicata precludes recovery by Plaintiff in a lawsuit involving claims which were previously asserted, litigated and adjudicated in Defendant’s favor.

 

Lastly, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

The Court next considers the viability of the subject case presented for purposes of determining the necessity of posting security in order to proceed. “The motion for an order requiring plaintiff to furnish security shall be based upon the ground, and supported by a showing, that the plaintiffs is a vexatious litigation and that there is not a reasonable probability that he or she will prevail in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 391.1(a), 391.3(a).) 

 

As stated above, based on the outcome of the 2021 action and its identical nature to the instant action, there is not a reasonable probability that Plaintiff will prevail against Defendant. Therefore, the Court orders the posting of a security. 

 

The statute requires the plaintiff to furnish security “for the benefit of the moving defendant, security in such amount and within such time as the court shall fix.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.3(a).) “The vexatious litigant statutes were enacted to require a person found a vexatious litigant to put up security for the reasonable expenses of a defendant who becomes the target of one of these obsessive and persistent litigants whose conduct can cause serious financial results to the unfortunate object of his attack. The purpose of the statutory scheme is to deal with the problem created by the persistent and obsessive litigant who has constantly pending a number of groundless actions, often against the judges and other court officers who decide or were concerned in the decision of previous actions adversely to him.” (Holcomb v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1504.) Given the complaint is against Defendant, damages come from a potentially successful claim and attorney fees defending the action. (Muller v. Tanner (1969) 2 Cal.App.3d 445, 452 [“the amount of the security is defined as the moving party’s ‘reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and not limited to taxable costs, incurred in, or in connection with’ the litigation”].)  

 

The Court cannot determine the reasonable expenses for attorney fees and costs at this point. The Court will therefore set a supplemental briefing schedule to determine the amount of security. Defendant is ordered to submit a supplemental brief in support of the security amount. Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the security order could lead to dismissal of the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.4.)  

 

“In addition to any other relief provided in this title, the court may, on its own motion or the motion of any party, enter a prefiling order which prohibits a vexatious litigant from filing any new litigation in the courts of this state in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the presiding justice or presiding judge of the court where the litigation is proposed to be filed. Disobedience of the order by a vexatious litigant may be punished as a contempt of court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.7(a).) The presiding judge “shall permit the filing of that litigation only if it appears that the litigation has merit and has not been filed for purposes of harassment or delay” and “may condition the filing of the litigation upon the furnishing of security for the benefit of the defendants. (Id. § 391.7(b).) 

 

The Court enters a prefiling order prohibiting Plaintiff from filing any new litigation in the courts of this state in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the court.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant is GRANTED in its entirety. The Court will discuss briefing schedule for security amount at the time of the hearing.

 

Defendant is to give notice.

Lopez-Reyes v. County of Los Angeles Department of Regional Planning (24STCP00384)

 

Counsel for Petitioner/opposing party:  Rigoberto Lopez-Reyes (Pro Per)

Counsel for Respondent/moving party:  Dawyn R. Harrison (Los Angeles County Counsel)

 

Demurrer with motion to strike (filed 11/22/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Respondent’s demurrer to the Petition of Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Respondent’s motion to strike the second, third, and fourth causes of action of the Amended Petition of Writ of Mandate is GRANTED, without leave to amend.

 

I.                    BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Petitioner Rigoberto Lopez-Reyes (“Petitioner”), in pro per, filed a petition against Respondent County of Los Angeles Department of Regional Planning and its director, Amy Bodek (together, “Respondent”). On July 26, 2024, Respondent filed a demurrer to the Petition. The Court sustained Respondent’s demurrer with leave to amend.

 

On October 11, 2024, Petitioner filed an Amendment to the Original Petition. In the Amend to Original Petition, the Petitioner alleged the following causes of action against Respondent: (1) violation of Gov. Code §65943, (2) violation of Gov. Code §§ 66031 and 65801, (3) violation of the Business and Professions Code §§ 16700 et seq., 16720, and 16726, and (4) violation of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment of the United States Constitution. Petitioner alleges that Respondent’s leadership violated several state laws by precluding the construction of a housing development for homeless senior veterans by denying a building permit.

 

On November 22, 2024, Respondent filed a demurrer and a motion to strike portions of the Amended Petition of Writ of Mandate.

 

As to the demurrer, Respondent argues:

·                 The entire Amended Petition is uncertain as to the statutory authority for relief sought.

·                 The second cause of action is uncertain, as there are no facts alleged to support the relief sought.

·                 There are no facts alleged to constitute a valid claim for the second, third, and fourth causes of action.

As to the motion to strike, Respondent argues:

·                 Petitioner added a second, third, and fourth cause of action, beyond the scope permitted by the Court’s order allowing leave to amend.

·                 Petitioner’s second cause of action in an unintelligible request to order the parties to mediation.

·                 Petitioner’s request for judicial notice must be stricken due to procedural deficiencies.

Under Civ. Code Proc. § 1005(b), any papers opposing the demurrer were due on or before December 26, 2024. On January 3, 2025, Respondent filed a notice of non-opposition. As of January 7, 2025, no opposition papers have been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)

 

On review of the declaration of Amy J. Cooper, the Court notes that Respondent has satisfied its meet and confer obligation prior to filing the instant motion, as Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), and section 435.5, subdivision (a), require meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” On November 19, 2024 counsel for Respondent and Petitioner discussed the merits of the demurrer by telephone but were unable to reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer. (Decl. of Cooper, ¶¶ 3, 4.)

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, at p. 747.)¿¿ 

 

Respondent demurs to the Amended Writ of Mandate Petition on the grounds that it is uncertain and fails to state sufficient facts to support the relief requested by Code Civ. Proc. §1094.5. (Demurrer, p. 5.) Petitioner maintains that Respondent unlawfully denied his application for proposed housing for homeless veterans. In the Amended Write of Mandate Petition, Petitioner reattaches the original denial letter from Respondent and related correspondence. (Amended Writ of Mandate Petition, Exhibits A and X.) As the Court previously ruled, the denial letter from Respondent does not provide a basis for bringing a Writ of Mandate under CCP 1094.5. Petitioner has failed to demonstrate that an administrative agency has rendered a final adjudication and has relied on the same evidence presented in the initially filed Writ of Mandate Petition. (id., p. 6.)

 

As such, the demurrer to the Petition of Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.

 

C.      Legal Standard for Motion to Strike

The Court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).) 

1.      Motion to Strike Second, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action

 

Respondent moves to strike the second, third, and fourth causes of action asserted in the Amended Writ of Mandate Petition on the grounds that Petitioner has added new causes of action beyond the scope authorized by Court order. (Notice, p. 2.)

 

“Following an order sustaining a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings with leave to amend, the plaintiff may amend his or her complaint only as authorized by the court’s order.  [Citation.]  The plaintiff may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so, unless the new cause of action is within the scope of the order granting leave to amend.”  (Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.

 

The Court’s October 4, 2024, order on Respondent’s demurrer granted Petitioner leave to amend the Petition of Writ of Mandate. (See 10/4/2024 Min. Order.) Petitioner must obtain permission before adding new causes of action against defendants.  (See Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 613 [“After expiration of the time in which a pleading can be amended as a matter of course, the pleading can only be amended by obtaining the permission of the court.”].)

 

Petitioner may amend his petition only as authorized by the Court’s order and may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so. Petitioner has not requested permission to add new causes of action beyond the state law claims initially alleged pursuant to Title 7 chapter 4.3 section 65915(a)(1), 65915(d)(1) and 65915(e)(1) in the original petition.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Respondent’s motion to strike to the Amended Petition of Writ’s second, third, and fourth causes of action.

 

2.      Motion to Strike Petitioner’s Request for Mediation

 

Respondent demurs to the second cause of action as an unintelligible request for mediation. (Demurrer, p. 3.) Petitioner cites Chapter 9.3, Section 66031, subdivision (a) for the proposition that all legal challenges relating to development projects (including denials) are not subject to demurrer because they are subject to mediation. (Amended Petition of Writ, p. 5.) Given that the second cause of action was improperly raised in this amendment, the request for mediation is rendered moot.

 

The Court renders Respondent’s motion to strike the second cause of action of the Amended Petition of Writ as moot.

 

3.      Motion to Strike Petitioner’s Request for Judicial Notice

 

Respondent also moves to strike Petitioner’s request for judicial notice, on the grounds that its form and substance fail to conform with State law or with the Rules of Court because Petitioner submits a declaration that fails to cite an Evidence Code section and fails to describe the documents to be noticed. (Motion, p. 6.)

 

The Court only takes judicial notice as to “the existence, content and authenticity of public records and other specified documents” under Evidence Code section 452(d); it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400.) A request for judicial notice can be stricken if it is not accompanied by certified copies of the documents to be noticed. In People v. Preslie, the court struck the request for judicial notice because it was not accompanied by certified copies of the documents desired to be noticed (People v. Preslie, (1977) 70 Cal.App.3d 486.)

 

Citing Evidence Code sections 452(d) and (h), Petitioner requests the Court to take judicial notice of certain subsections of Title 22 of the Los Angeles County Ordinance. Petitioner cites sections 22.226.040 and 22.160.10, and includes arguments, which are improperly included.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

              The demurrer to the Amended Petition of the Writ of Mandate is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

The motion to strike the second, third, and fourth causes of action alleged in the Amended Petition of Writ is GRANTED without leave to amend.

 

              The motion to strike Petitioner’s second cause of action is MOOT.

 

              The motion to strike Petitioner’s Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Compel Further Reponses to Special Interrogatories

 

Similarly, upon receipt of responses to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if they deem the responses to be “evasive or incomplete” or if “[a]n objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (§§ 2030.300, subd. (a)(1); 2030.300 subd. (a)(3).)  A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (§§ 2016.040; 2030.300 subd. (b)(1).) The motion must also be accompanied by a separate statement containing the text of the interrogatories at issue, the text of each response at issue, and a statement of the reasons why a further response is required, though the court may permit a party to submit “a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute” in lieu of a separate statement. (§ 2030.300(b)(2); Cal. Rules of Court, 3.1345(b).)¿¿ 

 

“To show an interrogatory seeks relevant, discoverable information ‘is not the burden of [the party propounding interrogatories].’ As a litigant, it is entitled to demand answers to its interrogatories, as a matter of right, and without a prior showing, unless the party on whom those interrogatories are served objects and shows cause why the questions are not within the purview of the code section." (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 422; see Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 378.) While the party propounding interrogatories may have the burden of filing a motion to compel if it finds the answers it receives unsatisfactory, the burden of justifying any objection and failure to respond remains at all times with the party resisting an interrogatory. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221.)” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal. 5th 531, 541.) 

 

B.      Discussion

 

To prevail on a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories, a propounding party must show (i) the responding party’s responses were evasive or incomplete, (ii) a meet-and-confer declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion,” and (iii) a separate statement of the reasons why further response is required.

 

1.                  Plaintiff’s Responses Are Evasive or Incomplete.

 

Plaintiff’s responses are evasive or incomplete for the reasons Partners provided. Plaintiff provided one responsive document addressed to all defendants, not to Partners. (Motion, Exh. C p. 1.) By not addressing a single party, the responses are evasive as to whom Plaintiff is answering.

 

Plaintiff only responded to 35 interrogatories out of the 104 interrogatories propounded on her. (Motion, Exh. C pp. 3-10.) Plaintiff’s answers are therefore incomplete.

 

It is unclear which interrogatories Plaintiff responded to because Plaintiff’s responses do not correctly identify any of the Interrogatories propounded on her. (Motion, Exh. C pp. 3-10.) As an example, Partners’ Special Interrogatory No. 17 asks “Please state the total amount of all economic damages you assert you are entitled to recover as a result of DEFENDANT’s conduct.” (Motion, Exh. A, p. 5.) Plaintiff’s responsive document states that Special Interrogatory No. 17 is “Please state all facts in support of the contentions asserted in the paragraph 23 of your.” (Motion, Exh. C p. 6.). Because the Plaintiff’s responses do not correctly identify or match the first 35 special interrogatories, Plaintiff’s answers are incomplete and evasive.

 

As the court finds Plaintiff’s responses evasive and incomplete, there is good cause to compel Plaintiff’s responses.

 

2.                  Defendant Attempted to Meet and Confer.

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under section 2016.040. (§ 2031.310.) Such a declaration must state facts a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (§ 2016.040.) A party may meet the informal resolution requirement by showing an exchange of letters or a telephone call. (§ 2023.010, subd. (I) - providing that “failing to confer in person, by telephone, or by letter” constitutes misuse of discovery process.) 

 

Partners’ meets the second requirement, because it has provided a declaration showing that it attempted to resolve its issues with the responses in reasonable and good faith. Partners attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel through email on April 15, June 7, and July 29 of last year. (Decl. Ghattas ¶¶ 4, 9, 11.) Each time Partners’ counsel offered to or granted an extension. (Decl. Ghattas ¶¶ 4, 9, 11.) Partners’ meet and confer letter from July 29, 2024 described its issues with Plaintiff’s response. Those issues are the same ones described above.

 

Therefore, Partners has shown a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of the issue regarding responses to the special interrogatories. 

 

3.                  Defendant’s Separate Statement

 

Partners’ meets the last requirement, because the motion must also be accompanied by a separate statement containing the text of the interrogatories at issue, the text of each response at issue, and a statement of the reasons why a further response is required. (Separate Statement pp. 2-29.) Partners either claims “Plaintiff did not provide a response to this interrogatory” or Plaintiff’s response “was nonresponsive to the interrogatory actually propounded by the defendant” and “[t]hus, Plaintiff’s response is entirely nonresponsive to the interrogatory posed.” As the separate statement includes the text of the interrogatories, the text of the responses, and its issues with the responses, the third requirement is met.

 

Thus, Partners meets all three elements and has shown good cause to compel further responses to written discovery and an attempt at resolving the issues outside of court. Partners' efforts having failed, the court will GRANT the Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

C.      Legal Standard for Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

Attorney’s fees are mandatory against a “party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response[s]” unless the court finds that the one to be sanctioned “acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (§ 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

D.     Discussion

 

“The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, 3.1348(b).) “When the trial court is informed of the extent and nature of the legal services rendered, it may rely on its own experience and knowledge in determining their reasonable value.” (In re Marriage of Huntington (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1524.)

 

Partners’ requests $2,140 in monetary sanctions for the motion at a rate of $260 per hour plus $60 to file. (Decl. Ghattas ¶¶ 14, 15.) Partners’ counsel contends that it took 4.5 hour for preparation of the motion and related documents, 1.0 preparing for preparing a reply to Plaintiff’s anticipated opposition, and 2.5 hours for preparing for and attending a hearing on this motion. (Decl. Ghattas ¶ 14.) The court finds the claimed hours to exceed the reasonable range for the execution of the tasks described. The motion is simple and the reply is less than a page long.

 

Instead, the court finds it proper to award Attorney Ghattas 7 hours of attorney work at a rate of $260 per hour. This comes to a total of $1,880, with $60 for filing. Sanctions are to be paid within thirty (30) days from the date of Plaintiff serving a notice of this Order. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Monetary Sanctions, compliance within  20 days

 

The court AWARDS Attorney Ghattas 7 hours of attorney work at a rate of $260 per hour. This results in a total of $1,880, with $60 for filing, payable within 20 days.

KEISHA MOREHEAD D.D.S. v. LAW OFFICE OF GREGORY J RAMIREZ (24STCV16819)

 

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Gregory J. Ramirez (Law Office of Gregory J. Ramirez)

Counsel for Plaintiff/ opposing party: N/A

 

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO CHANGE VENUE and FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s unopposed Motion to Change Venue is GRANTED. Venue is improper in Los Angeles County. Proper venue lies in Ventura County.

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Pursuant to CCP § 399(a), Plaintiff is liable for the fees and costs of transferring the action. Matter is ordered transferred to said county upon payment of the transfer fee in the sum of $50.00 payable to the Los Angeles Superior Court and the submission of a separate check for the filing fee payable to Ventura County Superior Court, within 30 days by Plaintiff in the Clerk's Office at the Los Angeles Superior Court.

 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of the court’s order and file a proof of service of such.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff Keisha Morehead brought this action against Defendant Gregory Ramirez, alleging a cause of action for legal malpractice.

The complaint alleges that in May of 2021, Defendant undertook to provide legal services for Plaintiff in connection with a case. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant committed malpractice and breached fiduciary duties by failing to explain that a countersuit could be filed, and depositions should be taken to strengthen the defense in her case, among other things. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant falsely claimed to no longer be Plaintiff’s attorney, without proper pre-trial evidence gathering, excluded the option of a trial and forced Plaintiff into a costly settlement.

On October 9, 2024, Defendant filed this Motion to Change Venue. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

On timely motion, the court must order a transfer of an action “when the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court.” (CCP §§ 397(a), 396b.) The burden is on the moving party to establish that the plaintiff's venue selection is not proper. (Fontaine v. Sup.Ct. (Cashcall, Inc.) (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 830, 836.) 

As a general rule, “the superior court in the county where the defendants or some of them reside at the commencement of the action is the proper court for the trial of the action.” (CCP § 395(a); see also Kaluzok v. Brisson (1946) 27 Cal.2d 760, 763 (“The right of a defendant to have an action brought against him tried in the county of his residence is an ancient and valuable right, safeguarded by statute and supported by a long line of decisions. The right of a plaintiff to have an action tried in a county other than that of the defendant's residence is exceptional.”).)

However, this “general rule” of venue has effect only when no other venue provision applies. When there is a more specific venue statute, the general rule is subordinated. (Brown v. Sup.Ct. (C.C. Myers, Inc.) (1984) 37 Cal.3d 477, 483.)

CCP § 395(a) provides in part: 

Subject to subdivision (b), if a defendant has contracted to perform an obligation in a particular county, the superior court in the county where the obligation is to be performed, where the contract in fact was entered into, or where the defendant or any defendant resides at the commencement of the action is a proper court for the trial of an action founded on that obligation, and the county where the obligation is incurred is the county where it is to be performed, unless there is a special contract in writing to the contrary.

“[T]he county in which such obligation is incurred shall be deemed to be the county in which it is to be performed.” (Armstrong v. Smith (1942) 49 Cal.App.2d 528.)

B.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves to transfer the case to Ventura County, arguing venue in Los Angeles County is improper as he lives in Ventura County, and his law practice is in Ventura County. (Ramirez Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s venue selection of Los Angeles County is improper.

Attorneys’ Fees and Costs 

Pursuant to CCP § 396b, “the court may order the payment to the prevailing party of reasonable expenses and attorney's fees incurred in making or resisting the motion to transfer.” (CCP §396b(b).) “In determining whether such order for expenses and fees shall be made, the court shall take into consideration (1) whether an offer to stipulate to change of venue was reasonably made and rejected, and (2) whether such motion or selection of venue was made in good faith given the facts and law the party making the motion or selecting the venue knew or should have known.” (Mission Imports, Inc. v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 921, 931-932.) 

Here, although Defendant’s counsel contacted Plaintiff about stipulating to transfer the case to Ventura County, there is no indication that Plaintiff’s venue selection was made in bad faith. Plaintiff may have believed venue was proper because it appears the underlying case at issue was in Los Angeles. However, as Defendant’s law firm is in Ventura County, the county where the obligation was incurred, this county would be deemed the county in which the obligation is performed. (Armstrong, supra, 49 Cal.App.2d 528.)

Accordingly, the court denies attorneys’ fees. Nevertheless, pursuant to CCP § 399(a), Plaintiff is liable for the fees and costs of transferring the action.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s unopposed Motion to Change Venue is GRANTED. Venue is improper in Los Angeles County. Proper venue lies in Ventura County.

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

Pursuant to CCP § 399(a), Plaintiff is liable for the fees and costs of transferring the action. Matter is ordered transferred to said county upon payment of the transfer fee in the sum of $50.00 payable to the Los Angeles Superior Court and the submission of a separate check for the filing fee payable to Ventura County Superior Court, within 30 days by Plaintiff in the Clerk's Office at the Los Angeles Superior Court.

 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of the court’s order and file a proof of service of such.

Janice Deutsch v. Braswell’s Colonial Care (24STCV24177)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party: Pro Per

Counsel for Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care/moving party: Wilson Getty LLP

 

[motion TO DECLARE VEXATIOUS LITIGANT] (filed November 27, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care’s motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant is GRANTED in its entirety.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care alleged lack of medical care to Plaintiff Janice Deutsch’s mother (Decedent) prior to her death.

 

On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff Janice Deutsch (Plaintiff) filed a complaint in pro per against Defendant Braswell’s Colonial Care (Defendant) alleging causes of action for: (1) Intentional Tort, (2) Fraudulent Concealment, (3) Deception, and (4) Intentional Misrepresentation.

 

On November 27, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant. No opposition was filed.  

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 391 subdivision (b) defines a vexatious litigant as a person engaged with the court and conducting any of the following activities:  

 

“(1) In the immediately preceding seven-year period has commenced, prosecuted, or maintained in propria persona at least five litigations other than in a small claims court that have been (i) finally determined adversely to the person or (ii) unjustifiably permitted to remain pending at least two years without having been brought to trial or hearing. 

(2) After a litigation has been finally determined against the person, repeatedly relitigates or attempts to relitigate, in propria persona, either (i) the validity of the determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined or (ii) the cause of action, claim, controversy, or any of the issues of fact or law, determined or concluded by the final determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined. 

(3) In any litigation while acting in propria persona, repeatedly filed unmeritorious motions, pleads, or other papers, conducts unnecessary discovery, or engages in other tactics that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. 

(4) Has previously been declared to be a vexatious litigant by any state or federal court of record in any action or proceeding based upon the same or substantially similar facts, transaction, or occurrence.”

 

“The trial court exercises its discretion in determining whether a person is a vexatious litigant. Review of the order is accordingly limited and the Court of Appeal will uphold the ruling if it is supported by substantial evidence.  Likewise, a court's decision that a vexatious litigant does not have a reasonable chance of success in the action is based on an evaluative judgment in which the court weighs the evidence. If there is any substantial evidence to support the court's determination, it will be upheld.”  (Singh v. Lipworth (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 813, 828.)

 

“Evidence that a litigant is a frequent plaintiff or defendant alone is insufficient to support a vexatious litigant designation.”  (Morton v. Wagner (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 963, 969.)  Where a pro per plaintiff repeatedly files motions revisiting issues that have already been determined, procedurally defective motions, constantly revisits issues and files voluminous supplemental and amended briefing, the Court may deem him a vexatious litigant under Code of Civil Procedure, section 391 subdivision (b)(3).  (See Singh, supra, 227 Cal.App.4th at 828.; see also Golin v. Allenby (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 616, 638-639.)

 

B.      Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court to take judicial notice of the following pursuant to Evidence Code Sections 452 and 453:

1.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, Case No. 21BBCV00309, including its October 8, 2021 order granting NBC’s anti-SLAPP motion.

2.      California Court of Appeal, Second District, docket for Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, Case No. B316571.

3.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch v. CBS News Los Angeles, Case No. 21BBCV00312, including its October 8, 2021 order granting CBS’s anti-SLAPP motion.

4.      California Court of Appeal, Second District, docket for Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, Case No. B316571.

5.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch, et al. v. Mission Hospital, Case No. 21STCV36156, including its January 24, 2023 order of dismissal.

6.      The May 4, 2020 decision by the United States District Court, Central District of California on the Petition for Writ of Habeas Corpus entitled Janice Deutsch v. Rick Hill, Case No. ED CV 19-824-JLS (KS).

7.      The May 4, 2020 judgment of dismissal by the United States District Court, Central District of California on the Petition for Writ of Habeas Corpus entitled Janice Deutsch v. Rick Hill, Case No. ED CV 19-824-JLS (KS).

8.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch, et al. v. Braswell’s Colonial Care, Case No. 21STCV36154, including its entry of judgment and dismissal on July 28, 2023.

9.      Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. First American Title Ins. Co., Case No. 24STCV23745, filed on September 13, 2024.

10.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. Barrett Daffin Frappier Treder & Weiss LLP, Case No. 24STCV23751, filed on September 13, 2024.

11.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. KNBC Los Angeles, Case No. 24STCV24058, filed on September 17, 2024.

12.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. KTLA Los Angeles, Case No. 24STCV24062, filed on September 17, 2024.

13.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. CBS Corporation, Case No. 24STCV24175, filed on September 18, 2024.

14.  Los Angeles Superior Court docket and case file for Janice Deutsch. v. DLI Properties, et al., Case No. 24STCV24180, filed on September 18, 2024.

Although a court may take judicial notice of court records and files, judicial notice is limited to matters that are indisputably true. (Evid. Code, section 452(d); Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 482.) The truth of the matters asserted in these records is not subject to judicial notice. (Arce, supra, 181 Cal.App.4th at p. 483.) However, the court may take judicial notice of a document’s legal effect. (Julian Volunteer Fire Co. Assn. v. Julian-Cuyamaca Fire Protection Dist. (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 583, 600 [“In taking judicial notice of an official document, a court may take notice not only of the fact of the document but also facts that can be deduced, and/or clearly derived from, its legal effect, such as the names and dates contained in the document, and the legal consequences of the document. This is different from taking judicial notice of the truth of specific factual representations within a document.”].)

Thus, the Court takes judicial notice of above as to their existence and legal effect but not as to the truth of the matters asserted in them.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves for (1) an order declaring Plaintiff a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b); (2) Requiring Plaintiff to provide security to assure payment of Defendant’s litigation costs, including attorney’s fees, incurred defending this frivolous action; (3) Staying this action until such time as Plaintiff has provided the security; and (4) issuing a prefiling order prohibiting Plaintiff from filing any new litigation in California state courts in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the Presiding Judge of the court in which the litigation is proposed to be filed pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391.7. 

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff should be declared as a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(1) because in the past seven years, Plaintiff has commenced and prosecuted at least five civil cases no in small claims court that were all adjudicated against her and dismissed.

 

Further, Defendant argues that Plaintiff should be declared as a vexatious litigant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(2) because Plaintiff has file and litigated an action in 2021 against Defendant for alleged false imprisonment and mistreatment of Decedent at Defendant’s skilled nursing facility from October 2012 to August 2015. (See Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Braswell’s Colonial Care, LASC Case No. 21STCV36154 (hereinafter “2021 action”), filed on October 1, 2021 at Request for Judicial Notice ¶ 8.) The complaint in the 2021 actionalleged the following causes of action: General negligence, violation of due process, unlawful seizure of person, violation of 6th Amendment, obstruction of justice – frustration of a subpoena and violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, Title 22 Section 72527. (Motion, Exhibit A.) After a sustained demurrer with leave to amend and Plaintiff filing a first amended complaint, the Court on July 28, 2023 sustained Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend as all the causes of action in the first amended complaint were barred by the applicable statutes of limitations. Defendant now contends that the instant action and alleges identical causes of action and contemplates the same underlying facts as the 2021 matter, given that the instant action deals with Defendant’s alleged unlawful confinement of Plaintiff’s mother, Patricia Deutsch, in its care facility from October 2012 until her death on July 21, 2015.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(1), Plaintiff is considered a vexatious litigant. In the last seven years, Plaintiff has filed five pro per civil lawsuits that were all adjudicated against her:

 

(1)   Janice Deutsch v. Rick Hill, Case No. ED CV 19-824-JLS (KS) (C.D. Cal.) filed on May 2, 2019, which resulted in a judgment of dismissal. (Request for Judicial Notice (JN), ¶¶ 6-7 and Exhibits 3 and 4.)

(2)   Janice Deutsch v. NBC News Los Angeles, LASC Case No. 21BBCV00309, filed on March 30, 2021, which resulted in the granting of an anti-SLAPP motion filed by NBC. (JN ¶ 1.) 

(3)   Janice Deutsch v. CBS News Los Angeles, LASC Case No. 21BBCV00312, filed on April 1, 2021, which resulted in the granting of an anti-SLAPP motion filed by CBS. (JN ¶ 3.)

(4)   Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Mission Hospital, LASC Case No. 21STCV36156, filed on October 1, 2021, which resulted in a sustained demurrer to the first amended complaint without leave to amend. (JN ¶ 5)

(5)   Janice Deutsch, Estate of Patricia Deutsch, Duff Shidaker v. Braswell’s Colonial Care, LASC Case No. 21STCV36154, filed on October 1, 2021, which resulted in a sustained demurrer to the first amended complaint without leave to amend based on the applicable statutes of limitations barring the alleged causes of action. (JN ¶ 8.)

 

Further, in addition to the instant action, Plaintiff has filed six other pro per actions in Los Angeles Superior Court. (JN ¶¶ 9-14.)

 

Plaintiff is also considered a vexatious litigant under Code of Civil Procedure section 391 subdivision (b)(2).

 

“What constitutes ‘repeatedly’ and ‘unmeritorious’ under subdivision (b)(3), in any given case, is left to the sound discretion of the trial court.”  (Morton, supra, 156 Cal.App.4th at 971 [three motions over three years involving two separate judgments did not qualify as repeated filing of unmeritorious motions].)  “While there is no bright-line rule as to what constitutes ‘repeatedly,’ most cases affirming the vexatious litigant designation involve situations where litigants have filed dozens of motions either during the pendency of an action or relating to the same judgment.”  (Id. at p. 972 [citing Bravo v. Ismaj (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 211, 225 [court found that approximately 20 motions constituted “repeated” because they all arose during the same action and many of the motions were identical to motions previously brought and denied]].) In the event a plaintiff’s conduct satisfies the above test, the court may require them to furnish security and/or dismiss the litigation. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 391, 391.1).

 

The 2021 action against Defendant alleged similar causes of action and was based on identical facts as the instant action: Defendant’s alleged false imprisonment and mistreatment of her mother at its skilled nursing facility from October 2012 to August 2015. The ultimate outcome of the 2021 action was a sustained demurrer without leave to amend, due a statutes of limitations defense, which qualifies as a “final determination against the same defendant or defendants as to whom the litigation was finally determined.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 391(b)(2).)

 

Additionally, Plaintiff has no reasonable probability of prevailing against Defendant in the instant action for the same reasons that the 2021 action failed. The instant action asserts identical causes of action as the 2021 action, arising from conduct that occurred prior to Decedent’s death on July 21, 2015 and is filed an additional 3 years after the 2021 action and more than 9 years after the alleged offending acts. Because the 2021 action was fully barred by the statute of limitations, the instant action, arising from the same conduct as the 2021 action, would also reasonably be barred by the applicable statutes of limitations, given that the instant action is filed three years after the 2021 action. Further, as Defendant contends, the doctrine of res judicata precludes recovery by Plaintiff in a lawsuit involving claims which were previously asserted, litigated and adjudicated in Defendant’s favor.

 

Lastly, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

The Court next considers the viability of the subject case presented for purposes of determining the necessity of posting security in order to proceed. “The motion for an order requiring plaintiff to furnish security shall be based upon the ground, and supported by a showing, that the plaintiffs is a vexatious litigation and that there is not a reasonable probability that he or she will prevail in the litigation.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 391.1(a), 391.3(a).) 

 

As stated above, based on the outcome of the 2021 action and its identical nature to the instant action, there is not a reasonable probability that Plaintiff will prevail against Defendant. Therefore, the Court orders the posting of a security. 

 

The statute requires the plaintiff to furnish security “for the benefit of the moving defendant, security in such amount and within such time as the court shall fix.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.3(a).) “The vexatious litigant statutes were enacted to require a person found a vexatious litigant to put up security for the reasonable expenses of a defendant who becomes the target of one of these obsessive and persistent litigants whose conduct can cause serious financial results to the unfortunate object of his attack. The purpose of the statutory scheme is to deal with the problem created by the persistent and obsessive litigant who has constantly pending a number of groundless actions, often against the judges and other court officers who decide or were concerned in the decision of previous actions adversely to him.” (Holcomb v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1504.) Given the complaint is against Defendant, damages come from a potentially successful claim and attorney fees defending the action. (Muller v. Tanner (1969) 2 Cal.App.3d 445, 452 [“the amount of the security is defined as the moving party’s ‘reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and not limited to taxable costs, incurred in, or in connection with’ the litigation”].)  

 

The Court cannot determine the reasonable expenses for attorney fees and costs at this point. The Court will therefore set a supplemental briefing schedule to determine the amount of security. Defendant is ordered to submit a supplemental brief in support of the security amount. Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the security order could lead to dismissal of the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.4.)  

 

“In addition to any other relief provided in this title, the court may, on its own motion or the motion of any party, enter a prefiling order which prohibits a vexatious litigant from filing any new litigation in the courts of this state in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the presiding justice or presiding judge of the court where the litigation is proposed to be filed. Disobedience of the order by a vexatious litigant may be punished as a contempt of court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 391.7(a).) The presiding judge “shall permit the filing of that litigation only if it appears that the litigation has merit and has not been filed for purposes of harassment or delay” and “may condition the filing of the litigation upon the furnishing of security for the benefit of the defendants. (Id. § 391.7(b).) 

 

The Court enters a prefiling order prohibiting Plaintiff from filing any new litigation in the courts of this state in propria persona without first obtaining leave of the court.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to declare Plaintiff a vexatious litigant is GRANTED in its entirety.

 

Defendant is to give notice.
