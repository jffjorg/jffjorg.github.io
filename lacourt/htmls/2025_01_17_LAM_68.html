DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV11602    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 1-17-25

Case 23STCV11602

Trial Date: 1-21-25

 

NON-DESTRUCTIVE SITE INSPECTION

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants, Arriaga USA, Inc., et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant, Bernadino Sanchez

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Wendy Viridinia Solano-Castro, et al. alleges the wrongful death of Raul Garcia-Leon caused from as a stone fabricator and installer. Plaintiffs contend working with “toxic” stone products led to the development of silicosis and death of Garcia-Leon on February 16, 2023.

 

On May 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed a 582 paragraph complaint for negligence, strict liability – warning defect, strict liability – design defect, fraudulent concealment, and breach of implied warranties. On May 31, 2023, Plaintiff a 235 page first amended complaint for negligence, strict liability – warning defect, strict liability – design defect, fraudulent concealment, and breach of implied warranties. The action names a multitude of parties comprised of suppliers, sellers, etc. The case was deemed non-complex on June 5, 2023, and assigned to Department 68 on June 7, 2023.

 

On October 11, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the first amended complaint brought by MS International, Inc. to the fourth cause of action with 20 days leave to amend. On October 18, 2023, the court overruled the demurrer to the fourth cause of action brought by M S International, Inc. On November 2, 2023, Caesarstone USA, Inc. filed a cross-complaint for indemnity, declaratory relief and apportionment. On December 29, 2023, Lowes Home Centers, LLC filed a cross-complaint indemnity, declaratory relief and apportionment.

 

On September 5, 2024, the court granted the motion for trial preference based on the age of Rebecca Garcia at the time of the filing of the motion.

 

RULING: Denied, without prejudice.

Defendants Arriaga USA, Inc. dba Stoneland USA, Color Marble Inc., and CSS Distributions Inc. move for a non-destructive site inspection of the fabrication workshop premises of Cross-Defendant Bernadino Sanchez located at 1802 1st Street, San Fernando. Defendants represent an initial agreement to allow for inspection and efforts to agree upon a mutual time, but the third party subpoena (prior to Sanchez’s appearance in the action as a cross-defendant) was subsequently disregarded thereby necessitating the subject motion. Defendants move on grounds that the condition of the premises is material to defense of the claims within the action. Defendants Jacobe Enterprises, Inc. and Paragon Industries filed joinders to the motion. Cross-Defendant Bernadino Sanchez in opposition on grounds of an improper service, untimely notice, and lack of accommodation to minimizing interference with operations. Defendants in reply maintain proper service of a third party deposition subpoena at the time, denies improper service, an entitlement to conduct the inspection, and denial of any significant disruption.

 

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b) ... may make an order … directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare...” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).) A party may demand that any other party produce and permit the party making the demand, or someone acting on the demanding party's behalf, to inspect and to photograph, test, or sample any tangible things that are in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010, subd. (c).)

 

The subpoena was served to counsel for Sanchez on November 19, 2024, with an inspection date of December 10, 2024. [Declaration of Rey Yeng, Ex. A.] Counsel served objections on December 5, 2024. [Id., Ex. C.] Sanchez appeared in the action via answer on December 10, 2024.

 

The motion lacks proof of service on Sanchez, and was only served on counsel prior to any appearance. (Code Civ. Proc., 1985.6, subd. (b-c).) RULING: Denied.

Evidentiary Objections to the Declaration of Ravinder Singh Johar: Overruled.

Evidentiary Objections to Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman: Overruled.

 

Defendant Southland Stone USA, Inc. (Southland) moves for summary judgment on the entire complaint, and alternatively, summary adjudication on the entire complaint, and the claim for punitive damages. Southland moves on multiple grounds, including: Lack of evidence establishing causation, privity, a duty to disclose, and corporate knowledge; and a complete defense based on the sophisticated intermediary/user doctrine. Plaintiffs in opposition challenge the motion on grounds that Southland fails to shift the burden of proof on ground of insufficient competent evidence, denial of application of the sophisticated intermediary/user defense, and challenges any defense to fraudulent concealment or the punitive damages claim.

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

 

A defendant may alternatively move for summary judgment on grounds that Plaintiff lacks sufficient evidence, and cannot reasonably obtain necessary evidence in support of the claims.¿(Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th at pp. 854-855; Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 582, 587; Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 101; Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.) The court adheres to this standard for product liability cases, such as asbestos and crystalline silica (e.g. latent exposure leading to mesothelioma or silicosis).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.”  (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

The court finds the subject matter analogous to asbestos litigation on the basis of latent injury caused by a dangerous product, and therefore applies the substantial factor standard in the subject action. (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415, 1419.) “In the context of a cause of action for asbestos-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective asbestos-containing products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a “legal cause” of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In an asbestos-related cancer case, the plaintiff need not prove that fibers from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing cancer.” Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982. “[W]e conclude that the proper analysis is to ask whether the plaintiff has proven exposure to a defendant's product, of whatever duration, so that exposure is a possible factor in causing the disease and then to evaluate whether the exposure was a substantial factor.”  (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 1416.)  Exposure by a contributing party, among many, is sufficient to establish triable issues of material fact.  The calculation of the probabilities of whether even limited exposure constituted a substantial factor is more appropriately addressed before the trial court.  (Id. at pp. 1417-1419.)

 

“In the context of a cause of action for [silica]-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective [] products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a ‘legal cause’ of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In a[] [silicosis] case, the plaintiff need not prove that [particulate] from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing [silicosis].” (Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982.)

 

Southland provides contradictory arguments and evidence regarding exposure, but its separate statement (which the court will rely on) does not dispute that Decedent worked with its product while at Bernadino Sanchez. [UMF No. 1.] However, Southland contends it only distributed natural stone, and thus there is no causation based on the substantial factor test and sophisticated users/intermediary doctrine. As to the substantial factor argument, Southland relies solely on the argument that natural stone does not contain the same elevated levels of crystalline silica or hazardous materials as artificial stone, therefore any potential exposure would not meet the substantial factor threshold required to prove causation. In support of this contention, Southland only cites to the allegations in the FAC in paragraphs 136 and 263. While the allegations indirectly state that natural stone has less crystalline silica content that artificial stone, it does not make any statement that completely negates causation for purposes of summary judgment. Southland otherwise fails to provide any other evidence or lack of evidence by Plaintiffs, and thus fails to shift the burden. The court alternatively finds triable issues of material fact that exposure to said silica containing stone products caused exposure and led to the silicosis diagnosis and death. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman.] Further, the court notes that there is testimony from Decedent’s co-worker Cesar Quiroz identifying both natural and artificial Southland products at the Decedent’s workplace, which undermines Southland’s argument and creates triable issues as to the type of stone product supplied and exposure to the extent Southland contends there was none. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Depositions of Cesar Quiroz, 66:23-67:12, 339:8-15, 929:9-23, 931:7-8.]

 

As to Southland’s lack of privity argument regarding the breach of implied warranty claim, a worker who is injured in the course of his work by a defective product purchased by his hirer is in privity with both the manufacturer and the supplier of the product. (Peterson v. Lamb Lumber Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 339, 347-348.) Southland fails to sufficiently address the legal standard.

 

The motion for summary judgment and motion for summary adjudication reliant on the absence of sufficient exposure, causation, and privity is therefore DENIED. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 81.) 

 

Southland also challenges the action under sophisticated/intermediate user doctrines. The court reviews the standard. “In general, a manufacturer or supplier has a duty to warn consumers about the dangers and risks inherent in the use of its product. (Citations.) The sophisticated user defense is an exception to the general duty to warn. (Citation.) The defense concerns warnings and does not apply to a cause of action for design defect. (Citation.) ... [¶] Under the sophisticated user defense, a manufacturer is not liable to a sophisticated user of its product for failure to warn about the product's dangers if the sophisticated user knew or should have known of the dangers. (Citation.) The ‘should have known’ portion of the formulation is an objective standard. (Citation.) ... The rationale supporting the defense is that the failure to warn about risks already known to a sophisticated user usually is not a proximate cause of the user's injuries. (Citation.)” (Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th at pp. 601–602.)

 

The “obvious danger” standard within the sophisticated user defense: “The obvious danger rule is an objective test, and the courts do not inquire into the user's subjective knowledge in such a case. In other words, even if a user was truly unaware of a product's hazards, that fact is irrelevant if the danger was objectively obvious. ...  Thus, under the sophisticated user defense, the inquiry focuses on whether the plaintiff knew, or should have known, of the particular risk of harm from the product giving rise to the injury.” (Johnson v. American Standard, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 56, 71.)

 

While the danger of silica dust finds medical support (See Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman), Southland relies on Decedent’s “experience” as a fabricator to comprehend the medical dangers of silica dust caused by the cutting of artificial stone. The argument provides no evidentiary support establishing the obvious nature of such an injury. The court declines to find as a matter of law that such a danger exists for purposes of ruling on the motion. The court therefore finds Southland, again, fails to shift the burden. (See McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc., supra, 98 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co., supra, 69 Cal.App.4th at p. 81.)

 

As to the sophisticated intermediary defense, Southland relies on an apparent denial of any necessity to warn based on OSHA/Cal OSHA regulations regarding employer responsibility to provide safety equipment, due to an admittedly known hazard of silica particulate matter from stone fabrication. Other than an admission to the hazards of silica dust, the motion lacks legal support actually addressing the standard for warning or evidence of the sufficiency of its warning, and instead offers deflection. The court finds no legal or evidentiary support based on reliance and deflection under uncited OSHA and Cal OSHA standards with insufficient legal address. (Webb v. Special Electric Co., Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 167, 187-188.) Plaintiff additionally points out that Southland characterizes Decedent as an independent contractor, not an employee for purposes of the uncited OSHA/Cal OSHA regulations. Further, the sophisticated intermediary defense requires the producer to provide adequate warnings to the intermediary, and then allows the producer to rely on the intermediary to pass along those warnings. (Id. at 185-186.) It is the producer’s burden to show it warned the intermediary. (Ibid.) Southland provides no evidence it adequately warned a sophisticated intermediary thus this argument fails. To the extent Southland relies on its arguments regarding lack of exposure and the known hazards of natural stone, the court incorporates its previous analysis.

 

The court therefore DENIES the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication on the failure to establish insufficient evidence or a complete defense on this basis.

 

On fraudulent concealment, Southland additionally challenges the lack of any basis of duty to disclose any specific potential harms from the failure to warn. Southland relies exclusively on the lack of any duty to disclose due to the absence of any fiduciary duty. “[T]he elements of a cause of action for fraud based on concealment are: ‘(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. [Citation.]’” (Kaldenbach v. Mutual of Omaha Life Ins. Co. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 830, 850 (internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) “Each of the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presupposes the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … As a matter of common sense, such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties.” (Id. at pp. 336–337.)

 

“The requirement's purpose is to inform consumers about a product's hazards and faults of which they are unaware, so that they can refrain from using the product altogether or evade the danger by careful use.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) Users include persons responsible for processing materials not privy to information regarding necessary safety protocols. The withholding of said information subjects a defendant to potential fraudulent concealment liability. (Id. at pp. 1199-1200.)

 

The operative complaint presents allegations regarding knowledge of the allegedly toxic substance, crystalline silica, dangers to workers engaged in the cutting, grinding, fabrication, polishing, etc. with the product, and failure to disclose potential toxicity upon working with said products. Decedent was specifically exposed and harmed as a result of the non-disclosed hazards and continued work with the supplied product. The allegations meet the standard for fraudulent concealment. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78-79.)

 

Under the consideration for product liability summary judgment, the court finds insufficient address of this standard by Southland for purposes of shifting the burden. Southland’s citation to Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276 is misplaced as it is not a toxic tort case and hence does not grapple with the unique public policies at play in such cases. Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co., supra, a toxic tort case involving parties with a similar relationship to the parties at present, is controlling. (See Jones, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at 1198-2000; LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at 336; Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1192.) The court therefore denies the motion for summary adjudication on both the failure to shift the burden, and triable issues of material fact regarding failure to warn on allegedly known hazards caused by fabrication of products containing silica. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exhs. 1 & 5.]

 

On punitive damages, Southland offers a conclusive challenge regarding the lack of evidence to support the claim for punitive damages. On punitive damages, Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c) authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, which are defined as follows:

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

“‘[M]alice does not require actual intent to harm. [Citation.] Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. [Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Consistent with the prior part of the order, the court again finds Southland fails to shift the burden. Southland presents insufficient evidence in support of its contentions, generally points to an absence of evidence without any address, and relies on its previous arguments which fail as discussed above. The court additionally finds there are triable issues of material fact on the basis of knowledge, ratification, and malice. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exhs. 1 & 5.] (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 576–577; Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied in its entirety.

 

In summary, the court denies the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication.

On punitive damages, Francini challenges the sufficiency against the corporate entity. Francini generally states that Plaintiffs have no evidence to support this claim and relies on the same arguments made for the fraud cause of action relating to devoid discovery responses.

 

On punitive damages, Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c) authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, which are defined as follows:

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

“‘[M]alice does not require actual intent to harm. [Citation.] Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. [Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Consistent with the prior part of the order, the court again finds that Francini presents insufficient evidence in support of its contentions, does not sufficiently address the discovery responses provided including witnesses identified as having corporate knowledge, and relies on its arguments regarding the fraud cause of action which fail as discussed above. The court additionally finds there are triable issues of material fact on the basis of knowledge, ratification, and malice. [Appendix of Evidence: Responses to Special Interrogatories; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exhs. 1-3.] (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 576–577; Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied in its entirety.

 

In summary, the court denies the motion for summary adjudication.

Evidentiary Objections to Declaration of Raphael Metzger: Overruled. (CCP § 437c(q).)

 

EIDP, Inc. (EIDP) moves for summary judgment on the entire complaint, and alternatively, summary adjudication on the entire complaint, and the claim for punitive damages. EIDP moves on multiple grounds, including: Lack of evidence establishing exposure, causation, privity, duty to warn, and corporate knowledge; and a complete defense under sophisticated intermediary/user doctrine. Plaintiffs in opposition challenge the motion on grounds that EIDP fails to shift the burden of proof on ground of insufficient competent evidence, denial of application of the sophisticated intermediary/user defense, and challenges any defense to fraudulent concealment or the punitive damages claim.

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

 

A defendant may alternatively move for summary judgment on grounds that Plaintiff lacks sufficient evidence, and cannot reasonably obtain necessary evidence in support of the claims.¿(Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th at pp. 854-855; Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 582, 587; Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 101; Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.) The court adheres to this standard for product liability cases, such as asbestos and crystalline silica (e.g. latent exposure leading to mesothelioma or silicosis).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.”  (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

The court finds the subject matter analogous to asbestos litigation on the basis of latent injury caused by a dangerous product, and therefore applies the substantial factor standard in the subject action. (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415, 1419.) “In the context of a cause of action for asbestos-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective asbestos-containing products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a “legal cause” of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In an asbestos-related cancer case, the plaintiff need not prove that fibers from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing cancer.” Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982. “[W]e conclude that the proper analysis is to ask whether the plaintiff has proven exposure to a defendant's product, of whatever duration, so that exposure is a possible factor in causing the disease and then to evaluate whether the exposure was a substantial factor.”  (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 1416.)  Exposure by a contributing party, among many, is sufficient to establish triable issues of material fact.  The calculation of the probabilities of whether even limited exposure constituted a substantial factor is more appropriately addressed before the trial court.  (Id. at pp. 1417-1419.)

 

“In the context of a cause of action for [silica]-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective [] products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a ‘legal cause’ of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In a[] [silicosis] case, the plaintiff need not prove that [particulate] from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing [silicosis].” (Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982.)

 

EIDP relies on denial of any supply, manufacture or distribution of silica containing products utilized by Decedent, while employed as a fabricator. The position relies on a challenge to Plaintiffs’ evidence, as well as the declaration of Barbara Hannah. Hannah provides that only “certified” employers are authorized by EIDP to purchase or receive Corian or Zodiac, and GTO Stone, Bernardino Sanchez, and San Remo Marble & Granite were not certified. [Declaration of Barbara Hannah, ¶¶3-4.]

 

Other than citation to discovery responses (without address in the moving papers) regarding the basis of the complaint, including the performance of stone fabrication work without sufficient protective equipment, the court finds no substantive basis for the challenge to the insufficiency of evidence by Plaintiffs regarding the placement of EIDP manufactured and/or distributed silica containing artificial stone products on the premises of any and all of Decedent’s employers during the relevant exposure period. [See Declaration of Deanne L. Miller, Appendix of Evidence: Responses to Special Interrogatories and Request for Production.] Plaintiffs present pages and pages of factually detailed discovery regarding the claim for exposure. Likewise, the deposition testimony EIDP relies on of Decedent’s co-workers and Sanchez are insufficient alone to negate any element. Admittedly, co-worker Caesar Quiroz does recall working with Zodiaq for at least a portion of his time at Bernadino Sanchez’s shop. [Declaration of Deanne L. Miller: Deposition of Cesar Quiroz.] The conclusive arguments and reliance on denial of sufficient evidence fails to shift the burden. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 81.)  

 

While EIDP also presents evidence that Decedent’s places of work were not “certified” to purchase or receive its product (Zodiac or Corian), this is insufficient to shift the burden when it also presented evidence that co-worker Caesar Quiroz recalls working with Zodiaq for at least a portion of his time at Bernadino Sanchez’s shop as referenced above.

 

The court alternatively finds triable issues of material fact based on the presence of silica containing EIDP products on the premises of Decedent’s place of work during the relevant time period. Both EIDP and Plaintiffs present testimony identifying EIDP products for Bernadino’s shop, and Plaintiffs present invoices and records of sale of EIDP products to Bernadino’s shop. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Cesar Quiroz, 419-421; Declaration of Simonetta Marhsall, Exh. A.] There is also evidence the exposure to said silica containing stone products caused exposure and led to the silicosis diagnosis and death. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman.]

 

As to EIDP’s lack of privity argument regarding the breach of implied warranty claim, a worker who is injured in the course of his work by a defective product purchased by his hirer is in privity with both the manufacturer and the supplier of the product. (Peterson v. Lamb Lumber Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 339, 347-348.) EIDP fails to sufficiently address the legal standard or provide any evidentiary support for its conclusory statement that the product is also not defective.

 

Therefore, the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication reliant on the absence of sufficient exposure, causation, and privity is therefore DENIED.

 

EIDP also challenges the action under sophisticated/intermediate user doctrines. The court reviews the standard. “In general, a manufacturer or supplier has a duty to warn consumers about the dangers and risks inherent in the use of its product. (Citations.) The sophisticated user defense is an exception to the general duty to warn. (Citation.) The defense concerns warnings and does not apply to a cause of action for design defect. (Citation.) ... [¶] Under the sophisticated user defense, a manufacturer is not liable to a sophisticated user of its product for failure to warn about the product's dangers if the sophisticated user knew or should have known of the dangers. (Citation.) The ‘should have known’ portion of the formulation is an objective standard. (Citation.) ... The rationale supporting the defense is that the failure to warn about risks already known to a sophisticated user usually is not a proximate cause of the user's injuries. (Citation.)” (Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th at pp. 601–602.)

 

The “obvious danger” standard within the sophisticated user defense: “The obvious danger rule is an objective test, and the courts do not inquire into the user's subjective knowledge in such a case. In other words, even if a user was truly unaware of a product's hazards, that fact is irrelevant if the danger was objectively obvious. ...  Thus, under the sophisticated user defense, the inquiry focuses on whether the plaintiff knew, or should have known, of the particular risk of harm from the product giving rise to the injury.” (Johnson v. American Standard, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 56, 71.)

 

While the danger of silica dust finds medical support (See Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman), EIDP relies on Decedent’s “experience” as a fabricator to comprehend the medical dangers of silica dust caused by the cutting of artificial stone. The argument provides no evidentiary support establishing the obvious nature of such an injury. The court declines to find as a matter of law that such a danger exists for purposes of ruling on the motion. The court therefore finds EIDP, again, fails to shift the burden. (See McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc., supra, 98 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co., supra, 69 Cal.App.4th at p. 81.)

 

As to the sophisticated intermediary defense, EIDP relies on an apparent denial of any necessity to warn based on OSHA/Cal OSHA regulations regarding employer responsibility to provide safety equipment, due to an admittedly known hazard of silica particulate matter from stone fabrication. Other than an admission to the hazards of silica dust, the motion lacks legal support actually addressing the standard for warning or evidence of the sufficiency of its warning, and instead offers deflection. The court finds no legal or evidentiary support based on reliance and deflection under OSHA and Cal OSHA standards with insufficient legal address. (Webb v. Special Electric Co., Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 167, 187-188.) Further, the sophisticated intermediary defense requires the producer to provide adequate warnings to the intermediary, and then allows the producer to rely on the intermediary to pass along those warnings. (Id. at 185-186.) It is the producer’s burden to show it warned the intermediary. (Ibid.) EIDP provide no evidence it adequately warned a sophisticated intermediary thus this argument fails.   

 

The court therefore DENIES the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication on the failure to establish insufficient evidence or a complete defense on this basis. Plaintiff additionally establishes triable issues of material fact on the known hazards of silica particulate matter resulting from fabrication, the warning to Decedent’s alleged employer, Decedent’s alleged employers’ sophistication, and the employment relationship. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Bernardino Sanchez, 50:3-16, 145:9-22, 159:8-16, 159: 25-160:10.]

 

On the fraudulent concealment cause of action, EIDP additionally challenges the lack of any basis of duty to disclose any specific potential harms from the failure to warn. EIDP relies on the lack of any duty to disclose due to the absence of any fiduciary duty, a lack of causation evidence that Decedent would have acted differently, and Decedent ever spoke with anyone from EIDP. “[T]he elements of a cause of action for fraud based on concealment are: ‘(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. [Citation.]’” (Kaldenbach v. Mutual of Omaha Life Ins. Co. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 830, 850 (internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) “Each of the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presupposes the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … As a matter of common sense, such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties.” (Id. at pp. 336–337.)

 

“The requirement's purpose is to inform consumers about a product's hazards and faults of which they are unaware, so that they can refrain from using the product altogether or evade the danger by careful use.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) Users include persons responsible for processing materials not privy to information regarding necessary safety protocols. The withholding of said information subjects a defendant to potential fraudulent concealment liability. (Id. at pp. 1199-1200.)

 

The operative complaint presents allegations regarding knowledge of the allegedly toxic substance, crystalline silica, dangers to workers engaged in the cutting, grinding, fabrication, polishing, etc. with the product, and failure to disclose potential toxicity upon working with said products. Decedent was specifically exposed and harmed as a result of the non-disclosed hazards and continued work with the supplied product. The allegations meet the standard for fraudulent concealment. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78-79.)

 

Under the consideration for product liability summary judgment, the court finds insufficient address of this standard by EIDP for purposes of shifting the burden, and a general lack of argument other than conclusory contentions. EIDP’s citation to Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276 is misplaced as it is not a toxic tort case and hence does not grapple with the unique public policies at play in such cases. Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co., supra, a toxic tort case involving parties with a similar relationship to the parties at present, is controlling. (See Jones, supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at 1198-2000; LiMandri, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at 336; Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1192.)  The court therefore denies the motion for summary adjudication on both the failure to shift the burden, and triable issues of material fact regarding failure to warn on allegedly known hazards caused by fabrication of products containing silica. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Barbara Hannah; EIDP Appendix of Evidence: Exh. M.]

 

As to punitive damages, EIDP offers a conclusive challenge regarding the lack of evidence to support the claim. On punitive damages, Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c) authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, which are defined as follows:

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

[Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)
