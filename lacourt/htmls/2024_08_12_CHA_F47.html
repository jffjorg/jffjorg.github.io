DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 21STCV18579    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 8/12/24                                                                    TRIAL DATE: 9/9/24

Case #21STCV18579

 

MOTION TO COMPEL PERSON MOST QUALIFIED DEPOSITION

 

Motion filed on 7/15/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Valeria Segoviano Hernandez

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant The Clorox Company

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling the deposition of the Person(s) Most Qualified of Defendant The Clorox Company, within 10 days, and for the production of documents and electronic data demanded in the Notice of PMQ Deposition.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an award of sanctions against Clorox and its counsel of record in the amount of $5,610.00.

 

RULING: The motion is denied.  Defendant The Clorox Company’s request for sanctions is denied.      

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of an incident that occurred on 5/25/20 at a Walmart located in Stevenson Ranch, California.  On that date at the Walmart, Plaintiff Valeria Segoviano Hernandez  (Plaintiff) grabbed a bottle of Clorox bleach from the top shelf.  Plaintiff claims that the cap on the bottle was not secured causing it to spill all over her right arm causing her injury and permanent scarring. 

 

Plaintiff’s operative Third Amended Complaint against Defendant Walmart Inc. (Walmart) and Defendant The Clorox Company (Clorox), filed on 1/10/24, contains causes of action for: (1) Strict Liability – Design Defect (against Walmart, Clorox and Does 1-25), (2) Negligence – Failure to Recall (against Clorox and Does 1-25) and (3) Negligence (against Clorox, Walmart and Does 1-25).

 

On 1/10/24, Plaintiff served a Notice of Deposition to the Person Most Qualified (PMQ) for Clorox.  (Manetti Decl. ¶2, Ex.1).  On 1/17/24, Clorox produced its PMQ for deposition, subject to its responses and objections.  (Id., Ex.2).  On 6/5/24, Plaintiff served Clorox with another PMQ Notice of Deposition which contained new matters for examination.  (Manetti Decl. ¶3).  In response, Clorox’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel indicating that a second PMQ Notice/Deposition is not permitted in the Code of Civil Procedure and objecting to the substance of the four matters of examination as overbroad and not reasonably related to the subject of the action, etc.  (Id., Ex.3).  On 6/27/24, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel that deposition.  On 7/3/24, Plaintiff withdrew the second PMQ Deposition Notice and served a third Notice of Deposition of the PMQ of Clorox.  (Id. ¶4, Ex.4).  On 7/10/24, Clorox’s counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter and objections, again, indicating that a second PMQ deposition is not authorized and objecting on the grounds that the topics of examination are duplicative and seek testimony that had already been provided on 1/17/24 in response to the 1/10/24 PMQ notice. (Id. ¶5, Ex.5).  On 7/12/24, in response to Plaintiff’s counsel’s 7/11/24 inquiry, Clorox’s counsel confirmed that it would not be producing a witness in response to the 7/3/24 PMQ Notice and on 7/15/24 no PMQ witness was produced by Clorox.  (Eagan Decl. ¶¶4-5, Ex.3, 4).

 

On 7/15/24, Plaintiff filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling the deposition of the Person(s) Most Qualified of Defendant The Clorox Company, within 10 days, and for the production of documents and electronic data demanded in the Notice of PMQ Deposition.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an award of sanctions against Clorox and its counsel of record in the amount of $5,610.00.  Clorox has opposed the motion and has requested monetary sanctions.  Plaintiff has filed a reply to the opposition. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Without citing any authority, Plaintiff contends that “There Is No Limit on the Number of PMQ Depositions.”  (See Motion, p.8:19-24).  The Court finds Plaintiff’s position to be without merit. 

 

CCP 2025.010 provides:

 

“Any party may obtain discovery within the scope delimited by Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 2017.010), and subject to the restrictions set forth in Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 2019.010), by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. The person deposed may be a natural person, an organization such as a public or private corporation, a partnership, an association, or a governmental agency.”  (emphasis added)

     

CCP 2025.230 provides:

 

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”  (emphasis added)

 

Based on the foregoing language in the statutes, the Court finds that a party is entitled to notice one (i.e., the) oral deposition of the person(s) most qualified to testify on behalf of an organization/corporation, such as Clorox, on various topics and it is up to the organization to determine whether one or more persons are most qualified to answer questions on the topics identified in the deposition notice.  Under Plaintiff’s reasoning, a party could notice separate and unlimited depositions for each topic on which it seeks to question an organization possibly resulting in multiple depositions of the same individual, if that person is the most qualified to testify on multiple topics set forth in different PMQ deposition notices.  Such a consequence clearly contravenes the purpose behind CCP 2025.010 and CCP 2025.230.  As such, the Court finds that Plaintiff improperly noticed a second deposition of Clorox’s PMQ(s) without leave of court.  See CCP 2025.610(a)-(b). 

 

Even if Plaintiff had made this motion pursuant to CCP 2025.610(b) seeking a second deposition of Clorox’s PMQ(s), Plaintiff has failed to show the requisite good cause for a subsequent deposition.  Contrary to Plaintiff’s assertion the nine topics set forth in the 7/3/24 PMQ notice are substantively the same as those contained in the 1/10/24 PMQ notice on which Clorox produced a witness on 1/17/24.  (See Opposition, p.4:1-p.5:18).  Therefore, if Plaintiff was not satisfied with responses to any of the questions asked at the 1/17/24 deposition, Plaintiff was required to timely move to compel further responses to such questions under CCP 2025.480 which she has not done.  The fact that Plaintiff’s counsel may now wish to ask additional questions on topics which are substantively the same as the prior deposition topics does not constitute good cause for a second deposition.   

 

Additionally, the motion is procedurally defective in that it is not accompanied by a separate statement.  CRC 3.1345 provides that a separate statement is required for any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request.  See CRC 3.1345(a).  A separate statement is not required when no response has been provided to the discovery request.  CRC 3.1345(b).  Here, Clorox did provide a response (i.e., objections) to the discovery request/deposition notice.  (See Manetti Decl., Ex.6).  Plaintiff’s claim that a separate statement was not required because Clorox’s objections were not valid is without merit.  (See Motion, p.8:15-18; Reply, p.8:2-13).  Plaintiff cites no authority for the proposition that she, or her attorney, may act as the arbiter of what constitutes a “valid” objection.  Rather, it is for the Court to determine whether an objection to a discovery request is valid.  The foregoing conclusion is supported by CRC 3.1345(c)(2) and (3) which require that a separate statement accompanying a discovery motion include “[t]he text of each response, answer, or objection, and any further responses or answers” and “[a] statement of the factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses, answers, or production as to each matter in dispute.”  As noted above, the Court finds Clorox’s objection to the 7/3/24 deposition notice on the ground that Plaintiff already served a deposition notice for Clorox’s PMQ and Clorox produced a witness in response to same to be valid.      

 

Clorox has failed to provide proper notice as to against whom it seeks sanctions.  In the beginning of the opposition, Clorox states that sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel are warranted.  (See Opposition, p.2:7-9).  However, in the sanctions section and conclusion of the opposition, Clorox states that sanctions are appropriate against Plaintiff and/or her counsel and Plaintiff and her counsel, jointly and severally.  (See Opposition, p.12:5-6, p.12:20-23, p.13:1-3, p.13:6-7); See also CCP 2023.040.  Plaintiff also fails to specifically identify against which of Plaintiff’s counsel sanctions are warranted (i.e., MLG, APLC, Attorneys at Law or attorney Travis Eagan or both).  See CCP 2023.040.  Also, the Court finds the amount of sanctions sought ($5,395.00) by Clorox to be excessive.  Further, the Court notes that similar errors in a request for sanctions by Clorox have been previously pointed out by the Court and Clorox’s counsel has still failed to make a proper request.  (See 6/9/23 Minute Order).

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied.  Clorox’s request for sanctions is denied.    

 

The Court further notes that both Plaintiff and Clorox have failed to properly electronically bookmark the exhibits attached to the declarations filed in support of the motion and opposition as required.  See CRC 3.1110(f)(4).  The exhibits attached to the Eagan declaration are not bookmarked at all while only Exhibit 2 to the Manetti declaration is electronically bookmarked.  Counsel for both parties have repeatedly failed to comply with this requirement despite being  warned by the Court in prior rulings that the failure to comply with this rule in the future may result in papers not being considered, matters being continued so that papers may be re-submitted in compliance, and/or the imposition of sanctions.  (See 6/9/23 Minute Order; 6/5/24 Minute Order).  Once again, counsel are warned that failure to comply with this rule may result in papers not being considered, matters being continued and/or the imposition of sanctions.  

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

       

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 


Case Number: 23CHCV03744    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 8/12/24

Case #23CHCV03744

 

DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer filed on 7/15/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Hector Gramajo

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Green Bay Properties, LP

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the entire First Amended Complaint for unlawful detainer.

 

RULING: The demurrer is overruled.  Answer is due within 5 days.    

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

On 12/8/23, Plaintiff Green Bay Properties, LP (Plaintiff) filed this commercial unlawful detainer action against Defendants Hector Gramajo dba Swiss Cleaners (Hector) and Edwin Gramajo dba Swiss Cleaners (Edwin).  On 2/20/24, Plaintiff filed its operative First Amended Complaint for commercial unlawful detainer against the same parties. 

 

On 3/22/24, Edwin filed an answer.  On 5/23/24, Hector filed a motion to quash service of summons which was denied on 6/28/24.  (See 6/28/24 Minute Order).  On 7/15/24, Hector filed and served the instant demurrer to the entire First Amended Complaint pursuant to CCP 430.10 (with no subsection specified) on the ground that it “[f]ails to state that the Plaintiff has met the applicable requirements of the local ordinance (Complaint, p. 3, ¶16).”  (See Demurrer, p.1:26-p.2:3).  The demurrer was originally scheduled for hearing on 8/23/24 but was advanced to 8/12/24 by the Court on 7/31/24.  (See 7/31/24 Minute Order; Notice of Ruling filed 7/31/24).  Since an opposition to the demurrer had already been filed and served, the Court ordered a reply was due by 8/7/24.  Id.  As of 8/8/24, no reply has been filed.    

 

ANALYSIS

 

In addition to failing to specify the subsection of CCP 430.10 on which the demurrer is based, Hector has failed to identify the “local ordinance” Plaintiff has purportedly failed to allege it complied with.  Hector merely concludes that the “property is subject to protections of local rent control or eviction control ordinances.”  (See Demurrer, p.4:11-12).  However, Plaintiff has specifically alleged, by not marking number 16 on page 3 of the First Amended Complaint, that Hector’s tenancy is not subject to the local rent control or eviction control ordinance.  (See First Amended Complaint, p.3, No.16).

 

Hector has failed to establish that the commercial tenancy at issue in this action is subject to any local rent control of eviction control ordinance.  Even if such an ordinance does apply, the First Amended Complaint alleges sufficient facts to state a cause of action for unlawful detainer and Hector may plead the purported failure to comply with the unidentified applicable local ordinance as an affirmative defense. 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The demurer is overruled.  Answer is due within 5 days.
