DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV24281    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: T

Motion For An Order Deeming All Matters Contained In Defendant’s Requests For Admission, Set One, Admitted

Moving Party: Defendant University of Southern California (“USC”)
Responding Party: N/A

Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff, Julianne Lattner (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint on June 30, 2021 stating a cause of action of negligence. Plaintiff amended her complaint on May 1, 2024, naming Defendant as Doe 1. Defendant filed an Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint on June 21, 2024, and the same day served Requests for Admission (Set One) onto Plaintiff. Responses to Defendant’s Requests were due by July 23, 2024; Plaintiff failed to serve responses. (Stockalper Decl. ¶ 3). On July 26, 2024, defense counsel sent a correspondence to Plaintiff’s counsel and requested that Plaintiff’s responses, without objections, be provided within 10 days of the date of the letter. (Stockalper Decl. ¶ 4). Defendant has still not received Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Requests for Admission (Set One).

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues that all matters specified in its Requests for Admission be deemed admitted, due to Plaintiff’s failure to provide responses to Defendant’s timely provided Requests for Admission. Defendant argues Plaintiff’s delay has continuing prejudice on Defendant by preventing Defendant from identifying additional witnesses and forcing Defendant to conduct the remaining discovery necessary to prepare for trial. (Motion to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted p. 5.)

Defendant also argues the Court should impose sanctions in the amount of $735.00 based on three hours of work at a $225.00 per hour rate, in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Declaration of Patrick E. Stockalper ¶ 7.)

OPPOSITION

No opposition was filed in response to this motion. 

ANALYSIS

I. Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under CCP § 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).) CCP § 2033.280(c) states, in relevant part: “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.

If a party to whom requests for production of documents were directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2031.300(b)-(c).) CCP § 2031.300 contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. The propounding party need not demonstrate good cause or satisfy a meet-and-confer requirement – all that needs to be shown in the moving papers is that a set of requests for production of documents was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403-04.)

Here, it is appropriate to grant Defendant’s motion. Defendant served its Request for Admission (Set One) upon Plaintiff on June 21, 2024, who has yet to provide responses or answers to these requests for admission.  Plaintiff’s lack of opposition to Defendant’s motion provides the Court no reason to find substantial justification for the failure to respond. Accordingly, the Court will grant Defendant’s request to deem requests for admission as admitted.

II. Sanctions

Defendant requests $735.00 in sanctions against Plaintiff based on two hours of work in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Declaration of Patrick E. Stockalper ¶ 7.) The Court finds Defendant’s hourly fee is reasonable as well as the number of hours worked. The Court therefore awards $675.00 in attorney fees for three hours of work, in addition to the $60.00 filing fee, totaling $735.00. 

RULING

Defendant’s motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted is GRANTED. Defendant’s request for sanctions against Plaintiff is GRANTED in the amount of $735.00.

Case Number: 22STCV05716    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: T

Motion To Compel Plaintiff’s Responses

Moving Party: Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.
Responding Party: Plaintiff Alison Zatta 
Tentative Ruling: Granted in part

BACKGROUND

This is a negligence case arising from a car accident. On February 15, 2022, Plaintiff Alison Zatta (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant alleging motor vehicle and general negligence arising from a February 18, 2020 car accident, contending Defendant is liable for her alleged damages. (Declaration of Pragya Sharma (“PS Dec.”), ¶ 2.) Defendant contests Plaintiff’s allegations, and served Plaintiff with Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two, Requests For Admission, and Special Interrogatories (collectively “Discovery Requests”) on May 14, 2024, seeking information on Plaintiff’s injuries, medical expenses, and claim for loss of income or earning capacity. (PS Dec., ¶ ¶ 3, 4, Exhibits 1 to 3.) Plaintiff’s responses to Discovery Requests were due on June 15, 2024. (PS Dec., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff failed to serve responses to Defendant’s Discovery Requests; accordingly on July 18, 2024, Defendant’s counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel requesting an update on the status of Plaintiff’s responses to Discovery Requests. (PS Dec., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (PS Dec., ¶ 7.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues Plaintiff should be compelled to respond to Discovery Requests, as Plaintiff has yet to serve responses despite Defendant’s attempt to meet and confer.

Defendant further requests monetary sanctions be imposed on Plaintiff and/or his counsel in the amount of $560.00 total: $360.00 for the preparation and filing of this motion, and $200.00 in anticipated future related costs for the preparation of a reply brief and appearance at the hearing. (PS Dec., ¶ 13.) 

OPPOSITION

“[A] trial court has broad discretion to accept or reject late-filed papers. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d).)”  (Rancho Mirage Country Club Homeowners Assn. v. Hazelbaker (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 252, 262.)  A trial court does not abuse its discretion by refusing to consider late-filed papers when a party makes no attempt to seek leave to file papers late and does not attempt to show good cause for the late filing. (Ibid.; see also Samaniego v. Empire Today, LLC (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1138, 1146 [party “provided no plausible explanation for its tardiness and the court acted within its discretion when it denied leave to file them”].)

Plaintiff filed an untimely opposition on September 30, 2024, but offered no explanation as to why the opposition was not filed earlier.  The Court exercises its discretion to consider the opposition only to the extent that it establishes that verified responses were served prior to the time of the hearing on Defendant’ motions. 

ANALYSIS

I. Motions to Compel 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).) If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no requirement to meet and confer before filing a motion to compel responses. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 906 [“Where no objections have been made within the statutorily permitted time, they are deemed waived. There is thus nothing to ‘resolve’ . . . .”].

Here, Plaintiff has responded to all written discovery with the exception of Request for Production #15.  As such the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Request for Production #15.  

II. Sanctions

The party who propounded the discovery request may bring a motion to compel and the court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for production of documents or interrogatories, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

Here, Defendant requests $560.00 in sanctions against Plaintiff, including $360.00 for costs incurred to prepare Defendant’s motion and $200.00 in anticipated future costs pertaining to this motion. The Court finds the requested sanctions to be reasonable.   

RULING

The Motion to Compel is granted as to Request for Production #15.  

The request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff is GRANTED in the amount of $560.00.

Case Number: 22STCV12531    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Strike

Moving Party: Cross-Defendants Hector P Estrada; Adriana Estrada
Responding Party: Cross-Complainant Orion Transit, Inc.
Tentative Ruling: Granted in Part

BACKGROUND

This is a car accident case arising from a November 5, 2021 incident in which liability is disputed and each adverse party claims the other ran a red light. Plaintiff Victor Pulido was a passenger in a vehicle driven for Defendant/Cross-Complainant Orion Transit, Inc. (“Orion”.) On April 14, 2022, Plaintiff filed his complaint against Orion for personal injuries. Orion, in turn, filed a Cross-Complaint for equitable indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief, against Cross-Defendants on August 10, 2023. In the Cross-Complaint, Orion seeks an award of attorney’s fees from Cross-Defendants.

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Cross-Defendants argue the Court should strike Orion’s request for attorney’s fees, as no statutory basis to award of attorney’s fees to Orion applies to the circumstances presented and no contract exists between the parties to provide a basis for Orion to recover attorney’s fees. (Motion to Strike p. 7.)

The portions of text of Cross-Defendants seek to strike from the Cross-Complaint include:

1. Paragraph 9, page 3, line 25: “all legal costs and fees, including attorneys’ fees, incurred”;
2. Paragraph 14, page 4, lines 19-21: “Additionally, Cross-Complainant has incurred and will continue to incur attorneys' fees and costs in defending such claims and is entitled to be reimbursed for these attorneys' fees and costs.”; 
3. Paragraph 20, page 5, lines 19 -20: “reasonable attorneys' fees incurred and expended by Cross-Complainant in defending the action”; 
4. Paragraph 21, page 5, lines 27- 28: “to reimburse Cross-Complainant for reasonable attorneys' fees and costs”; 
5. Paragraph 5, page 6, lines 19-20: “For attorneys' fees incurred by Cross-Complainant in the defense of this action”. 
(Id. at 4.)

OPPOSITION

Orion argues Cross-Defendants’ Motion seeks to strike allowable costs other than attorney fees including “all legal costs and fees, including attorneys' fees, incurred.” (Motion to Strike p. 4.) Cross-Defendants thus seeks to strike items that are recoverable, without justification. (Opposition p. 3.)

REPLY

Cross-Defendants argue the motion only seeks to strike portions of the Cross-Complaint that purport entitlement to compensation for attorneys’ fees, and that the motion identifies these passages with sufficient specificity.

ANALYSIS

A court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” Under CCP § 436(a), “[t]he court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper . . . [s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” Under CCP § 436(b), the court may “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” 

Attorney fees are not recoverable unless a fee award is expressly authorized by either statute or the parties' contract. (Real Property Services Corp. v. City of Pasadena (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 375, 379, 30 Cal.Rptr.2d 536.)

Here, Cross-Complainant fails to allege a statutory or contractual basis to request attorney fees in this action.  The Court thus finds Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike is appropriate as to attorney fees.  However, prevailing party costs are recoverable.

RULING

Cross-Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED IN PART. The Court strikes references to “attorneys’ fees” included in Paragraph 9 at page 3, line 25; Paragraph 14 at page 4, lines 19-21; Paragraph 20 at page 5, lines 19 -20; Paragraph 21 at page 5, lines 27- 28; and Paragraph 5 at page 6, lines 19-20 of the Cross-Complaint.
