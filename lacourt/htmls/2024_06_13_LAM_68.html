Case Number: 22STCV33658    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 6-13-24

Case #: 22STCV33658

Trial Date: 1-9-25 c/f 6-24-24

 

FURTHER DOCUMENTS

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, Samuel Welch

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Derrick Robinson

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiffs were tenants of a single family welling at 32270 Saticoy Street, West Hills, and allege unsanitary and/or unsafe conditions on the premises as a result of improper maintenance and upkeep.

 

On October 17, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their complaint for Failure to Provide Habitable Dwellings, Breach of Covenant of Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property, Nuisance, and Negligence. Defendants answered the complaint on February 23, 2023.

 

RULING: Granted.

Defendant, Samuel Welch moves to compel further responses to request for production of documents, numbers 4, 9, and 12 from Plaintiff Derrick Robinson. The responses consist of references to other items.

 

Such responses constitute an improper, factually incomplete answer. Responding Party may not refer to prior discovery. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783–784 [“Answers must be complete and responsive. Thus, it is not proper to answer by stating, ‘See my deposition’, ’See my pleading’, or ‘See the financial statement’”].)

 

The motion is therefore granted. Responding is ordered to serve responses in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.210-2031.240. No sanctions requested.

 

The court calendar shows eight remaining, scheduled motions to compel further responses scheduled through July 22, 2024The motions appear as a continuation of discovery issues addressed by the court in the 23 motions to compel responses granted on November 22, 2023.

 

The court does not conduct any informal discovery conferences. While the court understands the prior use and perhaps reliance on the IDC system, the court finds the number of items on the court calendar through the next three months presents a potentially inordinate burden. The court reserves the right to set an OSC re: Discovery Referee in lieu of a hearing on any given motion, and may take off any and all motions in lieu of the OSC hearing. The court invites the parties to continue meeting and conferring, including the provision of supplemental responses, when possible.

 

Next motions to compel further responses to request for production of documents and form interrogatories from James Richardson scheduled for June 4 and 6, 2024, with the next set addressing Noah Penn-El beginning on July 2, 2024.

 

Defendant to give notice.

Dept. 68

Date: 6-13-24 c/f 5-29-24 (3-25-24 stipulation and order)

Case #23STCV10162

Trial Date: Not Set

 

QUASH EMPLOYMENT RECORDS SUBPOENA

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff, Alexis Craney-Foster

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant, Skyhop Global, LLC

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Quash Subpoena for Records from XVI Global Solutions

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Alexis Craney-Foster was an employee of defendant SkyHop Global LLC from January 8, 2021, through September 27, 2022, as an airport shuttle driver for airport employees. During the course of employment, Plaintiff alleges defendant Timothy Boykin began a course of sexually inappropriate comments and touching. On June 5, 2021, Boykin visited her home, where he non-consensually engaged in sexual intercourse with Plaintiff while Plaintiff was intoxicated and incapacitated in her home after normal work hours.

5. Any and all documents from January 1, 2022, to the present, which you received from any other doctor, psychiatrist, psychologist, hospital, or other health care provider or health care facility, which concern, reference, or otherwise relate to CRANEY-FOSTER.

6. Any and all documents relating to CRANEY-FOSTER’s ability to work, including any documents reflecting or relating to any medical restrictions or leaves of absence from January 1, 2022, to the present.

7. All documents which YOU used in YOUR evaluation and/or analysis of the medical condition and/or mental health of CRANEY-FOSTER from January 1, 2022, to the present. 9. All documents, including, but not limited to, sign-in sheets, bills or other billing information, which concern, reference or otherwise relate to CRANEY-FOSTER from January 1, 2022, to the present.”

 

[Agopian Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. 8: Deposition Subpoena.]

 

“[T]he mere filing of a lawsuit does not trigger application of the patient-litigant exception.” (Patterson v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 927, 930.) A patient may continue to exercise a reasonable expectation of privacy of their medical information if the information is not directly relevant to a particular condition the patient placed in issue. (California Consumer Health Care Council v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 21, 31.) “‘The patient thus is not obligated to sacrifice all privacy to seek redress for a specific mental or emotional injury; the scope of the inquiry permitted depends upon the nature of the injuries which the patient-litigant himself has brought before the court.’” (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864.) “‘The party seeking the constitutionally protected information has the burden of establishing that the information sought is directly relevant to the claims. [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (See Barrenda L. v. Superior Court (1998) 65 Cal. App. 4th 794, 801-802.) The standard for good cause requires the moving party to produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) “[A]n implicit waiver of a party's constitutional rights encompasses only discovery directly relevant to the plaintiff's claim and essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit… [¶] … On occasion her privacy interests may have to give way to her opponent's right to a fair trial. Thus courts must balance the right of civil litigants to discover relevant facts against the privacy interests of persons subject to discovery.” (Id. at p. 842.)

 

Plaintiff’s medical emotional medical state was placed in issue through the filing of the subject action and damages claim, thereby providing SkyHop with the right to examine Plaintiff’s medical history prior to, during and after the time of the incident(s). [Agopian Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.] It is presumed from the discovery responses that at least some of the subject records directly relate to the anguish caused by the underlying incidents. Plaintiff seeks to limit production, but the argument itself lacks specificity as to a means for separating potentially unrelated consultation and treatment. (In re Lifschutz (1970) 2 Cal.3d 415, 439; Patterson v. Superior Court, supra, 147 Cal.App.3d at pp. 931-932; see Evid. Code, § 1016, subd. (a).)

 

The subpoena itself seeks information beginning in January 2022, the time of Plaintiff’s employment, through the present. Plaintiff maintains continuing suffering post employment thereby rendering the time frame reasonable and relevant. Plaintiff was given the option of dismissing the emotional distress claims, and elected to proceed. The court therefore finds a compelling basis for production in that no reasonable alternatives for discovery of the information exists.

 

The motion to quash the subpoena is therefore DENIED. Plaintiff makes no alternative request for a protective order, though the court finds no basis for limiting production either way. Nevertheless, the parties may seek to limit records to claims relating directly the subject action (e.g. subsequent to the incident), but may include prior records for purposes of establishing a baseline and noticeable changes following the course of events, if applicable.

 

SkyHop to arrange for service, compliance and production of the records pursuant to this order denying the motion for protective order and effectively allowing production pursuant to the subpoenas.

 

Neither party makes any request for sanctions. “[I]n making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section 1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2.)

RULING: Granted in Part/Denied in Part.

Plaintiff Craney-Foster moves to quash the deposition subpoena served by Defendant SkyHop Global, LLC (SkyHop) seeking employment records from third party Star of CA LLC – A Stepping Stone Group Co. Plaintiff contends the records sought are overbroad, infringe on privacy rights, and seek irrelevant information. SkyHop in opposition maintains the records are relevant for purposes of examining credibility in subject claims based on potential issues with prior employers, such as claims for accommodation, “preexisting conditions or alternative stressors.” SkyHop further maintains the subpoena is narrowly tailored. Plaintiff in reply reiterates the overbroad position in that the subpoena fails to limit discovery to the claims within the instant action, thereby invading privacy rights.

 

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness … the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), … may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

 

The subpoena itself was the result of the identification of the subject entity as the (current) employer following SkyHop. [Declaration of Regina Agopian, ¶¶ 4-6.] Following identification of the employer, SkyHop served a subpoena for employment records. SkyHop maintains the sought after records reflect on the credibility of the employment claims.

 

1. All documents CRANEY-FOSTER submitted to YOU expressing interest in employment, inquiring about employment, applying for employment, and/or accepting employment, including but not limited to LinkedIn communications with recruiters, email inquiry, a cover letter / email, a formal application, resume, and signed offer letter.

2. Documents sufficient to show the earliest contact YOU had with CRANEYFOSTER regarding employment with YOU, including but not limited to recruiting efforts and the date of such contact.

3. Documents reflecting any representations or statements CRANEY-FOSTER made to you regarding her past work experience and/or employment with YOU, including but not limited to cover letters and YOUR notes of interviews with CRANEY-FOSTER.

“Privacy interests generally fall into one of two categories: (1) an interest in making intimate personal decisions or conducting personal activities without observation, intrusion, or interference (‘autonomy privacy’); and (2) an interest in precluding the dissemination or misuse of sensitive and confidential information (‘informational privacy’).” (Ortiz v. Los Angeles Police Relief Assn. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1288, 1301.) The objecting party has the burden to file evidence of the preliminary facts establishing a privilege exists. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557; HLC Properties, Limited v. Sup. Ct. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 54, 59.)

 

Notwithstanding the burden on Plaintiff to establish the privacy protection, Plaintiff only offers a conclusive argument for privacy without specific support. An argument can be made for personnel records subject to privacy protections, to the extent employment records reflect autonomous protections, (see El Dorado Savings & Loan Assn. v. Superior Court (1987) 190 Cal.App.3d 342, 345 disapproved of in part by Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531.)

 

The standard for good cause requires the moving party to produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) “[A]n implicit waiver of a party's constitutional rights encompasses only discovery directly relevant to the plaintiff's claim and essential to the fair resolution of the lawsuit… [¶] … On occasion her privacy interests may have to give way to her opponent's right to a fair trial. Thus courts must balance the right of civil litigants to discover relevant facts against the privacy interests of persons subject to discovery.” (Id. at p. 842.)

 

Nevertheless, the Court finds certain issues pertaining to plaintiff’s performance and the reasons for her separation meet the discoverability standard and are not overbroad. The Court therefore denies the motion on request nos. 5 – 9, 12, and 13. Said documents pertaining to those categories must be produced. SkyHop to arrange for service, compliance and production of the records pursuant to this order denying the motion for protective order and effectively allowing production pursuant to the subpoenas regarding the specific categories only.

However, the Court grants the motion to quash as to the remaining category numbers (i.e., 1 – 4, 10, and 11.) The court finds the lack of a nexus between the purported conduct underpinning the present action with any potential issues several years before employment with SkyHop regarding those subject categories. Even if SkyHop presented a more founded basis, the court finds no showing regarding the lack of available alternatives given the identified key factors. SkyHop concurrently seeks medical information, which could presumably offer information regarding preexisting conditions, stressors or other (medically) based needs for accommodation. The motion to quash the subpoena is therefore DENIED in Part and GRANTED in part.

Neither party makes any request for sanctions. “[I]n making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section 1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2.)
