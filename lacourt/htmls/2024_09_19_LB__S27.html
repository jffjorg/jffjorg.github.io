DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 19STCV30280    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On August 23, 2019, Plaintiffs Kristin Hirai, individually and as successor-in-interest to her deceased husband Toshio Harai; Takeo Harai, Kristen and Toshio’s son, through Kristin as his guardian ad litem; Isao Hirai; and Brigitta Hirai sued twelve (12) defendants for seven (7) causes of action arising from an automobile-motorcycle collision that resulted in Toshio’s death.

The Court uses the plaintiffs’ and the decedent’s first names where necessary solely for clarity, not out of familiarity or disrespect.

Plaintiffs filed their operative second amended complaint (“SAC”) on August 28, 2023, naming (1) the City of Los Angeles, (2) the State of California (CalTrans), (3-5) the driver and owners of the vehicle that struck and killed Toshio (Linda, Lonnie, and Tanesha Phillips).

It is undisputed that Toshio died in the course of his employment as a police officer for the City of Gardena (“the City”); he was on his police motorcycle when he was struck and killed. Plaintiffs argue in the instant motion that the City’s negligence contributed to Toshio’s death because his motorcycle’s brakes were not properly maintained. However, Plaintiffs at no point named the City as a defendant.

Plaintiffs settled with CalTrans for $5 million, the City of Los Angeles for $1 million, and the Phillips for $100,000, for a total recovery of $6.1 million. (Clayton Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

2.     Legal Standard

Where a public employee pays into CalPERS, the employee may be entitled to benefits upon his injury or death. In this circumstance, Government Code section 20252 applies:

“If benefits are payable under this part because of an injury to or the death of a member and the injury or death is the proximate consequence of the act of a person other than his or her employer (the state or the employing contracting agency), the board may on behalf of this system recover from that person an amount that is the lesser of the following:

(1) An amount that is equal to one-half of the actuarial equivalent of the benefits for which this system is liable because of such injury or death.

(2) An amount that is equal to one-half of the remaining balance of the amount recovered after allowance of that amount that the employer or its insurance carrier have paid or become obligated to pay.”

(Gov. Code, § 20252.)

 

3.     Discussion

a. CalPERS has an enforceable lien on the settlement proceeds.

Plaintiffs assert CalPERS cannot assert a lien on Kristin’s recovery because the City, Toshio’s employer, was a proximate cause of his death, and section 20252 only permits CalPERS to recover where “the injury or death is the proximate consequence of the act of a person other than his or her employer”. (Gov. Code, § 20252.) Plaintiffs cite McQuillan v. Southern Pacific Co. (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 802 (McQuillan) to argue that where an employer makes any proximate contribution to a covered employee’s injury, CalPERS may not recover.

Even accepting Plaintiffs’ reading of McQuillan as correct, they have not shown the exception applies here. Plaintiffs acknowledge that in McQuillan, a jury determined the plaintiffs’ employer was partly liable. They wave away this crucial distinction by arguing that “there is no statutory or case authority that requires there be a ‘finding’ of employer negligence to bar [a] CalPERS subrogation claim.” (Reply, 2:25-28.)

Some “finding” of employer negligence is necessary for the Court to apply the exception in section 22052. The Court will not bar CalPERS’ multiple-hundred-thousand dollar recovery based on a handful of declarations and exhibits, none of which amount to a showing of negligence.

Plaintiffs argue that if the Court adopts CalPERS’ argument, then “ever[y] case in which CalPERS asserts a subrogation claim would necessarily have to go to verdict regardless of the desire of the parties to settle.” (Reply, 2:28-3:2.) Not so. In fact, case law acknowledges that parties to a settlement that implicates CalPERS subrogation are free to account for the adjusted recovery, or not. (E.g. Aetna Casualty & Surety Co. v. Board of Administration (1979) 89 Cal.App.3d 766 [CalPERS not bound by release in settlement to which it is not a party].)

Plaintiffs have not established that they are exempt from the application of section 22052.

b. Plaintiffs have not identified any legal doctrine that reduces CalPERS’ subrogation rights past a calculation under section 20252.

The parties also dispute the amount to which CalPERS is entitled. CalPERS contends it is entitled to $656,250.75 from Kristin’s settlement funds, based on the method stated in Government Code section 202252(1).

Plaintiff contends that CalPERS’ recovery should be reduced beyond its proposed amount, on several bases.

First, Plaintiffs argue CalPERS should recover less because Toshio paid into the CalPERS fund. But the plain language of section 20252 applies to all “members” of the CalPERS program. (Gov. Code, § 20252.) Section 20281 states that a State employee “becomes a member upon his or her entry into employment.” (Id., § 20281.) Section 20252 applies to Toshio and his survivors; his payment into the fund is irrelevant (and, in fact, mandatory). Plaintiffs also rely solely on McQuillan, supra, for their proposition. And as discussed above, McQuillan is distinguishable in a number of crucial respects. (See McQuillan, supra, 40 Cal.App.3d, at pp. 806-807; accord Bilyeu v. State Emp. Retirement System (1962) 58 Cal.2d 618, 622-623 [application of 20252 and payment into fund are separate, bargained-for terms of employment contract].)

Second, Plaintiff contends Kristin has not been “made whole” by her settlement, and that should affect CalPERS’ recovery. There is no reference to being “made whole” in section 20252. The Court would need to make sweeping factual findings about the extent of Kristin’s injury in order to determine that she recovered less than what would make her whole. And again, case law – including McQuillan – suggests the Court should not make such judgments when applying section 202252. (See McQuillan, supra, 40 Cal.App.3d 802, 807 [inconsequential that plaintiffs might doubly recover survivor benefits and damages].)

Plaintiffs have not shown either of theories requires a change to the calculation method clearly stated in section 202252.

c. The Court’s arithmetic confirms CalPERS’ calculation.

Section 20252 permits CalPERS to recover the lesser of one-half of (1) the “actuarial equivalent” of death benefits it will pay to Plaintiffs over their lifetimes as a result of the third party’s tort, or (2) the “remaining balance of the amount recovered” after subtracting any amount paid by Plaintiffs’ employer or its insurer.

/ / /

/ / /

            i. Actuarial Equivalent of Lifetime Benefits

For purposes of section 20252, “ ‘[a]ctuarial equivalent’ means a benefit of equal value when computed upon the basis of the mortality tables adopted and the actuarial interest rate fixed by the [Board of Administration]” for CalPERS. (Gov. Code, § 20013.) CalPERS submits the declaration of one of its Actuary Seniors, Wingcheong Lee, who attests that he calculated the value of Kristin’s benefit based on the relevant mortality tables and interest rate and arrived at a lifetime benefit of $1,312,501.50 at the time of Toshio’s death. (Lee Decl., ¶ 5 and Exh. A.) The $1.3 million result “considered [Toshio’s] salary, his age at the time of his death, the amount [he] had contributed to the retirement system ..., the age of his surviving spouse, the age of his surviving child, and life expectancies, among other factors.”

One-half of that proposed recovery is $656,250.75.

            ii. Remaining Balance of Amount Recovered

Alternatively, the Court could base the calculation on half of Kristin’s total recovery. No adjustment for employer payment is needed, because there is no evidence the City of Gardena paid Plaintiffs anything. The total recovery for Plaintiffs is $6.1 million. Adjusting for attorneys’ fees and costs, Plaintiffs recover a net amount of $3,490,745.38. Adjusting for Kristin’s 65% portion, she recovers a net $2,268,984.50. One-half of that amount is approximately $1.1 million.

In its calculation, CalPERS then further reduces the amount of Kristin’s recovery based on CalPERS’ share of attorneys’ fees (because CalPERS did not assist Plaintiffs with the litigation). But whether or not the calculation is adjusted for fees (either once or twice), the result exceeds the calculation under section 20252(1), so the Court adopts the former amount.

iii. Compromise Discussions

Plaintiffs argue in part that CalPERS’ counsel stipulated to a lien of only $525,356.19. (Reply, 6:13-17, citing Mot., Exh. 5.) After reviewing counsel’s email exchange, the Court finds that even if CalPERS’ counsel made a firm offer to compromise, and even if evidence of that offer is admissible in support of Plaintiffs’ motion, there is no evidence that Plaintiffs accepted the offer. In fact, this motion confirms they rejected it. Plaintiffs cannot rely on a rejected compromise proposal to limit CalPERS’ relief.

iv. Final Result

According to the undisputed declaration of Mr. Lee, one-half of the actuarial equivalent of benefits to be paid to Kristin – the recovery described in section 202252(1) – equals $656,250.75. CalPERS’ recovery under section 20252(2) would be higher, so the lesser calculation under subdivision (1) applies. CalPERS’ lien amounts to $656,250.75.

 

4.     Conclusion

Having reviewed the moving papers, opposition, and reply, the Court determines that CalPERS may recover $656,250.75 from the proceeds of Plaintiffs’ settlement.

1.     Motion to Tax Costs

Plaintiff moves to strike or tax costs detailed in Defendants, Ramin J. Javahery, M.D., Glenn Levine, M.D., and Mundi Attinasi, M.D.’s 2/5/24 memorandum of costs. She contends Defendants’ §998 offer was not in good faith and cannot form the basis of a request for costs. Further, she moves to strike additional unnecessary costs. 

 

2.     Opposition

Defendants filed timely opposition papers on 9/6/24. Defendants argue that Plaintiff provides no evidence in support of her motion. Defendants contend each item of costs was actually and necessarily incurred, and its §998 was made in good faith and not a token under the circumstances. Defendants ask the Court to deny the motion to tax costs in its entirety. 

 

3.     Law Governing Motions to Tax Costs

Allowable costs under CCP §1033.5 must be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation, and must be reasonable in amount.  An item not specifically allowable under §1033.5(a) nor prohibited under subdivision (b) may nevertheless be recoverable in the discretion of the court if they meet the above requirements (i.e., reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount).  If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.  (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774.)  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.  (Ibid.)  Whether a cost item was reasonably necessary to the litigation presents a question of fact for the trial court and its decision is reviewed for abuse of discretion.  (Ibid.)  However, because the right to costs is governed strictly by statute, a court has no discretion to award costs not statutorily authorized. (Ibid.)  Discretion is abused only when, in its exercise, the court “exceeds the bounds of reason, all of the circumstances being considered.” 

 

4.     Items in Memorandum of Costs

a.     Expert Fees

Defendants seek to recover $8,700 (worksheet, ¶8) in expert witness fees.  CCP §1033.5(b) precludes recovery of expert witness fees unless the expert was ordered by the court or such fees are expressly recoverable per law.   The exception to this rule is CCP §998, which permits recovery of expert fees if the defendant makes a statutory offer to compromise, the plaintiff refuses the offer, and the defendant ultimately achieves a better result than the offer.  Plaintiff concedes this is the case but contends the Court should tax the expert fees despite §998 because the offer was “token” and not made in good faith. 

 

The purpose of CCP §998 is to encourage the settlement of litigation without trial. To effectuate this purpose, a §998 offer must be made in good faith. (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1262.)  A “token” or “nominal” offer may not satisfy the “good faith” requirement and so may not be the basis for an award of expert witness fees and costs penalties under §998(c). (Arno v. Helinet Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1019, 1024-1025.)  “The offer must carry with it some reasonable prospect of acceptance. One having no expectation that his or her offer will be accepted will not be allowed to benefit from a no-risk offer made for the sole purpose of later recovering large expert witness fees.” People ex rel. (Lockyer v. Fremont Gen. Corp. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1271.)

 

If the defendant prevails at trial, or the verdict is less than defendant's §998 offer, that fact constitutes prima facie evidence that the offer was reasonable. The burden is therefore on the offeree to prove the offer was in “bad faith.” (Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1484.)

 

Defendants herein made a statutory offer to compromise of $25,000.  Additionally, if Plaintiff had accepted the offer, Defendants would not have recovered their costs, which were already substantial at that time.  Plaintiff makes no meaningful argument in her moving papers in support of her position that the offer was token and/or not in good faith. Defendants had the case reviewed by experts whose opinions were supportive, and each was prepared to file a motion for summary judgment. As such, at the time Defendants served their offers for waiver of costs and malicious prosecution, they concluded they had a significant likelihood of prevailing at trial. Accepting the offers would have allowed Plaintiff to avoid the dispositive ruling ultimately entered against her. 

 

The Court finds the offer was not token and was made in good faith, and the motion to tax expert costs is denied.

 

b.     Deposition Fees

Defendants seek to recover $762 in costs associated with transcribing the deposition of Plaintiff.  Plaintiff argues this cost was not reasonable because the deposition was unnecessary, unwarranted, and not utilized. Further, the costs are not recoverable pursuant to CCP § 1033.5(a)(3). Lastly, Plaintiff argues that no documentation for this cost was provided.

It is common knowledge that a deposition is necessary to litigation and that failing to depose the plaintiff in a civil action would be extremely poor practice. Further, the costs of taking, videotaping and transcribing depositions are specifically allowable pursuant to CCP §1033.5(a)(3). Lastly, Defendants need not submit documentation with their memorandum of costs. The request to tax this cost is denied.

 

c.     Service of Process

Plaintiff moves to strike the costs of $477 in service of process fees for lack of invoices provided to support this cost. In opposition, Defendants provide the invoice showing the total of $477 for service of process. (Masutani Decl., ¶2, Exh. A.) Accordingly, the motion to tax this cost is denied.

 

d.     Court Reporter Fees

Plaintiff moves to strike the costs of $2,425. in court reporter fees for lack of invoices provided to support this cost. Court reporter fees are an allowable cost as established by statute under CCP §1033.5(a)(11). Defendants make no argument or mention of this cost in their opposition. Thus, the Court is unable to determine if this cost was reasonable. Accordingly, the motion to tax this cost is granted.

 

e.     Jury Fees

Plaintiff moves to strike the costs of $150 in jury fees but makes no argument as to this item.  Accordingly, the motion to tax this cost is denied.

 

f.      Filing Fees for Motions

Plaintiff moves to strike the costs of $3,092.85 in filing and motion fees but makes no argument as to this item.  Accordingly, the motion to tax this cost is denied.

 

g.     Conclusion

The motion to tax the costs for court reporter fees is granted.  The motion to tax costs is otherwise denied.

Case Number: 22LBCP00448    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff Roger Kavigan sued defendant Cody Mattingly on December 1, 2022, for (1) civil harassment (Code Civ. Proc., § 527.6), (2) trespass, and (3) declaratory and injunctive relief.

As alleged in Plaintiff’s complaint: Defendant contacted him on an online dating site in November 19, 2022; later that day, Defendant entered Plaintiffs’ property without permission and falsely, and loudly, accused him of having sexual relations with minors. Defendant was not a minor. Defendant recorded his interaction with Plaintiff and posted it to the Internet along with Plaintiff’s personal information. Plaintiff’s employer placed him on administrative leave on November 21, 2022. Plaintiff sued ten days later.

The parties tried the case before the Court over three days in June and July 2024. On July 18, 2024, the Court entered judgment for Defendant on the first cause of action for civil harassment, but for Plaintiff on the second cause of action for trespass, awarding Plaintiff $10,000.00 to compensate him for emotional distress.

On July 11, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for attorneys’ fees. Plaintiff filed his opposition on August 26, 2024. Defendant filed no reply.

2. Legal Standard

California applies the “American Rule” to litigation fees and costs: a prevailing party to litigation is entitled to recover costs from its opponent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032(b).) But a prevailing party is presumptively not entitled to recover attorneys’ fees. (See id., § 1033.5(a)(10).)

Notwithstanding the default rule, a prevailing party may recover fees only if specially authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Ibid.) If a prevailing party establishes that contract, statute or law entitles it to fees, the court may award fees “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation” and “reasonable in amount”. (Id., § 1033.5(c)(2)-(3).)

3. Discussion

a. Defendant is not entitled to fees under the anti-SLAPP statute.

As an initial matter, the Court disposes of what it assumes is a typographical error. Defendant asserts in passing in his moving papers that he may recover under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, California’s “anti-SLAPP” statute. (See Mot., 3:12-16.) Section 425.16 was never raised in this case and is irrelevant to this motion.

b. Defendant may recover fees under section 527.6.

Code of Civil Procedure section 527.6 permits “[a] person who has suffered [civil] harassment” to “seek a temporary restraining order and an order after hearing prohibiting harassment”. (Code Civ. Proc., § 527.6(a)(1).) Subdivision (s) states that “[t]he prevailing party in an action brought pursuant to [section 527.6] may be awarded court costs and attorney’s fees, if any.” (Id., subd. (s).)

“[D]etermination of the prevailing party [for purposes of section 527.6] lies in the trial court’s sound discretion.” (Elster v. Friedman (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1439, 1443-1444 [interpreting predecessor section 527(h) by reference to CCP sections 1021.5 and 1032].) “A plaintiff prevails ... when he gets most or all of what he wanted by filing the action. ‘A plaintiff will be considered a prevailing party when the lawsuit “ ‘was a catalyst motivating defendants to provide the primary relief sought’ ” or succeeded in “ ‘activating defendants to modify their behavior.’ ” ’ ” (Ibid., internal citations omitted.)

If the primary relief Plaintiff sought was a monetary judgment, then it was his trespass claim, not his civil harassment claim, that secured it for him. If the primary relief he sought was discontinuance of Defendant’s harassment, the relief was unnecessary. The Court found in its judgment in part that “Defendant has not returned to the Plaintiff’s property”. (07-18-2024 Judgment, ¶ 3.) No injunctive relief was necessary because there was no need to induce Defendant to modify his behavior going forward.

Plaintiff brought a claim under section 527.6, and Defendant prevailed; Defendant is entitled to fees according to section 527.6.

c. The Court reduces Defendant’s fees considerably because Defendant’s claim is excessive.

            Defendant seeks $78,648.50 in attorney’s fees, plus another $8,400.00 for this motion, plus $5,443.24 in costs, for a total award of $92,491.74.

“[T]he starting point of every fee award ... must be a calculation of the attorney's services in terms of the time he has expended on the case. Anchoring the analysis to this concept is the only way of approaching the problem that can claim objectivity, a claim which is obviously vital to the prestige of the bar and the courts.”  (In re Vitamin Cases (2003) 110 Cal. App. 4th 1041, 1058, citation omitted.) The determination of reasonable amount of attorney fees is within the sound discretion of trial courts. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095; Akins v. Enterprise Rent-A-Car Co. (2000) 79 Cal. App. 4th 1127, 1134.) The Court “need not simply award the sum requested.” (Id.)

“[T]he primary method for establishing the amount of ‘reasonable’ attorney fees is the lodestar method. The lodestar (or touchstone) is produced by multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by counsel by a reasonable hourly rate.” (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2001) 92 Cal. App. 4th 819, 833.) Under this approach, a base amount is calculated from a compilation of time reasonably spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48; Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 639.)

Counsel’s $525 hourly rate is reasonable for an attorney of his experience in the Los Angeles legal market. But the hours Defendant seeks to recover for are unreasonably inflated.

The parties tried the case to the Court over three days, totaling approximately twelve (12) hours of court time. There was no extensive motion practice in this case. But Defendant claims his counsel spent a surprising nearly one hundred fifty (150) hours on the case, plus sixteen (16) hours to prepare this single motion for fees.

“[R]easonableness of attorney fees is ... to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) Parties may only recover fees “ ‘for all hours reasonably spent ... .’ [Citation.]” (Meister v. Regents of University of California (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 437, 448-449.)

Crucially here, “[a] fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether. [Footnote.] ‘If ... the Court were required to award a reasonable fee when an outrageously unreasonable one has been asked for, claimants would be encouraged to make unreasonable demands, knowing that the only unfavorable consequence of such misconduct would be reduction of their fee to what they should have asked for in the first place. To discourage such greed, a severer reaction is needful... .’ [Citation.]” (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635.)

Defendant is entitled to fees, but he seeks an extraordinarily inflated amount. The Court in its discretion cuts his requested award by approximately two-thirds to account for the unreasonable request, and to ensure an award of only those hours reasonably spent. The Court makes the same adjustment to Counsel’s legal secretary’s billing (8 hours at $175.00), cutting the secretary’s time to 2.5 hours.

Based on this adjustment, the Court awards fees totaling $26,687.50 (50 hours x $525.00 hourly, 2.5 hours x $175.00 hourly).

d. Costs

“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 enumerates certain costs that are presumptively allowable, other presumptively not allowable, and leaves those unenumerated to the discretion of the trial court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(a)-(b).) Regardless of whether a cost is expressly allowable, it may only be awarded if it is “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation”, and it must be “reasonable in amount.” (Id., subd. (c)(2)-(3).)

Defendant seeks three categories of costs: (1) filing fees, (2) deposition costs, and (3-5) costs of preparing trial binders, incurred at Office Depot, Staples, and Amazon.

The former two categories are awardable and reasonable. (Id., subds. (a)(1) [filing fees], (a)(3)(A) [deposition costs], (c)(3) [“reasonable in amount”].) The Court grants the request.

As to the third category, trial binder preparation: Defendant claims a total of $2,344.44 spent on binders, tabs, and copying. (Sattro Decl., 4:25-5:2.) The Court finds the amount only partly awardable. Specifically, $1,154.98 of Counsel’s costs are only evidenced by a debit card transaction record and a receipt that refers to the cost as “JDA GMILL ORDE” [sic]. (See Sattro Decl., Exh. F.) Particularly given Counsel’s otherwise inflated request, he has not demonstrated this ambiguous charge is reasonable or necessary. The Court strikes it in its entirety.

The Court finds the remaining $973.08 unreasonable solely for the preparation of exhibit binders, particularly where supplemental purchases were made from Staples and Amazon. (Id., Exhs. G-H.) As with the fee award, the Court cuts the Office Depot charge only by two thirds, to a total of $324.36.

The Court therefore awards costs as follows: $924.10 in filing fees, $2,174.00 in deposition costs, and $540.74 for preparation of trial binders and exhibits, for a total of $3,638.34.

 

4. Conclusion

The Court grants Defendant’s motion in part. Defendant is awarded $26,687.50 in fees and $3,638.34 in costs, for a total of $30,326.34.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Bernald Rock filed this action against Defendants, Patricia J. Danis, Successor Trustee of the Jean L. Davis Revocable Living Trush, Sati AH, Inc., Castlehead, Inc., Jona Howe, and Vashti Clisby for damages arising out of a real estate purchase agreement.

Cross-Complainant Sati Ah Inc. (“Sati”) filed a Cross-Complaint (“XC”) against Cross-Defendants Patricia J. Danis and Orange Coast Title Company on April 25, 2024.  The XC includes causes of action for breach of statutory duties (Civil Code § 1102, et seq.), fraudulent concealment, negligent misrepresentation, negligence, breach of contract, implied indemnity, equitable indemnity, express indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

 

            The XC alleges that Sati, a real estate investor, hired Kelsea Mazzocco as a real estate agent in late 2022 to help purchase a multi-family property known as the 6th Street Property, which was marketed as an “investor's dream.”  During negotiations, Mazzocco informed Sati of an issue with a detached 5-car garage on the property, which was being partially used by the neighboring property owner, Bernald Rock, without Sati’s knowledge.  To address the potential issue, Danis, the seller, engaged Orange Coast Title to conduct a title and physical inspection, which found no claims, encumbrances, liens, or clouds on the title. Relying on this assurance, Sati proceeded with the purchase on December 9, 2022, and later modified the purchase agreement to ensure the disputed garage would be vacated by Rock before the close of escrow.  However, Rock initiated legal action on December 8, 2023, asserting an alleged easement granting him access to one of the garage spaces.  Sati disputes the validity and existence of this claimed easement.

 

On May 31, 2024, Cross-Complainant amended Roe 1 as Real Advantage Title Insurance Company.

 

On June 3, 2024, Cross-Defendant Orange Coast Title Company (“OCTC”) filed a demurrer to the Cross-Complaint.  To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

On July 9, 2024, Cross-Defendant Real Advantage Title Insurance Company filed a demurrer to the Cross-Complaint.  On September 13, 2024, Cross-Defendant Real Advantage Title Insurance Company filed a notice to withdraw its demurrer, set for hearing on November 12, 2024.

2.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Counsel for OCTC, Shadi Namiranian, filed a declaration establishing the attorneys met and conferred prior to the filing of the demurrer, but were unable to agree on a resolution of the merits of the pleading challenge.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for resolution. 

 

c.     Fourth Cause of Action, Negligence

OCTC argues that Sati’s fourth cause of action for negligence should be dismissed because Sati has not sufficiently alleged that OCTC owed a duty of care or breached any such duty.  To establish a negligence claim, Sati must demonstrate the existence of a legal duty of care, a breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury.  OCTC contends that Sati fails to specify any law or facts that establish such a duty, and that there was no relationship or transaction between itself and Sati that would create a duty of care, pointing out that Sati admits OCTC was hired by Danis (the seller) and that Sati only learned of OCTC’s actions through its real estate agent.  Additionally, OCTC argues that even if it were involved in the Title Policy, a preliminary title report or title insurance policy does not guarantee the state of the title, according to California law.

 

Furthermore, OCTC claims that even if a duty of care existed, there was no breach of such a duty.  Sati alleges that OCTC failed to disclose or investigate a purported Garage Easement, but OCTC argues it had no obligation to disclose an unrecorded easement and had no knowledge of it.  (XC ¶ 41.)  OCTC asserts that Sati’s allegations are vague, lack supporting facts, and do not clearly establish a breach of duty.  Additionally, OCTC notes that Sati was already aware of an “issue” with the garage, which further undermines the claim of a breach.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the negligence claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

d.     Fifth Cause of Action, Breach of Contract

 

OCTC argues that Sati’s fifth cause of action for breach of contract should be dismissed because Sati has not established that OCTC was a party to the contract or that the contract terms were breached.  To claim a breach of contract, there must be a contract between the parties, the plaintiff’s performance or a valid excuse for nonperformance, the defendant’s breach, and resulting damages.  OCTC asserts that Sati’s claim fails on two primary grounds.  First, OCTC argues that it was not a party to the title insurance policy that Sati claims was breached.  The title policy was issued by a title insurance company, not OCTC, and OCTC is not named or referenced anywhere in the policy.  Under California law, a party that is not a signatory to a contract cannot be held liable for its breach.  Therefore, OCTC states that Sati has not adequately alleged the first element required for a breach of contract cause of action.

 

Second, OCTC contends that even if it were a party to the title insurance policy, the terms of the policy do not support a breach.  The policy does not guarantee the state of the title, and there is no contractual term requiring OCTC to disclose or investigate any title items, including the alleged Garage Easement.  The policy explicitly excludes coverage for easements, liens, or encumbrances not shown by public records, and the Garage Easement in question was never recorded.  Additionally, the policy excludes coverage for items not recorded in public records but known to the insured without written disclosure to the insurer, which applies here since Sati was aware of the garage issue but did not provide written notice to OCTC.  Given these policy terms, OCTC argues that Sati's allegations are conclusory and insufficient to establish a breach of contract.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the breach of contract claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

e.     Sixth Cause of Action, Implied Indemnity

OCTC argues that Sati’s sixth cause of action for implied indemnity fails because Sati has not provided sufficient facts to establish OCTC’s liability.  To claim implied indemnity, Sati must demonstrate fault on OCTC’s part and resulting damages for which OCTC is equitably responsible.  However, Sati merely makes a general assertion that OCTC should provide indemnity based on its conduct, without specifying how OCTC is at fault.  Additionally, OCTC notes that it was not a party to the Title Policy, and Sati’s conclusory statements about OCTC's fault are vague and lack the necessary factual support to establish a valid claim for implied indemnity.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the implied indemnity claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the sixth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

f.      Seventh Cause of Action, Equitable Indemnity

OCTC argues that Sati’s seventh cause of action for equitable indemnity fails because it lacks sufficient factual support.  Equitable indemnity requires showing fault on the part of the indemnitor (OCTC) and resulting damages for which the indemnitor is equitably responsible.  Sati claims that OCTC failed to make required disclosures, misrepresented facts, and was negligent, but these allegations are based on the same insufficient grounds as Sati’s negligence and breach of contract claims.  OCTC contends that Sati’s claims are conclusory, lacking any concrete facts to demonstrate OCTC’s fault, and are therefore vague and uncertain.  Additionally, OCTC states that Sati does not adequately explain how OCTC’s alleged failure to discover and disclose the unrecorded Garage Easement constitutes wrongdoing.  

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the equitable indemnity claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the seventh cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

g.     Eighth Cause of Action, Express Indemnity

OCTC argues that Sati’s eighth cause of action for express indemnity fails because Sati has not adequately established the required elements for such a claim.  Express indemnity requires a contractual relationship between the parties, the indemnitee’s performance of their contractual obligations, a demonstrated loss within the scope of the indemnification agreement, and the amount of damages sustained.  First, OCTC contends that there is no contractual relationship between itself and Sati.  Sati claims that OCTC is obligated to indemnify under a title insurance policy; however, OCTC states that the Title Policy attached to Cross-Complaint clearly shows that the policy was not issued by OCTC, nor is OCTC mentioned in it.  Therefore, OCTC concludes that Sati has not alleged sufficient facts to demonstrate a contractual relationship with OCTC.

 

Second, OCTC argues that Sati has not shown any loss under the indemnification agreement.  Sati claims that OCTC should have ensured the property’s title was free of any encumbrances, but the alleged loss arises from an unrecorded Garage Easement, which the Title Policy explicitly excludes from coverage.  Even if OCTC were a party to the Title Policy, California law establishes that a title insurance policy does not guarantee the state of the title.  Thus, OCTC states it had no duty to ensure that the property was free from all encumbrances.  Consequently, OCTC asserts that Sati cannot claim a loss based on OCTC’s failure to guarantee a clear title, making the basis for express indemnity uncertain and unsupported by the facts.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the express indemnity claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the eighth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

h.     Ninth Cause of Action, Contribution

OCTC argues that Sati’s ninth cause of action for contribution fails because it lacks the necessary legal and factual basis.  To establish a claim for contribution, there must be a money judgment rendered jointly against defendants in a tort action, the application of equitable principles, a payment by one tortfeasor of more than their pro rata share, and no intentional injury by the tortfeasor.

 

Sati claims that OCTC must contribute if Sati is found liable to Plaintiff.  However, OCTC contends that Sati has not provided any legal or factual grounds to support this claim.  Additionally, OCTC states that Sati’s contribution claim cannot be based in tort because Sati has not sufficiently alleged facts to support a negligence claim or any other tort against OCTC.  Moreover, OCTC asserts that Sati fails to present any facts showing joint liability, equitable considerations in its favor, or any payment made by itself or another tortfeasor.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the contribution claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

i.      Tenth Cause of Action, Declaratory Relief

OCTC argues that Sati’s tenth cause of action for declaratory relief fails because it does not meet the required elements.  To qualify for declaratory relief, there must be a proper subject for such relief and an actual controversy involving justiciable questions about the rights or obligations of a party.  Sati seeks a determination of OCTC’s indemnity obligations, but OCTC contends that Sati has not provided sufficient facts to establish a valid claim for indemnity.  OCTC argues that the Title Policy excludes indemnity coverage for the unrecorded Garage Easement, and Sati has not presented any other valid basis for indemnity.

 

Sati provides no arguments in opposition.  The Court finds that OCTC sufficiently shows that the declaratory relief claim fails as a matter of law.

 

The demurrer to the tenth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

3.     Motion to Strike

OCTC moves to strike Sati’s prayer for attorneys’ fees.  OCTC argues that Sati’s request for attorney’s fees against OCTC is not valid because such fees are not provided for under any contract or California law.  Sati’s claim for attorney’s fees is based on a Title Policy attached to the Cross-Complaint. However, OCTC states that the Title Policy only allows for attorney’s fees if arbitration is initiated, not for court actions.  Moreover, the Title Policy expressly excludes coverage for unrecorded easements, such as the disputed Garage Easement, which was never recorded in the public records.

 

Additionally, OCTC points out that it is not a party to the Title Policy, which was issued by another company.  According to California law, only parties to a contract can be held liable for breaches or obligated to pay attorney’s fees under that contract.  Since OCTC is not a signatory to the Title Policy, it cannot be liable for attorney’s fees. OCTC, therefore, requests that any claims for attorney’s fees against it be removed from the Cross-Complaint.

 

Despite the Court’s sustaining of the demurrer, OCTC has shown that there is no basis for an award of attorney’s fees.  Thus, the Court GRANTS OCTC’s motion to strike request for attorney’s fees.

 

4.     Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained as to the fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, and tenth causes of action without leave to amend.  The motion to strike is granted.

 

OCTC is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24LBCV00402    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

This is a habitability and tenant harassment dispute. On February 29, 2024, plaintiff Luis M. Falcon sued defendants (1) Yolanda Fernandez, as an individual (“Yolanda”, for clarity rather than out of familiarity or disrespect), (2-3) Albertina C. Fernandez, individually (“Albertina”) and in her capacity as trustee of the Albertina C. Fernandez 2002 Trust, dated February 15, 2002, and (4) the Albertina C. Fernandez 2002 Trust, dated February 15, 2022, separately named (“the Trust”). Plaintiff asserts causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (4) trespass, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (6) negligent infliction of emotional distress, (7) negligence, (8) wrongful eviction (Civ. Code, § 789.3), (9) constructive eviction, and (10) tenant harassment (Civ. Code, § 1240.2).

As alleged in Plaintiffs’ complaint and accepted as true for purposes of a demurrer and motion to strike (paragraph citations to the complaint unless otherwise noted):

Beginning in July 2020, Plaintiff rented and resided in a residential unit at 1456 Chestnut Ave., #6, Long Beach (“the Property”). (¶ 6.) The Trust owns the Property. (¶ 2.) Plaintiff is fifty-three (53) years old; he suffered a stroke in September 2021. (¶ 6.) He is now a dependent adult. (¶ 8.)

At some point after the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, Plaintiff failed to pay rent for the first time during his tenancy. (¶ 7.) Yolanda, who acted as “authorized agent for the [Property]” (¶4), “adamantly demanded rent and refused to cooperate with any type of government agency” (¶ 7).

Plaintiff is an African Cuban of Yoruban faith. (¶ 9.) In fall of 2021, while Plaintiff was hospitalized after his stroke, Yolanda approached Plaintiff’s son and caretaker and stated that “ ‘you guys need to get out you and your voodoo’ ”. (¶ 8.) On an unspecified date after Plaintiff returned home, Yolanda approached him directly and misrepresented the nature of a “unilateral pre-printed ‘Notice to Vacate’ ”, stating “it was needed for the ‘rental assistance program’ and [there was] ‘nothing to be worried about’ ”. (¶ 9.)

In addition to the above-mentioned conduct, “on February 2nd 2022, Defendant entered the Property illegally” over Plaintiff’s objection. (¶¶ 22, 25.)

The complaint appears to refer to the parties’ rental agreement, the Notice to Vacate, and the subsequent unlawful detainer judgment all as “written contract[s]”, and Plaintiff indicates he attached them as exhibits. No exhibits to the complaint appear in the Court’s record.

On May 23, 2024, Defendants demurred to and moved to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint. On August 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition nut without POS. Defendants filed no reply.

 

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Meet and Confer

Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.41 and 435.5 require a moving party to meet and confer with its opponents prior to filing a demurrer or motion to strike, respectively. Defendants’ counsel submitted a declaration that satisfies those sections. (Carini Decsl., ¶¶ 8-9.)

b.     Legal Standard

A demurrer is a relatively commonplace procedure used in the early stages of a lawsuit for one party to challenge the basis for the other party’s claim for relief. Where one party claims the other’s initial complaint or answer is defective, that party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.)

A demurrer addresses only “the face of the pleadings” – that is, it only deals with the allegations within the complaint itself. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) The court does not accept evidence in support of a demurrer, unless the evidence is properly subject to judicial notice (e.g., evidence that neither party can reasonably dispute, or that is readily available from public records). (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)

In fact, in ruling on a demurrer to a complaint, the court accepts all of the facts in the complaint as true. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court examines the complaint and asks whether, if all the facts in it are true, the plaintiff has shown he or she is entitled to relief. (Hahn v. Mirda, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) A court considers the allegations in the complaint in their full context, and if they are ambiguous the court construes them liberally in favor of the party who filed the pleading that has been challenged. (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.)

Even liberally construed, however, a pleading subject to demurrer must state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action on which a plaintiff bases his claim to relief. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10; Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220.) A valid complaint must state “facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.10, subd. (a)(1).) The allegations must be reasonably clear and certain, so the defendant can ascertain the precise facts on which the plaintiff bases his claim and deny them or defend against them as necessary. (Committee On Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 211–212.) “The complaint in a civil action serves a variety of purposes … : [among other things,] it serves to frame and limit the issues … and to apprise the defendant of the basis upon which the plaintiff is seeking recovery . . . .” (Ibid.)

Even if a complaint fails because it is uncertain or fails to state a cause of action, the court is required to give a party leave to file a new, amended complaint where it appears there is “a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment . . . .” (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District, supra, 2 Cal.4th  at p. 967.) In fact, “[a] demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint . . . can state a cause of action under any theory . . . .” (Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1037.)

c.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of an unlawful detainer judgment entered against Plaintiff, in Albertina’s favor, on May 9, 2022. The request is denied. The Court may not take notice of the contents of the document, and the fact that the judgment was entered is irrelevant to Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike.

d.     Demurrer by the Trust

The Court notes, as an initial matter, that the Trust is not a legally cognizable defendant on its own. “In contrast to a corporation, which the law often deems a person, a trust is not a person but rather ‘ “ ‘a fiduciary relationship with respect to property.’ ... ” ’ ... ‘Legal title to property owned by a trust is held by the trustee. ... “A ... trust ... is simply a collection of assets of liabilities.” ’ ” ... ‘[A]n ordinary express trust is not an entity separate from its trustees. [¶] A trust itself cannot sue or be sued. ... ‘As a general rule, the trustee is the real party in interest with standing to sue and defend on the trust’s behalf. ... .’ ...
‘A claim based on a contract entered into by a trustee in the trustee’s representation capacity, may be asserted against the trust by proceeding against the trustee in the trustee’s representative capacity ... .’ ... .” (Portico Management Group, LLC v. Harrison (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 464, 473, internal citations omitted.)

            Put simply: the Trust is not an entity that can be sued. In fact, under California law, it is not a separate entity at all. Barring some exception – and none is evident from the complaint – the proper defendant in a suit against the Trust is Albertina, as trustee. Plaintiff has named Albertina in her capacities as both individual and trustee, so the Trust is an improperly named defendant and a redundant one. The demurrer is sustained as to the Trust without leave to amend.

            c. Demurrer for Uncertainty

            Defendants demur to all Plaintiff’s claims on the basis of uncertainty. The complaint is not fatally uncertain. “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain ... .” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) In fact, it should be “ ‘ “granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” ’ ” (Morris v. JPMorgan Chase Bank (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 279, 292.) The demurrer is not so unintelligible that defendants cannot respond at all. Defendants’ argument on this point is unpersuasive.

            d. Demurrer for Failure to State a Claim

            Although the complaint is not fatally uncertain, the Court nonetheless sustains the demurrer for failure to state a claim. A complaint may describe a plaintiff’s claim with “ultimate facts” – that is, the complaint does not need to lay out all of the plaintiff’s evidence in order to state a claim. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 719; see also Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.) But the complaint must still contain sufficient facts to place a defendant on notice of the basis for the plaintiff’s claim. (See Committee On Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp., supra, 35 Cal.3d, at pp. 211–212.) Plaintiff’s complaint does not, as to any of his causes of action.

            Firs, all of the causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint are based on Yolanda’s misconduct. But no facts in the complaint establish Yolanda’s relationship to Albertina or the Trust. The allegations strongly imply Yolanda managed the Property on Albertina’s behalf, but the complaint does not state as much. Plaintiff alleges Yolanda was the “authorized agent of the real property”, not for any individual. (See Compl., ¶ 4.) The complaint contains the legal conclusion that Defendants acted as each other’s agents, with no supporting facts. (See id., 2:23-28.) Without facts establishing why Yolanda can be held liable as though she were Plaintiff’s landlord or why Plaintiff’s landlord can be held liable for Yolanda’s misconduct, Plaintiff has not stated a claim against any of the defendants.

            Apart from failure to plead Yolanda’s agency, Plaintiff also fails to plead facts sufficient to state his causes of action individually. He alleges (1) the parties had a rental contract; (2) Yolanda improperly induced him to sign a voluntary Notice to Vacate; (3) Defendants filed an unlawful detainer action against him; and (4) Yolanda entered his residence over his objection on February 2, 2022. (Compl., ¶¶ 9-10, 13, 21, 25.) Neither (1) or (3), by itself, is wrongful. And Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to place defendants on notice of claims (2) or (4).

            Based on the foregoing, the non-Trust defendants’ demurrer is sustained.

            e. Leave to Amend

            Plaintiff has more than demonstrated a capacity to amend his complaint and state a claim. His complaint refers to several crucial exhibits that the Court assumes were omitted from his filing in error. Plaintiff provides dates of incidents and facts that, if elucidated further, could easily state a claim under several of the legal theories he has proposed. The Court grants Plaintiff thirty days’ leave to amend, the ordinary period lengthened to account for any complications that might arise based on Plaintiff’s health.

 

            3.   Motion to Strike

            The demurrer having been sustained in its entirety, Defendants’ motion to strike is moot.

 

4.   Conclusion

As to defendant Albertina C. Fernandez 2002 Trust, dated February 15, 2002, the demurrer is sustained in its entirety without leave to amend.

As to the remaining defendants, the demurrer is sustained in its entirety with thirty (30) days’ leave to amend.

Defendants’ motion to strike is denied as moot.

Moving defendants are ordered to give notice.
