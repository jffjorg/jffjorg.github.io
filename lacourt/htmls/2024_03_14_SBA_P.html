DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV01289    Hearing Date: March 14, 2024    Dept: P

TENTATIVE RULING

JUDGE DAVID K. REINERT

TORRANCE DEPARTMENT P

Hearing Date: Thursday, March 14, 2024

Case Name: CALSAFE RESEARCH CENTER, INC. v. PANOS BRANDS, LLC, et al.

Case No.: 22TRCV01289

Motion: Motion to Approve Proposition 65 Settlement and Stipulated Consent Judgment

Moving Party: Plaintiff Calsafe Research Center, Inc.

Responding Party: None as of March 12, 2024

Notice: OK

Ruling: Plaintiff Calsafe Research Center, Inc.’s Motion to Approve Proposition 65 Settlement and Stipulated Consent Judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On November 18, 2022, Plaintiff CalSafe Research Center, Inc. filed this action alleging violation of the Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, codified at Health and Safety Code §25249.6, et seq. (“Proposition 65”). Plaintiff alleges Defendant Panos Brands, LLC violated Proposition 65 by failing to warn citizens of the State of California about the presence of Lead in its Walden Farms, Chipotle Ranch Dressing offered for sale throughout California.

On January 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the present motion to approve the proposed Consent Judgment. Plaintiff moves this court, per California Health & Safety Code §25249.7(f), for an order approving and entering the terms of A Proposed Stipulated Consent Judgment submitted with the moving papers. No opposition or reply papers have been filed.

APPLICABLE LAW

The Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, colloquially known as Proposition 65, was passed as a ballot initiative by the California voters, and was designed to prevent the contamination of drinking water with, and generally protect the public from unknowing exposure to, harmful chemicals. (12 Witkin, Summary of California Law (11th ed. 2017) Real Property, §967.) Health & Safety Code (“H&SC”) § 25249.7 governs the enforcement of Proposition 65. Proposition 65 has both public and private enforcement mechanisms. (H&SC § 25249.7(c), (d).) Violations are punishable by injunction (H&SC § 24259.7(a)) and civil penalty (H&SC § 24259.7(b)). In the case of private enforcement actions, parties may also recover attorney’s fees, pursuant to the provisions in CCP § 1021.5, governing actions concerning important rights affecting the public interest.

Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(f) governs the role of the Court in approving settlements and consent judgments for private actions to enforce Proposition 65. Section 25249.7(f)(4) provides, in relevant part, that:

“If there is a settlement of an action brought by a person in the public interest under subdivision (d), the plaintiff shall submit the settlement, other than a voluntary dismissal in which no consideration is received from the defendant, to the court for approval upon noticed motion, and the court may approve the settlement only if the court makes all of the following findings:

(A) The warning that is required by the settlement complies with this chapter.

(B) The award of attorney’s fees is reasonable under California law.

(C) The penalty amount is reasonable based on the criteria set forth in paragraph (2) of subdivision (b).” (Consumer Advocacy Group, Inc. v. Kintetsu Enterprises of America (2006)141 Cal.App.4th 46, 61 (“To stamp a consent agreement with the judicial imprimatur, the court must determine the proposed settlement is just….In the context of Proposition 65 litigation,

necessarily brought to vindicate the public interest, the trial court also must ensure that its judgment serves the public interest.”).)

APPLICATION TO FACTS

Compliancy of Warning

Health & Safety Code section 25249.6 states, in relevant part: “No person in the course of doing business shall knowingly and intentionally expose any individual to a chemical known to the state to cause cancer or reproductive toxicity without first giving clear and reasonable warning to such individual, except as provided in Section 25249.10.” (Health & Safety Code, § 25249.6.) “Health and Safety Code section 25249.6 (the central warning provision) requires that there be no ‘knowing and intentional’ exposure ‘without first giving clear and reasonable warning.’” (Consumer Defense Group v. Rental Housing Industry Members (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1185, 1214.) In order for a warning to be clear and reasonable, the manner of transmission must be reasonable, the message employed must be sufficiently clear to communicate the warning. (Environmental Law Foundation v. Wykle Research, Inc. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 60, 67 fn. 6.)

The Consent Judgment provides that sixty days from the Effective Date, Defendant will be permanently enjoined from manufacturing for sale in the State of California, or directly selling in the State of California, any Covered Product that exposes a person to a “Daily Lead Exposure Level” of more than .5 micrograms of lead per day, unless it meets the warning requirements under Sections 3.1 and 3.2 of the Consent Judgment.

For those Covered Products that were packaged, distributed, shipped or sold by Defendant before the Compliance date, all claims are released. Any Covered Products that are not exempt under Sections 3.1 and 3.2 of the Consent Judgment shall be accompanied by a warning as provided in Section 3.4 of the Consent Judgment. The acceptable warnings set forth

in Section 3.3 must be displayed in a reasonably conspicuous manner and they adequately inform the reader of the potential exposure to lead, which is known to the State of California to cause cancer and birth defects or other reproductive harm. The warnings reference Prop 65 and provide a website to obtain more information.

The Consent Judgment satisfies the “clear and reasonable warning” requirement under Section 25249.6.

Reasonableness of Civil Penalty

Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(b)(1) states, in relevant part: “A person who has violated Section 25249.5 or 25249.6 is liable for a civil penalty not to exceed two thousand five hundred dollars ($2,500) per day for each violation in addition to any other penalty established by law.” Section 25249.7(b)(2) states: “In assessing the amount of a civil penalty for a violation of this chapter, the court shall consider all of the following: (A) The nature and extent of the violation. (B) The number of, and severity of, the violations. (C) The economic effect of the penalty on the violator. (D) Whether the violator took good faith measures to comply with this chapter and the time these measures were taken. (E) The willfulness of the violator’s misconduct. (F) The deterrent effect that the imposition of the penalty would have on both the violator and the regulated community as a whole. (G) Any other factor that justice may require.”

The Consent Judgment imposes a civil penalty of $1600 on Defendant, with 75% earmarked for California’s Office of Environmental Health Hazard Assessment and the remaining 25% earmarked for Plaintiff in accordance with Health & Safety Code §25249.12(c)(1) & (d). Parties entered into the Consent Judgment after arm’s length negotiations. As indicated by Plaintiff, the amount is reasonable given that (1) the amount is sufficient to remind Defendant to comply with Prop 65; (2) the Consent Judgment already ensures that Defendant’s products will carry the Prop 65 warning; (3) Defendant claims it did not

intentionally ignore its obligations under Prop 65 and it immediately took action to withdraw the non-conforming product and will ensure that it complies with Prop 65 in the future; (4) Defendant sold very few nonconforming products prior to receiving Plaintiff’s notice and sold no such products since receiving Plaintiff’s notice; (5) Plaintiff believes the sale of products in violation of Prop 65 is a severe violation; and (6) payment of the penalty provides further incentive for Defendant to comply with Prop 65.

Reasonableness of Attorney’s Fees

Pursuant to 11 Cal. Code Regs. § 3201(b)(1), “a settlement that provides for the giving of a clear and reasonable warning, where there had been no warning provided prior to the sixty-day notice, for an exposure that appears to require a warning, is presumed to confer a significant benefit on the public.”

The fees setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award. The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.) Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary. (Id. at 48, n.23.) After the trial court has performed the lodestar calculations, it shall consider whether the total award so calculated under all of the circumstances of the case is more than a reasonable amount and, if so, shall reduce the section 1717 award so that it is a reasonable figure. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-96.)

Defendant agrees to pay attorney’s fees in the amount of $14,400 as complete reimbursement for Plaintiffs’ attorney’s fees and costs. The amount of attorney’s fees is reasonable given that Plaintiff’s counsel attests to spending 50 hours for a total of $31,025.69 in fees and costs.

Proposed Consent Judgment Proper under Health & Safety Code § 25249.7(f)(4)

For the aforesaid reasons, the Proposed Consent Judgment attached as Exhibit B to the Manning Declaration is approved. The court finds the Consent Judgment provides for warnings in compliance with Prop 65, the civil penalty provided for reasonable per Health & Safety Code §25249.7(b)(2) and the attorney’s fees included as part of the Consent Judgment are reasonable. The Consent Judgment is just and serves the public interest.

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s Motion to Approve Consent Judgment is GRANTED. The Consent Judgment is entered pursuant to CCP §664.6 and the Court retains jurisdiction pursuant to CCP §664.6 to enforce the judgment.

Case Number: 22TRCV01513    Hearing Date: March 14, 2024    Dept: P

Hearing Date: March 14, 2024

Moving Parties: Defendant Vortex Alliance, Inc.

Responding Party: N/A - Unopposed

DEFENDANT VORTEX ALLIANCE, INC.’s DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

RULING

The Court considered the moving papers. The Defendant Vortex Alliance, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

Jack Cheung dba Three-C Co. (Plaintiff) filed an Unlawful Detainer Complaint on December 16, 2022 against David Tran (Tran) and Vortex Alliance (Vortex, collectively Defendants). The motion before the Court is Vortex’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint. The Demurrer is unopposed.

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

Analysis

Vortex argues upon Demurrer that the Complaint fails for three reasons: (1) Plaintiff cannot demonstrate compliance with Business and Professions Code § 17918 (BPC § 17918), (2) the Complaint fails to attach a copy of the lease, and (3) the Complaint fails to allege proper service of the Notice to Quit. The Court agrees with the first two reasons and sustains the Demurrer.

Vortex argues first that Plaintiff fails to show compliance with BPC § 17918. This section provides:

“No person transacting business under a fictitious business name contrary to the provisions of this chapter, or his assignee, may maintain any action upon or on account of any contract made, or transaction had, in the fictitious business name in any court of this state until the fictitious business name statement has been executed, filed, and published as required by this chapter.”

Indeed, on page one, under questions 2(a) and 2(b), where Plaintiff is asked whether they have complied with the fictitious name laws and the fictitious name business is being conducted under, Plaintiff checks they have complied but then lists the name as “Vortex Alliance”, the business name the demurring defendant. Therefore, it is unclear whether Plaintiff has indeed complied or whether Plaintiff simply made a typographical error.

More substantively, Vortex contends that the lease alleged to have been breached is not attached. In order to establish breach of a contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish the existence of a contract. (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated

by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) However, here, Plaintiff does neither.

Finally, the Court notes that although the Demurrer contends that the three-day Notice to quit was not properly served, the Notice is attached along with proof of service demonstrating proper service. However, this does not remedy the first two deficiencies, therefore, the Demurrer is sustained.

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. As there is reasonable possibility of successful amendment, leave to amend is granted. Plaintiff has 20 days leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

The Defendant Vortex Alliance, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV01533    Hearing Date: March 14, 2024    Dept: P

Hearing Date: March 14, 2024

Moving Parties: Defendants Ford Motor Company and Autonation Ford Torrance

Responding Party: Plaintiff Maria Aranda

DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

RULING

The Court considered the moving, opposing, and reply papers. Defendants’ Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings is GRANTED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

On May 15, 2023, Maria Aranda (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Ford Motor Company (FMC) and Autonation Ford Torrance (AFT, collectively Defendants) alleging the following five causes of action:

1. Violation of 1793.2(d) as to FMC

2. Violation of 1793.2(b) as to FMC

3. Violation of 1793.2(a)(3) as to FMC

4. Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability as to FMC

5. Negligent Repair as to AFT

Plaintiff purchased a 2013 Ford Escape (the Vehicle) on February 9, 2013. (Complaint, ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that defects manifested themselves within the applicable express warranty period including engine defects, transmission defects, and electrical defects among others. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges that after several repair attempts Defendant FMC failed to conform the Vehicle to the warranty and failed to offer to repurchase the Vehicle. Attempts at repairs by presentation to an authorized repair facility included the following dates:

· May 12, 2014;

· January 17, 2015;

· July 18, 2017;

· October 7, 2017;

· June 5, 2018;

· July 17, 2019;

· April 4, 2020; and

· June 21, 2022

(Complaint, ¶¶ 23-30.)

Plaintiff then filed suit. The motion before the Court is the Defendants’ Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion, and Defendants file a reply.

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

“The grounds for a motion for judgment on the pleadings must appear on the face of the complaint or from a matter of which the court may take judicial notice.” (Richardson-Tunnell v. School Ins. Program for Employees (2007) 157 Cal.App. 4th 1056, 1061, disapproved on other grounds by Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Prot. Dist. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 798, 815 n.8. Accord Bufil v. Dollar Financial Group, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1202, disapproved on other grounds by Noel v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 955, 986; Saltarelli & Steponovich v. Douglas (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1, 5.)

A motion for judgment on the pleadings involves the same type of procedures that apply to a general demurrer. (Richardson-Tunnell v. School Ins. Program for Employees (2007) 157 Cal.App. 4th 1056, 1061, disapproved on other grounds by Quigley v. Garden Valley Fire Prot. Dist. (2019) 7 Cal. 5th 798, 815 n.8; Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 1057,

1064.) In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether properly pled factual allegations, assumed to be true and liberally construed, are sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Stone Street Capital, LLC v. Cal. State Lottery Com'n (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 109, 116; Fire Ins. Exchange v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 446, 452-53.) A motion for judgment on the pleadings does not lie as to only part of a cause of action. (Fire Ins. Exch. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) “[A] motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the office of a general, not a special, demurrer...” Fabbro v. Dardi & Co. (1949) 93 Cal. App. 2d 247, 252.)

Analysis

In their moving papers, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s first four causes of action are all time-barred because the statute of limitations on actions under the Song-Beverly Act is four years. Additionally, Defendant argues that the fifth cause of action for negligent repair fails to plead damages. The Court agrees and grants the Motion.

A. The first four causes of action are time-barred

Defendants’ primary argument is that the Vehicle at issue was purchased in 2013, and the initial claim was not presented until ten years later, when Plaintiff filed her Complaint on May 15, 2023. Plaintiff counters in their opposition papers that the Complaint does not affirmatively show a statute of limitations defense. The Court is not persuaded.

On the face of the Complaint, it is clear that: (a) the Vehicle was purchased in 2013, (b) there were several repair attempts, and (c) the Complaint was filed in 2023. As explained more fully below, the discovery rule states that the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff suspects or through diligence, when a reasonable person should suspect that wrongdoing has occurred. Neither the Complaint nor the opposition papers provide justification for Plaintiff waiting six years after the statute of limitations had run to file her Complaint.

Within the Complaint Plaintiff asserts four different defenses to an attack of the statute of limitations: class action tolling, discovery rule tolling, the repair doctrine, and fraudulent concealment tolling. However, the Complaint does little to establish how each applies, nor does Plaintiff elaborate in their opposition papers. The Court will take each in turn.

i. Class Action Tolling

Plaintiff argues that the class action tolling rule articulated in American Pipe applies here. The Supreme Court explained the rule: “…under limited circumstances, if class certification is

denied, the statute of limitations is tolled from the time of commencement of the suit to the time of denial of certification for all purported members of the class who either make timely motions to intervene in the surviving individual action [Citation] or who timely file their individual actions [Citation].” (Jolly v. Eli Lilly & Co. (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1103, 1119.) However, at no point does the Complaint identify the related class action that was pending certification. Therefore, American Pipe is inapplicable here.

ii. Discovery Rule Tolling

Under the general rule, a cause of action accrues when the wrongful act is done, not when a plaintiff discovers they have a cause of action to pursue. (Moreno v. Sanchez (2003) 106 Cal.App. 4th 1415, 1423.) “Under the discovery rule, the statute of limitations begins to run when the plaintiff suspects or should suspect that her injury was caused by wrongdoing, that someone has done something wrong to her.” (Jolly, supra, at 1110.) Here, the Complaint demonstrates that there were several attempts at repair during the ten year prior to the filing of the Complaint in 2023. The first four attempts occurred on (1) May 12, 2014; (2) January 17, 2015; (3) July 18, 2017; and (4) October 7, 2017. The Complaint fails to explain after four separate attempts, how Plaintiff was still unsuspecting that an issue was caused by wrongdoing, nor why there was a two-year gap between the second and third attempt at repair.

iii. The Repair Doctrine

Plaintiff asserts the repair doctrine citing Aced v. Hobbs-Sesack Plumbing Co. (1961), 55 Cal.2d 573 (“Aced”) which states that “The statute of limitations is tolled where one who has breached a warranty claims that the defect can be repaired and attempts to make repairs.” (Aced, supra, at 587.) Plaintiff argues this doctrine specifically as to the fourth cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of merchantability. Plaintiff cites Aced which holds that if a warranty relates to a future event, where the defect cannot be discovered by the exercise of reasonable diligence then the statute of limitations does not run until the discovery of the defect. (Aced, supra, at 584.) However, under the discovery rule cited above, it is still unclear how after four attempts at requesting Defendants to repair the Vehicle, Plaintiff remained unsuspecting, “…the limitations period begins once the plaintiff ‘has notice or information of circumstances to put a reasonable person on inquiry’”. (Jolly, supra, at 1110, citing Gutierrez v. Mofid (1985) 39 Cal.3d 892, 896-897.) Here, it took eight attempts before a Complaint was filed.

iv. Fraudulent Concealment Tolling

Fraudulent concealment can also toll the statute of limitations. However, “To take advantage of this doctrine ‘ “the plaintiff must show ... the substantive elements of fraud ... and ...

an excuse for late discovery of the facts.” (Britton v. Girardi (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 721, 734.) The plaintiff must plead that due to fraud, the plaintiff was hindered in bringing the action within the statutory period. (Ibid.) The Complaint fails to plead fraud with the requisite specificity; therefore, Plaintiff cannot avail themselves to the doctrine of fraudulent concealment to toll the statute of limitations.

B. Negligent repair is not adequately pled

Finally, Plaintiff contends Defendant AFT is liable for negligent repair, however, the element of damages has not been plead sufficiently. According to the Complaint, there is no allegations that the repairs were paid for out-of-pocket, nor that the repairs were not covered under warranty. Plaintiff alleges in the Complaint that the Vehicle’s value is de minimis, however, it is unclear how Defendants’ actions caused this.

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. As there is reasonable possibility of successful amendment, Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Motion for Judgement on the Pleadings is GRANTED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV02814    Hearing Date: March 14, 2024    Dept: P

Hearing Date: March 14, 2024

Moving Parties: Defendant Hyundai Motor America

Responding Party: Plaintiff Keith Scoggins

DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL BINDING ARBITRATION

RULING

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. Defendant’s Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

Keith Scoggins (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint on August 28, 2023. The Complaint alleged three causes of action grounded in the Song-Beverly Act against Hyundai Motor America (Defendant).

On March 20, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a 2021 Hyundai Palisade (the Vehicle). (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Express warranties accompanied the sale of the Vehicle to Plaintiff. (Id.) Plaintiff alleges that the Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with defects and nonconformities to the warranty. (Id., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff alleges that after he delivered the Vehicle to Defendant’s authorized repair facilities for repair Defendant was unable to conform the Vehicle to the warranty. (Id., ¶¶ 23-24.) Plaintiff then filed suit.

The motion now before the Court is Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion, and Defendant files a reply.

DISCUSSION

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests that this Court take judicial notice of the following:

1. Plaintiff’s Complaint, filed on August 28, 2023

2. Minute Order and Ruling in Jason Guaschino v. Hyundai Motor America, (United States District Court, Central District of California - Case No. CV 23-04354-MWF

3. Minute Order and Ruling in Paula Vargas-Lopez v. Hyundai Motor America, (United States District Court, Central District of California - Case No. 8:22-cv-01526-FWS-JDE)

4. Minute Order and Ruling in Virginia Mendoza v. Hyundai Motor America, (United States District Court, Central District of California - Case No. CV 22-210-DMG

5. Minute Order and Ruling in Nanci Aleman Garcia v. Hyundai Motor America, (LASC - Case No. 22STCV38870)

The request for judicial notice is redundant as to Plaintiff’s Complaint, as the Court’s ruling necessitates reference thereto, however, pursuant to CEC § 452(d)(1), the Court will grant judicial notice as to the remaining items.

Legal Standard

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) This is usually done by presenting a copy of the signed, written agreement to the court. “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330.)

The moving party must also establish the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) The filing of a lawsuit against the moving party for a controversy

clearly within the scope of the arbitration agreement affirmatively establishes the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. (Hyundai Amco America, Inc. v. S3H, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 572, 577.)

Analysis

In their moving papers Defendant asserts that there are two arbitration agreements that control. The first is the arbitration agreement in the retail installment sale contract (RISC), the second is the arbitration agreement in the owner’s handbook. The Court notes that although both are presented verbatim, the latter is unsigned. Plaintiff’s refusal to arbitrate has been clearly established by the filing of the Complaint.

As to the arbitration agreement within the RISC (RISC Arbitration Agreement), Plaintiff argues that it cannot be invoked because Defendant is a non-signatory to the contract. Any enforcement of the arbitration provision by Defendant, would come as being enforced by (1) equitable estoppel or (2) Defendant qualifying as a third-party beneficiary. Plaintiff argues that neither situation applies, therefore the RISC Arbitration Agreement cannot be enforced.

As to the arbitration agreement within the handbook (Handbook Arbitration Agreement), Plaintiff argues that it cannot be enforced because (1) there was no assent and (2) Defendant’s attempt to sneak a contractual obligation into a warranty must fail.

As explained below, the Court disagrees with both of Plaintiff’s arguments as to the RISC Arbitration Agreement and as to the Handbook Arbitration Agreement. The Court grants Defendant’s Motion.

A. The Arbitration Agreement within the Retail Installment Sale Contract

The RISC Arbitration Agreement provides in pertinent part as follows:

“1. EITHER YOU OR WE MAY CHOOSE TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN US DECIDED BY ARBITRATION AND NOT IN COURT OR BY JURY TRIAL.

2. IF A DISPUTE IS ARBITRATED, YOU WILL GIVE UP YOUR RIGHT TO PARTICIPATE AS A CLASS REPRESENTATIVE OR CLASS MEMBER ON ANY CLASS CLAIM YOU MAY HAVE AGAINST US INCLUDING ANY RIGHT TO CLASS ARBITRATION OR ANY CONSOLIDATION OF INDIVIDUAL ARBITRATIONS.

3. DISCOVERY AND RIGHTS TO APPEAL IN ARBITRATION ARE GENERALLY MORE LIMITED THAN IN A LAWSUIT, AND OTHER

RIGHTS THAT YOU AND WE WOULD HAVE IN COURT MAY NOT BE AVAILABLE IN ARBITRATION.

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action…”

In countering Plaintiff’s contention on estoppel, Defendant argues that equitable estoppel is available. Defendant relies on Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486, stating that Plaintiff’s warranty claims arise out of and are intertwined with the underlying contract obligations. (Also see, Boucher v. Alliance Title Co., Inc. (2005) 127 Cal. App. 4th 262, 271, [“[U]nder both federal and California decisional authority, a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined’ with the underlying contract obligations.”] and Goldman v. KPMG LLP (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 209, 217-18.)

In Felisilda vehicle buyers brought action against the dealership and the automobile manufacturer for violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. The dealership filed a motion to compel arbitration that included the manufacturer. The trial court ordered arbitration and dismissed the dealership. The decision was affirmed by the Court of Appeal. (Felisilda, supra, 486). Part of the Felisilda Court’s ruling was that the Complaint had alleged that the warranties accompanied the sale of the vehicle, and that the manufacturer undertook to maintain the utility of the vehicle or provide compensation if there was a failure in performance. (Felisilda, supra, 496.) Plaintiff alleges similarly here, for example: “Express warranties accompanied the sale of the Subject Vehicle to Plaintiff by which Hyundai Motor America undertook to preserve or maintain the utility or performance of Plaintiff’s vehicle or to provide compensation if there was a failure in such utility or performance.” (Complaint, ¶ 8.)

The court follows the recent 2nd District court of appeal case, Ford Motor Warranty Cases, Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, which addresses whether a non-signatory manufacturer can invoke the arbitration provision in a sales contract and declines to follow the holding in Felisilda. The appellate court rejected the manufacturer’s positions as to equitable estoppel, third-party beneficiary, and agency theories, stating: “We agree with the trial

court that FMC could not compel arbitration based on plaintiffs’ agreements with the dealers that sold them the vehicles. Equitable estoppel does not apply because, contrary to FMC’s arguments, plaintiffs’ claims against it in no way rely on the agreements. FMC was not a third party of those agreements as there is no basis to conclude the plaintiffs and their dealers entered into them with the intention of benefitting FMC. And FMC is not entitled to enforce the agreements as an undisclosed principal because there is no nexus between plaintiffs’ claims, any alleged agency between FMC and the dealers, and the agreements.”

The court finds “manufacturer vehicle warranties that accompany the sale of motor vehicles without regard to the terms of the sale contract between the purchaser and the dealer are independent of the sales contract.” Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 969. Therefore, the RISC does not create an enforceable contract between plaintiff and Hyundai.

B. The Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information

The parties also maintain contention that reach to the second arbitration agreement, the Handbook Arbitration Agreement. The Handbook Arbitration Agreement is similar to the RISC Arbitration Agreement; however, the key differences are that (a) there is a 30-day opt out option and (b) the following provision:

IF YOU PURCHASED OR LEASED YOUR VEHICLE IN CALIFORNIA, YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION. BY ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS.

The Handbook Arbitration Agreement, unlike the RISK Arbitration Agreement, is not signed; however, Defendant provides a copy within the moving papers and indicates that this handbook accompanied the subject warranty. Indeed, the text of the warranty is within the handbook. (See Declaration of Ali Ameripour, Exh 3, pg. 13.)

i. Assent

Plaintiff’s first contention is that there was no assent to the Handbook Arbitration Agreement. There is no signature to the Handbook Arbitration Agreement in the copy Defendant attached, however, there is a provision that counters Plaintiff’s contention: “…by accepting benefits under this warranty, including having any repairs performed under warranty, you agree to

be bound by these terms.” (Id.) Additionally, the clause notes that if the Vehicle was purchased in California, the warranty is subject to the terms of the arbitration agreement therein.

Here, contract law is instructive. The manner in which acceptance is to be communicated may be specified in the offer, in which case no other will suffice. But if the offer prescribes no means, “any reasonable and usual mode may be adopted.” (Witkin Summary of California Law, Eleventh Edition – Contracts.) Here, acceptance was specified as the action of purchasing or leasing the Vehicle within California, which the Plaintiff did. Plaintiff argues that silence cannot act as acceptance, we agree, however, the manner of acceptance was not silence, but making the purchase within state lines. Moreover, there was an opt-out provision in the Handbook Arbitration Agreement, yet there is no indication that Plaintiff ever availed himself to it. Therefore, Plaintiff assented to the Handbook Arbitration Agreement.

ii. Handbook Arbitration Agreement vs. Warranty

Plaintiff’s final argument is that Defendant’s “attempt to sneak contractual obligations into a Warranty necessarily fail”. (Opposition Papers, 8:18-20.) The Court disagrees. First, the Handbook Arbitration Agreement is noted in bold in the table of contents and clearly stated on page 13 and 14 of the warranty, there does not appear to be any clear attempt to hide the terms of the Handbook Arbitration Agreement. Second, Plaintiff attempts to separate the warranty from the arbitration agreement arguing that a warranty, which focuses on the seller’s behavior and obligation, does not impose any independent obligation on the buyer outside of the context of enforcing the seller’s promises. (Opposition Papers, 9:1-7.) The Court is unpersuaded as Plaintiff fails to provide authority as to why the two may not be combined. Here, in clear terms, Defendant provided the text of the Handbook Arbitration Agreement, and a 30-day opt-out option. Plaintiff did not avail themselves to the latter, therefore it cannot avoid the former.

Finally, to the extent that plaintiff pleads a cause of action for an implied warranty, the court finds that the causes of action asserting violations of the express warranty are wholly intertwined with the allegations regarding the implied warranty. Therefore, all of plaintiff’s allegations are subject to arbitration.

CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration is GRANTED.

Prevailing party on motion is ordered to give notice of ruling.
