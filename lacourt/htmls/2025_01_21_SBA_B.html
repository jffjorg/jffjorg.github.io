DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV01110    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

KIMBERLY LEWIS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV01110

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

THE TJX COMPANY, INC.,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          January 21, 2025

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant T.J. MAXX OF CA, LLC

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Attendance at Deposition

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED.  Plaintiff Kimberly Lewis is ordered to appear for her deposition on a mutually available date and time within fifteen days. 

Plaintiff is ordered to pay monetary sanctions to defendant in the amount of $705.00 within thirty days.

BACKGROUND

            On April 13, 2023, plaintiff Kimberly Lewis filed a complaint against The TJX Company, Inc. for general negligence and premises liability.  Plaintiff alleges that on April 21, 2021, defendant negligently maintained, owned, leased, occupied, and controlled the premises such that store items were not properly secured on the shelves and cascaded on top of plaintiff’s head.

            On April 18, 2023, plaintiff filed an amended complaint.

            On November 7, 2023, defendant filed an answer.

            On November 20, 2023, at the CMC, the Court set a trial date for October 2, 2024.

            On January 3, 2024, plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney.

            On May 1, 2024, plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney.

            On July 2, 2024, the parties stipulated to continue the trial date.  The Court ordered that the parties were to meet and confer to establish a detailed written discovery schedule, discuss mediation and other alternative dispute resolution options, and lead trial counsel attend the August 23, 2024 hearing.  The Court noted that it expected “to move this matter to trial quickly.”

            On August 23, 2024, the Court set a trial date for September 8, 2025.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            CCP §2025.450(a) provides:  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

CCP §2025.450(b) provides, “A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1)        The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2)        The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

DISCUSSION

Defendant requests that the Court compel plaintiff Kimberly Lewis to appear for a deposition.

Defendant explains that on March 14, 2024, plaintiff was served with a notice of deposition for May 8, 2024.  On March 14, plaintiff’s prior attorney objected based on not being available.  On March 15 and 19, defense counsel requested a mutually agreeable date.  On March 28, defendant served a notice of continuance of deposition for May 22.  On May 1, plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney.  On June 5 and 10, defense counsel requested a new date for plaintiff’s deposition.  On June 11, defendant served a notice of taking deposition for July 11.  On July 3, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel to confirm July 11.  On July 5, plaintiff’s counsel confirmed he was unavailable for July 11 and that he would provide alternative dates the following week.  On July 25, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel for an available date.  On August 1, defendant served a notice of taking deposition for September 11.  On August 9, counsel met and conferred to prepare a joint discovery schedule, which was filed, noting that plaintiff’s deposition was scheduled for September 11. 

On September 3, plaintiff objected to the deposition notice on the grounds that it was unilaterally noticed and that she and her counsel were unavailable.  On September 6 and 11, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel proposing five alternative deposition dates.  On September 11, plaintiff’s counsel responded that he was working on getting a date and would confirm a date before the end of the week.  On September 24, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel requesting an available date.  On September 25 and October 7, plaintiff’s counsel stated that he was still working on securing a deposition date.  On October 10, defense counsel followed up with plaintiff’s counsel.  On October 11, defendant served a notice of taking deposition for October 31.  On October 23, plaintiff objected on the grounds that it was unilaterally noticed and that she and counsel were unavailable.  On October 30, defense counsel requested a firm date by the end of the week.  On October 31, counsel met and conferred by phone. 

On November 11, defense counsel sent an email to plaintiff’s counsel to follow up.  On November 13, plaintiff’s counsel provided December 18 as an available date.  On November 15, defendant served a deposition notice for December 18.  On December 17, plaintiff’s counsel responded that plaintiff would not be available for her deposition.

There is no opposition.

The Court finds that defendant properly noticed plaintiff’s deposition on numerous occasions, and that she had agreed to appear on certain dates and did not file valid objections.  The motion is thus GRANTED.

Sanctions

Under CCP §2025.450(g)(1), “If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

Defendant requests sanctions against plaintiff and her attorney in the amount of $920 (at $215/hr.).  The Court finds that $705 ($215/hr. x 3 hrs., filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiff only.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            Plaintiff Kimberly Lewis is ordered to appear for her deposition within fifteen days at a mutually agreeable place and time.

Plaintiff is ordered to pay monetary sanctions to defendant T.J. MAXX OF CA, LLC in the amount of $705.00 within thirty days.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

The motion to vacate dismissal is GRANTED.  The dismissal entered on December 17, 2024 is VACATED.

BACKGROUND

On September 6, 2024, plaintiff Sri Panchamukha Hanuman Temple and Religious Academy filed an unlawful detainer complaint against Charles Thacker and Diana Thacker based on a three-day notice to pay rent or quit as to a commercial property at 23805 Hawthorne Blvd., Suite A, Torrance.

On November 12, 2024, the case was called for trial. Testimony was given.  The parties stipulated to allow the Court to conduct settlement conference.  A settlement conference was conducted, and a settlement agreement was reached.  Pursuant to oral stipulation, the trial was continued to December 17, 2024.

On December 17, 2024, there was no appearance by plaintiff or plaintiff’s attorney.  Defense counsel indicated that there was a settlement but there had been a breakdown in communication with the other party.  The Court placed the matter on second call to give defense counsel an opportunity to call plaintiff’s counsel.  On second call, defense counsel indicated that he was unable to make contact.  The Court granted defendant’s oral request for the case to be dismissed for failure to show to trial.  The case was dismissed without prejudice.

On December 18, 2024, defendants filed a motion for attorney’s fees and costs as the prevailing parties.

On December 24, 2024, plaintiff filed a motion for order setting aside dismissal.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a . . . dismissal . . . taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief . . . shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the . . . dismissal . . . . was taken. . . .  Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.  The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. . . .”

“[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Motion to Vacate Dismissal

Under CCP §473(b), plaintiff requests an order relieving plaintiff from the December 17, 2024 order of dismissal based on plaintiff’s attorney’s affidavit of fault.

            Plaintiff argues that it is entitled to mandatory relief due to mistake and neglect of its attorney.  Plaintiff’s counsel states in his declaration that after the November 12, 2024 hearing, he failed to calendar the Court’s continued trial date. As he had failed to calendar the new date, he failed to appear on December 17, 2024.  He states also that defense counsel had attempted to reach him by calling his cell phone that morning, but he was in conference with a client and was unable to take the call.  After he finished the client meeting, he attempted to return defense counsel’s call but did not reach him.  Plaintiff’s counsel then called the Court and learned that the Court had dismissed the action without prejudice due to his failure to appear.  Plaintiff also asserts that the case should be decided on the merits.

            In opposition, defendants argue that relief is not mandatory, and that plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration does not establish cognizable “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”

            In reply, plaintiff reiterates the law that relief is mandatory, and that plaintiff’s counsel neglected to put the date on his calendar.  Plaintiff also acknowledges that defense counsel is entitled to be compensated for his reasonable legal fees for appearing at the hearing on December 17, 2024 in the amount of $1,200, as shown in Exhibit B of defense counsel’s declaration, but that he is not entitled to recover all of his attorneys’ fees for the entire case.

            The Court finds that relief from dismissal is warranted and is mandatory based on attorney mistake and neglect.  Further, defense counsel is entitled to his reasonable compensatory legal fees in the amount of $1,200.

            The motion is therefore GRANTED.

 

            Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Costs

            Defendants request an order awarding them their reasonable prevailing-party attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $13,159.61, pursuant to Civil Code §1717, Rules of Court 3.1702, and section 31 of the lease agreement.

            Defendants argue that they are the prevailing parties in this action, having obtained the dismissal of the complaint.

            Based on the ruling on plaintiff’s motion to vacate the dismissal, the motion is MOOT.

 

ORDER

            The motion for order setting aside dismissal is GRANTED. 

The dismissal entered on December 17, 2024 is VACATED.

Plaintiff’s attorney of record Stephen B. Goldberg (SBN 125166) is directed to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees to defense counsel Ishimatsu Law Group, P.C. in the amount of $1,200.

The motion for attorneys’ fees and costs is MOOT.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.
