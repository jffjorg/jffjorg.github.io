Case Number: 22AVCV00693    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is an insurance bad faith case. On September 6, 2020, the Bobcat wildfire ignited in the San Gabriel Mountains, and its smoke permeated plaintiff Lawrence Simpson and Phyllis Price’s residence.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 2, 4.) As a result, Plaintiff hired a carpet cleaning company and paid $11,000.00 to replace all the carpets in his residence. (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 4.) Plaintiff submitted a claim to defendant California Automobile Insurance Company for the damage to the residence, and California Automobile Insurance Company denied it. (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 33.)

 

Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on February 14, 2023 alleging causes of action for the following: (1) breach of contract; and (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

On April 17, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, for Summary Adjudication. Plaintiff opposed on November 19, 2024.  Defendant replied on November 25, 2024.

 

Analysis

 

Standard for Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication – A party may move for summary judgment in any action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c subd. (a).)  Similarly, a party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (f)(1).)  Summary adjudication will be granted if it completely disposes of a cause of action, affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty. (Id.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made in the alternative to a motion for summary judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (f)(2).)  To prevail on a motion for summary judgment, the evidence submitted must show there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (c).)  

 

The defendant moving for summary judgment does not need to conclusively negate an element of the plaintiffs’ cause of action, but only to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action. . .cannot be established. . .or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Ridgefield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849 (citing Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (o)(2).)  Once the moving party has met the respective burden, the burden shifts to the responding party who must demonstrate that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Code Civ. Proc. §437c subd. (p).)  The responding party must not rely on allegations or denials in the pleadings or answer alone, but “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Id.)

 

When ruling on a summary judgment motion, the trial court must consider all inferences from the evidence, even those contradicted by the moving party’s evidence.  The motion cannot succeed unless the evidence leaves no room for conflicting inferences as to material facts; the court has no power to weigh one inference against another or against other evidence. (Murillo v. Rite Stuff Foods, Inc. (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 833, 841.)  In determining whether the facts give rise to a triable issue of material fact, “‘[a]ll doubts as to whether any material, triable, issues of fact exist are to be resolved in favor of the party opposing summary judgment . . . .’” (Gold v. Weissman (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1195, 1198–1199, as modified on denial of reh'g (Feb. 10, 2004).)  “In other words, the facts alleged in the evidence of the party opposing summary judgment and the reasonable inferences there from must be accepted as true.”  (Jackson v. County of Los Angeles (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 171, 179.)

-----

 

Request for Judicial Notice – Judicial notice may not be taken of any matter, unless authorized or required by law.  (Evidence Code § 450.)  “Judicial notice is the recognition and acceptance by the court, for use by the trier of fact or by the court, of the existence of a matter of law or fact that is relevant to an issue in the action without requiring formal proof of the matter.” (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882.)

 

Here, Defendant requests judicial notice of the following: (1) the original complaint; and (2) the first amended complaint. A court may take judicial notice of the contents of its own records. (Dwan v. Dixon (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 260, 265.)

 

Thus, the Court grants the requests.

 

-----

 

First Cause of Action (Breach of Contract) ¿ Defendant argues that no breach of contract occurred on the ground that the claim was properly excluded because there was no direct physical loss.

 

When an issue of coverage exists, the burden is on the insured to prove facts establishing that the claimed loss falls within the coverage provided by the policy's insuring clause. (MRI Healthcare Center of Glendale, Inc. v. State Farm General Ins. Co. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 766, 777.) Once the insured has made that showing, the burden shifts to the insurer to prove the claim is specifically excluded. (Ibid.)

 

The precondition to coverage under the policy's first party coverage is “direct physical loss” to insured property. (SSUMF No. 2.) The direct physical loss requirement is part of the policy’s insuring clause and thus falls within Plaintiff’s burden of proof. (MRI Healthcare Center of Glendale, Inc. v. State Farm General Ins. Co., supra, 187 Cal.App.4th 766 at pg. 778.)

 

To show that Plaintiff cannot meet this burden, Defendant submits as evidence a declaration by Jeffrey Graf, Supervisor of Property Large Loss at California Automobile Insurance Company. (SSUMF No. 21.) In addition, it submits a report by Forensic Analytical Consulting Services. The report made the following findings: (1) no wildfire smoke odors were observed; (2) no indications of smoke impact were identified; (3) unusual corrosion of metal, plastic or other materials that could be directly attributed to smoke impact was not observed; (4) sample results from representative surfaces at the property (exterior, interior, contents, HVAC system (i.e. registers), attic space and garage) indicated levels of combustion related particulate (char, ash & soot) ranging from 1 to 2 percent of the total dust sample, with 12 of the 15 samples having less than 1 percent combustion related particulate present; and (5) based on assessment findings collected during the investigation, Forensic Analytical Consulting Services did not recommend cleaning of the property (exterior, garage, interior, contents, attic space or HVAC system.) (SSUMF No. 25.)

 

The policy at issue does not define “direct physical loss.” (SSUMF No. 2.) In modern policies, “physical loss or damage” is the trigger for coverage. (MRI Healthcare Center of Glendale, Inc. v. State Farm General Ins. Co., supra, 187 Cal.App.4th 766 at pg. 778.) This threshold is met when an item of tangible property has been “physically altered” by perils such as fire or water. (Id., at pg. 779.) But when the structure of the property remains unchanged to the naked eye and the insured claims its usefulness for its normal purposes has been destroyed or reduced, it is less clear if the threshold is met. (Ibid.) That the loss needs to be “physical,” given the ordinary meaning of the term, excludes alleged losses that are intangible or incorporeal, and thus precludes any claim against the property insurer when the insured merely suffers a detrimental economic impact unaccompanied by a distinct, demonstrable, physical alteration of the property. (Ibid.) A direct physical loss “contemplates an actual change in insured property then in a satisfactory state, occasioned by accident or other fortuitous event directly upon the property causing it to become unsatisfactory for future use or requiring that repairs be made to make it so.” (Ibid.)

 

Given the report’s findings, Defendant has established there was no distinct, demonstrable, physical alteration of the dwelling. Thus, Defendant has established that there is no direct physical loss and hence, no coverage. Defendant has met the burden of proof that there is no triable issue of material fact as to breach of contract cause of action. The burden shifts to Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff argues that Plaintiff paid the monthly premiums and after Phyllis Price became ill from inhaling smoke during the Bobcat wildfire. Plaintiff also argues that Defendant disregarded the impact the smoke had on Phyllis Price’s health and the opinions of carpet cleaning professionals that Plaintiff contacted in favor of their biased vendors. Plaintiff further argues that Defendant did not meet their obligations under the policy and did not perform any further investigation after FACS had provided their report.

 

None of Plaintiff’s arguments support that the claimed loss falls within the coverage provided by the policy's insuring clause. Thus, Plaintiff has not met its burden.

 

The Court grants summary judgment as to this cause of action.

 

-----

 

Second Cause of Action (Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing) The second cause of action is moot because an insured cannot maintain a claim for tortious breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing absent a covered loss. (Benavides v. State Farm Gen. Ins. Co. (2006) 136 Cal. App. 4th 1241, 1249–50.)

 

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Moving Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the alternative for Summary Adjudication.

Case Number: 24AVCP00260    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

On October 8, 2024, Petitioner Transamerica Life Insurance Company (“Transamerica”) filed a petition for a transfer of structured settlement payment rights belonging to Fabiola Castro. Specifically, Transamerica seeks the transfer of $175,000.00 in future annuity payment rights to be paid on August 7, 2028 and August 7, 2033, the present discounted value of which is $125,924.27, to be transferred in exchange for a $97,972.00 lump sum payment to Castro.

 

 

Analysis

 

Legal Standard - A transfer of structured settlement payment rights is void unless a court reviews and approves the transfer and finds that (a) the transfer of the structured settlement payment rights is fair and reasonable and in the best interest of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of his or her dependents, and (b) the transfer complies with the requirements of Insurance Code Sections 10134 to 10139.5, will not contravene other applicable law, and that the court has reviewed and approved the transfer as provided in Section 10139.5. (See Ins. Code, § 10137, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

Section 10136 Requirements – No direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights by a payee to which Section 10136 applies shall be effective, and no structured settlement obligor or annuity issuer shall be required to make any payment directly or indirectly to a transferee, unless all of the provisions of Section 10136 of the Insurance Code are satisfied. (Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (a).)

 

As a threshold matter, the Court notes that the petition and accompanying documents redact certain information that is necessary for the Court to determine whether the transfer complies with all the requirements of Insurance Code Sections 10134 to 10139.5.

 

First, with the current redactions, the Court cannot verify that the transfer agreement satisfies the disclosure form requirements set out in Insurance Code section 10136, subdivision (b). (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (b); see also Petition, Ex. G, California Disclosure Statement.).

 

Second, the transfer agreement does satisfy the transfer-agreement requirements set out in Insurance Code Section 10136, subdivision (c) because the transfer agreement contains all the necessary language required from this statutory subdivision. (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (c); see also Petition, Ex. F, Purchase and Sale Agreement, §§ 4 [requirements (1) through (12)], p. 7 [requirement (13)].)

 

Third, the transfer agreement does not violate Section 10138 as none of the contents prohibited by this statutory section are contained in the transfer agreement. (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (d); see also Petition, Ex. F, Purchase and Sale Agreement.)

 

Fourth, the Court requires confirmation that Fabiola Castro has not sought cancellation of the transfer agreement prior to the date of this hearing. (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (e).).

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the Transamerica’s proposed transfer of Fabiola Castro’s settlement rights does not satisfy the requirements of Insurance Code section 10136 (a), because the petition does not satisfy the requirements of Insurance Code section 10136 subdivisions (b) or (e).

 

Section 10139.5 Requirements - A direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights is not effective and a structured settlement obligor or annuity issuer is not required to make any payment directly or indirectly to any transferee of structured settlement payment rights unless the transfer has been approved in advance in a final court order based on express written findings by the court subject to Section 10139.5. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a).)

 

Insurance Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)

 

The Court similarly does not find that the petition satisfies the entirety of the findings requirement set out in Insurance Code section 10139.5, subdivisions (a)(1) to (a)(6). Each findings requirement is discussed in turn:

 

(1) The Court does not have sufficient information to find that the transfer is fair and reasonable in the best interests of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee’s dependents. Fabiola Castro is collecting $97,972.00 for future payments of $175,000.00, the discounted present value of which is $125,924.27. Fabiola Castro is currently unemployed due to an arthritis disability. She is married and her husband earns $40,000 per year. If the transfer is approved, Castro intends to use the proceeds to pay credit card debt, pay back loans from family members, purchase a used vehicle, start a nail salon business, save for a future home purchase, and put the remaining funds in an emergency savings account. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(1) [framed by Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subds. (b)(1)-(15)]; see also Petition, Ex. F, Purchase and Sale Agreement, pp. 2-3; Ex. H, Decl., ¶¶ 12-13, 15.)

 

The petition is not clear as to Fabiola Castro’s dependent. (Compare Petition ¶ 10(B) [“Payee has one dependent”], with Ex. H, Decl. [“…we have no dependents.”].) Additionally, in determining whether the proposed transfer should be approved, the Court is required to consider the totality of the circumstances (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (b)(1) [“…the court shall consider the totality of the circumstances, including, but not limited to, all of the following: [15 considerations]”.) The redactions throughout the petition prevent the Court from adequately doing so.    

 

(2) The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent legal or financial advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive the advice. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(2); see also Petition, Ex. G, California Disclosure Statement [Payee was advised to seek independent professional advice]; Ex. H, Decl. ¶ 10 [“In the Disclosure Statement, Transamerica Life advised me in writing to seek independent professional advice regarding the legal, tax and financial consequences of the Agreement and the proposed transfer and I have received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing the opportunity to receive the advice.”]; Ex I, Independent Professional Advice Acknowledgement.)

 

(3) The transferee, i.e. Transamerica, has not complied with the notification requirements pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (f), and as discussed above, the Court does not find that Transamerica has complied with the requirements of Insurance Code section 10136. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(3); see also Ins. Code, § 10136, discussion supra & Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (f) infra.)

 

(4) The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(4).)

 

(5) The payee, Fabiola Castro, understands the terms sets forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136. However, the Court does not make an express finding that Castro understands the terms of the transfer agreement. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(5); see also Ex. H Decl., ¶ 7 [“I carefully reviewed the Disclosure Statement and fully and completely understand all terms of the Disclosure Statement.”].)

 

(6) The Court does not make an express finding that Fabiola Castro understands and does not wish to exercise the right to cancel the transfer agreement. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(6).)

 

Insurance Code, § 10139.5, subd. (c)

 

The petition does not satisfy all the payee information required from Section 10139.5, subdivision (c). Specifically, the Court notes that the payee has not sufficiently provided the information required in Insurance Code section 10139.5, subdivisions (c)(2)-(5).

 

Insurance Code, § 10139.5, subd. (f)

 

The petition does not satisfy the requirements of Section 10139.5, subdivision (f). Specifically, the Court notes that payee has not satisfied the requirements of Insurance Code section 10139.5, subdivision (f)(2)(J)-(K).

 

Insurance Code, § 10139.5, subd. (g)

 

The petition satisfies the requirements of Section 10139.5, subdivision (g) requiring that transferees bear the costs of transfer petitions of structured settlement rights. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (g); see also Petition, Ex. F, Purchase and Sale Contract, p. 2 [“An itemized listing of all commissions, estimated fees, costs, expenses, and charges payable by you or deductible from the gross amount otherwise payable to you is as follows: NONE”] & Ex. G, California Disclosure Statement, p. 2 [“To figure the net amount we are paying, we have charged you for the following expenses: NONE for a total of $0 in expenses.”].)

 

Insurance Code, § 10139.5, subd. (h)

 

The petition satisfies the requirements of Section 10139.5, subdivision (h) because, when the petition was filed, Transamerica advised the payee of the right to seek independent counsel and financial advice in connection with this transfer petition and advised the payee of the right to a reimbursement of $1,500 for incurring such costs. (See Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (h); see also Petition, Ex. G, California Disclosure Statement [Payee was advised to seek independent professional advice]; Ex. H, Decl. ¶ 10 [“In the Disclosure Statement, Transamerica Life advised me in writing to seek independent professional advice regarding the legal, tax and financial consequences of the Agreement and the proposed transfer and I have received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing the opportunity to receive the advice.”]; Ex I., Independent Professional Advice Acknowledgement.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Transamerica Life Insurance Company’s Petition for the Approval of a Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights is denied due to the deficiencies as described above.

 

Conclusion

 

Petitioner/Transferee Transamerica Life Insurance Company’s Petition for the Approval of a Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights is DENIED.
