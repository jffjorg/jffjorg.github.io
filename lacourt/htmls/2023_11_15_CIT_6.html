DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: KC069796    Hearing Date: November 15, 2023    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Pomona Valley Hospital Medical Center v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc., et al.

Pomona Valley Hospital’s Motion for Pre-judgment Interest 

TENTATIVE RULING

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for prejudgment interest. The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to prejudgment interest, that the applicable prejudgment interest rate is 10%, and that the date of accrual is December 28, 2022. 

Plaintiff shall submit a proposed Judgment within 10 calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND

This is a medical service provider payment dispute. On November 14, 2017, Plaintiff Pomona Valley Hospital Medical Center (Plaintiff) filed this action against Defendants Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (Kaiser), Kaiser Permanente Insurance Company, Kaiser Foundation Hospitals, The Permanente Medical Group, Southern California Permanente Medical Group, and Does 1 through 100, alleging causes of action for declaratory relief and unlawful business practices. On March 27, 2019, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint, alleging causes of action for breach of written contract and quantum meruit. 

On January 31, 2023, a jury trial for Phase I concerning the reasonable value of services Plaintiff provided to Kaiser was conducted. The jury found that the reasonable value of the services provided was $105,887,963.00. After deducting $39,796,251.00 for payments Kaiser previously made, the jury awarded Plaintiff a total of $66,091,712.00. 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant trial brief for prejudgment interest. On November 1, 2023, Kaiser opposed the motion.[1] On November 7, 2023, Plaintiff replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD

(a) A person who is entitled to recover damages certain, or capable of being made certain by calculation, and the right to recover which is vested in the person upon a particular day, is entitled also to recover interest thereon from that day, except when the debtor is prevented by law, or by the act of the creditor from paying the debt. This section is applicable to recovery of damages and interest from any debtor, including the state or any county, city, city and county, municipal corporation, public district, public agency, or any political subdivision of the state. 

(b) Every person who is entitled under any judgment to receive damages based upon a cause of action in contract where the claim was unliquidated, may also recover interest thereon from a date prior to the entry of judgment as the court may, in its discretion, fix, but in no event earlier than the date the action was filed. 

(Civ. Code § 3287, subds. (a), (b).) 

PRELIMINARY ISSUES

            The Court notes Kaiser’s argument that Plaintiff’s trial brief is procedurally defective on the grounds that it is not accompanied by a written notice. (Opp., p. 2 fn. 1.) While the Court agrees it is really a motion and not a trial brief, the Court finds Plaintiff gave adequate notice to Kaiser as evidenced by the caption page of Plaintiff’s moving papers identifying the date, time, and location for the hearing on this matter. The Court agrees that a more formal notice beyond just the caption page would be preferable, and Plaintiff did file such a written notice on November 7, 2023, but the Court nevertheless does not find the notice originally given to be insufficient. Additionally, Kaiser timely opposed the motion, so there does not appear to be any prejudice to Kaiser. The Court exercises its discretion to still consider Plaintiff’s motion, (Cal. Rules of Court, 3.1300, subd. (d); Juarez v. Wash Depot Holdings, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1197, 1202), but nevertheless admonishes Plaintiff to comply with the requirements of the California Rules of Court and Code of Civil Procedure going forward. 

            The Court also notes that Plaintiff submitted a supplemental declaration with its reply papers. Generally, new evidence is not permitted on reply unless it fills in gaps in the evidence created by the opposing party’s opposition and is not raising new substantive issues for the first time; otherwise, a further hearing would be required to permit the opposing party to respond. (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537-1538.) Thus, to the extent the new evidence submitted does not respond to issues raised in the opposition, the Court declines to consider it. 

OBJECTIONS

            The Court OVERRULES the parties’ evidentiary objections, as the Court does not rely on the objected to evidence in reaching its decision herein.   

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            The Court GRANTS Kaiser’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (a).) However, the Court takes judicial notice only as to “the existence, content and authenticity of public records and other specified documents”; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal. App. 5th 389, 400.) 

DISCUSSION

Whether Plaintiff is Entitled to Prejudgment Interest

"There is no authoritative list of criteria for courts to consider, and ‘[f]ew cases have discussed the standards by which a trial court's exercise of discretion under section 3287, subdivision (b) are to be judged.’ [Citation.]” (Hewlett-Packard Co. v. Oracle Corp. (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 506, 577; see also North Oakland Medical Clinic v. Rogers (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 824, 829 [“Under subdivision (b) the court has discretion to decide whether prejudgment interest should be awarded on an unliquidated contractual claim. It is up to the judge to determine the date from which interest runs, but in no event may the court fix a date earlier than the filing of the action.”) Some factors that have been considered include improper delay, whether the damages were highly contested and uncertain, the effect of the defendant’s partial performance under the contract, and whether the jury was aware of the delay and considered the delay when determining the amount to award. (Hewlett-Packard Co., supra, 65 Cal.App.5th at p. 577.) 

Plaintiff contends there is evidence of improper delay, as this action has been pending for six years, caused by Kaiser seeking and obtaining multiple trial continuances, plus the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic. Plaintiff argues Kaiser has had the ability to invest or use the money that belongs to Plaintiff elsewhere during this time period to Kaiser’s benefit and Plaintiff’s detriment. 

In opposition, Kaiser contends Plaintiff has no right to prejudgment interest because its claims were unliquidated and therefore has no right to prejudgment interest under Civil Code section 3287, subdivision (a). Kaiser further contends Plaintiff’s request for discretionary prejudgment interest under Civil Code section 3298, subdivision (b) should be denied because: (1) Plaintiff has not provided the date or dates it is asking the Court to fix from which prejudgment interest will accrue, this issue was not presented to the jury, the jury did not allocate any specific amount to any specific claim, and subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 3287 does not permit interest to accrue from before the action was filed; and (2) Plaintiff contributed to the delays by changing the scope of this action with its First Amended Complaint seventeen months into the lawsuit, damages were highly contested and uncertain, Kaiser partially performed, and the jury was aware of the delay in payment and Plaintiff used it to justify its requested award. 

The Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to prejudgment interest. First, the Court finds the sheer length of time that has lapsed between the filing of the lawsuit and now to weigh in Plaintiff’s favor. Six years is a long time to wait to get paid.[2] Even if Plaintiff modified the scope of this action with its First Amended Complaint seventeen months into this lawsuit or otherwise modified its claims later on, there is no indication this was done intentionally to delay or otherwise improperly protract the resolution of this action. Plaintiff has also shown, and Kaiser has not disputed, that Kaiser requested all of the trial continuances that were granted in this action. 

Second, Kaiser’s arguments regarding subdivision (a) of Civil Code section 3287 are irrelevant since Plaintiff clearly brought this motion under subdivision (b) of Civil Code section 3287, which gives the Court the discretion to award prejudgment interest based on a cause of action in contract where the claim was unliquidated. (Civ. Code § 3287, subd. (b).) 

Third, while the Court agrees that Plaintiff did not provide the dates for each of the underlying payment claims for purposes of determining the prejudgment interest date, that is not absolutely necessary for the Court to determine whether Plaintiff is entitled to prejudgment interest at this time. The Court also agrees with Plaintiff that this is a rather disingenuous argument to make since Kaiser requested the trial be bifurcated into two phases to first allow a determination of the reasonable value of services provided and then determine the other issues. 

Fourth, the Court also agrees with Plaintiff that Kaiser merely disputing the reasonable value of Plaintiff’s damages does not necessarily make the amounts at issue highly contested. Any defendant can and understandably is going to dispute the value of the plaintiff’s claimed damages. However, the Court finds Kaiser’s arguments about seven days of trial testimony with a wide variation in the evidence regarding the reasonable value of services to weigh in favor of the issues being highly contested. (Opp., 8:7-16.) 

Fifth, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that partial performance by itself is insufficient, as any payment constitutes partial performance. The Court recognizes that the jury credited Kaiser’s prior payment of $39,796,251.00 to its determination of the reasonable value of Plaintiff’s services and reduced the reasonable value owed to Plaintiff by approximately 40%, which is not an insignificant amount. However, the Court does not find this factor sufficient to deny Plaintiff prejudgment interest. 

Sixth, while it does appear the jury was aware of the delay (Opp., 9:3-21), there is no indication that the delay was factored into the jury’s determination of the reasonable value of Plaintiff’s services to Kaiser. In Hewlett-Packard, to which Kaiser cites in support of its arguments here, there was evidence that the jury in that case had factored the delay into its damage award. (Hewlett-Packard, supra, 65 Cal.App.5th at p. 578 fn. 19.) 

Based on the consideration of the foregoing, and balancing the concerns of fairness and just compensation, the Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to prejudgment interest. 

Applicable Interest Rate

“If a complete claim, or portion thereof, that is neither contested nor denied, is not reimbursed by delivery to the claimant's address of record within the respective 30 or 45 working days after receipt, the plan shall pay the greater of fifteen dollars ($15) per year or interest at the rate of 15 percent per annum beginning with the first calendar day after the 30- or 45-working-day period. A health care service plan shall automatically include the fifteen dollars ($15) per year or interest due in the payment made to the claimant, without requiring a request therefor.” (Health & Safety Code § 1371.35, subd. (b).) 

Plaintiff contends the applicable rate for prejudgment interest is 15% per subdivision (b) of section 1371.35 of the Health and Safety Code. Kaiser contends this rate is inapplicable because that code section applies to the claims adjudication process, and not in the context of litigation over the fair market value of claims. The Court agrees with Kaiser. 

The jury’s verdict was based on a claim of quantum meruit and not a claim for violation of Health and Safety Code section 1371.35. The Court is not bound by the regulatory provisions of such section in deciding the rate for prejudgment interest. (See Cohen v. Health Net of California, Inc. (2005) 129 Cal. App. 4th 841, 860 n. 4 [Court concluded that “Health and Safety Code section 1371.35 is of purely regulatory import and the DMHC has exclusive power to enforce its requirements.”]) Plaintiff has not cited any case law supporting its contention that the Knox-Keene Act, particularly section 1371.35, applies to prejudgment interest in litigation. 

The Court adopts the reasoning and rationale applied with respect to Cal. Code Regs., tit. 18, § 1300.71(a)(3)(B). As the Court of Appeal stated in Children's Hospital Central California v. Blue Cross of California (2014) 226 Cal. App. 4th 1260: 

“In the final statement of reasons for California Code of Regulations, title 28, section 1300.71, the DMHC explained that the intent was to establish a methodology for determining the reasonable value of health care services by noncontracted providers but that the criteria specified do not dictate a specific payment rate. Rather, the payor is required to calculate the appropriate reimbursement based on statistically credible information that takes the Gould factors into consideration. If a payor fulfills its claims payment obligation using these criteria, the DMHC will consider the payor compliant with Health and Safety Code sections 1371 and 1371.35, i.e., the reimbursement of the claim will be deemed timely. ‘However, the definition is not a substitute for traditional forums for contract dispute resolution. If a provider disputes the payor's calculation of the fair and reasonable value of the health care services he has rendered, the provider is free to seek resolution of that dispute in a court of law or through any other available civil remedy.’” 


(Id., at p. 1273.)

Bell v. Blue Cross of California (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 211 does not support Plaintiff’s contention. The Court of Appeal in Bell held that the Knox-Keene Act “left the physicians free to pursue alternate theories to recover the reasonable value of their services. Furthermore, the physicians’ claim under the UCL did not infringe on the Department of Managed Health Care’s jurisdiction, and there was no bar to the physicians’ common law quantum meruit claim.” (Id., at p. 212.) The Court went on to state: “Although the department has jurisdiction over the subject matter of Health & Saf. Code, § 1371.4, as well as the rest of the act, its jurisdiction is not exclusive, and there is nothing in § 1371.4 or in the act generally to preclude a private action under the UCL or at common law on a quantum meruit theory.” (Id., at p. 212; see also Id., at pp. 216-217.) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court declines to apply the prejudgment interest rate of 15% per Health and Safety Code section 1371.35, subdivision (b). 

The Court will apply the prejudgment interest rate of 10% set forth in Civil Code section 3289 to this action for quantum meruit. The Court finds this action to be considered an action on the contract. “[F]or most purposes, an action to recover the reasonable value of services is considered an action on the contract.” (George v. Double-D Foods, Inc. (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 36, 47.) 

Date of Accrual for Prejudgment Interest

Plaintiff contends the date of accrual for prejudgment interest is the date Kaiser underpaid Plaintiff’s claims. Kaiser argues against any award of prejudgment interest but alternatively argues that the accrual date should be the date of the jury verdict. The Court disagrees with both. 

Civil Code section 3287, subdivision (b), clearly states that the earliest date the Court can set for determination of prejudgment interest thereunder is the date the action was filed. (Civ. Code § 3287, subd. (b).) Although Plaintiff does not provide the dates for when its claims against Kaiser first accrued, the Court infers that some of the claims necessarily accrued before Plaintiff filed this action. Thus, the earliest date the Court can set for accrual here is November 14, 2017. 

The Court notes, however, that Plaintiff contends that the claims submitted to the jury had dates ranging from October 2017 through March 2020. (Tooch Decl., ¶ 8.) Since the case was decided on claims presented after the action was filed, the Court declines to set the accrual date as the date the action was filed. In balancing the interests of fairness and full compensation, the Court sets the accrual date as December 28, 2022, the date the parties settled upon the list of claims to be included in the Phase 1 trial. (Kaiser’s Opp. p. 7:9-10; Schlesinger Decl., ¶ 7m Ex. E.) The Court notes that Plaintiff does not dispute the accuracy of this date in its Reply. Accordingly, the Court finds that the appropriate date of accrual for prejudgment interest is December 28, 2022. 

CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for prejudgment interest. The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to prejudgment interest, that the applicable prejudgment interest rate is 10%, and that the date of accrual is December 28, 2022. 

Plaintiff shall submit a proposed Judgment within 10 calendar days of this order.
