[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: CROSS-DEFENDANTS' DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINT FILED BY TRUONG NGOC

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

May 13, 2024

 

On October 18, 2022, plaintiff Lyco 1, LLC (“Lyco 1”) filed this action for partition against defendant Michael Cheng (“Michael”) regarding real property at 3603 Vane Avenue, Rosemead, California 91770 (the “Property”). Lyco 1 is comprised of Randy Cheng (“Randy”) and Alice Liu (“Alice”), who are husband and wife; Randy and Michael are brothers. (As many of the parties in this action share the same last name, the Court refers to them by their first names for clarity and intends no disrespect.)

On August 23, 2023, Michael filed the First Amended Cross-Complaint against Lyco 1, Randy, Alice, Truong Ngoc (“Ngoc”), Steve Q. Cheng, Lydia Cheng, and Fiona Cheng. Ngoc is the mother of Randy and Michael.

On November 21, 2023, Ngoc filed a cross-complaint against Lyco 1, Randy, and Alice, claiming an undivided 50% tenant in common fee title to the Property. Ngoc seeks a declaratory judgment establishing a life estate in the Property for her benefit. Ngoc also seeks a judicial determination to settle and establish a resulting trust.

On January 25, 2024, Lyco 1, Alice, and Randy (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”) filed this demurrer to Ngoc’s Cross-Complaint. Cross-Defendants argue that the cross-complaint fails to join Michael and that the court is unable to impose a life estate on a property owner.

Failure to Join Michael as a Party

A party may demur to a cross-complaint on the ground that “[t]here is a defect or misjoinder of parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (d).) Code of Civil Procedure section 389 (a) provides:

A person who is subject to service of process and whose joinder will not deprive the court of jurisdiction over the subject matter of the action shall be joined as a party in the action if (1) in his absence complete relief cannot be accorded among those already parties or (2) he claims an interest relating to the subject of the action and is so situated that the disposition of the action in his absence may (i) as a practical matter impair or impede his ability to protect that interest or (ii) leave any of the persons already parties subject to a substantial risk of incurring double, multiple, or otherwise inconsistent obligations by reason of his claimed interest. If he has not been so joined, the court shall order that he be made a party.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 389, subd. (a).)

          Cross-Defendants argue that it is necessary to join Michael because Ngoc’s cross-complaint implicates his interests in the Property. But Ngoc’s cross-complaint only seeks a judicial determination of Cross-Defendant’s one-half interest in the Property. The controversy does not exist between Ngoc and Michael, but between Ngoc and Cross-Defendants. Also, Michael is already a party to this action because he has filed his own cross-complaint against Ngoc and Cross-Defendants. Therefore, there is no risk that his ability to protect his own interests in the Property are impaired or impeded. Accordingly, the demurrer to Ngoc’s entire cross-complaint on this ground is OVERRULED.

          Failure to State Sufficient Facts

          Next, Cross-Defendants demur to Ngoc’s cause of action for declaratory relief on the grounds that Ngoc fails to state sufficient facts to support her claim to a life estate in the Property. A party may demur to a pleading on the ground that the pleading “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

“A life estate is an estate whose duration is limited to the life of the person holding it or of some other person.” (Peterson v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 844, 850–851.) “A life estate may be granted or reserved by a deed, or it may be created by a will. If created by deed, it is created on the delivery of the deed; if created by will, it is created on the death of the decedent. It also may be created by implication, as when a grantor delivers a deed to a third party to be given to the grantee on the grantor's death. (Miller and Starr, 4 Cal. Real Est. § 12:20 (4th ed.)) “It is not essential that the term “life estate” be used in the instrument creating the estate nor that it be declared expressly to be a life estate if adequate words are used describing its characteristics and showing an intention to vest a life estate.” (Ibid.)

Ngoc does not allege the factual basis for her claim that a life estate was created for her, nor does Ngoc explain how the Court may create a life estate absent any written instrument. Ngoc does not allege that Cross-Defendants are estopped from asserting the statute of frauds, nor does she allege any facts supporting such a claim.

Accordingly, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer to the First Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days’ leave to amend.

I.       INTRODUCTION

         On December 6, 2023, plaintiffs Gabriela Quintero Lopez and Jorge Arnulfo Hernandez Lopez, individually and as successors-in-interest to Alma Rosa Lopez (“Decedent”), along with Claudia Lorena Martinez (“Martinez”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), filed this action against defendants Beador Construction Company (“Beador”), State of California (“Defendant”), County of Los Angeles, and City of Pasadena asserting causes of action for negligence, premises liability, and dangerous condition of public property. Plaintiffs allege that on or about August 25, 2023, Martinez and Decedent were driving on the westbound 134 Freeway when a piece of metal from a construction site became airborne and went through the vehicle striking Decedent in the head. (Compl., ¶ 17.) Decedent later passed away from her injuries on September 16, 2023.

Plaintiffs allege that Defendant negligently failed to maintain, inspect, control, and supervise the construction site and the equipment that was used on the site, and its failure to do so caused the piece of metal to strike Decedent through the vehicle. (Compl., ¶18.) Plaintiffs assert common law actions for negligence and premise liability as well as a claim for dangerous condition of public property pursuant to Government Code section 835.

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face. (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.)  “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.  [Citation.]” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) In construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations.  (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769.)

III.    DISCUSSION

“The California Tort Claims Act provides that ‘a public entity is not liable for an injury,’ ‘except as otherwise provided by statute.’  (Gov. Code, § 815, subd. (a).) As that language indicates, the intent of the Tort Claims Act is to confine potential governmental liability, not expand it.” (Eastburn v. Regional Fire Protection Authority (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1179.) Therefore, public entities cannot be liable for common law theories of general negligence and premises liability. (Miklosy v. Regents of University of California (2008) 44 Cal.4th 876, 899 [“section 815 abolishes common law tort liability for public entities”].) Instead, liability against a public entity must be authorized by statute. (Eastburn, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1179.) A demurrer is properly sustained where a plaintiff fails to allege a statutory basis for liability against a public entity. (Tilton v. Reclamation Dist. No. 800 (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 848, 863-864.)

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ common law claim for negligence and premises liability because Plaintiffs fail to allege a statutory basis for liability against a public entity. As part of both causes of action, Plaintiffs allege that Defendant “knew or should have known that [Beador] [was] unfit to operate the equipment at the [construction site] and knew or should have known the equipment was defective and therefore, [Defendant] [was] negligent in hiring, retaining, training, and entrusting the subject equipment to [Beador].” (Compl., ¶¶ 23, 34.) Since the First and Second Causes of Action do not identify a statutory basis for liability, Defendant’s argument is well-taken and the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the First Cause of Action with 20 days’ leave to amend. The demurrer to the Second Cause of Action for premises liability is SUSTAINED without leave to amend because the applicable statute imposing liability on a public entity for a dangerous condition of property is Government Code section 835. (Longfellow v. County of San Luis Obispo (1983) 144 Cal.App.3d 379, 383.)

The Court acknowledges that Plaintiffs refer to Government Code section 815.4 in their opposition brief, which provides that a public entity is liable for the torts of an independent contractor, such as Beador, “to the same extent that the public entity would be subject to such liability if it were a private person.” (Gov. Code, § 815.4.) But this section is only included as part of the Third Cause of Action. Also, there are no factual allegations from which an independent contractor relationship between Defendant and Beador may be inferred. (Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103.)

The Court additionally notes that while the demurrer to these two causes of action is sustained, the Court rejects Defendant’s claim that Plaintiffs are obligated to identify any particular employee who committed a tortious act in order to state a valid claim. (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) The Court also rejects Defendant’s blanket assertion that a public entity cannot be held liable for the acts and omissions of its independent contractors because Government Code section 840 prevents public employees from being liable for dangerous conditions of public property. The language of section 840 is more limited and provides that “except as provided in this article”, a public employee is not liable for injuries caused by a condition of public property where the condition exists because of any act or omission of the employee within the scope of employment. Government Code section 840.2, in turn, sets forth the conditions in which a public employee may be found liable.

Next, the Court addresses Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Third Cause of Action for dangerous condition of public property pursuant to Government Code section 835. Defendant argues that Plaintiffs do not identify a dangerous condition. The Court agrees. A dangerous condition of public property must be a physical defect. (Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1135.) Here, Plaintiffs only allege that Defendant (and its employees and/or agents) “chose to use defective equipment that ultimately led to a piece of metal going airborne and striking [P]laintifs’ vehicle.” They do not identify a physical condition on the property which is dangerous or defective.

Therefore, the demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days’ leave to amend.

IV.    CONCLUSION

The demurrer to the First and Third Causes of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days’ leave to amend.

The demurrer to the Second Cause of Action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
