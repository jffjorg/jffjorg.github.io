DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV00064    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint

 

Moving Parties: Defendants Dalton Erb and Energy Bee Farm                   

Responding Party: Plaintiff Nazila Mahgerefth

 

RULING

 

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED in part and OVERRULED in PART. Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED in part and GRANTED in part.


PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

On January 9, 2024, Plaintiff Nazila Mahgerefth (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Dalton Erb (“Erb”) and Energy Bee Farm (“EBF”) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for (1) Ralph Act; (2) Bane Act; (3) Assault; and (4) Negligence.

 

On March 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) alleging the same causes of action.

 

On May 21, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer and motion to strike certain portions of the FAC.

On July 10, 2024, the court sustained the demurrer in its entirety and granted the motion to strike in part.

 

On July 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) Ralph Act; (2) Bane Act; and (3) Negligence.

 

On September 30, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer and motion to strike portions of the SAC. On October 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On October 30, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

 

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff is an animal rights activist who often attends the City of Torrance’s Farmer’s Market (the “Market”) to buy produce and attend protests. (SAC, ¶¶ 9, 14.)

 

Defendant Erb works at EBF, a business that sells honey at the Market. (SAC, ¶¶ 17, 19.) EBF is owned by Erb’s father, Jeffrey Erb. (SAC, ¶ 17.)

 

The Market usually prohibits live animals inside its perimeter, but it allowed one vendor to sell live animals. (SAC, ¶¶ 25-26.) Plaintiff notified the Market manager of the live animal vendor, but the Market failed to take action. (SAC, ¶ 26.) Plaintiff began to verbally protest the live animal vendor. (SAC, ¶ 26.)

 

After a few minutes of protesting the live animal vendor, Plaintiff walked over to Defendants’ booth to protest the sale of honey products. (SAC, ¶ 30.) When Plaintiff came within five feet of Erb, he yelled profanities at her and threatened to knock her out. (SAC, ¶¶ 31-32.) Plaintiff notes that Erb is considerably larger than her and has an athletic build. (SAC, ¶ 35.) Plaintiff alleges that Erb believed Plaintiff’s protest would drive off customers, so he was incentivized to threaten her. (SAC, ¶ 36.) Alternatively, he threatened her because of her political affiliation as an animal rights activist. (SAC, ¶ 37.)

 

After Plaintiff filed the original complaint, EBF allowed Erb to continue working at the Market booth. (SAC, ¶ 40.) EBF did not take any disciplinary measures against Erb. (SAC, ¶ 40.) EBF continued to allow Erb to interact with Plaintiff, despite knowing that Plaintiff was a regular patron at the Market. (SAC, ¶ 40.)

 

Plaintiff also alleges that Jeffrey Erb shouted profanities at the process server who served him with the original complaint. (SAC, ¶ 44.)

 

Defendants demur to all three causes of action on the grounds that they fail to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain. Defendants also seek to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint which assert that “vegan” is a political affiliation; describe Jeffrey Erb’s actions; or discuss Plaintiff’s interactions with the live animal vendor. Defendants also seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.

           

LEGAL STANDARD         

 

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) 

 

DISCUSSION          

 

Moving Party’s Argument

 

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s SAC on the grounds that it fails to state sufficient facts and is uncertain.

 

Opposing Party’s Argument

 

As a preliminary matter, the court notes that Plaintiff’s opposition was filed late. However, the court exercises its discretion to consider late papers, pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (d). 

 

Plaintiff argues that she has alleged sufficient facts to support her causes of action. Further, Plaintiff’s claims vicarious liability against EBF based on ratification, which Defendants do not dispute in their demurrer.

 

Reply Argument

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff has provided no facts to support her theory of ratification. Further, Defendants disagree with Plaintiff’s analysis of the foreseeability of the incident.

 

Merits of the Motion

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) Motions to strike have a similar meet and confer requirement.

 

Defense counsel declares that he sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer email, but Plaintiff’s attorney did not respond. Plaintiff’s attorney declares that he did respond to the emails, but he got no response in return.

 

The court finds that the parties have not satisfied the meet and confer requirements. However, the court will still discuss the merits of the motion and rule accordingly.

 

B.  First Cause of Action for Ralph Act

 

The Ralph Civil Rights Act provides that “All persons within the jurisdiction of this state have the right to be free from any violence, or intimidation by threat of violence, committed against their persons or property because of political affiliation, or on account of any characteristic listed or defined in subdivision (b) or (e) of section 51, or position in a labor dispute, or because another person perceives them to have one or more of those characteristics." (Civ. Code, § 51.7, subd. (b)(1).)  

 

To plead a claim for violation of the Ralph Act, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant threatened or committed violent acts against the plaintiff; (2) the defendant was motivated by his perception of plaintiff’s protected characteristic; (3) the plaintiff was harmed; and (4) the defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing the plaintiff’s harm. (Austin B. v. Escondido Union School Dist., supra, 149 Cal.App.4th at pp. 880-81.)

 

Defendants demur to this cause of action as it applies to EBF on the basis that Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts to support a theory of vicarious liability. Plaintiff argues that she has stated sufficient facts to show that EBF ratified Erb’s conduct.

 

“‘As an alternate theory to respondeat superior, an employer may be liable for an employee's act where the employer either authorized the tortious act or subsequently ratified an originally unauthorized tort. [Citations.] The failure to discharge an employee who has committed misconduct may be evidence of ratification. [Citations.] The theory of ratification is generally applied where an employer fails to investigate or respond to charges that an employee committed an intentional tort, such as assault or battery.  [Citations.] Whether an employer has ratified an employee's conduct is generally a factual question. [Citation.]’ (Baptist v. Robinson (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 151, 169-170.) A principal may be liable when it ratifies an originally unauthorized tort. [Citations.] And generally, the ratification relates back to the time the tortious act occurred[Citations.] …[R]atification may [also] occur when an employer learns of misconduct and fails to discharge an agent or employee. [Citations.] (C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp. (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1110-1111.) Ratification, like agency, may be pleaded generally. (Id. at p. 1112.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged that Erb threatened violence against her due to her political affiliations. She has also alleged that EBF learned about Erb’s threats and did not investigate him. Further, EBF continued to employ Erb without taking any corrective action. At this stage of the proceedings, the court finds that Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to support a theory of ratification. Thus, EBF may be liable for Erb’s violation of the Ralph Act based on ratification.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

C.  Second Cause of Action for Bane Act

 

Civil Code, section 52.1 (the “Bane Act”) allows an individual to sue for damages if a person or persons “interferes by threat, intimidation, or coercion, or attempts to interfere by threat, intimidation, or coercion, with the exercise or enjoyment by any individual or individuals of rights secured by the Constitution or laws of the United States, or of the rights secured by the Constitution or laws of this state….” (Civ. Code, § 52.1, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

“The essence of a Bane Act claim is that the defendant, by the specified improper means (i.e.,‘threat[ ], intimidation or coercion’), tried to or did prevent the plaintiff from doing something he or she had the right to do under the law or to force the plaintiff to do something that he or she was not required to do under the law.” (King v. State of Cal. (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 265, 294; Austin B. v. Escondido Union School Dist. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 860, 883.) “The statute requires a showing of threatening conduct independent from the alleged interference or violation of a civil right. [Citations.]” (Doe v. State of California (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 832, 842–843.) It was passed primarily as hate crime legislation (Jones v. Kmart Corp (1998) 17 Cal.4th 329, 338) and was meant to narrowly supplement the Ralph Act to deter violence. (Stamps v. Superior Court (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1447.)  

 

The elements of a Bane Act claim are: (1) defendant interfered with or attempted to interfere with plaintiffs’ constitutional or statutory right by threatening or committing violent acts; (2) plaintiffs reasonably believed that if they exercised their constitutional right the defendant would commit violence against them or their property; (3) defendant injured plaintiffs or their property to prevent them from exercising their constitutional right or retaliated against plaintiffs for having exercised their constitutional right; (4) plaintiffs were harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiffs' harm. (Austin B. v. Escondido Union School Dist., supra, 149 Cal.App.4th at p. 882.) In context, the statutory framework of section 52.1 “indicates that the Legislature meant the statute to address interference with constitutional rights involving more egregious conduct than mere negligence.” (Shoyoye v. County of Los Angeles (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 947, 958.)

 

Defendant demurs to this cause of action as it applies to EBF on the same basis as above. Plaintiff argues that she has alleged sufficient facts to show that EBF ratified Erb’s violation of the Bane Act.

 

As discussed above, ratification may be pleaded generally. Further, an employer’s failure to take action after learning of misconduct may be evidence of ratification. Thus, Plaintiff has pleaded sufficient facts to support a theory of ratification.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

D.  Third Cause of Action for Negligent Supervision, Hiring, or Retention of Employee

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision are: (1) the employer’s hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee; (2) the employee was incompetent or unfit; (3) the employer had reason to believe undue risk of harm would exist because of the employment; and (4) harm occurs. (Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 828, 836-837.)

 

Defendants argue that this cause of action must fail because Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged that EBF knew or should have known that Erb was unfit before it hired him. Plaintiff argues that she has pleaded sufficient facts because EBF and Erb share a mutual disdain for Plaintiff. As such, EBF should have known that Erb would threaten violence against Plaintiff.

 

The court finds that these facts are insufficient to support a negligent supervision, hiring, or retention cause of action. An employee’s mere dislike of a certain customer does not indicate that the employee poses an increased risk of becoming violent. As there are no other facts alleging Erb’s propensity for violence, Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to maintain this cause of action.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

E.  Uncertainty

 

Generally speaking, “demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and thus are strictly construed because ambiguities can reasonably be clarified under modern rules of discovery. Such demurrers are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Cal.Jur.3d § 137.) “Where the complaint contains substantive factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled or plaintiff given leave to amend.” (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (2011) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139 fn.2.) 

 

In applying the stringent standard for demurrers filed on this ground, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s complaint is not “so incomprehensible” that Defendants cannot respond to it, particularly where Defendants have offered substantive arguments in opposition thereto. Even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. (A.J. Fistes Corp. v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695.) 

 

F.  Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., §436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

Defendants seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages in paragraphs 53, 60, and 68. As discussed above, Plaintiff has sufficiently pleaded EBF’s ratification of acts which could justify the imposition of punitive damages. Thus, Defendants’ motion to strike is DENIED as to these paragraphs.

 

Defendants seek to strike paragraphs 13-16 of the SAC in its entirety. The court GRANTS the motion to strike as to paragraphs 13, 14, and 16. The court has already determined in a prior ruling that “vegan” is not a political affiliation for the purposes of Plaintiff’s causes of action.  There is no assertion that Defendants were involved in other acts against Plaintiff, so other “examples” of consequences from Plaintiff’s beliefs are irrelevant.

 

The court denies Defendant’s motion to strike paragraphs 26, 44, and 45 in their entirety as they contain allegations that may be relevant to Plaintiff’s causes of action.


G.  Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff has not shown that she can successfully amend her complaint. Accordingly, leave to amend is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the court OVERRULES the demurrer to the first and second causes of action and SUSTAINS the demurrer to the third cause of action. Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. Leave to amend is DENIED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice of ruling.
