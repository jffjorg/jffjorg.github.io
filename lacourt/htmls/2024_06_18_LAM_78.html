DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV21303    Hearing Date: June 18, 2024    Dept: 78

Superior Court of California 
County of Los Angeles 
Department 78 
 
KENNY KAPOOR, et al., 
Plaintiffs,  
vs. 
ZALFA HALABY, et al., 
Defendants. Case No.: 20STCV21303 
Hearing Date: June 18, 2024 
 
[TENTATIVE] RULING RE:  
MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR ADJUDICATION FILED BY DEFENDANT FIRST AMERICAN TITLE COMPANY TO THE SAC FILED BY THE ELKINS PLAINTIFFS ON MAY 12, 2023
FATC’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.
Moving party to provide notice.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On May 12, 2023, Zalfa Halaby Elkins (Z. Elkins), Charles Elkins (C. Elkins), and Rez Properties, LLC (Texas) (Plaintiffs) filed their Second Amended Complaint (SAC).
On June 2, 2023, Defendant First American Title Company (FATC) filed this motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication as to the SAC filed by the Elkins Plaintiffs.
On May 9, 2024, the Elkins Plaintiffs filed an opposition.
On June 13, 2024, FATC filed a reply.
DISCUSSION
I. LEGAL STANDARD
A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c, subd. (a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)   
The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if he does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) On a plaintiff’s motion, the plaintiff must present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not. (Id. at p.845.) The plaintiff meets his burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if he has proved each element on that cause of action. (Id. at p.849.) The burden then shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense. (Id.)
“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., section 437c(c).) 
II. OBJECTIONS
The Elkins Plaintiffs object to the evidence presented by FATC, specifically to portions of the Declaration of Eugene Kim.

The following objections are OVERRULED: 2, 4, 5, 6, 7.
III. ANALYSIS
FATC moves for summary judgment as to the third cause of action for negligence, the only cause of action in which it is named.
“The elements of a cause of action for negligence are well established. They are (a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917, internal quotations omitted.) “The first element, duty, may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.” (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1128, internal quotations omitted.) “[T]he existence of a duty is a question of law for the court.” (Ky. Fried Chicken of Cal. v. Superior Court (1997) 14 Cal.4th 814, 819.)
Here, FATC moves for summary judgment on the grounds that it owed no duty to Plaintiffs. FATC argues that (1) Plaintiffs lack standing to assert a cause of action for negligence, (2) FATC had no duty and breached no duty to confirm whether Clifford Sullivan was authorized to sign a quitclaim deed on behalf of Rez Properties, (3) FATC had no duty and breached no duty to confirm whether Rez Properties was legally capable of conveying real property. 
This action concerns a dispute over a property located at 29321 Hacienda Ranch Court, Valencia, CA 91354 (the Hacienda Property). Zalfa Halaby Elkins and/or Charles Elkins had an ownership interest in a real estate development company, South Shore LLC, at all times relevant to the SAC. (SAC ¶¶ 5, 16.) 
In 2020, when Plaintiffs and Kapoor elected to dissolve South Shore, they also agreed that South Shore would convey the Hacienda Property to, REZ Properties, LLC, a Texas Limited Company (REZ-TX), an entity owned by Plaintiffs. (UMF Nos. 5-7.) Academy Escrow incorrectly conveyed the property to an entity of the same name in California (REZ-CA). (UMF Nos. 8-10.) At the time of the transfer, REZ-CA was suspended by the Franchise Tax Board (FTB). (UMF No. 11.) Sullivan, the purported owner of REZ-CA, then quitclaimed the Hacienda Property back to South Shore. (SAC ¶22.) In May 2021, FATC opened an escrow sale for the Hacienda Property from South Shore to Sullivan. (UMF No. 17.) By that point, Plaintiffs had paid an advance of $350,000 to pay off the note on the Hacienda Property. (SAC ¶24.) 
The parties do not dispute that these events led to FATC opening escrow as to the sale of the Hacienda Property. The parties dispute whether FATC had a duty not to allow the sale to clear escrow and whether FATC breached that duty by failing to confirm whether REZ-CA was capable of conveying real property.
1. Whether FATC owed Plaintiffs a duty of care
“It is indisputably true that ‘[a]n escrow holder is the limited agent and fiduciary of all parties to an escrow’ and that as such it has ‘a fiduciary duty to communicate to his principal knowledge acquired in the course of his agency with respect to material facts which might affect the principal’s decision as to a pending transaction. . . .’” (Siegel v. Fidelity Nat. Title Ins. Co. (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1193-94 (quoting Hannon v. Western Title Ins. Co. (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1122, 1128)).) “‘However, it is equally true that ‘the agency which exists (and the obligations pursuant thereto) is a limited one.’” (Id.) “‘If the several escrow instructions create in the escrow holder an agency, it must be one limiting the obligations of the escrow holder to each party to the escrow in accordance with the instructions given by such party . . . .’” (Id.) “‘[I]t is generally held that no liability attaches to the escrow holder for his failure to do something not required by the terms of the escrow or for a loss incurred while obediently following his escrow instructions.’” (Id.) 
“An escrow holder is an agent and fiduciary of the parties to the escrow.”  (Summit Financial Holdings, Ltd. v. Continental Lawyers Title Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 705, 711. ) (Summit) “The agency created by the escrow is limited—limited to the obligation of the escrow holder to carry out the instructions of each of the parties to the escrow.”  (Id.)  With respect to the scope of an escrow holder’s fiduciary duties, “[a]bsent clear evidence of fraud, an escrow holder’s obligations are limited to compliance with the parties’ instructions.”  (Id.)  “[A]n escrow holder ‘has no general duty to police the affairs of its depositors.’”  (Id.)  
Where a plaintiff is a third person not in privity with a defendant, the so-called Biakanja factors described in Biakanja v. Irving (1958) 49 Cal.2d647, 650 may be used to determine the defendant had a duty to exercise reasonable care to avoid negligently causing economic loss to others with whom they were not in privity. The Biakanja factors have been applied to determine whether an escrow agent owes a duty of care to a third party. (Summit at p.715.) The analysis involves the balancing of various factors set forth below.
1. The extent to which the transaction was intended to affect the plaintiff;
2. The foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff;
3. The degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury;
4. The closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered;
5. The moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct; and 
6. The policy of preventing future harm. 
Here, Plaintiffs were not parties to the escrow. The Escrow Officer responsible for the transaction, Eugene Kim, testifies that FATC was instructed that the seller was South Shore and the Buyer was Sullivan. (Kim Decl., ¶4.) Because Plaintiffs were not involved in the transaction, they were not parties to the escrow. Thus, the Court must proceed with an analysis of the Biakanja factors.
1. The extent to which the transaction was intended to affect Plaintiffs
On reply, FATC focuses primarily on the first factor, the extent to which the transaction was intended to affect Plaintiffs. (Reply., p. 16.) FATC argues that the transaction was not intended to affect Plaintiffs because the purpose of the transaction was to assist with the sale of the Hacienda Property from South Shore to Sullivan. FATC alleges that Plaintiffs should recover from Kapoor instead because their injury was caused by Kapoor misrepresenting that she was authorized to act on South Shore’s behalf.
FATC does not present evidence demonstrating that it was not aware of the dispute over the Hacienda Property at the time that it closed escrow. Kim’s declaration merely states that he relied on representations from Kapoor and Sullivan that they were authorized to enter into this transaction. (Kim Decl., ¶10.) Additionally, Kim was not instructed to investigate whether REZ-CA was capable of conveying an interest in the property or whether Kapoor was authorized to act on South Shore’s behalf. (Id., ¶¶11-12.) At no point does Kim state he had no knowledge that Plaintiffs were attempting to purchase the Hacienda Property and that they had paid a $350,000 advance to Fay Servicing. 
It would not be reasonable to conclude, as a matter of law, that this transaction was not intended to affect Plaintiffs. It is reasonable to infer that Kim, as the Escrow Officer responsible for facilitating Sullivan’s purchase, would have been in communication with Fay Servicing regarding the total payoff amount and any credit to be applied to that amount. If FATC knew of the parties’ competing efforts to purchase the Hacienda Property, the effect of the transaction would have also been to deprive Plaintiffs of the advance they paid and to deprive them of the Hacienda Property. 
If FATC knew of the dispute, this factor weighs in favor of a finding that FATC owed Plaintiffs a duty of care.
2. The foreseeability of the harm to Plaintiffs
As discussed above, FATC provides no evidence that it was unaware of the dispute between Plaintiffs and Kapoor over the Hacienda Property. If FATC knew of the dispute, it would be foreseeable that Plaintiffs would lose the $350,000 advance they paid toward the purchase and that Plaintiffs would lose the Hacienda Property.
3. The degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury
The third factor weighs in favor of a finding that FATC owed Plaintiffs a duty of care. There is no question that Plaintiffs lost the Hacienda Property despite their agreement with Kapoor to have the property conveyed to REZ-TX. FATC does not dispute that Plaintiffs and Kapoor agreed to give Plaintiffs the Hacienda Property after they decided to dissolve South Shore.
4. The closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered
The fourth factor also depends on whether FATC knew of the parties’ dispute over the Hacienda Property. Kim testifies and FATC argues that they did not investigate whether Sullivan and Kapoor were authorized to act on behalf of REZ-CA and South Shore respectively because they were not instructed to and they had no duty to. (Kim Decl., ¶10.) If Kim had investigated further, he would have discovered facts that REZ-CA was a suspended corporation or at least that Plaintiffs were also owners of South Shore who were also attempting to purchase the Hacienda Property. Thus, Kim’s failure to investigate further caused FATC to close escrow and allow Sullivan to purchase the Hacienda Property, injuring Plaintiffs who were competing to purchase the property. 
5. The moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct; and The policy of preventing future harm. 
Finally, the fifth and sixth factors also depend on whether FATC knew of the parties’ dispute over the Hacienda Property. If FATC knew of a dispute involving the purchase of the Hacienda Property and yet proceeded, the loss that ensued might have been easily prevented had FATC investigated whether Sullivan and Kapoor were authorized to enter into this transaction before closing escrow. 
A triable issue of material fact remains over whether FATC knew of the parties’ competing efforts to purchase the Hacienda Properties. If FATC knew of the dispute, the Biakanja factors would weigh in favor of a finding that FATC owed a duty of care to prevent Plaintiffs’ harm, even if they were not parties to the escrow. FATC fails to meet its burden of proving that FATC as a matter of law owed no duty of care in this particular factual scenario. 
Even if FATC did meet its burden on summary judgment, Plaintiffs provide evidence that Kim did not remember whether he knew of the lawsuit pending over the Hacienda Property. (Kim Depo., 31:15-25, pdf p.78.) However, he knew of the lis pendens which had been recorded on the Hacienda Property, meaning he would have at least been on notice that there was a dispute over title. (Id.) 
FATC also argued that it owed no duty to Plaintiffs as a title insurer because the grant deed was not prepared by FATC, but by a different entity, meaning FATC never issued title insurance for this transaction. However, even if FATC did not provide title insurance, FATC may have had a duty as an escrow agent to prevent Plaintiffs’ harm.
FATC’s arguments that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring a claim for negligence likewise fails because there is a triable issue of material fact over whether the Biakanja factors would weigh in favor of a finding that FATC owed Plaintiffs a duty of care. 
Summary judgment is denied on this ground. 
2. Whether FATC breached its duty of care
FATC also argues it did not breach a duty of care to Plaintiffs because it reasonably relied on representations by Sullivan that he was an authorized signer for REZ-CA. Additionally, FATC argues that it was aware of no evidence that Sullivan was committing fraud. (Motion, p. 19.) However, FATC refers to UMF Nos. 42-46, which merely state that FATC was never instructed to investigate Sullivan. As discussed above, there is a triable issue of material fact over whether FATC knew of the parties’ dispute over the Hacienda Property. If FATC had known of the dispute, it would have known of Plaintiffs’ allegations that the property was mistakenly transferred to REZ-CA and that Sullivan had fraudulently conveyed the property back to South Shore. Because a triable issue of material fact remains over whether FATC knew of the parties’ dispute over the Hacienda Property, FATC fails to meet its burden of proving no triable issue of material fact remains over whether it breached a duty of care to Plaintiffs.  
FATC’s motion for summary judgment is denied on this ground.
3. Whether FATC is liable under any conspiracy theory
In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that FATC failed to address whether it was liable under any conspiracy theory. However, the issue of whether FATC conspired with other Defendants to commit negligence is not dispositive of any cause of action. Summary judgment or adjudication may only be granted if the motion completely resolves a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty unless the parties jointly stipulate to adjudication of an issue. (Code Civ. Proc., §§437c(f)(1), 437c(t); See's Candy Shops, Inc. v Superior Court (2012) 210 CA4th 889, 899–900.) Here, the parties have not agreed to adjudicate the issue of whether FATC is liable under a conspiracy theory. The Court declines to rule on this issue.
4. Plaintiffs’ request for a continuance
Plaintiffs request a continuance on the grounds that Plaintiffs have been unable to take the deposition of FATC’s Title Officer who handled the Sullivan escrow.  
A party opposing a motion for summary judgment may file a declaration to continue the motion with its opposition or by ex parte motion before the opposition to the motion is due. (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c, subd. (h); Ambrose v. Michelin North America, Inc. (2005) 134 CA4th 1350, 1353.) The party must request the continuance through affidavits or declarations stating facts to justify opposition may exist. (Code Civ. Proc., section 437c, subd. (h).) “To be entitled to a continuance, the party opposing the motion for summary judgment must show that its proposed discovery would have led to facts essential to justify opposition.” (Scott v. CIBA Vision Corp. (1995) 38 CA4th 307, 325-326.)
Here, Plaintiffs’ counsel argues that this matter should be continued because he has been unable to depose FATC’s Title Officer. (Gerber Decl., ¶15.) Although he noticed the deposition months ago, Counsel has been unable to schedule the deposition due to scheduling issues. (Id., ¶16.) The Title Officer’s testimony is material to Plaintiffs’ opposition because Kim testified he had no knowledge of several topics because they were the Title Officer’s responsibility. (Id.) 
The parties have had ample time to complete discovery in this matter. Additionally, as discussed above, FATC’s motion was denied because it failed to meet its burden on summary judgment. Thus, further deposition would not affect the outcome of this motion. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ request for a continuance is denied.

[TENTATIVE] RULING RE: 
MOTION TO DISMISS FILED BY CROSS-DEFENDANT SAEED MASJEDI

The motion is denied.
Cross-Defendant Masjedi is ordered to file an answer to the cross-complaint within 15 days after the date of this order.
Moving party to provide notice.
FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”) and violations of Civ. Code, section 1942.5(d). Plaintiff alleges that he was Defendant’s tenant. In June 2022, when Plaintiff notified Defendant of a leak in his sink that required repair, Defendant began engaging in retaliatory conduct which caused him emotional distress.
PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On May 19, 2023, Plaintiff Saeed Masjedi filed his Complaint against Defendant Elizabeth Valdez.
On February 2, 2024, Valdez filed a Cross-Complaint against Saeed Masjedi.
On May 23, 2024, Saeed Masjedi filed this motion to dismiss.
DISCUSSION 
Cross-Defendant Saeed Masjedi moves to dismiss the Cross-Complaint filed by Elizabeth Vasquez on the grounds that (1) Valdez’s claims are barred by litigation privilege and (2) Valdez’s claims are subject to dismissal under the anti-SLAPP statute Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16. Thus, although styled as a motion to dismiss, this motion is a demurrer and a special motion to strike. 
A demurrer to a complaint or cross-complaint must be filed within thirty days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., section 430.40(a).) A defendant must file any special motion to strike within 60 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(f).) 
Here, Valdez’s Cross-Complaint was served on Masjedi via substituted service on March 21, 2024. Service was deemed complete on March 31, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc., section 415.20(a).) Masjedi’s deadline to file a demurrer was April 30, 2024. Because this motion was filed in May 2024, the motion is untimely as to the allegations that the action is barred by litigation privilege.
Masjedi’s anti-SLAPP motion to strike, however, is timely because his deadline to file this motion was May 30, 2024. The Court will rule on the special motion to strike.
Legal Standard
Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 provides that “[a] cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b).) 
Such a motion involves a two-step analysis, in which the court must first determine whether a movant “has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity ...” (Taus v. Loftus (2007) 40 Cal.4th 683, 712, quoting Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.) If the court so finds, it must then examine whether the respondent has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim. (Taus, supra, 40 Cal.4th at p. 712.) In determining whether the respondent has carried this burden, the trial court considers “the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(b)(2); see Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 291.)
Objections
Valdez objects to Masjedi’s anti-SLAPP motion to strike. The Court rules as follows:
1. Failure to provide mandatory factual declarations in support of SLAPP motion.

Although the Court is required to consider affidavits stating facts upon which liability or a defense is based, there is no law that a Court may not consider motions which lack supporting evidence. The objection is OVERRULED.

2. Failure to provide mandatory notice to Judicial Council upon filing a SLAPP motion.

Again, although Masjedi and Valdez were required to file notices to the Judicial Council under Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(j)(2) , there is no law that a Court may not consider motions which lack the required notices. However, Masjedi is ordered to file the required notice.

3. Meet and Confer is not required prior to filing a SLAPP motion.

Although Valdez is correct that the parties are not required to meet and confer prior to the filing of a SLAPP motion, the lack of a meet and confer was not pertinent to the Court’s analysis. The Court declines to rule on this objection.

4. A disbarred attorney, Martin Reiner (Bar No. 144024), should not be allowed to assist the moving party at the hearing of the SLAPP motion. (Business & Professions Code section 6125).

Reiner signed the proof of service for this motion. Additionally, Masjedi represented that he had an attorney in an email to another tenant. (Vergara Decl., Exh. 1.) However, there is no evidence that Reiner wrote the motion at issue here or that Reiner was the attorney mentioned in Masjedi’s email.   
Discussion
1. Protected Activity
Masjedi alleges that Valdez’s Cross-Complaint is based on Masjedi’s conduct which is protected by the First Amendment’s right to freedom of speech and freedom to petition. 
Moving parties have the initial burden to demonstrate that a cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike. (Martinez v. Metabolife Inter. Ins. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 186; Fox Searchlight Pictures Inc. v. Paladino (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 294, 304.) Specifically, courts decide whether moving parties have made a prima facie showing that the attacked claims arise from a protected activity, including defendants’ right of petition, or free speech, under the Constitution, in connection with issues of public interest. (Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1, 5; Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278; Paulus v. Bob Lynch Ford, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 659, 671; Equilon Ent. v. Consumer Cause (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67; Gov. Gray Davis Committee v. Amer. Taxpayers Alliance (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 449, 458-59; CCP §425.16(e).) Moving parties can satisfy their burden by showing (1) statements made before legislative, executive or judicial proceedings, or made in connection with matters being considered in such proceedings, or (2) statements made in a public forum, or other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional rights of petition or free speech, in connection with issues of public interest. (CCP §425.16(e); Equilon Ent., LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66.) 
“A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1062.) “‘[T] defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’”  (Id. at 1063 (quoting City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78) (emphasis in original).) “‘[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’”  Id. (quoting Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89).) “Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’” (Id. (quoting Navellier, supra, 29 Cal.4th at 92) (alteration in original).) “‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy that [‘arising from’] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . . . .’”  (Id. (quoting Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 66) (emphasis in original).)  “In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.”  (Id.)
“Allegations of a protected activity that merely provide context, without supporting a claim for recovery, cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 395.) The legislature indicated that particular alleged acts giving rise to a claim for relief may be the object of an anti-SLAPP motion. (Id.) In cases involving allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the plaintiff is required to establish a probability of prevailing on any claim for relief based on allegations of protected activity. (Id.) Neither the form of the complaint nor the primary right at stake is determinative. (Id.) 
Here, the Cross-Complaint brings three causes of action for elder abuse, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligent infliction of emotional distress. It alleges that in March 2014, Valdez and Masjedi entered into a written month-to-month rental agreement. (Cross-Compl., ¶5.) Masjedi made unwelcome advances towards Valdez, eventually becoming aggressive. (Id., ¶6.) After Valdez received complaints from a neighbor and a Homeowners Association that there was water leakage coming from the property, Valdez requested permission from Masjedi to inspect the property and discovered a water filter pipe had been improperly installed on the property. (Id., ¶7.) Masjedi also switched out her refrigerator for a used refrigerator with incorrectly installed water lines, changed the locks on the unit without Valdez’s permission, and tampered with electrical wires. (Id.) Masjedi became aggressive and did not allow Valdez to make repairs. (Id.) Valdez had no choice but to commence eviction proceedings. (Id., ¶8.) Masjedi thereafter began writing threating letters to Valdez, telephoning her at all hours, yelling at her, and berating her. (Id., ¶9.) Masjedi then sent a threatening email to a tenant in the adjoining unit telling her not to interfere with the litigation. (Id., ¶12.) After Valdez obtained judgment against Masjedi, Masjedi continued his aggression against Valdez, pulled out electrical wires from the property, cut the pipes under the kitchen sink, took the bulbs from the unit, failed to return the keys to the unit, and vandalized the bedroom window. (Id., ¶13.)
Each of Plaintiff’s causes of action are based on allegations that Masjedi’s conduct caused Plaintiff emotional and psychological harm. Thus, the issue is whether Masjedi’s conduct damaging Valdez’s unit, making harassing phone calls to Valdez, and sending a threatening email to prevent a witness from participating in litigation constitute protected activity.
The only means by which a defendant may satisfy the first prong of the anti-SLAPP statute is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct falls within one of the four categories described in Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e). (City of Montebello v. Vasquez (2016) 1 Cal.5th 409, 422.) The section provides that an act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue includes:
1. Any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law
2. Any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law
3. Any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest
4. Any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.
Here, Masjedi’s alleged conduct making harassing phone calls to Valdez, and damaging the unit Valdez rented to him do not fall within the scope of any of these four categories. This conduct does not become protected activity merely because it took place after Valdez filed her unlawful detainer action or after Masjedi commenced his own lawsuit. Because these acts are not among the acts described in Code Civ. Proc., section 425.16(e), they are not protected activities.
With respect to the email Masjedi sent his neighbor threatening her not to participate in litigation between Valdez and Masjedi, this communication is not protected activity because the activity is illegal as a matter of law.
The anti-SLAPP statute does not protect activity which is illegal as a matter of law. (Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 317.)
Penal Code, section 136.1 provides that any person who, among other things, knowingly and maliciously attempts to prevent or dissuade any witness or victim from attending or giving testimony at any trial, proceeding, or inquiry authorized by law is guilty of a public offense. 
Here, the Cross-Complaint alleges Masjedi emailed a neighbor stating in the email that “I do not want you to get involve (sic) in anyway (sic) in our litigation, or you will be part of the litigation and that is not what you want. I have a very good attorney. I do not want you (sic) spy or pass on any information to her or testify on her behalf. Enjoy your happy life and do not make it complicated for nothing. Litigation is costly and unpredictable and (sic) dangerous. . .” (Cross-Compl., ¶12.) 
In opposition, the neighbor, Maryjoy Vergara, testifies that she did receive the email described above and attaches a copy of the email. (Vergara Decl., ¶3, Exh. 1.) 
The language in the email was intended to dissuade Vergara from testifying or appearing in this litigation because Masjedi specifically threatened Vergara not to be involved in the litigation in any way, including testifying on Valdez’s behalf. Masjedi does not dispute that he sent this email. Masjedi cannot show that this email was not a threat against a witness which is illegal under Penal Code, section 136.1. Thus, the email to Vergara was not protected activity because it is illegal as a matter of law. 
The Cross-Complaint does not arise from protected activity. Masjedi fails to satisfy the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis. Therefore, Masjedi’s special motion to strike is denied.
Attorney’s Fees

Although Valdez requests an award of attorney’s fees, Valdez fails to provide evidence or argument pertaining to fees. If Valdez wishes to seek fees, Valdez must move separately for fees.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND
This is an action for wrongful termination, violations of the Labor Code, false imprisonment, intentional interference with prospective economic relations, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiffs allege that they were working as Defendants’ caretakers. Plaintiffs witnessed Defendant Hood abusing her wife, Defendant Tarble. After Plaintiff Villeda reported the abuse to adult protective services, a social worker visited Defendants’ home to interview Tarble and left a business card after no one answered the door. PROCEDURAL HISTORY 
On June 6, 2023, Plaintiffs Divina Villeda and Consuela “Lucy” Gamboa filed their Complaint against Vilma Hood, Jan Tarble, Dega Nalayeh, Radostina Videnova, and San Pasqual Fiduciary Trust Company, as trustee of the Jan Tarble Trust (“Trust”).

On May 28, 2024, Tarble’s former counsel filed a notice of stay of proceedings as to Tarble.
On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel filed a declaration requesting that the Court take the demurrer and motion to strike off calendar. 
DISCUSSION 
Plaintiff’s counsel filed a declaration requesting that the demurrer and motion to strike filed by Defendant Jan Tarble be taken off calendar on the grounds that the demurrer is moot now that Tarble is deceased. (Hakim Decl., ¶3.) 
An action does not abate upon the death of a party. (Prob. Code, section 377.21.) On motion, a court may allow an action to proceed against a decedent’s personal representative or successor in interest. (Prob. Code, section 377.41.) Although judgment may not be rendered for or against a deceased person, some judgments are allowed to stand if the error is harmless. (Sacks v. FSR Brokerage, Inc. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 950, 957.) This is particularly true if the decedent’s estate’s interests are not adequately represented. (Id at p.958.) However, a proceeding which continues against a deceased person is still irregular. (Id., ¶957.) 
Here, Defendant Tarble passed away on May 1, 2024. Thus, this action may only proceed against Tarble’s personal representative or successor in interest. Because no such party has been substituted in as a defendant, proceedings cannot continue against Tarble. Tarble’s counsel represented that he cannot take the demurrer off calendar because he no longer has a client. (Hakim Decl., ¶5(1).) Tarble’s counsel is correct because an attorney-client relationship terminates upon the client’s death and the attorney loses authority to act on the decedent’s behalf. (Swartfager v. Wells (1942) 53 Cal.App.2d 522, 528.) Tarble’s counsel represented that San Pasqual will be substituting in as Tarble’s personal representative and there is a petition pending to appoint Pasqual as Tarble’s personal representative. Because Tarble’s counsel is unable to act on Tarble’s behalf, the Court will continue this motion until Pasqual is substituted in as a defendant. 
Although Plaintiff’s counsel argues the demurrer is moot, there is no authority to support this assertion. Rather, the action does not abate by Tarble’s death. Although the action may not proceed without a proper substitution, Plaintiff’s claims against Tarble may survive her death. If Tarble’s motion was taken off calendar, Tarble’s estate’s interests would not be adequately represented because the estate would lose its opportunity to demur to the Complaint. Thus, the motion should not be taken off calendar. Instead, Tarble’s personal representative should have the opportunity to substitute in as a defendant.
