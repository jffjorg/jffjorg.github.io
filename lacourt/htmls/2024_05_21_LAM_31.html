DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling by emailing Dept. 31 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. The email address is smcdept31@lacourt.org. Please do not call the court to submit on the tentative. Please do not submit to the tentative ruling on behalf of the opposing party. Please do not e-mail the Court if you plan to appear and argue.

In deciding whether to submit on the tentative ruling or attend the hearing and present oral argument, please keep the following in mind:

The tentative rulings authored by this court reflect that the court has read and considered all pleadings and evidence timely submitted to the court in connection with the motion, opposition, and reply (if any). Because the pleadings were filed, they are part of the public record.

Oral argument is not an opportunity to simply regurgitate that which a party set forth in its pleadings. Nor, is oral argument an opportunity to "make a record" when there is no court reporter present and the statements and arguments of counsel are already part of the record because they were set forth in the pleadings. Finally, simply because a party or attorney disagrees with the court's analysis and ruling or is not satisfied with it does not necessarily warrant oral argument when no new arguments will be articulated.

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify all other parties email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. If all parties to the motion submit, this tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date and it will be memorialized in a minute order. This tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such document will be considered by the Court.

**Tentative rulings on Motions for Summary Judgment will only be available for review in the courtroom on the day of the hearing.


Case Number: 19STCV23201    Hearing Date: May 21, 2024    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:      May 21, 2024                                                                  TRIAL DATE:  N/A

                                                          

CASE:                         Tyler Evans v. AIDS Healthcare Foundation

 

CASE NO.:                 19STCV23201

 

 

MOTION TO STRIKE AND/OR TAX

DEFENDANT’S MEMORANDUM OF COSTS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Tyler Evans

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation, Inc.

 

 

I.          INTRODUCTION

 

            On July 2, 2019, Plaintiff Tyler Evans (“Evans” or “Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation (“AHF” or “Defendant”).  On October 9, 2019, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).  The FAC asserted causes of action for: 

 

1.      Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5; 

2.      Violation of Health & Safety Code § 1278.5; 

3.      Violation of Business & Professions Code § 510; 

4.      Sexual Orientation Discrimination in Violation of Government Code § 12940, et seq.;

5.      Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; 

6.      Breach of Written Contract;

7.      Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 

8.      Failure to Provide Employment Records in Violation of Labor Code §§ 226 & 1198.5. 

                         

Evans worked as AHF’s Director of Infectious Disease and Medical Director from December 16, 2016 to September 27, 2018.  He alleged that he was wrongfully terminated after complaining to AHF senior management about conduct he believed was unlawful and threatened patient safety.  In retaliation, AHF falsely accused Evans of communicating with other AHF employees in a sexual nature.  Evans also alleged the false accusations were motivated by Evans’s heterosexuality.

 

            Procedural Background

 

On July 6, 2023, AHF moved for summary adjudication on the ground that Evans’s termination was based upon good faith, non-retaliatory and non-discriminatory reasons.  The motion was set for September 21, 2023.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            A party challenging the amounts claimed may file a motion to tax costs, which must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum.¿ If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (a)(4).¿ (Cal. Rules of Court., rule 3.1700(b)(1).)¿ “Unless objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion ... must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(2).)¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

The memorandum of cost is a verified statement by the party, attorney, or agent that the costs are correct and were necessarily incurred in the case.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a)(1).)¿ “If the items appear to be proper charges, the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services therein listed were necessarily incurred by the defendant, and the burden of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable is upon the [objecting party].”¿ (Oak Grove School Dist. v. City Title Ins. Co. (1963) 217 Cal.App.2d 678, 698.)¿ “[I]f the correctness of the memorandum is challenged either in whole or in part by the affidavit or other evidence of the contesting party, the burden is then on the party claiming the costs and disbursements to show that the items charged were for matters necessarily relevant and material to the issues involved in the action.”¿ (Id. at p. 699.)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 lists the costs that are recoverable.¿ Section 1033.5 provides: “(1) Costs are allowable if incurred, whether or not paid. (2) Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation. (3) Allowable costs shall be reasonable in amount. (4) Items not mentioned in this section ... may be allowed ... in the court’s discretion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §¿1033.5, subd. (c).)¿¿¿ 

 

            However, an award of costs in a FEHA action is governed by a different statute.  A prevailing defendant in a FEHA action may be awarded fees and costs only when the court finds the action was frivolous, unreasonable, or groundless when brought, or the plaintiff continued to litigate after it clearly became so.  (Gov. Code, § 12965, subd. (c)(6).)  “By making a cost award discretionary rather than mandatory, Government Code section 12965[(c)(6)] expressly excepts FEHA actions from Code of Civil Procedure section 1032(b)’s mandate for a cost award to the prevailing party.”  (Williams v. Chino Valley Independent Fire Dist. (2015) 61 Cal.4th 97, 105.)  In determining whether an action is frivolous, unreasonable, or groundless, the court must not “engage in post hoc reasoning by concluding that, because a plaintiff did not ultimately prevail, his action must have been unreasonable or without foundation. . . . Even when the law or the facts appear questionable or unfavorable at the outset, a party may have an entirely reasonable ground for bringing suit.”  (Christiansburg Garment Co. v. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (1978) 434 U.S. 412, 421-422.) 

 

III.      DISCUSSION

 

            AHF seeks costs and expenses in the sum of $26,042.85.  (See Memorandum of Costs.) Plaintiff argues the costs should be stricken on three principal grounds: (1) AHF cannot show that Plaintiff’s FEHA action was unreasonable, frivolous, vexatious, or that he brought or continued litigating the matter without an objective basis for believing his claims had merit, (2) costs have not been allocated between the FEHA and non-FEHA claims, and (3) the facts supporting the FEHA and non-FEHA claims overlap and are thus inextricably intertwined.  

 

            In opposition, AHF argues that this is not a FEHA action and further, Plaintiff’s lone FEHA cause of action was frivolous and not inextricably intertwined with Plaintiff’s other claims.

 

            Based on the foregoing, the task before the court is to determine (1) whether this is a “FEHA action”; (2) whether Plaintiff’s FEHA claim was frivolous, and (3) if Plaintiff’s FEHA claim was not frivolous, whether the FEHA and non-FEHA claims are inextricably intertwined?[1]

 

1.      First Issue: Whether This is a “FEHA Action”

 

Plaintiff alleged eight causes of action, one of which is a FEHA claim for sexual orientation discrimination.  AHF argues that a lone FEHA cause of action does transform the case into a “FEHA action.”  And if it is not a FEHA Action, AHF is entitled to a mandatory award of costs as the prevailing party under Code of Civil Procedure section 1032.  

 

Implicit in AHF’s argument is that a FEHA action constitutes something more than one FEHA claim.  Case law does not support AHF’s position.  As AHF would have it, a “FEHA action” must include more than one FEHA claim.  And how many might that be?  No California Court of Appeal decision has adopted AHF’s more than one cause of action test.

 

Here, Plaintiff asserted a FEHA claim.  For lack of a better term, this is a FEHA action.  Further, because Plaintiff dismissed his FAC, which included the FEHA claim, AHF is the prevailing defendant.  Therefore, AHF may obtain discretionary costs as provided under Government Code section 12965, subdivision (c)(6).

 

2.      Second Issue: Whether Plaintiff’s FEHA Claim Was Frivolous

 

As the prevailing defendant in a FEHA action, AHF has the burden to show Plaintiff’s FEHA claim was frivolous.  (See Neeble-Diamond, supra, 99 Cal.App.5th at p.557 [“when the defense prevails in a FEHA action, it has no automatic right to recover costs under section 1032; instead, it must move the court to make a discretionary award of such costs, based in part on a specific finding that the action was frivolous.”].)  No doubt, Plaintiff’s case was primarily a whistleblower action.  But that, in and of itself, does not make the FEHA claim frivolous.  The court looks to the law and facts to make that determination.

 

            FEHA discrimination claims are subject to the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 (McDonnell Douglas) burden-shifting analysis.  (Sandell v. Taylor-Listug, Inc. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 297.)   First, the plaintiff-employee has the burden of establishing a prima facie case of discrimination.  (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354.) The plaintiff satisfies this burden by proving that “(1) he was a member of a protected class, (2) he was qualified for the position he sought or was performing competently in the position he held, (3) he suffered an adverse employment action, such as termination, demotion, or denial of an available job, and (4) some other circumstance suggests discriminatory motive.”  (Id. at p. 355.)  There must be a causal link between the discriminatory animus and adverse action.  (See Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  After proving a prima facie case of discrimination, the McDonnell Douglas test shifts the burden to the defendant to provide a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for its action.  (Guz, 24 Cal.4th at p. 379.) After the reason is articulated, the burden is shifted to the plaintiff to show that the defendant’s reason is pretext.  (Id. at p. 380.)

 

Plaintiff refers to the summary judgment motion to advance his argument that his FEHA claim was not frivolous.  If Plaintiff’s FEHA claim was sufficient to establish a prima facie case, his FEHA claim is likely not frivolous.  Probably so.  Turning to the summary judgment motion, Plaintiff points out that AHF attacked two elements of the prima facie test: the second and fourth. 

 

As to the second element (qualification), Plaintiff spends much time and effort detailing the evidence refuting Defendant’s claim that Plaintiff was not qualified.  Plaintiff succeeds in demonstrating a prima facie case regarding his qualification for his position (or conversely, the  lack of merit to Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff was not qualified). 

 

            Plaintiff does not fare so well with the fourth element – a discriminatory motive.  At most, Plaintiff points to a couple scattered sexual orientation comments made by others but Plaintiff fails to tie these comments to his termination.  The comments are peripheral, unconnected to the decision to terminate him.  Plaintiff points to certain statements –Plaintiff testified as to two specific derogatory comments about his sexual orientation: (1) that he was told by one of the medical directors and dental directors at a medical retreat, “We were hoping that you were gay, but you know it just turned out that you were straight, and we were so disappointed”; and (2) a staff member said that Plaintiff liked watching straight porn. (Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Material Facts (PMF) 124-126.) Plaintiff also testified that it would be “impossible to describe all of them [the derogatory comments about his sexual orientation]” and that there was a “clear and consistent pattern of not fitting in.” (PMF 127.) 

 

            Critically, however, Plaintiff does not make a connection between his termination and the allegedly derogatory comments about his sexual orientation.  There must be a causal link between the discriminatory animus and adverse action.  (See Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  Plaintiff does not submit any evidence to show the speakers of any derogatory comments played a role in an any adverse employment action taken against him.

 

            Plaintiff next argues there is direct evidence that Plaintiff’s sexual orientation factored into AHF firing him.  Plaintiff points to two comments.  Dr. Wohlfeiler, one of two individuals who decided to fire Plaintiff, admitted that if Plaintiff had been a homosexual male, the allegations of sexual harassment would have been dismissed sooner. (PMF 178, 179.)  This comment does not present evidence that Plaintiff’s sexual orientation was an animating factor or even considered as part of his termination, but rather was a consideration in the investigation whether Plaintiff sexually harassed a female employee.  The inference being that if he were a homosexual male he would be less likely to harass a female and the investigation would have terminated sooner.  The comment does not demonstrate a discriminatory motive related to his termination. Moreover, Plaintiff cannot lift a comment from the sexual harassment investigation a female employee and shoehorn it into the decision to terminate Plaintiff, at least not without some other connective evidence.  Plaintiff’s “direct evidence” argument is conclusory, misguided, and without any factual basis.  The “direct evidence” shows only that Plaintiff’s sexual orientation was considered in investigating sexual harassment complaints made against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff was cleared of wrongdoing as to one of the complaints.  (PMF 95.)  Plaintiff does not present direct evidence that his sexual orientation was considered in his termination.

 

In the end, Plaintiff admitted that none of the individuals responsible for disciplining or terminating him made any negative comments about his sexual orientation, nor did Plaintiff maintain that anyone else with “anti-straight” animus influenced the decision.  (See Grix Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A, p. 259:10-16.)[2]  Without any evidence of discriminatory animus connection to the termination decision, Plaintiff’s FEHA never gets off the ground.  The claim is “frivolous, unreasonable, or groundless.”  (See Bond v. PulsarVideo Productions (1996) 50 Cal.Capp.4th 918, 924 [trial court found that discriminatory action was not prosecuted in good faith where there was no evidence of discrimination]; see also Villanueva v. City of Colton (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1200 [holding that the trial court did not abuse its discretion in awarding attorney fees in FEHA action where defendant prevailed at summary judgment by showing there was no admissible evidence of discrimination].)  

 

Having so concluded, the court does not consider whether Plaintiff’s FEHA and non-FEHA claims are inextricably intertwined. 

 

Tax Costs

 

AHF is entitled to costs.  The remaining issue is whether AHF’s claimed costs should be taxed.  The court is empowered to tax costs that it finds were not reasonably necessary to the course of litigation.  (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-774.)  “The court’s first determination … is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item, and whether it appears proper on its face.”  (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131.)

 

Plaintiff seeks an order taxing the following line items from AHF’s Memorandum of Costs:

 

·         Line Item No. 1: Filing and motion fees for AHF’s discovery motions ($123.30)

·         Line Item No. 5: Service of Process of deposition subpoenas ($4,043.80)

·         Line Item No. 11: Court Reporter Fee ($475)

·         Line Item No. 14: Electronic Filing Fees ($96.84)

·         Line Item No. 16: Other Costs-Chamber copies ($312.31)

 

Line Item No. 1: Filing and motion fees

 

These are expressly allowed as costs under CCP section 1032.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).)  Further, the court finds the costs incurred were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation. 

 

Line Item No. 5: Service of Process of deposition subpoenas

 

When a memorandum of costs does not state how the subpoenas are served, the party seeking costs has the burden to establish the necessity and reasonableness of the service costs.  (Nelson, 72 Cal.App.4th at p. 132.)

 

Plaintiff argues the costs at Line Item No. 5 should be taxed because the Memorandum of Costs does not indicate the manner of service.  In response, AHF provides evidence to show the subpoenas were served by registered process server.  (See Grix Decl., Exs. B-F.)  Service of process by registered process server is an allowable cost.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(4)(B).)    Further, AHF demonstrates they were reasonably necessary to the litigation.  (See Opp., pp. 9-10.)

 

Line Item No. 11: Court Reporter Fees

 

The court exercises its discretion to award the challenged costs of $475 and $3,409.40. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿1033.5, subd. (c)(4).)¿¿¿ 

 

Line Item No. 14: Electronic Filing Fees

 

Plaintiff challenges Item Nos. 14.1 and 14.s, 14.u, 14.w, 14.x, and 14.y.  AHF withdraws the costs of $96.84 at Item No. 14.1.  (See Opp., p. 14, fn. 14.)  As to the other items, AHF acknowledges that those items are not electronic filings but should have been listed under filing and motion fees.  The court agrees.  The court will tax the withdrawn costs of $96.84 only.

 

Line Item No. 16: Other Costs

 

Plaintiff objects to various items in Line Item No. 16.  The court does not find any to be objectionable or unreasonable.  To the extent a cost is not expressly allowed by statute, the court exercises its discretion to award those costs.  (Code Civ. Proc., §¿1033.5, subd. (c)(4).)¿¿¿

            Plaintiff’s motion to tax costs is Granted in part.  The court taxes $96.84 from AHF’s Memorandum of Costs.

CASE:                         Elena Jahnke v. Blue Cross of California, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV13973

 

 

DEMURRER WITHOUT MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Elena Jahnke

 

 

I.          FACTUAL AND PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

 

            This action arises from an insurer’s delayed approval of a referral for medical treatment. On June 16, 2023, Plaintiff, Elena Jahnke, filed a Complaint against Defendants, Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross (“Anthem”) and Preferred IPA of California (“Preferred IPA”), asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (2) Breach of Contract, (3) Violations of Business & Professions Code section 17200, and (4) Negligence.  The First, Second, and Third Causes of Action are alleged against Anthem.  A copy of the policy is attached to the Complaint.

 

            On November 6, 2023, Anthem filed a demurrer to the First, Second, and Third Causes of Action of the Complaint, and concurrently filed a motion to strike punitive damages and attorney fees.  On January 24, 2024, the court overruled the demurrer to the First and Second Causes of Action and sustained the demurrer to the Third Cause of Action.  The court further granted the motion to strike as to attorney fees and denied as to punitive damages.  Leave to amend was granted.

 

            The FAC alleges that Plaintiff had health insurance with Anthem.  Per an agreement between Anthem and its medical group, Preferred IPA, any referral requests were to be reviewed for approval or denial by Preferred IPA.  On June 17, 2022, Plaintiff’s neurosurgeon determined Plaintiff needed prompt treatment to address arteriovenous malformations on her brain and submitted an urgent referral for an endovascular surgeon to Preferred IPA.  However, Preferred IPA did not respond to the referral within 96 hours as required under the policy.  Plaintiff’s request remained pending for six months.  In that time, Plaintiff’s physical symptoms worsened.  Defendants eventually approved the referral only after Plaintiff suffered a grand mal seizure. 

On February 22, 2024, Plaintiff timely filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC) asserting the same causes of action as the Complaint.

 

            On March 22, 2024, Anthem filed this Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action in the FAC. [1]

 

            Plaintiff filed an opposition.  Anthem replied.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

                A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face.  (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.)  “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.  [Citation.]”  (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)  Allegations are to be liberally construed.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  In construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations.  (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769.)  

 

            A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)  “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.”  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)   

            Where the complaint contains substantial factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty will be overruled or plaintiff will be given leave to amend.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)  Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully.  (Ibid.)  

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff’s Third Cause of Action is a UCL claim.  The UCL is codified at Business and Professions Code, section 17200 et seq.  Section 17204 of the UCL provides that a private person “who has suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property as a result of the unfair competition” may bring a 17200 action.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17204.)  “To bring a UCL claim, a plaintiff must show either an (1) unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice, or (2) unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising. [Citation.]  Because the UCL is written in the disjunctive, it establishes three varieties of unfair competition—acts or practices which are unlawful, or unfair, or fraudulent.”  (Adhav v. Midway Rent A Car, Inc. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 954, 970, citations and quotations omitted.)  The UCL is “not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.”  (Madrid v. Perot Sys. Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 440, 452 (citations omitted).)

 

Anthem argues that Plaintiff’s UCL claim is still deficient for two reasons: (1) Plaintiff does not allege any unlawful, fraudulent, or unfair acts, and (2) there are no remedies available.

 

A.     Unlawful, Fraudulent, or Unfair Acts

 

“[A]ny ‘unlawful,’ ‘unfair’ or ‘fraudulent’ business act or practice is deemed to be unfair competition.”  (Smith v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 700, 717.)  As relevant here, “unlawful” conduct is “anything that can properly be called a business practice and is forbidden by law.”  (Cel-Tech Commc’ns, Inc. v. Los Angeles Cellular Tel. Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 163, 180.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s UCL claim is based upon alleged violations of Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(3) and California Code of Regulations (CCR) Title 28 section 1300.67.2.2.  (See FAC, ¶¶ 26(k) and(l), 40.)  Anthem attacks Insurance Code section 790.03(h)(3) as inapplicable to Anthem because, as a health care service plan provider, the Department of Managed Health Care, not the Department of Insurance govern Anthem.  The uncontested point has merit. “The Legislature has elected to subject insurers and health care service plans to distinct regulatory regimes.  Insurers are regulated by the Insurance Code. … Health care service plans fall under the jurisdiction of the Department of Managed Care.”  (Smith v. PacifiCare Behavioral Health of California, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 139, 157–58; see also Bain v. United Healthcare Inc. (N.D. Cal. Aug. 30, 2016) 2016 WL 4529495, at *8 [“The distinction between health and disability insurance is underscored by the fact that California regulates insurance plans and health care service plans differently.”]; Blue Cross of California v. Superior Court (2010) 180 Cal.App,4th 1237, 1242 [noting that Anthem Blue Cross is “a managed health care service plan subject to the Knox-Keene Act and regulated by the DMHC.”].)  “A health care service plan, as defined in Chapter 2.2 (commencing with Section 1340) of Division 2 of the Health and Safety Code, shall not be subject to this section.”  (Cal. Ins. Code § 740(g).)  The Insurance Code does not apply to Anthem.

 

Anthem next targets CCR Title 28 section 1300.67.2.2.  Anthem points out Section 1300.67.2.2 does not authorize a private right of action.  “This section does not: . . . (c) Create a new cause of action or a new defense to liability for any person.”  (Cal. Code Regs. tit. 28 § 1300.67.2.2(a)(4)(c).)  As such, Anthem argues that Plaintiff cannot sustain a UCL claim predicated on violation of Section 1300.67.2.2.   Anthem’s argument fails.  Plaintiff has not alleged a separate cause of action pursuant to Section 1300.67.2.2.  Plaintiff has brought a UCL claim based on a violation of Section 1300.67.2.2.  The “UCL authorizes lawsuits to remedy unlawful conduct even if the underlying statute that renders the conduct unlawful does not itself create an independent right of action.”  (Blue Cross of California, Inc. v. Superior Ct. (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 1237, 1250.)  Further, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to state a violation of Section 1300.67.2.2 requires a health care service plan “to provide or arrange for the provision of access to health care services in a timely manner.”   In sustaining, in part, Anthem’s demurrer to the Complaint, the court found that Plaintiff alleged sufficient facts to show Anthem “did nothing to ensure Plaintiff received a timely decision on the referral nor did Anthem implement a policy or procedure to rectify Preferred IPA’s failure to timely act on the referral. As a result, Plaintiff’s necessary medical treatment was unnecessarily delayed.”  (Minute Order, 1/24/24.)  Plaintiff sufficiently alleges a violation of Section 1300.67.2.2 (unlawful act) to invoke the UCL.

 

B.     Remedies Under the UCL

 

Anthem next challenges Plaintiff’s entitlement to remedies under the UCL.  Here, Plaintiff seeks restitution and injunctive relief.  (See FAC, ¶ 43.)  The court focuses on the injunctive relief claim as it is dispositive of this issue. 

 

Injunctive relief is the primary focus of relief under the UCL.  (In re Tobacco II Cases (2009) 46 Cal.4th 298, 319.) In California, injunctive relief is not available where there is a plain, complete, speedy and adequate remedy at law.  (See Prudential Home Mortgage Co. Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1236, 1249-1250 (holding that equitable relief was unavailable to plaintiff because statutory remedies would be adequate).  To obtain injunctive relief, a plaintiff must show a possibility of irreparable harm.  (Bush v. Cal. Conservation Corps (1982) 136 Cal.App.3d 194, 204.)  Irreparable harm occurs only where pecuniary damages will not afford adequate relief, or it will be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount that would afford adequate relief.  (See Civ. Code § 3422.)  Moreover, injunctive relief under the UCL is appropriate “only when there is a threat of continuing misconduct.”  (Madrid v. Perot Systems Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 440, 462-65 (2005); see also Civ. Proc. Code § 526; Gafcon, Inc. v. Ponsor & Associates (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1403 fn. 6 [“injunctive relief is available to prevent threatened injury and is not a remedy designed to right completed wrongs . . . Unless there is a showing that the challenged action is being continued or repeated, an injunction should be denied.”].)

 

Contrary to Anthem’s position, Plaintiff alleges sufficient facts to support a request for injunctive relief.  Plaintiff alleges that she continues to suffer negative effects from her grand mal seizure and that Anthem’s practices are ongoing in that she has continued to encounter delays in her receipt of follow-up care.  (FAC, ¶¶ 5, 20, 21.)  These allegations show a threat of irreparable harm (speedy approval of health care services) that cannot be remedied by pecuniary damages.  Further, the threat is ongoing.  Plaintiff supports her request for injunctive relief under the UCL.

 

 IV.       CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the Demurrer is OVERRULED.

SUBPOENA FOR PRODUCTION OF BUSINESS RECORDS TO

ROCHE DIAGNOSTICS CORPORATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Linda Mansolillo

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant GRAIL, LLC

 

 

I.          BACKGROUND

 

            This is an employment discrimination and retaliation action. On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff, Linda Mansolillo, filed a Complaint against Defendant, GRAIL LLC (sued as “GRAIL, Inc.”), for (1) Harassment in Violation of FEHA, (2) Discrimination in Violation of FEHA, (3) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA, (4) Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment in Violation of the Fair Employment & Housing Act, (5) Violation of California Equal Pay Act, (6) Discrimination and Retaliation in Violation of California Equal Pay Act, (7) Whistleblower Retaliation, and (8) Labor Code Retaliation.  The action arises from Plaintiff’s employment with Defendant from 2020 to 2022.

 

            On February 9, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a Notice to Consumer for  deposition Subpoena for the Production of Business Records to Roche Diagnostics Corporation (the “Roche Subpoena”).  Plaintiff worked with Roche from 2004-2012.

           

On February 28, 2024, the parties participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) regarding issues with the Roche Subpoena, among others.  The issues were not resolved.  The court ordered Counsel for Plaintiff to file a Notice of Outcome regarding discovery. 

 

On March 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Notice of Outcome which stated in relevant part, “Plaintiff will produce all documents and communications from other employers she worked for during her career as a healthcare professional, including employment agreements with those employers, from March 9, 2010 (10 years before the start of her employment with GRAIL) through the present, located after a diligent search and reasonable inquiry.”   

 

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to quash Defendant’s deposition subpoena for production of business records to Roche.  Plaintiff does not seek sanctions.

 

            Defendant filed an opposition.  Plaintiff replied.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands such as unreasonable violations of the right of privacy.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1.)¿ 

III.       DISCUSSION

In the Notice of Outcome filed on March 5, 2024, Plaintiff noted the look back period would be ten years calculated from the start of Plaintiff’s employment with GRAIL.  On March 4, 2024, prior to the filing of the Notice of Outcome, Plaintiff’s Counsel emailed Defense Counsel to express disagreement with Defendant’s edits to the Notice of Outcome.  That disagreement involved the commencement of the 10-year look-back period.  Defendant took the position the time runs from the commencement of Plaintiff’s hire by GRAIL.  Plaintiff took the position the time commences from the current date.  Plaintiff offered a compromise of a look-back period commencing from the date of Plaintiff’s separation from GRAIL.  To avoid burdening the court, however, Plaintiff’s Counsel accepted the revisions without prejudice to her position GRAIL should withdraw the Roche Subpoena.  (See Atteberry Decl., Ex. 6.)

Based on the foregoing, the central issue to be decided is:  when should the look-back period commence?  There are various options identified by the parties.  The court rules out both the present day because that is a moving target, and date of the incident because, in the context of this case, that is too vague.  That leaves either the date of hire with GRAIL (February 2020) or the date of separation (2022).  Plaintiff properly points out that the Form Interrogatories use different time periods: a look back for ten years prior to the time of the response to the interrogatory with respect to any law suits filed by the employee against any employer (Emp. FROG 209; 210); a look back for five years prior to the date of the incident to identify prior employers (General FROG 2.6). 

The court selects the date of hire as the look back period.  The general purpose of the discovery request is to learn about relevant issues concerning Plaintiff’s employment prior to being hired by GRAIL.  And it makes little sense to include the time Plaintiff worked at GRAIL because, obviously, this time period will not yield any prior employment information.  To include the time Plaintiff worked at GRAIL would reduce the relevant look-back period to eight years, not ten.    

The Roche Subpoena seeks records from 2004 to 2012.  This time period exceeds the ten- year look back period. 

IV.        CONCLUSION

           

Based on the foregoing, the motion to quash the Roche Subpoena is GRANTED in part.  The court modifies the Roche Subpoena to seek records from February 2012 to February 2022.
