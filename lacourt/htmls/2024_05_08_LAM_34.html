DEPARTMENT 34 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court often posts its tentative several days in advance of the hearing. Please re-check the tentative rulings the day before the hearing to be sure that the Court has not revised the ruling since the time it was posted.

Please call the clerk at (213) 633-0154 by 4:00 pm. the court day before the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative.

Case Number: 22STCV39551    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion to Enforce Settlement

 

Moving Party: Plaintiffs Willie L. Fair and Julie Fair

Resp. Party:    Defendants BMW of North America, LLC and BMW of Monrovia

 

 

The Motion to Enforce Settlement is GRANTED in part. Judgment shall be entered to conform with the Parties’ written settlement, with dates amended to reflect that Defendants shall accept surrender of the subject vehicle within seven (7) days from the issuance of this Order.

 

 

PRELIMINARY COMMENT:

 

Although Defendant’s Opposition was filed late, the Court will still consider it.

 

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On December 21, 2022, Plaintiffs Willie L. Fair and Julie Fair filed their Complaint against Defendants BMW of North America, LLC and Sai Monrovia B, Inc. (d.b.a. BMW of Monrovia) on causes of action arising from the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

On March 18, 2024, by request of Plaintiffs, the Court dismissed with prejudice the Complaint. The Court retained jurisdiction to make orders to enforce any and all terms of settlement, including judgment, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.

 

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Motion to Enforce Settlement. In support of their Motion to Enforce Settlement, Plaintiffs concurrently filed: (1) Declaration of Amy R. Rotman; (2) Proposed Order; and (3) Proof of Service.

 

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Notice of Non-Opposition. Plaintiffs concurrently filed their Proof of Service.

 

On May 6, 2024, Defendants filed their Opposition to the Motion to Enforce Settlement.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Legal Standard

 

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)

 

“Section 664.6 was enacted to provide a summary procedure for specifically enforcing a settlement contract without the need for a new lawsuit.” (Weddington Prod., Inc. v. Flick (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 793, 809.) In deciding motions made under Section 664.6, judges “must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement.” (Kohn v. Jaymar-Ruby (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1530, 1533.)

 

II.       Discussion

 

A.      The Signed Section 998 Offer

 

Plaintiffs submit to the Court a signed document titled “Plaintiffs’ Amended Statutory Offer to Compromise Pursuant to C.C.P. Section 998” (“Signed Section 998 Offer”). (Decl. Rotman, Exh. 1.)

 

The document is signed by Plaintiffs’ Counsel and Defense Counsel. The Parties do not dispute the authenticity of the Signed Section 998 Offer.

 

 The following are the pertinent sections of the Signed Section 998 Offer.

 

“1. Plaintiffs shall recover from Defendants and Judgment is to be entered in the amount of $85,000.00 (Eighty-Five Thousand Dollars and zero cents) (Settlement Funds). This amount includes the loan payoff owed, if any, by Plaintiffs for the 2018 BMW 740i, VIN WBA7E2C52JB214901, that is the subject of this action (‘Subject Vehicle’).

 

“2. Plaintiffs will surrender the Subject Vehicle to Defendant BMW NA at BMW of Monrovia or an alternative mutually agreed-upon location on a mutually agreeable date. The surrender shall occur no later than 60 days after Defendants’ counsel has executed this Statutory Offer unless circumstances caused by Plaintiffs result in delay. BMW NA shall send payment of Settlement Funds via overnight mail to Plaintiffs’ counsel Wirtz Law APC to arrive within three (3) business days of the surrender of the subject vehicle, less the loan payoff amount, if any, which will be simultaneously sent directly to the lienholder.

 

“3. In addition, BMW NA shall pay attorney fees, costs, and expenses actually and reasonably incurred in the commencement and prosecution of this action, including post-offer acceptance attorney fees, costs, and expenses incurred in performing on the settlement and/or completing the case, pursuant to Civil Code section 1794(d), which may be resolved by agreement of the parties or, if the parties cannot agree, upon properly noticed motion to the Court. If a motion is needed, Plaintiffs shall be deemed the prevailing party in this action under section 1794(d). Defendants shall not object to the timing of filing the motion for attorney fees at the same time as the filing of a memorandum of cost in one consolidated motion rather than by filing a separate cost memorandum. Defendants waive the deadline, if any, for filing of Plaintiffs’ memorandum(a) of costs and motion for attorney’s fees.

 

“4. If Defendants do not timely perform the obligations set forth above, Plaintiffs shall be entitled to interest at the rate of 10% per annum, from the date of acceptance of this offer until the date the Settlement Funds are received by Plaintiffs’ attorneys. Nothing in this offer prohibits Plaintiffs from seeking recovery of enforcement fees, costs, expenses, or related amounts from Defendants, or pursuing alternative enforcement procedures or remedies, in the event that Defendants do not timely perform.

 

“5. Upon receipt of all payments, including the Settlement Funds, attorney’s fees, costs, and expenses, and interest (if any), Plaintiffs shall dismiss this entire action with prejudice.

 

“6. Except as otherwise provided herein, each party shall bear its own attorney fees and costs. Defendants expressly waive any costs or fees in this case.

 

“7. The parties stipulate that the Court may retain jurisdiction over the action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6 until all terms contained herein are performed, and all amounts owed to Plaintiffs and counsel are received. Upon acceptance, this Offer shall constitute a writing signed by both parties via their counsel in accordance with Section 664.6, subd. (b).”

 

B.      The Parties’ Arguments

 

Plaintiffs move the Court to:

 

(1)       enter judgment on the Parties’ settlement agreement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 and/or Civil Code section 1440;

 

(2)       order Defendant BMW of North America to accept surrender of the subject vehicle within three days after the hearing on the Motion to Enforce Settlement;

 

(3)       order Defendant BMW of North America to pay settlement funds within three days of surrender of the subject vehicle; and

 

(4)       order in the alternative that if Defendant BMW of North America does not accept surrender of the vehicle within three days after the hearing on the Motion to Enforce Settlement, then Defendant BMW of North America must pay the full $85,000.00 in settlement funds to Plaintiffs’ Counsel within seven days of the hearing, without surrender of the subject vehicle.

 

(Motion to Enforce Settlement, p. 3:14–22.)

 

Defendants request that the Motion to Enforce Settlement be denied, arguing: (1) that delay in proceeding with the surrender of the subject vehicle within sixty days of execution of the Signed Section 998 Offer was outside of Defendants’ control; and (2) failure to timely oppose this motion was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Opposition, pp. 3:22–23, 4:10–11.) Defendants also argue that the motion is moot because Defendant BMW of North America “has processed the settlement and its third-party surrendering agent is set to schedule the surrender of the Subject Vehicle in order to complete this settlement.” (Id. at p. 2:16–19.)

 

C.      Discussion

 

The Parties do not dispute the fact that there is a valid and binding settlement.

 

Further, it appears that the main goals of the settlement — the surrender of a vehicle and the subsequent payment of both the settlement and the attorney’s fees — have not yet occurred. Thus, contrary to Defendants’ argument, the Motion to Enforce Settlement is not moot.

 

        The Parties have not presented the Court with a reason why judgment should not be entered to conform with the written settlement.

 

The Court shall enter judgment that will confirm with the written settlement.

 

The judgment shall not include the “in the alternative” remedy requested by Plaintiffs. The Signed Section 998 Offer allows for the accrual of interest in case of Defendants’ breach of their obligation to timely perform, not a requirement that Defendants must pay the settlement amount prior to surrender of the vehicle. The Court will not enter judgment that does not conform with the written settlement.

 

At this time, the Court has not been presented with sufficient evidence to determine that Defendants were untimely in meeting their obligations. Rather, it appears that Defendants required certain paperwork in order to process the surrender, and Defendants received that paperwork six days before the deadline to perform. Given that approximately sixty more days has passed, there need be no further delay.

 

The Court will grant Defendants seven (7) days from the issuance of this Order to accept surrender of the vehicle before they are considered untimely in their obligations.

 

 

III.     Conclusion

 

The Motion to Enforce Settlement is GRANTED in part. Judgment shall be entered to conform with the Parties’ written settlement, with dates amended to reflect that Defendants shall accept surrender of the subject vehicle within seven (7) days from the issuance of this Order.

 

 


Case Number: 23STCV14999    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Default Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b)

 

Moving Party: Plaintiff Meera Paul

Resp. Party:    Defendant Gurmit Paul

 

 

The Motion for Relief from Dismissal is GRANTED. The dismissal entered against Plaintiff’s Complaint is VACATED. Plaintiff is GRANTED 10 days leave to file her amended pleading.

 

Defense Counsel may file a motion for reasonable fees and costs.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff Meera Paul filed her Complaint for Declaratory Relief.

 

On September 14, 2023, Defendant Gurmit Paul filed his Demurrer to the Complaint.

 

Plaintiff did not oppose the Demurrer.

 

On October 5, 2023, Defendant Gurmit Paul filed his Reply in support of his Demurrer, noting that Plaintiff did not oppose the Demurrer.

 

On October 12, 2023, the Court held a hearing on the Demurrer. There was no appearance by or for Plaintiff at the hearing, nor any communication with the Court as to why there was no appearance by or for Plaintiff at the hearing. The Court sustained the Demurrer without leave to amend the Complaint. The Court then ordered the Complaint dismissed with prejudice.

 

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Default Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) (“Motion for Relief from Dismissal”). Plaintiff concurrently filed her Proposed Order.

 

On April 25, 2024, Defendant Gurmit Paul filed his Opposition to the Motion for Relief from Dismissal.

 

On May 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Reply in support of her Motion for Relief from Dismissal.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Legal Standard

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . . No affidavit or declaration of merits shall be required of the moving party. Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. However, this section shall not lengthen the time within which an action shall be brought to trial pursuant to Section 583.310.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

II.       Discussion

 

A.      The Parties’ Arguments

 

Plaintiff moves the Court for relief from dismissal on the basis of attorney mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). (Motion for Relief from Dismissal, pp. 1:25–2:1.)

 

Plaintiff argues that this is appropriate because: (1) Plaintiff’s Counsel includes an affidavit of fault in the moving papers; (2) a declaration under penalty of perjury satisfies the affidavit requirement; and (3) the motion is timely. (Motion for Relief from Dismissal, pp. 3:25–26, 5:1–2, 5:5.)

 

Defendant Gurmit Paul disagrees, arguing: (1) that the motion does not show that any fault of counsel caused the dismissal in fact; (2) that the Court should look skeptically at the declaration of Plaintiff’s Counsel; and (3) that Plaintiff’s Counsel must pay legal fees and costs if his declaration prevails. (Opposition, pp. 4:21–22, 6:20, 7:19.)

 

In her Reply, Plaintiff argues: (1) that relief from default is mandatory; and (2) that no proposed amended complaint is necessary. (Reply, p. 3:4, 3:16.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

On October 12, 2023, the Court: (1) held a hearing on the Demurrer to the Complaint; (2) sustained the Demurrer, without leave to amend the Complaint; and (3) dismissed with prejudice Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

Plaintiff’s Counsel did not oppose the Demurrer. Neither Plaintiff nor Plaintiff’s Counsel appeared at the hearing on the Demurrer. Plaintiff’s Counsel did not contact the Court to explain why no one appeared for Plaintiff at the hearing on the Demurrer. This Motion for Relief from Dismissal was filed just one day before the deadline.  The declarations submitted by Plaintiff’s Counsel (with the Motion for Relief from Dismissal and the Reply) do not provide any explanation as to why Plaintiff’s Counsel did not oppose the Demurrer, appear at the hearing, or promptly notify the Court of Counsel’s inability to appear at the hearing.

 

The Motion and Rudd’s declarations also raise numerous concerns for the Court.  First, paragraphs 2 and 3 of Rudd’s Declaration are irrelevant to the issue before the Court; they are simply his own paean of praise to what he views as his stellar law career.  Second, paragraphs 5-7 of Rudd’s Declaration are also irrelevant; they simply lay out his disagreement with the family law court that decided that he was to pay child support and tell the Court how much he loves his sons. Third, whatever problems Rudd had with payment of his child support obligations, he was still a practicing attorney at the time he failed to oppose the demurrer or appear at the hearing on the demurrer.  The demurrer was heard on October 12, 2023; Rudd was suspended twice for failing to pay child support – once from November 17, 2022 through January 10, 2023 and again from February 3, 2024 through May 2, 2024.  However, neither suspension overlapped with this demurrer.  Fourth, the attorney who filed the Motion is listed as a member of the Rudd Law Firm, so it is not clear why Rudd “could not engage Mr. Keleti” earlier.  (Cf. Rudd Declaration, ¶ 8 with Caption to Motion, p. 1.) Fifth, Rudd either did not write or did not proof-read his Reply Declaration; there are numerous instances where the declaration switches between the first person (as if Rudd prepared the Declaration himself) and the third person (as if an attorney prepared the Declaration for Rudd).  Sixth, although not objected to by Defendant, most of paragraph 3 of Rudd’s Reply Declaration is inadmissible hearsay.

 

It is obvious to the Court that Plaintiff’s Counsel has engaged in inexcusable neglect.  Discretionary relief is inappropriate here.

 

Yet the Court agrees with Plaintiff’s Counsel that mandatory relief must issue and that it does not matter for the purposes of mandatory relief why Plaintiff’s Counsel acted neglectfully.

 

“More specifically, section 473, subdivision (b)'s mandatory relief provision has three purposes: (1) to relieve the innocent client of the consequences of the attorney's fault; (2) to place the burden on counsel; and (3) to discourage additional litigation in the form of malpractice actions by the defaulted client against the errant attorney.

 

“These purposes are advanced as long as mandatory relief is confined to situations in which the attorney, rather than the client, is the cause of the default, default judgment, or dismissal. In other words, the purpose of the mandatory relief provision under section 473, subdivision (b) is achieved by focusing on who is to blame, not why. Indeed, in many cases, the reasons for the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect will be irrelevant; that is because, as noted above, the mandatory relief provision entitles a party to relief even when his or her attorney's error is inexcusable. We are reluctant to construe section 473, subdivision (b), to require in every case the production of information that will in many cases be of no use in deciding whether to grant relief.”

 

(Martin Potts & Assocs., Inc. v. Corsair, LLC (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 432, 439–440, cleaned up.)

 

In short, the Court must vacate the dismissal.

 

As to the Demurrer, the Court would have granted Plaintiff leave to amend the Complaint had Plaintiff’s Counsel appeared at the hearing on the Demurrer. The Court shall grant Plaintiff 10 days leave to file her amended pleading.

 

Finally, as a consequence of granting mandatory relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), the Court shall direct Plaintiff’s Counsel to pay Defense Counsel’s reasonable fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) Defense Counsel may file a motion for reasonable fees and costs.

 

III.     Conclusion

 

The Motion for Relief from Dismissal is GRANTED. The dismissal entered against Plaintiff’s Complaint is VACATED. Plaintiff is GRANTED 10 days leave to file her amended pleading.

 

Defense Counsel may file a motion for reasonable fees and costs.
