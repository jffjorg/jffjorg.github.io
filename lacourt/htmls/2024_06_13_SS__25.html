Case Number: 19STCV12706    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Thursday, June 13, 2024

Case Name:                             SCOT L. BENNETT v. LUIS HERNANDEZ, DAMIEN HERNANDEZ, a minor, JOHN KOUTSOUKOS, “JANE DOE” KOUTSOUKOS and DOES 1 to 50                   

Case No.:                                19STCV12706

Motion:                                   Motion to Set Aside Dismissal Entered on 04/1/2022 and to Deem the 5 Year Dismissal Statute Tolled

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Scot L. Bennett

Responding Party:                   None

Notice:                                    Not OK (no proof of service filed)

 

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Plaintiff Scot L. Bennett’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and to Deem the 5 Year Dismissal Statute Tolled is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE. 

 

 

BACKGROUND     

 

On April 12, 2019, Plaintiff Scot L. Bennett (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Luis Hernandez, Damien Hernandez (a minor), John Koutsoukos, “Jane Doe” Koutsoukos, and Does 1 to 50, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) fraud, (3) common counts, and (4) conversion.

 

The Attachments to the Complaint allege the following.  Plaintiff is a senior paralegal with 30 years of experience. While sitting in a courtroom at Stanley Mosk Courthouse, Plaintiff heard the court deny a petition for approval of a minor’s compromise that had been filed by Defendant Luis Hernandez (“Hernandez”) on behalf of Defendant Damien Hernandez (a minor and Hernandez’s son). Outside the courtroom, Plaintiff approached Hernandez, the parties spoke about the case, and Plaintiff stated he will refer Hernandez to attorney John Koutsoukos (“Koutsoukos”). Plaintiff then brought the case to the Koutsoukos law firm and had Hernandez sign a standard contingency fee retainer agreement with everyone understanding that Plaintiff was a third-party beneficiary to the agreement and that Plaintiff would be given referral fees from Koutsoukos’s attorney fees. However, when the Damien Hernandez case settled, Koutsoukos cut off all communication with Plaintiff and kept all fees he received to himself, depriving Plaintiff of at least $6,250. Around the same time Plaintiff introduced Hernandez to Koutsoukos and Hernandez signed the contingency fee agreement, Koutsoukos and Plaintiff entered into a separate agreement whereby they agreed, among other things, that Koutsoukos would open a private general practice and Plaintiff would lend his civil litigation knowledge and expertise to the firm. However, Koutsoukos reneged on that agreement by failing to advertise his practice, affecting Plaintiff’s potential income.

 

On September 21, 2020, Koutsoukos filed his Answer to the complaint.

 

On March 15, 2022, the case was called for trial, and Plaintiff failed to appear. Therefore, the case was dismissed without prejudice.

 

On March 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed a motion to set aside the dismissal, arguing he could not appear because he could not catch a train on time and call the courtroom. Minute Order dated April 18, 2022, p. 2, the second to the last paragraph.

 

On April 18, 2022, the Court granted the motion to set aside the dismissal. ANALYSIS

 

I.          MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL

A.        Legal Standard

1.      Relief Under Section 473(b)

 

Pursuant to Section 473(b), a court may “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” However, application for relief under the statute must be brought “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).

 

2.      Tolling the 5-Year Statute of Limitations

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310 (“Section 583.310”) states: “An action shall be brought to trial within five years after the action is commenced against the defendant.”

 

“In computing the time within which an action must be brought to trial …, there shall be excluded the time during which any of the following conditions existed:

 

(a)      The jurisdiction of the court to try the action was suspended.

(b)      Prosecution or trial of the action was stayed or enjoined.

(c)      Bringing the action to trial, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile.”

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 583.340 (“Section 583.340”).

 

“Section 583.130 instructs that ‘the policy favoring trial or other disposition of an action on the merits [is] generally to be preferred over the policy that requires dismissal for failure to proceed with reasonable diligence in the prosecution of an action in construing the provisions of this chapter.’” Seto v. Szeto (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 76, 85 (“Seto”).

 

“‘Accordingly, the tolling provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340 must be liberally construed consistent with the policy favoring trial on the merits.’ [Citation.]” Seto, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at p. 85.

 

“When applying the tolling exceptions in section 583.340(c) [i.e., impossibility, impracticability, or futility], a trial court ‘must consider “‘all the circumstances in the individual case, including the acts and conduct of the parties and the nature of the proceedings themselves. [Citations.] The critical factor in applying these exceptions to a given factual situation is whether the plaintiff exercised reasonable diligence in prosecuting his or her case.’”’ [Citation.] ‘“The question of impossibility, impracticability, or futility is best resolved by the trial court, which ‘is in the most advantageous position to evaluate these diverse factual matters in the first instance.’ [Citation.] The plaintiff bears the burden of proving that the circumstances warrant application of the ... exception. [Citation.] ... The trial court has discretion to determine whether that exception applies, and its decision will be upheld unless the plaintiff has proved that the trial court abused its discretion. [Citations.]’ [Citation.]”’” Seto, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at p. 86.

 

            In Tanguilig v. Neiman Marcus Group, Inc. (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 313, 324, the California Court of Appeal held “that for the tolling provision in section 583.340(c) to prevent a case from being dismissed, ‘a plaintiff must prove (1) a circumstance establishing impossibility, impracticability, or futility, (2) a causal connection between the circumstance and the failure to move the case to trial within the five-year period, and (3) that she was reasonably diligent in prosecuting her case at all stages in the proceedings.’” Seto, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at p. 93.

 

However, in Seto, the Court of Appeal clarified that “[t]he main authority for the causation requirement is [Sierra Nevada Memorial-Miners Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 464, 471-473 (“Sierra Nevada”)].” Seto, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at p. 94.

 

 “Sierra Nevada required a ‘causal connection’ between the circumstance alleged to justify tolling and the impossibility or impracticability of bringing the action to trial in order to distinguish between run-of-the-mill illnesses of counsel during litigation, which do not cause it to be impossible, impracticable, or futile to bring an action to trial, and extended illnesses or illnesses on the eve of the expiration of the five-year period, which do.” Seto, supra, 86 Cal.App.5th at p. 94.

 

In sum, to obtain an order tolling the 5-year statute of limitations pursuant to Section 583.340(c), a plaintiff must prove (1) that bringing the action to trial within the statute of limitations period was impossible, impracticable, or futile, and (2) a causal connection between the circumstance alleged to justify tolling and the alleged impossibility, impracticability, or futility. 

 

B.        Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moves to set aside the dismissal entered on December 5, 2023.

 

            However, Plaintiff has not yet filed a supplemental declaration attaching the missing exhibits. See Bennett Decl., ¶¶ 5, 6, and 7 (stating that Exhibit A contains his medical records substantiating his period of infirmity, Exhibit B documents showing he was prepared to go to trial, and Exhibit C email from Plaintiff to Koutsoukos requesting that he stipulates to extending the 5-year statute of limitations). 

 

            Plaintiff has also not yet filed (1) a proof of service of the moving papers on the defendants and (2) proof of service of the notice of the Court’s ruling on May 14, 2024, continuing the hearing to June 13, 2024.

 

            This Court warned Plaintiff that failure to comply with the Court’s order on May 14, 2024, would result in the motion being placed off calendar or denied.

 

            Accordingly, the Court hereby denies the motion.

 

II.        CONCLUSION

 

            Plaintiff Scot L. Bennett’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and to Deem the 5 Year Dismissal Statute Tolled is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

 

The Moving party is ordered to give notice and to attach a copy of the Court's Tentative Ruling, as an exhibit “A” to said notice, as the final order of the Court.

BACKGROUND

Compl., ¶¶ 1, 2, 13. “Prior to the commencement of this action, the claims herein sued upon were assigned to Plaintiff by Plaintiff’s Assignor for collection purposes ….” Compl., ¶ 4.

 

            On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motions, seeking orders (1) compelling COAL and Nunn (collectively, “Defendants”) to serve their initial responses to Plaintiff’s first set of demands for inspection, and (2) imposing sanctions against Defendants and their counsel of record, Peter C. MOVING PARTY’S POSITION

 

This Court entered judgment against Felix and Yelena Bobritsky for $7,412, which remains unsatisfied. Therefore, Plaintiffs served “Interrogatories to Enforce Judgment” on those defendants, but both have refused to respond to the discovery.

Each of the document requests in question focuses on the core issues of this case and is limited to the relevant time and scope.             Defendants’ late-served documents remain deficient.

ANALYSIS

 

I.          MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSE TO DEMAND FOR PRODUCTION

 

A.        Legal Standard

Within 30 days after service of a demand for inspection, the responding party shall serve responses to the discovery unless, on motion of the propounding party, the court has shortened the time for response, or, on motion of the responding party, the court has extended the time for response.  Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).

 

Failure to serve timely responses results in the responding party waiving their right to object to the discovery unless the court, on motion, relieves the party from the waiver.  Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).

 

If a party fails to serve timely responses, the party making the discovery request may move for an order compelling responses.  Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b).

 

Monetary sanctions shall be imposed against any party who unsuccessfully opposes motions to compel responses to requests for production of documents unless the court finds that “the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).

 

B.        Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel Defendants’ initial response to COAL Demand, Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 5, and Nunn Demand, Nos. 1 and 2.

 

            Nunn Demand Nos. 1 and 2 asked Nun to produce.

 

·         The original and/or a legible copy of any and all WRITINGS or any other written documents or written records of communication between Republic Master Chefs and Defendant.

 

·         The original and/or a legible copy of any and all WRITINGS or other written documents or records of any sort which YOU intend to utilize as a basis or ground for any defense in this action which are not privileged.

 

Motion to Compel Nunn’s Responses, Exhibit 1, p. 3:7-11.

 

            COAL Demand, Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 5 asked COAL to produce the following.

 

·         The original and/or a legible copy of any and all WRITINGS or any other written documents or written records of communication between Republic Master Chefs and Defendant.

·         The original and/or a legible copy of any and all WRJTINGS or other written documents or records of any sort which YOU intend to utilize as a basis or ground for any defense in this action which are not privileged.

·         Produce any and all WRITINGS, as that term is defined by Evidence Code §250, reflecting and/or relating to payments made by defendant to Republic Master Chefs, including but not limited to copies of checks.

·         Produce all WRJTINGS, as that term is defined by Evidence Code section 250, reflecting and/or relating to any written complaints YOU made to Republic Master Chefs regarding the goods or services provided under the Rental Agreement.

 

Motion to Compel COAL’s Responses, Exhibit 1, p. 3:7-14, 3:18-20.

 

Defendants argue in their opposition that the motions are moot because they already produced the responsive documents at issue. (See Opposition, Declaration of Peter C. Freeman, 4; Exhibit 1 – a copy of the responses [attaching two (2) Exhibits in response to all requests for production of documents at issue above].)

 

            As Plaintiff argues, the response Defendants recently served is not code compliant. For example, Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.280, subd. (a) states: “Any documents or category of documents produced in response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall be identified with the specific request number to which the documents respond.” Here, Defendants did not identify which document was responsive to which demand. In addition, although Defendants can respond to the demands by stating that they lack the ability to comply with the demands: “A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210, subd. (a)(2); 2031.230. Here, Defendants argue in their opposition that they could not find certain documents responsive to the demands at issue yet failed to include the required statement of inability in their verified responses to the demands.

(Motions, Declarations of Joseph Yoo, ¶ 8.)

Defendants argue that sanctions “in excess” of $3,300 would be impermissibly punitive.

 

A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791. The Court will only impose sanctions of $1,344, with each defendant paying Plaintiff sanctions of $672. The request for sanctions against defense counsel is denied.

BACKGROUND

 

On January 18, 2023, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Antonio Hernandez Perez aka Antonio Hernandez Perez (“Defendant” or “Perez”) and Yulsa Gonzales aka Yulis Asucely Escobar Gonzalez (collectively “Defendants”) for subrogation to recover $8,655.16 in damages.  On December 11, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer.

 

            Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted (the “Motion”) as to Defendant Perez on April 23, 2024.  No opposition has been filed as of June 11, 2024.

           

ANALYSIS

 

I.                    Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A. Requests for Admission

 

A party must respond to requests for admissions within 30 days after service of such requests. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)  “If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response…(a) [that party] waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7.”  (Id. at subd. (b).)  A motion dealing with the failure to respond, rather than with inadequate responses, does not require the requesting party to meet and confer with the responding party. (Deymer v. Costa Mesa Mobile Home Estates (1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 393, 395, fn. 4 [disapproved on other grounds in Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973]. There is no time limit within which a motion to have matters deemed admitted must be made. (Brigante v. Huang (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 1569, 1585.)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel served Defendant Perez with Requests for Admission, Set One, on January 5, 2024 via electronic service.  (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 1., Ex. “A.”)  Defendant’s responses became due 32 days later on February 8, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 2.)  Plaintiff granted an extension to respond by April 16, 2024, but Defendant has not served responses upon Plaintiff as of the date of this Motion.  (Id.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming the Requests for Admission, Set One, admitted against Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.)

 

B. Request for Sanctions  

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)  Furthermore, it is “mandatory that the Court impose a monetary sanction…on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

            The Court finds Defendant’s failure to respond to Plaintiff’s discovery request a misuse of the discovery process.  In addition, the Court is required to impose a monetary sanction on Defendant for his failure to respond to the Requests for Admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c).

 

            Plaintiff seeks sanctions of $460.00 based on two hours of attorney time billed at $200.00 per hour and one filing fee of $60.00.  (Espinosa Decl., ¶ 6.)  However, the amount sought is excessive given the simplicity of this Motion and the lack of opposition and reply.  The Court finds $360.00 based on 1.5 hours of attorney time and one filing fee, to be reasonable.  Sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within thirty (30) days of notice of this order.

 

CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted is GRANTED.  

 

Plaintiff’s request for Sanctions is also GRANTED in the amount of $360.00 to be paid by Defendant Antonio Hernandez Perez within thirty (30) days of notice of this Court’s order.

ANALYSIS 

 

I.                   Enforcing the Settlement Agreement    

 

A.                Legal Standard  

 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides a summary procedure to enforce a settlement agreement by entering judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Hines v. Lukes (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182.)  “[I]f the parties to pending litigation enter into a settlement either in writing signed by the parties or orally before the court, the court, upon a motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Id.)  “The court retains jurisdiction to enforce a settlement under the statute even after a dismissal, but only if the parties requested such a retention of jurisdiction before the dismissal.”  (Id.)  “Such a request must  be made either in writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.”  (Id.)  

 

“A court ruling on a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement.”  (Id.)  “A settlement is enforceable under section 664.6 only if the parties agreed to all material settlement terms.” (Id.) “The court ruling on the motion may consider the parties’ declarations and other evidence in deciding what terms the parties agreed to.”  (Id.)  “If the court determines that the parties entered into an enforceable settlement, it should grant the motion and enter a formal judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Id.)  Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6 “expressly provides for the court to enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.”  (Id. at p. 1183.)

 

 

B.     Discussion   

 

In support of the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel, Jay W. Smith (“Smith”), declares that on or about October 3, 2023, Plaintiff and Defendant agreed to settle this case by signing the Agreement.  (Smith Decl., ¶ 5.)  Defendant agreed to pay Plaintiff principal sum of $8,931.95, plus filing fees in the sum of $450.00, plus service fees in the sum $115.50, plus mandatory E-filing fees in the sum of $36.62, for the total sum of $9,534.07 in monthly payments.  (Id. at  ¶ 5; Ex. “1.”)  Defendant made the initial payment, but she only made three payments of $264.83 for a total amount of $1,080.50 with the last payment having been made on February 20, 2023 and has made no further payments since then.  (Id. at  ¶ 6.)  Counsel’s office has not received emails from Defendant.  (Id.)  Regular and consistent efforts have been made to encourage Defendant to complete all of the obligations under the settlement.  (Id.)  Defendant has only paid $1,080.50 towards the principal amount, there is now due and owing the sum of $9,719.09.  (Id. at ¶ 7.) Counsel requests that the Court enforce the Agreement and enter judgment against Defendant in the principal amount of $9,534.07, plus costs in the amount of $644.34, plus interest of 10% from August 31, 2023 through April 25, 2024 in the sum of $621.18, less payment received in the sum of $1,080.50, for a total judgment amount of $9,719.09.  (Id. at  ¶ 8.)  

 

 The Court finds that it has authority to enforce the Agreement pursuant to Hines v. Lukes, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182.  Plaintiff and Defendant signed the Agreement.  The Court also has jurisdiction to enforce the Agreement as the parties explicitly requested that the Court would retain jurisdiction to enforce such agreement.  The Court furthermore determines that the parties entered into a valid and binding agreement.  The declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel sets forth the terms of the settlement and indicates that Defendant has breached the Agreement.  

 

However, the Court notes the Agreement provides that in the event of default, judgment may be entered against Defendant “without notice in the sum of $8,931.95, plus court costs including all first appearance fees and interest, less payments received.”  (Smith Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. “1” at ¶ 6.)  Counsel’s declaration does not indicate any first appearance fees that were incurred.  Defendant has made payments totaling $1,080.50.  (Smith Decl., ¶ 8.)  Further, counsel’s declaration provides costs in the amount of $644.34, plus interest of 10% from August 31, 2023 through April 25, 2024 in the sum of $621.18.  (Id.)  As such, the Court will enter judgment in the amount of $9,116.97,which represents the among of judgment the Court can enter pursuant to the Agreement, which is $8,931.95, plus costs in the amount of $644.34, plus interest of 10% from August 31, 2023 through April 25, 2024 in the sum of $621.18, less payment received in the sum of $1,080.50.  (Smith Decl., ¶ 8; Ex. “1” at ¶ 6.) 

 

The Motion to Enforce Settlement Agreement is therefore GRANTED IN PART. The Court will enforce the Agreement and enter judgment thereto but only in the amount of $9,116.97 pursuant to the Court’s discussion above.  

 

II.        Conclusion  

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED IN PART. The Court will enforce the Agreement and enter judgment thereto but only in the amount of $9,116.97 pursuant to the Court’s discussion above.  

 

The Dismissal entered on 11/06/2023 is set aside and vacated.

Case No.:                                24STLC02014

Responding Party:                   None

Notice:                                    OK

 

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Defendant Prennella Johnson’s Demurrer (titled Motion to Dismiss) is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant is to file an answer or responsive pleading to the Complaint within 30 days.

 

 

BACKGROUND     

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiff Los Angles Federal Credit Union (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action by filing a “Complaint Pursuant to CCP § 366.2 And Probate Code § 5670, et seq.) for: Breach of Written Contract” (the “Complaint”) against Defendants Prennella Johnson aka Prenella Royal, and Does 1 through 40, inclusive.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On November 4, 2008, Mary Frances Adams (“Adams”), the mother of Defendant Prennella Johnson aka Prenella Royal, entered into a written Visa credit card agreement with Plaintiff. Compl., ¶ 8; Exhibit A – a copy of the contract. Plaintiff has duly performed all of the conditions precedent on its part required to be performed under the loan agreement. Defendant (as the surviving heir of MOTHER is liable to Plaintiff) pursuant to Probate Code § 5672 to the extent MOTHER’s property passed to Defendant without administration. Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereon alleges that all of MOTHER’s property passed without administration to Defendant after MOTHER passed away on September 20, 2023.” Compl., ¶ 9. “No part of said $8,713.22 Visa card balance due has been paid, although payment has been demanded and there is now due, owing and unpaid from Defendant to Plaintiff the sum of $8,713.22.” Compl., ¶ 10.

 

On April 9, 2024, Defendant Prennella Johnson (“Defendant”), in pro per, filed the instant demurrer (titled motion to dismiss).

 

On May 16, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the demurrer, but continued the hearing because Defendant had failed to file a declaration showing she had met and conferred with Plaintiff before filing the demurrer. The Court ordered the parties to meet and confer before the next hearing on June 13, 2024, and Defendant to file and serve a supplemental declaration, at least five (5) court days before the next hearing, informing the Court about the outcome of the meet and confer. The Court warned Defendant that failure to file that supplemental declaration may result in the Motion being placed off calendar or denied.

 

On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition to the demurrer.

 

As of June 11, 2024, no reply has been filed.

 

MOVING PARTY’S POSITION

 

The Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because Defendant is the wrong defendant; she has never entered into a written visa credit card agreement with Plaintiff and her name is not mentioned on the credit card agreement attached to the Complaint. The signatory to the agreement shows it was signed by Mary F Adams, deceased, and Claudia Johnson, deceased.  Defendant is the daughter of Mary F. Adams.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            Defendant did not meet and confer with Plaintiff or serve the moving papers on Plaintiff. Plaintiff only found out about the demurrer through the minute order that the clerk mailed to Plaintiff.

 

            In any event, Opposition asserts that the Complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant. Defendant is the beneficiary of Decedent Mary Frances Adams’s estate and, therefore, liable for the decedent’s debt. As shown in the decedent’s death certificate, the decedent died on September 20, 2023, and Defendant is listed on the death certificate as the decedent’s beneficiary. Defendant also admits that she is the daughter of the decedent. Therefore, pursuant to Probate Code sections 5672 and 5674, Defendant is liable for the decedent’s death since there is no evidence that any administration of the estate (i.e., probate) has been filed with the Los Angeles County Superior Court.

 

 

REPLY

 

            None.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          DEMURRER

A.        Legal Standard

A party may demurer a pleading on several statutory grounds, including that “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).

“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315. “‘We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation.] We also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.’ [Citation.] Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.

“If a complaint does not state a cause of action, but there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment, leave to amend must be granted.” Milligan v. Golden Gate Bridge Highway & Transportation Dist. (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1, 6.

B.        Request for Judicial Notice

 

            On May 20, 2024, Defendant filed a request for judicial notice of Mary Frances Adams’s Certificate of Death issued by the County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health (Exhibit A).

 

            The request for judicial notice is granted. See People v. Terry (1974) 38 Cal.App.3d 432, 439 (“Evidence Code section 452 provides that judicial notice may be taken of official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy. Accordingly, judicial notice may be taken of a death certificate”).

 

C.        Meet and Confer

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. ….

 

(1)   As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies. The party who filed the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer shall provide legal support for its position that the pleading is legally sufficient or, in the alternative, how the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer could be amended to cure any legal insufficiency.

 

(2)   The parties shall meet and confer at least 5 days before the date the responsive pleading is due. If the parties are not able to meet and confer at least 5 days before the date the responsive pleading is due, the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension of time within which to file a responsive pleading, by filing and serving, on or before the date on which a demurrer would be due, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer. The 30-day extension shall commence from the date the responsive pleading was previously due, and the demurring party shall not be subject to default during the period of the extension. Any further extensions shall be obtained by court order upon a showing of good cause.

 

(3)   The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either of the following:

 

(A)   The means by which the demurring party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer.

 

(B)   That the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.”

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a) (emphasis added).

 

On May 16, 2024, the Court continued the hearing to allow Defendant to meet and confer with Plaintiff regarding the issues raised in the demurrer.

 

The Court ordered Defendant to file and serve a supplemental declaration, at least five (5) court days before the next hearing, informing the Court about the outcome of the meet and confer. The Court warned that failure to do so may result in the motion being placed off calendar or denied.

 

As of June 11, 2024, Defendant had not filed the supplemental declaration.

 

Therefore, there is no proof that Defendant met and conferred with Plaintiff. Indeed, on May 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition to the motion. In support of that opposition, Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration, attesting that Defendant never served Plaintiff with the moving papers and did not call counsel to meet and confer (she only called to ask why she was being sued for her mother’s debt). Opposition, Declaration of Alana Anaya (“Anaya Decl.”), ¶ 2. The only reason counsel became aware of the subject demurrer was because counsel received the Court’s minute order through the mail. Anaya Decl., ¶ 3. 

 

Accordingly, the Court finds it proper to overrule the demurrer based on Defendant’s failure to meet and confer with Plaintiff.

 

Even if the Court considered the demurrer on its merits, it would still overrule it because the Complaint has alleged facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. As Plaintiff argues in its opposition, Probate Code section 5672 states: “Each beneficiary is personally liable to the extent provided in Section 5674 for the unsecured debts of the transferor. Any such debt may be enforced against the beneficiary in the same manner as it could have been enforced against the transferor if the transferor had not died.” Section 5674 states: “A beneficiary is not liable under Section 5672 if proceedings for the administration of the transferor’s estate are commenced and the beneficiary satisfies the requirements of Section 5677 or 5678.” Prob. Code, § 5674, subd. (a) (emphasis added). Here, the Complaint alleges: “Defendant (as the surviving heir of [the decedent] is liable to Plaintiff) pursuant to Probate Code § 5672 to the extent [the decedent’s] property passed to Defendant without administration. Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereon alleges that all of [decedent’s] property passed without administration to Defendant after [the decedent] passed away on September 20, 2023.” Compl., ¶ 9 (emphasis added). Therefore, based on Probate Code sections 5672 and 5674, the Complaint has alleged facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant.

 

For those reasons, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

II.        CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Prennella Johnson’s Demurrer (titled Motion to Dismiss) is OVERRULED.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer or responsive pleading to the Complaint within 30 days.

 

            Plaintiff Los Angeles Federal Credit Union is ordered to give notice and to attach a copy of the Court's Tentative Ruling, as an exhibit “A” to said notice, as the final order of the Court.
