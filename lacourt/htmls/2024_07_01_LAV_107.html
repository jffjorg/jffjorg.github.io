DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV05649    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Laura Ramirez,

 

         Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Tesla Motors, Inc.,         

          Defendant.

   Case Number             Department

23VECV05649                         107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

(1) Demurrer

(2) Motion to Strike

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:       

 

Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. demurs to the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action and moves to strike portions of plaintiff Laura Ramirez’s complaint. This court grants the motion to strike as to the first sentence of paragraph 8 and all references to “plaintiff’s” or “her” vehicle of paragraphs 53, 64, and 91 of the complaint. This court overrules the demurrer as to the third and fifth causes of action and sustains the demurrer as to the fourth cause of action. This court grants plaintiff leave to amend the complaint. Defendant’s request for judicial notice is denied.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On December 23, 2021, plaintiff Laura Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) was driving a certain vehicle (the “Vehicle”) manufactured by defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”) when the Vehicle malfunctioned and caused a collision with a highway median divider and injuring Plaintiff. (Complaint ¶¶ 9–10.)

 

On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this action by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) strict products liability, (2) negligence—products liability, (3) breach of implied warranty of merchantability, (4) breach of implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose, and (5) breach of express warranty.

 

On March 18, 2024, Defendant moved to compel arbitration, which Plaintiff opposed on March 25, 2024. Concurrently with her opposition to Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration, Plaintiff filed a declaration (the “Ramirez Declaration”). On April 5, 2024, Defendant withdrew its motion to compel arbitration.

 

On May 31, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and motion to strike (the “Motion”), in response to which Plaintiff filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) on June 17, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿412.20, subd. (a)(3).) Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.)¿¿ 

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of personal service indicating that the Complaint was personally served on Defendant on February 1, 2024. In light of the withdrawn motion to compel arbitration, this court will consider the Demurrer and Motion, although they appear untimely. The Opposition is timely.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§¿430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)¿¿ 

 

Defendant submits a declaration by its counsel describing his efforts to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel. (Bruce Decl. ¶¶ 7–11.) He indicates that he and Plaintiff’s counsel discussed but were unable to resolve the issues raised by the Demurrer and the Motion at a case management conference. (Id. at ¶ 11.) He also indicates that Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond to a number of Defendant’s counsel’ attempts to meet and confer with him. (Id. at ¶¶ 7–10.) We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met, though we remind Plaintiff’s counsel of his obligation to meet and confer with Defendant’s counsel in good faith.

 

  1. Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests that this court take judicial notice of the Complaint. The Complaint is already in the record in the instant action and therefore this court does not need to take judicial notice of it.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Defendant’s request for judicial notice.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.)¿“[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

In the Ramirez Declaration, Plaintiff states that her mother Ana Maria Amarales (“Amarales”) purchased the Vehicle and that she only assisted her mother with creating an online account on Defendant’s website to do so. (Ramirez Decl. ¶¶ 6–7.) Plaintiff declares that her mother, and not she, placed the order for the Vehicle, signed the Motor Vehicle Purchase Agreement and binding arbitration agreement “on her own,” and was “indicated as a buyer and receiving party of the said Vehicle in the invoice issued by Defendant.” (Id. at ¶¶ 7–9.) Plaintiff further declares that “I have not signed any document related to the purchase of the Motor Vehicle Order Agreement… nor have I authorized my mother to sign any document on my behalf.” (Id. at ¶ 10.) Plaintiff declares, “[s]tarting from the date of the purchase of the Vehicle my mother has been the registered owner of the Vehicle.” Plaintiff also certifies the copies of the Motor Vehicle Purchase Agreement and the invoice for the Vehicle’s purchase that are attached to her opposition to the motion to compel arbitration. (Id. at ¶¶ 8–9.) The Motor Vehicle Purchase Agreement attached to the opposition is unsigned by the buyer but indicates that only Amarales was the buyer of the Vehicle. (Opposition Exh. A.) The invoice attached to the opposition similarly indicates that the Vehicle was “sold to” Amarales. (Id. at Exh. B.)

 

Defendant moves to strike paragraphs 8, 53, 64, and 91 of the Complaint.

 

Paragraph 8 reads, “[o]n or around July 2021, Plaintiff purchased her [Vehicle] from [Defendant] in Los Angeles County, California. The failed autopilot feature is the original equipment that came with the initial purchase of the [Vehicle].”

 

Paragraph 53 reads, “[h]ad Defendants, and each of them, performed those tests and studies necessary to determine that the autopilot feature was not safe to use as designed, Plaintiff’s autopilot feature would not have malfunctioned causing Plaintiff’s [Vehicle] to forcefully strike the median divider during a late-night drive on December 23, 2021 causing her to sustain significant injuries.”

 

Paragraph 64 reads, “Plaintiff, in using her [Vehicle] to operate under the autopilot feature, reasonably relied upon the skill and judgment of Defendants, and each of them, as to whether the [Vehicle] was of merchantable quality and safe for its intended, reasonably foreseeable and/or ordinary use.”

 

Paragraph 91 reads, “Plaintiff reasonably relied upon Defendants’ warranty that the autopilot feature was safe and effective when it came with the purchase of her [Vehicle] and put the autopilot to normal and proper use.”

 

The first sentence of Paragraph 8 is contradicted by the Ramirez Declaration. While Plaintiff, in the Opposition, avers that she “does not consider that any paragraphs from the complaint shall be stricken,” she does not dispute that the statement in the Ramirez Declaration that Amarales and not she purchased the Vehicle. The Ramirez Declaration, being based on personal knowledge and signed under penalty of perjury, meets the requirements of an affidavit and therefore is considered submitted evidence. Because this court may strike any false matter from the Complaint, we agree that the first sentence of Paragraph 8 may be struck.

 

Paragraphs 53, 64, and 91 each refer to the Vehicle as “Plaintiff’s” Vehicle or “her” Vehicle, and Paragraph 53 refers to “Plaintiff’s autopilot feature.” We agree that because Plaintiff has submitted evidence and does not in the Opposition contest that the Vehicle does not belong to her, these paragraphs are false and may be properly subjected to a motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to the first sentence of Paragraph 8 and all references to the Vehicle being “Plaintiff’s” or “her” vehicle in Paragraphs 53, 64, and 91.

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to the Complaint’s third cause of action for breach of implied warranty of merchantability, fourth cause of action for breach of implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose, and fifth cause of action for breach of express warranty. Defendant contends that these three causes of action all require privity of contract, which Plaintiff does not have with Defendant. Defendant additionally contends that the Complaint does not plead the terms of any express warranty.

 

“The general rule is that privity of contract is required in an action for breach of either express or implied warranty.” (All West Electronics, Inc. v. M–B–W, Inc. (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 717, 725.) “Privity of contract is a prerequisite in California for recovery on a theory of breach of implied warranties of fitness and merchantability.” (Blanco v. Baxter Healthcare Corp. (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1058.)

 

Plaintiff cites Hauter v. Zogarts (1975) 14 Cal.3d 104 (hereafter, Hauter) to support her assertion that family members of a purchaser of a defective product are excepted from the privity requirement for warranty claims. (Opposition pp. 4–5, 7.) In Hauter, supra, at 114, fn. 8, the “fact that [the plaintiff] [was] not in privity with defendants [did] not bar recovery” because “[p]rivity is not required for an action based upon an express warranty,” and “[a]lthough privity appears to remain a requirement for actions based upon the implied warranty of merchantability, [the plaintiff] [came] within a well-recognized exception to the rule: he [was] a member of the purchaser's family.” (see also Rodriguez v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 500 [“[P]rivity is not a requirement for actions based upon an express warranty.”].) Hauter’s assertion that privity is not required for actions based on express warranties is limited to only “when a consumer relies on representations made by a manufacturer in labels or advertising material.” (Fundin v. Chicago Pneumatic Tool Co. (1984) 152 Cal.App.3d 951, 957; see also Burr v. Sherwin Williams Co. (1954) 42 Cal.2d 682, 696 [“Another possible exception to the general rule is found in a few cases where the purchaser of a product relied on representations made by the manufacturer in labels or advertising material, and recovery from the manufacturer was allowed on the theory of express warranty without a showing of privity.”].)

 

The Complaint alleges that “[t]hrough aggressive marketing and advertising campaigns, Defendants, and each of them, expressly warranted that the [Vehicle’s] autopilot feature was sale and effective to members of the consuming public, including Plaintiff” and that “Plaintiff reasonably relied upon Defendants' warranty that the autopilot feature was safe and effective when it came with the purchase of her [Vehicle] and put the autopilot to normal and proper use.” (Complaint ¶ 87, 91.) Although the reference to the Vehicle being “her” Vehicle was struck by this court, the portion of Paragraph 91 quoted above about Plaintiff’s reasonable reliance remains in the Complaint. Thus, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff relied on representations made by Defendant in its advertising materials in her decision to operate the defective Vehicle. These allegations adequately put Plaintiff’s cause of action for breach of the express warranty within the exception to the general rule that privity is required between parties for express warranties. And, they adequately plead the existence of an express warranty. As stated above, they plead that the advertisements published by Defendant created an express warranty. Although Defendant argues that the allegations of the Complaint “do no more than set forth a ‘commendation of the goods’ of the sort found wanting” by the court in Keith v. Buchanan (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 13, 20, this case cited by Defendant does not deal with a demurrer but with a motion for judgment at the close of a party’s evidence, which has a higher standard of proof than the pleading standard to overcome a demurrer.

 

The third and fourth causes of action relate to the implied warranty of merchantability and the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose, respectively, in the Motor Vehicle Purchase Agreement between Amarales and Defendant. Following Hauter, supra, 14 Cal.3d 104, the Ramirez Declaration states “[m]y mother’s name is [Amarales].” (Ramirez Decl. ¶ 2.) Amarales similarly submitted a declaration signed under penalty of perjury that declares that she is the mother of Plaintiff. (Amarales Decl. ¶ 1.) Plaintiff, as Amarales’ daughter, falls within the exception to the rule that privity is required for a cause of action based upon the implied warranty of merchantability for members of the purchaser’s family. We do not, however, see authority establishing that the same exception applies to a cause of action based upon the implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer as to the third and fifth causes of action and SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the fourth cause of action.

 

  1. Leave to Amend

 

“Where the defect raised by a¿motion¿to¿strike¿or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave¿to¿amend¿is routinely and¿liberally¿granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) “[An order granting a motion to strike] is analogous to an order sustaining a demurrer.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303.)¿ 

 

We note that this is the first time the Complaint has been subject to a motion to strike or demurrer and believe that the defects in the Complaint that are struck are reasonably capable of cure.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS leave to amend.

I. BACKGROUND

On November 24, 2023, plaintiff Nafiseh Arjomand (“Plaintiff”) leased a certain vehicle (the “Vehicle”) warrantied by the Vehicle’s manufacturer, defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“Defendant”). To do so, Plaintiff executed a motor vehicle lease agreement (the “Agreement”) that contained an arbitration clause (the “Arbitration Clause”). (Motion p. 3; Ameripour Decl. Exh. 2.) The Arbitration Clause provides, in relevant part,

 

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort or otherwise (including any dispute over the interpretation, scope, or validity of this lease, arbitration section or the arbitrability of any issue), between you and us or any of our employees, agents , successors, assigns, or the vehicle distributor, including Mercedes-Benz USA LLC (each a “Third Party Beneficiary”), which arises out of or relates to a credit application, this lease, or any resulting transaction or relationship arising out of this lease (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at the election of either you, us, or a Third Party Beneficiary, be resolved by a neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.

 

…

 

This lease evidences a transaction involving interstate commerce. Any arbitration under this lease shall be governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 USC 1, et seq).

 

(Motion pp. 3–4; Ameripour Decl. Exh. 2.)

 

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff initiated this action by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act—breach of express warranty, (2) violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act—breach of implied warranty, and (3) negligent repair.

 

On May 17, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel arbitration (the “Motion”). On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) to the Motion, to which Defendant replied on June 24, 2024.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing.

 

We are satisfied that all filings made in connection with the Motion are timely.

 

  1. Page Limits

 

Per California Rules of Court rule 3.1113(d), “no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages,” which limit “does not include the caption page, the notice of motion and motion, exhibits, declarations, attachments, the table of contents, the table of authorities, or the proof of service.” 

 

The Opposition is 18 pages in length and therefore exceeds the page limit imposed by California Rules of Court rule 3.1113(d). Although we do not decline to consider any part of the Opposition on this ground, we remind Plaintiff of the existence of and the importance of abiding by the California Rules of Court.

 

  1. Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests that this court take judicial notice of the Complaint. The Complaint is already in the record in the instant action and therefore this court does not need to take judicial notice of it.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract. (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co. (1998) 68 Cal. App.4th 83, 88.) The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc., 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.) “In determining whether an¿arbitration¿agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing¿arbitration.” (Weeks v. Crow¿(1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.)  

 

This court is empowered by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so. “The court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists….” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿1281.2.) Thus, “Section 1281.2 requires a court to order arbitration if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate exists.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841.)¿ 

¿ 

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971–72.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.)¿The party opposing arbitration bears the burden of showing that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the claims at issue. (EFund Capital Partners v. Pless (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1311, 1321.) 

 

  1. Defendant’s Standing to Compel Arbitration

 

“The general rule is that only a party to an arbitration agreement may enforce it.” (Ronay Family Limited Partnership v. Tweed (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 830, 837.) However, “the law recognizes exceptions to the general rule and allows a nonparty to enforce an arbitration agreement provided the nonparty has a sufficient ‘identity of interest’ with a party to the agreement.” (Id. at 838.)

 

1.      Equitable Estoppel

 

“The doctrine of equitable estoppel is an exception to the general rule that a nonsignatory to an agreement cannot be compelled to arbitrate and cannot invoke an agreement to arbitrate, without being a party to the arbitration agreement. Under that doctrine, as applied in both federal and California decisional authority, a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined’ with the underlying contract obligations. By relying on contract terms in a claim against a nonsignatory defendant, even if not exclusively, a plaintiff may be equitably estopped from repudiating the arbitration clause contained in that agreement.” (Kielar v. Superior Court (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, 619, internal quotation marks omitted.) “Merely ‘making reference to’ an agreement with an arbitration clause is not enough. The plaintiff's actual dependence on the underlying contract in making out the claim against the nonsignatory is always the sine qua non of an appropriate situation for applying equitable estoppel. Even if a plaintiff's claims ‘touch matters’ relating to the arbitration agreement, the claims are not arbitrable unless the plaintiff relies on the agreement to establish its cause of action. ‘But-for causation” alone is insufficient.” (Ibid.)

 

In Kielar v. Superior Court (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, 620–21 (hereafter, Kielar), the plaintiff brought a cause of action for breach of express and implied warranties under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, but the express warranty “was not part of his sales contract with the dealership. Indeed, the sales contract acknowledges this separate warranty and disclaims any implied warranties by the dealership.” On the basis that “manufacturer vehicle warranties that accompany the sale of motor vehicles without regard to the terms of the sale contract between the purchaser and the dealer are independent of the sale contract,” the court in Kielar found that “a manufacturers’ express or implied warranties that accompany a vehicle at the time of sale” do not “constitute obligations arising from the sale contract, permitting manufacturers to enforce an arbitration agreement in the contract pursuant to equitable estoppel.” (Ibid.)

 

Like the sales contract that contained no warranties in Kielar, supra, 94 Cal.App.5th at 620–21, the Agreement contains no express warranties and contains a provision that disclaims and implied warranties. (Ameripour Decl. Exh. 2.) As Plaintiff points out in the Opposition, the warranties under which Plaintiff brings her Song-Beverly causes of action create “statutory obligations under the Song-Beverly Act to adequately repair, replace, or repurchase the defective car.” (Opposition p. 5, emphasis original.) And, as the court in Kielar found, Defendant’s express and implied warranties that accompanied the Vehicle at the time it was leased do not constitute obligations arising from the Agreement, which would permit Defendant to enforce the Arbitration Clause pursuant to equitable estoppel.

 

Thus, we find that Defendant may not enforce the Arbitration Clause on an equitable estoppel basis.

 

2.      Third-Party Beneficiary

 

“A third party beneficiary is someone who may enforce a contract because the contract is made expressly for his benefit. The test for determining whether a contract was made for the benefit of a third person is whether an intent to benefit a third person appears from the terms of the contract. The mere fact that a contract results in benefits to a third party does not render that party a ‘third party beneficiary.’” (Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 973, internal citations omitted.) “[I]n considering third party beneficiary contract claims, a court should carefully examine the express provisions of the contract at issue, as well as all of the relevant circumstances under which the contract was agreed to, in order to determine not only (1) whether the third party would in fact benefit from the contract, but also (2) whether a motivating purpose of the contracting parties was to provide a benefit to the third party, and (3) whether permitting a third party to bring its own breach of contract action against a contracting party is consistent with the objectives of the contract and the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties. All three elements must be satisfied to permit the third party action to go forward.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendant is named in the Arbitration Clause as a third-party beneficiary who may elect to have controversies arising out of the Agreement resolved by arbitration and not by court action. We find that Defendant may therefore enforce the Arbitration Clause because the Agreement was made expressly for its benefit. We also consider that (1) Defendant indeed benefits from the Arbitration Clause by being given the option to elect to have controversies involving it resolved by arbitration; (2) per the text of the Arbitration Clause and the context in which Plaintiff was entering the Agreement to lease the Vehicle, which was manufactured by Defendant and which made Defendant therefore a likely third party to be involved in any controversies arising out of the Agreement, Plaintiff and the dealer/lessor intended for Defendant to benefit from the Arbitration Clause and this benefit to Defendant motivated them to enter into the Arbitration Clause; and (3) permitting Defendant to enforce the Arbitration Clause against Plaintiff is consistent with the objectives of the Agreement and the reasonable expectations of Plaintiff and the dealer/lessor. Because all three elements of this test are satisfied, we find that Defendant may enforce the Arbitration Clause.

 

Plaintiff argues in the Opposition that “the ‘Parties’ definition provision, found on the first page of the Lease, expressly sets forth which delineated parties have agreed to be bound by ‘the terms and conditions’ of the Lease.” However, Defendant does not seek to bind some non-signatory third party by the terms of the Agreement. Rather, it seeks to bind Plaintiff, a signatory to the Agreement. Additionally, the Agreement’s identification of the parties to the Agreement does not preclude the existence of third-party beneficiaries.

 

Thus, we conclude that Defendant has standing to enforce the Arbitration Clause as a third-party beneficiary of the Agreement.

 

  1. Scope of the Arbitration Clause

The Arbitration Clause describes its own scope as covering, in relevant part, “[a]ny claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort or otherwise… which arises out of or relates to a credit application, this lease, or any resulting transaction or relationship arising out of this lease (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract).” (Ameripour Decl. Exh. 2.)

 

The Agreement reads, in relevant part, “[i]f the vehicle is new, it is covered by a standard new vehicle warranty from the manufacturer…. We assign to you all rights we have under any of these warranties. You acknowledge that you have received a copy of the indicated warranties. (Ameripour Decl. Exh. 2.)

 

By assigning to Plaintiff all rights under Defendant’s warranties, the Agreement thus created a relationship between Plaintiff and Defendant arising out of the warranties and the Agreement. All of the Complaint’s causes of action arise out of or relate to the warranties that the Agreement assigned to Plaintiff. Therefore, all of the Complaint’s causes of action are claims or disputes that arise out of or relate to the relationship between Plaintiff and Defendant arising out of the warranties and the Agreement.

 

Thus, we find that the causes of action alleged by the Complaint fall within the scope of the Arbitration Clause.

 

  1. Stay of Proceedings

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4 provides, in relevant part, “[i]f a court of competent jurisdiction, whether in this State or not, has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” “In accordance with choice-of-law principles, the parties may limit the trial court's authority to stay or deny arbitration under the CAA by adopting the more restrictive procedural provisions of the FAA.” (Valencia v. Smyth (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.) “Absent such an express designation, however, the FAA's procedural provisions do not apply in state court.” (Id. at 174.)

 

The Arbitration Clause specifies that the FAA shall govern any arbitration under the Agreement, but it does not specify that the FAA’s procedural provisions should apply to a motion to stay or deny arbitration. Thus, this being a state court, the CAA applies. Following California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4, this action should therefore be stayed pending the outcome of arbitration.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion and STAYS all proceedings pending the outcome of arbitration.
