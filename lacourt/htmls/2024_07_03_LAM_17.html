DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV28098    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

 

INFERNO CALIFORNIA, INC.

 

 

         vs.

 

NEW CITY RELEASING, INC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  21STCV28098

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 3, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. Defendant’s answer is stricken and Plaintiff is to seek the entry of default with the clerk’s office. Defendant is sanctioned $8,676, payable within 30 days.

 

On 7/30/2021, Plaintiff Inferno California, Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against New City Releasing, Inc. and Alan B. Bursteen (collectively, Defendants), alleging: (1) fraud in the inducement; and (2) breach of contract.

 

            On 2/21/2024, Plaintiff moved for terminating sanctions striking Defendant Nile Niami’s answer. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Niami in the sum of $8,676 as the reasonable costs and attorney’s fees incurred by Plaintiff as a result of Niami’s abuse of the discovery process in this case pursuant to California Civil Procedure Code section 2023.030(a).

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff seeks terminating sanctions against Niami on the grounds that throughout this litigation he has repeatedly failed and refused to participate in any meaningful way in this case with the exception of the filing of his Answer.

 

            In support, Plaintiff submits evidence that:

 

-         The Court issued an Order dated February 16, 2023 granting Plaintiff’s motion to compel, which instructed Niami to appear for his deposition as the PMQ of his shell company Contemporary Vista. (Fabricant Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. 1.) Then, less than three months later, the Court issued another Order dated April 5, 2023 in which it fully granted another motion to compel filed by Plaintiff and instructed Niami to appear for depositions in both his individual capacity and in his capacity as the PMQ of his shell company Marbella Construction. (Fabricant Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. 2.)

 

-         After the Court granted Plaintiff’s second discovery motion compelling Niami’s deposition, Niami appeared to be interested in settlement, and his counsel at the time, Raines Feldman LLP (“Raines Feldman”), approached Plaintiff’s counsel to invite a settlement demand. Fabricant Decl. ¶ 5. However, after Plaintiff’s counsel transmitted Plaintiff’s initial settlement demand to Niami’s counsel, they never received a response or counter-offer. Ibid. Instead, the next substantive correspondence from opposing counsel indicated Raines Feldman would be seeking to withdraw as Niami’s counsel in this case. Ibid. It then became evident Niami had feigned interest in settlement and purported to engage in settlement negotiations in bad faith and to further delay Plaintiff’s efforts to depose him in accordance with the Court’s Orders dated February 16 and April 5, 2023.

 

-         After Raines Feldman successfully withdrew from this case and the holidays had passed, Plaintiff recommenced its diligent efforts to depose Niami pursuant to the Court’s February 16, 2023 and April 5, 2023 Orders. Fabricant Decl. ¶ 6. On January 4, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel reached out to Niami via the email addresses provided by his former counsel at Raines Feldman upon their withdrawal from the case. Ibid. Plaintiff’s counsel asked Niami to confirm whether he had found new representation for himself and his companies and to provide deposition dates for his Court-ordered depositions; reiterated Plaintiff’s settlement demand Niami had previously solicited; and advised Plaintiff would seek terminating sanctions against him as needed and applicable. Fabricant Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. 3. One week later, on January 11, 2024, Niami finally responded to Plaintiff’s counsel’s email. Ibid. In his email, Niami did not address the settlement offer. Ibid. Instead, he stated that had not retained counsel for himself or his companies and represented he would not sit for deposition in person since “[t]raveling back to the USA [] especially for multiple days of depositions [] would be a hardship for [him] now both financially and logistically.” Ibid.

 

-         Defendant ignored the April 5, 2023 order, and has to date failed to appear for deposition. (See Timeline of Events, Motion, 6:21-7:27.)

 

 

After review, the Court finds grounds to strike Defendant’s answer. Defendant’s conduct, alongside his failure to oppose this motion, indicate that Defendant is either unwilling or unable to engage in this litigation.

 

The Court considers Defendant’s failure to oppose to be a concession on the merits to Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions is granted. Defendant’s answer is stricken and Plaintiff is to seek the entry of default with the clerks office. Defendant is sanctioned $8,676.

On 2/22/2022, Plaintiff Skyler Wells (Plaintiff) filed suit against Strategic Acquisitions, Inc., BDR, Inc., Open House Realty & Investments, LLC, and Peter Baer. On 2/15/2023, Plaintiff filed a third amended complaint (TAC) alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) construction defects; (3) construction defects; (4) negligence; (5) negligence; (6) negligent infliction of emotional distress; and (7) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

 

            On 6/2/2024, Defendant BDR (BDR) moved to compel further responses to: (1) Form Interrogatories – Construction Litigation (Set One); (2) Request for Admissions (Set One); (3) Special Interrogatories (Set One); (4) Request for Production of Documents (Set One); (5) Form Interrogatories – Construction Litigation (Set Two); and (6) Request for Production of Documents (Set Two) (collectively, the Subject Discovery).

 

Discussion

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff clarified that he had served responses to the Subject Discovery requests on 5/29/2024.

 

            In reply, BDR concedes that this renders BDR’s request for further responses moot. However, BDR still seeks the imposition of monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel to reimburse it for the fees/costs incurred in having to bring the Motions.

           

            In support, BDR argues that:

 

… it is undisputed Plaintiff originally had between four and six months to provide responses and/or further responses to the Subject Discovery, depending upon whether it was part of the first or second set of discovery that was served, before these Motions here were filed and that Plaintiff failed to do so.

($350/hr x 1 hr), for a total of $2,100.

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

TENTATIVE RULING

 

STACEY CARR-SUSOR, et al.

                          

         vs.

 

BARRINGTON PACIFIC LLC, et al.

 

 

 Case No.:  22STCV23799

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 3, 2024

 

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel further discovery is DENIED.

 

            On 7/22/2022, Plaintiffs Stacey Carr-Susor, Chi Cho, Deanne Turner, and Jean Mejia filed suit against Barrington Pacific, LLC, alleging: (1) violations of the Reporting Act; and (2) unfair business practices.

 

            On 5/14/2024, Defendant moved to compel further responses from Plaintiffs to its Requests for Production (RFPs) and Special Interrogatories.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that good cause exists to compel Plaintiffs to provide further responses to their RFPs and Special Interrogatories which pertain to their affirmative defense.

 

Cal. Civ. Code section 1786 et seq., which includes section 1786.50(c), provides parties “shall not be liable to a consumer who is the subject of the report where the failure to comply results in a more favorable investigative consumer report than if there had not been a failure to comply.”

 

            Here, Defendant argues that this discovery is based on an expert consultant’s guidance as to what evidence is necessary to establish this affirmative defense.

 

            After review, the Court finds no good cause exists to grant the motion.

 

            As contended by Plaintiff, Defendant’s motion turns on an overstatement of the meaning and purpose Investigative Consumer Reporting Agencies Act (ICRAA). There is no indication that the statute is prospective, or that it is not meant to impose liability when a consumer is not subject to any adverse decision.

 

            Rather, the purpose of ICRAA was to protect consumer privacy and prevent identity theft, and to automatically provide consumers with copies of any investigative consumer reports made of them.

 

            As such, Defendant has not sufficiently supported its position that if Plaintiff’s credit score increased, Defendant is not liable. As noted by Plaintiffs, “Defendant ignores the actual situation the language describes: one in which the report is more favorable than it would otherwise have been, such as a report which erroneously omits felony convictions, for example.” (Opp., 7: 11-12.)

 

            Here, the discovery sought here—credit reports of the Plaintiffs and copies of statements from Netflix, Disney Channel, Hulu, HBO, AT&T, Spectrum, Verizon, and all other public utility accounts including power, gas, cable, pay TV/Satellite TV, landline phone, cellular phone service, water, waste/sanitation service, and Internet service—would speak to the impact of the consumer report on their credit report prospectively speaking, but would not tend to show one way or the other whether or not there was a violation of ICRAA. Moreover, the question of whether or not the report resulted in a more favorable investigative consumer report is determined at the time the report was generated, and does not depend on the future generative result of that report. Defendant’s legal authority failed to persuasively establish otherwise.

             

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel further discovery is denied.

 

 

It is so ordered.

 Case No.:  23STCP00500  

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 3, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion for an order directing compliance is GRANTED.

 

On 2/21/2023, Walter Pearce (Plaintiff) filed suit against Gardena Retirement Center, Inc. dba Gardena Retirement Center (Defendant), alleging: (1) dependent adult abuse/neglect; and (2) negligence.

 

            On 5/16/2024, Defendant moved for an order directing Plaintiff’s compliance with a subpoena.

 

Discussion 

 

            Defendant argues that an order is necessary to produce Plaintiff’s relevant phone records because while Plaintiff has complied by signing an Authorization of Release, T-Mobile will not produce the records absent a Court order.

 

            In response, Plaintiff filed a limited opposition, arguing that the motion should be granted but “any cell phone records involving anyone besides Mr. Pearce’s daughter and facility fails to meet the relevancy standard under Code Civ. Proc. §2017.010.” (3, 26-27.)

 

            As such, Plaintiff requests that his counsel receive and have a right to review and redact the subpoenaed cell-records. The information to be redacted shall include any information revealing Mr. Pearce’s contacts with anyone besides his daughter and facility staff including the names and contact information of any other third-party and the contents of those calls.

 

            In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff should be permitted to investigate the circumstances surrounding the call between him and his daughter by obtaining phone records that would identify other calls and the duration of other such calls, before and after any call(s) with plaintiff’s daughter. This is because Plaintiff has claimed that during the day of the records at issue he was in a bathtub and used his phone to communicate with his daughter and others. As such, Plaintiff has opened up for analysis the context of such communications and the duration of communications. Moreover, given that there is little evidence to objectively verify the amount of time that plaintiff was in the bathtub on the date that he suffered burn injuries, these calls function as circumstantial evidence towards the timeline of events.

 

            The Court is persuaded that Plaintiff’s calls on that day with individuals beyond his daughter are relevant. 

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for an order directing compliance is granted.

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

TRESSIA MITCHELL

 

         vs.

 

AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV05560 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: July 3, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. 

 

            On 3/14/2023, Plaintiff Tressia Mitchell (Plaintiff) filed suit against American Honda Motor Co. Inc. (Defendant), alleging: (1) breach of express warranty; and (2) concealment.

 

            Now, Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate her Complaint and stay the action pending the completion of arbitration proceedings.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where the Court has determined that an agreement to arbitrate a controversy exists, the Court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy …unless it determines that…  grounds exist for rescission of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) Among the grounds which can support rescission are fraud, duress, and unconscionability. (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 239.) The Court may also decline to compel arbitration wherein there is possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 (c).)

 

Discussion

 

The party moving to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving [the] existence [of an arbitration agreement] by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) The moving party also bears the burden of demonstrating that the claims fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

 

A.    Existing Agreement

 

Defendant submitted evidence that on 9/28/2021, Plaintiff entered into a purchase contract (RISC) for a new 2021 Honda CR-V (Subject Vehicle). The Purchase Agreement reads “ARBITRATION PROVISION” and “PLEASE REVIEW – IMPORTANT- AFFECTS YOUR LEGAL RIGHTS”).” (Zandi Decl., Exh. A.) 

 

The arbitration provision provides:

 

1.      EITHER YOU OR WE MAY CHOOSE TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN US DECIDED BY ARBITRATION AND NOT IN COURT OR BY JURY TRIAL.

(Ibid.)

 

The arbitration provision also states broadly that any claim arising out of the Sales Contract or any resulting relationship with “third parties who do not sign this contract” could be resolved by binding arbitration:

 

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.

 

(Ibid, emphasis added.)

 

            Defendant submitted evidence that Plaintiff’s claim arises out of the purchase and condition and the vehicle and thus falls within the scope of the arbitration agreement.

 

            Accordingly, the Court concludes that the preponderance of evidence indicates the existence of an arbitration agreement that covers Plaintiff’s claim. Plaintiff did not oppose this motion and thus has not set forth any basis for not enforcing the agreement.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is granted. This matter is ordered stayed pending the completion of arbitration proceedings.

 

 

 

It is so ordered.
