DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV46503    Hearing Date: January 24, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

curtis r. olson,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

vidala aaronoff, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV46503

Hearing Date:

January 24, 2024

Hearing Time:

10:00 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR STATUTORY APPELLATE ATTORNEY’S FEES UNDER C.C.P. § 425.16, SUBD. (c)(1)

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Curtis R. Olson (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on December 23, 2019 against a number of defendants, including Vidala Aaronoff, Gloria P. Martinez-Senftner, and the Martinez Law Group, P.C.

Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on November 17, 2020, alleging ten causes of action.

On August 29, 2023, Jane Doe filed a Cross-Complaint in this action against, inter alia, Plaintiff. The Cross-Complaint alleges nine causes of action.

On July 23, 2021, the Court issued an Order concerning Defendants Vidala Aaronoff, Gloria Martinez-Senftner, and Martinez Law Group, P.C.’s (collectively “Defendants”) special motion to strike Plaintiff’s SAC. The Court’s July 23, 2021 minute order provides, inter alia, that “the anti-SLAPP motions to the initial Complaint and the FAC are denied as moot. Accordingly, the Court only considers the anti-SLAPP motion to the SAC.” (July 23, 2021 minute order at p. 4.) The July 23, 2021 minute order further provides that “Defendants’ special motion to strike is denied. Defendants have 30 days from the date of this Order to answer the SAC.” (Id. at p. 8.)

Plaintiff indicates that “Defendants appealed the denial of their anti-SLAPP motions to the Second District Court of Appeal…in appellate case no. B315237.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 8.) On February 24, 2023, the Court of Appeal, Second Appellate District, Division Two issued an opinion in Case No. B315237. (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C.) The Court of Appeal’s February 24, 2023 opinion provides, inter alia, that “Aaronoff, Martinez-Senftner, and MLG filed a special motion to strike Olson’s suit under the anti-SLAPP…statute…which the trial court denied. In challenging that order, they point to two constitutionally protected activities they contend are endangered by Olson’s suit: (1) litigation funding and (2) religious expression. Because Aaronoff, Martinez-Senftner, and MLG have not made the requisite showing for striking a suit under the anti-SLAPP statute, we affirm the order.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, pp. 2-3.) On June 1, 2023, a Remittitur was filed in this action providing, inter alia, that “the attached is a true and correct copy of the original order, opinion or decision entered in the above-entitled cause on February 24, 2023 and that this order, opinion or decision has now become final.”

Plaintiff indicates that Defendants sought review by the California Supreme Court. (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. D.) On May 31, 2023, the California Supreme Court denied the petition for review. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff now moves “for a statutory award of attorneys’ fees as the prevailing plaintiff who successfully prevailed against the appeals by Defendants Vidala Aaronoff, Gloria Martinez-Senftner, and the Martinez Law Group, P.C…” The motion is unopposed.

Discussion

In the memorandum of points and authorities in support of the motion, Plaintiff states that he “seeks to recover not less than $64,941.50 in reasonable fees he incurred defending against Defendants’ appeals from the denials of their…anti-SLAPP motions, not less than $7,126.50 in reasonable fees preparing and defending this fee motion, for a total award of $72,068.” (Mot. at pp. 5:27-6:2). Plaintiff asserts that he “should be awarded his appellate attorney’s fees as the prevailing plaintiff-respondent under section 425.16, subdivision (c)(1).” (Mot. at p. 10:19-20.)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (c)(1), “[i]f the court finds that a special motion to strike is frivolous or is solely intended to cause unnecessary delay, the court shall award costs and reasonable attorney’s fees to a plaintiff prevailing on the motion, pursuant to Section 128.5.” (Emphasis added.) “The imposition of sanctions for a frivolous anti-SLAPP motion is therefore mandatory.” ((Foundation for Taxpayer & Consumer Rights v. Garamendi (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1375, 1388.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, subdivision (a) provides, “[a] trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, subdivision (b)(2) defines “frivolous” as “totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.” In addition, “[a] motion is totally and completely without merit for purposes of a finding of frivolousness under section 425.16, subdivision (c)(1) or section 128.5 only if any reasonable attorney would agree that the motion is totally devoid of merit. This is an objective standard. Whether the sole purpose of the motion is to harass an opposing party or the motion is solely intended to cause unnecessary delay, in contrast, concerns the subjective motivation of the moving defendant. The moving defendant’s subjective motivation can be inferred from the absence of any arguable merit.” ((Chitsazzadeh v. Kramer & Kaslow (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 676, 683–684 [internal citations omitted].)

Plaintiff cites to City of Alhambra v. D'Ausilio (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1301, 1303-1304, where “Appellant Robert D’Ausilio sued respondent City of Alhambra (City) for civil rights violations and then settled the case with the City. The City then sued appellant for breach of the settlement agreement and declaratory relief. Appellant moved to strike the declaratory relief claim under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, the ‘anti-SLAPP’ statute…The trial court denied the motion and awarded the City its attorney fees.” The Court of Appeal “agree[d] that appellant failed to demonstrate that the declaratory relief claim arose from activity protected under the statute,” and therefore “affirm[ed] the order denying the motion.” (Id. at p. 1304.) the Court of Appeal also “grant[ed] the City’s request for attorney fees on appeal, the amount of which [was] to be determined by the trial court.” The City of Alhambra Court noted as follows:

 

“The City requests attorney fees on appeal. It argues that because appellant’s appeal ‘raises no new permissible arguments that change the result, his appeal [like his motion] is frivolous and was only intended to cause further delay of this litigation.’ We agree. ‘The right to attorney fees extends to attorney fees on appeal as well.’ (Morrow v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1446 [57 Cal. Rptr. 3d 885];…see also Carpenter v. Jack in the Box Corp. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 454, 461 [59 Cal. Rptr. 3d 839] [‘The trial court’s authority to award fees and costs under section 425.16, subdivision (c), includes authority to award fees incurred in responding to an appeal of an order granting or denying a special motion to strike, or of an order awarding attorney fees in connection with such motion.’].) The City is awarded its attorney fees on appeal.” (City of Alhambra v. D'Ausilio, supra, at pages 1309-1310, emphasis added.)

Plaintiff asserts that “[h]ere, this Court already determined that Defendants’ special motions to strike were frivolous, and it accordingly awarded costs and reasonable attorney’s fees to Olson after he successfully defeated their motions.” (Mot. at p. 11:20-22.)

On April 28, 2022, the Court issued an Order on a previous motion by Plaintiff for statutory attorney’s fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. As set forth in the April 28, 2022 Order, the Court continued the hearing on the motion to May 17, 2022, finding that a supplemental declaration from Plaintiff’s counsel was necessary. On May 17, 2022, the Court issued an Order granting Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees in the amount of $41,697.00. The Court’s May 17, 2022 Order provides, inter alia, that “[a]s discussed in the Court’s April 28, 2022 Order, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendants’ anti-SLAPP motion was frivolous for purposes of Code of Civil Procedure section ¿425.16, subdivision (c)(1)¿.” (May 17, 2022 Order at p. 2:13-15.)

Plaintiff asserts that “Defendants’ appeals [sic] were as frivolous as their special motions to strike, and solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (Mot. at p. 12:4-5.)

The Court of Appeal’s February 24, 2023 opinion affirming the Court’s order denying Defendants’ special motion to strike provides, inter alia, that “[t]he theory behind defendants’ anti-SLAPP motion is that Olson is, in essence, challenging their payment of legal fees to prevent defendants from engaging in further litigation against him. Specifically, defendants argue, without supporting citations to the record, that because the $800,000 secured by the deed of trust was ‘necessary to maintain their current actions and appeals and to perfect new actions against Olson,’ the transfer of the condominium to Martinez-Senftner and MLG is protected as litigation funding. ‘Gathering the money was inseparable from Aaronoff and [the] ATW Trust’ for existing and future litigation, they argue. Defendants’ thinking is flawed for two reasons: First, the $800,000 payment secured by the conveyance did not constitute litigation funding. Second, Olson’s claims do not arise from the payment.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, p. 10.) Plaintiff notes that the Court of Appeal further found that “[h]ere, contrary to defendants’ unsubstantiated arguments in their briefs, the purported funding was not obtained for the purpose of litigation.” (Id. at p. 12.)

The Court of Appeal also noted in its February 24, 2023 opinion that “Defendants contend Olson’s claims arise from protected activity because the Ancient Temple of Wings is a religious entity. They do not, however, coherently describe the basis for this theory.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C, p. 13.) The February 24, 2023 opinion also provides that “[i]n our decision in Aaronoff v. Olson, supra, B295388, a related case, we concluded the trial court exceeded its jurisdiction by amending an earlier order awarding attorney fees to Olson after Aaronoff had timely appealed from that order.” (Id. at p. 15.) Plaintiff notes that the Court of Appeal found that “[t]o the extent they can be understood, defendants’ contentions are that our B295388 decision means Olson cannot name the ATW Trust as a defendant in his second amended complaint and, without the Trust, Olson cannot allege a conspiracy theory. Defendants’ position fails for two reasons. To begin, defendants hold a marked misperception of our B295388 decision and the law…” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff thus asserts, as set forth above, that “Defendants’ appeals [sic] were as frivolous as their special motions to strike…” (Mot. at p. 12:4-5.) Based on the foregoing, and in light of the lack of any opposition by Defendants, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendants’ subject appeal was frivolous for purposes of Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (c)(1).

Next, “[i]t is well established that the amount of an attorney fee award under the anti-SLAPP statute is computed by the trial court in accordance with the familiar lodestar method.” ((569 East County Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc. (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 426, 432 [internal quotations and brackets omitted].) Under the lodestar method, the court tabulates the attorney fee lodestar by multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by the reasonable hourly rate prevailing in the community for similar work.” (Ibid.)

With regard to the number of hours reasonably expended, “the verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.” ((Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.) In determining the reasonable hourly rate, the “burden is on the successful party to prove the appropriate market rate to be used in calculating the lodestar.” ((MBNA America Bank, N.A. v. Gorman (2006) 147 Cal.App.4th Supp. 1, 13.) The trial court may reduce the award where the fee request appears unreasonably inflated, such as where the attorneys’ efforts are unorganized or duplicative. ((Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635, fn. 21.)

In his supporting declaration, Plaintiff’s counsel states that his hourly rate “during the relevant course of events leading up to and including filing this declaration was $510-600/hour.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s requested hourly rate for associate Nina Moreno is $445/hour, and Plaintiff’s requested hourly rate for “of counsel” Robert M. Dato is $550/hour. (Kennedy Decl., ¶¶ 13-14.) The Court finds that the requested hourly rates are reasonable. “The courts repeatedly have stated that the trial court is in the best position to value the services rendered by the attorneys in his or her courtroom, and this includes the determination of the hourly rate that will be used in the lodestar calculus.” ((569 East County Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc., supra, 6 Cal.App.5th at pp. 436-437 [internal citations omitted].) “In making its calculation, the court may rely on its own knowledge and familiarity with the legal market, as well as the experience, skill, and reputation of the attorney requesting fees…” ((Id. at p. 437.)

However, the Court notes that it is unclear what the requested hourly rate is for attorney Robert Little. Plaintiff’s counsel states that “Mr. Little has an hourly rate of $845, which has been discounted in this matter to $590-$625.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 12.) However, Plaintiff’s counsel then states that “Mr. Little’s current discounted rate is $680.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 18.)

Plaintiff’s counsel also states that “in connection with Defendants’ frivolous anti-SLAPP motions, Olson has incurred at least about [sic] $64,941.50 in fees for at least 108.1 hours of combined attorney time successfully defending against her [sic] multiple (three) anti-SLAPP motions and defeating them. This required significant analysis and opposition briefing involving a protracted and unwieldy procedural history, supplemental briefing caused by Defendants, and preparing for and attending multiple court hearings.” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 17.) But the instant motion concerns fees sought by Plaintiff for “defending against Defendants’ appeals [sic] from the denials of their frivolous anti-SLAPP motions” as well as fees for “preparing and defending [the instant] fee motion.” (Mot. at p. 5:28-6:1.) In light of paragraph 17 of Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration, it is unclear whether Plaintiff purportedly incurred $64,941.50 in connection with Plaintiff’s defense of Defendants’ appeal, or in connection with other matters that are not the subject of the instant motion.

Accordingly, the Court will require a supplemental declaration from Plaintiff’s counsel more clearly setting forth the fees incurred by Plaintiff in responding to Defendant’s appeal. In addition, the Court will require additional information concerning Mr. Little’s requested hourly rate.

 Plaintiff also estimates he will incur an additional $7,126.50 in connection with the instant motion, which includes “7.8 hours of Nina Moreno’s time drafting this motion; 1.3 hours of Robert C. Little’s time reviewing and revising the motion; plus an anticipated 4.7 hours of Nina Moreno’s time reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply brief in support of the motion; and 1 hour of Robert C. Little’s time appearing at the hearing…” (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 18.) As set forth above, it is unclear what Mr. Little’s requested hourly rate is. (Compare Kennedy Decl., ¶¶ 12, 18.) In addition, no opposition or reply¿was¿filed in connection with the motion. Thus, the Court finds that the requested “4.7 hours of Nina Moreno’s time reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply brief in support of the motion” shall be deducted from the total amount requested. (Kennedy Decl., ¶ 18.)
