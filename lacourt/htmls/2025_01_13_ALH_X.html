#20 - Vangelisti, et al. v. City of Pasadena, et al.

MOTION TO COMPEL DISCOVERY RESPONSES AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

On October 28, 2024, Plaintiff Paul Vangelisti filed a Motion to Compel Defendant DM Contracting Inc. to provide responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Inspection Demands.  Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant for the costs incurred in bringing this motion.  Defendant opposes, asserting that the motion is moot because responses were served on November 7, 2024, and further argues that Plaintiff’s motion was filed needlessly and in bad faith.  Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel for unnecessary litigation.


Background

On November 28, 2022, Plaintiff fell on a sidewalk owned by the City of Pasadena.  DM Contracting Inc. contracted with the City of Pasadena (“City”) and completed sidewalk repairs sometime between 2018 and 2019.  The City accepted that work, and the subject sidewalk may have uplifted again because of tree root growth.  Plaintiff and his wife settled with the defendant city and the defendant residential owner fronting the subject sidewalk.  DM Contracting argues this is a frivolous lawsuit aimed at pressuring money from a non-fault party.

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel is DENIED as moot.  Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.  Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel is Moot

A propounding party may move for an order compelling responses to interrogatories at any time “[i]f a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).)  “The party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

Within 30 days after service of a demand for inspection, testing, or sampling, the party to whom the demand is made shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).)  In responding to a request for production of documents, the responding party shall provide a statement of compliance; a statement of inability to comply in part or in whole; or an objection.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210.)

On July 22, 2024, Plaintiff served discovery requests – form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and inspection demands – on Defendant.  (Steiner Decl., ¶ 6.)  Responses were due no later than August 26, 2024.  (Motion, p. 1.)  At the time, Defendant was represented by prior counsel, Matthew Tesfai, who failed to provide responses.  (Steiner Decl., ¶ 7.)  On September 6, 2024, Defendant substituted new counsel, Daniel Ip of Litchfield Cavo, LLP. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 8.)  Defendant’s new counsel requested an extension to respond to the discovery, citing delays in receiving the case file from prior counsel.  Plaintiff granted an extension until October 4, 2024.  (Steiner Decl., 10.)  However, Defendant did not serve responses by that date.  On October 7, 2024, Defendant’s counsel requested an additional extension until November 7, 2024, to serve responses.  (Ip Decl., ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff did not confirm or reject this request and filed the instant motion to compel on October 28, 2024.  (Ip Decl., ¶ 8.)

Defendant ultimately served verified, objection-free responses on November 7, 2024, one day before Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter on November 8, 2024.  (See Ip Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.)  Despite receiving the responses, Plaintiff refused to take the motion off calendar (Ip Decl., ¶ 10-11), asserting that the responses were untimely and insufficient.  (Reply, p. 1.)

Plaintiff’s motion to compel is moot.  Defendant served verified, objection-free responses to the discovery requests on November 7, 2024, prior to the hearing on this motion, albeit after its filing.  While Plaintiff argues that the responses were untimely, the filing of a motion to compel does not automatically entitle the moving party to relief if the requested discovery has already been provided.

The Mutual Requests for Sanctions are Denied

The Court shall impose monetary sanctions against any party or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless it finds that the party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (h).)

Plaintiff seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant for the costs incurred in bringing this motion.  The Court notes that Defendant’s delay in responding to discovery was caused, in part, by the substitution of counsel and the transfer of the case file.  Defendant’s new counsel requested extensions and ultimately served responses within a reasonable time.  Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied.

Defendant also requests sanctions against Plaintiff’s counsel for filing a motion to compel in bad faith.  Defendant’s request is denied.

#27 - Hernandez v. Young, et al.

MOTION TO STRIKE PUNITIVE DAMAGES

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint which refer to punitive damages.

Tentative Ruling

Defendants’ Motion to Strike the identified portions of Plaintiffs’ FAC is GRANTED.  Plaintiffs are granted 10 days leave to amend.

On its own motion, the trial is advanced to this date and continued to Thursday, August 12, 2025 at 9:00 a.m. in Department X of the Alhambra Courthouse.

The Final Status Conference is also advanced to this date and continued to Tuesday, July 31, 2025 at 10:00 a.m. in Department X of the Alhambra Courthouse.

The Court sets an Order to Show Cause re: Amended Complaint and Responsive Pleading for March 26, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. in Department X of the Alhambra Courthouse.

Legal Authority and Analysis

On a motion to strike made under Code of Civil Procedure section 435, the Court may strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter in a pleading, on terms deemed proper.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).)  A motion to strike may be used to remove a claim for punitive damages that is not adequately supported by the facts alleged in the complaint.  (Cryolife, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1145; Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. v. Superior Court (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 696.)

In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  Per Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  As set forth in the Civil Code,

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.  (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. 

(Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1)-(3), emphasis added.) 

Further, a plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.  To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.  (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)  “When nondeliberate injury is charged, allegations that the defendant’s conduct was wrongful, willful, wanton, reckless or unlawful do not support a claim for exemplary damages; such allegations do not charge malice.”  (G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29.)  Punitive damages are not ordinarily recoverable in actions for negligence.  (Ibid.)  Punitive damages are only recoverable if the plaintiff establishes oppression, fraud, or malice, as defined by Civil Code section 3294(c).

Here, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have failed to allege sufficient facts to support their claims for punitive damages, asserting that the allegations are conclusory and lack the requisite showing of malice, oppression, or fraud.  Plaintiffs oppose the motion, contending that their FAC sufficiently pleads facts to support punitive damages and, alternatively, request leave to amend should the Court find deficiencies in their pleading.

The FAC alleges Defendants failed to maintain the subject property in a habitable condition, citing numerous health and safety violations, including severe rodent and cockroach infestations, lack of plumbing/electrical facilities in good working order, and foundation/structural damage.  (FAC, ¶ 14.)  It further alleges Defendants acted with a willful and conscious disregard for their rights and safety (FAC, ¶ 36), characterizing Defendants’ actions or inactions under the nuisance claim as intentional and unreasonable (FAC, ¶ 73).

The motion argues that Plaintiffs’ allegations are conclusory and fail to demonstrate the requisite intent to harm or conscious disregard for Plaintiffs’ rights.  Defendants contend that the FAC merely describes negligent maintenance of the property, so the allegations do not rise to the level of malice required for punitive damages.  Defendants further assert that because Plaintiffs had two opportunities to allege sufficient facts and failed, the motion should be granted with prejudice.

In opposition, Plaintiffs counter that their FAC sufficiently alleges facts to support punitive damages, pointing to the egregious nature of the uninhabitable conditions and Defendants’ prolonged failure to address them despite repeated complaints.  Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ conduct constitutes despicable behavior carried out with a willful and conscious disregard for their rights.  Plaintiffs refer to cases where punitive damages were awarded in breach of warranty of habitability cases where malice or oppression were shown.

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ allegations are insufficient to support punitive damages.  The FAC lacks specific factual allegations demonstrating that Defendants acted with the intent to harm Plaintiffs or with a conscious disregard for their rights.  The conditions described in the FAC support uninhabitability, but they primarily suggest negligence rather than the malice, oppression, or fraud required under Civil Code section 3294.

For the foregoing reasons, the Court grants Defendants’ Motion to Strike the portions of Plaintiffs’ FAC in the entirety.

#28 – Herrera v. Davtyan

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

Counsel for Plaintiff moves to be relieved as counsel, on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in communication.

Tentative Ruling

Attorney Oscar Richard Swinton’s motion to be relieved as counsel is continued to February 19, 2025.  Swinton must comply with Rule 3.1362(d) and provide proof of service upon Defendant’s counsel regarding the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order consistent with the notice requirements for bringing the instant motion.

Legal Authority and Analysis

“The question of granting or denying an application of an attorney to withdraw as counsel (Code Civ. Proc., § 284, subd. (2)) is one which lies within the sound discretion of the trial court ‘having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.’  [Citation.]”  (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406 [internal quotations omitted].)  The Court should also consider whether the attorney’s “withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client’s interests.”  (Ramirez v. Sturdivant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)).  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (d), (e).)

Counsel for Plaintiff filed a motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) and California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362.  A declaration on Judicial Council Form MC-052 accompanies the motion, as required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, stating: 

“Unfortunately, there is irreparable breakdown of communication with Plaintiff.  Lamentably, Plaintiff has on various occasions has failed to listen, cooperate or even to follow directions.  Thus, this lack of cooperation significantly hinders my ability to effectively represent Plaintiff.  Therefore, I am respectfully requesting to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff.” 

Under California Rules of Professional Conduct, 1.16(b)(4), “a lawyer may withdraw from representing a client if: . . . the client . . . renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively[.]”  Counsel Swinton’s declaration regarding the breakdown of communication and Plaintiff’s lack of cooperation meets this standard.

However, Swinton only partially complies with the mandatory requirements of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  He has submitted the required notice on Form MC-051, declaration on Form MC-052, proposed order on Form MC-053, and service on Plaintiff, but he has not shown service “on all other parties who have appeared in the case.”

For this reason, the motion to be relieved as counsel is continued to allow Counsel Swinton to file supplemental documents which establish proof of service on Defendant’s counsel.
