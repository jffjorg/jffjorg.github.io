DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV00960    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: W

CATHERINE ROCHERON, et al. v. MOSHE GABAY, et al.

 

defendantS MOSHE AND ORIT GABAY’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        October 17, 2024                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21VECV00960

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Moshe Gabay and Orit Gabay

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs James Cruce and Robert Borsuk

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs are each owners of lots within Tract 13564, located in Sherman Oaks, California. Plaintiffs allege each acquired their properties in reliance on the restrictions contained in a recorded Declaration of Restrictions governing Tract 13564, which include that all garages be "detached." On January 13, 2021, defendants Moshe and Orit Gabay purchased a lot within Tract 13564 located at 4512 Sunnyslope. Plaintiffs further allege violated the restrictive covenant by obtaining a permit with the intention to mansionize the property, construct a second story, and/or eliminate the detached garage on the property.

 

On July 22, 2021, Plaintiffs Catherine Rocheron, Guillaume Rocheron, James Cruce, Ruth Holzman, and Robert Borsuk filed a complaint against Defendants Moshe Gabay and Orit Gabay asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Recorded Declaration of Restrictions; (2) Injunctive Relief; and (3) Declaratory Relief.

 

The instant action is related to Eileen Duffy v. Eliran Levi (23VECV02343) and Ivor Brogger, et al. v. Michael Shabtai, et al. (21VECV01006).

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Moshe Gabay and Orit Gabay’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of Exhibits A through J. Defendants object to the request for judicial notice of certain excerpts of Exhibits E, F, and G on the grounds the exhibits are irrelevant to interpretation of the restrictive covenant. The court OVERRULES the objections and GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice. 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs submit objections to the declaration of Yossi Noudel and Moshe Gabay.

 

The court OVERRULES objections nos. 1 and 2.

 

In reply, Defendants submit objections to the declarations of Mark Savel, Tyle Hamil, Robert Borsuk, James Cruce, Robert Jones, and Thomas M. Ware II.

 

The court SUSTAINS the objection to Mr. Savel’s and Mr. Hamil’s declaration in its entirety. (See In re Tobacco Cases I (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 42, 51.) The court also SUSTAINS the objections to Mr. Borsuk, Mr. Jones’ and Mr. Cruce’s declarations.

 

The court OVERRULES the objections to Mr. Ware’s declaration.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants move for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication on the grounds the only reasonable interpretation of the restrictive covenant at issue permits the use to which Defendants intended to put the Subject Property.

 

A party may move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (CCP § 437c(a).) “[I]f all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law,” the moving party will be entitled to summary judgment.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) A motion for summary adjudication may be made by itself or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment. (CCP § 437c(f)(2).)    

 

The moving party bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact, and if the party does so, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850; accord CCP § 437c(p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi, supra, 0159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also CCP § 437c(c).)

 

Timeliness

 

The court briefly notes Plaintiff objects to the motion on the grounds Defendants failed to schedule the hearing for the motion on summary judgment no later than 30 days before trial. The motion is currently set for October 17, 2024 and trial is set for November 12, 2024. The court agrees Defendants failed to secure a hearing at least 30 days ahead of trial.

 

However, the court may allow the hearing within 30 days of trial upon a finding of good cause. (CCP §437c(a)(3); Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1268.) While generally the moving party should seek a determination of good cause prior to filing their motion for summary judgment, “the appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on its merits is a waiver of any defects or irregularities in the notice of motion.” (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697; see also Beroiz v. Wahl (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 485, fn. 7.) Here, Defendants have demonstrated good cause to hear this action within 30 days of trial as October 17, 2024 was the earliest they could reserve a hearing on the motion for summary judgment and the interpretation of the restrictive covenant is dispositive of this action. Plaintiffs also were able to fully brief their opposition.  As such, the court will address the rest of the motion for summary judgment on the merits.

 

Unambiguous Meaning

 

Defendants first argue the restrictive covenant is clear and unambiguous. It mandates that “[t]he lots in said tract shall be used for no purpose other than the erection and construction of a detached, single family dwelling and private garage […].” The restrictive covenant does not specify the location of the garage nor does it say the garage must be detached.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the placement of a comma after the word “detached” demonstrates that the word “detached” is intended to modify both the “single family dwelling” and the “garage.” There would have been no need to add the word “detached” if it was only intended to modify the phrase “single family dwelling.” Plaintiffs point to Exhibit E, the 1945 Municipal Code, which provides a single family dwelling as “a detached dwelling containing only one dwelling unit” and a “dwelling unit” was further defined as “a group of two or more rooms, one of which is a kitchen, designed for occupancy by one family for living and sleeping purposes.” Moreover, Section A1 goes on to describe the “single family dwelling and private garage” in the plural rather than as one unit, i.e., “which structures shall not exceed one and one-half stories in height . . . .” Therefore, the reference to the “single family dwelling and private garage” as “structures” connotes an intent that they each would be separate structures, i.e., “detached” from one another.

 

The court disagrees with Plaintiffs’ interpretation. In contract interpretation disputes, the analysis is two-fold.¿¿First, the question of ambiguity is a question of law.¿¿(De Guere v. Universal City Studios¿(1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 482, 501.)¿¿To determine ambiguity, courts consider the face of the contract and any extrinsic evidence that supports a reasonable interpretation.¿¿(Pacific Gas & E. v. G.W. Thomas Drayage Etc. Co.¿(1968) 69 Cal.2d 33, 37, 39-40.)¿¿If the court determines that a contract is unambiguous, the analysis is complete.¿¿If the court determines that a contract is ambiguous, a party is entitled to introduce extrinsic evidence to aid the interpretation of the contract.¿¿(Appleton v. Waessil (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 551, 554-555;¿Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. Zuckerman¿(1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1113, 1140-1141.)

 

In the instant matter, the court interprets “detached” as primarily modifying ‘single family dwelling’, presumably to prevent the construction of duplexes or multi-family dwellings. If the developers truly intended for garages to be ‘detached’, they would have put the modifier immediately prior to the word “garage,” to clearly indicate that it was the garage that had to be detached. Furthermore, as noted by Plaintiff, the Los Angeles Municipal Code defined “one family dwelling” as “a detached dwelling containing only one dwelling unit” and a “dwelling unit” defined as “a group of two or more rooms, one of which is a kitchen, designed for occupancy by one family for living and sleeping purposes.” This definition does not preclude single-family homes from including an attached garage as part of their dwelling unit.

 

Even considering Plaintiffs’ extrinsic evidence that nearly three years after defendants commenced construction of an attached garage, only 18 (including the Gabay Property) of the 101 homes have attached garages, the court finds this does not aid in its interpretation. The fact that nearly 20% of the homes in the tract have attached homes does not weigh in Plaintiff’s favor. Unlike Starlight Ridge South Homeowners Assn. v. Hunter-Bloor (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 440, 448 where the court found the homeowner produced no evidence that the association ever inconsistently enforced the CC&R provisions, there has been inconsistencies in the enforcement of Plaintiff’s interpretation of the restrictive covenant. 

 

Lastly, even if the reading was ambiguous, which the court finds it is not, “restrictive covenants are construed strictly against the person seeking to enforce them, and any doubt will be resolved in favor of the free use of land.” (Zabrucky v. McAdams (2005) 129 Cal. App. 4th 618, 622.) As such, the interpretation of the restrictions finds in favor of Defendants.

 

Accordingly, the court finds there is no triable issue of material fact and GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary judgment. The court will briefly address Defendants’ remaining arguments.

 

Presumption of Declaration of Restrictions

 

Defendants argue it is Plaintiffs’ burden to provide evidence sufficient to show the declaration of restrictions was duly recorded and indexed, and not Defendants’ burden to disprove recordation.

 

Plaintiff argues Civil Code section 1600 actually establishes the presumption that the Declaration is valid.

 

The Declaration was duly recorded and reflected in the chain of title for the Tract and, correspondingly, Defendants’ lot. Mr. Borsuk obtained a preliminary title report in 1978, which identified the subject Declaration. (Borsuk Decl. ¶ 3-6; Exhs. 5, 6.) The Title Report also contains the Declaration at issue. (RJN, Exh. H.)  The court finds this sufficient to establish that the declaration of restrictions was duly recorded. 

 

Race Restrictions

 

Defendants argue the Declaration contains a provision prohibiting persons “of any race other than the Caucasian,” other than domestic servants, to use or occupy the

affected properties. Whether or not racial, religious, or other prejudice is the actual motivation behind this action, the Declaration is repugnant, against public policy, and should be denied enforcement on this ground alone.

 

While repugnant, the racial restriction is severable and void but does not invalidate other use restrictions contained in the same instrument. (See 34 Am. Jur. Proof of Facts 3d 339; and Government Code Section 12956.1.)

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV03123    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: W

American Express National Bank v. Balyan

 

defendant balyan’s motion to set aside the default judgment

 

Date of Hearing:        October 17, 2024                  Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV03123 

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Grigor Balyan    

Responding Party:     No opposition   

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff American Express National Bank alleges Defendant Grigor Balyan became indebted to Plaintiff on an American Express credit card in the amount of $5,334.77. On July 20, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant asserting a claim for common counts.

 

Request for entry of default was entered February 22, 2024. Default Judgment was entered February 26, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Balyan’s Motion to Set Aside the Default Judgment is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Balyan moves to set aside the default judgment and default against Balyan on the grounds Defendant was not properly served and therefore, lacked actual notice of the lawsuit and there was excusable neglect.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 permits the court to vacate a default when service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him. (CCP § 473.5(a).) The phrase actual notice means genuine knowledge and does not include constructive or imputed notice to the client. (Tunis v. Barrow (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 1069, 1077.) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) allows a court to vacate a prior order upon a showing that the order was entered due to a party’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Additionally, the motion “shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”

 

“ ‘[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.] Thus, a default judgment entered against a defendant who was not served with a summons in the manner prescribed by statute is void. [Citation.]’ “ (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.) “Under section 473, subdivision (d), the court may set aside a default judgment which is valid on its face, but void, as a matter of law, due to improper service.” (Ellard v. Conway (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 540, 544.) 

 

Defendant Balyan first argues the court should set aside the default and default judgment on the grounds service occurred at a residence at which Defendant no longer resided. As a result, he did not receive actual notice of the lawsuit and did not have an opportunity to defend himself. Moreover, at the time, he was under medical care due to injuries sustained during a robbery and therefore, failed to timely respond to the lawsuit.

 

The court finds grounds to set aside the default and default judgment. Substituted service occurred at a residence where Defendant no longer resides. Therefore, service did not occur at his dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business or usual mailing address and is void on its face. (See CCP §§415.20, 473(d).)

 

Accordingly, Defendant Balyan’s Motion to Set Aside the Default Judgment is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to file and serve his Answer within 10 days.
