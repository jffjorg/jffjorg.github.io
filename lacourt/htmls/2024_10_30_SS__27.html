Case Number: 22STCV17883    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 10/30/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV17883 ANN DEFUSCO vs CITY OF COVINA

Moving Party: Defendant City of Covina

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: DENIED; THE COURT WILL, HOWEVER, DISCUSS A POTENTIAL BRIEF CONTINUANCE IN LIGHT OF THE PENDING MSJ

 

On June 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present trip-and-fall case. Trial is currently set for November 27, 2024. On October 17, 2024, the Court heard Defendant City of Covina’s Ex Parte Application and held that “The parties knew the trial date and failed to attend mediation in a timely manner given that trial date. That is not good cause. … a substitution of counsel is not generally a basis for a continuance -- parties substitute or change counsel subject to the pending dates. Simply, the parties have had more than 2 years to be ready for trial. They have already continued the matter. They should be ready to try the case, as the CCP advises that trial dates are to be treated as ‘firm’.”

The current motion does not present any new arguments beyond those raised in the ex parte application. Both filings are substantively identical, relying on the same rationale for a continuance: a stipulated trial continuance, additional time for mediation, and recent substitution of counsel. These points were already addressed in the Court’s order on Defendant's ex parte application, in which the Court found no good cause. The Court is responsible for ensuring that civil cases conclude within two years (see CRC 2.2). This case, a straightforward slip-and-fall incident, has been pending for over two years. The parties were aware of the trial date and did not pursue mediation promptly in light of that timeline. Furthermore, parties who substitute or change counsel are subject to existing deadlines. The Court does not find good cause to continue the trial; therefore, the motion is denied. 

That all said, the Court will discuss with the parties the Court’s willingness to BRIEFLY continue the matter in light of the fact that moving party’s MSJ is pending decision.

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION IS GRANTED IN PART

 

Background

This action arises from an incident on or about October 21, 2020, in which Plaintiff Moses Kwok allegedly tripped and fell on a curb near the entrance of a Bank of America (“BofA”) location at 300 N. Atlantic Boulevard in Monterey Park, California. Plaintiff filed this lawsuit against defendants, including BofA and Jones Lang LaSalle (“JLL”), seeking damages for his alleged personal injuries. M. Arthur Gensler Jr. & Associates, Inc. (“Gensler”), as the architect responsible for the curb improvements where the Incident occurred, had an agreement with BofA and JLL that included an indemnity provision. In response to Plaintiff’s complaint, BofA and JLL filed cross-complaints against Gensler for implied indemnity, equitable indemnity, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief to enforce the indemnity provision. Gensler now moves the Court for summary adjudication on each cause of action in BofA and JLL’s cross-complaints.

 

Legal Standard 

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. [Citation.] Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.  If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true. [Citation.] Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

 

Evidentiary Ruling 

The Parties did not make any objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)  

 

Discussion

 

Duty to Defend

Standard comprehensive liability insurance policies provide that the insurer must both indemnify and defend the insured against claims within the scope of the policy coverage. The insurer's duty to defend is broader than its duty to indemnify. The latter duty runs only to claims that are actually covered by the policy, while the duty to defend extends to claims that are merely potentially covered. (Crawford v. Wearther Shield Mfg. Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541, 546.) 

"The [insurer's] defense duty is a continuing one, arising on tender of defense and lasting until the underlying lawsuit is concluded [citation], or until it has been shown that there is¿no¿potential for coverage. . . ." (Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Superior Court, (1993) 6 Cal.4th 287, 295.) “An insurer may owe a duty to defend its insured in an action in which no damages ultimately are awarded." (Horace Mann Ins. Co. v. Barbara B. (1993) 4 Cal.4th 1076, 1081.) The insurer’s duty to defendant is not unlimited. (Waller v. Truck Ins. Exchange, Inc. (1995) 11 Cal.4th 1, 19.) It extends beyond claims that are actually covered to those that are merely potentially so — but no further. (Ibid.) “The insurer need not defend if the third party complaint¿can by no conceivable theory raise a single issue¿which could bring it within the policy coverage."¿(Gray v. Zurich Ins. Co. (1996) 65 Cal.2d 263, 276, fn. 15.)  

        "The determination whether the insurer owes a duty to defend usually is made in the first instance by comparing the allegations of the complaint with the terms of the policy. Facts extrinsic to the complaint also give rise to a duty to defend when they reveal a possibility that the claim may be covered by the policy.” (Gray, supra, 65 Cal.2d at 276.) Any doubt as to¿whether the facts establish the existence of the defense duty must be resolved in the insured's favor. (Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Superior Court, (1993) 6 Cal.4th 287, 299.) 

               The duty to indemnify arises immediately upon a proper tender of defense by the indemnitee, and thus before the litigation to be defended has determined whether indemnity is actually owed. This duty, as described in the statute, therefore cannot depend on the outcome of that litigation. The duty to defend encompasses all claims, at the time of tender, that allege facts that would potentially give rise to a duty of indemnity unless the indemnity agreement specifies otherwise. Crawford v. Wearther Shield Mfg. Inc., (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541, 558.

The Parties’ indemnity provision provides as follows:

To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law, Consultant [Gensler] shall defend, indemnify and hold harmless Owner [BofA] and Manager [JLL] and their respective officers, directors, employees, partners, joint ventures, affiliates, successors and assigns from and against any and all claims, demands, losses, expenses, damages, liabilities, fines, and penalties (each a “Claim”) which arise out of, are alleged to arise out of, or relate to the following:

(a) Consultant’s negligent performance of (or failure to perform) the Services;

(b) A breach in a representation, covenant or obligation of this Agreement by Consultant or any of its affiliates, subcontractors, agents or employees;

(c) Any negligent act or omission or willful misconduct by Consultant or its affiliates, subcontractors, agents or employees;

Gensler’s core argument against the cross-complainants’ breach of contract claim is that the indemnity provision requires Gensler to indemnify JLL and BofA only “when there is proof that Gensler negligently performed its services” and since the court found Gensler was not negligent, Gensler therefore has no duty to indemnify the cross-complainants.

However, Gensler’s contention that its duty to defend JLL and BofA arises only “when there is proof that Gensler negligently performed its services” is unsupported by the plain language of the indemnity provision. The provision specifically states that Gensler must “defend, indemnify, and hold harmless” JLL and BofA from claims that “arise out of, are alleged to arise out of, or relate to” three specific situations: (a) Gensler’s negligent performance or failure to perform services, (b) a breach of any representation, covenant, or obligation under the agreement, or (c) any negligent act, omission, or willful misconduct by Gensler or its affiliates. The language of the indemnity clause clearly and plainly states that it covering claims that "are alleged to arise out of" Gensler’s services. This imposes a duty to defend upon Gensler at the point of allegations, not at the point of proven negligence.

Furthermore, the clause’s language, including the phrase “To the fullest extent permitted by applicable law,” reflects standard terms designed to create broad indemnity and defense obligations, consistent with Cal. Civ. Code § 2778 and California law regarding the duty to defend (Crawford v. Weather Shield Mfg. Inc. (2008) 44 Cal.4th 541.) Therefore, Gensler's interpretation of the provision contradicts both the contract's intent and California law, which establishes that the duty to defend extends to all claims that, at the time of tender, allege facts potentially giving rise to a duty of indemnity, unless otherwise specified in the indemnity agreement. (Id. at 558.)

For Gensler to prevail on a motion for summary adjudication on the breach of contract cause of action, Gensler must demonstrate that when BofA tendered its defense, Plaintiff’s claims—that the curb where he fell, designed by Gensler, was a dangerous condition and non-compliant with ADA and other government regulations—were not potentially covered by the indemnity clause. Plaintiff failed to do so in the moving papers.

The duty to defend “is a continuing one, arising on tender of defense and lasting until the underlying lawsuit is concluded, or until it has been shown that there is no potential for coverage…” (Montrose Chemical Corp. v. Superior Court, (1993) 6 Cal.4th 287, 295). Gensler did not present any evidence when Cross Complainants tendered its defense. Although the Court recently granted BofA and JLL’s motions for summary judgment, finding Gensler was not negligent in causing Plaintiff’s injuries, these rulings were issued in August 2024. Until that point, Gensler’s potential negligence remained in question. Defendant needed to demonstrate that, at the time of tender, there was no potential for coverage. However, without indicating when the tender occurred, this burden was not met. Gensler’s MSA as to breach of contract cause of action and declaratory relief cause of action are denied.

In fact, the cross-complainants demonstrated that they tendered their defense of Plaintiff’s claims to Gensler on July 29, 2021 (Declaration of Adam Popkowski ¶ 3, 6, 7), more than a year before Plaintiff filed the case and three years prior to the Court’s finding that Gensler was not negligent. The 2021 tender and subsequent 2023 tenders were rejected by Gensler. (Id.)

Negligence/ Apportionment

Gensler has demonstrated that it was not negligent in designing the curb and ramp where the alleged incident occurred and is, therefore, not liable for apportionment of fault. Gensler introduced evidence showing the following:

·        Gensler was retained by JLL to provide architectural design services for the curb where Plaintiff’s incident took place (Exhibit “2” to JLL’s Cross-Complaint, Exhibit “B” to Evidence).

·        Gensler designed a code-compliant curb (Joelson Dec. ¶¶ 17-19E, Exhibit “F” to Evidence; Gibbens Dec. ¶¶ 14-16, Exhibit “G” to Evidence).

·        The wheelchair access ramp and adjoining curbs were clearly delineated and compliant with all ADA standards and regulations (Joelson Dec. ¶ 17, Exhibit “F” to Evidence; Gibbens Dec. ¶ 14, Exhibit “G” to Evidence).

·        JLL/BofA’s expert consultant confirmed that Gensler “neither failed to use reasonable care to prevent harm to others, nor did any condition of the property create a dangerous condition or an unreasonable risk of harm” (Joelson Dec. ¶ 19, Exhibit “F” to Evidence).

·        On August 21, 2024, the Court granted BofA’s MSJ, finding that the wheelchair access ramp and curb, where Plaintiff fell, were constructed in compliance with the California Building Code (CBC) and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) (Supplemental RJN, Exhibit “I”; Loera Dec. to Supplemental Brief ¶ 5).

Gensler has met its initial burden. The cross-complaints do not dispute the aforementioned facts alleged by Gensler, nor do they challenge Gensler’s lack of negligence. Therefore, summary adjudication as to the cause of action for apportionment is granted.

Implied Contractual Indemnity and Equitable Indemnity

Implied contractual indemnity is now considered “a form of equitable indemnity.” (Prince v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1151, 1157.) “Where . . . parties have expressly contracted with respect to the duty to indemnify, the extent of that duty must be determined from the contract and not by reliance on the independent doctrine of equitable indemnity.” (Regional Steel Corp. v. Superior Court (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 525, 529.) Here, the parties do not dispute the existence of the indemnity clause; thus, implied and equitable indemnity are inapplicable. Accordingly, summary adjudication is granted as to these two causes of action.

Conclusion

MSA as to cross-complainants BofA and JLL's causes of action for breach of contract and declaratory relief are denied. MSA as to cross-complainants' causes of action for apportionment, equitable indemnity, and implied contractual indemnity are granted.
