Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling.

In the absence of any appearing party or notice to the Court acknowledging the Court’s Order, the Court is to provide notice.


Case Number: 20STCV33913    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: X

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Tax Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs

 

            Plaintiffs move to tax or strike costs from Defendant’s memorandum of costs.  Plaintiffs alleged a personal injury claim stemming from a dog bite by Defendant Filippo Marchino’s dog. Plaintiffs filed their complaint on September 4, 2020.  On May 6, 2024, a jury returned a special verdict in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiffs.  On June 24, 2024, Defendant filed a memorandum of costs seeking a total of $39,555.98.

 

Plaintiff’s motion to tax costs on Defendant’s memorandum of costs is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.  Defendant’s memorandum of cost is reduced by $10,516.63, with total costs awarded in the amount of $29,039.35.

           

On December 13, 2021, Defendant served a Code of Civil Procedure section 998 Offer to Compromise.  This offer was not accepted by Plaintiffs and the jury found in favor of Defendant at trial.  Because Plaintiffs did not accept Defendant’s 998 Offer, and they failed to obtain a more favorable judgment, Plaintiff cannot recover his costs incurred after December 13, 2021 and must pay Defendant’s post-December 13, 2021 costs.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 998(c)(1).)  The Court has discretion as to whether and in what amount Plaintiffs may be required to pay actual and reasonable costs of the services of expert witnesses who are not regular employees of any party in preparation for or during trial.  (Ibid.)

 

Item 1: Filing and motion fees $2,178.14

GRANT motion to tax in the amount of $435.00 for the Answer, which was filed on July 16, 2021, before the 998 Offer.  Defendant is not entitled to recover the cost of the Answer pursuant to Section 998 (c)(1).

 

Item 2: Jury fees $525.11

GRANT motion to tax in the amount of $150.00 for 12/08/21 jury fees.  This cost was incurred before the 998 Offer.

 

Item 4: Deposition costs $7,653.47

DENY motion to tax Julie Choi’s certificate of non-appearance.

GRANT motion to tax in the amount of $445.15 for witness Allison Klein, who did not testify at trial, and in the combined sum of $1,716.82 for Meghedi Badall and Omar Perciado, who did not testify at trial.  The Court finds their costs are not reasonable or necessary.  The total amount taxed for deposition costs is $2,161.97.

 

Item 5: Service of process $2,816.16

DENY motion to tax Julie Choi’s registered process fee.

GRANT motion to tax in the amount of $353.48 for service of process fees on medical personnel who did not testify: Wilson H. Park, Nees Chiropractic, Pasadena Primary Care, Dr. Jon Greenfield, and Adventist Health.  


Item 8: Witness fees $12,659.00

GRANT motion to tax expert fees as to experts that did not testify: Beau LeBlanc ($3,477.50), Ryan Wiley ($450.00), Kathrina Boyles ($387.00), Blake Newton ($560.00), Ryan Porter ($874.00), Le Sun ($1,417.50), and Drew Sauvola ($18.00).  The total amount taxed for witness fees is $7,184.00.

 

Item 12: Models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits $4,323.20

Case Number: 23BBCV01882    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: X

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney Fees

 

            On August 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Sunrise Ford North Hollywood and Ford Motor Company, which included a claim for Violation of Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act-Civil Code §1790 et seq.  On or about May 22, 2024, Plaintiff accepted Defendants’ CCP § 998 Offer to Compromise.

 

Plaintiff moves for an order that determines the reasonable attorney fees payable to Plaintiff by Defendant Ford Motor Company.  Plaintiff seeks fees in the amount of $81,997.50 and reasonable costs in the amount of $1,044.45.

 

The Court GRANTS the motion and determines $21,915.00 to be a reasonable award of attorney fees.  Costs are also awarded in the amount of $1,044.45, bringing the total award to $22,959.45.

 

Generally, each party is responsible for their attorney fees unless a statute or contract provides otherwise for the prevailing party.  (Eden Township Healthcare Dist. v. Eden Medical Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 418, 425.)  In determining which party is the prevailing party, trial courts may consider all factors reasonably indicating success in the litigation but may not abuse their discretion and deny fees arbitrarily.  (Sears v.¿Baccaglio¿(1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1136, 1149, 1155, 1158.)¿  A court abuses its discretion by denying fees where the party obtained a “‘simple, unqualified win,’” and the results were not mixed.  (Pacific Custom Pools, Inc. v. Turner Construction Co.¿(2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1254, 1268-1271.)

 

“[T]he starting point of every fee award…must be a calculation of the attorney’s services in terms of the time he has expended on the case.  Anchoring the analysis to this concept is the only way of approaching the problem that can claim objectivity, a claim which is obviously vital to the prestige of the bar and the courts.”  (In re Vitamin Cases¿(2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1058.)  ¿“[T]he primary method for establishing the amount of ‘reasonable’ attorney fees is the lodestar method.  The lodestar (or touchstone) is produced by multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by counsel by a reasonable hourly rate.”  (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.¿(2001) 92¿Cal.App.4th 819, 833.)  Under this approach, a base amount is calculated from a compilation of time¿reasonably spent¿and¿reasonable hourly compensation¿of each attorney.¿ (Serrano v. Unruh¿(1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 639.)  While “a fee request ordinarily should be documented in great detail,” the absence of time records and billing statements did not deprive the court of “substantial evidence” to support an award when the attorney provided a declaration under the penalty of perjury which described the work and permitted the trial court to make its own evaluation of the reasonableness of the work done in light of the nature of the case and on the credibility of counsel’s declaration.  (Weber v.¿Langholz¿(1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1578, 1587.)

 

Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff is the prevailing party.  Further, Section 1794, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of damages and other legal and equitable relief when the action involves a buyer of consumer goods that is damaged by an implied or express warranty of a contract.  Section 1794, subdivision (d) further authorizes the recovery of attorney’s fees “based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.”  (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).)

 

Turning to the hourly rate, Plaintiff’s counsel, Sean M. Novak, declares his to be $975.00.  (Novak Decl., ¶ 6.)  The opposition complains that the hourly rate of $975.00 is unreasonable for even the most experienced attorney in a simple garden variety lemon law case.  (Proudfoot Decl. ¶ 4.)  The opposition states Novak has not presented any evidence of prior lemon law experience to justify his rate and his declaration only evidences his experience in purely contingent cases such as personal injury matters.  (See Novak Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

A reasonable hourly rate is determined by the prevailing rate charged to attorneys of similar skill and experience in the relevant community.  (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.)  However, the court may also consider the attorney’s skill and expertise, the nature of the work performed, the relevant area of expertise and the attorney’s customary billing rates.  (Flannery v. California Highway Patrol (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 629, 632.)  The court is the best judge of the value of professional services provided and may use its discretion to apply rates in line with the market rates for the services provided.  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)  Based on the court’s knowledge and experience, it determines that $450.00 represents a reasonable hourly rate.

 

As to the number of hours expended in prosecuting this action, Plaintiff submitted the Declaration of Sean Novak, which includes an attorney billing statement attached as Exhibit C.  Plaintiff’s counsel’s seven months of litigation work included participation in discovery and responding to Defendants’ raised issues.

 

A court “must carefully review attorney documentation of hours expended” to determine whether the time reported was reasonable.  (See Ketchum, supra, 24 Cal.4th at p. 1132 (quoting Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48).)  Thus, evidence provided by the fee applicant “should allow the court to consider whether the case was overstaffed, how much time the attorneys spent on particular claims, and whether the hours were reasonably expended.” (Christian Research Inst. v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1320.  The court must exclude “duplicative or excessive” time from its fee award.  (Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 161; see also Ketchum, supra, at p. 1132 [stating “inefficient or duplicative efforts [are] not subject to compensation.”].)

 

The billing records submitted by Plaintiff’s counsel indicate that Mr. Novak expended 74.1 hours and anticipates an additional 10 hours of time through the hearing on this motion. (Novak Decl. ¶ 5.)  The opposition claims the reported hours were not reasonably incurred and points to several litigation facts, some of which are addressed by Plaintiff in his Reply.  The Court determines that 48.7 hours are reasonable, which includes the time associated with the bringing and hearing of this motion.  At an hourly rate of $450.00, attorney fees are awarded in the amount of $21,915.00.

 

Finally, on the issue of costs, Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d) states, “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses,…, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.  The Court awards $1,044.45 in recoverable costs.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS the motion in the reduced amount of $21,915.00 and costs in the amount of $1,044.45.  In total, Plaintiff is awarded $22,959.45 in attorney fees and costs.
