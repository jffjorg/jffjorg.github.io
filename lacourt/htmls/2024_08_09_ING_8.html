DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV09554    Hearing Date: August 9, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 August 9, 2024 

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   20STCV09554 [consolidated case: 20STCV35557]

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Maria Lopez; Martin Gonzalez v. Matrix Service, Inc., et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant, Matrix Service, Inc

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiffs, Christian Gonzalez, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Sonia Soto and Maria Lopez and Martin Gonzalez

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        September 9, 2024

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication

                                               

Tentative Rulings:                  (1)  ARGUE.

 

 

             

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A.    Factual¿¿ 

 

On March 6, 2020, Plaintiffs Maria Lopez and Martin Gonzalez (“Parent Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants, Matrix Service, Inc., Brandon K. Austin, PBF Energy Limited, Christian Martin Gonzalez, Sonia Jasmin Soto, and DOES 1 through 50 in the leading case. On May 8, 2020, Plaintiffs, Maria Lopez and Martin Gonzalez filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence – Wrongful Death; (2) Strict Liability – Product Liability; and (3) Professional Negligence.

 

Shortly after, on September 17, 2020, in the consolidated case, Plaintiffs, Sonia Soto, individually and successor-in-interest to The Estate of Gamaliel Gonzalez, Jayden Salas, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Sonia Soto, Christian Gonzalez, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Sonia Soto (“Soto Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Strict Products Liability; and (3) Breach of Warranties.

 

Defendant, Matrix Services, Inc. (“Matrix”) filed a Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication on the grounds that it argues that Soto Plaintiffs and Parent Plaintiffs causes of action for negligence, strict liability, and breach of warranties asserted in their pleadings provide no triable issues of material fact as to such causes of action, thereby entitling Matrix to judgment as a matter of law.

 

 

B.    Procedural

 

On May 24, 2024, Matrix filed a Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication. On July 25, 2024, Soto Plaintiffs and Parent Plaintiffs filed a joint opposition brief. On July 29, 2024, Soto Plaintiffs and Parent Plaintiffs filed a notice of Errata Opposition. On August 2, 2024, Matrix filed a reply brief.

 

II. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿ 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520. ) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ 

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.¿¿¿ 

 

To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) 

 

B.    Summary Judgment Discussion

 

                                               i.          Soto Plaintiffs’ Standing Issue

 

First, Matrix argues that the Soto Plaintiffs lack standing on the grounds that Sonia Soto is not the putative spouse of Decedent as a matter of law, and therefore, lacks standing to assert the causes of action for Negligence, Strict Products Liability, and Breach of Warranties. The Court requires oral argument from both parties on this issue as it does not see anything in the opposition brief that addresses the standing issue. The moving papers submit deposition testimony from Sonia Soto as well as requests for admission that she knew she was not married to decedent, was aware of facts inconsistent with a rational belief in a valid or lawful marriage, including that she and decedent were saving money for her to be able to pay for a divorce, and have a civil ceremony with the decedent. These facts remain undisputed, and thus, the Court is inquiring whether Sonia Soto is conceding this fact.

 

However, the issue of standing of both Jayden Salas and Giancarlo Salas are still at issue. The Court seeks oral argument from both of the parties on this issue as Soto Plaintiffs’ opposition also failed to address these issues.

 

                                             ii.          Misuse and Foreseeable Misuse Affirmative Defenses

 

As to the misuse issue, the Court tentatively finds that Matrix carried its initial burden on summary judgment as to the complete defense of misuse.  Matrix carried its initial burden by illustrating to this Court that the drawings included the instructions for the user to “field tack weld bar to base plate [of jack stand] for bottle jack retention,” and did not include any indication for the use of airbags with the subject jack stand. (Matrix’s SSUMF Nos. 63-64.) Further, Matrix submitted opinions of three different professional engineers bearing on the lack of foreseebioity of the use of a jackstand without a bottle jack or airbag including: (1) Andrew Yearwood (“Yearwood”), who opined that “[w]here design specification drawings for a product include in them the manner in which the product is to be used or other devices to which the product is to be used with, the intended and reasonably foreseeable use of such product is derived from that which is contained within the design drawings.” (Declaration of Andrew Yearwood (“Yearwood Decl.”), ¶¶ 10-13); (2) Dough Bayles (“Bayles”), the designer of the Hagen Exemplar Drawings, which Austin used as the basis for the design of the subject jack stand and which also specified the use of a bottle jack, opined that “the jack stands are intended to be used with the load at the base, with top plate in contact with the load being moved” and that it would be contrary to use an airbag where the designs call for a bottle jack (Watts Decl., Ex. 40 [Bayles Depo. (Vol. 1) 17:8 - 21:9], Ex. 42 [Bayles Depo. (Vol. 3) at 255:18 - 256:11, 300:5-21, 351:2 - 352:6], Ex. 43 [Bayles email]); and (3) Austin, who noted in his deposition that he would have expected that the subject jack stand that he designed would have been used with a bottle jack because it was on his drawings.

 

            However, this Court also tentatively finds that Plaintiffs have carried their shifted burden in raising triable issues of fact that a jury needs to decide as to whether the use of the jack stand with the airbag and without a bottle jack would have been reasonably foreseeable. Plaintiffs’ evidence includes other portions of the Austin deposition, where he had discussions of the use of an inflatable airbag on top of the top plate of the subject jack stand that was being designed, and that Austin had heard about the application of a jackstand plus airbag combination being used in other industries. Austin also stated in deposition that he knew Tussey was wanting to put an airbag on top of the top plate and between the load being lifted, but he did not communicate in writing to anyone that such a combination should not be employed.

 

Plaintiffs have also cited to the deposition of Kennith Erdmann, the designated PMK for Matrix, who happens to be an engineer and employee of another division of Matrix Parent Company. Erdmann confirmed in his deposition that Matrix NAC had used air bags in combination with jack stands previously for the purpose of lifting floating roofs – the task at issue in this case. (Plaintiff’s Ex. B, 22:1-5, 41:20-25, 44:2-11.) Erdmann’s deposition indicates to this Court that since Matrix has previously used airbags with jack stands in the past for the purpose of lifting floating roofs, that J.T. Thorpe and Decedent’s use of the Matrix jack stand in combination with an airbag could found by a reasonable juror to be a reasonably foreseeable use as such use.

 

                                           iii.          Jack Stand Component Part Defect

 

The next issue the Court has identified is whether Matrix has carried its burden on the issue of whether the jack stand itself had a defect. Matrix has argued that the jack stand itself was not defective, but that when used incorrectly (with an air bag), such misuse was the cause of the incident in the case at bar. The Court tentatively finds that Matrix has carried its initial burden as well on this issue for similar reasons as cited above.

 

In their opposition, Plaintiffs argue that the causation issue is not the airbags, but was the allegedly defective hitch ball that was a component part of the jack stand itself. The Court tentatively finds that Plaintiffs have carried their shifted burden by reference to the EAG Labs Report and Dial Engineering Report (Moving Papers, Exhibits 52 and 53), that were cited to by Matrix expert Yearwood. Plaintiff further notes and relies on their experts, Eric Deyerl, M.S., P.E. (“Deyerl”) and Michael Neff, SM, P.E. (“Neff”), who have opined that Matrix used a deficient hitch ball and attachment method, comprising the jack stand’s strength and contributing to its failure.  While the Court recognizes that Matrix has submitted over 50 pages of evidentiary objections for the Court to rule on, the Court has not yet made its rulings but will do so after taking oral argument on the other issues. 

 

As to Plaintiffs’ expert evidence submitted in opposition to this motion, Deyerl opined that “by placing the weld where they did (on the nut), because the upper section of the ball was not directly connected to the ram end plate, Matrix had exposed the shank of the hitch ball to loads being applied to the ball during the use of the jack stand.” (Deyerl Decl., ¶ 13e.) Deyerl further opined that “stress calculations by Dial Engineering indicate that had Matrix selected one of two other commercially available hitch balls which were stronger…the ball would have been much less likely to have deformed under loads.” (Deyerl Decl., ¶¶ 13d, 13f.) Deyerl’s declaration indicates that a different area where the hitch ball should have been welded – around the circumference of the shoulder of the ball and states that “had Matrix made this circumferential weld connection with one of the stronger commercially available balls, it would have prevented that ball from fracturing under loads applied by JT Thorpe crew and thus prevented Mr. Gonzalez’s fatal injuries.” (Deyerl Decl., ¶¶ 13e-13f.) He concluded that “the selection and connection of the hitch ball to the jack stand by Matrix rendered the subject jack stand defective due to its inability to withstand more than a fraction of its rated 20- ton load before failing during normal and foreseeable use by the Thorpe crew. (Deyerl Decl. ¶ 14c.)

 

In Matrix’s reply they point out that Plaintiffs’ former allegation that the alleged defect of the subject jack stand’s inability to withstand ‘side loading’ is completely gone, and that Deyerl’s declaration stating that he “disagree[s] that such side loading had anything to do with the failure of the ball itself,” is now being relied on because a greater amount of horizontal (side) loads/rotational forces are imparted on the jack stand via the use of the air bag, despite Matrix maintaining it had no knowledge J.T. Thorpe would use an airbag with the subject jack stand, and that this use was entirely out of Matrix’s control.

 

            This Court will hear oral argument from the parties as to Matrix’ reply argument that Plaintiff should be barred from changing its theory of defect when opposing a summary judgment motion as compared to what had been previously alleged in the pleadings.  The operative complaint does contain allegations of component part defect and of manufacturing or design defects.

¿II. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A. Demurrer

¿ 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)¿¿ 

 

Fraud, Concealment, and Misrepresentation

 

As this Court has previously ruled on a demurrer by GM as to Plaintiff’s original complaint, in the interest of brevity, the Court will forgo analyzing the motion in the same level of extensiveness as the previous tentative ruling. GM still argues that Plaintiff’s fraud causes of action fail because Plaintiff did not plead fraud with the requisite specificity. Specifically, GM argues Plaintiff failed to identify a material fact regarding the Subject Vehicle that GM concealed or suppressed, including: (1) failing to identify the individual at GM who purportedly concealed material facts or made untrue representations about her Bolt; (2) their authority to speak and act on behalf of GM; (3) GM’s knowledge about the alleged defects in Plaintiff’s vehicle at the time of sale; (4) any interactions with GM before or during the purchase of her vehicle; or (5) GM’s intent to induce reliance by Plaintiff to purchase the specific vehicle at issue.

 

As noted in the Court’s previous tentative ruling on the demurrer to the original complaint, this Court explained – in great detail – that the requirements listed above are required for fraudulent misrepresentation, not fraudulent concealment. One of the issues identified by this Court about Plaintiff’s original complaint was that Plaintiff failed to specify whether it was moving on a cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation or fraudulent concealment. Since then, Plaintiff has amended her FAC to indicate that she is moving on a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. As noted previously, “[t]he elements of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment are: (1) concealment of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose; (3) the defendant intended to defraud by failing to disclose; (4) plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as it did had it known the fact; and (5) damages.” (Butler America, LLC v. Aviation Assurance Company, LLC (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 136, 144.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Next, this Court notes that when it came to Plaintiff’s allegations in the original complaint, this Court previously held that although the original complaint alleged all required elements for fraudulent concealment, crucial information was still missing. Further, as previously noted, Pursuant to California law, a duty to disclose material facts may arise (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant has exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; or (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. (Falk v. General Motors Corp. (N.D. Cal. 2007) 496 F.Supp.2d 1088, 1098-1099 citing LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326.) Specifically, Plaintiff argues, in her opposition, that the Complaint contains allegations supporting that Defendant GM had exclusive knowledge.

Duty to Disclose

 

As this Court noted in its previous tentative, absent a fiduciary relationship between the parties, a duty to disclose can arise in only three circumstances: (1) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of the material fact; (2) the defendant actively concealed the material fact; or (3) the defendant made partial representations while also suppressing the material fact. (BiglerEngler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 311; LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) Again, GM argues that Plaintiff’s first cause of action fails as a matter of law because Plaintiff did not identify a material fact regarding the Subject Vehicle that GM concealed or suppressed.

 

Plaintiff’s opposition argues that she sufficiently alleged Defendant’s knowledge of the Battery Defect because, she alleges, GM knew this information through its exclusive knowledge of non-public information such as pre-release reviews and testing, customer satisfaction notices, software updates, internal investigations, and recalls as early as 2017.  In her FAC, Plaintiff did allege that Martin Chevrolet in Torrance California, where she purchased the vehicle, is an authorized dealership of GM. (FAC, ¶ 8.) Further, Plaintiff has also added that GM possessed both superior and exclusive knowledge regarding the defect through various means including, but not limited to, customer complaints, internal investigations, and internal testing, NHTSA reports. (SAC, ¶¶ 33, 45.) However, despite these allegations, the FAC also includes further allegations that prior to Plaintiff’s purchase of the vehicle, GM had customer complaints, customer service satisfaction, releasing new software updates, and recalls, etc. (FAC, ¶¶ 13-24.) As such, it is not clear how the information regarding the alleged defect was exclusive to GM.  NHTSA reports and recall campaigns are available to the public. Nothing in the FAC indicates that Plaintiff made specific inquiries as to potential defects, or the battery specifically, nor that only a partial disclosure or active concealment occurred in response. There are also no allegations in the FAC that Plaintiff previously made inquiries on her own, prior to going to the dealership or purchasing the vehicle, nor in doing research on the NHTSA website. As such, the FAC is contradictory as to the exclusive or superior knowledge issue.

 

Nonetheless, this Court will allow oral argument as to what addition fats can be alleged to overcome the fatal inconsistency between public disclosure of defect in an NHTSA recall campaign and in TSBs n the one hand, and a cause of action asserting that GM failed to disclose those same facts at the point of sale. There is no duty for a manufacturer to disclose the contents of a TSB or a recall campaign at the point of sale in this context.  This Court believes the creation of such a duty seems better left to the policy-making branches of government rather than the judiciary. Such a claimed duty seems to implicate balancing the burden that would be placed on manufacturers of such a broad duty against the perceived value that disclosures of such a wide array of information might yield for consumers. The law already recognizes a duty to recall or retrofit (see, e.g., Hernandez v. Badger Construction Equipment Co. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1791, 1827; Lunghi v. Clark Equipment Co. (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 485, 494), but such a cause of action requires harm in the form of personal injury rather than the economic losses alleged in the FAC here. (The Court is mindful that a claim for fraudulent inducement by concealment is not subject to demurrer on the ground it is barred by the economic loss rule, pending a decision by the California Supreme Court on certified question from the Ninth Circuit and on petition for review of Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828.)

Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200

 

Again, GM also demurs to the Plaintiff’s cause of action for Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200. To set forth a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) A cause of action for unfair competition “is not an all-purpose substitute for a tort or contract action.” (Cortez v. Purolator Air Filtration Products Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 163, 173.)

 

For the same reasons above, Plaintiff fails to adequately plead a cause of action for fraud. Because Plaintiff’s second cause of action is based on fraud, this cause of action is sustained as well.

A.    Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿ 

 

Here, GM again bases its Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s prayer for Punitive Damages on the argument that Plaintiff has not plead the fraud cause of action with the specificity required. Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages, and Defendant’s argument against it, are intrinsically intertwined with the fraud causes of action, and as such, the Court’s ruling as to the Motion to Strike is determined by the Court’s ruling of the demurrer. The Motion to Strike is thus Mooted.
