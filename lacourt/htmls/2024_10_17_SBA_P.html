DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV03792    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: P

Motion for Protective Order

Moving Parties: Defendants Pacific Bell Telephone Co. and Murray Dennis Jr.

Responding Party: Plaintiff Keith Pennetti

RULING

The Court considered the moving papers and opposition. Defendants’ motion for protective order is DENIED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff Keith Pennetti (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against AT&T Services, Inc. d/b/a AT&T and Murray Dennis Jr. (“Dennis”) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle Negligence and (2) General Negligence.

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the complaint to fix an incorrectly named defendant. Plaintiff erroneously named AT&T Services, Inc. when the defendant’s true name was Pacific Bell Telephone Company d/b/a AT&T California (“AT&T”).

On September 9, 2024, Defendants filed an admission of liability. Defendants admit that they are liable for the accident but do not admit that they caused all of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries.

On September 18, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion for a protective order to (1) limit the scope of deposition and (2) stay the deposition. On September 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiff alleges that Murray is an employee of AT&T. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) On November 20, 2021, Dennis was operating a vehicle owned by AT&T and crossed a double yellow center-line, colliding head-on with Plaintiff’s car. (Complaint, ¶¶ 11-12.)

LEGAL STANDARD

The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).) The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).) This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).)

The court shall restrict the frequency or extent of use of a discovery method if it determines either of the following:

1. The discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.

2. The selected method of discovery is unduly burdensome or expensive, taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420, subdivision (a) provides, in relevant part: “Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).)  “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. The decision upon whether to enter a protective order lies within the sound discretion of the court.  (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 588, 591; Meritplan Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 237, 242.) Moving parties have the burden to show good cause for protective order. (Emerson Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 16 Cal.4th 1101, 1110.)

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Defendants argue that the deposition should be limited to matters of causation, speed, damage to vehicles, injuries, and conversations at the scene of the accident because Defendants already admitted fault, and any other issues would be irrelevant.

Opposing Party’s Argument

Plaintiff contends that his counsel already agreed to avoid impermissible topics during the deposition. As such, this motion was unnecessary. Further, Defendants have not shown that there is good cause for granting the motion.

Merits of the Motion

A. Meet and Confer

 “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

Defense counsel declares that the parties met and conferred but were unable to reach a resolution. The court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

B. Good Cause

“In accordance with the liberal policies underlying the discovery procedures, California courts have been broad-minded in determining whether discovery is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 172; Pettie v. Superior Court (1960) 178 Cal.App.2d 680, 687.) As a practical matter, it is difficult to define at the discovery stage what evidence will be relevant at trial. Therefore, the party seeking discovery is entitled to substantial leeway. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co., supra, 2 Cal.3d at p. 172.) Furthermore, California’s liberal approach to permissible discovery generally has led the courts to resolve any doubt in favor of permitting discovery. (Id. at p. 173.) In doing so, the courts have taken the view if an error is made in ruling on a discovery motion, it is better that it be made in favor of granting discovery of the nondiscoverable rather than denying discovery of information vital to preparation or presentation of the party's case or to efficacious settlement of the dispute.” (Norton v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1750, 1761.)

Defendants cite to Diaz v. Carcamo (2011) 51 Cal.4th 1148, 1154-58 to support their contention that the information sought by Plaintiff is irrelevant and inadmissible. The Diaz Court found that an employer’s admission of vicarious liability made claims of negligent entrustment, hiring, or retention irrelevant. (Id. at p. 1158.) Defendants argue that they would be prejudiced without a protective order because the deposition would be longer and more expensive for the sake of eliciting irrelevant information.

Plaintiff argues that the Diaz Court did not rule on claims of punitive damages. While Plaintiff does not currently seek punitive damages, Plaintiff’s counsel argues that he reserves the right to do so after conducting the Murray deposition. Further, Plaintiff contends that Defendants have only made bare assertions that the deposition is “irrelevant, oppressive, or unduly burdensome.” Thus, the protective order is premature and unjustified and should not be granted.

In light of the liberal policy surrounding discovery, the court determines that Defendants have not shown that there is good cause for staying the deposition or limiting its scope at this time. If Defendants determine that Plaintiff has obtained information that is prejudicial or irrelevant, it may raise an objection during the deposition or file a motion in limine afterwards.

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion is DENIED in its entirety.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion is DENIED. Defendants must submit Murray Dennis Jr. for deposition within 14 days of this hearing. Parties may stipulate to extend this deadline.

Plaintiff to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 24TRCV00119    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint

Moving Party: Defendants America Rodriguez and Oscar Rodriguez

Responding Party: None

RULING

Having considered the moving papers, Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED in its entirety without leave to amend.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On January 1, 2024, Plaintiffs Estate of Jeffrey B. Lanie (the “decedent”); Jeffrey E. Lanie (“Jeffrey E.”) and Denise A. Lanie (“Denise”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants America Rodriguez (“America”) and Oscar Rodriguez (“Oscar) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle; (2) Negligence; and (3) Wrongful Death.

On March 18, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to strike Plaintiffs’ request for exemplary damages.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On January 16, 2022, Defendants were involved in a motor vehicle accident with the decedent. (Complaint, p. 5.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendants caused the accident by driving or operating the vehicle negligently. (Complaint, p. 5.)

LEGAL STANDARD

The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 431.10 states, “(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following: (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense. (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense. (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. (c) An “immaterial allegation” means “irrelevant matter” as that term is used in Section 436.”

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Defendants contend that Plaintiffs’ factual allegations do not support a request for punitive damages.

Merits of the Motion

A. Meet and Confer

“Before filing a motion to strike . . . the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 435.5, subd. (a).) If no agreement is reached, the moving party shall file and serve with the motion to strike a declaration stating either: (1) the means by which the parties met and conferred and that the parties did not reach an agreement, or (2) that the party who filed the pleading failed to respond to the meet and confer request or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith. (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).)

Defense counsel declares that the parties met telephonically to discuss the deficiencies in the complaint but were unable to reach an agreement.

Accordingly, the court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

B. Punitive Damages

In non-contract actions, punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (Ibid.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (Ibid.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.)

A motion to strike punitive damages is properly granted where a plaintiff does not state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, including allegations that defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. California, Inc., 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63 (2010).) “Mere negligence, even gross negligence, is not sufficient to justify such an award” for punitive damages. (Kendall Yacht Corp. v. United California Bank, 50 Cal. App. 3d 949, 958 (1975).) The allegations supporting a request for punitive damages must be alleged with specificity; conclusory allegations without sufficient facts are not enough. (Smith v. Superior Court, 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-1042 (1992).)

Here, Plaintiffs only allege that “Defendants, and each of them, so negligently owned, maintained, operated, entrusted, and/or drove a motor vehicle so as to cause injuries and damages to Plaintiffs.” Plaintiffs do not allege that Defendants acted in an oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious manner. Thus, Plaintiffs have not met the pleading requirements for punitive damages.

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED in its entirety.

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768 (“When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.”).) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

Plaintiffs do not argue that they can successfully amend the complaint to maintain a request for punitive damages. Thus, the court finds that there is no possibility of successful amendment.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion is GRANTED in its entirety without leave to amend.

Defendants to give notice.
