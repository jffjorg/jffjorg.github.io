DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 21STCV23920    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:

            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Mikel Rastegar’s Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint

Plaintiff/cross-defendant Mikel Rastegar demurs to all 10 causes of action alleged in the first amended cross-complaint (FACC) by Mark Blankenship and Jennifer Harper. 

Cross-complainants filed untimely oppositions on May 2, 2024.  In the past the court has considered cross-complainants’ untimely oppositions.  This time it will not.  The court exercises its discretion not to consider the untimely oppositions by cross-complainants.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d).)  Cross-complainants are admonished that they must file their papers in a timely way in the future.   

1. Fraud

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts to constitute fraud.  Fraud requires “(a) misrepresentation; (b) defendant’s knowledge of the statement’s falsity; (c) intent to defraud (i.e., to induce action in reliance on the misrepresentation); (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.”  (Hunter v. Up-Right, Inc. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 1174, 1184.)  “[F]raud must be pled specifically.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “ ‘This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.’ ”  (Ibid.) 

            The FACC does not specifically allege a misrepresentation.  It alleges, “Cross Defendant misrepresented to Cross Complainant that in exchange for the Cross Complainants agreeing to pay rent that the Cross Defendants would provide habitable premises and would not stalk and harass and abuse process and destroy the lives of the Cross Complainants at every level, essentially manipulating the entire Agreement to become a torture chamber for the Cross Complainants.”  (¶ 44.) 

Assuming the alleged representation that Rastegar “would provide habitable premises” is sufficient, the FACC does not specify how, when, where, or by what means those representations were tendered.  The remainder of the alleged misrepresentation, that Rastegar “would not stalk and harass and abuse process and destroy the lives of the Cross Complainants at every level, essentially manipulating the entire Agreement to become a torture chamber for the Cross Complainants” is so vague and jumbled that it does not satisfy the general standard of pleading, let alone the higher standard for alleging fraud.

2. Breach of contract

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  Its elements are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

            The FACC alleges a contract: “a de facto periodic month-to-month tenancy” with $4,800 in monthly rent.  (¶ 15.)  It does not, however, allege cross-complainants performed their obligations under the contract—i.e., paying their rent.  The FACC refers to various excuses for nonperformance.  For example, it alleges that after February 2020, “Defendant believes that the $4800 monthly rent payment as of that point should be reduced significantly (at least by Fifty Percent (50%) during this period.”  (¶ 19.)  But it neither alleges (a) sufficient facts to establish such an excuse for nonperformance, nor (b) that they paid 50% of the rent. 

3. Breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  The elements of this cause of action are the same as for breach of contract, except with breach of the implied covenant instead of breach of an express contract term.  Thus, the plaintiff must allege: (1) a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant prevented plaintiff from receiving the benefits under the contract; (4) in doing so, defendant did not act fairly and in good faith; and (5) defendant’s conduct harmed plaintiff.  (See CACI No. 325.)  As discussed above, the FACC does not allege sufficient facts to constitute the second element.

4. Conversion and theft

            The FACC alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  “The basic elements of [conversion] are (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) the defendant’s disposition of the property in a manner that is inconsistent with the plaintiff’s property rights; and (3) resulting damages.”  (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.) 

            The FACC alleges Rastegar effected an “illegal lockout, in the process converting and stealing the personal property of Cross Complainants and marooning it under lock and key.”  (¶ 33.)  It alleges Rastegar did not “inventory the personal property and keep it safe” or “notify the Cross Defendants in writing and providing official Notice.”  (¶ 34.)  It further alleges the property is “still not yet returned.”  (¶ 54.)

            Rastegar’s demurrer argues the FACC “ignore[s] the fact that the personal property was left or abandoned on the Premise was wholly abandoned by the Cross Plaintiffs despite several notices’ and warnings.”  (Demurrer, p. 11.)  In reviewing a complaint challenged by demurrer, the court must “accept as true all the material facts properly pleaded” and does not “go beyond the four corners of the complaint, except as to matters which may be judicially noticed.”  (Thornburn v. Department of Corrections (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1284, 1287-1288.)  The demurrer to this cause of action relies on extrinsic facts.  

5. Abuse of Process

The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for abuse of process.  The “essential elements of’ abuse of process are “ ‘an ulterior purpose’ ” and “ ‘a wilful act in the use of the process not proper in the regular conduct of the proceeding.’ ”  (Templeton Feed and Grain v. Ralston Purina Co. (1968) 69 Cal.2d 461, 466.)  “ ‘Some definite act or threat not authorized by the process, or aimed at an objective not legitimate in the use of the process, is required; and there is no liability where the defendant has done nothing more than carry out the process to its authorized conclusion, even though with bad intentions.  The improper purpose usually takes the form of coercion to obtain a collateral advantage, not properly involved in the proceeding itself, such as the surrender of property or the payment of money, by the use of the process as a threat or a club.’ ”  (Spellens v. Spellens (1957) 49 Cal.2d 210, 232.)

The FACC alleges Rastegar committed abuse of process “By obtaining possession of the premises by stealth Default Judgment with a bogus Substituted Service Proof of Service” and “By extracting Nineteen Thousand Two Hundred Dollars ($19,200.00) from the Cross Complainants based upon a false promise to rescind the Notice to Vacate and then locking out the Cross Complainants and their two (2) small toddlers by surprise, WITHOUT THEIR PERSONAL BELONGINGS, for one (1) month.”  (¶ 27.)

These allegations constitute an improper method of carrying out the process to its authorized conclusion.  Evicting a tenant and collecting damages are proper purposes of an unlawful detainer action. 

6. Violation of COVID 19 Tenant Protection Act

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  It alleges, “As alleged with specificity in this Verified Cross Complaint, the Cross Defendant violated almost every single section of the COVID 19 Tenant Protection Act to the damage of the Cross Complainants thus entitling the Cross Complainants to statutory damages and penalties.”  (¶ 58.)  The numerous factual allegations preceding this paragraph do not suffice to apprise Rastegar of what conduct allegedly violated this law, specifically.  Alleging this legal conclusion is not enough.

7. Intentional and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

            The FACC alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  The elements of intentional infliction of emotional distress are “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.”  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.)  “A claim of negligent infliction of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence to which the traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.”  (Wong v. Jing (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1354, 1377.)  

The demurrer argues that the FACC’s conclusory allegations contained in paragraph 61 is insufficient.  The court must read the complaint “as a whole and all its parts in their context” (Moore v. Regents of University of California (1990) 51 Cal.3d 120, 125) and must liberally construe it in favor of the plaintiff (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238). 

When applying this standard, the FACC sufficiently alleges each element.  It alleges Rastegar engaged in a scheme to fraudulently evict cross-complainants, including “[b]y obtaining possession of the premises by stealth Default Judgment with a bogus Substituted Service Proof of Service” (¶ 27.A.)  It alleges Rastegar told Blankenship, “ ‘I will burn all of your personal belongings and torch the unit before you ever set foot in it’ ” (¶ 27.E.2) and “ ‘I will burn your children’s clothes they will never have them again.’ ”  (¶ 27.E.5.)  It further alleges Rastegar “prematurely enforced the Lock Out of Defendants and their toddlers on March 15, 2023 AFTER DARK.”  (¶ 28.)  Finally, it alleges cross-complainants have “suffer[ed] harm and extreme emotional distress.”  (¶ 38.)

8. Rescission based upon fraud

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for rescission based upon fraud.  “A party to a contract may rescind the contract … [i]f the consent of the party rescinding, or of any party jointly contracting with him, was given by mistake, or obtained through duress, menace, fraud, or undue influence, exercised by or with the connivance of the party as to whom he rescinds.”  (Civ. Code, § 1689(b)(1).)  This cause of action relies on the FACC’s underlying claim for fraud.  As discussed above, the FACC does not allege sufficient facts for fraud.

9. Imposition of a constructive trust against property acquired by fraud

            The FACC alleges sufficient facts for this cause of action.  “The essence of the theory of constructive trust is to prevent unjust enrichment and prevent a person from taking advantage of his or her own wrongdoing.”  (American Master Lease LLC v. Idanta Partners, Ltd. (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 1451, 1484.)  “Imposition of a constructive trust is an equitable remedy to compel the transfer of the property by one who is not justly entitled to it to one who is.”  (Id. at p. 1485.)  The same allegations that support conversion suffice for a constructive trust over that personal property. 

10. Abuse of process against senior citizen

            The FACC does not allege sufficient facts for this cause of action.  It alleges, “For the purposes of harassment and emotional terrorism, the Cross Complainant attempted to serve upon Virginia Greco Blankenship, aged Ninety (90) Years, who is also the mother of Plaintiff Mark Blankenship, a Subpoena Duces Tecum for her financial records at her place of residence and then caused to be filed a FRAUDULENT PROOF OF SERVICE under penalty of perjury claiming that Virginia Greco Blankenship was personally served at her residence during a period in which Virginia Greco Blankenship was residing pursuant to physician’s directives approximately four (4) miles away from her home a full time residential nursing facility.”  (¶ 67.) 

            These allegations are insufficient because cross-complainants were not the target of the alleged abuse of process.  Virginia Greco Blankenship is not a party to this action. 

Disposition

Plaintiff/cross-defendant Mikel Rastegar’s demurrer to the first amended cross-complaint’s first, second, third, fifth, sixth, eighth, and tenth causes of action is sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend.  The demurrer to the fourth, seventh, and ninth causes of action is overruled.


Case Number: 21STCV42444    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Michelman & Robinson, LLP’s Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Cross-Complaint

Defendant/cross-complainant Michelman & Robinson, LLP moves for leave to file a first amended cross-complaint.  It seeks to add new causes of action for equitable indemnity, comparative contribution, and declaratory relief against a new cross-defendant: Carole Ann Crotty.

Courts have discretion to permit an amendment to any pleading “in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper.” (CCP § 473(a)(1).)  “This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.)  Courts exercise their discretion “liberally to permit amendment,” and “[t]he policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) 

Plaintiff/cross-defendant Spitz Technologies Corporation argues the proposed first amended cross-complaint’s new causes of action fail as a matter of law.  In ruling on a motion for leave to amend, courts generally do not consider the amended pleading’s merits.  (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 760.)  The court exercises its discretion not to consider the merits of the proposed amended cross-complaint.

 Plaintiff also argues Michelman & Robinson, LLP unreasonably delayed seeking leave to file a first amended cross-complaint.  Courts have discretion to deny leave to amend when (a) the moving party has delayed bringing the proposed amendment; and (b) the delay in seeking leave to amend will cause prejudice to an opposing party.  (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Assuming cross-complainant delayed bringing this proposed amendment, plaintiff shows insufficient prejudice to justify denying this motion.  The trial is set for November 2024.  There is ample time to conduct any additional discovery and to prepare for trial.

Disposition

Defendant/cross-complainant Michelman & Robinson, LLP’s motion for leave to file a first amended cross-complaint is granted.  Michelman & Robinson, LLP shall file the first amended cross-complaint (attached as Exhibit 1 to the motion) forthwith.
