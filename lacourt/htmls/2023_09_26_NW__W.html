DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV00909    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: W

DEBRA B. SILBERT v. TARZANA MEDICAL CENTER, LLC, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion for trial preference

 

Date of Hearing:        September 26, 2023                         Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV00909

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Debra B. Silbert

Responding Party:     No opposition   

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 28, 2023, Plaintiff Debra B. Silbert filed a complaint for negligence against Tarzana Medical Center, LLC, Providence Health System – Southern California, and Cedars-Sinai Medical Center. Plaintiff alleges while admitted to Tarzana Medical Center, LLC, she suffered from a preventable fall. Plaintiff alleges Tarzana Medical Center was aware of Plaintiff’s s Morse Fall Risk Assessment which indicated her fall risk score was 100 yet Plaintiff was found on the ground in the hallway outside her room at Tarzana. Plaintiff further alleges Tarzana Medical Center concealed the incident from the California Department of Public Health and did not report the incident despite its obligation to do so.

 

Plaintiff dismissed Providence Health System – Southern California on April 7, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Trial Preference is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Debra B. Silbert move this court for an order granting a trial preference pursuant to California Rules of Court 3.1335 and California Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivisions (a), (c), (e), and (f), on the ground that Plaintiff is over 70 years of age with an unstable health condition who has a substantial interest in the action as a whole thereby necessitating a preferential trial setting to prevent prejudicing Plaintiff’s interest in the litigation.

 

"A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings: (1) [t]he party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole[; and] (2) [t]he health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.”  (CCP § 36(a).)  

 

A motion made under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivision (a) “may be supported by nothing more than an attorney’s declaration ‘based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party’. [Citation.]”  (Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534, citing CCP § 36.5 [“[a]n affidavit in support of a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party”].) The affidavit may also consist entirely of hearsay and conclusions. (Id.) 

 

The court finds Plaintiff has made an adequate showing she has a substantial interest in the action as a whole and her health is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation. Plaintiff is 73 years old. (Silbert Decl. ¶2.) As the named Plaintiff in this case and the injured party, Plaintiff has a substantial interest in this action. (Silbert Decl. ¶¶3-10.) While admitted at Tarzana Medical Center from a rehabilitation facility for abdominal pain and related symptoms, Plaintiff suffered from a fall outside of her hospital room in the hallway while unattended. (Silbert Decl. ¶5.) Plaintiff’s Morse Fall Risk Assessment had indicated her fall risk score was 100 and further indicated she was at high risk for injury. (Silbert Decl. ¶4.) Since her fall, Plaintiff has undergone a craniotomy with evacuation of subdural hematoma and drain placement as well as craniotomies, CT scans, X-rays, MRIs, PET scans, neurological examinations, and more. (Silbert Decl. ¶¶6-9.) As a result, Plaintiff continues to suffer fall related adverse consequences and is confined to a bed at Valley Palms Care Center in North Hollywood, California. (Silbert Decl. ¶10.) Substantial prejudice therefore could result as it is reasonably likely she may pass or become incapable of participating in trial unless preference is granted.  (Markarian Decl. ¶17.)

 

The motion is unopposed. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for trial preference is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV01461    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: W

13630 VICTORY BOULEVARD, LLC v. VICTOR COBAR

 

demurrer to complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        September 26, 2023                         Trial Date:  None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.: 23VECV01461

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Victor Cobar

Responding Party:     Plaintiff 13630 Victory Boulevard, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a breach of contract action. Plaintiff 13630 Victory Boulevard, LLC alleges Plaintiff, as landlord, and Defendant, as tenant, entered into a written lease agreement dated August 14, 2020 wherein Defendant agreed to lease the Premises for a term ending on October 31, 2023. Plaintiff alleges under the Lease, Defendant was required to pay base rent and additional rent monthly, all as more particularly set forth in the Lease. Plaintiff alleges Defendant vacated the Premises and returned possession of the Premises to Plaintiff on January 17, 2021. At that time, Defendant owed base rent and additional rent of approximately $85,000.00.

 

On April 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant for breach of lease and common count.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Victor Cobar’s Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Victor Cobar demurs to the complaint on the grounds the complaint fails to allege whether the contract is written, oral, or implied in conduct as well as fails to allege the essential terms of the alleged contract or contracts. Defendant also demurs to the complaint on the grounds “[w]hen a common count is used as an alternative way of seeking the same recovery demanded in a specific cause of action, and is based on the same facts, the common count is demurrable if the cause of action is demurrable.” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 394.)

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) “A written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.) “In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must allege the substance of its relevant terms.” (Id.) When an action is founded upon a contract, the complaint is subject to demurrer if it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct. (CCP § 430.10(g); Maxwell v. Dolezal (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 93.)

 

The court finds the complaint sufficiently alleges the parties entered into a written agreement. (Compl. ¶6.)  Moreover, Plaintiff has attached a copy of the agreement as an exhibit. (Compl., Exh. A.) The court notes Defendant also failed to file a meet and confer declaration. Section 430.41(a) requires a demurring party to “meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. . . .  As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies.” 

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the complaint is OVERRULED.


Case Number: 23VECV01951    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: W

PACIFIC AVIATION DEVELOPMENT LLC v. ARASH "ALEX" ABBASSI, et al.

 

plaintiff’s WRIT OF ATTACHMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        September 26, 2023             Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23vecv01951

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Pacific Aviation Development LLC

Responding Party:     Defendant Arash “Alex” Abbassi  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 4, 2023, Plaintiff Pacific Aviation Development LLC filed a complaint against Defendants Arash “Alex” Abbassi, Encore Flight Corporation, Elevate Flight Academy LLC dba Elevate Flight Academy Inc. for breach of lease and breach of guaranty. Plaintiff is the master tenant under a lease with the City of Los Angeles for the lease of thirty acres of land located at the northwest corner of the Van Nuys Airport. Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff and Encore entered into a Tie Down Agreement on May 3, 2017 to sublease eleven “parking” spaces for the tying down and storage of certificated, currently registered aircraft in airworthy condition at the Park. Plaintiff further alleges it and Encore entered into a sublease for premises known as Hangar Delta 3. Plaintiff also entered into an office sublease with Elevate. Defendant Abbassi provided a personal guaranty on the Hangar Lease and Office Lease. Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached the lease and guaranty by failing to pay any rent and comply with the terms of the agreements.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

 Plaintiff’s Writ of Attachment is Continued. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In opposition, Defendants request the court take judicial notice of the following documents: (1) The Complaint filed on January 17, 2022 in Pacific Aviation Development LLC v. Encore Flight Corp., LASC Case No. 23VEUD00106 (Exh. A); (2) The Complaint filed on January 17, 2022 in Pacific Aviation Development LLC v. Elevate Flight Academy Inc., LASC Case No. 23VEUD00107 (Exh. B); and (3) The Los Angeles Eviction Moratorium, LAMC § 49.99 et seq. (Exh. C).

 

The court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §452(c), (d).)

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Defendants objections 1-14 are sustained for lack of foundation.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Pacific Aviation Development LLC requests the court grant Plaintiff’s Application and issue a Right to Attach Order and a Writ of Attachment against Defendant Abbassi on the grounds Plaintiff’s applications meet the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 483.010. Furthermore, Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant Abbassi, an individual, are not exempt form attachment as Plaintiff’s claims against Abbassi arise form Abbassi’s alleged conduct.

 

A writ of attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500). (CCP §483.010(a).) A claim is “readily ascertainable” where the amount due may be clearly ascertained from the contract and calculated by evidence; the fact that damages are unliquidated is not determinative. (CIT Group/Equipment Financing, Inc. v. Super DVD, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 537, 540-41 [attachment appropriate for claim based on rent calculation for lease of commercial equipment].)

 

The application shall be executed under oath and must include: (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based;  (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101 et seq.); and  (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment. (CCP § 484.020.) Code Civil Procedure section 487.010(a) provides that “[w]here the defendant is a corporation, all corporate property for which a method of levy is provided” is subject to attachment.

 

“The declarations in the moving papers must contain evidentiary facts, stated ‘with particularity,’ and based on actual personal knowledge with all documentary evidence properly identified and authenticated.” (Hobbs, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 79–80 [citing CCP § 482.040].) “In contested applications, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties and make a determination of¿the probable outcome of the litigation.” (Hobbs, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 80.) 

  

Plaintiff argues the amount is readily ascertainable according to the terms of the December 1, 2017 sublease between Encore and Plaintiff for the Delta 3 Premise (“Delta 3 Sublease”) and the October 10, 2019 sublease between Elevate and Plaintiff for the Echo 111 Premises (“Echo 111 Sublease”). Plaintiff contends there is also evidence of all invoices and reminder statements sent by Plaintiff to Encore and Elevate for the payment of their outstanding balances.

 

In opposition, Defendant Abbassi argues the amount is not ascertainable on the grounds the declaration of Steve Argubright only makes generalized statements about the amounts owed and fails to provide any basis for Mr. Argubright’s personal knowledge, foundation or authentication of Mr. Argubright’s statements nor provides the basis for the amount requested.

 

The court agrees the declaration does not sufficiently provide who Mr. Argubright is in relation to Pacific Aviation. A proper foundation must be established for declarations and affidavits showing the declarant had personal knowledge of each fact stated. (See Generale Bank Nederland v. Eyes of the Beholder Ltd. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1384, 1390.) As a result, Paragraph 1 of Mr. Argubright’s declaration that the information provided is based on his “personal, first-hand knowledge” is insufficient. Moreover, there are some discrepancies between the Argubright declaration and application.

 

The court notes the declarations, however, are not entirely conclusory. The declaration provides a readily ascertainable amount which includes $105,635.00 for the Delta 3 sublease (the monthly was $7,200.) and Elevate will owe Plaintiff a total of $39,944.75 in outstanding rent due. The court notes Plaintiff also seeks $3,240 in late fees on the Delta 3 sublease and $695.19 in late fees, $1,615.50 in utility charges, $6,321.25 in legal fees and $562.13 in costs on the lease with Elevate. However, the legal fees and costs are inappropriate at this time. Code of Civil Procedure section 483.010(a) provides “readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500) exclusive of costs, interest, and attorney's fees.” As such, Plaintiff cannot seek the additional costs in their writ of attachment.

 

Defendant Abbassi also argues the writ of attachment claims illegal late fees. Defendant contends the Writ of Attachment must also fail because the total requested amount contains illegal late fees as late fees were prohibited by the City of Los Angeles COVID Moratorium, which also pertained to commercial tenancies. (L.A.M.C. §§ 49.99.3.) The court agrees the late fees are also not ascertainable at this time. A Landlord may not charge any late fees, interest, penalties, or other related fees on timely late payments covered under the Moratorium. (L.A.M.C. §§ 49.99.3.)

 

Accordingly, the court continues the hearing so Plaintiff may provide a proper declaration.

 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’ Writ of Attachment is Continued
