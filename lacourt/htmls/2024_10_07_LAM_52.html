Case Number: 20STCV13190    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:   

Plaintiff American Honda Finance Corporation’s Motion Objecting to Undertaking Filed by Judgment Debtors

Plaintiff/judgment creditor American Honda Finance Corporation objects under Code of Civil Procedure 995.930 to the undertaking filed by defendants/judgment debtors Hooman Nissani and Babak Sarraf. 

Request for Judicial Notice

            Plaintiff requests judicial notice of four documents.  The first document is a certificate of status issued by the California Secretary of State indicating that 3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC is “Suspended – FTB.”  (Sheldon Decl., Ex. B.)  This document and the underlying facts are subject to judicial notice as official acts of the State’s executive branch.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d); see Belen v. Ryan Seacrest Productions, LLC (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1145, 1161, fn. 2.) 

            The other three documents are court records in a bankruptcy proceeding and an action in the United States District Court for the District of Arizona.  (Sheldon Decl., Exs. C-E.)  The records’ existence and legal effects are subject to judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d); Steed v. Department of Consumer Affairs (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 112, 120.)

            Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice are granted.

Surety

            Plaintiff objects that “[t]he sureties are insufficient” (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.920, subd. (a)) for two reasons.  Judgment debtors’ notice of undertaking states the surety is “3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC.”

            The surety is insufficient because it is not an admitted insurer.  “[A] bond shall be executed by two or more sufficient personal sureties or by one sufficient admitted surety insurer or by any combination of sufficient personal sureties and admitted surety insurers.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.310.)  3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC does not purport to be an admitted surety insurer.  The declaration in support of the undertaking states it is made “in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure Section 995.510.”  (Neyssani Decl., ¶ 1.)  That section concerns personal sureties, not admitted surety insurers.  A single personal surety is facially insufficient. 

            The surety is also insufficient because 3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC is a suspended business entity.  (Sheldon Decl., Ex. B.)  Suspended entities generally lack the power to participate in legal proceedings.  (Cal-Western Business Services, Inc. v. Corning Capital Group (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 304, 310.)  This suspended LLC may not serve as a surety.

Amount

            Plaintiff further objects that “[t]he amount of the bond is insufficient.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.920, subd. (b).)  An “undertaking shall be for double the amount of the judgment or order unless given by an admitted surety insurer in which event it shall be for one and one-half times the amount of the judgment or order.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 917.1, subd. (b).)  For this purpose, “[c]osts awarded by the trial court … shall be included in the amount of the judgment.”  (Id., subd. (d).)  This computation is “rigidly formulaic.”  (Leung v. Verdugo Hills Hospital (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 205, 212.) 

            As required under Code of Civil Procedure section 995.930, subdivision (a), plaintiff provides an “estimate of the amount that would be sufficient.”  On June 25, 2024, the court entered judgment of $899,005.42, plus costs to be set by further order.  On July 8, 2024, plaintiff served and filed a memorandum of costs claiming $26,825.90 in expenses.  On September 17, 2024, the court granted plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees in part and awarded $833,981.50 in attorney fees.  The judgment therefore totals $1,759,812.82.  Plaintiff states the undertaking must guarantee payment of at least $3,519,625.64.  That is double the judgment.  Plaintiff is correct.

            Defendants’ undertaking states the surety guarantees payment of “$899,005.42 and such further additional sum as the Court may award in attorney’s fees and costs.”  It neither specifies that additional sum nor accounts for doubling the judgment.  The amount of the undertaking is therefore insufficient.

Other Grounds

            Finally, plaintiff objects that “[t]he bond, from any other cause, is insufficient.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.920, subd. (c).) 

            Plaintiff shows the bond is insufficient because the supporting affidavit does not meet the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 995.520, subdivision (c).  That subdivision provides that, for sureties exceeding $5,000, the affidavit must include: “(1) A description sufficient for identification of real and personal property of the surety situated in this state and the nature of the surety’s interest therein that qualifies the surety on the bond.  [¶] (2) The surety’s best estimate of the fair market value of each item of property.  [¶] (3) A statement of any charge or lien and its amount, known to the surety, whether of public record or not, against any item of property.  [¶] (4) Any other impediment or cloud known to the surety on the free right of possession, use, benefit, or enjoyment of the property.”

            The affidavit in support of the undertaking states, “3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC, at the time herein was worth at least $3,000,000 in assets and holds the real property commonly known as 3170 Cherry Avenue, Long Beach, California 90230.  The value of that real property, net of any debts and liabilities, exceeds $3,000,000.”  (Neyssani Decl., ¶ 3.)  The declaration purports to summarize the equity in the property but does not separately estimate the value of the real property (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.520, subd. (c)(2)) and identify the debts or liabilities (id., subds. (c)(3) & (4)). 

            Plaintiff also shows the bond is insufficient because the supporting affidavit is not true.  On July 26, 2024, 3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC, submitted a summary of its assts and liabilities, and accompanying schedule forms, in a bankruptcy proceeding.  (Sheldon Decl., Ex. D.)  The summary states the LLC’s assets totaled $4,451,334.29 and its liabilities totaled $11,011,167.16.  (Id., p. 1.)  The net amount is therefore negative $6.5 million.  Houshang Neyssani executed the filing under penalty of perjury as the LLC’s managing member.  (Id., p. 24.) 

The affidavit in support of the undertaking, meanwhile, states the LLC is worth at least $3,000,000.  (Neyssani Decl., ¶ 3.)  That would mean that, in less than two months, the LLC acquired $9.5 million to offsets its prior net liability of $6.5 million.  That is not credible.  Judgment debtors fail to show any evidence showing that occurred.  The court finds 3170 Cherry Avenue Property, LLC does not have sufficient assets to guarantee the required payment.

Disposition

            Plaintiff/judgment creditor American Honda Finance Corporation’s motion objecting to undertaking is granted. 

The court hereby determines that the bond defendants/judgment debtors filed on September 18, 2024, is insufficient.  Under Code of Civil Procedure 995.960, subdivision (b)(1), the court specifies that the bond is insufficient because: (I) the surety is insufficient because it is a single personal surety and because it is a suspended entity, (II) the amount is insufficient, and (III) the bond is otherwise insufficient because the supporting affidavit is defective.  The court hereby orders that a bond with sufficient sureties and in a sufficient amount, at least $3,519,625.64, shall be given within five days.


Case Number: 23STCV30345    Hearing Date: October 7, 2024    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:

Cross-Defendant Universal Protection Service, LP’s Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint

Cross-defendant Universal Protection Service, LP, doing business as Allied Universal Security Services (Universal) demurs to the first amended cross-complaint by Jamison Services, Inc. and Jamison Properties, Inc. (collectively, “Jamison”).

Universal demurs only to Jamison’s sixth cause of action for negligence.  Jamison does not allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for negligence.  The economic loss rule bars this claim.  “In general, there is no recovery in tort for negligently inflicted ‘purely economic losses,’ meaning financial harm unaccompanied by physical or property damage.”  (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 922.)  “[T]he rule functions to bar claims in negligence for pure economic losses in deference to a contract between litigating parties.”  (Ibid.)  

“ ‘[T]he economic loss rule requires a [contractual party] to recover in contract for purely economic loss due to disappointed expectations, unless [the party] can demonstrate harm above and beyond a broken contractual promise.’ ”  (Rattagan v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2024) 17 Cal.5th 1, 20 (Rattagan).)  “[T]he economic loss doctrine applies when the parties have entered into a contract; the plaintiff sues for tort damages, alleging the defendant failed to perform as the contract requires; and negligently caused economic losses flowing from the breach.  In such a case, the plaintiffs are generally limited to recovery of those economic damages and cannot seek to expand their remedies beyond those available in contract.  The doctrine does not apply if the defendant’s breach caused physical damage or personal injury beyond the economic losses caused by the contractual breach and the defendant violated a duty flowing, not from the contract, but from a separate, legally recognized tort obligation.”  (Id. at p. 44.)

The complaint in this action by plaintiff Jane Doe D.R. asserts claims against Jamison and Universal arising from sexual assault she suffered at a property where Jamison and Universal provided security or other services.  Jamison’s first amended cross-complaint alleges Jamison Services, Inc. entered a written contract with Universal’s predecessor.  (FACC, ¶ 19.)  The sixth cause of action for negligence alleges Universal “owed a duty to Cross-Complainants to exercise reasonable care in supplying the product and/or service, and/or performing the work required of them arising out of their express and/or implied agreements with Cross-Complainants that Jane Doe alleges caused her injuries.”  (¶ 32a.)  (The first amended cross-complaint includes two paragraphs numbered 32.  The court refers to them as 32a and 32b.)    

Jamison thus alleges Universal negligently performed the services for which Jamison hired it via the Universal’s “agreements with Cross-Complainants.”  (FACC, ¶ 32a.)  Jamison does not allege it suffered physical damage or property damage.  Only plaintiff Jane Doe D.R. alleges a physical injury.  Jamison’s alleges it was injured because it “incurred costs and expenses associated with the resolution of” plaintiff’s claims against Jamison.  (¶ 32b.)  Those are purely economic losses arising out of the contractual relationship between Jamison and Universal.

Even if Jamison alleged it personally suffered property damage, negligence requires both an injury “beyond the economic losses caused by the contractual breach and [that] the defendant violated a duty flowing, not from the contract, but from a separate, legally recognized tort obligation.”  (Rattagan, supra, 17 Cal.5th at p. 44, italics added.)  Jamison alleges no facts establishing Universal owed it a tort duty independent of its contractual duties.

Disposition

Cross-defendant Universal Protection Service, LP, doing business as Allied Universal Security Services’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action alleged in the first amended cross-complaint by Jamison Services, Inc. and Jamison Properties, Inc. is sustained with 20 days’ leave to amend.
