DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV12000    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer

Maria Martinez v. Oportun, Inc., et al.

Moving Party: Cross-Defendant Oportun, Inc.
Responding Party: Cross-Complainant 1234 Madison Ave., LLC 
Tentative Ruling: OVERRULE

BACKGROUND 

This action arises out of an incident where plaintiff Maria Martinez was injured by a falling sign while she sat at a restaurant at a strip mall owned by Cross-Complainant after a plastic sign face for Cross-Defendant separated from the sign pole.  Cross-Complainant obtained leave to file an amended cross complaint and added two causes of action for express indemnity and breach of contract. (Motion to Strike, p. 4; see also, First Amended Cross Complaint, p. 3-4.) The Lease Agreement (“Lease”) between parties here includes an arbitration provision. The language of the provision states: 

Except as provided in Paragraph B below, the Parties agree to resolve any and all claims, disputes or disagreements arising under this Lease, the Lease under Paragraph 12 of this Lease, any other defaults by Lessor, or any defaults by Lessee by and through arbitration as provided below and irrevocably waive any and all rights to the contrary. The Parties agree to at all times conduct themselves in strict, full, complete and timely accordance with the terms hereof and that any attempt to circumvent the terms of this Arbitration Agreement shall be absolutely null and void and of no force or effect whatsoever. (First Amended Cross Complaint, Ex. A, para. 59(A).)  

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Cross-Defendant argues the cross complaint’s two new causes of action allege violations of provisions of the Lease constitute an attempt by Cross-Complainant to circumvent the terms of the Lease arbitration provision. (Demurrer, p. 6.) Cross-Defendant asserts the Court thus does not have jurisdiction as to the two causes of action, as the contract they are purportedly based on mandates arbitration. (Id.)

OPPOSITION 

Cross-Complainant argues that the instant demurrer is improper, and should have been brought as a motion to compel arbitration. (Opposition, p. 3.) Additionally, Cross-Complainant argues the arbitration provision provides several exceptions by enumerating categories of disputes that are expressly excluded from arbitration procedures. (Id. at 4.) Cross-Complainant further contends Cross-Defendant waived its right to compel arbitration.

REPLY

Cross-Defendant argues its demurrer is not a motion to compel arbitration, and reiterates that Cross-Complainant waived its right to include its causes of action in the instant action when it signed the Lease. (Reply, p. 4.)

ANALYSIS 

Demurrer

A. Causes of Action

The first amended cross complaint’s fourth cause of action for express indemnity alleges Cross-Defendant executed a written agreement requiring Cross-Defendant to defend and indemnify Cross-Complainant for its use and/or occupancy of the premises.  (First Amended Cross Complaint ¶ 12.) The cross complaint further alleges its agreement with Cross-Defendant contains an express indemnity clause which provides: 

“Except for Lessor’s negligence, gross negligence or willful misconduct, Lessee shall indemnify, protect, defend and hold harmless the Premises, Lessor and its agents, Lessor’s master or ground lessor, partners and Lenders, from and against any and all claims, loss of rents and/or damages, liens, judgments, penalties, attorneys’ and consultants’ fees, expenses and/or liabilities directly and solely arising out of, involving, or in connection with, the use and/or occupancy of the Premises by Lessee. If any action or proceeding is brought against Lessor by reason of any of the foregoing matters, Lessee shall upon notice defend the same at Lessee’s expense by counsel reasonably satisfactory to Lessee and Lessor shall cooperate with Lessee in such defense. Lessor need not have first paid any such claim in order to be defended or indemnified.”
(First Amended Cross Complaint ¶ 13.)

Based on these pleadings, the first amended cross complaint successfully alleges a cause of action for indemnity against Cross-Defendant. Namely, the cross complaint specifically alleges a that Cross-Defendant assented in a written a contract to establish a duty to indemnify Cross-Complainant for damages. The plain language of the indemnity provision applies to the damages involved in this case, as it arises out of and relates to the Premises. As a result, the Court finds the cross complaint states a cause of action for indemnity.

The cross complaint’s cause of action for indemnity in turn provides the basis for the fifth cause of action against Cross-Defendant for breach of contract. Specifically, the cross complaint alleges Cross-Defendant’s failure to indemnify Cross-Complainant, pursuant to their written agreement, constitutes a breach of contract. (First Amended Cross Complaint ¶¶ 18-25.) The cross complaint thus states a clear cause of action that Cross-Defendant has breached its contractual obligations to Cross-Complainant.

B. Application of Arbitration Agreement

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

The Court finds that the language of the arbitration provision in the Lease does not clearly apply to the causes of action in Cross-Complainant’s first amended cross complaint. Specifically, the plain language of the provision in the Lease requires claims, disputes, or disagreements “arising” under the Lease must be subject to arbitration. (First Amended Cross Complaint, Ex. A, para. 59(A).) The definition of “arising” is not clear. Cross-Defendant asserts the causes of action in this demurrer are clearly subject to the arbitration provision and arise under the Lease because the Lease is central to the determinations for each cause of action. The Court disagrees. The language of the arbitration provision is ambiguous as to where Cross-Complainant’s causes of action arise from; for instance, the causes of action could arise from Maria Martinez’s injury that gave rise to the original action.

Cross-Complainant here additionally argues that the plain language of the arbitration provision provides for exceptions that can apply here. Namely, the Lease identifies several categories of disputes “expressly excluded” from arbitration procedures, including “[d]isputes for which a different resolution determination is specifically set forth in [the] Lease.” (First Amended Cross Complaint, Ex. A, para. 59(b).) Pertinent to this exception is paragraph 13.2 of the Lease (Remedies) which provides that “if Lessee fails to perform any of its affirmative duties or obligations,” the Lessor may “[pursue] any other remedy now or hereafter available under the laws or judicial decisions of the state wherein the Premises is located.” (First Amended Cross Complaint, Ex. A, para. 13.2(c).)  The Court finds that Cross-Complainant provides a plausible construction of the Lease agreement’s arbitration provision, under which the cross complaint could be adjudicated in this Court. Because the Court finds Cross-Complainant’s legal theory is credible, the demurrer cannot be sustained. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.).

The Court notes that Cross-Defendant’s interpretation is a plausible reading of the arbitration provision of the Lease. However, the Court finds that the Lease is subject to multiple interpretations regarding its potential application. As a result, the Court finds that, on demurrer, the Court does not, as a matter of law, lack jurisdiction to consider Cross-Complainant’s causes of action for indemnity and breach of contract.

C. Waiver

Waiver of the right to compel arbitration generally requires a showing that the party seeking arbitration engaged in conduct inconsistent with the right to arbitrate, and that the conduct resulted in prejudice to the opposing party. Courts consider the totality of the parties' actions, including whether the litigation machinery has been substantially invoked, whether important intervening steps have taken place, and whether the delay has affected, misled, or prejudiced the opposing party, to determine whether waiver has occurred. (St. Agnes Medical Center v. PacifiCare of California (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187.) Courts look at whether the party's actions are inconsistent with the right to arbitrate. This includes engaging in discovery, filing motions, or otherwise participating in litigation (Bower v. Inter–Con Security Systems, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 1035.) The party opposing arbitration must demonstrate that they have been prejudiced by the delay in seeking arbitration. Prejudice in this context can result from incurring court costs and legal expenses, or from the opposing party gaining an advantage through litigation that would not be available in arbitration. A significant delay in seeking arbitration can also support finding waiver if the delay affected the opposing party's ability to benefit from arbitration as an expedient and cost-effective method of dispute resolution. (Diaz v. Professional Community Management, Inc. (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 1190.)

As this is a demurrer, the issue of waiver is not before the Court.  However, the Court here takes judicial notice of the age of this case; the original complaint was filed on April 9, 2019, so the instant action has been ongoing for nearly four years. This raises significant concerns to the Court that the litigation machinery has been invoked so substantially and with sufficient intervening steps to constitute a waiver of the right to arbitration.  While waiver is not addressed by way of this challenge to the pleadings, the Court finds that the potential or even likely finding of waiver argument further buttresses the conclusion that denial of the demurrer is appropriate.  Specifically, even if the terms of the arbitration agreement do apply to the subject causes of action, this Court may find that the right to arbitration was waived and that the Court could continue to exercise jurisdiction over those claims.  

D. Conflicting Rulings

Under Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 1281.2(c), if a party to an arbitration agreement is also involved in a pending court action with a third party arising out of the same transaction, and there is a possibility of producing conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact, the court has several options — including denying arbitration, staying the arbitration pending the resolution of the court action, or staying the court action pending the completion of arbitration. (Metis Development LLC v. Bohacek (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 679.) In Metis Development LLC v. Bohacek, the Court noted that the possibility of inconsistent rulings is not dispositive, and that the court has discretion to choose among its several options to avoid inconsistent rulings. (Id.) Thus, even if the Lease arbitration provision applies to the causes of action at issue in the instant demurrer, the Court here would nevertheless have the discretion to deny a motion to compel arbitration based on the potential that doing so would produce conflicting rulings. (See Abaya v. Spanish Ranch I, L.P. (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1490) (denying motion to compel arbitration as it risked conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact.)

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.  Cross-Defendant to answer the remaining allegations within 20 days.

Motion to Strike

Eduardo Tizoc v. George Anthony Moran, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant VCI Construction, LLC
Responding Party: Plaintiffs Eduardo Tizoc; Jocelyn Tizoc
Tentative Ruling: GRANT WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

BACKGROUND

This is a car accident case wherein defendant George Anthony Moran (“Moran), employed by Defendant, failed to stop at a traffic light intersection and collided with a car Plaintiff Eduardo Tizoc was a passenger in. Plaintiff Jocelyn Tizoc is making a loss of consortium claim. 

The Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleges Moran violated Defendant’s safe driving policies three times during his employment — on January 31, 2018, April 16, 2019, and May 31, 2019 — and established cause for his termination. (TAC ¶¶ 31–33.) Moreover, the TAC alleges that Defendant chose to delay suspending Moran for his third violation in the week prior to the accident here, thus allowing Moran to continue to operate a company vehicle on public roads and cause the subject accident of June 7, 2019. (TAC ¶¶ 34–39.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues the Court should grant the instant motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages claim on the basis that the TAC — though sufficient to allege negligence — is not enough to support punitive damages claim. (Motion p. 7.)
Defendant argues this motion is made on the grounds that Plaintiffs have not plead facts that Defendant’s behavior was fraudulent, oppressive or malicious, nor that it engaged in “despicable conduct.” (Motion p. 3.) Defendant also argues the facts pled in the TAC do not support a claim for punitive damages. (Id. at 4.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiffs argue the TAC alleges sufficient facts to supports injunctive relief, specifically on the basis that Defendant had advance knowledge that Moran was unfit to drive its truck and continued to employ him as a driver in conscious disregard of public safety. (Opposition, p. 6.)

REPLY

Defendant argues Moran’s prior driving conduct and any knowledge of that conduct by Defendant — even if proven — would not rise to the level of malice, fraud or oppression, but rather mere negligence. (Reply, p. 2.)

ANALYSIS

A court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

“[P]unitive damages can only be awarded “where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the Defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.” (Civil Code §3294(a).) Civil Code §3294 defines malice, oppression and fraud as follows: (1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights. (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material complaint known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Noonan v. Rousselot (1966) 239 Cal.App.2d 447, 452.) A motion to strike punitive damages allegations lies where the claim sued upon would not support an award of punitive damages, and the punitive damages claim is irrelevant, false or improper. (Code of Civil Procedure sections 435, 436.)

An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a) based upon acts of an employee unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation. (Civil Code §3294(a).)

It is appropriate here to grant Defendant’s motion to strike on the basis that the TAC’s allegations are far too vague to support a viable claim for punitive damages. Plaintiffs’ characterization of Defendant’s alleged conduct is insufficient to show oppression or malice—expressed or implied—within the meaning of Section 3294. (Nazar v. Rodeffer (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 546, 551.) Specifically, Plaintiff’s punitive damages claim relies on ambiguous allegations regarding Moran’s prior conduct as a driver, including allegations of driving under the influence and reckless driving, a hit and run collision, driving on a suspended license, running a red light, speeding, and being involved in a motor vehicle accident.  (TAC ¶¶ 23–30.) However, Plaintiff’s allegations conspicuously opt not to allege any specific facts regarding when any of Moran’s traffic violations actually occurred. Plaintiff further fails to allege whether any of the alleged violations occurred together (e.g., speeding while driving on a suspended license) or whether he was at fault for the motor vehicle accident.  While the Court is obligated to read Plaintiff’s complaint liberally, given that this is Plaintiff’s Third-Amended Complaint, the Court concludes that the lack of specificity was intentional.  

Plaintiff also alleges Moran violated Defendant’s company safety policies repeatedly — emphasizing the proximity of Moran’s violations to the time of the accident by alleging specific dates — but conspicuously leaves absent any factual allegations about the nature of Moran’s violations. (TAC ¶¶ 63–68.) In doing so, Plaintiff provides allegations so broad that to make any determination on their significance would be tantamount to baseless speculation. With no concrete nexus of factual allegations of Moran’s conduct, Court here finds that no significant inferences can be gleaned regarding Defendant’s oppression, fraud, or malice. 

Plaintiff here relies heavily on CRST, Inc. v. Superior Court (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1255 (“CRST”), to support his punitive damages claim. In CRST, the Court found that an employer who knows its employee is unfit to drive and, in violation of its policies, allows the employee to continue driving on its behalf, demonstrates a conscious disregard for others’ safety that is sufficient to support a punitive damages claim if the employee’s negligent driving causes an accident. (Id. at 1272.)  While this was the holding in CRST, it is important to analyze the facts in that case.  

The employer in CRST knew that its driver was involved in four preventable accidents over the short period of just seven months and had been pulled over for speeding and tailgating.  Additionally, CRST failed to follow its own safety procedures relating to criminal background checks, failed to investigate after later learning the driver had a criminal conviction, failed to make inquiries of prior employers which would have revealed multiple convictions for possession and use of illegal substances, a DUI and grand theft auto.  CRST also failed to maintain a federally required random drug testing policy, allowed the driver to drive without a communication system and let him drive without a required co-driver.  Despite this laundry list of explosive facts, the Court discounted most of them.  Regarding the four preventable accidents in seven months, the Court observed:

“We assess CRST's “advance knowledge” and “conscious disregard” in light of its policies, which required the termination of a driver for serious traffic violations or causing six preventable accidents within an eight-month period. In view of these policies, CRST did not act improperly by retaining Contreras, even though he may have been involved in as many as four preventable accidents, because nothing in the record suggests that they were serious.” (Id. at 1271.)

The Court also discounted the driver’s checkered past, explaining, “[a]lthough Contreras had a lengthy record of substance abuse, poor driving, and criminal activity up to 2007, it is undisputed that CRST complied with federal regulations in conducting the preemployment background check and did not discover those facts regarding Contreras. We therefore examine whether CRST acquired knowledge of Contreras's unfitness as a driver after he was hired, yet improperly continued to employ him.” (Id.)

Ultimately, the Court rested its finding on just one incident – the speeding and tailgating allegation.  “The evidence regarding Contreras's traffic citation, however, raises triable material issues regarding CRST's “advance knowledge” and “conscious disregard.” … Contreras was issued a [traffic] citation in 2014. According to Oliver, although Contreras was tailgating and speeding while driving through a construction zone, the officer issued a ticket only for a seatbelt-related violation. Oliver allegedly reported the incident to Davis. Davis denied hearing any such report from Oliver, but acknowledged that speeding in a construction zone or a seatbelt-related violation could support a driver's termination. In our view, Oliver's testimony, if credited by a jury, is sufficient to show that CRST had “advance knowledge of [Contreras's] unfitness ... and employed him ... with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Id. at 218.)

Plaintiff’s TAC here is distinguishable because it fails to provide specific allegations, as opposed to conclusory allegations, that reasonably support the claim conclusion that CRST had advance knowledge that Moran was unfit to drive.  Plaintiff leaves the period of when Moran’s traffic violations occurred completely undefined and likewise fails to detail Moran’s violations of Defendant’s safety rules.  As such, the Court finds no basis to support Plaintiff’s often repeated allegations that “it was highly foreseeable and likely that Moran would cause a collision and injure innocent persons including Plaintiff.”   

The Court accordingly finds that no legal significance can be gleaned in either category of Plaintiff’s allegations regarding Moran’s prior conduct as a driver. As a result, the Court concludes that the TAC fails to allege facts on which a claim for punitive damages can be made, as no meaningful inference of Defendant’s conscious disregard or malice can be concluded.

RULING

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED.  While Plaintiff contends that additional facts have been discovered to support its claims, no such showing was made.  As such, the motion is granted without leave to amend.

On February 22, 2024 Defendants served an initial set of written discovery, which included Form Interrogatories, Set One (“Interrogatories”) on Plaintiff Shahid Shaikh (“Plaintiff”). I. Motions to Compel Discovery

Written responses to form interrogatories must be made under oath. (Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.210(a), 2030.250(a).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection to the interrogatories. (Section 2030.290(a).) It is appropriate here to grant Defendants’ motion to compel. Plaintiff here has failed to provide timely responses to Defendants’ Interrogatories. (Fierro Decl. ¶5.) Demurrer

Jane Y.G. Doe v. Residences on Main L.P., et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Residences on Main, L.P.; Residences on Main, G.P.; Sonny’s Construction, LLC, (“SCL”); Coalition of Responsible Community Development 
Responding Party: Plaintiff Jane Doe
Tentative Ruling: OVERRULED

BACKGROUND 

This action arises from a sexual assault by third party Christopher Hicks on Plaintiff on July 24, 2021, at 6901 South Main Street, Los Angeles, CA 90003 (“Premises”; FAC ¶¶ 20, 30–42.) Plaintiff sought remedies for the incident by initiating proceedings with the Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board (“WCAB”), which remains ongoing. Plaintiff initiated this Superior Court action on December 3, 2021, based on the same remedies sought in the WCAB proceeding. On July 3, 2024, this Court granted LA Family Housing Corporation’s (“LAFH”) Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, which was joined by Defendants. This Court found that (1) the Privette doctrine barred Plaintiff’s claims, (2) the primary assumption of risk barred Plaintiff’s claims, and (3) no duty of care existed with respect to third-party criminal conduct. The Court’s findings were based upon facts alleged in Plaintiff’s initial complaint; specifically, that Plaintiff was an employee of GTO Security, a company that the Defendants contracted with to provide security services on the Premises. (Complaint ¶¶ 16, 18, 23, 24). This Court granted Plaintiff leave to amend. Plaintiff subsequently filed her First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on July 23, 2024. 

In her FAC, Plaintiff named 11 entities and individuals as defendants, including Defendants here, alleging their acts or omissions — whether individually, jointly, and/or severally — were a proximate cause of her assault. (FAC ¶¶ 2 15). Specifically, Plaintiff alleged that Defendants were aware that Plaintiff’s assailant had previously made physical threats to other residents of the building (FAC ¶ 32), failed to advise Plaintiff of the danger (FAC ¶54), were informed by Plaintiff that her assailant was on the premises and that he made her uncomfortable and disregarded her fears and did not address her concerns. (FAC ¶¶ 34–35.) Plaintiff’s FAC also alleges they did not purchase workers compensation insurance for her benefit. (FAC ¶ 24.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendants argue Plaintiff is unable to plead around the deficiencies of her initial Complaint. Plaintiff provides no explanation of mistake or excuse where her FAC contradicts harmful facts in the initial Complaint and instead offers up legal conclusions which are unsupported by any new facts. (Demurrer, p. 4.) Defendants specifically assert Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the Privette doctrine and primary assumption of risk doctrine. (Id. at 6, 9.) Additionally, Defendants argue they had no duty to protect against third party criminal conduct. (Id. at 10.) Defendants also argue that alternative theories of liability relying on agency and vicarious liability fail. (Id. at 8.) Lastly, Defendants argue Plaintiff’s FAC is a sham pleading. (Id. at 12.)

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues she has pleaded sufficient facts to state a cause of action against all Defendants. (Opposition, p. 4.) Plaintiff asserts her allegations provide support for multiple theories of liability that do not implicate the Privette doctrine, and are sufficient to allege joint-venture liability against all Defendants. (Id. at 5, 7.) Plaintiff challenges Defendants’ arguments regarding the primary assumption-of-the-risk doctrine, arguing they incorrectly presume Plaintiff was an employee of “Defendants’ contractor,” and argues her allegations are sufficient to establish that Defendants owed her a duty. (Id. at 8–9.)

REPLY

No reply has been filed.

ANALYSIS 

I. Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) 

The demurrers do not contend that Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts to generically support causes of action for negligence or premises liability.  Rather, the demurrers argue that Plaintiff’s claims fail based on procedural or other legal grounds.  They will be addressed in turn. 

a. Sham Pleading

California law generally allows plaintiffs to plead inconsistent causes of action in separate counts when the exact nature of the facts or the legal rights are in doubt. (Batta v. Hunt (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 295.) This practice accommodates uncertainties about what actually occurred or what can be established by the evidence. (Id.) However, there are limitations. A plaintiff may not avoid a demurrer by pleading facts or positions in an amended complaint that contradict the facts pleaded in the original complaint or by suppressing facts that prove the pleaded facts false (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 857; McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457.) “Under the sham pleading doctrine, allegations in an original pleading that rendered it vulnerable to demurrer or other attack cannot simply be omitted without explanation.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 751.) “The purpose of the doctrine is to enable the courts to prevent an abuse of process.” (Id.) “The doctrine is not intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations or to prevent the correction of ambiguous facts.” (Id.)

The FAC in this case does not amount to a sham pleading. Plaintiff is entitled to allege alternative facts to accommodate the uncertainty in this case. Namely, a great deal of uncertainty exists as to Plaintiff’s actual employer, the extent of ownership control over the Premises by the parties, and the scope of the security agreement. Alternative factual allegations relying on alternative legal theories are permissible — provided the allegations do not contain antagonistic or contradictory facts. (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936.) Plaintiff’s FAC does provide a wide swathe of factual allegations to support different legal theories; however, the FAC does not include allegations of fact that are mutually exclusive or antagonistic. 

b. The Privette Doctrine

The Privette doctrine generally holds that a hirer is not liable for injuries sustained by an independent contractor or its employees while on the job, as the responsibility for workplace safety is typically delegated to the contractor. (Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021)12 Cal.5th 29; Acosta v. MAS Realty, LLC (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 635.)

There are two primary exceptions to the Privette doctrine: Retained Control and Concealed Hazard. Under the Retained Control Exception, a hirer may be liable if it retains control over any part of the contractor's work and negligently exercises that control in a manner that affirmatively contributes to the worker's injury. (Ramirez v. PK I Plaza 580 SC LP (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 252 (2022); Sandoval v. Qualcomm Incorporated (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256.) The Concealed Hazard Exception exposes a hirer to liability if it knew or should have known of a concealed hazard on the property that the contractor did not know of and could not have reasonably discovered, and the hirer fails to warn the contractor of the hazard. (Ramirez v. PK I Plaza 580 SC LP (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 252.)
Here, Plaintiff alleges in the FAC that each of the demurring parties owned, managed, leased, controlled and operated the Premises (FAC ¶ 20), were aware that Plaintiff’s assailant had previously made physical threats to other residents of the building (FAC ¶ 32) and failed to advise Plaintiff of the danger that her assailant presented. (FAC ¶ 54). These allegations of fact establish a theory of liability that does not implicate the Privette doctrine — namely under the Concealed Hazard exception. (Ramirez v. PK I Plaza 580 SC LP (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 252.) Defendants argue that hazardous conditions refers to a physical danger or defect in the property; however, Defendants’ cited case, Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29 does not make any ruling supporting this proposition — nor do Defendants explain how the analysis in Gonzalez applies here.

Moreover, the FAC alleges that Defendants retained control of all or part of Plaintiff’s work and “negligently exercised that control in a manner that affirmatively contributed to her injury.” (FAC ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants controlled when she worked at the Premises and gave instructions on what Plaintiff would need to do while performing her work there. (FAC ¶ 25.) Plaintiff’s allegations, taken as true, support a basis for negligence liability that does not implicate the Privette doctrine under the Retained Control exception. (Ramirez v. PK I Plaza 580 SC LP (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 252 (2022); Sandoval v. Qualcomm Incorporated (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256.) These allegations allege conduct by Defendants that falls below reasonable care. Thus, the FAC alleges sufficient facts to state a cause of action against the Defendants outside the applicability of the Privette doctrine. 

c. Primary Assumption of Risk

Under the primary assumption of risk doctrine, the defendant owes no duty to protect the plaintiff from particular harms arising from ordinary or simple negligence. (Truong v. Nguyen (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 865; Shin v. Ahn (2007) 42 Cal.4th 482.) This is because the plaintiff is considered to have assumed the inherent risks of the activity by choosing to participate. (Truong v. Nguyen (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 865; Shin v. Ahn (2007) 42 Cal.4th 482.) The application of the doctrine depends on the nature of the sport or activity in question and the parties' general relationship to the activity. (Peart v. Ferro (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 60; Moser v. Ratinoff (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1211.) The court evaluates the fundamental nature of the activity and the defendant's role in or relationship to that activity to determine whether the defendant owes a duty to protect the plaintiff from the particular risk of harm. (Peart v. Ferro (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 60.)

Thus, whether the primary assumption of risk doctrine applies to a security guard attacked while on duty will depend on whether the risk of such an attack is considered inherent to the job and whether the employer or other parties increased the risk beyond what is inherent in the job. (Hamilton v. Martinelli & Associates (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1012.) In the context of a security guard, if the attack is considered an inherent risk of the job, the primary assumption of risk doctrine may bar Plaintiff’s claim against her employer or other parties. (See, e.g., Tilley v. CZ Master Assn. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 464) (concluding the primary assumption of risk doctrine applied to a security guard who was assaulted while on duty as confronting such risks was part of the job.)

The doctrine of primary assumption of risk has also been applied to employment-related activities. For instance, in Herrle v. Estate of Marshall (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1761, 53 Cal.Rptr.2d 713, the plaintiff was a nurse's aide employed at a convalescent hospital, who was regularly exposed to patients with Alzheimer's disease who could be combative and violent. She was injured when one of these patients struck her. (Id. at pp. 1763–1764, 53 Cal.Rptr.2d 713.) The Herrle court held that the plaintiff assumed the risk of her injuries, as the “plaintiff, by the very nature of her profession, placed herself in a position where she assumed the duty to take care of patients who were potentially violent and to protect such patients from committing acts which might injure others.” (Herrle, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at p. 1766, 53 Cal.Rptr.2d 713.) Thus, the doctrine of primary assumption of risk applies where a defendant is impliedly relieved of any duty of care by the plaintiff's acceptance of employment “involving a known risk or danger.” (Farnam v. State of California (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1448, 1452, 101 Cal.Rptr.2d 642.)

Here, the primary assumption of risk doctrine does not bar Plaintiff’s claims because Plaintiff’s acceptance of employment here did not involve a risk or danger that was “known” to her — and thus Defendants have not been impliedly relieved of their duty. Here, the FAC alleges that she did not expect or have any reason to expect she would be required to confront or otherwise physically engage anyone on the Premises for any reason. (Complaint ¶ 26.) Rather, Plaintiff alleges her job was to observe and report crime. (Complaint ¶ 26.) There is case law to suggest that security officers with similar responsibilities may expressly assume the risk of being assaulted by third parties under particular circumstances. (See, Tilley v. CZ Master Assn. (2005) 131 Cal.App.4th 464, 469.) However, the facts alleged here do not support such a conclusion. Based on the pleadings in the FAC, the Court declines to conclude that Plaintiff “by the very nature of her profession” placed herself in a position where she assumed a duty to physically confront assailants or endure rape. The primary assumption of risk doctrine thus does not apply to Plaintiff’s claims under the facts alleged in the FAC against Defendants here.

d. Third Party Criminal Conduct

In Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal.4th 666, the California Supreme Court analyzed the scope of a landlord's duty to provide protection from foreseeable third party crime.  The Court explained its position:

“[W]e observe that, before and after our decision in Isaacs, we have recognized that the scope of the duty is determined in part by balancing the foreseeability of the harm against the burden of the duty to be imposed. In cases where the burden of preventing future harm is great, a high degree of foreseeability may be required. On the other hand, in cases where there are strong policy reasons for preventing the harm, or the harm can be prevented by simple means, a lesser degree of foreseeability may be required. Or, as one appellate court has accurately explained, duty in such circumstances is determined by a balancing of “foreseeability” of the criminal acts against the burdensomeness, vagueness, and efficacy of the proposed security measures.”  (Id. at 678-679 (internal citations and quotes omitted).)  

Here, the Complaint alleges that “Defendants were aware that HICKS had previously made physical threats to residents of the SUBECT PREMISES, and thus knew his presence on the property was a hazard.  Plaintiff did not know, and was not informed, that HICKS was a danger and thus his presence was a concealed danger at the SUBJECT PREMISES for which Defendants could of and should have warned Plaintiff of the danger.”  (Compl., ¶32.) 

These allegations, assumed to be true, are sufficient to trigger a duty on behalf of Defendants to, at the very least, warn Plaintiff of physical threats by Hicks.  As such, Plaintiff complaint does not fail based on intervening 3rd party criminal acts.  

RULING

Based on the foregoing, the Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.  Defendants to answer within 20 days.

Case Number: 22STCV06771    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: T

Motions to Compel Discovery

Yves Benaroch v. Sharli Hayun, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Sharli Hayun; Kfir Hayun
Responding Party: N/A
Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

This is a car collision case arising from a February 25, 2020 incident. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶ 3; Index of Exhibits, Ex. A.) On December 29, 2023, Defendants filed their answer to Plaintiff Yves Benaroch’s complaint. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶ 8; Index of Exhibits, Ex. B.) On June 10, 2024, Defendants served the Requests for Admission, Form Interrogatories (Set One), and Requests for Production on Plaintiff. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶ 5; Index of Exhibits, Ex. C.) 

Defendants’ Requests for Admission seek that the following facts are true: 

(1) Plaintiff operated a car involved in the accident and “the operator cannot establish his or her financial responsibility as required by the financial responsibility laws of this state.” 
(2) The insurance policy claimed by Plaintiff was not issued to Cedric Benaroch. 
(3) The insurance policy claimed by Plaintiff does not cover the car Plaintiff was driving when the accident occurred. 
(Index of Exhibits, Ex. C.)

Plaintiff failed to provide responses to Defendants’ discovery. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶6.) On August 27, 2024, counsel for Defendants requested Plaintiff provide responses to the propounded discovery by September 3, 2024. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶7; Index of Exhibits, Ex. D.) Defendants did not receive any response to this email, nor to their propounded discovery. 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendants argue they are entitled to the information sought in their Requests for Production and Form Interrogatories (Set One), and Plaintiff’s failure to provide responses justifies granting their motions to compel responses without objection. (Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production, p. 4; Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, p. 4.) Defendants argue Plaintiff’s failure to provide responses to the Requests for Admission constitutes a response deeming the requests for admission as admitted. (Motion to Deem Responses Admitted to Requests for Admission, p. 4.)

Additionally, Defendants request $1,110.00 in sanctions for the costs incurred as to each motion. On each motion, Defendants assert 5 hours worked at a $210.00 hourly rate — including time spent drafting each motion in addition to anticipated time required to review and reply to any opposition and to prepare for and attend each hearing — in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶ 8).

OPPOSITION

No opposition has been filed to any of the instant motions.

ANALYSIS

I. Motions to Compel Discovery

Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.010 allows a party to propound a written request for document production, to be answered under oath. This discovery may be served by a defendant at any time, without leave of court. (C.C.P. § 2031.020(a).) Written responses to the document production request must also include a statement asserting that the party will comply, that the party cannot comply, or alternatively objecting to the request. (C.C.P. § 2031.210(a).) Responsive documents shall be produced on the date specified in the demand, or other date agreed by the parties. (C.C.P. § 2031.280(d).) If a party fails to respond to a document production request, the requesting party may move for an order compelling a response. (C.C.P. § 2031.310(b).) 

Written responses to form interrogatories must be made under oath. (C.C.P. §§ 2030.210(a), 2030.250(a).) The response must be served within thirty days after the interrogatories are served on the responding party. (C.C.P. § 2030.260(a).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection to the interrogatories. (C.C.P. § 2030.290(a).) If a party fails to respond to form interrogatories, the party serving the discovery may move for an order compelling a response. (C.C.P. §2030.300(a).) The court shall also impose monetary sanctions against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel a response, unless it finds the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

“If a party to whom requests for admission have been directed fails to serve a timely response ... [t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).” (C.C.P. § 2033.280(b).) A nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein. (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal. 4th 973, 979.)

The Court finds it is appropriate to grant all of Defendants’ motions to compel in this case. Plaintiff here has failed to provide responses to Defendants’ propounded Requests for Production, Form Interrogatories, and Requests for Admission. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶6.) Here, Defendants’ Form Interrogatories seek basic information from Plaintiff regarding the incident at issue, as well as Plaintiff’s basic identifying details and insurance information — information Defendants are entitled to. (Index of Exhibits, Ex. C.) Defendants’ Request for Production similarly requests basic pertinent information in the form of a copy of Plaintiffs’ auto insurance declarations page. (Index of Exhibits, Ex. C.) Plaintiff’s failure to provide responses in turn prevents Defendants from conducting essential discovery they are entitled to. The Court therefore agrees with Defendants that requiring Plaintiff to provide responses to Defendants’ unanswered Requests for Production and Form Interrogatories (Set One) is justified. Moreover, due to Plaintiff’s failure to respond, the Court concludes that the truth of the matters contained in Defendants’ Requests for Admission must be deemed admitted. (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal. 4th 973, 979.)

II. Sanctions

Defendants request $1,110.00 in sanctions for each motion, attesting to 5 hours worked at a $210.00 hourly rate from the time already spent bringing each motion in addition to the time Defendants’ anticipate working — in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Declaration of Anthony Bell, ¶ 8). Defendants’ counsel asserts he spent 2 hours preparing each motion, anticipated another 3 hours to review opposing papers, reply as needed, prepare for and attend the hearing as to each motion. (Id.)

The Court finds Defendants’ $210.00 hourly rate is reasonable. Defendants are also entitled to recover for the $60 filing fee incurred in bringing each motion, for a total of $180.00.

However, the Court reduces Defendants’ total number of hours worked from 5 hours on each motion to 2 hours — 1.5 hours in preparing each motion and 1.5 hours to prepare for and attend the hearing — for a total of 6 hours, or $1,260.00. Defendants’ motions are uncomplicated, unopposed, and boilerplate; the language and substance of Defendants’ motions overlap considerably. The Court’s reduction thus reflects the reasonable number of hours spent preparing each motion, and the likely anticipated additional work Defendants’ counsel is likely to spend. Defendants are thus entitled sanctions in the amount of $1,440.00 total, or $480.00 for the reasonable costs for each of Defendants’ three motions. 

RULING

Based on the foregoing, the Court rules as follows:

Defendants’ motion to compel responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One) is GRANTED. Defendants’ request for sanctions pertaining to the motion is GRANTED in the amount of $480.00.  Plaintiff to serve code-compliant responses without objections within 20 days of this order.

Defendants’ motion to compel responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED. Defendants’ request for sanctions pertaining to the motion is GRANTED in the amount of $480.00. Plaintiff to serve code-compliant responses without objections within 20 days of this order.

Defendants’ motion to deem responses admitted to Requests for Admission is GRANTED. Defendants’ request for sanctions pertaining to the motion is GRANTED in the amount of $480.00.

Case Number: 24NNCV01970    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer

Sarkis Gezalyan v. Azat Gezalyan, et al.

Moving Party: Cross-Defendants Sarkis Gezalyan; Susana Muradian
Responding Party: Cross-Complainants Azat Gezalyan; Shapiro Ventures LLC.
Tentative Ruling: SUSTAIN, IN PART; OVERRULE, IN PART

BACKGROUND 

The Cross-Complainant alleges that in 2023, Cross-Defendants fraudulently procured several loans in the name of Cross-Complainant company Shapiro Ventures, LLC, in which Cross-Complainant Azat Gezalyan (“Azat”) and Cross-Defendant Sarkis Gezalyan (“Sarkis”) were joint venturers. They also allege that Sarkis perpetuated a Ponzi scheme using personal and third-party money in as collateral, with interest, to force Azat out of the business and acquire his equity for himself. The Cross-Complaint alleges that Sarkis embezzled funds, creating false claims, documents and accounts. (See generally, Cross-Complaint; Demurrer, p. 3.)  The only operative factual allegation relating to Cross-Defendant Muradian is that she is married to Sarkis.

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendants argue that the Court should sustain their demurrer on the basis that the Cross-Complaint fails to state any allegations as to the “embezzled funds, created false claims, documents and accounts” and specifically fails to make any allegation as to "how it was embezzled, what was embezzled, what false claims were made on what documents and on what accounts.” (Demurrer, p. 3.) 

As a result, Defendants argue the Cross-Complaint fails to properly plead the causes of action is alleged. Specifically, the first and second causes of action for breach of contract, third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, fourth cause of action for conversion, fifth cause of action for violation of Penal Code section 496, sixth cause of action for unjust enrichment, seventh cause of action for accounting, and eighth cause of action for injunctive relief. (Id. at 4–8.)

OPPOSITION 

Cross-Complainants argue that the facts pled in the Cross-Complaint are sufficient to support their causes of action and meet the requirements overrule a demurrer. (Amended Opposition, p. 3.) Cross-Complainants alternatively request leave to amend if the Court finds the Cross-Complaint is not sufficiently pled. (Id. at 4.)

REPLY

Defendants emphasize that Cross-Complainants “provided no indication as to what investigations have they done, what documents they are referencing, and what third party assets have been misappropriated.” (Reply, p. 3.) Defendants emphasize that “[e]very allegation within their Cross-Complaint is ambiguously and vaguely stated without any supporting evidence.” (Id.)

ANALYSIS 

Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  Parties may also demur to causes of action individually.  In either case, the procedure is also called a ‘demurrer for sufficiency,’ and it tests the pleading by itself, alone and disconnected from extraneous matters.  (Hahn at 747.) 

A complaint is required to give the defendant notice of the issues sufficient to enable preparation of the defense. (Ponte San Diego Residential Community, LP. v. Procopio, Cory, Hargreaves & Smith, LLP (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 265, 278.) Mere recital, or reference to, material facts that are left to surmise are subject to demurrer for uncertainty. (Bernstein v. Piller (1950) 98 Cal.App.2d 441, 443-444.) Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and granted only if the pleading is so unclear that the defendant cannot reasonably understand the issues to be met. (A.J. Fistes Corp. v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695.) The Court addresses each cause of action in turn.

I. Breach of Contract 

“If the action is based on an alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written instrument must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Otworth v. S. Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 459.) Here, the Cross-Complaint does not attach (or even identify) the contract at issue and does not recite verbatim the terms of the contract. As a result, the Cross-Complaint is deficient with respect to this cause of action.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the first cause of action.

II. Breach of Implied Contract

The breach of implied contract cause of action is deficient on similar grounds as the first cause of action; namely, the Cross-Complaint alleges facts too vague to put Defendants on notice of what contract is implied, and by extension what actions Defendants took in violation of a potential implied contract. While the Cross-Complaint does allege Cross-Defendant took possession of monetary proceeds delivered to them by third parties, the Court finds this allegation is not, by itself, sufficient to support a breach of implied contract cause of action. (Cross Complaint, p. 5.) Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the second cause of action. 

III. Breach of Fiduciary Duty 

A fiduciary duty arises “when one person enters into a confidential relationship with another.” (Hasso v. Hapke (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 107, 140.) Fiduciary duties are also imposed as a matter of law in certain technical, legal relationships. (Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App. 4th 1315, 1339.) "In all proceedings connected with the conduct of the partnership every partner is bound to act in the highest good faith to his copartner and may not obtain any advantage over him in the partnership affairs by the slightest misrepresentation, concealment, threat or adverse pressure of any kind." (Enea v. Sup. Ct. (3-D) (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1559, 1564.)

The Cross-Complaint here pleads sufficient facts to support this cause of action. Where allegations are sufficiently clear to apprise a defendant of the issues — even if not as detailed as might be desired — the demurrer should be overruled. (Bacon v. Wahrhaftig (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 599, 605.) In this instance, the Cross-complaint provides several specific allegations as to how Defendant Sarkis allegedly violated a fiduciary duty to Cross-Complainants, including embezzling money and secretly incurring debt in Cross-Complainants’ name without their consent. (Cross Complaint, p. 5–6.)   These allegations are sufficient to support a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.  As a result, the demurrer is overruled as to the third cause of action.

IV. Conversion

Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over another person's personal property. (See Fremont Indem. Co. v. Fremont Gen. Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.) Conversion requires a defendant to have committed a wrongful act or disposed of the property in a manner inconsistent with the plaintiff's property rights, causing damages to plaintiff. (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1141; Berry v. Frazier (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1258.) Conversion is a strict liability tort, meaning that the defendant's knowledge, intent, good faith, or motive are generally immaterial to the claim. (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202.) 

The Cross-Complaint pleads sufficient facts to support the fourth cause of action for conversion as to Sarkis, but not as to Cross-Defendant Muradian. The Cross-Complaint alleges sufficiently specific facts as to Defendant Sarkis’ involvement, such as embezzling Cross-Complainants’ money. (Cross Complaint, p. 5–6.) 

However, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege any facts regarding Cross-Defendant Muradian’s potential involvement. While Defendant Muradian need only intentionally take an action inconsistent with Cross-Complainants’ property rights, the Cross-Complaint here provides no allegation whatsoever as to what action she potentially took that implicated Cross-Complainants' rights. Cross-Defendant Muradian is alleged to have interfered with Cross-Complainants’ ownership interest by “misappropriating, embezzling and converting” business proceeds, relying singularly on the operative factual allegation that she is married to Sarkis; it does not allege Cross-Defendant Muradian is an owner, agent, or manager involved in Shapiro Ventures. (Cross Complaint, p. 3.) Cross-Defendant Muradian’s marriage to Sarkis is not sufficient to put her on notice of what action she took that constitutes conversion, nor how such conversion occurred. As a result, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled with respect to Sarkis and sustained as to Cross-Defendant Muradian.

V. Violation of Penal Code Section 496

To violate Penal Code § 496, the property in question must be stolen or obtained through theft or extortion. Additionally, the accused must have received, concealed, sold, withheld, or aided in concealing, selling, or withholding the property from the owner, and must have known that the property was stolen or obtained through theft or extortion. (Penal Code§ 496; People v. Coca (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 451.)

The Cross-Complaint pleads sufficient facts to support the fifth cause of action for violation of Penal Code section 496 as to Sarkis, but not as to Cross-Defendant Muradian. The Cross-Complaint here alleges Sarkis covertly incurred debts in Cross-Complainants’ name and embezzled funds for his personal benefit. (Cross Complaint, p. 5–6.) This is sufficient to give notice to Sarkis as to the nature of the issue involved in this cause of action.

Conversely, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege sufficient facts as to Cross-Defendant Muradian’s potential involvement in violating Penal Code Section 496. Cross-Complainants’ amended opposition states that the Cross-Complaint pleads sufficient facts — stating that in 2016 Cross-Defendant Muradian “fraudulently executed a grant deed” with the intent and purpose of deceiving Cross-Complainant Azat Gezalyan, and alleges that Defendants collected, and misappropriated Cross-Complainants’ money. (Opposition, p. 7.) This allegation was not contained in the Cross-Complaint. The Court accordingly finds the Cross-Complaint to be unjustifiably vague as to Cross-Defendant Muradian as to this cause of action. As a result, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is overruled with respect to Sarkis and sustained as to Defendant Muradian.

VI. Unjust Enrichment 

The elements of a cause of action for unjust enrichment are the receipt of a benefit and the unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another. (Professional Tax Appeal v. Kennedy-Wilson Holdings, Inc. (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 230.)

The Cross-Complaint pleads sufficient facts to support the sixth cause of action for unjust enrichment as to Sarkis, but not as to Cross-Defendant Muradian. The reasoning as to this cause of action is identical to the Court’s reasoning as to the cause of action for conversion, and demands the same result. Namely, that the demurrer to the sixth cause of action is overruled with respect to Sarkis and sustained as to Cross-Defendant Muradian.

VII. Accounting

A cause of action for accounting requires a relationship between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that a balance is due to the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting. (Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861.) The purpose of an accounting is to discover how much money, if any, is owed to the plaintiff. (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156.)

The Cross-Complaint here pleads sufficient facts to support the seventh cause of action for accounting as to Sarkis, but not as to Cross-Defendant Muradian. The Cross-Complaint alleges Sarkis’ involvement and partnership in a business venture with Cross-Complainants, and that money owed to Cross-Complainants was misappropriated. The Cross-Complaint thus alleges facts as to both the issues on the relationship with Cross-Complainants, as well as an alleged misappropriation that requires an accounting. (Cross Complaint, p. 3–4.) However, the Cross-Complaint fails to make allegations about the relationship linking Cross-Defendant Muradian to Cross-Complainants that creates a duty for an accounting. Cross-Complainants do not allege, for instance, that Defendant Muradian is an owner or partner of the business. Accordingly, the demurrer to the seventh cause of action is overruled with respect to Sarkis and sustained as to Defendant Muradian.

VIII. Injunctive relief

Injunctions are equitable in nature and the general purpose of prejudgment injunctive relief is to preserve the status quo pending a determination on the merits. (Jamison v. Department of Transport. (2016) 4 CA5th 356, 36.) Courts weigh two factors in considering injunctive relief, including the likelihood of success on the merits, and the balance of harms. (Department of Fair Employment and Housing v. Superior Court of Kern County (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 356; Tulare Lake Canal Company v. Stratford Public Utility District (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 380.) A preliminary injunction can be granted even if the complaint is subject to a general or special demurrer, and any defects in the complaint can be corrected by filing an amended complaint showing good grounds for the injunction. (Handyspot Co. of Northern Cal. v. Buegeleisen (1954) 128 Cal.App.2d 191.) The court has the discretion to grant a preliminary injunction based on a verified complaint, affidavits, or oral testimony. (People v. Black's Food Store (1940) 16 Cal.2d 59.)

The Cross-Complaint here pleads sufficient facts to support the eighth cause of action for injunctive relief as to Sarkis, but not as to Cross-Defendant Muradian. The reasoning here is similar to the Court’s reasoning as to the seventh cause of action and demands the same result. Namely, that the demurrer as to the eighth cause of action is overruled with respect to Sarkis and sustained as to Cross-Defendant Muradian.

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, the Court rules as follows:

As to Defendant Sarkis Gezalyan, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the first and second causes of action for breach of contract and breach of implied contract, and OVERRULED as to the remaining causes of action.

As to Defendant Susana Muradian, Cross-Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to causes of action four through eight.

Cross-Complainants are granted 20 days leave to amend.
