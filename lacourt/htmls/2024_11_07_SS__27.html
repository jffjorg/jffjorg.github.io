Case Number: 20STCV42239    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT¿OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 11/7/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV42239 WALTER RAGSDALE vs COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES et al.

Moving Party: Defendant County of Los Angeles

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: COURT TO HEAR FROM PARTIES 

 

On November 4, 2020, Plaintiff Walter Ragsdale filed the present complaint, alleging a dangerous condition of public property against the County of Los Angeles. Plaintiff claims he was injured while sitting in the lobby area of the probation office located at 1660 W. Mission Blvd., Pomona, CA 91766 ("Subject Property"), waiting for his probation officer, when his chair suddenly collapsed. Defendant now moves the Court for summary judgment, asserting that Plaintiff’s discovery responses are factually devoid and that the County had no actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition.

In its opposition to the motion, Plaintiff contends he has other necessary discovery to do to adequately oppose the motion.  The Court will discuss this issue with the parties.

 


Case Number: 22STCV0339    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION TO VACATE

Hearing Date: 11/7/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV30339 PORTIA KANEABDULLAH vs AYSIA MONET RANDALL, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient

 

Ruling: GRANTED

 

Background

On September 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present case. On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement, stating that the entire case had been settled. The Court set an OSC re dismissal for September 18, 2024.  On that date, no one appeared nor did anyone file any declaration regarding the status of the settlement.  Left with no appearances and no good cause shown for the action to remain active, the Court, on its own motion, dismissed the matter.

Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that he intended to appear and inform the Court that the matter had not yet settled and that Plaintiff wished to set a trial date for late 2025. However, due to a calendaring error, specifically a mis-entry in the appearing attorney’s calendar, counsel did not appear. Plaintiff now moves the Court to vacate its September 18, 2024, dismissal.

Discussion

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

        Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision, and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616.)  ¿¿ 

To gain the statutory benefits, a party or legal representative must seek such relief “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); see Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980 (Rappleyea).   

“The¿mandatory¿relief provision of section 473(b) is a “narrow exception to the discretionary relief provision for default judgments and¿dismissals.” [Citation.] Its purpose “‘was to alleviate the hardship on parties who¿lose their day in court¿due solely to an inexcusable failure to act on the part of their attorneys.’” [Citation.] An application for¿mandatory¿relief must be filed within six months of entry of judgment and be in proper form, accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. [Citation.] The defaulting party “must submit sufficient evidence that the default was actually caused by the attorney's error. [Citation.] ‘If the prerequisites for the application of the¿mandatory¿relief provision of section 473, subdivision (b) exist, the trial court does not have discretion to refuse relief.’” [Citation.]” (Henderson v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co.¿(2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 215, 226.) 

All requirements for mandatory relief have been met. The dismissal occurred on September 18, 2024, and the motion was filed on October 8, 2024, within the six-month deadline. Dismissal is a type of court order that falls under the narrow exception for mandatory relief. The motion includes counsel’s declaration attesting to his mistake, and the court dismissed the case due to this error—specifically, counsel’s failure to inform the court that the settlement was no longer in effect and that the parties intended to proceed with litigation. Thus, the motion is granted. A Trial Setting Conference is hereby scheduled for November __, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

MOTION TO QUASH AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 11/7/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03495 JOSIAH ORELLANA, et al. vs OVIG VARTANIAN, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiffs Josiah Orellana and Vanessa Smith

Responding Party: Defendant Ovig Vartanian

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO QUASH IS MOOT AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED 

 

This motion concerns workers’ compensation subpoenas served in a personal injury case. Defendant alleges that the subpoenas have been withdrawn and reissued with the correct forms. Plaintiffs Josiah Orellana and Vanessa Smith acknowledge this in their reply, rendering the motion moot. However, the issue of sanctions remains.

Timeline

On September 20, 2024, Defendant issued subpoenas to nine facilities seeking Plaintiffs’ employment and medical records, with a production date of October 15, 2024. Motion to quash subpoena must be served at least 5 days before the date set for production of the subpoenaed records. (CCP § 1985.3.) Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ deadline to file a motion to quash was October 10, 2024.

On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel met and conferred with defense counsel, alerting them that the subpoenas were incorrectly served on a form intended for the “Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board.” Plaintiff’s counsel requested that the subpoenas be withdrawn.

On October 2, 2024, Defense counsel confirmed that the subpoena vendor would withdraw the incorrect subpoenas and reissue correct ones. Defense counsel declares that every business day since October 2, 2024, their office followed up with the vendor by phone and/or email seeking confirmation of the withdrawals. However, defense counsel was unable to reach the vendor until October 9, 2024 (Decl. of Amy Lee, ¶ 9.)

On October 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter to Defense counsel, stating that Plaintiff had been informed the incorrect subpoenas had not been withdrawn. Plaintiff’s counsel demanded proof of withdrawal by the end of the day and indicated that Plaintiff would proceed with a motion to quash if confirmation was not received.

On the same day, Defense counsel responded to Plaintiff’s counsel, advising her that the vendor had confirmed on October 2, 2024, that they would withdraw the incorrect subpoenas and issue corrected ones. Defense counsel noted they were actively following up with the vendor for proof of withdrawal.

However, Defendant did not provide proof that the subpoenas had been withdrawn by October 9, 2024, and Plaintiff filed the motion to quash the following day, meeting the deadline.

It appears that Defendant’s vendor did not complete the withdrawal of the subpoenas until October 18, 2024 (Id. ¶ 10), when the Confirmation of the withdrawal sent to Plaintiff.

Discussion

It is undisputed that Plaintiff had until October 10, 2024, to file the motion. Although Defendant made repeated efforts to contact the vendor to withdraw the subpoenas starting on October 2, 2024, the subpoenas were not officially withdrawn until October 18, 2024—8 days after Plaintiff’s filing deadline. While the Court does not find that Defendant acted in bad faith, Plaintiff alerted Defendant to the issue on October 2, 2024, well before the deadline, providing Defendant ample time to withdraw the subpoenas. Despite Defendant’s assurances on October 2 that the subpoenas would be withdrawn, deponents continued to receive notices through October 9, 2024. Without timely confirmation of withdrawal, Plaintiff was compelled to file the motion and incur attorney’s fees and therefore should be reimbursed

Considering Defendant’s diligent efforts to resolve the issue, the Court reduces the sanction to $1000, payable jointly and severally by Defendant Ovig Vartanian and his attorney to Plaintiff within 20 days of this order.

MOTION FOR TRIAL CONTINUANCE

Hearing Date: 11/7/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03664 BRYANT CRUZ vs DEREK ALLEN VOGLER, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Lyft 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: GRANTED IN PART

 

Plaintiff filed this case on January 28, 2022, and trial is currently set for December 4, 2024. Defendant Lyft requests a six-month trial continuance on the basis that Lyft has recently changed its attorney. Counsel for Defendant Derek Allen Vogler is scheduled to begin trial in Kern County on December 2, 2024, in Gonzalo Ramirez, et al. v. Grimmway Enterprises, Inc., Case No. BCV-22-100428, which is expected to last seven to ten days. Vogler’s counsel has also issued over 70 subpoenas for records related to police reports, Plaintiff’s medical and billing information, employment history, and other claims, with more than 30 of these subpoenas still outstanding. Additionally, the parties have agreed to participate in mediation on February 19, 2025.

The Court notes that this case is now more than two years old, and discovery and mediation should have been completed by now. The Court is supposed to ensure that 100 percent of civil cases conclude in 2 years. (see CRC 3.714). A change in counsel does not constitute good cause for a continuance, as new counsel assumes all pre-existing deadlines in the case. Thus, the only persuasive good faith basis to continue this case is counsel’s December 2 trial conflict.  The fact that counsel scheduled a mediation AFTER the trial date is a particularly unpersuasive basis for a continuance – the parties have known the trial date for more than a year and, thus, had ample opportunity to schedule a mediation before trial.  Nonetheless, as an accommodation to the parties, the Court will grant a trial continuance to March 24, 2025, at 8:30 a.m.  The FSC shall be continued to March 10, 2024, at 10:00 a.m. Based on the purported need to do further discovery (albeit, it is unclear why that has not been completed in the year that this case has been at issue), the pre-trial dates, including discovery cut-off dates, will follow the new trial date.

The parties should consider the new trial date as firm.

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 11/7/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV29373 RUBEN MELIKOFF vs SAFEWAY INC., et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff

Responding Party: Defendant The Vons Companies

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: SANCTIONS GRANTED 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion

Plaintiff moves the Court to do the following:

1. Striking of defendant THE VONS COMPANIES, INC.’S answer and entering default (i.e., Terminating Sanction);

2. Deeming all liability and causation issues determined in favor of plaintiff against the defendant THE VONS COMPANIES, INC. (i.e., an Issue Sanction);

3. For an order precluding defendant THE VONS COMPANIES, INC. from contesting liability (i.e., an Evidentiary Sanction) by way of the affirmative defenses of comparative fault or assumption of risk; and/or

4. Order monetary sanctions against defendant THE VONS COMPANIES, INC. and/or their attorneys of record, in the sum of $2,469.95 (i.e., Monetary Sanctions).

Background

On August 13, 2024, the Court heard Plaintiff’s motion to compel depositions and issued a monetary sanction of $1,699.95. The Court ordered that Defendant’s PMK (Person Most Knowledgeable) deposition, along with the depositions of four additional employees, be completed by August 26, 2024, the discovery cutoff date. Both parties submitted to the tentative, indicating that both parties had notice of the Court’s ruling.[1]  The trial date has since been continued to November 22, 2024; discovery remains closed.

The Person Most Knowledgeable (PMK) deposition was completed; however, the depositions of the other four employees were not conducted before the discovery cutoff. Although Defendant attempted to schedule depositions for two of these employees after the cutoff and informed Plaintiff that the other two employees were no longer under Defendant’s employment, these actions occurred past the date set for them to occur. The proposed depositions were set for dates after the discovery cutoff, and the information regarding the two employees' departure was provided only six days before the cutoff, making it untimely for Plaintiff to subpoena and depose these individuals prior to the deadline.

Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.050, discovery beyond the cutoff date requires the Court's approval. Discovery is considered closed after the cutoff date unless the Court specifically permits it by approving a stipulation or granting a motion to reopen discovery. To prevent such issues, the Court expressly ordered that the depositions occur before the discovery cutoff. Defendant, however, failed to comply with the Court’s order. Given these circumstances, sanctions are warranted.  The question is which of the requested sanctions are appropriate.

The Civil Discovery Act supports an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, beginning with monetary sanctions and escalating only as needed. (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.) Sanctions should be proportionate to the misconduct and “should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) Terminating sanctions are generally appropriate only where violations are willful, there is a history of abuse, and less severe sanctions have proven ineffective. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)uTerminating sanctions are not appropriate in this instance, as this is Defendant’s first clear violation, the monetary sanction has been paid, the PMK deposition has been completed, and Defendant attempted, albeit untimely, to produce two employees for deposition. No history of discovery abuse exists, and terminating sanctions would create a “windfall” for Plaintiff, placing Plaintiff in a better position than if Defendant had fully complied.Given the upcoming trial date, reopening discovery at this late stage is somewhat impractical, though not entirely unfeasible. Monetary sanctions alone would be inadequate to address the prejudice caused to Plaintiff, particularly with trial imminent. Allowing Defendant to present testimony from employees who had not been deposed would create a “windfall” for Defendant. In light of these considerations, the court orders that (1) discovery be re-opened for the limited purpose of permitting Plaintiff to take Defendant employee depositions; (2) Defendant make the two store employees available for deposition in the next 7 business days; (3) Defendant is to pay for any costs associated with expediting the employee depositions, including expedited transcripts; (4) Defendant is barred from introducing any testimony or declarations from the two former employees whose depositions were not conducted by the discovery cutoff, as previously ordered by the Court and is barred from introducing testimony from the 2 current employees if they are not made available for deposition as ordered herein; and (5) Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally, are to pay monetary sanctions of $2,000 related to the cost of this motion.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM WAIVER 

Hearing Date: 11/7/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV31815 MONY OREN-BENHAMOU vs SMART & FINAL STORES, LLC

Moving Party: Defendant Smart & Final

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: GRANTED

 

Background

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff Mony Oren-Benhamou served multiple discovery requests to Defendant Smart & Final Stores, LLC (“Defendant”). Defendant’s responses were served on September 6, 2024, but were late. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s failure to provide timely, verified responses resulted in a waiver of all objections, including those based on privilege. Defendant seeks relief from this waiver, attributing the delay to inadvertent calendaring errors during new personnel onboarding. Defendant asserts that the delay was unintentional and was promptly corrected upon discovery.

Legal Standard

Under California Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), and 2033.280(a), a party waives objections to discovery requests if responses are not timely served. However, the court may grant relief from this waiver if the responding party demonstrates substantial compliance with discovery requirements and establishes that the delay was due to “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”

Discussion

Plaintiff challenges the adequacy of Defendant’s verification, arguing that the statement “The matters stated in the foregoing document are true of my knowledge” fails to satisfy the verification requirements of California Code of Civil Procedure § 2015.5, which mandates verification “under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California.” The Court finds, however, that the verification is substantially compliant for the purposes of relief from waiver. Any issue with the verification language could have been addressed through a meet-and-confer process as proposed by Defendant.

The Court finds Defendant’s explanation that the delay was due to a clerical error related to calendaring deadlines during new personnel onboarding credible. Plaintiff did not provide any evidence disputing this. Defendant’s actions show that the mistake was unintentional, and responses were served promptly upon discovery of the error. Courts recognize excusable neglect when an unintentional error occurs despite reasonable precautions, and here, Defendant’s immediate corrective action demonstrates diligence.

Plaintiff claims prejudice from the delay in receiving responses but has not shown that the delay materially impacted Plaintiff’s ability to conduct discovery or proceed with litigation. Defendant has committed to providing any necessary privilege log and has indicated that all responsive documents have been disclosed in good faith.

Plaintiff also points to minor deficiencies in Defendant’s responses, such as insufficient linkage of documents to specific requests and the need for clarification on privilege claims. However, the Court finds that these issues are better addressed through a motion to compel further responses rather than through waiver of objections.

Conclusion

Overall, the Court finds that Defendant’s responses minimally satisfy the requirements for substantial compliance, and grants relief from waiver.
