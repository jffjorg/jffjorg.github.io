DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV47443    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S EX PARTE APPLICATION FOR AN ORDER CONTINUING THE TRIAL AND DENYING PLAINTIFF’S APPLICATION FOR AN ORDER SHORTENING TIME.

The Court has read and considered the moving papers filed by Plaintiff.  The bases for this ex parte application are: 1. The parties have scheduled a ten-hour mediation session in January 2025; and 2. The current trial date of 10/1/24 runs up against Rosh Hashana and Plaintiff’s counsel, Mr. Levin, will be unavailable from October 2, 2024 to October 4, 2024, inclusive. 

  The Court rules as follows: Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application for an Order Continuing the Trial Date, Final Status Conference Date, and All Statutory Associated Dates, or, Alternatively, for an Order Shortening Time to Hear a Noticed Motion is Denied. The court finds that Plaintiff has not satisfied the requirement of California Rules of Court, rule 3.1202(c), that an applicant for ex parte relief “must make an affirmative factual showing in a declaration containing competent testimony based on personal knowledge of irreparable harm, immediate danger, or any other statutory basis for granting relief ex parte.”

The Court notes that this case was filed on December 29, 2021, almost three years ago.  The Court further notes that there have been three previous trial continuances: 1. 6/28/23 to 11/7/23; 2. 11/7/23 to 6/4/24; and 3. 6/4/24 to 10/1/24.

As to the first basis for the application, the January 2025 mediation date, given the age of the case it is not unreasonable to expect litigants to schedule mediation prior to a fourth trial date.  As to the second basis for the application, this Court respects Mr. Levin’s need to be unavailable on October 2, 3 and 4 and, while keeping the October 1 trial date, will resume trial on October 5.

 

Dated August 14, 2024

 

                                                                                                                     JARED D. MOSES

                                                                                    JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT


Case Number: 22AHCV00268    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER ON CROSS-COMPLAINANT’S MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO INTERROGATORIES, REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, AND REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS, AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

The motion is granted in part, on the terms set forth below. No sanctions.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a commercial loan dispute. Plaintiff Cadlerock Joint Venture, L.P. sued defendants Edith Enterprise Ltd., Helen Young, Does I-X, and Roe Corporations XI-XX on May 9, 2022, for breach of contract, unjust enrichment, and promissory estoppel. Plaintiff sought to recover an outstanding balance of approximately $25,000.00 on a line of credit issued to Defendants by CitiBank, who later assigned the contract to Plaintiff.

          On April 3, 2023, defendant Edith Enterprise, Ltd. (“Edith”) cross-complained against cross-defendant CitiBank, N.A. ( “Citi”); Edith filed its second amended cross-complaint (“SAXC”) on August 28, 2023. The SAXC asserts causes of action for (1) breach of written and implied-in-fact contract; (2) conversion; (3) negligence; (4) fraudulent transfers; (5) unfair business practices; and (6) common count.

          On November 1, 2023, the Court entered judgment for Cadlerock and against Edith and Young in the amount of approximately $50,000.00. Edith’s cross-claim against Citi remains unresolved.

Edith alleges it maintained a line of credit and business checking account with Citi from at least 2008 to 2020. (SAXC, ¶ 7.) In April 2020, Citi notified Edith that “it could ‘no longer service Edith’s account ending – 8207 and your other related Citibank accounts[.]’” (Id., ¶ 10.) Edith continued to use its account for several months. (Id., ¶ 12.) Citi then zeroed out and closed Edith’s account. (Id., ¶ 14.) Edith assumed the approximate $30,000.00 balance would be used to pay off its line of credit, with the surplus disbursed to it by check. (Id., ¶¶ 17, 20.) But it never received any funds. (Id., ¶¶ 20-25.)

Despite the closure of Edith’s checking account, the outstanding balance on its line of credit remained. (Id., ¶ 26.) In August 2021 Citi sold its rights under the contract to Cadlerock. (Ibid.) Cadlerock sent Edith a letter requesting that she redirect her payments to Cadlerock; surprised, Edith disputed the debt and notified Cadlerock that Citi should have paid off the balance. (Id., ¶¶ 27-29.) Cadlerock declared Edith in default. (Id., ¶ 31.) In May 2022, it sued. (Id., ¶¶ 31, 34.)

On April 29, 2024, Edith moved for an order compelling Citi to respond to five (5) sets of discovery: Edith’s Form Interrogatories (“FR”), Special Interrogatories (“SR”), two subsets of its Requests for Production (“RPD”), and Requests for Admission (“RFA”). On August 2, 2024, Citi opposed Edith’s motions. On August 9, 2024, Edith replied.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, 2031.310, and 2033.290 govern motions to compel further responses to interrogatories, requests for production, and requests for admission, respectively. A party propounding any of these three methods of discovery has a right to responses within thirty (30) days of when the party propounds its requests. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260(a), 2031.260(a), 2033.250(a).) The Code prescribes the form and contents of discovery responses. (See id., §§ 2030.210-250, 2031.210-250, 2033.210-250.) If the responding party provides responses but the propounding party contends the responses do not satisfy the relevant provisions of the Code, the propounding party may move for an order compelling the responding party to provide further responses. (See id., §§ 2030.300, 2031.310, 2033.290.)

 

          III.     ANALYSIS

          A. Procedural Considerations

                    1. Timeliness

          A party moving for further responses must file its motion within forty-five (45) days of the response that it contends is deficient. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c), 2033.290(c).)

          Citi provided its most recent supplemental responses to all the disputed discovery ten days before Edith filed its motion on April 29, 2024. (Chien RPD1 and SR Decls., ¶¶ 52; RFA Decl., ¶ 51; RPD2 and FR Decls., ¶¶ 48.) The motion is timely.

                    2. Meeting and Conference

          A motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration establishing the moving party made a good-faith effort to meet and confer as described in Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b)(2), 2033.290(b)(1).)

Counsel submitted a declaration, supported by exhibits, that satisfies section 2016.040. (See Chien FR Decl., ¶¶ 14-57.)

         

B. Interrogatories

Each response to an interrogatory must be either an answer, a reference to external writing from which the answer may be found, or an objection to a specific interrogatory. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.210(a).) If the response is an answer, it must be “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Id., § 2030.220 (a).) “If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible,” and “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” (Id., § 2030.220(b)-(c).)

 

1. Form Interrogatories

          Edith argues Citi has not provided sufficient responses to its FR Nos. 15.1 or 17.1. The former interrogatory asks the responding party to state all facts supporting each denial or special or affirmative defense in its pleadings; the latter asks for all facts justifying any whole or partial denial in response to an RFA.

Citi answered No. 15.1 for each of its thirty-five (35) affirmative defenses and 17.1 for each of Edith’s thirteen (13) RFAs. Each of Citi’s responses recites substantially the same page-long factual narrative explaining a few internal transactions that affected the balance of Edith’s checking account.

Edith argues (1) Citi only provided facts regarding the affirmative defenses, but not the denials, in its answer, and (2) Citi “narrowed the scope of its response by stating that it has not ‘directly’ received any fraud claim”. (Sep. St., 11:19-20.) Edith also contends Citi failed to provide addresses or telephone numbers for prospective witnesses and Citi “significantly reduced the number of PERSONS who have knowledge of the facts from twenty (20) identified in its prior responses to only three in its current iteration.” (Id., 12:7-9.)

Citi’s responses are vague and evasive in one uniform respect. Citi begins every response by stating that “Citibank has not directly received any claim of fraud directly any persons claiming that they were fraudulently induced to transfer funds to Edith’s Citibank account.” (Sep. St., 6:19-20.) This sentence is syntactically incoherent. It also does not define the term “directly”. The Court agrees with Edith that the qualification is evasive.

Citi has also failed to provide contact information for percipient witnesses, as it was asked to do.

As to FR No. 15.1, however, the Court agrees with Citi that the term “material allegation” is impermissibly vague.

The motion is granted on narrower terms: Citi must provide contact information for the witnesses it has identified. It must also clarify the meaning of its unintelligible statement that it has not “directly received any claim of fraud directly any persons.”  Citi must also provide a sworn certification that it has answered each interrogatory as completely and straightforwardly as possible following a reasonable and good-faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations.

I.                INTRODUCTION

            On December 9, 2022, Plaintiffs Jane M.A.T. Doe (“MAT”) and Jane M.I.G. Doe (“MIG”) brought an action for 1) sexual assault, 2) negligence, and 3) negligent failure to educate, train, or warn against Defendants Doe 1, Doe 2, and Does 3 through 100.  The Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs were sexually abused from approximately 1979 until 1981 or 1982, when they were between the ages of 6 and 9 years old, during school hours and on school grounds by an employee of Defendant Doe 1’s elementary school where they were students.  During that time, Defendant Doe 1 personnel received complaints of alleged abuse by the employee, Frank Miller.  However, school personnel conducted no investigation and Frank Miller was not terminated from employment.

            On March 9, 2023, Emmaus Evangelical Lutheran Church of Alhambra (“Emmaus”) as Defendant Doe 1, and The Pacific Southwest District of the Lutheran Church – Missouri Synod (“Pacific”) as Defendant Doe 2, each filed answers to the Complaint.

            On March 27, 2024, the action was reassigned to Judge Jared D. Moses in Department P at the Pasadena Courthouse.

            On March 28, 2024, Emmaus filed an Amended Answer to the Complaint.

            On April 17, 2024, Emmaus filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication (“Motion”).  On August 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an Opposition to the Motion and on August 9, 2024, Defendant filed a Reply.  On August 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply to Defendant’s objections.

            On July 26, 2024, Pacific also filed a Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication, set for hearing on November 8, 2024.

Having considered the moving papers, opposition, objections, and reply, the Court denies Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment and Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, second, and third causes of action.

II.             EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Under the California Rules of Court, rule 3.1352, a party moving for summary judgment must present its objections to the evidence presented either in writing or at the hearing.  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (q):

In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court need rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion. Objections to evidence that are not ruled on for purposes of the motion shall be preserved for appellate review.

            For judicial economy, the Court only rules on the objections to evidence it has deemed material to the disposition of the instant Motion.

The Court SUSTAINS the following objections: 1-7.

The Court OVERRULES the following objections: 8-31.

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle him/her to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  The moving party must make an affirmative showing that he/she is entitled to judgment regardless of whether the opposing party files an opposition.  (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742-743.)  Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)

When a plaintiff seeks summary judgment, he/she must produce admissible evidence on each element of each cause of action on which judgment is sought.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)  When a defendant seeks summary judgment, he/she has the “burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets his or her initial burden.  (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.)  Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p).)  The opposing party may not simply rely on his/her allegations to show a triable issue but must present evidentiary facts that are substantial in nature and rise beyond mere speculation.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162.)  Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

As to any alternative request for summary adjudication of issues, such alternative relief must be clearly set forth in the Notice of Motion and the general burden-shifting rules apply but the issues upon which summary adjudication may be sought are limited by statute.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)

IV.       ANALYSIS

Defendant Emmaus moves for summary judgment of the Complaint or in the alternative, for summary adjudication of all three causes of action, on the basis that there are no triable issues of material fact as to any of the causes of action.

The following facts are undisputed.  Emmaus Evangelical Lutheran Church of Alhambra is a nonprofit corporation that operated an elementary school during the years relevant to the Complaint.  (Undisputed Material Facts (“UMFs”) 1-2.)  Frank Miller (“Miller”) was a voting member of the church as early as 1956 and was hired as a custodian before 1971.  (UMF 3.)  He retired from his position around February 1, 1981.  (UMF 4.)  Plaintiff MAT, now 50 years old, attended the elementary school from 1979 to 1982.  (UMF 5.)  MAT alleges that Miller sexually abused her in the school’s gardening shed, girls’ bathroom, and playground yard.  (UMFs 6-7.)  Prior to reaching adulthood, MAT did not tell anyone about Miller’s actions, but during third grade, she did tell her mother that Miller had touched her legs on the playground when questioned by her mother.  (UMFs 8-9.)  Plaintiff MIG, now 51 years old, attended the elementary school from 1979 to 1981 or 1982.  (UMF 10.)  She alleges that she was sexually abused by Miller in the school’s gardening shed and the girls’ bathroom.  (UMF 11.)  Prior to reaching adulthood, she never told anyone about Miller’s actions.  (UMF 12.)  Pastor Carl Kaye conducted a diligent search of Emmaus’s records but did not find any record of complaints against Miller for inappropriate conduct toward students prior to the filing of the instant Complaint.  (UMF 13.)  Furthermore, he did not find any documents dating to or in effect between 1979 and 1982 that relate to policies regarding interaction between adults and children, supervisors of children, or hiring and training of employees who work near children.  (UMF 15.)  Frank Miller and the principal at the time of the alleged abuse, George Zehnder, are deceased.  (UMF 16.)  According to Defendant, most YMCAs and Big Brothers/Big Sisters organizations began to implement abuse prevention programs in 1997 and 1998 for screening and selecting adults to work with children, which took place 15 years after the alleged abuse in this case.  (UMF 24.)

A.     First Cause of Action - Sexual Assault

Civil Code section 1708.5, subdivision (a), states that “[a] person commits a sexual battery who does any of the following: [¶] (1) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with an intimate part of another, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. [¶] (2) Acts with the intent to cause a harmful or offensive contact with another by use of his or her intimate part, and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results. [¶] (3) Acts to cause an imminent apprehension of the conduct described in paragraph (1) or (2), and a sexually offensive contact with that person directly or indirectly results.”

Generally, an employer may be held liable for employee misconduct under the respondeat superior theory or vicarious liability.  (C.R. v. Tenet Healthcare Corp. (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1110-1111.)  Alternatively, “an employer may be liable for an employee's act where the employer either authorized the tortious act or subsequently ratified an originally unauthorized tort. [Citations.]  The failure to discharge an employee who has committed misconduct may be evidence of ratification.  [Citation.]  The theory of ratification is generally applied where an employer fails to investigate or respond to charges that an employee committed an intentional tort, such as assault or battery.  [Citations.]  Whether an employer has ratified an employee's conduct is generally a factual question.  [Citation.]’  [Citations.]”  (Ibid.; City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1973) 33 Cal.App.3d 778, 782–783.)

Defendant argues that it is entitled to summary adjudication of the first cause of action for sexual assault because California does not recognize vicarious liability for sexual assault by school employees against students.  (Mot. p. 14.)  Defendant cites Steven F. v. Anaheim Union High School Dist., for the finding that a school district cannot be held vicariously liable for a teacher’s sexual misconduct toward a student, except through liability for negligent hiring and supervising.  (112 Cal.App.4th 904, 908.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant misread the Complaint because Plaintiffs do not allege vicarious liability through respondeat superior, but rather through the theory of ratification.  (Oppos. pp. 12-13.)  They argue that Emmaus ratified Frank Miller’s sexual abuse of minors because the school principal was aware of the inappropriate conduct and conducted no investigation or took remedial action.  Plaintiffs present the declarations of former students, C.P. and K.S., who were targets of Miller’s sexually inappropriate conduct and spoke to the principal regarding Miller’s actions.  (DeMarco Decl., Exs. 5-6.)  They also state that other staff members were aware that Miller inappropriately touched C.P. in the girls’ restroom and unbuttoned his pants to show his clothed genitals to three elementary school girls on the campus field.  (Ibid.)  However, as Pastor Kaye states in his declaration, there is no record of discipline, supervision, or investigation into Miller’s conduct.  (See UMF 13 – Kaye Decl. ¶ 8.)  Plaintiffs state that their evidence demonstrates that much of this inappropriate conduct was “open and obvious to other children and staff at the school” as presented in the deposition testimony of MAT, MIG, and the declaration of C.P.  (Oppos. p. 13, citing to Exhibits 2, 3, 5, 8.)  Plaintiffs also refer to the note that C.P. saw the principal take down when she made her complaint.  However, no evidence of this note has been found.  (Ex. 5 ¶ 5; Ex. 8 at 2:20-3:10.)

In its Reply, Defendant argues that the evidence presented by Plaintiffs is not sufficient to conclude that Defendant ratified the alleged sexual abuse by Miller.  (Reply p. 3.)  The evidence shows that the school principal was informed that Miller had touched C.P. on the shoulder and an unnamed teacher may have observed tickling on the playground.  However, the Plaintiffs did not report Miller’s conduct.

Having considered the evidence and the arguments presented by the parties, the Court finds that a reasonable fact finder could conclude that Defendant had sufficient notice of Miller’s conduct to have ratified his actions by failing to terminate his employment or take other remedial action.  The evidence presented by Plaintiffs does not have to show that Defendant ratified Miller’s conduct, but rather it must raise a triable issue of material fact.  Here, there is a triable issue of material fact regarding Defendant’s ratification and liability for the first cause of action for sexual assault.  Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first cause of action is denied.

B.     Second and Third Causes of Action – (2) Negligence and (3) Negligent failure to educate, train or warn.

“To establish a cause of action for negligence, the plaintiff must show that the ‘defendant had a duty to use due care, that he breached that duty, and that the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.’”  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal. 5th 204, 213). “Recovery for negligence depends as a threshold matter on the existence of a legal duty of care.” (Ibid.)  “Whether a duty exists is a question of law to be resolved by the court.”  (Ibid.; see Regents v. University of California v. Superior Court (2018) 4 Cal.5th 608, 620 [“The determination whether a particular relationship supports a duty of care rests on policy and is a question of law.”].)

The elements of a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision are: (1) the employer’s hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee; (2) the employee was incompetent or unfit; (3) the employer had reason to believe undue risk of harm would exist because of the employment; and (4) harm occurs.  (Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 828, 836-837.)

Here, the parties dispute the threshold matter of the existence of a legal duty of care.

a.     Duty: Special Relationship

“Under some circumstances, a defendant may have an affirmative duty to protect the plaintiff from harm at the hands of a third party, even though the risk of harm is not of the defendant's own making.”  (Brown, supra, 11 Cal. 5th at 215.)  “In particular, a person may have an affirmative duty to protect the victim of another's harm if that person is in what the law calls a ‘special relationship’ with either the victim or the person who created the harm.”  (Ibid. at 215.)

“In Brown, the Supreme Court established a two-step inquiry to determine whether a defendant has a legal duty to take action to protect a plaintiff from injuries caused by a third party: ‘First, the court must determine whether there exists a special relationship between the parties or some other set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty to protect. Second, if so, the court must consult the factors described in [Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 70 Cal.Rptr. 97, 443 P.2d 561 (Rowland)] to determine whether relevant policy considerations counsel limiting that duty’.”  (Doe v. Roman Cath. Archbishop of Los Angeles, 70 Cal. App. 5th 657, 670 [citing Brown, supra, 11 Cal.5th at p. 209 and Regents, supra, 4 Cal.5th at p. 627 (special relationship doctrine is an exception to the general rule that there is no duty to protect others from the conduct of third parties)].)

“A special relationship between the defendant and the victim is one that ‘gives the victim a right to expect’ protection from the defendant, while a special relationship between the defendant and the dangerous third party is one that ‘entails an ability to control [the third party's] conduct.” (Brown, supra, 11 Cal. 5th at 216.)  “Where such a special relationship exists between the defendant and a minor, the obligation to provide such protection and assistance may include a duty to protect the minor from third party abuse.”  (Brown, supra, 11 Cal. 5th at 504).

Moreover, “ ‘[i]t is the duty of the school authorities to supervise at all times the conduct of the children on the school grounds and to enforce those rules and regulations necessary to their protection. [Citations.]…’ [Citation.]”  (J.H. v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2010) 183 Cal. App.4th 123, 139; C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School District (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 869.)  As a result, “[a] special relationship is formed between a school district and its students resulting in the imposition of an affirmative duty on the school district to take all reasonable steps to protect its students. . . .” (M. W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist.¿(2003)¿110 Cal.App.4th 508, 517.)

Once a special relationship is established, Courts move on to the second step in the inquiry to determine whether the duty should be limited and in doing so they balance “a number of considerations; the major ones are the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, the closeness of the connection between the defendant's conduct and the injury suffered, the moral blame attached to the defendant's conduct, the policy of preventing future harm, the extent of the burden to the defendant and consequences to the community of imposing a duty to exercise care with resulting liability for breach, and the availability, cost, and prevalence of insurance for the risk involved.  (Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 113.)

The parties do not dispute that a special relationship existed between the school and its students.  However, Defendant argues that even if a special relationship did exist, analysis under the Rowland factors justifies limiting Defendant’s duty in the instant case.

b.     Duty: Rowland Factors

Defendant argues that all of the Rowland factors weigh in favor of limiting the general duty of care.

                                                              i.     Foreseeability

First, Defendant states that the harm was not foreseeable and presents the declaration of Dr. Applewhite, who holds a Ph.D. in Social Work, in support of this assertion.  (Mot. p. 17.)  Defendant argues that a trier of fact would need to rely on expert assistance to assess the standard of care imposed on similar institutions between 1979-1982, instead of substituting a modern understanding of childhood sexual abuse.  Dr. Applewhite asserts that during the 1979-1982 timeframe, the risk of sexual abuse by adults against minors was largely unknown to academics or the public and there was no established standard of care for selecting adults to work with children or protocols for proactively preventing sexual abuse of children.  (UMFs 17-30, Applewhite Decl. ¶¶ 14-19, 21, 23, 26.)  Thus, according to Defendant, the type of harm Plaintiffs suffered was not reasonably foreseeable in 1979-1982.

Plaintiffs argue that there is ample evidence that Defendant knew or had reason to know of the dangerous propensities of Frank Miller, which demonstrates the foreseeability of the harm they suffered.  (Oppos. pp. 15-19.)  In support, Plaintiff presents the following evidence (1) the school principal received a complaint from at least one child and her father that Miller was hanging out in the girls’ restroom, where he touched a girl in the same age range as Plaintiffs (Ex. 6 ¶¶ 5-6), (2) at least one schoolteacher witnessed a tickle fight between Miller and three young female students when Miller revealing his jock strap covered genitals on the school field (Ex. 6 ¶¶ 4-5), (3) in plain view of supervising teachers on the school playground, Miller rubbed the thighs of little girls, gave them candy, and in at least one instance struck a female student on the face (Exs. 2, 6, 10); (4) during school recesses, Miller had little girls in his work shed, which was visible from the lunch and playground areas where teachers were stationed to supervise children, this work shed was supposed to be off limits to children and clearly displayed pictures of scantily clad women (Exs. 2, 3, 5, 9, 10).  Plaintiffs also present the testimony of Defendant’s former employees who acknowledge the significance of Miller’s actions and the fact that he should have been dismissed as a result of his conduct.  (Ex. 9 – Deposition of Dr. Larry Stotereau at 20:7-21, 21:17-22:9, 22:18-25, 23:9-14, 25:24-26:5, 26:7-28:2; Ex. 10 – Deposition of Maralyn Malucky at 28:5-13, 29:24-31:11, 38:21-39:12, 46:7-47:24, 58:22-59:20.) 

Plaintiffs present a sampling of articles from the Los Angeles Times and other sources demonstrating the media coverage of child molestation cases by trusted adults, including school personnel, within the 1977 to 1983 timeframe.  (See Exs. 11-37.)  Plaintiffs also refer to the 1962 California Court of Appeals case, Wallace v. Der-Ohanian, in which the Court referred to public awareness of the problem of child molestation.  (See Wallace v. Der-Ohanian (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 141, 146.)

“In examining foreseeability [in determining duty], ‘the court's task ... “is not to decide whether a particular plaintiff's injury was reasonably foreseeable in light of a particular defendant's conduct, but rather to evaluate more generally whether the category of negligent conduct at issue is sufficiently likely to result in the kind of harm experienced that liability may appropriately be imposed.” ’ [Citations.]” (Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Los Angeles (2021) 70 Cal. App. 5th 657, 676.)

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have presented significant evidence that the type of harm was foreseeable based on the specific factual circumstances in the instant case, as well as societal awareness of the problem of child abuse at the time.  Thus, the foreseeability factor weighs toward imposing a duty of care on Defendant.

                                                            ii.     Certainty

Defendant argues that because the harm in question occurred so long ago, the fact of the Plaintiffs’ injury is inherently less certain due to unavailability of evidence, the deaths of witnesses, and Plaintiffs’ own memories.  (Mot. p. 17.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendant does not cite any authority or facts to support its assertion that the passage of time makes the injury resulting from abuse less certain.  (Oppos. pp. 19-20.)  Defendant’s argument is unsupported by cases such as Doe v. Lawndale, which held that this factor should not limit claims based on physical sexual abuse and assault.  (Doe v. Lawndale (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 132-133.)

The Court finds that the factor of certainty also weighs against limiting Defendant’s duty of care.

                                                          iii.     Closeness of Connection

Defendant argues that there was no closeness of connection between Emmaus’s conduct and Plaintiffs’ injury because the connection between a youth-serving organization’s conduct and risk of child abuse by its employees was not understood in 1979-1982, as supported by Dr. Applewhite’s declaration.  (Mot. p. 18.)  Plaintiffs argue that Emmaus’s failure to take any action against Miller or to protect children under its care from molestation would still have been effective during the time period in question.  (Oppos. p. 20.)

The Court refers back to its finding regarding foreseeability and the fact that Defendant had opportunities to protect its students from the abuse.  Thus, the Court finds that the connection between Defendant’s conduct and Plaintiffs’ injuries does not weigh in favor of limiting Defendant’s duty of care.

                                                          iv.     Moral Blame

Fourth, Defendant argues that moral blame for the harm lies with the deceased Frank Miller, not Emmaus.  (Mot. p. 18.)  Given the inability of academics, institutions, and the public to anticipate or apprehend the danger of unreported childhood sexual abuse during the period in question, moral blame cannot be assigned to Emmaus.

Plaintiffs argue that moral blame also attaches to persons involved in hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee, when they become aware of a substantial risk posed by that employee but fail to take appropriate action to respond to this risk.  (Oppos. p. 21.)  This is particularly true in instances where a school fails to protect minors from alleged sexual abuse while those minors are under the school’s supervision and control.

In Reply, Defendant reiterates its stance that there is no evidence of prior knowledge of Miller’s conduct and Emmaus should be held to the standard applicable in 1979-1982, not the 2000s.  (Reply pp. 6-7.) The Court finds that the allegations in the instant action, particularly the allegation that minor Plaintiffs were harmed in an elementary school setting where an employee openly displayed inappropriate conduct, weigh against limiting Defendant’s duty of care.

                                                            v.     Policy of Preventing Future Harm

Fifth, Defendant argues that the evidence establishes that Emmaus met the standard of care in 1979-1982, thus, it would not serve public policy to punish Emmaus for failing to foresee harm that was not understood at the time, nor would it prevent future harm.  (Mot. p. 18.)  Plaintiffs argue that safeguarding children from sexual abuse “weighs strongly in favor of imposing a duty on school districts to take reasonable measures to identify and respond to potential misconduct.”  (Oppos. pp. 21-22.)

            In Reply, Defendant reiterates that retroactively punishing Emmaus for not meeting the modern standards of care will not further the public policy of preventing future harm.  (Reply p. 7.)  Moreover, there is no indication that Defendant is not currently meeting the modern standard of care in such situations.  The Court finds that not limiting the duty of care for similarly situated parties would further the general public policy of preventing future sexual abuse of minors.

                                                          vi.     Consequences of Imposition of Duty of Care

Defendant argues that the consequence of imposing a duty where none would have been recognized in 1979-1982 would unfairly expose youth-serving organizations to legal expenses and negatively impact the communities they serve.  (Mot. pp. 18-19.)

Plaintiffs argue that Defendant fails to present any evidence or arguments regarding the burden of preventing molestation of minors after receiving complaints about an alleged perpetrator or generally educating and training staff, children, and parents how to prevent such abuse.  (Oppos. pp. 22-23.)  Given the role Defendant plays in the lives of the youth and the community, imposing a burden that would result in the prevention of childhood sexual abuse would not be too onerous.

The Court finds that, although there may be consequences related to exposing similar organizations to legal actions, Defendant has not shown that this burden outweighs the protection of minors from sexual abuse when under the control or supervision of such institutions.  Thus, this factor does not weigh in favor of limiting Defendant’s duty.

 

                                                         vii.     Availability of Insurance

Defendant has not been able to locate an insurance policy for the time period and is completely exposed to the costs of litigation.  (Mot. p. 19.)  As stated in Dr. Applewhite’s declaration, neither institutions nor insurers in 1979-1982 could anticipate these types of claims.  In response, Plaintiffs present the deposition testimony of Dr. Lenhardt, a former pastor at Defendant’s institution, who states that Defendant would have had insurance.  (Ex. 9 at 50:17-23, 51:5-10.)

The Court does not find that Plaintiffs’ evidence is sufficient to show the existence of an insurance policy.  Thus, this factor does weigh in favor of limiting Defendant’s duty of care.  As discussed above, the Court finds that a special relationship existed between Defendant and Plaintiffs such that Defendant owed a duty of care to Plaintiffs.  As most of the Rowland factors weigh against limiting this duty, the Court concludes that Defendant owed a duty of care toward Plaintiffs.

Given that Defendant does not dispute the other elements of negligence and negligent hiring, retention, or supervision, the Court denies Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the second and third causes of action.

C.    Unconstitutional Violation of Due Process Rights

Defendant argues that California Code of Civil Procedure, section 340.1, which was amended by Assembly Bill No. 218 in 2019, unconstitutionally violates Emmaus’s due process rights as applied.  (Mot. pp. 19-22.)  It exposes Defendant to a stale claim and the possibility of deprivation of property without the opportunity to defend itself.  Defendant states that prior to 1998, Code of Civil Procedure, section 340.1, did not extend the statute of limitations for childhood sexual abuse/assault claims against non-perpetrator defendants.  Thus, until 2019, Plaintiff’s causes of action were barred by the statute of limitations.  However, in 2019, Assembly Bill 218 amended Code of Civil Procedure, section 340.1, to suspend the statute of limitations for three years.  The Amendment created a private action where none existed before.

Defendant argues that pursuant to the U.S. Constitution, Amendment 5, and California Constitution, Article I, Section 7, it does not have a meaningful opportunity to defend itself against Plaintiffs’ claims due to the passage of time, the death of the alleged offender and the school principal, and inability to determine Emmaus’s relevant written policies.  (UMFs 15-16.)

Moreover, Defendant argues that the Court in Deutsch v. Masonic Homes of California, Inc., which found that a similar revival provision of section 340.1 did not violate the due process clause, was wrong.  (Deutsch v. Masonic Homes of California, Inc. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 748.)[1]

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that several appellate cases have addressed whether the revival of previously time-barred actions violates the due process clause and other constitutional provisions.  (Oppos. pp. 24-26.)  The same arguments presented by Defendant regarding the unavailability of witnesses were dismissed in Deutsch.  Furthermore, there are potentially hundreds of witnesses who are still available in the instant action.

Here, the Court finds that the matter of constitutionality of a similarly revived provision has already been determined by Deutsch.  (Deutsch, 164 Cal.App.4th 748.)  In Deutsch, the Court reviewed legislation passed in 2003, allowing plaintiffs with sexual abuse claims to revive their claims pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1, against individuals or entities who owned a duty of care to those plaintiffs.  (Ibid. at 752.)  First, the Court found that the provision was not unconstitutional on its face as appellant had no constitutional right to be free of the obligation to defend stale claims.  (Ibid. at 759-761.)  Second, the Court found that the provision was not unconstitutional as applied because the delay in bringing the action did not constitute state action and was not subject to the due process clause.  (Ibid. at 761-762.)

The Court finds that the instant case is analogous to Deutsch and notes that Defendant’s challenges to the constitutionality of a similar provision have been addressed by the Court of Appeals in Deutsch.  Thus, the Court denies Defendant’s Motion based on an unconstitutional violation of its due process rights.

V.        CONCLUSION AND ORDER

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.  Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first, second, and third causes of action is also DENIED.

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this ruling.
