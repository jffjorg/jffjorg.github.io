DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02397    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Zeferino Juarez Vazquez,

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

Uber Technologies, Inc., et al.

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    23VECV02397                             107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]: Denied.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On or about June 2, 2021, Plaintiff Zeferino Juarez Vazquez (“Plaintiff”) was riding a bicycle when they were hit by a vehicle driven by Defendants Caeros O. Durias and Does 1 through 10. (Complaint ¶¶ 20-21, 24.) Defendants PV Holding Corp. and Does 61 through 70 were the owners, lessors, and/or lessees of the vehicle. (Complaint ¶ 13.) At the time of the incident, Defendants Caeros O. Durias and Does 1 through 10 were providing services for Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc., Raiser, LLC, Raiser-CA, LLC, Raiser-DC, LLC, Raiser-PA, LLC, and Does 11 through 60. (Complaint ¶ 14.)

 

On June 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc., Raiser, LLC, Raiser-CA, LLC, Raiser-DC, LLC, Raiser-PA, LLC, Caeros O. Durias, PV Holding Corp., and Does 1 to 70, alleging causes of action for negligence/negligence per se, negligence (hiring, training, supervision and retention) and negligent entrustment.

 

On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel (“Counsel”) filed a motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”). No opposition has been filed.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284(2).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.     Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051));

 

2.     A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052));

 

3.     Service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case, and

 

4.     The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) “The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.” (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal.Rptr.2d 554, 559.)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

“A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-051).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a).)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

“The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(c).)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on Form MC-052 that “There has been a complete breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The client has repeatedly failed to comply with essential litigation requirements, including appearing for scheduled depositions and meeting court deadlines, despite numerous attempts to communicate and coordinate compliance. The client has also been unresponsive to communications and requests for necessary information and cooperation. Furthermore, the client has made explicit threats to report counsel to the State Bar, creating a situation where continued representation would be unethical and unworkable, impairing counsel's ability to effectively advocate on the client's behalf. Therefore, withdrawal is necessary to preserve counsel’s ethical obligations and avoid prejudice to the client.”

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

“The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service, electronic service, or mail.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).)

 

“If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.     The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

 

2.     The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.”

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1).)

 

“As used in this rule, ‘current’ means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned or no electronic delivery failure message was received is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011(b) applies.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).)

 

“When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40.” (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

The Motion does not comply with this requirement. Counsel submits a proof of service showing Plaintiff was served by mail with the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and proposed order. (Proof of Service by Mail filed 10/23/24.) Counsel also submits a declaration stating Counsel confirmed by telephone and conversation, within the past thirty days, that the address is current. (Declaration p. 1.) However, Rule 3.1362(d) requires the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order to be served on Counsel’s client “and on all other parties who have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d).) Counsel’s submitted proof of service and declaration do not show the documents were served on any parties other than Plaintiff.

(D) Proposed Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

“The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel-Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies the motion.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

Demurrer with Motion to Strike  

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

Sustain with leave to amend.

¿ 

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

On or about May 1, 2022, Plaintiff Amir Baher (“Plaintiff”) was crossing an intersection when he was struck by a vehicle driven by John Doe. (Complaint p. 6.) John Doe fled the scene. (Complaint p. 6.) On April 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against John Doe and Does 1 to 50, alleging four causes of action for motor vehicle, general negligence, violation of Vehicle Code 20001(a) – misdemeanor hit and run, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. (Complaint pp. 5-8.)

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint, substituting the true name of Defendant John Doe as Eugenia Berrio (“Defendant”).

 

On October 16, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer with motion to strike. On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike. On November 8, 2024, Defendant filed replies.

 

II.             PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

a.     Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40(a).)

 

Defendant submits an exhibit showing the parties agreed to extend the time to file a responsive pleading to October 16, 2024. (Ex. C; Boyd Declaration ¶ 7.) Defendant filed the demurrer with motion to strike on October 16, 2024.

 

This court finds that the demurrer with motion to strike was timely filed.

b.     Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure section¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating they called Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss the deficiencies in the complaint and sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter detailing the deficiencies in the complaint. (Boyd Declaration ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel states the parties did not reach an agreement. (Boyd Declaration ¶ 8.)

 

This court finds that Defendant satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).) A party may also demur because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

 

Demurrer for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s four causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain. (Notice of Demurrer pp. 1-2.)

 

Plaintiff’s opposition states he agreed to withdraw his third and fourth causes of action and the punitive damages request.  (Opposition to Demurrer p. 5.) Accordingly, this court only considers Defendant’s arguments relating to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action.

 

A.    Statute of Limitations

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s causes of action are barred by the statute of limitations because Plaintiff failed to timely substitute Defendant into the lawsuit. (Demurrer p. 7.) Defendant further argues “[t]he Complaint does not allege sufficient facts demonstrating why Plaintiff was ignorant of [Defendant’s] identity” and “the proposed DOE amendment contains no facts to support the substitution of Defendant, e.g., including a statement from Plaintiff explaining the precise date Plaintiff or Plaintiff[‘s] Counsel learned of Defendant’s true identity or any explanation of whether and why her identity could not have been learned before the doe amendment was filed.” (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

Actions involving injuries to an individual “caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another” are governed by a two-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 335.1.)

 

“When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint . . .  and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 474.) “Section 474 allows a plaintiff who is ignorant of a defendant's identity to commence suit—before the statute of limitations runs—by using a fictitious name for that defendant and then amending her complaint when the defendant's true name is discovered.” (Hahn v. New York Air Brake LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 895, 898.) “Section 474 contains two mandatory requirements, each of which must be met before the benefits of the statute may be claimed by a plaintiff.” (Stephens v. Berry (1967) 249 Cal.App.2d 474, 477.) “The first requirement is that of ignorance of the true name of the defendant.” (Id.) “The second requirement of section 474 is that a plaintiff make a clear statement in his pleading that he is ignorant of the true name of the defendant sued by a fictitious name.” (Id.)

 

 “If the statute's requirements are satisfied, the amendment relates back and the substituted defendant is considered to have been a party from the action's start.” (Hahn v. New York Air Brake LLC, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at p. 898.) “[W]here an amendment is sought after the statute of limitations has run, the amended complaint will be deemed filed as of the date of the original complaint provided recovery is sought in both pleadings on the same general set of facts.” (Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 Cal.2d 596, 600.) Section 474 does not identify a “specific procedure” and courts have held that a defendant may employ “a motion for dismissal, or for summary judgment or a demurrer” to challenge a plaintiff’s failure to comply with section 474. (See Maier Brewing Co. v. Flora Crane Service, Inc. (1969) 270 Cal.App.2d 873, 875.)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges the accident occurred on May 1, 2022. (Complaint p. 6.) Plaintiff filed the complaint on April 19, 2024. Plaintiff filed the amendment substituting Defendant for John Doe on August 6, 2024. Accordingly, the issue is whether Plaintiff satisfied section 474’s requirements, such that the amendment relates back to the complaint.

 

Plaintiff’s amendment substituted Defendant’s name for John Doe’s name in the original complaint but added no new facts. (Amendment to Complaint; Complaint.) Thus, recovery is sought in both pleadings on the same general set of facts.

 

The next issue is whether Plaintiff satisfied section 474’s requirements and properly substituted Defendant for John Doe.

 

Citing Victor Woo v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 169, 176, Ingram v. Superior Court (1979) 98 Cal.App.3d 483, 490-492, and A.N. v. County of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1068, Defendant argues “[t]he general rule in California is that the addition of a new defendant does not relate back to the date of filing of the original complaint and the statute of limitations is applied as of the date of the amendment” and “the proposed DOE amendment contains no facts to support the substitution of Defendant.” (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

In Woo v. Superior Court, supra, 75 Cal.App.4th at p. 176, “[t]he amended complaint add[ed a defendant] as a defendant but d[id] not identify him as a substitute for a previously named fictitious defendant.” In Ingram v. Superior Court, supra, 98 Cal.App.3d at p. 490, “[t]he complaint charge[d] that [a defendant’s] negligence caused petitioner’s injuries” but did “not designate [the defendant] or his estate as a defendant, entirely omitting them from the caption where defendants are listed.” Further, the Court stated that “the petitioner did not attempt to substitute the [defendant] for one of the Does, nor does it appear under the circumstances that he would have been entitled to do so[.]” (Id. at p. 491.)

 

Finally, in A.N. v. County of Los Angeles, supra, 171 Cal.App.4th at p. 1060, less than a month before trial, the plaintiff filed and served four Doe amendments and served two previously filed Doe amendments. “The individual defendants filed a ‘motion to quash’ the Doe amendments on the ground that [the plaintiff] had unreasonably delayed in filing and serving the amendments.”  (Id.) The trial court granted the motion and the Court of Appeal affirmed. (Id.) The Court stated that “section 474 includes an implicit requirement that a plaintiff may not ‘unreasonably delay’ his or her filing of a Doe amendment after learning a defendant’s identity[,]” “a defendant named in an action by a Doe amendment under section 474 may challenge the amendment by way of an evidence-based motion, which argues that the plaintiff ‘unreasonabl[y] delayed’ his or her filing of the challenged amendment” and “’unreasonable delay’ . . . includes a prejudice element, which requires a showing by the defendant that he or she would suffer prejudice from plaintiff’s delay in filing the Doe amendment.” (Id. at pp. 1066-1067.) The Court found these elements were satisfied in A.N. because there was no reasonable explanation for plaintiff’s two-year delay in filing and serving the Doe amendments, and the defendants were prejudiced because they “were brought into the case less than one month before the case was sent to begin trial, and it does not require speculation to recognize that a party who is drawn into litigation on the eve of trial will face difficulties in preparing a defense in such short order.” (Id. at 1068.)

 

Plaintiff filed an amendment substituting Defendant for John Doe. Thus, Woo and Ingram do not apply here because those cases involved situations where the parties did not identify the added defendant as a substitute for a previously named fictious defendant. Further, unlike A.N., where the defendants were substituted in less than a month before trial, no trial has been set here. Accordingly, Defendant has not been prejudiced by the delay and it is unnecessary for this court to determine whether Plaintiff unreasonably delayed filing his amendment.

 

Defendant also argues “[t]he Complaint does not allege sufficient facts demonstrating why Plaintiff was ignorant of [Defendant’s] identity.” (Demurrer p. 7.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) Plaintiff alleges in the complaint that “[t]he true names of defendants sued as Does are unknown to plaintiff.” (Complaint p. 2.)  Plaintiff also alleges that John Doe “immediately fled the scene after the collision.” (Complaint p. 6.) Nothing in the complaint indicates Plaintiff knew Defendant’s identity when the complaint was filed. Thus, Plaintiff’s complaint sufficiently alleges that Plaintiff was ignorant of Defendant’s identity when the complaint was filed.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s causes of action are not barred by the statute of limitations.

                                                                                                                                 

B.    Causes of Action

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state a cause of action for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence because the “causes of action are factually vague and ambiguous[.]” (Demurrer p. 8.)

The elements of a negligence action are: (1) a legal duty to use due care; (2) a breach of such legal duty; and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917-918.)

Plaintiff does not specifically allege that Defendant John Doe owed Plaintiff a legal duty to use due care.

Plaintiff alleges Defendant John Doe breached a duty by alleging Defendant John Doe “operated his vehicle . . . and entered the intersection without caution and in such an unsafe manner that it violently collided into Plaintiff’s person.” (Complaint p. 6.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant John Doe “negligently, carelessly, unskillfully, unlawfully, tortuously, wrongfully, maliciously, willfully, oppressively, and despicably entrusted, managed, maintained, operated, controlled and drove said vehicle.” (Complaint p. 6.)

Finally, Plaintiff alleges Defendant John Doe’s breach was a proximate and legal cause of Plaintiff’s resulting injury by alleging, that, “[a]s a direct and proximate result of said acts and omissions on the part of the Defendants and each of them, Plaintiff was hurt, and physically injured, sustaining medical bills, loss of earnings/earnings capacity, pain, suffering, loss of enjoyment of life and other damages, as well as substantial property damage, loss of use, towing, storage and other associated charges and damages, and will continue to sustain such damages in the future.” (Complaint p. 6.)

Finally, Plaintiff’s amendment identified Defendant as the substitute for the complaint’s fictitious name, John Doe.

Because Plaintiff does not sufficiently allege that Defendant owed Plaintiff a legal duty to use care, this court sustains the demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action.

C.    Uncertainty

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action for negligence and motor vehicle negligence are uncertain. (Notice of Motion p. 2.) Defendant further argues the complaint is insufficient because “[i]t is unclear from the vague allegations if either [Defendant] or her vehicle were involved in the subject motor vehicle accident, as the Complaint alleges that the driver is a male operating ‘his’ vehicle.” (Demurrer p. 8.)

 

Demurrer for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in his complaint the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)

 

This court disagrees with Defendant that the complaint is uncertain because it identifies the operator of the vehicle as a male, while Defendant is a woman. (Demurrer p. 8; Reply to Opposition to Demurrer p. 4.) However, the complaint alleges that “Defendant operated his vehicle at or near Victory Blvd. and Balboa Blvd. and entered the intersection without caution and in such an unsafe manner that it violently collided into Plaintiff's person” and “Defendant JOHN DOE immediately fled the scene after the collision.” (Complaint p. 6.) This paragraph could be interpreted in one of two ways. First, it could be interpreted as alleging one of the defendants was operating the vehicle, and Defendant was merely a passenger or bystander who fled after the collision. Or it could be interpreted as alleging the Defendant was operating the vehicle and Defendant fled after the collision. This court finds that the complaint is uncertain because it does not clearly allege which defendant was operating the vehicle when it struck Plaintiff or whether it was the person now identified as Defendant. (Complaint p. 6.)

 

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 297, 303-304.)¿ 

 

This court finds that there is a reasonable possibility Plaintiff can successfully amend the complaint and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

VI.           MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Defendant seeks an order striking portions of Plaintiff’s complaint. (Motion to Strike p. 4.) This court has sustained the demurrer and granted Plaintiff leave to amend. Accordingly, the motion to strike is moot.

 

VII.         CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer and grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

 

Motion to Quash Subpoena Duces Tecum

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]: Granted

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On April 24, 2022, Plaintiff Nelson Alexander Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) was driving a vehicle that collided with Defendant Levy Sharon’s (“Defendant”) vehicle. (Complaint ¶¶ 3, 5.) On April 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 10 alleging causes of action for negligence and negligence per se.

 

On October 22, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to quash service of summons (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed no opposition.

 

II.             PROCEDURAL ISSUES

 

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion. . . [t]o quash service of summons[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10(a).) Generally, a responsive pleading is due within thirty days after service of the summons. (Code Civ. Proc., § 412.20(a)(3).)

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service showing Defendant was served by personal service on April 29, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 05/07/24.) Service of a summons “by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served . . .  is deemed complete at the time of such delivery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)

 

Defendant filed the Motion on October 22, 2024, which is more than thirty days after service of the summons. But Plaintiff has not objected or sought to strike the Motion as untimely, so this court will consider the Motion. (See In re Marriage of Obrecht (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 1, 16-17.)

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

“A summons may be served by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)

 

“A defendant, . . . may serve and file a notice of motion…to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿418.10(a)(1).) A defendant may move to quash service of summons for lack of jurisdiction based on ineffective service of process. (See Ziller Elecs. Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1229.)

 

When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) The plaintiff must establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence. (Aquila, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568.)

 

“The filing of a proof of service declaration ordinarily creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper, but only if the service declaration ‘complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.’” (Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1164.) Further, that presumption may be rebutted by contrary evidence submitted by a defendant. (See, e.g., Fernandes v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932, 941, fn. 6.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

If service of summons is not properly and validly effectuated by one or more of the approved methods, California courts lack personal jurisdiction over the defendants, and any judicial actions taken without such service violates principles of fundamental due process and is therefore void. (County of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1226-1227.)¿

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s proof of service does not satisfy the statutory requirements because “Plaintiff failed to serve Defendant given that Defendant has not been residing at the address listed in the proof of service for more than one year” and “Plaintiff never offered any evidence indicating that this address was current and still a valid address for Defendant.” (Motion pp. 3-4.)

 

Plaintiff submitted a proof of service showing Defendant was served by personal service on April 29, 2024, at 18010 Collins St Encino, CA 91316-1031. (Proof of Service filed 05/07/24.) Thus, Plaintiff’s proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper. (See Lebel v. Mai, supra, 210 Cal.App.4th at 1163.)

 

However, that presumption can be rebutted by Defendant’s contrary evidence. (See Fernandez v. Singh, supra, 16 Cal.App.5th at 941, fn. 6.)

 

Defendant submits a declaration stating Defendant “was not served with the summons and complaint, nor was it delivered to [Defendant] in any other authorized fashion.” (Levy Declaration ¶ 3.) Defendant also states Defendant “was never served via personal service on April 29, 2024” and has “not lived at 18010 Collins St., Encino, CA 91316-1031 for at least one year.” (Levy Declaration ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendant’s declaration rebuts the presumption that Plaintiff was properly served by personal service on April 29, 2024.

 

Thus, this court finds that service was improper.

 

V.               CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s Motion is granted.
