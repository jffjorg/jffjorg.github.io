DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCP03439    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 73

9/04/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

THOMAS WESLEY PENTZ v. MARCHEL GABRIELLE AUGUSTE (22STCP03439) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff Pentz/opposing party: Bryan Freedman; Brian Turnauer; Tamar Yeghiayan (Freedman Taitelman + Cooley, LLP)

Counsel for Defendant Auguste:  Marchel Gabrielle Auguste (Pro Per) 

 

PETITOINER PENTZ’S MOTION FOR AN ORDER ALLOWING ALTERNATIVE SERVICE OF (1) APPLICATION AND ORDER FOR APPEARANCE AND EXAMINATION, AND (2) CIVIL SUBPOENA (DUCES TECUM) TO RESPONDENT (filed 08/28/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Petitioner Pentz’s Motion is GRANTED. The Court grants Pentz’s request to electronically serve the (1) Application and Order for Appearance and Examination, and (2) Civil Subpoena (Duces Tecum) on Respondent Auguste.

BACKGROUND

 

On April 30, 2021, Plaintiff Thomas Wesley Pentz (“Pentz”) filed a Complaint (the “Pentz Lawsuit”) against Defendant Marchel Gabrielle Auguste, aka Shelly Auguste (“Auguste”). On September 16, 2021, Pentz filed a First Amended Complaint for (1) Stalking; (2) Trespass; and (3) Distribution of Private Materials (Revenge Pornography) Civ. Code section 1708.85.

 

Pentz alleges, among other things, in his Complaint that Auguste stalked Pentz online, showed up at his residence uninvited and repeatedly sent sexually explicit photographs and videos of Pentz to his friends and family.

 

On June 25, 2021, Marchel Gabrielle Auguste filed a Complaint (the “Auguste Lawsuit”) against Thomas Wesley Pentz. In Auguste’s First Amended Complaint filed on August 10, 2022, she alleges causes of action for (1) Sexual Battery; (2) Gender Violence; (3) Violation of the Ralph Civil Rights Act; (4) Violation of the Tom Bane Civil Rights Act; (5) Action Based on Civil Code § 1708.85; (6) Intentional Intrusion Into Private Affairs; (7) Battery; (8) Assault; (9) Defamation; (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (11) Fraud.  

  

Auguste has sued alleging, among other things, the following: (i) Pentz communicated with her when she was a minor; (ii) groomed her; (iii) gave her an STD; (iv) ejaculated inside her; (v) raped her; (vi) defamed her and (vii) committed revenge porn against her. Auguste alleges that all the aforementioned acts caused her severe emotional distress.

 

On August 1, 2021, Pentz filed a Notice of Related Case. On September 14, 2021, the Court ruled the Pentz Lawsuit 21STCV16321 and the Auguste Lawsuit 21STCV23647 were related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 21STCV16321, the Pentz Lawsuit, is the lead case.  

 

On September 21, 2022, Petitioner Pentz filed a petition to confirm arbitrator’s award in favor of Pents against Respondent Auguste, with the case no. 22STCP03439.

 

On April 28, 2023, the Court found the following cases, 21STCV16321 and 21STCV23647 and 22STCP03439, are related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a). 21STCV16321 is the lead case.  

 

On May 24, 2024, Petitioner Pentz filed the instant Motion for an Order Allowing Alternative Service of (1) Application and Order for Appearance and Examination, and (2) Civil Subpoena (Duces Tecum) to Respondent.

 

No opposition was filed.

 

On August 28, 2024, Pentz filed a notice of non-opposition.

 

ANALYSIS

JANET DIXON v. THE PEOPLE CONCERN (23STCV15571) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Janet Dixon (Pro Per) 

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Norman Ayotte (Jones & Ayotte) 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AN AMENDMENT TO THE COMPLAINT AND SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 07/31/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File an Amendment to the Complaint and Second Amended Complaint is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 5, 2023, Plaintiff Janet Dixon filed the instant action against Defendant The People Concern.

 

On June 12, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file First Amended Complaint.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following. Plaintiff is a resident of Defendant The People Concern, which is a temporary housing shelter. Plaintiff informed Defendant’s staff that she does not eat meat, such as beef, chicken or turkey. On April 27, 2023, a member of Defendant’s staff served Plaintiff dinner that contained chicken. The staff told Plaintiff the chicken was vegan. However, Plaintiff alleges the chicken was not vegan and that after eating the food, she became very ill. Plaintiff alleges she was ill 3 days following the incident and it took her a day and a half to recover. She alleges that she reported the issue to Defendant’s staff, but they still continue to serve her meat.

 

On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to File an Amendment to the Complaint and Second Amended Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff seeks leave to file an amendment to the complaint and a Second Amended Complaint to recover damages for additional acts of (gross) negligent, unlawful, and or inadequate care performed onto the Plaintiff that materially differ from the allegations already presented in the First Amended Complaint filed on June 5th, 2024.

·       The allegations Plaintiff proposes in the amendment to complaint and Second Amended Complaint commenced on or about July 20th, 2023, after the Plaintiff submitted her complaint.

·       The proposed amendment provides an addition of 15 causes of action that are materially different.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally defective

·       It appears that the Plaintiff is asking for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint, although the motion is also portrayed as seeking to amend the current complaint. The leave requested is mutually exclusive – the Plaintiff cannot have both.

·       Assuming that the Plaintiff is seeking leave to file an entirely new pleading (which it appears she is), then she does not fulfill the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324.

o   Plaintiff does not state when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered. (CRC Rules 3.1324(b)(3).) Plaintiff’s supporting declaration also fails to offer any explanation as to why the amendment was not made earlier. (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324(b)(4). The motion fails to state meaningfully why the amendments are necessary and proper. (CRC Rules 3.1324(2))

 

Plaintiff filed a reply stating that Defendant’s opposition is untimely and should be disregarded. Plaintiff also maintains that her motion to file an amendment and SAC is warranted.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Timeliness of Opposition

 

As an initial matter, Plaintiff argues in her reply that Defendant’s opposition was untimely and should be disregarded.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). However, a court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.)

 

Here, Defendant filed its opposition on August 20, 2024. The Court finds Defendant’s opposition is timely as it was filed at least nine court days prior to the hearing date of September 4, 2024. Thus, the Court considers Defendant’s opposition.

 

B.     Legal Standard

 

The trial court has discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding.” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.) “An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion.” (Ibid.)   

 

Under Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, a separate declaration must accompany a motion for leave to amend and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) A party seeking leave to amend must attach a copy of the proposed pleading to the motion for leave to amend. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)  

 

C.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order granting Plaintiff leave to file an amendment to the Complaint and Second Amended Complaint. Plaintiff provides a declaration in support of the instant motion, an Appendix of Proposed Changes to the First Amended Complaint and separately filed the proposed Second Amended Complaint.

 

However, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has complied with the requirements of Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).

 

Under Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, Plaintiff’s declaration must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)

 

Here, Plaintiff’s declaration states “[t]he allegations added in the Second Amended Complaint transpired after the original Complaint was filed on July 5th, 2023, with the first allegation beginning on or about July 20th, 2023 and the most recent dating to February 8th, 2024. Plaintiff also needed to file her First Amended Complaint before proceeding to file the amendment to the complaint, as the allegations are materially different. Plaintiff was also collecting evidence to form her case.” (Dixon Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

The Court does not find Plaintiff’s declaration specifies when each of the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered or why the amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) Plaintiff states the alleged acts all occurred before February 8, 2024, which predates the filing of her First Amended Complaint. Thus, Plaintiff does not sufficiently explain in her declaration why she did not make the amendments earlier.

 

Since Plaintiff has not complied with the requirements of Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b), Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File an Amendment to the Complaint and Second Amended Complaint is DENIED.

SCHUMAN v. CITIBANK, N.A.  (23STCV22314)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Wendy Giberti (iGeneral Counsel, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Alexandra Whitworth (Bryan Cave Leighton Paisner LLP)

 

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 04/08/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff Steven Schuman filed this action against Defendants Citibank N.A. and Jesse Nathan Tyre-Karp. This action arises from alleged wrongful actions regarding a loan refinance as to a property located at 1500 North Beverly Drive, Beverly Hills, CA 90210 (the “Beverly Property”).

 

On March 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) fraud, and (3) promissory estoppel.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff is and has been the owner of the single-family residence commonly known as 1500 North Beverly Drive, Beverly Hills, California 90210 (the “Beverly Property”). On or about March 15, 2022, Plaintiff entered an escrow to purchase a new home located at 691 Sheehy, Nipomo, California (the “Sheehy Property”) for a purchase price of $2,905,000. On or about March 16, 2022, Plaintiff contacted Tyre-Karp, who agreed to assist with a purchase money loan for the Sheehy Property as well as help to refinance the Beverly Property on advantageous terms: $1,500,000 loan, 3.375% fixed for 30 years and no points. Schuman ultimately decided to cancel the purchase of the Sheehy Property but was still interested in the refinance loan.

 

On April 20, 2022, Citibank issued a conditional loan approval. (FAC, Ex. 3.) The conditional loan approval stated that Schuman was required, as a condition of the loan, to deposit at least $50,000 of new funds in a Citibank account. Tyre-Karp told Schuman that if he deposited $500,000, the rate would drop to 3.125%. On April 22, 2022, Schuman deposited over $500,000 into two Citibank accounts paying nominal interest. On May 5, 2022, Citibank issued a refinance “Mortgage Loan Commitment,” confirming the terms: $1,500,000 loan, 3.125% fixed for 30 years and no points. As of May 5, 2022, Citibank had begun performance pursuant to the agreed upon contract. In particular, Citibank unilaterally and without Plaintiff’s knowledge or consent, closed Plaintiff’s home equity line of credit (HELOC) with First Citizens Bank. Schuman knew and understood that the HELOC would be closed when the loan from Citibank closed. Schuman never agreed, however, to allow Citibank to close the HELOC in advance and without making the loan.

 

Thereafter, on May 6, 2022, Citibank sent Schuman a Closing Disclosure, which contained new and materially different terms, specifically that Citibank proposed to take about 90% of the cash to borrower and use that money to pay off two unrelated loans on investment properties in Seattle. At no time was paying those two loans off a condition of the loan. When Schuman refused to close on those terms, Tyre-Karp stated that the Loan had to be resubmitted to underwriting.

 

Schuman reasonably relied on Citibank to make the loan, both by performing as set forth above, but also by not seeking the same or a similar loan otherwise and by proceeding through that point with the purchase of a second home. Schuman spent at least 30 hours, and probably much more than that, collecting documents requested by Citibank and communicating with Citibank to get this loan. Schuman’s actions changed his position to his detriment based on Citibank’s promise to perform.

On April 8, 2024, Defendants filed a Demurrer to the FAC, arguing:

·       The first, second and third causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action

·       First cause of action for breach of contract fails because: (1) Plaintiff does not allege the existence of a valid contract, only a conditional loan approval; (2) the alleged contract is barred by the statute of frauds; and (3) Plaintiff does not allege damages caused by Citibank

·       Plaintiff’s fraud claims all fail because Plaintiff does not allege fraud with the required specificity. The economic loss rule also bars Plaintiff’s causes of action.

·       Plaintiff’s promissory estoppel cause of action fails because Plaintiff fails to identify any clear and unambiguous promise. Plaintiff also does not sufficiently plead detrimental reliance.

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Breach of contract: Plaintiff sufficiently pleads breach of contract. The Final Loan agreement is an enforceable contract. The statute of frauds does not bar this claim because Defendant partially performed. Plaintiff also sufficiently alleges damages.

·       Fraud: the economic loss doctrine does not apply to actual fraud. Further, Plaintiff has pled fraud with required specificity.

·       Promissory estoppel: The FAC adequately alleges a promise, precise and reasonable reliance thereon, and very specific resulting damages.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of: The Court’s ruling on Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s initial Complaint.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) The Court may take judicial notice of its own file. (See Stepan v. Garcia (1974) 43 Cal.App.3d 497, 500.)

 

The Court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

C.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel on March 19, 2024. (Root Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

D.     First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) “The terms of a contract are reasonably certain if they provide a basis for determining the existence of a breach and for giving an appropriate remedy.” (Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 777.) “Where a contract is so uncertain and indefinite that the intention of the parties in material particulars cannot be ascertained, the contract is void an unenforceable.” (Ibid., internal quotations omitted.)

 

“[A] loan commitment is not binding on the lender unless it contains all of the material terms of the loan, and either the lender’s obligation is unconditional or the stated conditions have been satisfied.” (Peterson Development Co. v. Torrey Pines Bank (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 103, 115.) “The material terms of a loan include the identity of the lender and borrower, the amount of the loan, and the terms for repayment.” (Ibid.) An “agreement to agree . . . is unenforceable under California law.” (Bustamante v. Intuit, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 199, 213.) “Preliminary negotiations or [agreements] for future negotiations are not the functional equivalent of a valid, subsisting agreement.” (Id. at p. 213-214.)

 

First, Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to allege the existence of a valid contract, but rather only a conditional loan approval.

 

The Court agrees. As the Court discussed in its order sustaining the breach of contract claim in the original Complaint, the Court does not find the exhibits attached to the FAC support Plaintiff’s allegation that he and Defendant Citibank entered into a contract for a refinancing loan.

 

As the Court previously found, it appears the Mortgage Loan Commitment is a notification that Plaintiff’s loan application was approved and that such approval was subject to further conditions. (FAC, Ex. 5.) The Mortgage Loan Commitment has a Borrower’s Checklist attached thereto which states that “[i]n order to complete the processing of [Plaintiff’s] real estate loan application” further documents were required and if such documents were not approved then any conditional approval would be revoked. (FAC; Ex. 5 at Borrower’s Checklist, p.1.) Moreover, the Mortgage Loan Commitment provides that Defendant Citibank reserved the right to terminate the Mortgage Loan Commitment “prior to the settlement of the loan in the event of an adverse change in [Plaintiff’s] personal or financial status.” (FAC, Ex. 5 at Mortgage Loan Commitment at p.1.) The Closing Disclosure—which was sent to Plaintiff after the Mortgage Loan Commitment—gave Plaintiff an opportunity to review his loan prior to the closing date. (FAC, Ex. 6.) Taken together, the exhibits attached to the FAC evidence a series of negotiations and “agreements to agree.” The Mortgage Loan Commitment further provides: “We reserve the right to verify your credit, income, assets, and employment at any time.” The Broker’s Checklist provides: “Other information may be requested during the processing of your loan which are not known at this time.” This is even more evident given Plaintiff’s allegation that he refused to close on the terms in the Mortgage Loan Commitment and Closing Disclosure, which led to the loan being resubmitted to underwriting. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants partially performed by closing Plaintiff’s existing HELOC, an act which was necessary to close the new loan. However, “a loan commitment is not binding on the lender unless it contains all the material terms of the loan, and either the lender’s obligation is unconditional, or the stated conditions have been satisfied.” (Peterson Dev. Co. v. Torrey Pines Bank (1991) 233 Cal. App.3d 103, 115.) Thus, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s argument persuasive. The Court does not find Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a valid contract.

 

Based on this ground, the Court SUSTAINS the first cause of action with leave to amend. Thus, the Court does not discuss the parties arguments as to whether the first cause of action also fails due to the statute of frauds or for failure to sufficiently plead damages.

 

E.      Second Cause of Action: Fraud

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action for fraud.

 

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiffs must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

The FAC alleges the following. Defendants repeatedly lied to Plaintiff regarding the amount of cash, almost $1,000,000, he would receive when the Loan closed. At all times, Defendants knew Plaintiff would not receive anywhere close to $1,000,000 of the promised cash out at closing, as they intended to impose the additional condition that he pay off the two Seattle loans through escrow. Plaintiff reasonably relied on the representations made by Defendants regarding the amount of cash out at closing. Defendants knew Schuman intended to move, as the matter had been discussed repeatedly in writing. Defendants repeatedly represented that that was not a problem, and that remaining in the Beverly Property was not a condition of the loan. Yet, in fact, Defendants always intended to require Schuman to execute a deed of trust stating that he had to remain in the Beverly Property for at least 12 months, on pain of foreclosure. Defendants then engaged in a last minute “bait and switch,” which rendered the Loan of little or no value to Plaintiff. Specifically, Defendants knew the locked rate expired on May 20, 2022, just two weeks after Citibank switched the Loan terms, leaving Schuman no time to obtain other financing. (FAC, ¶¶ 49-56.)

 

First, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s fraud cause of action fails because it is barred by the economic loss rule. The damages for Plaintiff’s fraud claim are the same as his damages for breach of contract. (FAC, ¶ 60.) Therefore, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not identified a duty separate from the breach of contract. (See Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 554 (“A breach of contract is tortious only when some independent duty arising from tort law is violated”).)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that “Defendants engaged in a scheme, created at the outset, to lure him into a contract, deprive him of his liquid assets, and then bind him to an entirely different contract.” (Oppos., p. 10.) Thus, Plaintiff contends his fraud claim is not barred by the economic loss rule because Defendants’ conduct in fraudulently inducing him to enter a contract is separate from Defendants’ later breach of the contract. (See Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828.)

 

However, as discussed above, the Court does not find Plaintiff sufficiently alleged the existence of a valid contract. Instead, the Mortgage Loan Commitment only indicates that Plaintiff’s loan application was approved and that such approval was subject to further conditions. Therefore, since Plaintiff has not shown the existence of a valid contract, Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendants lured him into a contract fails.

 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

F.       Third Cause of Action: Promissory Estoppel

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for promissory estoppel.

 

“The elements of a promissory estoppel claim are (1) a promise clear and unambiguous in its terms; (2) reliance by the party to whom the promise is made; (3) the reliance must be both reasonable and foreseeable; and (4) the party asserting the estoppel must be injured by his reliance.” (Flintco Pacific, Inc. v. TEC Management Consultants, Inc. (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 727, 734, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant Citibank made a clear and unambiguous written promise to loan Plaintiff $1,500,000 at a fixed rate of 3.375% with a thirty-year term.” (FAC, ¶ 63.) Plaintiff relied on Citibank’s promise by depositing $500,000 in a separate Citibank account, foregoing other lenders, by paying for an appraisal, and by spending upwards of 30 hours providing documents and answering questions. (FAC, ¶ 64.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not stated a cause of action for promissory estoppel. A conditional commitment is “not a clear and unambiguous offer.” (Laks v. Coast Fed. Sav. & Loan Assn. (1976) 60 Cal.App.3d 885, 891.) A letter of commitment cannot serve as the basis for a promissory estoppel claim unless “the lender’s obligation is unconditional or the stated conditions have been satisfied.” (Peterson Development Co. v. Torrey Pines Bank (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 103, 115.) As discussed above, the purported Conditional Loan Approval and Mortgage Loan Commitments appear to be conditional commitments. Thus, Plaintiff’s promissory estoppel claim fails because Plaintiff has not alleged a clear and unambiguous promise.

 

Therefore, the Court SUSTAINS the third cause of action with leave to amend.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.   

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff Dejur McDaughtery filed the instant employment action against Defendant Serenity Recuperative Care, Inc.

 

On February 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Gender based harassment in violation of FEHA;

2.      Discrimination on the basis of gender identity in violation of FEHA;

3.      Discrimination on the basis of gender expression in violation of FEHA;

4.      Failure to investigate and prevent discrimination and harassment from occurring in violation of FEHA;

5.      Wrongful termination in violation of public policy;

6.      Violation of Labor Code section 1102.5.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff is a transgender male. (FAC, ¶ 1.) He was born a female but is recognizably a male. Plaintiff began working at Defendant in or around October 2022. (Ibid.) At the beginning of Plaintiff’s employment, Defendant and staff was made aware of Plaintiff’s preferred pronouns, and staff had been calling Plaintiff by his preferred pronouns (he/him). (FAC, ¶ 2.)  However, as time went on, certain staff members began utilizing “she/her” pronouns to refer to Plaintiff. (Ibid.) After numerous occasions, Plaintiff made formal complaints and was later transferred to another facility. (Ibid.)

 

On June 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Request for Default Judgment. Plaintiff seeks judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $1,251,277.74, consisting of $1,200,000 in damages, $39,452.05 in interest, $10,070 in attorney fees and $1,705.69 in costs. 

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.)

Here, Plaintiff submitted a declaration in support of his request for default judgment. In Plaintiff’s declaration he provides a summary of the case and computation of prejudgment interest. (McDaughtery Decl., ¶¶ 1-10, 12.)

 

However, the Court finds Plaintiff must provide additional evidence supporting his claimed damages. “[I]f the relief requested in the complaint is more complicated than that, consisting of either nonmonetary relief, or monetary relief in amounts which require either an accounting, additional evidence, or the exercise of judgment to ascertain (such as emotional distress damages, pain and suffering, or punitive damages), the plaintiff must request entry of judgment by the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 585, subd. (b).) In such cases, the plaintiff must affirmatively establish his entitlement to the specific judgment requested.” (Kim v. Westmoore Partners, Inc. (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 267, 287.) The Court may award emotional distress damages “as appears by the evidence to be just,” or order the damages be assessed by a jury. (Code Civ. Proc. § 585(b).)

 

Plaintiff seeks general and special damages in the amount of $400,000 and punitive damages in the amount of $800,000 which is solely supported by the following statement: “for the severe emotional distress caused by the severity of Defendants’ harassment, discrimination, and retaliation.” (McDaughtery Decl., ¶ 11.) Accordingly, the declaration fails to meet the particularity requirement of Code of Civil Procedure section 585(d): “[t]he facts stated in the affidavit or affidavits shall be within the personal knowledge of the affiant and shall be set forth with particularity.” The Court cannot grant any emotional distress damages based upon the evidence provided.

Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

INTERIOR EXPERTS GENERAL BULDERS, INC. v. THE WCCD, INC., et al. (24STCV10449) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Nick Campbell (Builders Law Group, Inc.) 

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Kyle Kring (Kring & Chung, LLP) 

 

MOTION TO TRANSFER action (second and sixth causes of action) to proper venue (filed 8/07/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion. The Court grants Defendants’ request to transfer the first, second, fifth and sixth causes of action to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendants’ request for attorneys fees against Plaintiff and their attorney of record in the reduced amount of $7,105.00. Said attorneys fees are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On April 25, 2024, Plaintiff Interior Experts General Builders, Inc., filed the instant action against Defendants The WCCD, Inc.; Centermark Properties, Inc.; The Chainery, Inc.; Westfield Topanga Owner, LLC; Lovisa America, LLC; UTC Venture, LLC; and Melrose, Crown, LLC.

 

The Complaint asserts the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Contract (Count I)

2.      Foreclosure of Mechanics’ Lien (Count I)

3.      Breach of Contract (Count II)

4.      Foreclosure of Mechanics’ Lien (Count II)

5.      Breach of Contract (Count III)

6.      Foreclosure of Mechanics’ Lien (Count III)

7.      Breach of Contract (Count IV)

8.      Foreclosure of Mechanics’ Lien (Count IV)

9.      Breach of Contract (Count V)

10.  Recovery of Mechanics’ Lien Release Bonds(s)

11.  Open Book Account

12.  Account Stated

13.  Quantum Meruit

 

According to the Complaint, Plaintiff filed claims relating to breach of contract involving four separate properties it worked on under Defendant The WCCD, Inc. Additionally, Plaintiff seeks to foreclose on four of those properties for which it recorded mechanic's liens.

 

On August 7, 2024, Defendants’ filed a Motion to Transfer Action (Second and Sixth Causes of Action) to Proper Venue, arguing:

·       Defendants move this Court for an Order dismissing, without prejudice, or transferring the venue for the Second Cause of Action for Foreclosure of Mechanics Lien (Count 1) and the Sixth Cause of Action for Foreclosure of Mechanics Lien (Count III) to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure sections 396b and 397(a) as venue in this court is improper because the two properties are located in San Diego County.

·       Defendants also assert in their Notice that the First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract and the Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract, relating to the two properties in San Diego, should also be transferred to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego pursuant to the doctrine of Forum Non Conveniens as set forth in California Code of Civil Procedure sections 410.30 and 418.10.

·       Defendants are requesting $9,355.00 in attorney fees and costs pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 396b (b) and section 399(a).

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendants’ request for transfer is inappropriate. Plaintiff is located in Chino, California. Defendant, The WCCD, Inc. is in located in Los Angeles, California. Counsel for plaintiff is located in Los Angeles and counsel for the Defendants is located in Orange County. Two of the properties involved in the lien claims (the fourth and eighth causes of action) are located in Los Angeles.

o   Here, there are common questions of fact and law as it relates to the contractual relations between Plaintiff and The WCCD, Inc., the work performed by Plaintiff, the value of that work and whether or not the remaining Defendants are subject to the remedy of foreclosure.

o   There is very little inconvenience to any potential parties or witnesses as only a minute portion of the overall claims related to the San Diego properties and most of the claims are against The WCCD, Inc.

o   Judicial economy will not be promoted. Judicial resources and facilities will be more efficiently utilized if the claims are kept consolidated because there will be a single judge in a single courtroom hearing any and all pretrial motions and a single trial which will avoid duplication of judicial and staff time in two separate counties

·       The request for attorneys fees is inappropriate and excessive

·       Plaintiff filed this opposition late due to a mistake in calendaring

 

Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s mechanics lien claims as to the two San Diego properties are untimely because they were not filed in the correct county.

·       Plaintiff’s attempt to coordinate and/or consolidate actions via its opposition is inappropriate

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Late Filed Opposition

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to timely file its opposition.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). 

 

Here, the motion is set for hearing on September 4, 2024 and Plaintiff’s opposition was to be filed and served no later than August 21, 2024. Plaintiff’s opposition was filed on August 26, 2024 one day before Defendants’ reply was due. Plaintiff asserts the opposition was filed late because of counsel’s mistake in calendaring the hearing on this motion. (Oppos., p. 4.) The Court does not find Plaintiff’s excuse warranted the late filing. However, a court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.) Since Defendants were able to respond to Plaintiff’s opposition in its Reply, the Court, in its discretion, considers the arguments raised in Plaintiff’s late filed opposition.

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

The court may, on motion, transfer an action to the proper court.  CCP § 396(b).  The moving party must overcome the presumption that the action is filed in the proper court.  Therefore, Defendants bear the burden to demonstrate that the plaintiff's venue selection is not proper under any of the statutory grounds.  Fontaine v. Sup. Ct. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 830, 836.  “Venue is determined based on the complaint on file at the time the motion to change venue is made.”  Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1984) 37 Cal.3d 477, 482. 

 

Pursuant to CCP § 392(a): “Subject to the power of the court to transfer actions and proceedings as provided in this title, the superior court in the county where the real property that is the subject of the action, or some part thereof, is situated, is the proper court for the trial of the following actions:

 

(1) For the recovery of real property, or of an estate or interest therein, or for the determination in any form, of that right or interest, and for injuries to real property.

(2) For the foreclosure of all liens and mortgages on real property.”

 

Code Civ. Proc. § 396b(a) provides as follows: “If an action or proceeding is commenced in a court having jurisdiction of the subject matter thereof, other than the court designated as the proper court for the trial thereof, under this title, the action may, notwithstanding, be tried in the court where commenced, unless the defendant, at the time he or she answers, demurs, or moves to strike, or, at his or her option, without answering, demurring, or moving to strike and within the time otherwise allowed to respond to the complaint, files with the clerk, a notice of motion for an order transferring the action or proceeding to the proper court, together with proof of service, upon the adverse party, of a copy of those papers.” “Upon the hearing of the motion the court shall, if it appears that the action or proceeding was not commenced in the proper court, order the action or proceeding transferred to the proper court.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 396b(a).) Code Civ. Proc. § 397(a) also provides “The court may, on motion, change the place of trial…[w]hen the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court.”

 

C.      Discussion

 

Defendants move the Court for an Order dismissing, without prejudice, or transferring the venue for the Second Cause of Action for Foreclosure of Mechanics Lien (Count 1) and the Sixth Cause of Action for Foreclosure of Mechanics Lien (Count III) to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure sections 396b and 397(a).

 

Defendant argues transfer is warranted because venue in this Court is improper since the real properties that are subject of the lien foreclosure actions are located in San Diego County, not Los Angeles County. According to the Complaint:

 

“On information and believe, at all relevant times hereto, CMPI was the owner of that real property commonly known as 3030 Plaza Bonita Rd., Space 1076, National City, CA 9150 (“Property 1”).” (Complaint, ¶ 9.)

 

“On information and belief, at all relevant times hereto, UTCV was the owner of that real property commonly known as 4545 La Jolla Village Dr. #F14, San Diego CA 92122 (“Property 3”).” (Complaint, ¶ 21.)

 

Thus, it is undisputed that the Properties 1 and 3 are located in San Diego County. Defendants also assert the First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract and the Fifth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract, relating to the two properties in San Diego, should also be transferred to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego pursuant to the doctrine of Forum Non Conveniens as set forth in California Code of Civil Procedure sections 410.30 and 418.10.

 

In Plaintiff’s opposition, Plaintiff cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 404.1, which provides that:

 

“Coordination of civil actions sharing a common question of fact or law is appropriate if one judge hearing all of the actions for all purposes in a selected site or sites will promote the ends of justice taking into account whether the common question of fact or law is predominating and significant to the litigation; the convenience of parties, witnesses, and counsel; the relative development of the actions and the work product of counsel; the efficient utilization of judicial facilities and manpower; the calendar of the courts; the disadvantages of duplicative and inconsistent rulings, orders, or judgments; and, the likelihood of settlement of the actions without further litigation should coordination be denied.”

 

However, the Court does not find that this provision is appropriate as CCP 404.1 applies to separate civil actions sharing a common question of fact or law. Here, the actions are currently not separately pending in different venues.

 

The Court finds transfer to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego is appropriate. In lien foreclosure actions, the action must be tried in the county in which the land is situated. (Case v. Kirkwood (1931) 119 Cal. App. 207, 211.) The proper court in lien foreclosure actions is a court of the county in which the land is located. (Douglas v. Donner Pines, Inc. (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 268, 270.) Since the first, second, fifth and sixth causes of action pertain to properties located in San Diego, the Court grants Defendants’ motion to transfer these causes of action to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego is appropriate.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff should be ordered to pay the reasonable expenses and attorney’s fees incurred in bringing this motion.

 

“In its discretion, the court may order the payment to the prevailing party of reasonable expenses and attorney’s fees incurred in making or resisting the motion to transfer whether or not that party is otherwise entitled to recover his or her costs of action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 396b(b).) “In determining whether that order for expenses and fees shall be made, the court shall take into consideration (1) whether an offer to stipulate to change of venue was reasonably made and rejected, and (2) whether the motion or selection of venue was made in good faith given the facts and law the party making the motion or selecting the venue knew or should have known.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendants submit the declaration of its’ counsel stating that they offered to stipulate to a change of venue to San Diego County, where Property 1 and Property 3 are situated and which is otherwise a proper court for trial of this action. (Kring Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel did not agree to stipulate. (Ibid.) Defendants request $9,355.00 in attorney fees and costs. Defendants’ counsel states he expended 7.5 hours preparing the moving papers and anticipates spending another 4-5 hours reviewing and replying to any opposition filed and appearing at the hearing on this motion. (Kring Decl., ¶ 5.) Counsel’s customary hourly rate is $500.00. (Ibid.) Thus, Defendants request $9,355.00 calculated as follows: $6,250.00 attorney fees + $3,105.00 in filing fees/costs ($435.00 x 7 Parties = $3,045.00 + $60 = $3,105.00).

 

Since the Court grants Defendants’ motion, the Court finds attorneys fees are warranted. However, the Court finds the amount requested is excessive due to the type of motion at issue and the fact that counsel can appear at the hearing remotely. Therefore, the Court awards attorneys fees in the total amount of $7,105.00 (8 hours of attorney time at $500 an hour and $3,105.00 in filing fees/costs).

 

In sum, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion. The Court grants Defendants’ request to transfer the first, second, fifth and sixth causes of action to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendants’ request for attorneys fees against Plaintiff and their attorney of record in the reduced amount of $7,105.00. Said attorneys fees are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion. The Court grants Defendants’ request to transfer the first, second, fifth and sixth causes of action to the Superior Court of California, County of San Diego.

 

The Court further GRANTS Defendants’ request for attorneys fees against Plaintiff and their attorney of record in the reduced amount of $7,105.00. Said attorneys fees are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order
