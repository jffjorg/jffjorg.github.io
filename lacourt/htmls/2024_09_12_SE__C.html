John G.Z. DOE #1, et al. vs. ROE #1, Case No. 21STCV11698

 

This is action for child sexual assault. Defendant moves ex parte to specially set the hearing date on LAUSD’s motion for discovery of John G.Z. Doe #1’s Pitchess Motion on a different date than the trial date. The Pitchess Motion is currently scheduled November 6, 2024, which is the same date as trial.  The parties agreed to set the motion for October 15, 2024 at the earliest.

 

The ex parte application is DENIED.  The Court cannot accommodate Defendant’s request for a hearing on the Pitchess Motion before trial.  The parties are not foreclosed from seeking a trial continuance in Dept. SE-F.  It appears to the Court that a trial continuance will be needed in any event for Defendant to obtain the discovery it seeks. 

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 22NWCV01253    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: C

PEHRSSON v. DOE SCHOOL DISTRICT

CASE NO.:  22NWCV01253

HEARING:  09/12/24

 

#7

 

Defendant EAST WHITTIER SCHOOL DISTRICT’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. The Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication is MOOT.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action for the sexual assault of a minor was filed by Plaintiff JOHN PEHRSSON (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant EAST WHITTIER CITY SCHOOL DISTRICT (“Defendant” or “District”) on November 8, 2022. On December 5, 2022, the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) was filed.

 

Plaintiff alleges that he is a survivor of childhood sexual assault within the meaning of CCP §340.1(d). Plaintiff alleges sexual conduct by a teacher, Jim Wiggins (“Wiggins”) when Plaintiff was in the 8th grade in the 1967-1968 school year at Hillview Middle School. “Plaintiff was a minor throughout the period of childhood sexual assault alleged herein.” (FAC ¶2.) “Plaintiff was a 12-year-old, eighth grade student at the Doe Intermediate School in East WHITTIER SCHOOL DISTRICT when he suffered the childhood sexual assault, abuse, harassment, and other misconduct perpetrated by his teacher…. Defendants employed [Wiggins] as a teacher and were charged with Plaintiff’s supervision and safety. Nevertheless, [the District] allowed Defendant [Wiggins] to assault Plaintiff while under their custody, care, and control.” (FAC ¶4.)

 

The FAC asserts the following causes of action:

 

(1) Negligence;

(2) Negligent Supervision of a Minor; and

(3) Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision of an Unfit Employee


The Court notes that a Stipulation and Order Re: Dismissal of the fourth cause of action for Violation of Civil Rights – 42 U.S.C. §1983 and fifth cause of action for Violation of Civil Rights – Monell Claim against the District was entered on February 2, 2023. (See, 02/02/23 Stip. and Order.)

 

Defendant District now moves for summary judgment, or alternatively, summary adjudication of the following issues:

 

(1) Issue 1- Plaintiff’s Negligence causes of action fail because Plaintiff cannot establish one or more elements of those causes of action;

(2) Issue 2 – Plaintiff’s negligence causes of action fail because Plaintiff has no evidence of actual or constructive notice by a specific administrator or supervisor;

(3) Issue 3 – Plaintiff’s negligence causes of action fail because Plaintiff cannot establish a specific act that was a legal cause of assault that the District may be liable for;

(4) Issue 4 – Plaintiff cannot sustain the first cause of action for general negligence or for failing to make a report under the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act (“CANRA”);

(5) Issue 5 – Plaintiff has no evidence in support of his second cause of action for Negligent Supervision of a Minor;

(6) Issue 6 – Plaintiff has no evidence in support of his third cause of action for Negligent Supervision, Hiring, Retention, and Supervision of an Unfit Employee; and

(7) Issue 7 – Plaintiff’s entire lawsuit is barred because the statute that Plaintiff brings this lawsuit under is unconstitutional.

 

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues that summary judgment/adjudication should be denied because the District should be held vicariously liable for Wiggins’ conduct; and/or that there are disputed issues of material fact as to whether the District breached their duty to supervise Wiggins. Plaintiff further argues that the District’s breach of its duties caused Plaintiff to be repeatedly sexually assaulted by their teacher in the classroom, and invokes the doctrine of res ipsa loquitor. Lastly, Plaintiff argues that the entire lawsuit is not barred and that CCP §340.1 is constitutionally sound under the recently published Appellate Court Opinion, West Contra USD v. Sup. Ct. of Contra Costa (A169314).

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(2).)  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Id.)  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Avivi, supra, 159 Cal.App.4th at p. 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.   Issue 7 – Whether CCP §340.1 is Unconstitutional

 

On July 31, 2024, the First Appellate District of California published the West Contra Costa Unified School District v. Sup. Ct. of Contra Costa County Decision (A169314), which holds that AB 218 does not violate the gift clause, and is not unconstitutional.

 

Summary judgment/adjudication is not granted on this basis.

 

B.   Issues One, Two, Three, Four, Five, and Six – Negligence Claims

 

a.    Negligent Supervision of Minors

 

The District argues that they cannot be held vicariously liable for the misconduct of Wiggins because Wiggins’ misconduct went outside the course and scope of his employment, and the District did not have actual or constructive notice of Wiggins’ propensity for sexual misconduct at any time throughout the course of his employment with the District.

 

Gov. Code §815.2 states: “(a) A public entity is liable for injury proximately caused by an act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment if the act or omission would, apart from this section, have given rise to a cause of action against that employee or his personal representative. (b) Except as otherwise provided by statute, a public entity is not liable for an injury resulting from an act or omission of an employee of the public entity where the employee is immune from liability.” §815.2 makes the District vicariously liable for the act of an employee only if the employee, while acting within the scope of employment would be liable for the injury to Plaintiff. The District’s liability, therefore, depends on its employees’ allegedly negligent supervision of Plaintiff, and/or knowledge of any alleged acts or assault that had occurred.  

 

“The law regarding the duty of supervision on school premises is very, very well established. It is the duty of the school authorities to supervise at all times the conduct of the children on the school grounds and to enforce those rules and regulations necessary to their protection. The school district is liable for injuries which result from a failure of its officers and employees to use ordinary care in this respect.” [Citations Omitted.]  (J.H. v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 123, 139.) “Ample case authority establishes that school personnel owe students under their supervision a protective duty of ordinary care, for breach of which the school district may be held vicariously liable.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 865.) “Students are not at risk merely because they are at school, and schools, including school restrooms, are not dangerous places per se. [Citation Omitted.] Foreseeability is determined in light of all the circumstances and does not require prior identical events or injuries. [Citation Omitted.] It is not necessary to prove that the very injury which occurred must have been foreseeable by the school authorities…Their negligence is established if a reasonably prudent person would foresee that injuries of the same general type would be likely to happen in the absence of [adequate] safeguards…... Further, the issue of ‘foreseeability’ does not depend upon the foreseeability of a particular third party’s act, but instead focuses on whether the allegedly negligent conduct at issue created a foreseeable risk of a particular kind of harm.” [Citations Omitted] (M.W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal. App.4th 508, 518-519.)

 

It is undisputed that Plaintiff was molested during the 1967-1968 school year while he was a student in the 8th grade by Jim Wiggins, his teacher at Hillview Middle School. (SS Nos.1 and 2.) It is also undisputed that Wiggins resigned from his employment with the District on March 15, 1968. (SS. No. 3.) On July 9, 1968, Wiggins was convicted of child molestation. (Drake Decl., ¶8, Ex. F.) 

 

In support of the Motion, the District proffers Plaintiff’s deposition transcript.  Plaintiff admitted during his deposition that: no one else saw any inappropriate touching; no one heard any inappropriate comments; Plaintiff did not report any inappropriate acts to his parents, the District, or the police; and that to Plaintiff’s knowledge, no one else reported Wiggins’ misconduct to the school district. (SS Nos. 7-9; Plaintiff Depo. Pp. 90:7-93; 112:24-115:8; 116:12-118:14; 126:1-18; 137:21-138:2.)

 

No evidence has been submitted to show that Wiggins had any complaints, disciplinary history regarding inappropriate behavior or sexual misconduct, or complaints from students or staff related to sexual misconduct. (SS No. 12.)

 

The District has met it’s burden in establishing that Plaintiff has failed to establish a claim for negligent supervision of minors. The burden now shifts to Plaintiff.

 

In Opposition, Plaintiff fails to provide facts and evidence that dispute the District’s evidence that it did not have knowledge of Wiggins’ inappropriate sexual propensities. Without evidentiary support, Plaintiff maintains that the District should have known about Wiggins’ misconduct. However, only 2 persons were identified by Plaintiff in his deposition testimony who may have suspected or witnessed any purported misconduct—Marc Day and Bruce Quail. Neither Mr. Day nor Mr. Quail were deposed by Plaintiff. No evidence is presented to support Plaintiff’s claims of the District’s knowledge of Wiggins’ misconduct. Moreover, Wiggins resigned on March 15, 1968 and was convicted of any child molestation when he was no longer employed by the District, on July 9, 1968.

 

By failing to dispute the District’s evidence that it did not have knowledge of Wiggins’ inappropriate sexual propensities/misconduct, Plaintiff fails to meet his evidentiary burden.

 

b.   Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention

 

“An employer may be liable to a third person for the employer’s negligence in hiring or retaining an employee who is incompetent or unfit.” (Federico v. Superior Court (Jenry G.) (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1207, 1213.) “[A]n employer’s duty…is breached only when the employer knows, or should know, facts which would warn a reasonable person that the employee presents an undue risk of harm to third persons in light of the particular work to be performed.” (Id. at 1213.) “[A] public school district may be vicariously liable under section 815.2 for the negligence of administrators or supervisors in hiring, supervising and retaining a school employee who sexually harasses and abuses a student.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 879.)

 

Here, Plaintiff fails to demonstrate that the District had any knowledge that Wiggins presented an undue risk of sexual misconduct to students. No evidence has been presented to show that the District knew or should have known about Wiggins’ dangerous propensities at any point throughout the course of his employment with the District.

 

c.    Child Abuse Neglect and Reporting Act (“CANRA”)

 

Gov. Code §815.6 provides for liability against public entities that violate a “mandatory duty” imposed by an enactment. “Where a public entity is under a mandatory duty imposed by an enactment that is designed to protect against the risk of a particular kind of injury, the public entity is liable for an injury of that kind proximately caused by its failure to discharge the duty unless the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty.” (Id.) As alleged, Plaintiff argues that Defendant should be held liable for a failure to report child abuse pursuant to Penal Code §11166.

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s claim against the District based on their breach of mandatory duty to act pursuant to Gov. Code §815.6 fails, as a matter of law, because Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged or shown that the District violated a statute, regulation or ordinance. School Districts (such as Defendant) are not “mandated reporters” under Penal Code §11165.7. Therefore, the District  has no mandatory duty under the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act to report incidents of child abuse to agencies specified in Penal Code §11168.9.

 

Since CANRA does not purport to impose a reporting duty on the District, and Plaintiff fails to identify any employees of the District who violated a mandatory duty, CANRA cannot support a claim against the District under Gov. Code §815.6.

 

The Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. The Motion for Summary Adjudication is MOOT.

GONZALEZ v. PIH HEALTH HOSPITAL - WHITTIER

CASE NO.:  22STCV02241

HEARING:  09/12/24

 

#8

 

Defendant BARLOW RESPIRATORY HOSPITAL’s Motion Requiring Plaintiff to File an Undertaking is GRANTED in part.  Plaintiff Wanda M. Gonzales is ordered to post an undertaking in the amount of $10,000 within 30 days of the issuance of this order.

 

 

Opposing Party to give Notice.

 

This medical negligence action was filed by Plaintiff WANDA M. GONZALES, individually and as Representative of the Estate of Daniel Gonzalez on January 19, 2022. On September 25, 2023, the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) was filed.

 

Defendant BARLOW RESPIRATORY HOSPITAL (“Barlow”) moves for an order requiring that Plaintiff file an undertaking in the amount of $69,016.00 on the grounds that Plaintiff Wanda M. Gonzales currently resides outside of the state of California, in Montana, and that Defendant Barlow will obtain a judgment in it’s favor in this action.

 

Defendant PIH HEALTH HOSPITAL – WHITTIER (“PIH”)’s unopposed motion to join in Barlow’s Motion is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants have not met their burden of showing that they are likely to prevail in this action; and that the Estate of Daniel Gonzales is a California Plaintiff. (See Plaintiff’s RJN.)

 

CCP §1030 provides, in pertinent part: “(a) When the plaintiff in an action or special proceeding resides out of the state, or is a foreign corporation, the defendant may at any time apply to the court by noticed motion for an order requiring the plaintiff to file an undertaking to secure an award of costs and attorney’s fees which may be awarded in the action or special proceeding. For purposes of this section, ‘attorney’s fees’ means reasonable attorney’s fees a party may be authorized to recover by a statute apart from this section or by contract. (b) The motion shall be made on the grounds that the plaintiff resides out of the state or is a foreign corporation and that there is a reasonable possibility that the moving defendant will obtain judgment in the action or special proceeding. The motion shall be accompanied by an affidavit in support of the grounds for the motion and by a memorandum of points and authorities. The affidavit shall set forth the nature and amount of the costs and attorney’s fees the defendant has incurred and expects to incur by the conclusion of the action or special proceeding. (c) If the court, after hearing, determines that the grounds for the motion have been established, the court shall order that the plaintiff file the undertaking in an amount specified in the court’s order as security for costs and attorney’s fees.”  

 

Here, in its joinder motion, PIH submits the Declaration of Daniella Cho who sets forth a list of expenses PIH has incurred and expects to incur in this litigation.  The declaration states that PIH has retained a “wound care nurse” who will offer expert opinion on standard of care, causation, and damages in this matter. (Cho Decl., ¶5a.) “The expert is supportive of Defendant’s overall care and treatment of Decedent and is of the opinion that this does not appear to be a case of liability for Defendant.” (Id.) The declaration also states that PIH has retained a “medical doctor who is Board Certified in Critical Care and Pulmonary Medicine” who will offer expert opinion on standard of care, causation, and damages in this matter. (Cho Decl., ¶5b.) “The doctor is supportive of Defendant's overall care and treatment of Decedent and is of the opinion that Decedent's death was unrelated to his decubitus ulcer.” (Id.)  Based thereon, the Court finds that Defendants have established a reasonable possibility that they will obtain judgment in the action. 

 

Accordingly, the motion requiring Plaintiff to file an undertaking is GRANTED. 

 

Amount of undertaking

 

Plaintiff argues that it would be unreasonable to require her to post a bond that would cover all of Defendants’ expenses.  Plaintiff points out that although she is a non-resident, her lawsuit on behalf of the Estate of Daniel Gonzalez is not subject to a bond requirement because the estate is in California.  Defendants argue that in the event of a defense verdict, Plaintiff would be jointly and severally liable for the entirety of the costs awarded.

 

CCP § 1030(a) “provides for a pretaking hearing which permits an inquiry into the validity of the claim, the reasonableness of the amount of the bond to be posted, and, inferentially, the reasonableness of not requiring any bond, and the ability of a person to furnish a bond.” (Shannon v. Sims Service Center, Inc. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 907, 912.) 

 

Here, the Court considers that Plaintiff, in her individual capacity, may only recover damages for loss of companionship, and the like, in connection with the third cause of action for Wrongful Death.  The estate stands to recover damages for the Professional Negligence and Elder Abuse causes of action, including an award of punitive damages in connection with the latter. 

 

In the exercise of discretion, the Court orders Plaintiff Wanda M. Gonzales to post an undertaking in the amount of $10,000 within 30 days of the issuance of this order.

LEE v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

CASE NO.:  23NWCV00051

#2

 

Plaintiff’s Renewed Motion to Compel Compliance with the Court’s January 24, 2024 Order is MOOT. The Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED. The Alternative Requests for Evidentiary or Issue Sanctions are DENIED. The Alternative Request for Terminating Sanctions is DENIED.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Counsel Soheyl Tahsildoost filed a Substitution of Attorney on behalf of Defendant AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO. (“Defendant”) on May 10, 2024 in place of former counsel Armen Hairpetian.

 

This matter was originally set for hearing on June 20, 2024. However, at the June 20, 2024 hearing, Counsel for Plaintiff indicated that Defendant’s new counsel of record was not served with the Subject Motion, and there was no appearance on behalf of the Defendant at the hearing. Consequently, this matter was CONTINUED from June 20, 2024, to today’s date, to ensure that defendant and their new counsel of record received notice of this hearing.

 

Plaintiffs move to compel Defendant’s compliance with the Court’s January 24, 2024 Compliance Order, and for monetary sanctions. Alternatively, Plaintiffs move for the issuance of evidentiary, issue, and/or terminating sanctions. 

 

In Opposition, Defendant argues that the Motion is moot because Defendant produced “the verification and all responsive documents in compliance with this Court’s Order” on August 29, 2024. (Opp. 3, ¶10; Zipser Decl., ¶10.)

 

In Reply, Plaintiffs acknowledge that Defendant produced verified responses to the January 24, 2024 Order on August 29, 2024. (See Reply 1:2-5.) However, Plaintiffs maintain that monetary sanctions should still issue due to Defendant’s dilatory tactics which caused a delay of this case.

 

To the extent authorized by this chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose [sanctions] against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process …” (CCP §2023.030.) Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are both misuses of the discovery process. (CCP §§2023.010 (d) and (g).)

 

It is a commonly stated axiom that discovery sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793.) However, a court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with discovery obligations. The refusal to reveal material evidence is deemed to be an admission that the claim or defense is without merit. (Id. at 794-795.)

 

The request for issue, evidentiary, and terminating sanctions is DENIED. It is undisputed that untimely responses were ultimately served on August 29, 2024. The imposition of issue, evidentiary, or terminating sanctions would be unduly punitive under these facts because Defendant has not completely abandoned this case or its responsibility to participate in the discovery process.

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,285.00.  Monetary sanctions are warranted here because it is undisputed that compliance with this Court’s January 25, 2024 Discovery Order was untimely, and required Court intervention.  However, the Court also considers the fact that new counsel for Defendant came into the case on May 10, 2024, and as of June 20, 2024, new counsel had not received the Subject Motion.  Therefore, Defendant is ORDERED to pay Plaintiffs and their counsel of record sanctions in the amount of $2,000.00 within 60 days from the date of the Court’s issuance of this Order.

Case Number: 23NWCV03762    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: C

BR CO I, LLC v. COOKIES CREATIVE CONSULTING & PROMOTIONS, INC.

CASE NO.:  23NWCV03762

HEARING:  09/12/24

 

#5

 

Petitioner BR CO I, LLC’s petition for writ of mandate to compel inspection of company records is GRANTED.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

Petitioner BR CO I, LLC (“Petitioner”) moves to compel compliance with Corp. Code §1601.

 

“The accounting books and records and minutes of proceedings of the shareholders and the board and committees of the board of any domestic corporation, and of any foreign corporation keeping any such records in this state or having its principal executive office in this state, shall be open to inspection upon the written demand on the corporation of any shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate at any reasonable time during usual business hours, for a purpose reasonably related to such holder’s interests as a shareholder or as the holder of such voting trust certificate.” (Corp. Code §1601(a).) “Such inspection by a shareholder…includes the right to copy and make extracts.” (Corp. Code §1601(b).) “Upon refusal of a lawful demand for inspection, the superior court…may enforce the right of inspection….” (Corp. Code §1603(a).) “In any action or proceeding under Section 1600 or Section 1601, if the Court finds the failure of the corporation to comply with a proper demand thereunder was without justification, the court may award an amount sufficient to reimburse the shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate for the reasonable expenses incurred by such holder, including attorneys’ fees, in connection with such action or proceeding.” (Corp. Code §1604.)

 

In Opposition, Respondent argues that the right of a shareholder to inspect corporate records is limited to purposes reasonably related to the holder’s interests as a shareholder—and that Petitioner’s request goes beyond this right. Respondent maintains that the instant Petition is no more than a fishing expedition to aid Petitioner in their four other lawsuits against Respondent in the LASC and AAA. (See Ward Decl., ¶¶3, 7, and 11.) Respondent further argues that the Petitioner is improperly engaging in “forum shopping” by filing the instant Petition in Norwalk, despite the fact that numerous related cases are ongoing throughout Los Angeles County.

 

The Court notes that no cases have been related to the subject Petition. (See Reply, Ex. B.)

 

Corp. Code §1603(a) does not limit this court’s authority to “enforce the right of inspection” of a shareholder when the claim is related to pending litigation.  It merely provides that the court has authority to enforce the shareholders’ rights in “any action or proceeding under Section 1600 or Section 1601.”  This hearing constitutes a “proceeding” under Corp. Code §1601, et seq.

 

To prevail on the instant motion, Defendants must demonstrate: (1) that they are shareholders, (2) have made a written demand; and (3) that their purpose is reasonably related to its interests as a shareholder. (Corp. Code §1601.)

 

The Court finds that Petitioner’s written request for Respondent’s Bylaws; accounting books and records; annual financial reports; and minutes of all shareholder and board meetings is specifically authorized by Corp. Code §1601, and that the request is for a purpose reasonably related to Petitioner’s interest as a shareholder because the foregoing records would help Petitioner evaluate the financial condition of the company.

 

The Petition for Writ of Mandate is GRANTED. Respondent is ORDERED to produce the documents referenced in the Petition within 30 days of the date of the Court’s issuance of this Order. Costs may be sought by separately noticed motion.

 

 

 


Case Number: 23NWCV04186    Hearing Date: September 12, 2024    Dept: C

MITCHELL v. SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA SPECIALTY CARE, LLC

CASE NO.:  23NWCV04186

HEARING:  09/12/24

 

#6

 

Defendant KINDRED HEALTHCARE, LLC’s unopposed Motion to Quash Service of Summons is GRANTED.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

No Opposition filed as of September 10, 2024.  

 

This personal injury action was filed on December 21, 2023.

 

Defendant KINDRED HEALTHCARE, LLC now moves to quash service of the summons and complaint.

 

The unopposed motion to quash is granted. Once a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction. (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Food, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 449.) To establish general jurisdiction, a plaintiff must show that defendant’s contacts with the forum are “substantial” or “continuous and systematic.” (Id. at 445.) If a defendant is not subject to general jurisdiction, it may still be subject to specific jurisdiction if plaintiff can show that Defendant (1) purposefully established contacts with the forum state, (2) the controversy is related to or “arises” out of a defendant’s contacts with the forum, and (3) the exercise of personal jurisdiction comports with “fair play and substantial justice.” (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475-478.) 

 

Here, Moving Party submits the Declaration of Kathy Teague, which is signed under penalty of perjury. Ms. Teague declares that Kindred Healthcare, LLC is incorporated in the State of Delaware, with its principal place of business in Kentucky; Kindred Healthcare, LLC does not own any property or have any employees within the State of California; Kindred Healthcare, LLC has never maintained an office in California and has never incurred or paid taxes within California, and has never held a bank account within California; Kindred Healthcare, LLC is not registered to do business in California; and Kindred Healthcare, LLC has no appointed registered agent for service of process in the State of California; and Kindred Healthcare, LLC does not own or operate any hospitals in the State of California. (Teague Decl., ¶¶2-7.)

 

As of September 10, 2024, Plaintiff has not filed an Opposition to the instant motion. Accordingly, the Court finds that it has no basis to confer its jurisdiction over this Moving Party.

 

Service of the Summons and Complaint are hereby quashed as to KINDRED HEALTHCARE, LLC.
