DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV01373    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT (LEED)

On January 20, 2023, Plaintiffs Roberto E. Sotomayor and Patricia Bilgin filed this action alleging Sotomayor developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure to asbestos-containing products from Defendant Leed Plastics Corporation.  On October 27, 2023, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment. 

A defendant seeking summary judgment must “conclusively negate[] a necessary element of the plaintiff’s case, or . . . demonstrate[] that under no hypothesis is there a material issue of fact that requires the process of trial.”  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 334.)  To show that a plaintiff cannot establish an element of a cause of action, a defendant must make the initial showing “that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)  “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action.  The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence – as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.”  (Id. at p. 855.)  A plaintiff’s deposition testimony that the plaintiff has no knowledge of any exposure to the defendant’s products may be sufficient to shift the burden to the plaintiff to demonstrate the existence of triable issues of fact. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104.)  The plaintiff’s deposition testimony that he did not recall ever working with a product manufactured by the defendant may not be sufficient to shift the burden if the plaintiff is able to prove his case by another means.  (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1439.)  “ ‘If plaintiffs respond to comprehensive interrogatories seeking all known facts with boilerplate answers that restate their allegations, or simply provide laundry lists of people and/or documents, the burden of production will almost certainly be shifted to them once defendants move for summary judgment and properly present plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses.’”  (Id. at p. 1440.)

Defendant contends Plaintiffs have no evidence Sotomayor was exposed to asbestos from Defendant’s products, citing Sotomayor’s deposition testimony.  (Motion at p. 12.)  Sotomayor did not recall hearing of “Leed Plastics Corporation” before his deposition.  (Ex. 17 at p. 776.)  He bought most of the material for his plastics machining business but did not know about any specific services, contracts, writings, or invoices with respect to Defendant.  (Id. at pp. 778-779.)  He did not readily have documents relating to contracting with Defendant.  (Id. at p. 779.)  He had no knowledge of asking Defendant to fabricate a custom plastic product.  (Id. at 782.)  Neither he, his brother, nor his company, S&K Precision, were clients of Defendant.  (Id. at p. 783.)  He recalled seeing Defendant’s “different kinds of plastics” products between the 1960s and 2014 with the Defendant’s name glued to the plastic.  (Id. at pp. 784-785.)  He thought he did work on some kind of plastic with Leed.  (Id. at p. 779.)

In response to an interrogatory asking for all facts supporting exposure to asbestos from Defendant’s products, Plaintiffs respond vaguely and without any specific evidence that Sotomayor was exposed to asbestos from Defendant’s machining plastic parts, including plastic molding compounds, phenolic molding compounds, and/or phenolic resin materials such as Micarta.  (Ex. 13 at pp. 1-3.)  Plaintiffs identified Sotomayor and Defendant’s manager, officers, directors, corporate designees and/or agents” as individuals with knowledge of facts and information supporting Plaintiffs’ claims.  (Id. at pp. 4-5.)  In response to requests for production of documents supporting exposure to asbestos from Defendant’s products, Plaintiffs generally identified Sotomayor’s interrogatory responses, deposition testimony, and employment and medical records.  (Defendant’s Index, Ex. 15 at pp. 1-2.)

While these responses could support the conclusion that Defendant worked with plastic from Defendant, they do not contain evidence that Defendant’s plastic supplied to Sotomayor contained asbestos.  Defendant submitted evidence that it did not make plastic parts containing asbestos.  (Kendrick Decl., ¶ 4.)  Defendant has shown that Plaintiffs do not have evidence that Sotomayor used asbestos-containing products from Defendant, and thus shifted the burden.

In their December 29, 2023 opposition, Plaintiffs contended without evidence that Defendant supplied Micarta brand asbestos-containing plastic to Sotomayor.  (Opposition at p. 5.)  Plaintiffs asked for the hearing to be continued so they could take the deposition of Defendant, apparently to show that Defendant sold asbestos-containing plastic to Plaintiff’s employer.  With their opposition, Plaintiffs filed a notice for the deposition of Defendant dated December 29, 2023.  (Steward Decl., Ex. 2 at p. 27.)  At the January 12, 2024 hearing, the court continued the hearing to February 6, 2024 to allow the deposition to go forward.  Thereafter, the parties filed supplemental briefs. 

On January 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a supplemental brief stating that the defense witness did not do an adequate search of documents, Plaintiffs will file a motion to compel the production of further documents and a further deposition of Defendant, Plaintiffs will be subpoenaing a third party, and therefore the hearing on this motion must be continued yet again.  That argument has no merit for several reasons.

First, the preference trial is in seven days.  Plaintiffs do not explain how they plan to conduct all of this discovery, file yet an additional opposition, and have this motion heard in the next seven days.  And the fact discovery cutoff was on December 22, 2024.  Therefore all of this additional planned discovery cannot take place without Plaintiffs first moving to reopen discovery, which Plaintiffs have not done. 

Second, Plaintiffs did not show good cause for not taking Defendant’s deposition until January 19, 2024, almost three months after being served with Defendant’s motion on October 27, 2023.  Upon being served with the motion, Plaintiffs knew that the issue of asbestos in Micarta was key.  Yet, Plaintiffs waited two months, until the day their opposition was due on December 29, 2003, to serve a deposition notice on Defendant.  Plaintiffs provided no valid excuse for not serving a notice of deposition immediately after receiving the motion on October 27, 2023.  Rather Plaintiffs’ delay shows a lack of diligence in pursuing the discovery that Plaintiffs claim is critical to oppose the motion.  Plaintiffs chose to litigate this case as a preference case.  That means that Plaintiffs have the obligation to pursue discovery expeditiously and diligently.  Plaintiffs’ lack of diligence is not grounds to continue a preference trial date. 

Third, Plaintiffs’ argument that Defendant failed to conduct an adequate search is not well-founded.  Defendant’s representative testified that Defendant ceased to exist in 2002 and was sold to PlastiFab.  (Supp. Stewart Decl., Ex. 1 at p. 16.)  PlastiFab acquired all of Defendant’s documents and equipment.  (Id. at pp. 17, 26-27.)  The witness has no idea if any of the pre-2002 documents from Defendant still exist at PlastiFab.  (Id. at p. 26.)  He looked in his house for documents from Defendant and found some documents about the termination of the company and a document about asbestos and cancer.  (Id. at pp. 42-47.)

Plaintiffs contend that Defendant should have called up PlastiFab and required that third party to conduct a search of its documents.  (Supp. Opposition at p. 5.)  Plaintiffs cite no law that a party responding to a request for documents must search for documents in the possession, custody, or control of a third party.  Indeed, the law is to the contrary.  Code of Civil Procedure requires a party to search for and produce documents “that are in the possession, custody, or control” of the responding party.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.220.)  Plaintiffs did not show that Defendant controls currently PlastiFab, even though Defendant ceased to exist twenty-two years ago.  Plaintiffs did not show that the witness or Defendant has any control over PlastiFab, or any relationship at all with PlastiFab apart from knowing one employee at PlastiFab.  Defendant and the witness had no obligation to try to go through PlastiFab’s files.

In sum, Plaintiffs request to continue the hearing on the motion again and/or to continue the trial date is denied.

Plaintiffs’ supplemental filings did not include any evidence that Defendant supplied asbestos-containing plastic to Roberto Sotomayor or his employer.  The defense witness testified that Defendant was an approved distributor of Micarta, and Defendant purchased Micarta from Westinghouse.  (Supp. Steward Decl., Ex. 1 at pp. 21, 23, 36.)  Other than that, Plaintiffs’ supplemental papers provided no additional information about Micarta or asbestos-containing materials from Defendant.  Therefore, Plaintiffs did not show the existence of a material disputed issue.

The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Defendant is to file a proposed judgment within five days.

The moving party is to give notice.

Honeywell’s Motion to Compel

            Honeywell served requests for admissions and form interrogatory No. 17.1.  Plaintiffs objected, and Honeywell filed a motion to compel further responses to some of the RFAs and the form interrogatory.  Most of the RFAs address issues already covered by the standard interrogatories.  That means that the form interrogatory No. 17.1 applied to those RFAs results in discovery requests that duplicate the standard interrogatories.  Honeywell failed to address this problem.  The court rules as follows:

RFA Nos. 34-40:  Granted.  Plaintiffs are to serve verified responses within 30 days.

Form Interrogatory No. 17.1:  Granted in part and denied in part.  The RFAs asked Plaintiffs to admit they have no evidence supporting their causes of action and request for punitive damages.  The standard interrogatories require Plaintiffs to state the facts, and identify witnesses and documents, supporting the contention that Defendant’s products exposed Plaintiff to asbestos, concerning friction products (such as Defendant’s brakes), warnings about asbestos, defects in the products at issue, misrepresentations about asbestos containing friction products, the harm and damages to Plaintiffs caused by the asbestos exposure.  (See, e.g., Standard Interrogatory Nos. 18, 22, 23, 24, 25, 26, 27, 28, 50, 55-62.)  The only non-duplicative portion of form interrogatory No. 17.1 concerns RFA No. 40 about the request for punitive damages.  Therefore, the motion is denied as to form interrogatory No. 17.1 concerning all RFAs except as to RFA No. 40, and is granted as to form interrogatory No. 17.1 concerning RFA No. 40.  Plaintiffs are to serve verified responses within 30 days.

The motion is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.  The moving party is to give notice.

Case Number: 23STCV16412    Hearing Date: February 6, 2024    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR PREFERENCE

On July 13, 2023, Plaintiffs Norayr Arutunyan and Sedik Kirakosyan filed this action for personal injury caused by asbestos exposure.  On January 8, 2024, Plaintiffs moved for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivisions (a) and (e).

A party who is over 70 years old may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if it makes both of the following findings: (1) the party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole; and (2) the health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).)  An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under this subdivision may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.  (Id., § 36.5.)  Moreover, the court in its discretion may grant a motion for preference supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.  (Id., § 36, subd. (e).) 

“Upon the granting of such a motion for preference, the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party’s attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record.”  (Id., § 36, subd. (f).)  “Any continuance shall be for no more than 15 days and no more than one continuance for physical disability may be granted to any party.”  (Ibid.)

Arutunyan is 70 years old and was diagnosed with malignant mesothelioma.  (Sandoval Decl. at ¶¶ 2-3.)  Arutunyan suffers from constant physical pain, shortness of breath and difficulty breathing, physical and mental fatigue, weight fluctuations, and trouble sleeping.  (Id. at ¶ 9.)  Arutunyan’s difficulty effectively communicating, remaining alert, and focusing results in exhaustion and stress.  (Ibid.)  His condition is in serious decline.  (Sandoval Decl., at ¶ 10.)

Defendants do not directly oppose Plaintiffs’ motion for trial preference but request the court to impose certain conditions, such as shortened notice periods for motions for summary judgement.  (CVS Pharmacy’s Conditional Non-Opposition at pp. 3-4.)  The court cannot shorten the notice periods for motions for summary judgement.  (McMahon v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 112, 115-18; see also Urshan v. Musician’s Credit Union (2004) 120 Cal. App.4th 758, 765-66 [discussing McMahon and reversing judgement for failure to provide notice by the statutorily required minimum time period].)  The parties may stipulate to waive the statutorily mandated minimum notice period.  (Credit Suisse First Boston Mortgage Capital v. Danning, Gill, Diamond & Kollitz (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1290, 1301.)

The court finds that Arutunyan has a substantial interest in the action as a whole.  The evidence shows that granting preference is necessary to ensure Arutunyan’s participation in the litigation and trial while his health permits and prevent prejudicing his interest in the action.

Plaintiffs’ motion for trial setting preference is GRANTED.  The parties are to meet and confer on a trial setting order.  The trial is scheduled for June 3, 2024 at 9 a.m.  The final status conference is scheduled for May 20, 2024 at 9 a.m.  A status conference regarding the trial setting order is set for February 20, 2024 at 9:00 a.m.

The moving party is to order to give notice.
