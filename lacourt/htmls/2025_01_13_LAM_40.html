DEPARTMENT 40 - MICHAEL J. SHULTZ  - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 24STCV18129    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: 40

24STCV18129 Auto Fraud Legal Center, LLP v. Knight Law Group, LLP

Monday, January 13, 2025

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEMURRER TO THE CROSS-COMPLAINT

 

I.       BACKGROUND

      This action arises from a dispute over failure to pay for legal services allegedly owed to Plaintiff, formerly known as Rosner, Barry & Babbit, LLP. Plaintiff allegedly agreed to provide legal services in partnership with Defendant, Knight Law Group, LLP (“Knight” or “Defendant”) with respect to seven different cases. Knight received full payment for the services, but has not paid Plaintiff for the services that Plaintiff performed (“Earned Fees”). Plaintiff alleges claims for breach of contract, breach of implied contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and for conversion.

      Knight filed an interpleader cross-complaint against Plaintiff and Hallen Rosner (“Cross-Defendant” or “Rosner”), a former partner of the RBB. Cross-complainant Knight alleges it is a stakeholder only and cannot resolve the conflicting demands made by Plaintiff and Rosner to Earned Feeds, totaling $107,185.64.

      On November 19, 2024, the court denied Cross-Complainant’s motion to discharge Knight from liability and for related orders.

II.     ARGUMENTS 

      Plaintiff demurs to the Knight interpleader cross-complaint for Knight’s failure to demonstrate that Knight is subject to conflicting claims by Cross-Defendants (Plaintiff and Rosner). There is no allegation that Rosner has staked a claim in the Earned Fees, but rather Plaintiff and Rosner seek different debts from different obligors. Nor does the cross-complaint allege that Rosner demanded the Earned Fees at issue in the complaint.

      In opposition, Cross-complainant Knight contends it has alleged sufficient facts to support the interpleader cross-complaint. Cross-Defendant Rosner has some interest in the Earned Fees allegedly owed to Plaintiff. The facts show that Knight faces a reasonable probability of double vexation. While Rosner is suing former partners in a different action that is presently being arbitrated, Plaintiff would not represent to Knight that there was no dispute between Plaintiff and Rosner over the Earned Fees.

      In reply, Plaintiff states the court has already ruled that the cross-complaint is devoid of facts necessary to support an interpleader action, because the cross-complaint is ambiguous as to whether Rosner has shown any interest in the Earned Fees at issue in this action or that Rosner has made a completing claim to the Earned Fees subjecting Knight to double vexation.

III.    LEGAL STANDARDS

      A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.) In testing the complaint’s sufficiency, the court must assume the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations as well as facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded facts. The court may also consider matters properly subject to judicial notice. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

      The court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.4th 634, 638.) Plaintiff is required to allege facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer. (Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1126; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)

      Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case stated, "with reasonable precision and with particularity that is sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  Whether the Plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant. (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)

      A demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing, they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

      A pleading is required to assert general allegations of ultimate fact. Evidentiary facts are not required. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 47; Lim v. The.TV Corp. Internat. (2002) 99 Cal. App. 4th 684, 690.)

 

 

IV.   DISCUSSION

      The court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of the complaint in a San Diego case titled Hallen Rosner v. Christopher Barry, Gregory Babbitt, Michael Klitzke, Rosner, Barry & Babbit LLP (“the San Diego action”). (PRJN Ex. A.) (Evid. Code, § 452(d).) The court denies Knight’s request for judicial notice of Hallen Rosner’s Notice of Change of Statute of a Limited Liability Partnership, an application to register a limited liability partnership filed with the Secretary of State, and Plaintiff’s certificate of registration filed with the State Bar. None of the documents are relevant to any issue raised by the demurrer.  

      A party “against whom double or multiple claims are made,” that give rise to double or multiple liability, may bring an action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their claims. (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (b); Placer Foreclosure, Inc. v. Aflalo (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1113.) The complaint must show that defendants are making conflicting claims to the subject matter, and that the plaintiff “cannot safely determine which claim is valid and offers to deposit the money in court [citation omitted].” (Id.) An interpleader action may not be maintained upon the “mere pretext or suspicion of double vexation.” (Id.)

      The purpose of an interpleader is to prevent a multiplicity of suits and double vexation. (City of Morgan Hill v. Brown (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1114, 1122.) The right to the remedy depends on the fact that the disinterested stakeholder “is threatened with double vexation in respect to one liability, not on the consideration that a person may be subject to double liability. (Id.)

      On November 19, 2024, this court denied Knight’s motion for an order discharging Knight from liability and related orders because Knight failed to allege or establish that Knight was subject to competing claims to the Earned Fees at issue in the complaint. (M.O. 11/19/24.) The basis for denying Knight’s motion is the same rationale that supports sustaining Plaintiff’s demurrer to the cross-complaint. An interpleader complaint must show that Cross-Defendants, Plaintiff and Rosen, are making conflicting claims to the funds, and that the interpleader (Knight) cannot safely determine which claim is valid. (Westamerica Bank v. City of Berkeley (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 598, 608.

      The cross-complaint alleges that Rosner asserts a claim to the Earned Fees because Rosner filed an action in San Diego against his former partners which is currently being arbitrated (Cross-complaint, ¶ 9-11.) Knight also alleges that Plaintiff made a demand for the Earned Fees on February 22, 2024. (Cross-complaint ¶ 12.) Knight alleges that counsel for Plaintiff could not represent to Knight that there was no dispute to the Earned Fees as between Plaintiff and Rosner. (Cross-complaint ¶ 14.)

      In the San Diego action, Rosner seeks damages against his former partners for their attempt to oust Rosner from the partnership, to purchase Rosner’s share for a “pittance,” using the partnership’s money, and attempting to fire Rosner as an employee all in breach of the partner’s fiduciary duties owed to Rosner. (PRJN Ex. A, 5:2-11.) The San Diego action does not mention the Earned Funds in this case nor is Knight a defendant in that action. (Id.) Rosen alleges 13 causes of action for claims including fraud, emotional distress, violations under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, wrongful termination, and claims related to that alleged misconduct by the former partners. (Id.)

      The Knight cross-complaint does not allege that Rosner made a competing demand for the Earned Funds now held by Knight to which Plaintiff also claims an interest which would otherwise establish an actual conflict for the Earned Fees. The Rosner claims are unrelated to the Earned Fees purportedly withheld by Knight as alleged by Plaintiff in this action.

      The interpleader cross-complaint "must allege facts showing a reasonable probability of double vexation … or a valid threat of double vexation.” (Westamerica Bank at 608.) The cross-complaint is ambiguous. (Cross-complaint ¶ 20 [“yet Rosner also appears to have some right, claim or interest in the funds.”].) Even if Rosner may have an interest in the Earned Fees, the cross-complaint does not allege that Rosner has made a competing claim for them.

      Knight mischaracterizes the statute governing interpleaders as permitting “potential adverse claims.” (Opp. 4:19.) However, as the foregoing cases demonstrate, an interpleader action must demonstrate an actual and valid threat of double vexation or a reasonable probability of double vexation as the “mere pretext or suspicion of double vexation” is not sufficient. (Placer at 1113.)

      Knight requests leave to amend based on new facts recently discovered showing that Plaintiff does not have the right to the Earned Fees generated by its former entity, Rosner, Barry & Babbit, LLP. Whether or not Plaintiff’s claim to the Earned Fees is valid does not bear on the material issue: whether Knight is subject to conflicting claims for the Earned Fees which is necessary to state an interpleader claim.

 

 

V.     CONCLUSION

      Leave to amend is ordinarily given if there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured. However, it is pleading party’s burden to demonstrate how the complaint can be amended and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading. (Association of Community Organizations for Reform Now v. Department of Industrial Relations (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 298, 302.) Knight’s offering of newly discovered, immaterial facts does not meet that burden. Accordingly, demurrer to the cross-complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
