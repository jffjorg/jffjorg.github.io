Counsel who wish to submit on the tentative ruling may do so by emailing BURDeptB@lacourt.org

PLEASE WRITE THE CASE NUMBER AND PARTY YOU REPRESENT.  YOU MAY ONLY SUBMIT ON BEHALF OF THE PARTY YOU REPRESENT.  YOU MAY NOT SUBMIT ON BEHALF OF ANOTHER PARTY. Counsel are directed to cc all other counsel if you are submitting on the tentative ruling.

IF YOU HAVE QUESTIONS OR NEED CLARIFICATION ON THE TENTATIVE, YOU MUST APPEAR AND ADDRESS YOUR QUESTIONS TO THE COURT.

IF BOTH SIDES SUBMIT ON THE TENTATIVE RULING, THE TENTATIVE RULING THEN BECOMES THE ORDER OF THE COURT ON THE MOTION DATE AND NO APPEARANCES ARE NECESSARY.


THERE WILL BE NO RESPONSES TO ANY INQUIRIES SUBMITTED THROUGH THIS SITE.

Warning regarding electronic appearances:    All software for remote or electronic appearances is subject to malfunction based on system weakness and human error, which can originate from any of the multiple parties participating each morning. The seamless operation of the Court’s electronic appearance software is dependent on numerous inconstant and fluctuating factors that may impact whether you, or other counsel or the Court itself can be heard in a particular case. Not all these factors are within the control of the courtroom staff. For example, at times, the system traps participants in electronic purgatories where they cannot be heard and where the courtroom staff is not aware of their presence. If you call the courtroom, please be respectful of the fact that a court hearing is going on, and that the courtroom staff is doing their best to use an imperfect system. If it is truly important to you to be heard, please show up to the courtroom in the normal way. Parking is free or reasonable in Burbank.

Case Number: 24BBCV00107    Hearing Date: January 17, 2025    Dept: NCB

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

North Central District

Department B

 

 

Thelma rosas,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

target corp., et al.,  

 

                        Defendants.

 

Case No.: 24BBCV00107

 

  Hearing Date:  January 17, 2025

 

 [TENTATIVE] order RE:

motions to compel discovery responses; requests for sanctions

 

 

On October 31, 2024, Defendant Target Corp. (“Defendant”) filed 3 motions to compel initial responses from Plaintiff Thelmas Rosas (“Plaintiff”) for: (1) Form Interrogatories (“FROG”), set one; (2) Special Interrogatories (“SROG”), set one; and (3) Request for Production of Documents (“RPD”), set one.  

On June 14, 2024, Defendant served the discovery requests on Plaintiff.  The responses to the requests were originally due on July 14, 2024.  Defendant had not received any responses from Plaintiff by October 2, 2024, so Defendant’s counsel reached out to Plaintiff’s counsel and offered an extension to respond to October 7, 2024.  On October 3, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel responded and asked for an extension to October 11, 2024.  Defendant’s counsel granted this extension.  Defendant did not receive any responses on October 11, 2024, and as of the date of filing these motions, Defendant had not received any responses. 

Defendant’s unopposed motions to compel responses to the FROG, SROG, and RPD are granted pursuant to CCP §§ 2030.290 and 2031.300.  Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses to Defendant’s discovery requests, without objections, within 20 days of notice of this order.   

Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel of record in the amount of $1,086.65 for each motion (= 3 hours to prepare the motion + 2 anticipated hours to prepare the reply and attend the hearing at $205/hour, plus $61.65 in filing fees).  The requests are granted but in the reasonable amount of $820 total for these relatively simple and identical motions that were unopposed, plus $184.95 in filing fees.  Plaintiff and her counsel of record, jointly and severally, are ordered to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,004.95 to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 20 days of notice of this order.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

According to the complaint, Plaintiff Nancy Ruiz (Plaintiff) went to the IKEA in Burbank and slipped on some type of liquid that was on the floor in a heavily trafficked area of the restaurant. (Compl., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff claims she was injured as a result.

On January 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants IKEA US Retail, LLC (IKEA) and Linda Pelser-Braun (Pelser-Braun), alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence - Premises and (2) Negligence – General.

B.     Motion on Calendar

On December 2, 2024, IKEA filed the instant motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to

Request for Production of Documents, set one (RFPDs) and to deem IKEA’s Requests for Admission, set one (RFAs).

            No opposition to either motion has been filed.

DISCUSSION

            IKEA is moving to compel Plaintiff’s responses to its RFPDs and moving to deem its RFAs that were served upon Plaintiff admitted, or in the alternative, compel further responses.

            IKEA served discovery requests, including its RFPDs and RFAs, upon Plaintiff on June 25, 2024. (Lee Decl. ¶ 3.) Thus, the deadline for Plaintiff to have served responses to both IKEA’s RFPDs and RFAs was July 29, 2024, which includes the additional two court days for electronic service. (Id. ¶ 4.) IKEA has provided Plaintiff’s counsel multiple extensions, including an extension until August 15, 2024, another one until August 30, 2024, another one until September 30, 2024, and another one until November 4, 2024. (Ibid.) November 4, 2024 was the date of the hearing for Plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel, and on that date, Plaintiff’s counsel only served identical objections to IKEA’s discovery requests, including the RFPDs and RFAs. (Id., Exhibit 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel motion to relieve as counsel was granted, and on November 14, 2024, IKEA’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff to Plaintiff’s provided address to meet and confer on further responses. (Id. ¶ 5, Exhibit 4.) On November 22, 2024, IKEA’s counsel attempted to reach Plaintiff by phone at around 10:24 a.m., but received the following message: "The number you are calling is temporary unavailable." (Id. ¶ 6.)

Under California law, objections must be specific, “boilerplate” objections and “nuisance objections” are inappropriate and even sanctionable. (Korea Data Systems Co., Ltd. v. Superior Court, (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1513, 1516; Standon Co., Inc. v. Superior Court, (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 898, 902-903.) Plaintiff also bears the burden of justifying its objections in response to a motion to compel. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court, (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 355.) Plaintiff has failed to provide justification for its objections because no opposition was filed.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            IKEA’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Requests for Production, set one and to deem IKEA’s Requests for Admission, set one are both GRANTED.

Plaintiff is to serve verified, further code compliant responses to RFPDs without objection – except attorney client privilege/work product and privacy – within 20 days of notice of this order.

Plaintiff is to serve verified, further code compliant responses to RFAs without objection with 20 days notice of this order.

           

Moving party shall provide notice of this order.

BACKGROUND

A.    Allegations

Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on May 4, 2022, she carpooled with a group of friends, which included Defendant Arthur Wuthong Shi (“Defendant”), to attend a gathering at a friend’s house.  Plaintiff alleges that she was a minor at the time of the incident.  She alleges that she did not consume alcohol, but Defendant consumed alcohol while at the gathering.  Plaintiff alleges that while carpooling back, Defendant proceeded to put his hand up her shirt before grabbing and fondling her breasts.  She alleges that Defendant then put his hands in her pants and began touching her vaginal area before inserting his fingers into her vagina.  Plaintiff alleges that during the incident, she was paralyzed by fear and shock. 

The complaint, filed May 6, 2024, alleges causes of action for: (1) sexual battery; (2) IIED; and (3) negligence.

On October 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint, alleging the same three causes of action.

On November 13, 2024, Defendant filed his Cross-Complaint, alleging a single cause of action for defamation. On December 27, 2024, Defendant filed request for dismissal of his Cross-Complaint, which was entered on the same day.

B.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Two

On December 20, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s

further responses to Special Interrogatories (“SROGs”), Set Two. The Court ordered Plaintiff to provide complete responses within thirty (30) days of this order. The Court denied sanctions in light of the defects in the moving papers.

 

C.     Motion on Calendar

There are 4 discovery motions on calendar.

On October 31, 2024, Defendant filed a (1) motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Document Demands, Set One (“RPDs”) (CRS 8943); (2) motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, (“FROGs”) (CRS 5389); motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to SROGs, Set One (CRS 2256); and motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to SROGs, Set One (CRS 2189). In connection with each motion, Defendant requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her attorney of record, Jason A. Feazell and Shioda Langley & Chang, LLP, in the amount of $3,660.00.

Plaintiff filed oppositions on January 6, 2025, and Defendant filed replies on January 10, 2025.[1]

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Timeliness

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories and demand requests must be brought within 45 days of service of the verified response, supplemental verified response, or on a date to which the propounding and responding parties have agreed to in writing; otherwise, the propounding party waives the right to compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c); 2031.310, subd. (c).)

Here, Defendant’s four motions are timely as Plaintiff served her responses on September 16, 2024, and Defendant filed and served his motions on October 31, 2024, forty-five days later.

 

B.     Meet and Confer

“A motion [to compel further responses to interrogatories and demand requests] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b)(1); 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).) Section 2016.040 states: “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”

Defendant attached to his motions the meet and confer correspondences. (Declarations of Bin Li, ¶ 6; Ex. “E.”) Plaintiff does not contend any motion should be denied due to inadequate meet and confer efforts.

 

C.     Separate Statement

Defendant has satisfied the separate statement requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b)(2); 2031.310, subd. (b)(3) [requiring separate statements for motions to compel further responses to interrogatories and demand requests]; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(2) and (3) [same]; Separate Statements in support of motions, filed on December 31, 2024.)

Defendant’s motion to compel further Plaintiff’s responses to RPDs, Set One, is GRANTED in light of Plaintiff’s response that she will comply and produce all responsive documents in her possession and that she will continue to search for additional documents and then voluntarily supplement responses. Since the filing of this motion, Plaintiff provided a  supplemental response, as represented; however the response does not appear to be code compliant because Plaintiff merely states regarding RPD No. 2 that “[she] did not identify any documents in response to Form Interrogatories Set One Number 12.3.” (Declaration of Jason A. Feazell (“Feazell Decl.”), ¶ 8.; Ex. “5”; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230 [“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. It appears that Plaintiff has produced two pages in response to these interrogatories. Plaintiff should be reminded that any failure to produce documents that were in her possession, custody and control will result in their exclusion at trial.

F.      Motions to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One (CRS 2256 and 2189)

Defendant requests an order the Plaintiff provide further answers to SROG, Set One, Nos. 10 and 11 without objections, arguing that Plaitiff’s responses are generic, fact-deficient, and bereft of the requested information.

Plaintiff asks the Court to deny the motion, arguing that she served compliant responses to SROGs, Set One, on September 16, 2024.

SROG No. 10 asks Plaintiff to:

IDENTIFY all DOCUMENTS (meaning writing as that term is defined by Evidence Code §250). In the context of IDENTIFYING DOCUMENTS, meaning to state facts sufficient such that a Notice to Produce or a Subpoena Duces Tecum may issue) that REFER TO (meaning being, or tending to prove or disprove) the sexual battery alleged in the operative complaint. This category specifically includes reports to law enforcement or other governmental entity, or the human relations department of responding party's employer. This category also specifically includes correspondence between responding party and all other persons via social media or electronic messaging.

(SROGs Separate Statement CRS 2256.)

            Plaintiff responded with the following:

Respondent objects to the discovery on the following grounds: (1) The discovery is vague, overbroad and ambiguous and forces this party to speculate as to the meaning of the discovery request; (2) The discovery request seeks attorney client work product and/or privilege; (3) the discovery request is vague as to time; (4) the discovery request seeks a legal conclusion; (5) the discovery request seeks information that is protected by third party privacy rights; (6) the discovery request seeks information that is equally available to the propounding party; (7) the request is burdensome, oppressive, calls for speculation as to meaning of records; (8) the request seeks information that is not relevant and will not lead to the discovery of admissible evidence; (9) the request seeks duplicitous information. However, without waiving said objections, Respondent responds as follows: Respondent is still searching for any responsive documents, thus documents responsive to this question are currently unknown. Discovery is ongoing, and Respondent reserves the right to supplement responses.

(Ibid.)

SROG No. 11 asks Plaintiff to:

IDENTIFY all DOCUMENTS that REFER TO the intentional infliction of emotional distress alleged in the operative complaint. This category specifically includes reports to law enforcement or other governmental entity, or the human relations department of responding party's employer.

(SROGs Separate Statement CRS 2189.)

Plaintiff responded with the following:

Respondent objects to the discovery on the following grounds: (1) The discovery is vague, overbroad and ambiguous and forces this party to speculate as to the meaning of the discovery request; (2) The discovery request seeks attorney client work product and/or privilege; (3) the discovery request is vague as to time; (4) the discovery request seeks a legal conclusion; (5) the discovery request seeks information that is protected by third party privacy rights; (6) the discovery request seeks information that is equally available to the propounding party; (7) the request is burdensome, oppressive, calls for speculation as to meaning of records; (8) the request seeks information that is not relevant and will not lead to the discovery of admissible evidence; (9) the request seeks expert information. However, without waiving said objections, Respondent responds as follows: Respondent does not possess any documents related to this interrogatory. Discovery is ongoing, and Respondent reserves the right to supplement responses.

(Ibid.)

As to both responses, Plaintiff’s objections are overruled. As to SROG No. 10, Plaintiff responds that she “is still searching for any responsive documents, thus documents responsive to this question are currently unknown. Discovery is ongoing, and Respondent reserves the right to supplement responses.” (SROGs Separate Statement CRS 2256.) This is not a response. Plaintiff  must answer to the extent possible, even if investigation continues and requires later supplement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220, subd. (b).) As to SROG No. 11, Plaintiff responds that she “does not possess any documents related to this interrogatory. Discovery is ongoing, and Respondent reserves the right to supplement responses.” (SROGs Separate Statement CRS 2189.) This is not a response also. “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220, subd. (c).) Therefore, Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to SROGs, Set One, are GRANTED.

 

G.    Sanctions

In connection with each motion, Defendant requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her attorneys of record in the amount of $3,660.00. Defendant’s requests for monetary sanctions are DENIED in light of the denial of Defendant’s FROGs motion and the defects in the moving papers of each motion. Namely, the exhibits are mislabeled, and Defendant failed to attached proof of service of the disputed discovery. Further, Defendant filed two separate SROGs motions rather than one. 

Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions against Defendant in amount of $1,800 for the FROGs motion, $2,700 for the RPDs motion, and $2,700 for the SROGs motions combined, arguing that opposing counsel did not make any reasonable effort to clearly explain the issues during meet and confer. However, the meet and confer correspondence attached as Exhibit E to Defendant’s counsel’s declarations, reflect several meet and confer efforts where Plaintiff’s counsel could have sought clarification. Therefore, Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions are DENIED.
