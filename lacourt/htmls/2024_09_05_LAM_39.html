DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV12070    Hearing Date: September 5, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 5, 2024

CASE NUMBER

19STCV12070

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Muranaka Farm, Harry Toshiaki Muranaka, Roy Muranaka, Janie Muranaka, and Carolyn Muranaka

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Miguel Magdaleno

 BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Miguel Magdaleno (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Muranaka Farm, Harry Toshiaki Muranaka (“Harry”), Roy Muranaka (“Roy”), Janie Muranaka (“Janie”), and Carolyn Muranaka (“Carolyn”) (collectively, “Defendants”) for breach of contract.  In the operative first amended complaint, Plaintiff alleges he loaned Muranaka Farm $500,000 with no interest to assist with Muranaka Farm’s business, which was heavily in debt (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 11); Muranaka Farm then hired Plaintiff to work as its general manager, and agreed to pay Plaintiff 50% of Muranaka Farm’s profits (ibid.); Defendants refused to pay Plaintiff the profits of Muranaka Farm, and instead terminated Plaintiff’s employment (id., ¶ 14). 

Defendants move for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of issues.  After Defendants filed this motion, Plaintiff agreed to dismiss the second and eighth causes of action.  The motion is, therefore, moot as to those causes of action. 

In its order of July 15, 2024, the Court continued the matter for Defendants to correct procedural deficiencies with their moving papers.  Defendants filed corrected documents, which the Court now considers.  While Plaintiff contends Defendants’ amended separate statement fails to correct the issues the Court identified in its order of July 15, 2024, the Court finds Defendants’ amended separate statement substantially complies with the Court’s order, and the Court exercises its discretion to consider it.  (Brown v. El Dorado Union High School Dist. (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 1003, 1019-1020.)

In its order of August 27, 2024, the Court continued the matter to this date, as counsel for Plaintiff, Brett L. Francisco (“Francisco”) represented he was ineligible to practice law.  Counsel reported the State Bar of California soon thereafter reinstated Counsel’s eligibility.  As Counsel is now eligible to practice law, the Court considers the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

EVIDENCE

            Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections Nos. 1, 8, 9, and 14 are sustained.  Plaintiff’s other objections are overruled.

Defendants’ evidentiary objections to Plaintiff’s Declaration, paragraphs 7, 11, 12, 15, 18, 19, 23, 27, 29, 34, 39, 43, 44, 46, and 48 are sustained.  Defendants’ other evidentiary objections are overruled.

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action:  Breach of Contract

            Plaintiff alleges that, in 2014, Plaintiff entered into an agreement with Muranaka Farm, Harry, and Roy, that Muranaka farm would hire Plaintiff as its general manager, and Muranaka Farm would pay Plaintiff 50% of its profits.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 11.)  Plaintiff alleges Muranaka Farm, Harry, and Roy breached this agreement by failing to pay Plaintiff.  (Id., ¶ 17.) 

Defendants contend Plaintiff cannot prevail because the statute of frauds bars Plaintiff’s claim.   The statute of frauds provides that a contract that falls under it is invalid unless memorialized in writing and signed by the party against whom the contract is to be enforced. (Civ. Code, § 1624.)  An agreement that cannot, by its terms, be performed within one year is subject to the statute of frauds. (Civ. Code, § 1624, subd. (a)(1).)  In response to Defendants’ discovery, Plaintiff stated the parties orally agreed that Plaintiff’s employment with Muranaka Farm would last at least ten years.  (Declaration of Artyom Baghdishyan, Exhibit D, p. 52.)  The parties’ agreement is, therefore, unenforceable unless memorialized in a writing Defendants signed. (Civ. Code, § 1624, subd. (a).)  However, Plaintiff alleges he completely performed under the contract.  Where the contract has been fully performed by one party, the remaining promise is taken out of the statute such that the party who performed may enforce the contract against the other party.  (See Zakk v. Diesel (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 431, 454 [allegation plaintiff “fully performed all of his obligations under the alleged oral or implied-in-fact contract was sufficient to take the contract out of the statue of frauds”]; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 1972, subd. (a) [although in context of an interest in real property, partial performance of a contract takes the contract out of the statute of frauds].)  Defendants concede Plaintiff began working for Muranaka Farm after the purported oral agreement between the parties.  (Declaration of Charles Muranaka, ¶ 8.)  Therefore, at a minimum, the parties partially performed the agreement.  As such, Defendants fail to meet their burden to show the statute of frauds bars Plaintiff’s claim for breach of an oral contract. 

Defendants also argue Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for breach of contract because the parties did not orally agree Plaintiff would receive 50% of Muranaka Farm’s profits.  Defendants advance the declaration of Chiaki Harami (“Harami”), who is the chief financial officer for Muranaka Farm.  Harami states Plaintiff asked Muranaka Farm for a regular salary and benefits, to which Muranaka Farm agreed.  (Declaration of Chiaki Harami, ¶ 12.)  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on the first cause of action for breach of oral agreement, because the parties did not reach an oral agreement on the terms Plaintiff alleges.  Thus, Defendants shift the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on the first cause of action.

            Plaintiff advances his own testimony in opposition.  Plaintiff states he agreed with Harry that Plaintiff would receive 50% of the profits for Muranaka Farm while Plaintiff worked as its general manager.  (Declaration of Miguel Magdaleno (filed August 15, 2024), ¶ 12.)  Plaintiff’s evidence raises triable issues of fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on his first cause of action.

Third Cause of Action: Quantum Meruit

In a claim for quantum meruit, “[e]ven if the entire contract was illegal and unenforceable, a plaintiff may recover the reasonable value of services rendered provided that those particular services were not legally prohibited.” (MKB Management, Inc. v. Melikian (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 796, 805.)  In support of this claim, Plaintiff alleges Muranaka Farm failed to pay Plaintiff for services he rendered as its general manager.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 24-27.)  Defendants present evidence Plaintiff asked Muranaka Farm for a regular salary and benefits, which Muranaka Farm agreed to and did pay Plaintiff, accordingly.  (Harami Decl., ¶ 12.)  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for quantum meruit because Defendants paid Plaintiff the sums due to him.  Defendants, thus, have shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on this claim.

            Plaintiff again advances his own testimony in opposition.  He states Muranaka Farm paid him. only $320 per week.  (Magdaleno Decl., ¶ 24.)  A triable issue of material fact exists as to whether $320 per week represents the reasonable value of Plaintiff’s services to Muranaka Farm.  Plaintiff’s evidence, therefore, has raised a triable issue of fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on his third cause of action.

Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action: Failure to Pay All Wages Due

Plaintiff alleges Muranaka Farm failed to pay Plaintiff all wages and other compensation that were due when Plaintiff left his employment with Muranaka Farm.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 32, 38.)  Again, Defendants advance evidence that Plaintiff asked Muranaka Farm for a regular salary and benefits, which were paid to him.  (Harami Decl., ¶ 12.)  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on his fourth and fifth causes of action because Defendants have paid the sums due to Plaintiff.  Thus, the burden has shifted to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on the causes of action for failure to pay all wages due.

As set forth above, Plaintiff states Muranaka Farm paid Plaintiff only $320 per week  (Magdaleno Decl., ¶ 24), and this raises a triable issue of material fact exist as to whether $320 per week constitutes all wages and compensation that were due to Plaintiff for his work at Muranaka Farm. 

Sixth and Seventh Causes of Action: False Promise

In support of his claims for false promise, Plaintiff alleges Muranaka Farm, through Harry, promised to pay Plaintiff 50% of its profits, and Plaintiff relied on that promise in deciding to work for Muranaka Farm.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 42-45, 50-52.)  To prevail on his claim for promissory fraud, Plaintiff must advance evidence of specific factual circumstances beyond contract breach, which reflect Defendants’ contemporaneous intent not to perform.  (Hills Transportation Co. v. Southwest Forest Ind., Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 702, 707.) 

As set forth above, Defendants present evidence Plaintiff asked Muranaka Farm for a regular salary and benefits, which were paid to Plaintiff.  (Harami Decl., ¶ 12.)  This evidence meets Defendants’ burden to show Plaintiff cannot prevail on the sixth and seventh causes of action for false promise because Defendants did not promise Plaintiff employment on the terms Plaintiff alleges.  Defendants, therefore, have shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on these claims.

As set forth above, Plaintiff submitted evidence he agreed with Harry that Plaintiff would receive 50% of the profits for Muranaka Farm while Plaintiff worked as its general manager.  (Magdaleno Decl., ¶ 12.)  Plaintiff’s evidence raises a triable issue of fact as to whether Defendants promised Plaintiff employment in exchange for 50% of the profits of Muranaka Farm. 

Alter Ego Liability

            Defendants purport to move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether Defendants Harry, Roy, Janie, and Carolyn are liable as the alter egos of Muranaka Farm. This is not a proper issue for summary adjudication, as the alter ego contention does not constitute any of the statutory bases for seeking summary adjudication and would not completely dispose of any cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1) [“A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty”].)   In any event, Plaintiff alleges Muranaka Farm is the alter ego of the individual defendants, and there was a unity of interest and ownership between Muranaka Farm and the individual defendants.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 5, 6.)  Defendant counters by offering facts in dispute of Plaintiff’s contentions.  By alleging the individual Defendants generally utilized Muranaka Farm’s corporate form for fraudulent purposes, Plaintiff effectively debunked Defendant’s contrary contentions, thereby raising a triable issue which should be determined by the trier of fact.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Plaintiff meets his burden to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on his claims against Defendants.  Defendants’ motion for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication, is, therefore, denied. 

Defendants are to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 [requiring, among other things, the service and filing of a separate statement]; Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040 [requiring a meet and confer declaration], 2031.310, subd. (b)(2) [same].) 

Accordingly, the Court continues the matter for hearing on October 24, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  Plaintiffs are to personally serve and file their moving papers under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310 no later than October 2, 2024.  Defendant’s opposition and Plaintiffs’ reply, if any, are due pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 1005, subdivision (b).
