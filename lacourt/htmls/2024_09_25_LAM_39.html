DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV26414B    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV26414B

MOTIONS

Motion to Seal

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Nina Pusateri

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 MOTION

Plaintiff Nina Pusateri (“Plaintiff”) moves to seal Exhibit 2 attached to the Declaration of Kirill Lavinski in support of Plaintiff’s opposition to the pending motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.    

ANALYSIS

Civil court proceedings are presumptively open to the public. (See Richmond Newspapers, Inc. v. Virginia (1980) 448 U.S. 555, 580, fn. 17.)  Before sealing records, “a trial court must hold a hearing and expressly find that (i) there exists an overriding interest supporting closure and/or sealing; (ii) there is a substantial probability that the interest will be prejudiced absent closure and/or sealing; (iii) the proposed closure and/or sealing is narrowly tailored to serve the overriding interest; and (iv) there is no less restrictive means of achieving the overriding interest.” (NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1217-1218.)

Plaintiff filed the document at issue publicly.  As such, the document is already part of the public record, which makes a sealing order futile.  (Hurvitz v. Hoefflin (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1232, 1245.)  Further, the Court has reviewed the document and is not convinced sealing the entire document is warranted.  The Court cannot order the document sealed simply because of the parties’ stipulated protective order.  (See In re Marriage of Nicholas (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1566, 1578.) 

     Accordingly, the motion to seal is denied.

 


Case Number: 22STCV6414A    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV26414

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant Medical Management International, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Nina Pusateri

 BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Nina Pusateri (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant Medical Management International, Inc. (“Defendant”) for employment claims.  Plaintiff worked as a veterinarian at Defendant’s veterinary hospital.  Plaintiff alleges she discovered prescription medications were dispensed under her name without her knowledge or permission, and Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff for raising concerns about this practice by terminating her employment. 

Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action for retaliation and intentional infliction of emotional distress, as well as the claim for punitive damages.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

EVIDENCE

            Defendant’s objections to Plaintiff’s evidence are overruled.

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Retaliation

            To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must allege Plaintiff engaged in a protected activity; Defendant subjected Plaintiff to an adverse employment action; and there is a causal link between the two.  (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 874; Patten v. Grant Joint Union High School Dist. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1384.)  In the instant case, Plaintiff has asserted her retaliation claim pursuant to Labor Code section 1102.5, the “whistleblower” statute.  To do so, Plaintiff must disclose “reasonably based suspicions of illegal activity [and] [to] have a reasonably based suspicion of illegal activity, the employee must be able to point to some legal foundation for [her] suspicion--some statute, rule or regulation which may have been violated by the conduct [s]he disclosed.”  (Ross v. County of Riverside (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 580, 592, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Defendant may defeat Plaintiff’s claim for retaliation by demonstrating with clear and convincing evidence Defendant “would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.”  (See Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 712, 718 [the Court of Appeal held Labor Code section 1102.6, and not the burden shifting framework in the McDonnell Douglas v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 case, supplies the framework for adjudicating a whistleblower claim] .)

In support its motion, Defendant advances Plaintiff’s deposition testimony where Plaintiff testified she had longstanding issues with the practice manager (Teresa Montoya (“Montoya”)) of the animal hospital where Plaintiff worked, and Plaintiff had been “trying to bring up to the higher-ups all the incorrect problematic things that [Montoya had] been doing wrong because there [are] a lot of things she’s done poorly at her job . . . .”  (Compendium of Evidence in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication of Issues (Compendium), Exhibit 2, pp. 206, 207, 212.)  Plaintiff also testified at deposition she understood her supervisors required her to meet in person, and Plaintiff refused to do so (id.,  Exhibit 3, pp. 407-409); and she understood her refusal to meet in person could be grounds for termination, but Plaintiff refused to participate anyway (id., Exhibit 3, pp. 420, 424). 

Defendant also advances the testimony of Taneeka Bautista (“Bautista”), another veterinarian who worked for Defendant and supervised Plaintiff.  Bautista testified at her deposition she participated in the decision to terminate Plaintiff’s employment (Compendium, Exhibit 4, p. 82); and Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Plaintiff left work early without permission and refused to meet in person with Plaintiff’s direct manager, Lisette Farve (“Farve”), or Bautista (id., Exhibit 4, p. 85).   

Taken together, this evidence meets Defendant’s burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for retaliation because Defendant terminated Plaintiff for non-discriminatory reasons.  Defendant, therefore, shifted the burden to Plaintiff to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant’s termination of Plaintiff was due to Plaintiff’s reporting of issues concerning Defendant’s practices prescribing medications.

In opposition, Plaintiff presented her own declaration wherein Plaintiff concedes she refused to meet in person with Farve or Bautista but relates her refusal to do so “should not matter if the conversation was in person or over email . . . .”  (Declaration of Nina Pusateri, ¶ 43.)  This evidence does not raise triable issues of fact as to whether Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff because Plaintiff does not dispute she refused Defendant’s requirement of an in-person meeting.  In this regard, Plaintiff cannot raise triable issues of material fact by citing inferences “derived from speculation, conjecture, imagination, or guesswork.” (Joseph E. Di Loreto, Inc. v. O'Neill (1991) 1 Cal.App.4th 149, 161.)

Nonetheless, the circumstances of this case are such that the Court can infer retaliatory intent from circumstantial evidence.  As set forth above, Defendant was aware of Plaintiff’s report of the issuance of prescriptions that identified Plaintiff as the prescribing physician when Plaintiff had not prescribed the drugs, which is forbidden.  (See, e.g., Cal. Code Regs., tit. 16, § 2032.25, subd. (a) [“Absent establishing a veterinarian-client-patient relationship . . ., prescribing, dispensing, or furnishing dangerous drugs . . .  constitutes unprofessional conduct”].)  Given Defendant was aware of Plaintiff’s report, the proximity in time between when Plaintiff reported this wrongful conduct and Plaintiff’s termination is circumstantial evidence of retaliatory intent.  (See Morgan v. Regents of Univ. of Calif. (2000) 88 Cal.App.4th 52, 69[““The causal link may be established by an inference derived from circumstantial evidence, ‘such as the employer’s knowledge that the [employee] engaged in protected activities and the proximity in time between the protected action and allegedly retaliatory employment decision’””].) 

In reply, Defendant argues temporal proximity is insufficient.  However, the authority Defendant cites in support of this contention suggests otherwise.  For example, Defendant Points out Zirpel v. Alki David Productions, Inc. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 563, in which the Court of Appeal stated, “Temporal proximity alone, when very close, can in some instances establish a prima facie case of retaliation.”  (Id., at p. 578, internal quotations and citation omitted.)  In this case, the events at issue unfolded rapidly.  Defendant also argues Plaintiff cannot rely on temporal proximity because Plaintiff disobeyed Defendant’s requirement to meet in person.  In support of this argument, Defendant relies on federal opinions that are not from the Supreme Court of the United States.  Such decisions do not have binding authority with respect to this Court.  (Mesler v. Bragg Mgt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 299; see also Ferruzzo, Inc. v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 501, 503 [“Neither federal district court procedures nor decisions are binding on this court”].)  Likewise, “a written trial court ruling has no precedential value . . . .” (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshe (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831.)  

While the record Defendant presents shows the efforts it undertook to address the concerns Plaintiff raised and the opportunities it afforded Plaintiff to participate in in-person meetings, this court cannot say as a matter of law Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of material fact concerning whether Defendant retaliated against her for reporting issues concerning the prescribing of medications.  Therefore, under the circumstances of this case, the trier of fact must have the opportunity to determine whether Defendant’s termination was retaliatory or for legitimate reasons based on Plaintiff’s refusal to engage in an in-person meeting with her superiors. 

Second Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Workers’ compensation provides the exclusive remedy for emotional distress caused by an employer’s conduct in employment actions when the misconduct attributed to the employer are a normal part of the employment relationship.  (See Cole v. Fair Oaks Fire Prot. Dist. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 148, 160.)

As set forth above, Plaintiff contends Defendant required her to participate in an in-person meeting regarding her allegations of unlawful conduct in prescribing prescription medications.  Plaintiff did not participate, and Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment.  Both requirements to attend in-person meetings and termination are normal parts of the employment process.  As such, Defendant meets its burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on her claim for IIED because the workers’ compensation exclusivity rule applies.

In opposition, Plaintiff submitted a declaration in which she states she experienced distress due to “the treatment [Plaintiff] was receiving from [Defendant]’s management (not least, the threat of termination) . . . ,” and because Bautista and Farve “were intending to show up at the hospital . . . and force [Plaintiff] into an off-the-record oral discussion whether [Plaintiff] wanted it or not.”  (Pusateri Decl., ¶ 45.)  Again, employment termination and requirements that employees attend in-person meetings are normal aspects of the employment process, regardless of whether Plaintiff experienced emotional distress.  Therefore, the Court concludes Plaintiff has failed to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Plaintiff can prevail on her claim for IIED.

Punitive Damages

            Defendant moves to summarily adjudicate Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages, arguing Plaintiff cannot recover punitive damages because an officer, director, or managing agent of Defendant was not involved in the decision to terminate Plaintiff’s employment.  For purposes of punitive damages, a managing agent must have “substantial discretionary authority over significant aspects of a corporation's business.” (Davis v. Kiewit Pacific Co. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 358, 366, internal quotations, citations, and emphasis deleted.)

            In support of its contention, Defendant presents Bautista’s deposition testimony where she relates t she did not have the authority to “make policies that would affect” the veterinary hospital, or to “recommend certain policies that would affect [Defendant’s] operations.”  (Compendium, Exhibit 4, p. 23.)  This testimony is conclusory and vague.  Bautista does not describe the scope of her authority, as she only offers conclusions.  This evidence fails to meet Defendant’s burden of showing Plaintiff is not entitled to punitive damages because Bautista is not a managing agent of Defendant.

Defendant also argues Plaintiff cannot recover punitive damages because none of the actions Defendant took with respect to Plaintiff’s employment were intended to harm Plaintiff.  As set forth above, Plaintiff raises triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment in retaliation for Plaintiff’s report of wrongful conduct.  That may constitute extreme and outrageous conduct, which could support an award of punitive damages.  (See Mathews v. Happy Valley Conference Center (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 236, 266 [“punitive damages are recoverable for a violation of the whistleblower statute”].)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s second cause of action is granted.  Defendant’s motion for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication, is otherwise denied.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

Defendant Homeboy Industries (“Defendant”) demurs to the complaint of Plaintiff Jose Sevilla (“Plaintiff”).  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the Court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint. (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.” (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Plaintiff alleges Defendant hired Plaintiff to work in its 18-month training program, his case manager did not like him, and that individual harassed him until he was wrongfully terminated.  (First Amended Complaint, pp. 2-3.)  Plaintiff asserts causes of action for harassment, retaliation, and failure to accommodate disability.  Such causes of action arise under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA).  An aggrieved employee must file an administrative complaint with the California Department of Fair Employment and Housing before suing under FEHA.  (Gov. Code, §§ 12960, 12965, subds. (b), (c)(1)(A) &(D).) The administrative complaint must set forth the “particulars” of the alleged discrimination. (Gov. Code, § 12960, subds. (b), (c).)  Plaintiff does not allege he filed an administrative complaint. 

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer, but Plaintiff is afforded an opportunity to amend the complaint to cure this deficiency.  Plaintiff, therefore, is to file an amended complaint within 20 days of notice of this order.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file a proof of service of same.
