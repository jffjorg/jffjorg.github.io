[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to request for admission, set eight and for sanctions

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D. Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant Yang Lu

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Admission, Set Eight, and for Sanctions

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc.’s evidentiary objections, filed on April 19, 2024, as follows:

Objections Nos. 1 and 7 are overruled.

Objections Nos. 2-6, and 8 are sustained.

 

 

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc. (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Yang Lu (“Defendant”) to provide a further response to Requests for Admission, Set Eight, number 105, and (2) awarding sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $4,381.65.

The court finds that Defendant has not shown (1) that Plaintiff’s motion is untimely on the ground that it was filed after Defendant filed a motion for a protective order or (2) that, for purposes of this motion, Defendant was not required to answer this request for admission on the ground that Plaintiff requested that she admit more than 35 matters because Plaintiff submitted evidence showing that its discovery request was accompanied by a declaration that complies with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2033.030, subdivision (b) and 2033.050.[1]  (Chan Decl., Ex. A, pp. 4-5 [declaration of Chan for additional discovery].)

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further response to Request for Admission number 105 because (1) the objections to the request are without merit, and (2) the answer to the request is evasive and incomplete.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (d).)  The court finds that $1,661.65 ((4 hours x $400 hourly rate) + $61.65 filing fee) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Defendant in connection with this motion.  (Chan Decl., ¶ 18.)

The court denies Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff, made in her opposition.

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc.’s motion to compel further response to request for admission and for sanctions as follows.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290, the court orders defendant Yang Lu to serve a further, full and complete response to plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc.’s Requests for Admission, Set Eight, number 105, that complies with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2033.210-2033.240 within 20 days of the date of service of this order.

            The court orders defendant Yang Lu to pay monetary sanctions to plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc. in the amount of $1,661.65 within 30 days of the date of service of this order.

            The court orders plaintiff Seran Ng, D.M.D., Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:  April 26, 2024

MOVING PARTIES:              Plaintiff and cross-defendant KBS Holdco, LLC, d/b/a Regency Outdoor Advertising, and defendant and cross-complainant Thomas M. Carlson      

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       n/a

Joint Motion to Seal

The court considered the moving and supplemental papers filed in connection with this motion.  

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff and cross-defendant KBS Holdco, LLC, d/b/a Regency Outdoor Advertising (“Plaintiff”), together with defendant and cross-complainant Thomas M. Carlson (“Defendant”) (collectively, the “Parties”) move the court for an order sealing portions of Defendant’s Cross-Complaint.  Specifically, the Parties seek the seal the information set forth on the following pages of the Cross-Complaint: (1) page 2, lines 8-11; (2) page 2, lines 13-16; (3) page 2, lines 17-24; (4) page 3, lines 2-4; (5) page 3, lines 8-11; (6) page 5, lines 12-14; (7) page 5, lines 15-17; (8) page 5, lines 18-20; (9) page 6, lines 1-2; (10) page 6, lines 4-7; (11) page 6, lines 9-14; (12) page 7, lines 13-15; (13) page 7, lines 16-17; and (14) page 8, lines 21-23.   (Angioni Decl., Ex. A [proposed redacted Cross-Complaint].)  

Generally, court records are presumed to be open unless confidentiality is required by law.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (c).)¿ If the presumption of access applies, the court may order that a record be filed under seal “if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550, subd. (d).)¿¿ 

The Parties have asserted that, “as part of the[ir] settlement, the Parties jointly agreed to seek to redact allegations in the Cross-Complaint that [Plaintiff] contends damage Plaintiff’s (and its owner and employees’) character, business, future business opportunities, and professional reputation.”  (Mot., pp. 5:12-15, 6:2-5; Angioni Decl., ¶¶ 1, 3 [declaration from Plaintiff’s counsel stating that “As part of the Parties’ agreed upon settlement, the Parties agreed to jointly bring this Motion to Seal”]; Sylla Decl., ¶¶ 1, 4 [declaration from Defendant’s counsel stating that “As part of the Parties’ agreed upon settlement terms, this motion to seal the documents is being filed jointly” by the Parties].)  The portions of the Cross-Complaint that the Parties seek to seal allege facts supporting the causes of action for sex discrimination, sexual harassment, wrongful discharge, and retaliation.  (Angioni Decl., ¶ 4; Angioni Decl., Ex. A.)

On March 7, 2024, the court issued an order on the Parties’ motion (1) noting that the Parties’ settlement agreement might include a provision that does not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 1001, and (2) continuing the hearing to allow the Parties an opportunity to file supplemental briefs addressing the effect of section 1001 on their motion to seal.

The court finds that it may not enter an order sealing the proposed portions of the Cross-Complaint and therefore denies the Parties’ joint motion to seal.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (b).)

“[A] provision within a settlement agreement that prevents or restricts the disclosure of factual information related to a claim filed in a civil action or a complaint filed in an administrative action, regarding any of the following, is prohibited: [¶¶] (3) An act of workplace harassment or discrimination, failure to prevent an act of workplace harassment or discrimination, or an act of retaliation against a person for reporting or opposing harassment or discrimination, as described in subdivisions (a), (h), (i), (j), and (k) of Section 12940 of the Government Code.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (a)(3).)  In such a civil matter, “a court shall not enter, by stipulation or otherwise, an order that restricts the disclosure of information in a manner that conflicts with subdivision (a).”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (b).)

            As set forth above, the Parties have each stated that they have filed the pending motion to seal “[a]s part of the Parties’ agreed upon settlement” of this action.  (Mot., pp. 5:12-15, 6:2-5; Angioni Decl., ¶ 3; Sylla Decl., ¶ 4.)  The allegations that the Parties seek to seal describe workplace harassment under the Fair Employment and Housing Act.  (Angioni Decl., Ex. A.)  Thus, the “provision within [the Parties’] settlement agreement that” requires the Parties to seal this information is a provision that “prevents or restricts the disclosure of factual information related to a claim filed in a civil action” for workplace harassment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (a)(3).)  The court therefore “shall not enter . . . an order” sealing, and therefore restricting the disclosure of, this information pursuant to the Parties’ settlement agreement.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (b).)

            The court notes that Plaintiff has argued, in its supplemental brief, that section 1001 does not apply to the Parties’ motion because that statute applies only to settlement agreements.  (Pl. Supp. Brief, p. 2:7-11, 2:20-23.)  The court disagrees. 

As set forth above, section 1001, subdivision (a) prohibits the inclusion of provisions in settlement agreements that prevent or restrict the disclosure of information related to certain claims filed in civil or administrative actions.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (a).)  The court agrees that this provision applies to settlement agreements.  (Ibid.)  However, subdivision (b) of section 1001 is not so limited.  Pursuant to that subdivision, “a court shall not enter, by stipulation or otherwise, an order that restricts the disclosure of information in a manner that conflicts with subdivision (a).”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1001, subd. (b).)  Thus, this provision prohibits a court from entering “an order” preventing or restricting the disclosure of factual information described in subdivision (a).  The plain language of subdivision (b) does not limit its application to motions involving settlement agreements.

The court also notes that Defendant, in his supplemental brief, has asserted that the unverified allegations made in the Cross-Complaint do not constitute “factual information” within the meaning of section 1001.  The court, however, disagrees, because the allegations here set forth factual information describing the existence of workplace harassment.

Thus, the court finds that the Parties have not met their burden to show that the court is not prohibited from entering an order sealing portions of Defendant’s Cross-Complaint under Code of Civil Procedure section 1001.  The court also finds that, based on the public policy expressed in section 1001, there is not an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record as required.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.550, subd. (d)(1).)

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff and cross-defendant KBS Holdco, LLC, d/b/a Regency Outdoor Advertising and defendant and cross-complainant Thomas M. Carlson’s joint motion to seal.

            The court orders defendant and cross-complainant Thomas M. Carlson to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

cross-defendants’ demurrer to second amended cross-complaint

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Cross-defendants Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty                       

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant and cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC

Demurrer to Second Amended Cross-Complaint

Plaintiff Patricia Galea (“Plaintiff”) filed this fraud action on December 9, 2022 against, inter alia, defendant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC (“Prosodos”).

On February 10, 2023, Prosodos filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty.

On June 12, 2023, the court issued an order sustaining the demurrer to Prosodos’s Cross-Complaint filed by cross-defendants Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty (“Cross-Defendants”).  Pursuant to the court’s order granting it leave to amend, Prosodos filed its First Amended Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff and Cross-Defendants on June 21, 2023.

On November 28, 2023, the court issued an order sustaining Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to Prosodos’s third, fourth, and fifth causes of action in the First Amended Cross-Complaint with leave to amend.  (Nov. 28, 2023 Order, p. 5:1-5.)  The court granted Prosodos 20 days leave to file a Second Amended Cross-Complaint that cured the deficiencies in the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action.  (Nov. 28, 2023 Order, p. 5:12-14.)

Now pending before the court is the demurrer filed by Cross-Defendants to the third, fourth, and fifth causes of action alleged in “the Second Amended Cross-Complaint of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC[.]”  (Demurrer, p. 2:1-3.)  However, Prosodos did not file a Second Amended Cross-Complaint with the court.  Moreover, the parties did not attach the Second Amended Cross-Complaint as an exhibit for the court’s review.

Thus, it appears that, pursuant to the court’s November 28, 2023 order granting it leave to amend, Prosodos (1) served on Cross-Defendants and Plaintiff[1] a Second Amended Cross-Complaint but (2) inadvertently failed to file the Second Amended Cross-Complaint with the court.

Because Prosodos has not filed its Second Amended Cross-Complaint with the court, the court cannot rule on Cross-Defendants’ pending demurrer.

The court therefore exercises its discretion to continue the hearing on Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to give Prosodos an opportunity to file its Second Amended Cross-Complaint.

The court orders that the hearing on cross-defendants Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty’s demurrer is continued to June 5, 2024, at 10:00 a.m., in Department 53.

The court orders defendant and cross-complainant Prosodos Holdings V, LLC to file the Second Amended Cross-Complaint that it served on plaintiff and cross-defendant Patricia Galea and cross-defendants Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty with the court no later than May 6, 2024.

           

 

 

The court orders cross-defendants Andrew Morgan, and Westchester Playa Realty Alliance, Inc., d/b/a Pacific Playa Realty to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
