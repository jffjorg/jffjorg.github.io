DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00952    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER INTERROGATORIES AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION – SET ONE and DENYING REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS.

I.         INTRODUCTION

On April 28, 2023, Plaintiff Perla Mageno (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Prapatsorn Group, Inc. (“Defendant”) for violations of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, California Civil Code § 51.

 

On August 18, 2024, Defendant filed motions to compel further responses to Special and Form Interrogatories- Set One and Request for Production - Set One.

On December 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed opposition to both motions. No reply has been filed.

Meet and confer

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating, “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)

Here, Defendant’s counsel initiated the meet-and-confer process by sending a letter to Plaintiff’s counsel on May 16, 2024, outlining specific concerns regarding Plaintiff’s initial responses to the Requests for Production. Defendant allowed Plaintiff additional time to address these concerns, setting a deadline of June 28, 2024. Plaintiff submitted supplemental responses on June 28, 2024. However, Defendant reviewed the supplemental responses and found them insufficient. Defendant emailed Plaintiff’s counsel on July 11, 2024, to raise further concerns but did not engage in any additional follow-up before filing the motions to compel on August 14, 2024. (Sahelian Decl., ¶¶ 5-15.)

The Court finds that while Defendant made an initial attempt to meet and confer, these efforts lacked sufficient follow-up to fully resolve the disputes before filing the motions. 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A motion to compel a further response to interrogatories or production of documents must be noticed within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c); see also Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409; Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685).) Otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel further responses. (Id.) The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  (Sexton, supra,58 Cal. App. 4th at 1410.)   

            An unverified response is tantamount to no response at all. (See Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)  However, objections to interrogatories and requests for production are not required to be verified because “objections are legal conclusions interposed by counsel, not factual assertions by a party.” (Blue Ridge Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 339, 345.)

III.      ANALYSIS

            Defendant moves the Court to compel Plaintiff to provide further responses to Form and Special Interrogatories - Set One and Request for Production - Set One, and for monetary sanctions in the amounts of $2, 551.02 and $2,166.02.

Defendant served the discovery requests on May 15, 2024. While Plaintiff provided initial responses, Defendant argues that they were incomplete and evasive. Defendant filed the motions on August 14, 2024, asserting that Plaintiff failed to provide substantive responses to several interrogatories and document requests and raised meritless objections. Motion, p. 3; Sahelian Decl., ¶¶ 4-15).

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Plaintiff had already served further supplemental responses to Defendant’s discovery requests before the hearing. Plaintiff contended that the supplemental responses fully addressed the alleged deficiencies and rendered the motions moot. Additionally, Plaintiff argued that Defendant failed to properly meet and confer before filing the motions, as required by California Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040. 

The Court finds that given that Plaintiff has provided further responses prior to the hearing, the motion is rendered MOOT.

Therefore, the Court DENIES Defendants motions to compel further.

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure §2023.030 grants the court discretion to impose sanctions against any party engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. Misuse includes the failure to respond to authorized discovery methods or disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., §2023.010, subds. (d), (g).) Further, CCP §2030.300(d) mandates that the court impose monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories, unless that party acted with substantial justification or if imposing sanctions would be unjust. 

Here, Defendant seeks monetary sanctions for both motions totaling $4,717.04 (Sahelian Decl., ¶¶ 16-17).

Plaintiff opposes the imposition of sanctions, arguing that the motions are moot because Plaintiff has provided supplemental responses both in the past and prior to the hearing. Plaintiff supplemented its discovery responses on June 28, 2024, following Defendant’s initial meet-and-confer efforts, and again submitted further responses shortly before the hearing to address any remaining concerns. (Opposition, pp. 2-3)

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s ongoing efforts to respond demonstrate substantial justification. Additionally, Plaintiff provided further responses prior to this hearing. Furthermore, the Court finds that Defendant’s meet-and-confer efforts lacked sufficient follow-up to efficiently resolve disputes before the filing of the motions to compel further. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant's request for monetary sanctions.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court DENIES Defendant’s motions to compel further as moot and DENIES request for monetary sanctions.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO WITHDRAW STIPULATION FOR REFEREE and DENYING NON-PARTY STEPHEN J. DONELL’S REQUEST FOR ORDER

I.         INTRODUCTION

Plaintiffs, Scott Marshall-Kimball, and Julie Kimball, as Co-Trustees of the Gail Diann Fromm Family Trust dated November 11, 1980 (“Plaintiffs”), filed this action on June 5, 2023, against Defendants Cheryl L. Cuccia and Layne M. Cosloff, as Trustees of their respective trusts (“Defendants”). On November 22, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action for partition, accounting, breach of fiduciary duty, and declaratory relief. The dispute arises from the co-ownership of a multi-unit commercial property located at 9331 East Valley Blvd., Rosemead, California (“Subject Property”), where Plaintiffs seek a partition by sale due to irreconcilable differences regarding the management and disposition of the property.

On November 12, 2024, the parties entered into a stipulation to appoint Stephen J. Donell as the partition referee to oversee the sale and accounting of the Subject Property. However, on December 11, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to withdraw the stipulation, citing unforeseen financial burdens and mistake. On December 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing that Defendants had not demonstrated good cause to withdraw from the stipulation and that Mr. Donell had diligently fulfilled his duties.

On December 3, 2024, Mr. Donell filed an application with the Court seeking an order authorizing the referee to retain legal counsel, a real estate broker, and a certified public accountant. 

The Court now considers both the application and Defendants’ motion to withdraw the stipulation for determination.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Rule 3.906(a) provides:  

 

A motion to withdraw a stipulation for the appointment of a referee must be supported by a declaration of facts establishing good cause for permitting the party to withdraw the stipulation. 

 

(Cal. Rule of Court 3.906(a).) 

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendants argue that good cause exists under California Rules of Court, rule 3.906(a), to withdraw the stipulation appointing Stephen J. Donell (“Mr. Donell”) as the partition referee due to the unforeseen financial burden imposed by Mr. Donell’s retention of outside counsel and other professionals. Specifically, Defendants contend that the anticipated costs, exceeding $80,000, are disproportionately higher than the estimated costs of hiring an alternative referee such as Matthew Taylor who estimates $15,000-$20,000 costs in fees. (Motion, p. 3; Niman Decl., ¶¶ 3, 5) Defendants assert that this substantial and unanticipated financial burden constitutes reasonable ground for the relief sought. (Motion, p.4)

Alternatively, Defendants argue that the stipulation should be set aside due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b). Defendants assert that their counsel mistakenly believed Mr. Donell would handle all legal services in-house and were unaware of his intention to retain outside counsel at a significant additional cost. They contend that this misunderstanding constitutes a valid basis for relief under section 473(b), as the stipulation would not have been agreed to had the true financial implications been known (Motion, p. 5; Niman Decl., ¶¶ 3, 7, 14).

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants have failed to establish good cause for withdrawing from the stipulation appointing Mr. Donell as the partition referee. Plaintiffs contend that the stipulation explicitly contemplated the retention of professionals, including legal counsel, and that Defendants had ample opportunity to investigate and understand the potential costs before agreeing to the stipulation (Opposition, pp. 3-4). Plaintiffs emphasize that Mr. Donell’s fees and the anticipated costs of hiring professionals were foreseeable and disclosed in the stipulation and related documents, including Mr. Donell’s declaration stating his hourly rate (Opposition, p. 3; Luc Decl., ¶ 5).

Plaintiffs further argue that Defendants’ claim of mistake is unfounded, as Defendants’ counsel failed to exercise reasonable diligence in reviewing the terms of the stipulation and understanding the role of the referee. (Opposition, p.4). Plaintiffs argue that Mr. Donell has diligently fulfilled his duties as the partition referee by addressing property maintenance issues, coordinating with tenants, and organizing the sale process. Plaintiffs assert that replacing Mr. Donell would disrupt the progress thus far and unnecessarily complicate the proceedings. (Opposition, p. 5). They contend that Defendants’ proposal to appoint Mathew Taylor is not a viable alternative, as Mr. Taylor would rely on a property management company, which violates the terms of the stipulation (Opposition, p. 6).

While good cause is not bound by rigid legal formalism, it must be reasonably related to legitimate needs and supported by a concrete factual exposition (Estate of Kerkorian (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 709, 713; People v. Accredited Surety & Cas. Co. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 548, 558). Here, Defendants highlight that Mr. Donell’s fees, coupled with those of his outside counsel, far exceed the anticipated costs, arguing this constitutes good cause to revisit the stipulation (Niman Decl., ¶¶ 3-4).

The Court finds that Defendants have sufficiently demonstrated that the unanticipated financial burden associated with Mr. Donell’s retention of outside professionals constitutes good cause under rule 3.906(a). Professionals in the field, such as Mr. Taylor, estimate costs significantly lower—between $15,000 and $20,000—which starkly contrasts with the upwards of $80,000 projected for Mr. Donell’s services (Niman Decl., ¶ 5). While the stipulation permitted Mr. Donell to retain professionals, the possibility of such high costs was not clearly indicated, making the financial burden beyond the parties’ reasonable expectations.

Given that Defendants did not foresee the substantial costs associated with Mr. Donell’s services, and good cause is a discretionary determination based on the circumstances of the case, the Court finds that Defendants have shown good cause to withdraw the stipulation. The Court orders a conference to appoint a new referee and ensure that Mr. Donell is compensated for his services rendered thus far.

Motion for issuance of an order authorizing Mr. Donell to retain professionals

Mr. Donell filed a motion seeking authorization to retain (1) legal counsel, (2) a real estate broker, and (3) a certified public accountant. However, given that the Court grants Defendants’ motion to be relieved from the stipulation appointing Mr. Donell as the partition referee, the Court DENIES Mr. Donell’s motion to retain these professionals.

            IV.       CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to be relieved from the stipulation appointing Mr. Donell as the partition referee.

The Court DENIES Mr. Donell’s motion to retain legal counsel, real estate broker and certified public accountant. 

The Court orders a status conference to address the appointment of a new referee or an alternative method for appraisal.

The Court further orders that Mr. Donell be compensated for his services rendered to date.

Defendants to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT BARRY NUA’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES - SET ONE, SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES - SET ONE AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION - SET ONE and GRANTING MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.                INTRODUCTION

This is a premises liability case. On January 5, 2024, Plaintiff Salvador Carrillo ("Plaintiff") filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against Defendants Barry Nua, PGK Partners, and Greystar Properties ("Defendants"), alleging causes of action for (1) Battery, (2) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention, and (3) Negligent Premises Liability.

On December 10, 2024, Defendant Barry Nua (“Defendant”) filed motions to compel Plaintiff’s initial responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production, Set One. On December 27, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motions and served responses to the discovery. On January 2, 2025, Defendant filed a reply, arguing that Plaintiff’s responses were untimely and inadequate.

The Court combines both discovery motions—motions to compel initial responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production, Set One—into this tentative ruling.

II.  LEGAL STANDARD

A propounding party may move to compel responses to requests for production of documents where the responding party fails to provide responses. (CCP § 2031.300.) The responding party must provide responses within thirty days after the demand is served. (CCP § 2031.030(c)(2) & (3).) The responding party waives all objections, including privilege and work product, by failing to timely respond to requests for production of documents. (CCP § 2031.300.)¿

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(a), if a party fails to respond to interrogatories, they waive any objections, including those based on privilege or work product protection. When a party does not provide timely responses, the propounding party is entitled to move for an order compelling responses. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(b), the Court must order the responding party to provide responses without objections.

III.  ANALYSIS

Here, Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiff to respond to Request for Production, Set One, and Form and Special Interrogatories, Set One. Defendant propounded these discovery requests on August 26, 2024. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff failed to serve timely responses by the statutory deadline of September 27, 2024. (Lee decl., ¶¶ 3–8) Defendant further argues that Plaintiff served responses only after the motions were filed and that the responses provided are untimely and inadequate.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the motions should be denied as moot because responses were served on December 12, 2024, prior to the hearing. Plaintiff contends that the delays were caused by administrative oversight and asserts that Defendant has not suffered any prejudice. (Opposition, pp. 1–3; Declaration of Ralph Rios, ¶¶ 2–5¿)

In reply, Defendant argues that the untimely responses fail to comply with the Code of Civil Procedure and that significant deficiencies remain. Defendant highlights that Plaintiff’s responses contain improper objections, fail to include promised documents, and inadequately address specific interrogatories and requests for production. (Reply, pp. 2–4)

Here, while Plaintiff’s responses were served late, they were provided before the hearing. The Court acknowledges Defendant’s frustration with the untimely responses and notes the long list of alleged deficiencies. However, the proper remedy to address these issues is a motion to compel further responses, not a motion to compel initial responses.

The Court finds that Defendant’s motions to compel initial responses to Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production are moot, as Plaintiff served responses prior to the hearing. Under California law, once discovery responses are served, the appropriate remedy to address the adequacy of those responses is a motion to compel further responses pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300 (interrogatories) and 2031.310 (requests for production). Such motions must be filed within 45 days of service of the responses and only after good faith meet-and-confer efforts to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c)).  The Court orders counsel to meet and confer to attempt to resolve these outstanding discovery matters without the Court’s involvement.  The Court reminds the parties that civil discovery is intended to be a self-executing system with minimal judicial intervention.  (See Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Ct. (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 331; Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1291-1292.)

 

Accordingly, the motions to compel initial responses are MOOT.

V.        SANCTIONS

CCP § 2023.030 authorizes the Court to issue sanctions against a party engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process. Failure to respond to discovery, evasive responses, and objections lacking substantial justification are “misuses of the discovery process.” (CCP § 2023.010, subd. (d)-(f).)¿

Here, Defendant seeks monetary sanctions for Plaintiff’s failure to timely respond to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production, arguing that Plaintiff’s delay necessitated the filing of these motions. Defendant initially requested $888.00 in sanctions for the motion to compel responses to Form and Special Interrogatories, based on an hourly rate of $185.00 and an estimated 4.8 hours of work. (Lee Decl., ¶ 11.) For the motion to compel responses to Requests for Production, Defendant requested $499.50 in sanctions, based on an hourly rate of $185.00 and an estimated 2.7 hours of work, including 0.5 hours on legal research, 0.8 hours drafting the motion, 0.3 hours preparing exhibits, 0.5 hours reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition, and 0.6 hours drafting the reply. (Lee Decl., ¶ 10.)

Plaintiff opposes sanctions, contending that the failure to respond was due to administrative oversight which was corrected upon notice from Defendant. (Opposition, p. 3.) However, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s explanation is insufficient to constitute substantial justification. 

Given this, the Court reduces Defendant’s monetary sanctions request to reflect reasonable time spent on these motions.  The Court finds that 2 hours of preparation and drafting, 1 hour for replying to the opposition, and 1 hour for attending the hearing for both motions, at an hourly rate of $185.00. This amounts to a total of $740.00 for both motions.

The Court awards $740 in sanctions to be paid within 20 days of this order. 

 

VI.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motions to compel as MOOT.

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $740.00 to be paid within 20 days of this order.

Defendants to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING HG’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT WITHOUT PREJUDICE

I.         INTRODUCTION

This case involves a contractual dispute between three brothers: Plaintiffs Yu Li and Yong Li, and Defendant Ji Li. Plaintiffs allege that Ji Li defrauded them in connection with their family business, Plaintiff JWY Group, Inc. (collectively, "Plaintiffs").

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the instant action alleging seven causes of action: (1) Cancellation of Written Instruments, (2) Fraud, (3) Intentional Concealment, (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (5) Conversion, (6) Breach of Contract, and (7) Injunction.

On February 27, 2024, Defendant HG Realty Investment & Management Inc. ("HG") filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (FACC) against JWY Group, Inc., and Ji Li (collectively "JWY") for ten causes of action, including multiple claims for breach of promissory notes, judicial foreclosure, fraud, and declaratory relief.

On December 12, 2024, HG filed the instant motion for leave to amend its cross-complaint to file a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (SACC). HG seeks to add new causes of action and additional parties based on facts allegedly discovered by its new counsel. On January 6, 2025, JWY filed an opposition to the motion. No reply has been filed by HG.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) provides: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.¿ The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)¿¿¿ ¿ 

Permissible amendments pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) include amendments which add causes of action.¿ A plaintiff that fails to plead a cause of action arising out of the transaction or occurrence that gave rise to the complaint may apply to the court for leave to amend the complaint to assert the cause of action, whether that failure was the result of oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50; see Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c).)¿ The motion to amend may be filed at any time during the course of the action, on notice to the adverse party.¿ (Id., § 426.50.)¿ ¿ 

The court has broad discretion to permit amendments to pleadings, and “the court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.”¿ (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)¿ “The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend . . ..”¿¿ (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)¿ Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.”¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿ ¿ 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1).)¿ A motion to amend a pleading must also be supported by a declaration which specifies the following: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)¿

III.      ANALYSIS

            HG brings this motion seeking leave to amend to file a SACC arguing that the proposed amendments are necessary to address new facts discovered by its counsel regarding the authority of Ji Li and the involvement of Yu Li, Yong Li, and Jia Wang in managing JWY Group. HG contends that the amendments will allow it to add causes of action for recovery of the loan deficiency and appointment of receiver and for damages. (Jung Decl. ¶¶3-5) 

HG asserts that its motion complies with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, citing the inclusion of the proposed SACC and a declaration outlining the necessity of the amendments. HG also argues that no party will be prejudiced, as the trial date is set for January 2026, leaving sufficient time for discovery.  (Motion, p.7) HG claims it sought to amend earlier through stipulations, but opposing counsel’s failure to cooperate delayed the filing of the motion. (Motion, p.3)

In opposition, JWY contends that HG’s motion fails to comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a) and (b). JWY argues that HG’s declaration does not identify the specific amendments or their location in the SACC, nor does it adequately explain the effect of the amendments, when the facts were discovered, or why the motion was delayed. (Opposition, p. 2.) JWY further argues that the proposed amendments are meritless and inconsistent with prior allegations in HG’s pleadings. 

The Court finds that HG’s motion does not comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a) and (b). Although HG includes a copy of the proposed SACC, the motion fails to identify the specific allegations being added or removed, along with their precise location in the amended pleading, as required under Rule 3.1324(a)(2)-(3). The lack of specificity impedes the Court’s ability to evaluate the proposed changes.

Moreover, HG’s declaration does not fully address the requirements of Rule 3.1324(b). While HG generally describes the necessity of the amendments, it fails to specify when the facts underlying the amendments were discovered. Additionally, although HG asserts that prior counsel did not include the additional claims, it does not adequately explain why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES HG’s motion for leave to amend SACC without prejudice.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court DENIES HG’s motion for leave to file the SACC without prejudice.

HG is to give notice.

Case Number: 24AHCV00221    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINT and DENYING MOTION TO STRIKE

I.         INTRODUCTION

In this action, Plaintiffs The One Pioneer LLC dba One Up Insurance Agency (“TOP”), Dandan Song aka Alex Song (“Song”), Peiran Li aka Alice Li (“Li”), Feng Tang aka Jenny Tang (“Tang”), and Annie Yuan On (“On”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege that their competitors in the Asian-American insurance and wealth management industry, Defendants Pom Insurance Agency Inc. dba Peace of Mind Wealth Management (“POM”), Alan Wen (“Wen”), and Angel Wenjun Tang (“Tang”) (collectively, “Defendants”), caused the termination of Plaintiffs’ contracts (allegedly valued in millions and billions), the loss of Plaintiffs’ clients, and significant financial damages to Plaintiffs. The complaint asserts 15 causes of action, including breach of contract, intentional interference with contractual relations, misappropriation of trade secrets, defamation, assault, and battery.

On August 27, 2024, Cross-Complainants POM and Wen (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendants TOP, Song ,Li, Tang, and On (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”), asserting eight causes of action: (1) Trespass, (2) Conversion, (3) Assault, (4) Defamation, (5) Defamation, (6) Defamation, (7) Unfair Business Practices, and (8) Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage.

On October 1, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed a demurrer with motion to strike to the Cross-Complaint. On December 26, 2024, Cross-Complainants filed an opposition, and on January 2, 2025, Cross-Defendants submitted a reply.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, Cross-Defendants counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Cross-Complainants’ counsel on September 16, 2024, outlining alleged deficiencies in the Cross-Complaint and requesting amendments or dismissal (Rothman Decl., ¶ 3). Cross-Complainants’ counsel responded via email on September 23, 2024, declining to amend or dismiss the claims and inviting Cross-Defendants to proceed with their motions (Rothman Decl., ¶ 4).

While correspondence occurred, no meet and confer discussion was held via the required methods of phone, video, or in person. Thus, the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 were not satisfied.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Cross-Defendants demur to the Cross-Complaint’s third cause of action for Trespass, fourth cause of action for Assault, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action for Defamation, eighth cause of action for Unfair Business Practices, and ninth cause of action for Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, arguing that the allegations fail to state sufficient facts to constitute valid claims under California law.

First cause of action: Trespass

Cross-Defendants contend that this cause of action fails to state a cause of action against Cross-Defendant Song because the Cross-Complaint does not allege that Song personally entered the Las Vegas office on October 26, 2023 (Demurrer, p. 2). Cross-Defendants maintain that Cross-Complaint merely alleges that Song “directed” Feng Tang and Annie On to access the premises, which is insufficient to support a claim for trespass.  Cross-Defendants further argue that the allegations do not demonstrate the absence of permission to enter, particularly given that the office was used for business purposes and open to the public (Reply, p. 3)

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that under California law, trespass may be committed not only by an unauthorized physical entry but also by causing another person to enter the property unlawfully (Opposition, p. 2) They rely on Martin Marietta Corp. v. Ins. Co. of N. Am. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1132, to assert that a defendant may be liable for trespass if they directed or caused a wrongful entry.

To set forth a cause of action for trespass, Defendants must allege (1) Defendants’ lawful possession or right to possession of real property; (2) Plaintiffs’ wrongful, intentional, reckless, or negligent act of trespass on the property; (3) Defendants did not give permission for the entry or scope of permission was exceeded; and (4) damage to Defendants caused by the trespass. (Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262.)  

The Court finds that the allegations in the Cross-Complaint are sufficient to state a cause of action for trespass against Cross-Defendant Song at the pleading stage. Cross-Complainants allege that Cross-Defendant Song directed Feng Tang and Annie On to enter the Las Vegas office without authorization, intending to disrupt the meeting between Angel Tang and Alan Wen (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 50, 54). Under California law, trespass may be committed not only through direct physical entry but also by causing or directing another person to unlawfully enter the property.

At this stage of the proceedings, the allegations are taken as true, and the Cross-Complaint adequately pleads the requisite elements for trespass, including Song's liability for directing the unauthorized entry. Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

Third cause of action: Assault

Cross-Defendants argue that the third cause of action for assault fails because the allegations in the Cross-Complaint are contradictory. Specifically, Cross-Defendants highlight that while the Cross-Complaint alleges that Angel Tang "was assaulted and knocked to the ground" (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 28), it simultaneously asserts, in support of the trespass claim, that Alan Wen interceded to prevent harm to Tang by pushing Feng Tang away (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 53). Cross-Defendants argue that this contradiction undermines the claim for assault. (Demurrer, p.6) 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that the allegations are not contradictory and that a cause of action for assault does not require actual physical harm or contact. Under California law, an assault claim is established if the defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or if the plaintiff reasonably believed that such contact was imminent (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669). Cross-Complainants contend that the alleged actions of Feng Tang and Annie On, including their refusal to leave and aggressive behavior, created a reasonable belief on Tang's part that she was about to be harmed (Opposition, p. 4). They further argue that the allegation of Wen’s intervention does not negate the assault claim, as the fear and harm were already caused by the actions of the Cross-Defendants prior to Wen's involvement.

“The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed [he or] she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.) 

Here, the Cross-Complaint alleges that Feng Tang and Annie On acted aggressively and verbally abused Angel Tang, which escalated into a physical altercation during which Tang was allegedly assaulted and knocked to the ground (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 28, 50-53). While the allegations regarding Alan Wen’s intervention suggest that Tang may not have been physically harmed as initially alleged, an assault claim does not require actual contact. The key inquiry is whether Tang reasonably believed she was about to be harmed, and the allegations support such a belief based on the conduct of Feng Tang and Annie On.

At this pleading stage, inconsistencies do not defeat the claim. The Court must accept the allegations as true and construe them in the light most favorable to the Cross-Complainants. The allegations that Tang, a 60-year-old disabled woman, faced aggressive and threatening behavior, coupled with the Defendants’ refusal to leave, are sufficient to establish the elements of assault.

Thus, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to this cause of action.

Fourth cause of action: Defamation

Cross-Defendants argue that this cause of action fails because the alleged defamatory statements lack specificity. Specifically, Cross-Defendants contend that the statements attributed to Dandan Song in the October 26, 2023, letter, including assertions that Angel Tang’s behavior was “unethical” and violated TOP’s ethical standards, are not actionable because they are protected opinions rather than provable falsehoods (Demurrer, p. 7). Cross-Defendants also argue that the letter was distributed internally to TOP’s agents and, under the “totality of the circumstances” test, cannot reasonably be construed as defamatory (Demurrer, p. 8).

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that the statements in the letter are defamatory because they falsely imply that Tang engaged in unethical and unprofessional behavior. There are not mere opinions, but false factual assertions intended to harm Tang’s reputation in the insurance industry. (Opposition, p.5) 

The elements of a defamation claim are (1) a publication that is (2) false, (3) defamatory, (4) unprivileged, and (5) has a natural tendency to injure or causes special damage. (See Wong v. Tai Jing (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1354, 1369.

Here, the Cross-Complaint alleges that Cross-Defendant Song published a letter to TOP’s agents falsely accusing Tang of unethical behavior (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 71-75). These statements are actionable if they imply provably false factual assertions. The allegations that Song’s statements were knowingly false and made with the intent to damage Tang’s professional reputation sufficiently plead the falsity and defamatory nature of the statements.

The publication element is met because the letter was shared with TOP’s agents, and California law does not require publication to the public; communication to even a single third party is sufficient (Smith v. Maldonado (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 637, 645). The Cross-Complaint further alleges that Tang suffered financial harm and reputational damage within her professional community as a result of the statements, satisfying the element of injury or special damages.

At the pleading stage, the Court accepts the allegations as true and evaluates them in the light most favorable to the Cross-Complainants. The allegations sufficiently state a claim for defamation,

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to this cause of action.

Fifth cause of action: Defamation

Cross-Defendants argue that this cause of action fails because the allegations regarding the social media post on We-Chat by Cross-Defendant Li fail to specify defamatory statement. They contend that the statements allegedly made are non-actionable opinions. (Opposition, p.9) They further assert that the Cross-Complaint fails to attach the defamatory posts, which is insufficient and prevents them from understanding the allegations against them. (Opposition, p. 10)

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that the social media post by Li is defamatory because it falsely portrays Wen as unethical and incompetent, harming his reputation in the Asian-American financial services community (Opposition, p. 6). They contend that Li’s statement falsely suggested that Cross-Complainants were engaged in improper or immoral conduct, which amounts to a false factual assertion rather than an opinion. Additionally, Cross-Complainants allege that Li’s false claim that Cross-Defendants called the police during the October 26, 2023, altercation is intended to mislead readers and harm Wen’s credibility. Cross-Complainants argue that these statements were made maliciously and with the intent to cause reputational harm (Opposition, p. 7).

The Court finds that the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges that Cross-Defendant Peiran Li made false and defamatory statements about Alan Wen on the social media platform WeChat, including claims that impugned his business ethics and professional reputation, with the intent to cause harm (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 79-84). The allegations further assert that these statements led to financial harm, including the resignation of a top-performing agent. At the pleading stage, these allegations are sufficient to state a claim for defamation. 

The Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to this cause of action.

Sixth cause of action: Defamation

Cross-Defendants argue that this cause of action fails because the allegations regarding defamatory emails are vague. They contend that the Cross-Complaint does not provide the exact language or the full context of the alleged emails, making it impossible to determine whether the statements are actionable as defamation. Cross-Defendants further argue that the alleged statements, such as accusing Alan Wen of being a “criminal mastermind,” are likely non-actionable opinions rather than provably false factual assertions (Demurrer, p. 10).

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that the email alleging Alan Wen was a “criminal mastermind” engaged in criminal conduct (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 88) sufficiently supports a claim for defamation. While Cross-Defendants contend that the Cross-Complaint fails to specify the recipients or locations of the emails (Demurrer, p. 10), Cross-Complainants counter that they have adequately alleged the email’s date (e.g., May 5, 2024) and defamatory content. They assert that these allegations provide sufficient notice of the defamation claim, and they are entitled to use pre-trial discovery to determine the recipients and scope of the communications (Opposition, p. 6)

The Court finds that the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges that Cross-Defendants sent emails containing false and defamatory statements about Alan Wen, including accusations of criminal conduct, which were intended to harm his professional reputation (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 86-91). At the pleading stage, these allegations are sufficient to state a claim for defamation.

Accordingly, the demurrer to the Sixth Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

Seventh cause of action: Unfair Business Practices

Cross-Defendants argue that this cause of action fails because it is derivative of the defamation and trespass claims, which they contend are insufficiently pleaded (Demurrer, p. 11). However, as discussed above, the Court finds that the defamation and trespass claims withstand demurrer at this stage. Accordingly, the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges that Cross-Defendants engaged in unlawful and unfair business practices, including defamation and trespass, with the intent to harm Cross-Complainants’ business operations and reputation (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 92-97). At the pleading stage, these allegations are sufficient to state a claim under section 17200. The demurrer to the Seventh Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

Eighth cause of action: Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

Cross-Defendants argue that this cause of action fails because the Cross-Complaint does not allege specific economic relationships with which Cross-Defendants interfered. In response, Cross-Complainants argue that they have adequately alleged these elements, pointing to their reasonable expectation of business with groups seeking insurance in California, Nevada, and other states where they are licensed, including their association with carriers like NLG (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 99). They further assert that they are active participants in the Asian-American financial services industry and service many clients, which supports their claim of existing relationships subject to disruption.

Under Youst v. Longo (1987) 43 Cal.3d 64, 71, the elements of the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic relationship (IIPER) include the following: 

(a) An economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff. 

(b) The defendant's knowledge of the relationship. 

(c) Intentional acts by the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship. 

(d) Actual disruption of the relationship. 

(e) Economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant. 

(f) Conduct that was wrongful by some legal measure other than the fact of interference Itself.  

“[W]hile intentionally interfering with an existing contract is a wrong in and of itself, intentionally interfering with a plaintiff's prospective economic advantage is not. To establish a claim for interference with prospective economic advantage, therefore, a plaintiff must plead that the defendant engaged in an independently wrongful act. An act is not independently wrongful merely because the defendant acted with an improper motive…We conclude, therefore, that an act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1158–59, internal citations omitted.)  

Here, the Court finds that California law does not require an existing contractual relationship to state a claim for interference with prospective economic advantage; rather, a probable economic relationship with the likelihood of future benefit is sufficient (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1158–59; Ixchel Pharma, LLC v. Biogen, Inc. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 1130).

Accordingly, the Court finds that the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges the elements of interference with prospective economic advantage. Cross-Complainants have pleaded probable economic relationships, Cross-Defendants’ knowledge, intentional and independently wrongful acts, actual disruption, and economic harm across the Cross-Complaint.

Thus, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to this cause of action. 

Motion to strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

(1) Punitive Damages 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)  

“‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to defendant with the intention on the part of defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3).) 

Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95.) And specific facts must be pleaded in support of punitive damages. (See Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-92.)  

Cross-Defendants seek to strike the following: the prayer for punitive damages in connection with the Fourth, Fifth, and Sixth Causes of Action for Defamation (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 76, 85, and 92) and the general prayer for punitive damages against Cross-Defendants (Cross-Complaint, Prayer, ¶ 14:28).

Cross-Defendants argue that generalized statements accusing them of "reckless disregard" and "malicious intent" are insufficient to meet the heightened pleading standard for punitive damages under Civil Code § 3294. They further claim that the allegations lack factual detail, which cannot support an award of exemplary damages (Motion, p. 7).

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that their allegations are sufficient to support claims for punitive damages at the pleading stage. They contend that the Cross-Complaint includes specific allegations showing that Cross-Defendants knowingly made false and defamatory statements with the intent to harm Cross-Complainants’ business reputation (Opposition, p. 4). Cross-Complainants further assert that these allegations demonstrate malice as defined by Civil Code § 3294(c)(1), which includes intentional conduct carried out with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights of others.

The Court finds that the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges facts to support a claim for punitive damages at the pleading stage. Cross-Complainants assert that Cross-Defendants knowingly made false and defamatory statements with the intent to harm Cross-Complainants’ professional reputation and business relationships. Specifically, the Cross-Complaint alleges that Cross-Defendants falsely accused Alan Wen of criminal conduct and disseminated these defamatory statements through emails and social media, causing harm to Cross-Complainants’ business and standing in the Asian-American financial services community (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 76, 85, 92, and Prayer, ¶ 14:28).

These allegations could establish malice under Civil Code § 3294(c)(1), as they describe intentional conduct carried out with a willful and conscious disregard for Cross-Complainants’ rights. 

Based on the foregoing, the motion to strike is DENIED in its entirety.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer to the Cross-Complaint.

            Cross-Defendants motion to strike is DENIED in whole.

            Cross-Defendants are to give notice.
