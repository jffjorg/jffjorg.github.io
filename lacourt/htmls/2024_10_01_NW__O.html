ORDER GRANTING AYKEM LLC’S MOTION TO EXPUNGE LIS PENDENS

 

ORDER GRANTING IN PART AYKEM LLC’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is an action between Plaintiffs Mikhail Siretskiy (“Mikhail”) and Marina Siretskiy (“Marina”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) and Defendants Aykem LLC (“Aykem”), Mariya Ayzenberg (“Mariya”), Mike Kemel (“Mike”), Alex Kemel (“Alex”), Vadim Bogdanov, and Vadim Gurman.

On April 26, 2024, this Court granted Aykem’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens against the property located at 5325 Cangas Drive, Agoura Hills, California 91301 (“Property”). On May 31, 2024, Mikhail recorded another notice of lis pendens against the Property. On June 28, 2024, this Court dismissed Aykem, Mariya, and Alex from this action with prejudice.

Aykem now seeks to expunge Mikhail’s second recorded lis pendens. Aykem, Mariya, and Alex also seek attorney’s fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a).[1] Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition to either.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On September 21, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud, Deceit, Misrepresentation; (3) Negligent Misrepresentation; (4) Violation of Fin. Code 22000 et seq.; (5) Quiet Title; (6) Assault; (7) Battery; (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress/Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (9) Conversion; (10) Trespass to Land; and (11) Steering to Hard Money Loan (Self-Dealing).

          On February 20, 2024, this Court sustained Aykem, Mariya, Mike, and Alex’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint.

          On April 22, 2024, this Court granted Aykem, Mariya, Mike, and Alex’s Ex Parte Application for Order Dismissing the Action with Prejudice.

         

          On April 23, 2024, Mike filed his Answer.

          On April 23, 2024, this Court denied Mikhail’s Ex Parte Application for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint.

On April 26, 2024, this Court granted Aykem’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens.

          On May 30, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint.

          On May 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging (1) Steering to Hard Money Loan (Self-Dealing); (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Wrongful Eviction; (5) Wrongful Foreclosure; (6) Rescission/Specific Performance; (7) Assault; (8) Battery; and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress/Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

          On July 12, 2024, this Court set aside its inadvertent dismissal of Mike and denied Mikhail’s Ex Parte Application for Relief from Dismissal under Code of Civil Procedure 473(b).

          On August 26, 2024, Aykem filed the instant Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens (“Motion to Expunge”).

          On August 26, 2024, Aykem, Mariya, and Alex filed the instant Motion for Attorney’s Fees (“Fees Motion”).

          Plaintiffs did not file an opposition to either the Motion to Expunge or the Fees Motion.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Expunge Lis Pendens

“[A] lis pendens is a recorded document giving constructive notice that an action has been filed affecting title or right to possession of the real property described in the notice. Its effect is that anyone acquiring an interest in the property after the action was filed will be bound by the judgment…Once a lis pendens is filed, it clouds the title and effectively prevents the property's transfer until the litigation is resolved or the lis pendens is expunged.” (BGJ Associates, LLC v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 952, 967.)

“At any time after notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party, or any nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby, may apply to the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice…Evidence or declarations may be filed with the motion to expunge the notice. The court may permit evidence to be received in the form of oral testimony, and may make any orders it deems just to provide for discovery by any party affected by a motion to expunge the notice. The claimant shall have the burden of proof under Sections 405.31 and 405.32.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 405.30.)

The court shall order the expungement if it finds the pleading on which the lis pendens was based “does not contain a real property claim,” or if it finds the party who filed the pleading “has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 405.31 & 405.32.) “The party opposing a motion to expunge a lis pendens has the burden to show that a real property claim has been alleged and has probable validity based upon a preponderance of the evidence.” (Nunn v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 346, 363.)

B.             Attorney’s Fees

“A party is entitled to an award of¿attorney¿fees¿if there is a specific authorization therefor by statute or private agreement.” (Beverly Hills Properties v. Marcolino (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d Supp. 7, 10.)  

 “With respect to attorney fees incurred to litigate a contract claim, the recovery of such fees are governed by¿Section 1717.” (Intelligent Investments Corp. v. Gonzales (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th Supp. 1, 4.) Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) provides in, pertinent part, “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that¿attorney's¿fees¿and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the¿prevailing¿party, then the party who is determined to be the¿party¿prevailing¿on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable¿attorney's¿fees¿in addition to other costs.” “California courts liberally construe the term ‘on a contract’ as used within¿section 1717. As long as the action involves a contract it is ‘on the contract’ within the meaning of¿Section 1717.” (Dell Merk Inc. v. Franzia (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 443, 455, (internal citations omitted).) 

“The court…shall determine who is the party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section, whether or not the suit proceeds to final judgment…the party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract. The court may also determine that there is no party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1717(b).)

Code of Civil Procedure § 1032(b) provides that, “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled … to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” Attorneys’ fees awarded pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1717 are allowable as costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1032 as they are attorneys’ fees authorized by contract. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B); Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(a)(10)(A).) Attorneys’ fees are therefore recoverable as an element of costs.

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens

1.               Request for Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Aykem’s Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”) Exs. O-P, the “Notice of Pending Action (Lis Pendens) recorded on September 26, 2023” and the Notice of Pending Action (Lis Pendens) recorded on May 31, 2024.”

The Court denies Aykem’s other RJNs.

2.               Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens  

“Once a notice of pending action has been expunged, the claimant may not record another notice of pending action as to the affected property without leave of the court in which the action is pending.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 405.36.) If a claimant re-records a notice of pending action without leave of court, the notice must be expunged. (See De Martini v. Superior Court (2024) 98 Cal.App.5th 1269, 1275–1278.)

On April 26, 2024, the Court granted Aykem’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens (“Order”), thereby expunging the lis pendens Mikhail had recorded against the Property on September 26, 2023. In its Order, the Court specifically stated “…pursuant to CCP § 405.36, Plaintiffs…shall not record another Notice of Pendency of Action (Lis Pendens) against the Property without affirmative leave of Court.” (Order, pg. 2.) Despite this, Mikhail recorded another notice of pending action against the Property on May 31, 2024. (See RJN Ex. P.) Mikhail did not request leave of Court to record another notice of pending action against the Property. As such, Mikhail’s May 31, 2024, notice must be expunged and the Court GRANTS Aykem’s Motion to Expunge.

Aykem requests attorney’s fees in conjunction with its Motion to Expunge. (See Motion to Expunge, pp. 11-12.) Parties prevailing on motions to expunge a lis pendens are entitled to recover reasonable attorney fees associated with making or opposing the motion, unless courts find the other parties acted with substantial justification, or other circumstances of injustice. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 405.38; Castro v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 1010, 1018.) The fee award goes only against the losing party and not counsel. (See Doyle v. Superior Court (1991) 226 Cal.App.3d 1355, 1359.)

Aykem requests attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $7,588.91, which accounts for “5 hours and 24 minutes meeting and conferring, speaking with clients, researching the law, and drafting the moving papers and declarations in support thereof. Further, I estimate spending an additional two (2) hours reviewing, researching, and replying to the Opposition, if any, and two (2) hours preparing and attending the hearing in this matter for a total of 9 hours and 24 minutes (9.4) hours at my regular rate of $800.00,” plus $68.91 in court costs. (Motion to Expunge Declaration of Gina Lisitsa filed August 26, 2024, ¶ 49.)

As the Court has previously noted, given Aykem’s counsel, Gina Lisitsa’s (“Lisitsa”), experience, the prevailing rates within the Greater Los Angeles Area, the level of complexity of the issues, and the skill required, the Court finds counsel her requested rate of $800/hour to be reasonable. However, the Court finds Aykem’s requested 9 hours and 24 minutes unreasonable, considering Lisitsa’s previous experience with this exact same motion in this case and the lack of opposition. The Court thus finds it appropriate to reduce Lisitsa’s fee award to $1,668.91, which accounts for (2) hours to draft the Motion to Expunge, (1) hour to attend the hearing, and $68.91 in court costs. Having addressed the fees Aykem incurred in connection with its Motion to Expunge here, the Court will exclude them from its analysis of Aykem’s concurrently pending Fees Motion below.

          Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Aykem’s Motion to Expunge and awards Aykem $1,668.91 in attorney’s fees and court costs.

          In connection with its Motion to Expunge, Aykem shall serve and file a proposed order that is suitable for recording. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 405.60.)

          Plaintiffs shall pay Aykem $1,668.91 in attorney’s fees and court costs on or before October 31, 2024, with interest accruing at the legal rate thereafter.

B.             Motion for Attorney’s Fees

1.               Aykem is Entitled to an Award of Reasonable Attorney’s Fees and Costs Under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a).

Aykem, Mariya, and Alex request a total of $176,560.00 in attorney’s fees and costs incurred in connection with this action and instant Motion.

As an initial matter, the Court finds Aykem, Mariya, and Alex are the prevailing parties for the purposes of their Motion. The Court also finds attorney’s fees are authorized pursuant to Paragraph 23 of the Deed of Trust (the “Deed”), which is broad, as well as Paragraph the Promissory Note dated May 26, 2021, and signed by Mikhail on behalf of Cangas RE LLC as its Managing Member on June 14, 2021 (the “Note”). (See Declaration of Gina Lisitsa dated August 26, 2024, ¶¶ 3-4, Exs. B & C.) That is because although the Note’s Attorney’s Fees provision in Paragraph 9 is limited to where suits are brought by Aykem “to compel payment of [the] Note or any portion of the indebtedness… or to defend the priority of the Deed,” it also incorporates the Deed’s broad fee provision by reference.

The Court does not find, however, that Alex or Mariya are entitled to fees as they are not signatories to the contract from which the fees arise. The Court also finds Mikhail is the only Plaintiff from which Aykem is entitled to receive attorney’s fees, as Mikhail is the only Plaintiff that is a signatory to the contract from which the fees arise. “A party is entitled to recover its attorney's fees pursuant to a contractual provision only when the party would have been liable for the fees of the opposing party if the opposing party had prevailed. Where a nonsignatory plaintiff sues a signatory defendant in an action on a contract and the signatory defendant prevails, the signatory defendant is entitled to attorney’s fees only if the nonsignatory plaintiff would have been entitled to its fees if the plaintiff had prevailed.” (Real Property Services Corporation v. The City of Pasedena (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 375, 382.) Here, as a nonsignatory, Marina would not be responsible for the attorney’s fees of any of the defendants had the Plaintiffs prevailed; likewise, Alex, and Mariya would not be responsible for the Plaintiffs’ fees had the Plaintiffs prevailed.

Accordingly, Aykem is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs under Code of Civil Procedure § 1717(a) as the prevailing party in this action as against Mikhail only.

2.       Aykem’s Fees Are Granted in Part.

a.       Lisitsa’s Rates are Reasonable.

As the Court has previously addressed above, the Court finds Lisitsa’s $800.00/hr billing rate reasonable.

b.       Not All Lisitsa’s Hours Are Reasonable.

Lisitsa declares she will have spent a total of 225.70 hours on this matter after the hearing on the Motion. (See Motion Declaration of Gina Lisitsa filed August 26, 2024 (“Lisitsa Decl.”), ¶ 18.) Lisitsa declares generated $176,560.00 in fees for her services, plus $5,453.19 in costs; Lisitsa requests this Court award $176,560.00 in attorney’s fees for 220.7 hours of work. (See Lisitsa Decl., ¶ 21, Ex. C.)

First, the Court has already addressed fees and costs Aykem incurred in connection with both of its Motions to Expunge Lis Pendens. In its April 22, 2024, Minute Order addressing Aykem’s first Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens, the Court accounted for 32 hours of Aykem’s counsel’s time and $68.91 in costs. Then, earlier in this Order, the Court addressed the 9.4 hours and an additional $68.91 in costs Aykem sought for bringing the Motion to Expunge. By including the time for both of those motions in their Fee Motion, Aykem is impermissibly seeking double recovery of that time.

Next, the Court identified one entry on April 23, 2024, for researching and drafting an answer for 2.2 hours that should not be included here because it relates to a party who has not yet prevailed and even then, arguably is not entitled to fees because he is not a party to either the Grant Deed or Note.

Third, Aykem seeks 12 hours in connection with time anticipated to be spent on reviewing Mikhail’s opposition to the Fee Motion, drafting a reply, and appearing at the hearing. Since no opposition has been filed and the Court anticipates handling the matter quickly at the time of hearing, the Court is reducing that time to 1.0 hour.

V.       CONCLUSION

          Plaintiffs shall pay Aykem $1,668.91 in attorney’s fees and court costs on or before October 31, 2024.  

The Court also GRANTS Aykem’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees in the amount of $121,440 and costs of $5,158.11 as against Mikhail only.

Accordingly, the Court orders Aykem to prepare and submit an Amended Judgment that reflects these additional fees and costs.
