[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTIONS TO QUASH SUBPOENAS; CONTINUING HEARING ON MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

February 27, 2024

 

On July 9, 2020, Plaintiff Hilda Rodriguez-Guzman filed this action against Defendants The Accelerated Schools and Johnathan Williams.

Pending before the Court are two discovery motions.

MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENAS

On November 7, 2023, Defendants served two subpoenas on Kaiser Permanente for Plaintiff’s medical records (Department of Psychiatry) and billing records.

On December 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to quash the subpoenas or for a protective order to limit the subpoenas.  Plaintiff argues that the requests violate her right to privacy and records prior to 2016 are not relevant.

“‘[F]or discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement’ and ‘[a]dmissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.’  These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery . . . and (contrary to popular belief) fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases.”  (Cruz v. Superior Court (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 646, 653, citations omitted.)  The Court must “balance the public need against the weight of the privacy right,” and only serious invasions of privacy will bar discovery.  (Crab Addison, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 958, 966.)  There is not an egregious invasion of privacy every time there is a request for private information, and courts must “place the burden on the party asserting a privacy interest to establish its extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.)

Plaintiff alleges that she suffers “severe and permanent emotional and mental distress and anguish, humiliation, embarrassment, fright, shock, pain, discomfort and anxiety” due to Defendants’ actions.  (Complaint ¶ 81; see Complaint ¶¶ 105, 134, 164, 208, 238, 270, 278, 301, 305-306, 308, 331, 378, 397.)  There is no privilege for communications relevant to a patient’s health if that issue has been tendered by the patient.  (Slagle v. Superior Court (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1309, 1313.)  “A patient tenders the issue of his physical health if he files an action for personal injuries but only as to information which relates to the claimed injuries.”  (Ibid.)  As Defendants set forth, Plaintiff’s discovery responses and deposition also asserted physical, mental, and emotional injuries.  (Opposition at pp. 3-4.)  Accordingly, Plaintiff has put her physical and mental health at issue for these injuries, and Defendants are entitled to her medical records as they relate to these injuries.  As to Plaintiff’s request to limit the time period of the record, Defendants’ request for records since 2012 is reasonable and relevant because Plaintiff was employed by Defendants since September 2007, became a Health Service Coordinator in 2012, and was terminated in July 2019.  (Complaint ¶¶ 16, 41.)

The motion to quash is DENIED with respect to the medical records.

For the billing records, Defendants are willing to withdraw this request on the condition that Plaintiff is not seeking those damages.  (Opposition at p. 5.)  Plaintiff has not responded to this offer.  At the hearing, Plaintiff should inform the Court and Defendants if she is willing to stipulate to this condition.  Otherwise, the motion is DENIED with respect to the billing records because Plaintiff alleges damages that include “medical and related expenses for care and procedures both now and in the future.”  (Complaint ¶ 80; see, e.g., Complaint ¶¶ 104, 133, 163, 205, 237, 269, 307, 309 [“Plaintiff has been forced to incur expenses for medical care, X-rays, and laboratory costs, and is informed and believe, and thereon allege, that Plaintiff will in the future be forced to incur additional expenses of the same nature.”].)

The motion to quash is DENIED.

Kaiser Permanente is ordered to produce the requested records pursuant to the subpoena within 60 days.

The request for sanctions is denied.  Defendants do have a legitimate discovery interest in Plaintiff’s medical records related to her claimed injuries.  Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant was substantially justified in opposing the motion.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090, subd. (d).)

Defendants’ request for sanctions is also denied.

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

Plaintiffs WC Insurance Agency LLC, Omni Safe Insurance Agency LLC, and Joshua Sawicki served various discovery requests on Defendants James S. Bekhor Insurance Services Inc. (“JSB Insurance”) and JSB Holdings LLC (“JSB Holdings”).  (See Mankey Decl. ¶ 6 & Exs. 2, 5, 6, 9, 10, 12, 13, 15, 16, 18.)  The parties granted each other extensions to respond and to file discovery motions.  (See Mankey Decl. ¶¶ 7-8, 15; id., Ex. 25 at pp. 4-5.)

On December 1, 2023, Plaintiff Joshua Sawicki filed a motion to compel further responses to discovery and for sanctions of at least $18,860.00.

On January 16, 2023, Defendants filed an objection to the motion.

At the January 25, 2024 hearing, the Court continued the hearing and ordered the parties to meet and confer and file a joint statement for the requests to compel production of documents for deposition.

A.        The Motion’s Memorandum Complies with This Department’s Procedures.

Defendants object to Plaintiff’s failure to serve the memorandum of points and authorities until January 12, 2024.  (Objections at p. 2.)

A party moving to compel further responses must file a Separate Statement or follow the Court’s alternative method of outlining the disputes.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (b), 2031.310, subd. (b); California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b).)  This Department requires the parties to meet and confer on the substantive issues before filing a joint statement, following the procedures outlined in Exhibit A of Department 48’s Courtroom Information (available on the court’s website).  This Department also specifies: “After the Joint Stipulation is filed, each party may file a supplemental memorandum of law not later than ten days before the hearing date.  A supplemental memorandum may not exceed five pages in length.  No other separate memorandum of points and authorities may be filed by either party in connection with the motion.”

Plaintiff’s motion complies with these procedures.  However, for the reasons set forth below, that is the extent of the motion’s compliance.

B.        The Motion Does Not Comply with Electronic Filing Requirements.

Defendants object to the “improper, confusing, difficult to navigate” form of the motion, which “seeks to compel further responses to up to approximately 16 different discovery requests, which are not electronically bookmarked, as required.”  (Objections at p. 2.)

Under the Court’s First Amended General Order for electronic filing, the table of contents and all attachments, including exhibits, must be bookmarked.  (General Order No. 2019-GEN-014-00, at ¶¶ 6(b)-(d); California Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(f)(4).)

Plaintiff’s motion contains no bookmarks, which is particularly troublesome for a 168-page Joint Statement with 11 different sections.  If Plaintiff continues to electronically file noncompliant documents, the Court may strike the filings or issue sanctions.

C.        Plaintiff Did Not Provide Proper Notice of His Request for Sanctions.

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.)

The Notice of Motion states that “Plaintiff Joshua Sawicki will, and hereby does, move this Court for an [order] to compel Defendants’ Further Responses To Discovery and Production of Documents and for Sanctions.”  Plaintiff did not identify the specific parties against whom sanctions are sought, the type of sanction sought, or the amount of monetary sanctions.

It was not until Plaintiff’s January 12, 2024 supplemental memorandum’s Introduction section that Plaintiff requested that Defendants “pay monetary sanctions of at least $18,860.00” and “be prohibited from introducing or entering into evidence in this case any documents that Defendants themselves have not produced to date and testimony or other evidence of the subject matters of the interrogatories to which they did not provide timely substantive responses.”  Defendants did not have notice of the type or amount of sanctions before that.

At the original hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel argued that sanctions are nevertheless mandatory because the governing statutes state that the Court “shall impose” a monetary sanction against the unsuccessful party.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (j) [depositions]; 2030.300, subd. (d) [interrogatories]; 2031.310, subd. (h) [production of documents].)]

The same statutes also provide an exception to mandatory monetary sanctions: when the Court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

Here, Defendants were substantially justified in opposing Plaintiff’s motion.  Additionally, the Court denies the majority of Plaintiff’s motion, so sanctions against Defendants would be unjust.

D.        The Motion is Brought By Only One Plaintiff, But It Seeks Discovery Responses to Requests Propounded by Other Plaintiffs.

The Notice of Motion, Joint Statement, and supporting memorandum state that they are being brought by only Plaintiff Joshua Sawicki.  However, some of the requests were propounded by a different defendant.  (See Mankey Decl. ¶ 6 & Exs. 2, 5, 6, 9, 10, 12, 13, 15, 16, 18.)

Plaintiff WC Insurance Agency, LLC d/b/a BMP Insurance propounded (1) Special Interrogatories to Defendant James S. Bekhor Insurance Services, Inc. D/B/A Active Insurance, Set One; (2) Special Interrogatories to Defendant JSB Holdings, LLC, Set One; (3) Requests for Production of Documents for James S. Bekhor Insurance Services, Inc. D/B/A Active Insurance, Set One; (4) Requests for Production of Documents for JSB Holdings, LLC, Set One; (5) Requests for Admission to James S. Bekhor Insurance Services, Inc. D/B/A Active Insurance, Set One; and (6) Requests for Admission to JSB Holdings, LLC, Set One.  (Mankey Decl., Exs. 2, 6, 10, 13, 16, 18.)

Plaintiff Joshua Sawicki cannot compel further responses to these requests.

E.        The Motion is Untimely For Most Requests and Must Be Denied In Part.

In the absence of a written agreement between the parties, a motion to compel further responses to RFPs, interrogatories, and RFAs must be filed within 45 days after the insufficient response.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c), 2031.310, subd. (c), 2033.290, subd. (c).)  This deadline is jurisdictional and “renders the court without authority to rule on motions to compel other than to deny them.”  (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409-1410.)

On October 18, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendants’ counsel to confirm the parties’ current deadlines and proposed new deadlines.  (Mankey Decl., Ex. 26 at pp. 1-2.)  Included in the chart was a timeline for meeting and conferring about the discovery issues, Defendants’ additions to the Joint Statement, and Plaintiff’s deadline to file a motion to compel further responses.  Plaintiff stated a current deadline of October 24, 2023 for “File and serve Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses and Production of Documents,” noted that the “Current deadline is pursuant to CCP 2030.300(c) and can be extended,” and proposed a new deadline of November 6, 2023.

On October 20, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendants’ counsel to confirm their phone discussion the day before.  (Mankey Decl., Ex. 26 at p. 1.)  Plaintiff’s counsel confirmed: “Your clients agree to provide substantive responses to discovery and responsive documents by November 6, 2023, in order to eliminate the need for a discovery motion (which is currently scheduled for hearing on Nov. 28, 2023).”  The remainder of the email confirmed the dates for depositions and Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication.

Plaintiff did not file a motion on November 6, 2023, and there is no evidence of a later written agreement between the parties to extend the filing deadline.  At the original hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel argued that by extending Defendants’ time to respond, that also extended the deadline to file a motion.  But November 6, 2023 was the last written extension for filing a motion.  Although Plaintiff granted Defendants additional time to respond before requiring a motion, Defendants did not confirm any additional time for filing that motion.

On November 16, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel informed Defendants’ counsel that “we will be filing the attached motion on Monday, November 20, given the continued failure of the Defendants to satisfy their discovery obligations despite our efforts to meet and confer,” and requested any of Defendants’ arguments for the Joint Statement by the end of November 17.  (Mankey Decl., Ex. 25 at pp. 1-2.)  Defendants’ counsel replied that he was out of town and would get back to her on Sunday.  Plaintiff’s counsel replied that she would “wait until December 1 to file the motion to compel and will include your arguments if you get them to me by noon on November 30.”  There is no evidence of a response or agreement by Defendants to extend the filing deadline to December 1, 2023.

At the original hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel argued that when a party does not respond at all, there is no 45-day deadline to move to compel responses.  That is correct.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2031.320.)  However, even when Defendants provided only objections, those objections are a complete response.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.240, 2031.240.)  This argument is also perplexing in light of Plaintiff’s exhibits and presentation of the motion.  Counsel’s October 18, 2023 email made clear that the motion deadline was “pursuant to CCP 2030.300(c) and can be extended.”  (Mankey Decl., Ex. 26 at pp. 1-2.)  The motion is captioned “NOTICE OF MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANTS’ FURTHER RESPONSES . . .”  The Joint Statement includes Defendants’ original responses and “PLAINTIFF’S REASONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSE.”  In all respects, this motion was presented as—and is—a motion to compel further responses, and it is subject to the 45-day filing deadline.

This motion was filed on December 1, 2023—long after Defendants’ 2022 responses, and after the parties’ last written and agreed-upon motion deadline of November 6, 2023.  This Court has no jurisdiction to rule on the merits of the motion.

The motion is DENIED for all non-deposition discovery.

F.         Defendants Must Produce the Documents Requested in the Amended Deposition Notices.

At the original hearing, Plaintiff’s counsel argued that the request to compel production of documents for deposition was timely.  The depositions of each Defendant’s PMK occurred on November 21, 2023.  (Mankey Decl. ¶ 14 & Exs. 27-28.)  The motion is therefore timely with respect to the depositions.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (b).)  The Court therefore ordered the parties to file a joint statement for these items after meeting and conferring.  Defendants did not respond to Plaintiff’s attempts to meet and confer, and they did not provide arguments for the joint statement.

Plaintiff seeks an order requiring production of documents and permitting Plaintiff to reopen the depositions of Jeremy Bekhor and Defendants’ PMKs after substantive responses and documents have been produced.  (First Joint Statement at p. 154.)

1.         The Motion is Denied for the Deposition of Jeremy Bekhor Individually.

Jeremy Bekhor, individually, is no longer a party to this action and was not served with notice of this motion.  Neither counsel’s declaration nor the Joint Statement provide any information about his deposition notice or subpoena.  (See Mankey Decl. ¶ 14, First Joint Statement at p. 154.)  The motion is DENIED as to Jeremy Bekhor individually.

2.         Defendants Must Produce the Requested Documents.

Plaintiff took the deposition of Defendants’ PMK on November 21, 2023.  (Mankey Suppl. Decl. ¶ 4.)  Defendants did not serve any written objections or responses to either of the Amended Deposition Notices, and they did not produce any documents.  (Mankey Suppl. Decl. ¶ 4.)

Defendants’ January 16, 2024 objection to the motion did not substantively respond to any discovery requests.  After the original hearing, the parties met and conferred, and Defendants indicated that they were willing to produce documents.  (Mankey Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 8-11.)  Defendants did not provide any objections to Plaintiff before the joint statement was due, and they have not provided any documents or argument for the joint statement.  (Mankey Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 11-12.)

When a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.)   A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

The motion is GRANTED for the documents requested in the Amended Deposition Notices.  Defendants must produce responses, without objections, within 30 days.

On November 9, 2021, Plaintiffs Carina Herrera Garrett and Randy T. Garrett filed this action against Defendant General Motors LLC arising from Plaintiffs’ purchase of an allegedly defective vehicle.

On June 22, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a motion to compel further responses.

MOTION DEFICIENCIES

For a motion to compel further, Plaintiffs must meet and confer with Defendant and file a Separate Statement or follow the Court’s alternative method of outlining the disputes.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b); California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b).)  This Department requires the parties to follow the procedures outlined in Exhibit A of Department 48’s Courtroom Information (available on the Court’s website) and file a joint statement.

The parties did not follow Department 48’s procedures for a joint statement.

DISCUSSION

A party may move to compel a further response to a demand for production of documents if the demanding party deems that the statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; the representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)  The motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).)

Plaintiffs seek further responses to six categories of documents: (1) the subject vehicle (RFP Nos. 1, 7); (2) Defendant’s knowledge and investigation of defects in similar vehicles (RFP Nos. 20-27, 32, 41, 44-51, 56, 65); (3) defects in similar vehicles (RFP Nos. 87, 100); (4) Defendant’s Song-Beverly and buyback policies and procedures (RFP Nos. 110, 111); (5) communications with third parties about defects in similar vehicles (RFP Nos. 125, 127, 128, 129, 131-135); and (6) other documents about the subject vehicle’s defects (RFP Nos. 139, 142, 143).

RFP Nos. 1, 7 requests documents relating to the subject vehicle.  Defendant stated that it would “comply in part” and produce selected documents.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP Nos. 20-27, 32, 41, 44-51, 56, 65 seek Defendant’s knowledge and investigation of the same brake defects in the same make of vehicle.  Defendant responded that no documents would be produced.  These requests are limited to the same defects and vehicle alleged by Plaintiffs, and they are relevant to Defendant’s knowledge and willfulness.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP Nos. 87, 100 seek executive summaries, summaries, reports, analyses, evaluations, or memoranda about problems with the same brake system and engine.  Defendant responded that no documents would be produced.  These requests are limited to the same defects and vehicle alleged by Plaintiffs, and they are relevant to Defendant’s knowledge and willfulness.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP Nos. 110, 111 seek training materials for handling lemon law repurchase requests and manuals provided to authorized repair facilities.  Defendant responded that no documents would be produced.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP Nos. 125, 127, 128, 129, 131-135 seek communications between Defendant and any government agency or entity regarding defects in the same brake system and engine, all Early Warning Reports submitted to NHTSA and all complaints about the same brake system and engine, and all TREAD submitted about the same brake system and engine.  Defendant responded that no documents would be produced.  These requests are relevant to Defendant’s knowledge and willfulness.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP Nos. 139, 142 seek documents reflecting performance standards relating to the same brake system and engine.  These requests are limited to the same defects and vehicle alleged by Plaintiffs, and they are relevant to Defendant’s knowledge and willfulness.  Defendant’s objections are overruled.  The motion is GRANTED.

RFP No. 143 seeks all documents including electronically stored information and electronic mails, concerning Technical Service Bulletin 19-NA-032.  Defendant agreed to produce a copy of the bulletin.  The request for all documents about the bulletin is overbroad.  The motion is DENIED.

CONCLUSION

The motion is GRANTED IN PART as set forth above.  Defendant is ordered to provide verified, supplemental responses within 10 days.

The requests for sanctions are DENIED.
