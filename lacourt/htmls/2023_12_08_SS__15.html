DEPARTMENT 15 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23GDCV00516    Hearing Date: December 8, 2023    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

            On September 25, 2023, Defendant Autozone West LLC filed and served a motion for summary adjudication with a hearing date of December 8, 2023.  Plaintiffs Lorenzo Ruiz and Linda Ruiz filed objections to the motion arguing it is untimely.

            Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (a) requires a party filing a motion for summary adjudication to give “at least” 75 days’ notice.  The August 15, 2023 trial setting order in this case set December 8, 2023 as the deadline for hearing motions for summary judgment and set trial on December 11, 2023.  (Trial Setting Order, ¶¶ 2, 26.)  (Because the court is dark on December 11, 2023, the trial date was recently continued to December 12, 2023.)  While the trial setting order allows the parties to stipulate to shorten the time for summary adjudication motions (ibid.), Plaintiffs and Defendant did not stipulate to shorten the time on Defendant’s motion.

            Plaintiffs argue that 75 days before December 8 was September 24, a Sunday.  (Objections at p. 3.)  To ensure that Plaintiffs had at least 75 days’ notice, Defendant needed to serve Plaintiffs by September 22.  By serving Plaintiffs on September 25, Defendants gave Plaintiffs only 74 days’ notice.  Also, Defendant accomplished service on September 25 at 6:08 p.m. (Rosenthal Decl., Ex. 3 at p. 1), but the trial setting order requires electronic service by 5 p.m. for electronic service to be equivalent to personal service on that day.  (Trial Setting Order, ¶ 7.)  Therefore Plaintiffs were technically served on September 26.

            Defendant argues Plaintiffs failed to meet and confer on a stipulation to shorten the notice period even though Plaintiffs gave other defendants shorter notice periods.  However, Defendant cites no law obligating Plaintiffs to consider giving Defendant a shorter notice period.  Further, Defendant states it did not ask for a shorter notice period until September 25, 2023, when the deadline to file the motion had already passed.  (Reply at p. 2.)

            Defendant suggests the court has the authority to shorten the 75-day period, citing Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690.  In that case, the plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion for summary judgment on the merits and thereby waived any alleged defect in the service of the motion.  (Carlton, supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at p. 697.)  Here Plaintiffs did not file an opposition on the merits; they only filed objections to the motion based on untimely service.  Further, the law is clear that “trial courts do not have the authority to shorten the minimum notice period for summary judgment hearings.”  (McMahon v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 112, 118.)

            Defendants argue that because Plaintiffs have violated the trial setting order, Defendant does not need to comply with the 75-day notice requirement.  (Reply at p. 5.)  Defendant cites no law that because Plaintiffs served late deposition notices, Defendant can give less than 75 days’ notice.

            Finally, Defendant contends the court can move the hearing date to late December to give Plaintiffs time to file an opposition.  (Reply at p. 6.)  This is not possible for at least two reasons.  First, trial is on December 12, 2023, and this is a preference case.  Defendant does not cite any law that a preference trial date can be continued because a defendant failed to give adequate notice on a summary adjudication motion.  Second, when a court continues a summary adjudication motion hearing date so that the moving party can give 75 days’ notice, the notice period must “begin anew.”  (Robinson v. Woods (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1268.)  The court cannot merely continue the hearing date a few days to account for the few days missing from the notice period.  Rather, it must continue the hearing date 75 days or take the motion off calendar.  (Ibid.) 

            The motion was served late.  The court cannot start the 75-day notice period all over again because that would require continuing the trial date for two and a half months, which the court cannot do in a preference case.  Therefore, the court takes the motion OFF CALENDAR.

            The moving party is to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION TO QUASH (COSMETIC)

Plaintiff Maria Lozano filed this action alleging she developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure to asbestos in Defendant Cosmetic Specialties, Inc. products.  Defendant filed a motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction.  The court continued the hearing on the motion several months to allow jurisdictional discovery.

A defendant may move to quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over the defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).)  The court may dismiss without prejudice the complaint in whole, or as to that defendant, when dismissal is made pursuant to Section 418.10.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 581, subd. (h).)  “A court of this state may exercise jurisdiction on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.)  “The Due Process Clause protects an individual’s liberty interest in not being subject to the binding judgments of a forum with which he has established no meaningful ‘contacts, ties, or relations.’”  (Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 471-472.)  A state court may not exercise personal jurisdiction over a party under circumstances that would offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.”  (Asahi Metal Industry Co., Ltd., v. Superior Court of California, Solano County (1987) 480 U.S. 102, 113.) 

When a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.  (Jayone Foods, Inc. v. Aekyung Industrial Co. Ltd. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 543, 553.)  Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, the defendant has the burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.  (Ibid.)  “The plaintiff must provide specific evidentiary facts, through affidavits and other authenticated documents, sufficient to allow the court to independently conclude whether jurisdiction is appropriate.  [Citation.]  The plaintiff cannot rely on allegations in an unverified complaint or vague and conclusory assertions of ultimate facts.  [Citation.]”  (Strasner v. Touchstone Wireless Repair & Logistics, LP (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 215, 222.)

A defendant is subject to a state’s general jurisdiction if its contacts “are so continuance and systematic as to render [it] essentially at home in the forum State.”  (Saimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 127.)  A nonresident defendant may be subject to the specific jurisdiction of the forum “if the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits [citation], and the ‘controversy is related to or “arises out of” a defendant’s contacts with the forum.’  [Citations.]”  (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446.)  This test does not require a “causal relationship between the defendant’s in-state activity and the litigation.”  (Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Judicial District Court (2021) 141 S.Ct. 1017, 1026.)  The “arise out” of standard “asks about causation,” but “relate to” does not.  (Ibid.)  “[W]hen a corporation has ‘continuously and deliberately exploited [a State’s] market, it must reasonably anticipate being haled into [that State’s] court[s]’ to defend actions ‘based on’ products causing injury there.”  (Id. at p. 1027.) 

Defendant provided evidence that it is incorporated in New Jersey and has its principal place of business in New Jersey.  (Motion at p. 4; Katerndahl Decl., Exs. C, D.)  This evidence establishes California has no general jurisdiction over Defendant, and Plaintiff does not argue otherwise.

In the original motion, Defendant argued it had not done business in California related to Plaintiff’s claims.  (Motion at p. 10.)  Plaintiff contended, without much evidence, that Defendant had supplied talc to Mary Kay, Revlon, Max Factor, and Johnson & Johnson, and then Plaintiff used their products.  (Opposition at pp. 1, 2.) 

In advance of the continued hearing date, the parties filed supplemental briefs.  Plaintiff says she needed more time because depositions scheduled for December 5 and 21, 2023 will be relevant to Defendant.  Therefore, the hearing is CONTINUED to January 24, 2023 at 9 a.m.  The parties may file supplemental opposition and reply briefs according to the statutory schedule.  The parties should expect no further continuances because the hearing has now been continued more than four and a half months.

            The moving party is to give notice.

 [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION TO COMPEL (IMI FABI, LLC)



Plaintiff Maria Loznao
filed this action alleging she developed mesothelioma as a result of exposure
to asbestos in products from Defendants IMI Fabi (USA), Inc., IMI Fabi, LLC,
and IMI Fabi (Diana), LLC.  Defendants filed
motions to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction.  The court continued the hearing on those
motions to allow jurisdictional discovery. 
Specifically, the court stated: 
“Plaintiff first needs to disclose to Defendants the specific products
Plaintiff alleges caused her injury and that contained Defendants’ talc.  Plaintiff then may conduct jurisdictional
discovery concerning Defendants’ activities in and contacts with California and
its sale of talc for those particular products during the time Plaintiff was
using those products.” 



Plaintiff filed a motion
to compel further responses to requests for production of documents from IMI
Fabi, LLC in connection with its jurisdictional discovery.  But Plaintiff failed to file a separate
statement.  If Plaintiff fails in that
regard in the future, the court will deny the motion on that ground alone.  Also, because Plaintiff did not file a
separate statement and did not address each request separately in its motion,
the court cannot determine the relevance of many of the requests, as discussed
below.



Also, Plaintiff does not
show where she identified the specific products she alleges caused her
injury.  For example, the motion states Plaintiff
“alleges that IMI Fabi, LLC supplied asbestos-containing talc to manufactures
of products that [Plaintiff] used (Mary Kay, Revlon, and Max Factor).”  (Opposition at p. 2.)  But the motion does not specific the
particular Mary Kay products, the particular Revlon products, and the
particular Max Factor products she used. 
These companies sell many different types of cosmetic and personal care products.



Regarding the specific
discovery requests, the court rules as follows:



No. 1:  Denied. 
This request seeks all documents that include the word “California.”  This request is overbroad and seeks documents
not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  First, many documents could mention
California.  For example, if Defendant’s employees
send emails about their vacation plans, they could refer to going to
California.  Second, the request is not
limited to the time period when Plaintiff was using the specific products she
contends contain Defendant’s talc.  If
Defendant engaged in business in California decades before Plaintiff started
using those products, documents about that business would be irrelevant.  Third, the request requires Defendant to
search the files of dozens of entities and people who are not parties in this
case.  Plaintiff has not shown Defendant
has any control over those other entities, or that those other entities (many
of which appear to be located in foreign countries) have any connections to California.  Also, the request would require Defendant to
search the files of former owners, former employees, etc.  Defendant has no right to access former
employees’ and former owners’ records or the records of individuals who are not
parties in this case or current employees of Defendant.



No. 2:  Denied. 
This request seeks all documents referring to Proposition 65.  This is overbroad and seeks documents not
reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence for the
reasons discussed above.  In particular, Defendant
could have documents about Proposition 65 that are not related to the cosmetic
and talcum powder grade talc at issue here.



Nos. 3, 5, 6:  Denied. 
These requests seek all documents referring to Revlon, Coty, Mary Kay.  They are overbroad and seeks documents not
reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence for the
reasons discussed above.  For example, documents
discussing Revlon’s manufacturing and sales in foreign countries are not
relevant and would have nothing to do with Plaintiff’s use of Revlon
products. 



No. 4:  Denied. 
This request seeks all documents referring to Cosmetic Specialties.  It overbroad and seeks documents not
reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence for the
reasons discussed above.



No. 7:  Denied. 
This request seeks all documents referring to Procter & Gamble.  It overbroad and seeks documents not
reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence for the
reasons discussed above.  Procter &
Gamble makes and sells many, many different kinds of products throughout the country
and the world that have no connection to this case. 



Nos. 8, 9, 10, 11, 12:  Denied. 
These requests seek all documents referring to Barretts Minerals, Cyprus,
Imerys, Charles B. Chrystal, and Color Techniques.  They are overbroad and seek documents not
reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence for the
reasons discussed above.  Also, Plaintiff
does not explain what Barretts, Cyprus, Imerys, Charles B. Chrystal, and Color
Technique have to do with Defendant, Defendant’s connections to California
during the relevant time period, or the specific disputed products Plaintiff
claims exposed her to asbestos.



No. 13:  Denied. 
This request seeks all MSDS sheets for talc that refer to
California.  This request is not limited
to the relevant time period.



If Plaintiff wants
jurisdictional discovery, she needs to serve reasonably tailored requests about
Defendant’s activities in and contacts with California during the time
Plaintiff was using the products that allegedly contained Defendant’s talc and
about Defendant’s sale of talc for the particular disputed products Plaintiff
used during the period Plaintiff was using the talc.  The discovery is to be limited to Defendant,
not third parties and entities and people over which and whom Defendant has no
control or which have no dealings with California and the disputed products
Plaintiff used.



The motion is DENIED.



            The
moving parties are to give notice.


Case Number: ¿23STCV12358    Hearing Date: December 8, 2023    Dept: 15

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE MOTION FOR PREFERENCE

On June 1, 2023, Plaintiff Vito Mirabile filed an action for personal injury caused by asbestos exposure.  On November 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion for trial preference under Code of Civil Procedure section 36, subdivisions (a) and (e).

A party who is over 70 years old may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if it makes both of the following findings: (1) the party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole; and (2) the health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).)  An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under this subdivision may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.  (Id., § 36.5.)  Moreover, the court in its discretion may grant a motion for preference supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.  (Id., § 36, subd. (e).) 

“Upon the granting of such a motion for preference, the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party’s attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record.” (Id., § 36, subd. (f).)  “Any continuance shall be for no more than 15 days and no more than one continuance for physical disability may be granted to any party.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff Vito Mirabile is 75 years old and was diagnosed with malignant pleural mesothelioma.  (Fuchs Decl., ¶¶ 3-4; Fuchs Decl., Ex. A.)  Vito Mirabile has had a significant decrease in his physical and mental stamina, is not able to engage in regular daily activities such as walking and focusing, and has increased trouble breathing and difficulty sustaining focused communications.  (Fuchs Decl., ¶ 5.)  He suffers from congestive heart failure, a severely elevated PSA deemed “probable prostate cancer,” and limited mobility due to knee injury.  (Fuchs Decl., ¶¶ 7-9; Fuchs Decl., Ex. C; Fuchs Decl., Ex. D; Fuchs Decl., Ex. E.)  He takes numerous medications.  (Fuchs Decl., ¶ 11.)  His condition is declining at an increasing pace.  (Fuchs Decl., ¶ 12.)

Defendants argue the medical records are stale and unreliable (Pneumo Abex Opposition, pp. 2-3) and Ms. Fuchs’ declaration contradicts the medical records.  (Morse Tec’s Opposition at p. 6.) 

The court finds that Vito Mirabile has a substantial interest in the action as a whole. 

The May 26, 2023 medical records state Plaintiff “denies any symptoms related to the mesothelioma,” has no pain or respiratory symptoms, is eating well, is gaining weight, had just gotten married, was walking without difficulty, and was cheerful.  (Ex. B at p. MIRABILE000239.)  The August 7, 2023 medical records state he is “doing well overall and denies any symptoms related to the mesothelioma,” has no pain or respiratory symptoms, is eating well and gaining weight, had recently traveled to Italy, had normal breathing and mental status, and was cheerful.  (Ex. D at p. MIRABILE000409.)  However, Plaintiff’s November 6, 2023 declaration states his condition is getting worse.  He cannot walk far without getting very tired, cannot maintain his property, is depressed, and has other serious health problems.  (Mirabile Decl., ¶¶ 5, 10, 11.)

While medical records indicate Plaintiff was doing well until the last few months, Plaintiff’s declaration describes his physical and mental decline since then.  The most recent evidence supports the conclusion that preference is necessary to allow Plaintiff to participate in this litigation and trial while he is still physically and mentally able and to prevent prejudicing his interest in the litigation.

Plaintiffs’ motion for trial setting preference is GRANTED.  The parties are to meet and confer on a trial setting order.  The trial is scheduled for April 1, 2024 at 9 a.m. The final status conference is scheduled for March 18, 2024 at 9 a.m. A status conference regarding the trial setting order is set for December 12, 2023 at 9:00 a.m.

The moving party is to order to give notice.
