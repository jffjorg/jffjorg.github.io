DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV42104    Hearing Date: August 6, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     August 6, 2024                       VERDICT: May 30, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Ozelia Harris v. Pomona Unified School District, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV42104           

 

MOTION FOR PERMANENT INJUNCTION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Ozelia Harris

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendants Pomona Unified School District and Rebecca Norwood

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for employment discrimination that was filed on November 3, 2020. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants engaged in retaliation and harassment against her on the basis of her use of medical leave and/or her perceived disability.

 

Plaintiff moves for a permanent injunction.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for a Permanent Injunction is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

            Plaintiff moves for a permanent injunction compelling Defendant Pomona Unified School District to expunge Defendant Norwood’s October 6, 2020 written warning from Plaintiff’s personnel file.

 

Legal Basis for Permanent Injunction

 

Permanent injunctions are governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 526, which authorizes such relief, as relevant here:

 

(1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually;

 

. . .

 

(4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief.

 

(5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., §526(a).)

 

Outcome of Jury Trial

 

            On May 15, 2024, the Court commenced a jury trial in this action. (May 15, 2024.) On May 30, 2024, the jury determined that, while Plaintiff had engaged in protected activity in taking medical leave and submitting a Uniform Complaint against Defendant Norwood, Defendant Norwood did not engage in conduct that materially and adversely affected the terms and conditions of Plaintiff’s employment. (Special Verdict Nos. 1-2.) The jury also concluded that Plaintiff was not subjected to harassing conduct based on a perceived disability. (No. 6.) Upon entry of this conclusive verdict in Defendants’ favor, the Court heard from Plaintiff’s counsel regarding an order to rescind the letter of warning that is the subject of this motion and ordered that any such motion was to be served and filed on or before June 14, 2024. (May 30, 2024 Minute Order.)

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff seeks a permanent injunction compelling Defendant Pomona Unified School District to expunge Defendant Norwood’s October 6, 2020 written warning from Plaintiff’s personnel file.

 

            Plaintiff originally submitted this motion as a two-page notice of motion without supporting papers. (See Defendant’s Exh. 1. [June 14, 2024 Notice of Motion].) None of the supporting papers were included in that original filing. A motion must include or be simultaneously accompanied by a memorandum of points and authorities and any supporting materials. (See Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.112.) Plaintiff served and filed an Amended Motion which corrected this deficiency on July 16, 2024. According to Plaintiff’s counsel, the remainder of the materials were not served or filed “due to an error,” which was not discovered until July 10, 2024. (Declaration of Monique Harris ISO Amended Motion ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel does not explain why this “error” went uncorrected for a full month. Further, Plaintiff’s Memorandum of Points and Authorities is 38 pages in length, discounting the tables of contents, authorities, and exhibits. The maximum length for a memorandum of points and authorities other than on a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is 15 pages. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1113(d).) Although a party may seek leave to file an overlength brief, Plaintiff did not do so. (See Rule 3.1113(e).) Unauthorized overlength briefs are to be considered in the same manner as a late-filed paper, and the Court may, in its discretion, refuse to consider late-filed papers. (Rules 3.1113(g); 3.1300(d).) The Court would therefore be within its authority to refuse to consider the motion in its entirety, or to refuse to consider any portion of the motion beyond page 24.

 

            Moreover, even if the Court were to overlook the procedural violations, Plaintiff’s motion entirely lacks merit. Plaintiff asserts that she is entitled to injunctive relief because the written warning constituted an adverse employment action and harassment. As Defendants observe in opposition, the jury determined otherwise on May 30, 2024. (See Special Verdict Nos. 2, 6.) That verdict may not be challenged in this Court except by a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict or a motion for new trial—neither of which have been brought. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 657, 659.) Although Plaintiff cites Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, to argue that she may seek injunctive relief notwithstanding the jury’s verdict, that opinion offers no support. Harris v. City of Santa Monica states that when a plaintiff establishes that an adverse employment action was motivated by discrimination, but an employer demonstrates that the same decision would have been made regardless, that showing does not preclude injunctive relief. (Harris v. City of Santa Monica, supra, 56 Cal.4th at 241.) Here, however, Plaintiff did not demonstrate an adverse employment action occurred, nor that she was subjected to harassment.

 

            In sum, Plaintiff has not shown she is entitled to injunctive relief because the jury’s verdict conclusively establishes that she was not subjected to an adverse employment action or harassment. That verdict cannot be challenged by a motion of this nature.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for a Permanent Injunction is DENIED.

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Shah’s Motion for Summary Judgment

 

Defendant Shilpa Shah moves for summary judgment.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiffs request that the Court take judicial notice of the First Amended Complaint in this action. Plaintiffs’ request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

Defendant’s Evidentiary Objections

 

            Defendant raises evidentiary objections to Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement and to the supporting declaration of Dr. Pushpa Shivaram. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

Objection No. 1: OVERRULED. Defendant does not specify what facts are referenced in the papers and not in Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement in this objection. Moreover, the specific responses which Defendant challenges with subsequent objections Nos. 2 through 7 are not material to the Court’s ruling.

 

The Court therefore declines to rule on Objections 2 through 7 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(q).

 

Objection Nos. 8-29: OVERRULED. The Court is not persuaded that Dr. Shivaram is unqualified to testify to the care and treatment of infants such as Maia Hansen. While it is true that qualifications “in one field vaguely related to another” are not sufficient, (see California Shoppers, Inc. v. Royal Globe Ins. Co. (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 1, 66-67) both sides have presented testimony from board certified pediatric cardiologists concerning the conduct of Defendants. To that extent, the expert declarations are equal. The Court also does not accept Defendant’s assertions that Dr. Shivaram’s evaluation is without foundation, speculative, or unreliable because she did not review deposition testimony. The declaration plainly states that it is based on Melissa’s prenatal and Maia’s post-natal medical records and describes the specific records being referenced, i.e., the same records which Defendant produced in support of her motion. (See Defendant’s Exhs. E-G.) Nor does the Court interpret Dr. Shivaram’s expert opinion as failing to reach a “reasonable medical probability.”

 

Professional Negligence

 

            Defendant Shah moves for summary judgment on the grounds that she cannot be liable under a theory of professional negligence because she complied with the standard of care during her treatment of the decedent and did not cause or contribute to the alleged injuries.

 

Plaintiffs assert a claim for wrongful death due to professional negligence under the first cause of action and a survivorship claim under the same theory in the third cause of action. (FAC ¶¶ 52-62; 74-77.) The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.” (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) In a medical negligence action, the standard of care is a matter that must be established by expert testimony. (See Howard v. Owens Corning (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 621, 632-33.) The causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury is similarly a matter of expert testimony, and that testimony must establish that the injury was more likely than not the result of medical negligence. (Jones v. Ortho Pharmaceutical Corp. (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 396, 402-403.)

 

            The First Amended Complaint alleges that the doctor Defendants failed to adhere to the standard of care in treating the infant decedent Maia Hansen by (1) failing “to deem Maia an emergent ENCI level four case after discovering pulmonary vein flow reversal on her fetal echocardiogram on August 31, 2021” (FAC ¶ 56); (2) failing to instruct Plaintiffs to deliver at the closest available hospital, rather than Huntington Memorial Hospital  “to facilitate immediate treatment for suspected atrial restriction,” (Id.) and (3) failing “to act on the clear and demonstrated need to perform a balloon atrial septostomy prior to the Norwood procedure after Maia’s first postnatal echocardiogram.” (Id.) Plaintiffs allege that this failure was a direct cause of Maia’s death. (FAC ¶ 60.)

 

            In support of her contention that she complied with the standard of care, Dr. Shah offers the expert testimony of Dr. Nancy Halnon, a board-certified pediatrician and pediatric cardiologist licensed to practice medicine in California. (Declaration of Nancy Halnon ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-3.) Dr. Halnon testifies that, based on her review of the medical records and her own expertise, Dr. Shah complied with the standard of care in providing treatment. (Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact Nos. 40-41.) Dr. Halnon testifies that the decision regarding interventional procedures such as a balloon atrial septostomy did not rest with Dr. Shah (SSUMF No. 42) and that, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, it was appropriate for the cardiac team to consider a balloon atrial septostomy and ultimately not to perform the procedure. (Nos. 43-45.) Based on her review of the records and her own expertise, Dr. Halnon is also of the opinion, to a reasonable degree of medical probability, that no act or omission by Dr. Shah caused Plaintiffs injuries and, even if the balloon atrial septostomy had been performed, Maia would have had the same complications after the performance of the Norwood procedure. (SSUMF Nos. 46-47.) Defendant has offered competent expert testimony establishing that she acted within the standard of care and did not cause Plaintiffs’ injuries to a reasonable degree of medical probability and, therefore, has met her burden to demonstrate that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on their claims on this basis. The burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to offer competent evidence establishing a triable issue of fact with respect to these arguments.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs put forward contrary expert testimony by Dr. Pushpa Shivaram, a board-certified pediatrician and specialist in pediatric cardiovascular disease licensed to practice medicine by the State of Georgia. (Declaration of Pushpa Shivaram ISO Opp. ¶¶ 1-2.) Dr. Shivaram opines, based upon her review of the medical records and her own expertise, that the physicians’ treatment of Maia fell below the standard of care and directly led to Maia’s death. (Separate Statement of Disputed Fact Nos. 41-47; Shivaram Decl. ¶ 7.) Dr. Shivaram testifies that Maia should have been delivered in a specialized labor and delivery unit, should have been designated ENCI category 4 during her prenatal care, and should have had a balloon atrial septostomy performed immediately after birth. (SSDF Nos. 41-47; Shivaram Decl. ¶ 8.) Dr. Shivaram contends that Maia’s treating physicians should have recognized the need for emergent care and the balloon atrial septostomy on October 5 and 6, 2021. (SSDF Nos. 41-47; Shivaram Decl. ¶ 9.) Critically, Dr. Shivaram states that the critical care physicians, which include Dr. Shah, should have and raised the severity of Maia’s condition and the need for that procedure with the cardiology team but failed to do so. (Id.) Dr. Shivaram opines, to a reasonable degree of medical certainty,” that these failures and the delay in providing proper emergent care they engendered “led to Maia’s death.”  (Id., ¶ 10.)  By offering this contrary testimony, Plaintiffs have demonstrated the existence of a triable issue of fact regarding Dr. Shah’s adherence to the standard of care and whether her conduct caused Plaintiffs’ injury.

 

            As Plaintiffs have established a triable issue of fact as to whether Dr. Shah breached the standard of care and caused the death of the decedent, Defendant is not entitled to summary judgment on this basis.

 

Statute of Limitations

 

            Defendant moves for summary judgment on the alternative theory that Plaintiffs’ claims are barred by the statute of limitations.

 

            Claims for wrongful death against a healthcare provider are governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5, which states, in relevant part:

 

In an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person’s alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 340.5.) For the purposes of this statute as it applies to wrongful death claims, “injury” means both “the death of [the] decedent and its negligent cause.” (Larcher v. Wanless (1976) 18 Cal.3d 656, 657-58 [emphasis added].) Defendant argues that the discovery provision of section 340.5 required Plaintiffs to assert their claims against her within one year of the death of Maia on October 25, 2021. As the Court stated in its ruling on Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings on November 16, 2023, Defendant misconstrues the holding of Larcher by making this argument. (See November 16, 2023 Ruling on Matter Taken Under Submission.) As the Court stated then:

 

[t]he reality is that Larcher clarified section 340.5’s references to “injury” in the one-year discovery clause as encompassing wrongful death claims rather than excluding them. (Larcher, supra, 18 Cal.3d at 658-69.) In so holding, the California Supreme Court stated that, as applied to wrongful death actions, the word “injury” “must be read to refer to the wrongfully caused death of the plaintiff’s decedent.” (Id at 659 [emphasis added].) Put differently, a plaintiff in a wrongful death action against a healthcare provider has one year to file suit “measured from the date on which they discovered or should have discovered their “injury” in the form of the death of their decedent and its negligent cause,” (Id. at 657-58 [emphasis added]), so long as the suit is filed within three years of the death of the decedent.

 

(November 16 2023 Ruling on Matter Taken Under Submission p.3.)

 

            Although Defendant misstated the law in framing the issue, Defendant’s factual contention—reflecting the proper statement of the law—is that Plaintiffs were aware of or suspected wrongdoing during Maia’s treatment. Defendant states that Plaintiff Melissa Hansen testified at her deposition that Dr. Votava-Smith had stated that a ventricular septal defect (VSD) was seen on Maia’s fetal echocardiogram and that this condition, while normally not desirable, would be of assistance in Maia’s situation. (SSUMF Nos. 31-32.) The evidence shows, however, that Plaintiffs learned on October 6, 2021 that the VSD was not present. (SSUMF No. 30.) Defendant contends that Mrs. Hansen requested that Dr. Votava-Smith not visit her during Maia’s admission because of the absent VSD, but that assertion is not clearly supported by the deposition testimony to which Defendant cites. (SSUMF No. 31, Defendant’s Exh. H pp. MHANSEN028-029.) When asked if her request not to be visited by Dr. Votava-Smith was because of her failure to diagnose the lack of VSD, Plaintiff testified “I more so did not want to be visited by [Dr. Votava-Smith] again due to the comment she made about my daughter’s head.” (Id.) Defendant also contends that Plaintiffs testified at their depositions that multiple healthcare providers stated that Maia’s post-operative complications were caused by Maia’s poor condition prior to the Norwood procedure, to which the absent VSD was a contributing factor. (SSUMF No. 34.)

 

Despite the breadth of this evidence presented by Defendant, none of the testimony or records cited directly states that Plaintiffs suspected wrongdoing by any party in October 2021, nor does it support an inference of that suspicion when construed in the light most favorable to the Plaintiffs. Defendant’s assertion that “it is clear” that Plaintiffs suspected wrongdoing does not make it so. In particular, Plaintiffs’ request for Maia’s medical records on November 16, 2021 to help them “understand why [Maia] died during her recovery” does not support an inference that Plaintiffs suspected wrongdoing under the favorable construction required on this motion. (SSUMF No. 28.) A bulk citation to the entirety of Plaintiffs’ deposition testimony and responses to requests for production, as offered by Defendant (see SSUMF No. 29), does not constitute evidence to the contrary.

 

Defendant also argues that Plaintiffs were aware that the statute of limitations would expire on October 25, 2022, one year after Maia’s death, and that it was for that reason that they sent several letters of intent to sue on October 3, 2022. (SSUMF No. 35.) Once again, Defendant misstates the evidence. Plaintiffs testified that they were aware of a possible reading of the statute of limitations which would require them to present their claims by October 25, 2022, not that they were aware that their claims would certainly be barred if not presented by that date. (Defendant’s Exh. H. p. MHANSEN044; Exh. I p. CHANSEN026.)  

 

Defendant has not presented evidence which would establish, when construed in the light most favorable to Plaintiffs, that they were aware of the wrongful cause of Maia Hansen’s death at any point before October 3, 2022, when the first letters of intent to sue were sent. Thus, Defendant has only offered evidence that Plaintiffs’ claims accrued no later than October 3, 2022, and, therefore, that the statute of limitations ran on October 3, 2023. Because Dr. Shah was substituted in for a Doe Defendant on March 15, 2023, she has not offered evidence demonstrating that the claims asserted against her are barred by the statute of limitations. Defendant is therefore not entitled to summary judgment on this basis.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Defendant Shah’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

            This is a toxic tort action alleging Plaintiff Montoya-Bautista developed silicosis and consequential injuries by breathing in dust from Defendants’ stone products generated by his work as a fabricator and polisher of stone countertops.

 

Specially Appearing Defendant Spectrum Quartz, LLC.  moves to quash service of the summons and complaint for lack of personal jurisdiction.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Specially Appearing Defendant Spectrum Quartz, LLC’s Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Specially Appearing Defendant Spectrum Quartz, LLC moves to quash service of the summons and complaint lack of personal jurisdiction.

Special Appearance 

 

No motion under Code of Civil Procedure 418.10 “shall be deemed a general appearance by the defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 418.10(d).) Here, Specially Appearing Defendant (“Defendant”) brought this motion under section 418.10. Thus, filing this motion does not constitute a general appearance.  

 

Legal Standard

 

On a motion to quash service of the summons and complaint, the moving party must first present some admissible evidence, such as declarations or affidavits, to place the issue of minimum contacts before the Court. (School Dist. of Oskaloosa County v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1126, 1131.) “When a motion to quash is properly brought, the burden of proof is placed upon the plaintiff to establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence.”  (Aquila, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 568; see also Elkman v. National States Ins. Co. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1312-13 [“Where a nonresident defendant challenges jurisdiction by way of a motion to quash, the plaintiff bears the burden of establishing by a preponderance of the evidence that minimum contacts exist between the defendant and the forum state to justify imposition of personal jurisdiction.”].) Evidence of the facts giving rise to personal jurisdiction or its absence may be in the form of declarations. (Arensen v. Raymond Lee Organization, Inc. (1973) 31 Cal.App.3d 991, 995.) The Court should exclude evidence that would be inadmissible at trial. (See, e.g., Judd v. Superior Court (1976) 60 Cal.App.3d 38, 43-44 [Court of Appeal excluded inadmissible hearsay evidence offered in support of affirmation of trial court’s denial of motion to quash, and subsequently reversed the trial court’s denial].) A non-resident defendant may be subject to either general or specific jurisdiction. (See Elkman v. National States Insurance Co., supra, 173 Cal.App.4th at 1314.)

 

Personal Jurisdiction

 

Specially Appearing Defendant contends that the Court lacks both general and specific personal jurisdiction over Spectrum Quartz LLC because the company does not have a sufficient relationship with the State of California.

 

1.      General Jurisdiction

 

Specially Appearing Defendant contends that the Court does not have general jurisdiction over it. General jurisdiction exists when a defendant is domiciled in the forum state or his activities there are substantial, continuous, and systematic.  (F. Hoffman-La Roche, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 782, 796.)  “In such circumstances, it is not necessary that the specific cause of action alleged be connected with the defendant’s business relationship to the forum.”  (Id.) “The standard for establishing general jurisdiction is ‘fairly high,’ [citation] and requires that the defendant’s contacts be of the sort that approximate physical presence.” (Elkman, supra, 173 Cal.App.4th at 1315 (emphasis in original).)  “Factors to be taken into consideration are whether the defendant makes sales, solicits or engages in business in the state, serves the state’s markets, designates an agent for service of process, holds a license, or is incorporated there.” (Id.) A corporate defendant will be considered “at home” in either its place of incorporation or its principal place of business. (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 137; Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown (2011) 564 U.S. 915, 923-24.)

 

Specially Appearing Defendant contends it has no contacts with California whatsoever. In opposition, Plaintiffs assert that Specially Appearing Defendant has a principal place of business in the State of California. This assertion is based on a single webpage bearing the name “Spectrum Quartz” which states only that there is an office at 1222 East Howell Avenue, Suite B. Anaheim, CA 92805. (Declaration of Raphael Metzger ISO Opp. Exh. A.) This exhibit does not state that Specially Appearing Defendant’s principal place of business is at that location. Further, Specially Appearing Defendant’s supporting declaration states that Spectrum Quartz is a fictitious business name registered to Hirsch Glass Corporation, not Specially Appearing Defendant. (Declaration of Helen Zhao ISO Mot. ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs offer no other evidence of any activities undertaken by Specially Appearing Defendant which would render it “essentially at home” and subject to general jurisdiction. Plaintiffs have thus failed to carry their burden to demonstrate general jurisdiction.

 

2.      Specific Jurisdiction

 

“Where general jurisdiction cannot be established, a court may assume specific jurisdiction over a defendant in a particular case if the plaintiff shows the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; [i.e.] the nonresident purposefully directed its activities at forum residents or purposefully availed itself of the privilege of conducting activities within the forum state, thus invoking the benefits and protections of local law.  (Hanson v. Denckla (1958) 357 U.S. 235.)

 

Specific jurisdiction involves a 3-part test in California: “(1) The nonresident defendant must do some act or consummate some transaction with the forum or perform some act by which he purposefully avails himself of the privilege of conducting activities in the forum, thereby invoking the benefits and protections of its laws; (2) the claim must be one which arises out of or results from the defendant's forum-related activities; and (3) exercise of jurisdiction must be reasonable.” (Panavision International, L.P. v. Toeppen (9th Cir. 1998) 141 F.3d 1316, 1320 [applying California law].).” (Jewish Defense Organization, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. of Los Angeles County (Rambam) (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1054.)

 

Specially Appearing Defendant argues that it did not purposefully avail itself of the privilege of conducting activities in California.  Purposeful availment in tort cases is based upon “(1) intentional actions (2) expressly aimed at the forum state (3) causing harm, the brunt of which is suffered –and which the defendant knows is likely to be suffered –in the forum state.” (Jewish Defense Organization, Inc., supra, 72 Cal.App.4th at 1057-58.)

 

Specially Appearing Defendant argues that it neither sells nor distributes any of the products at issue in this case. According to the Declaration of Helen Zhao, Spectrum Quartz LLC was formed in 2019 and has never sold products of any kind. (Zhao Decl. ¶ 2.) Specially Appearing Defendant claims that its only business interest is the leasing of real estate which it owns to Elite Quartz Manufacturing LLC. (Zhao Decl. ¶¶ 3-4.) Plaintiffs offer nothing save the unsupported assertion that Specially Appearing Defendant engages in “significant business activity” in the State of California. To the extent this conclusion is based on the above-referenced webpage, Plaintiffs have not shown that the webpage listing a California address actually belongs to this Defendant, rather than to another entity operating under a similar fictitious business name. Nor have Plaintiffs produced evidence of any other activity undertaken by Spectrum Quartz LLC. Plaintiffs have thus entirely failed to carry their burden to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction is proper in this case.

 

Although Plaintiffs request, in the alternative, that the Court permit jurisdictional discovery on this issue, Plaintiffs do not explain how such discovery is likely to lead to the production of at least some evidence of facts establishing jurisdiction. (See In re Automobile Antitrust Cases I & II (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 100, 127.) The Court therefore does not find good cause to permit jurisdictional discovery.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Specially Appearing Defendant Spectrum Quartz, LLC’s Motion to Quash Service of the Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on September 15, 2023. Plaintiff purchased a 2023 Mercedes-Benz C300W on December 11, 2022 which developed defects to critical systems including the body, electrical, braking, and powertrain systems.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to four sets of discovery served on Defendant.

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED.

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel responses to four sets of discovery served on Defendant.

 

Multiple Motions

 

            Multiple motions should not be combined into a single filing.¿(See¿Govt. Code,¿§ 70617(a)(4) [setting forth the required filing fee for each motion, application, or any other paper or request requiring a hearing];¿see¿also¿Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011)¿[“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”].)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks in one motion to compel responses to form and special interrogatories, requests for admission, and requests for production and special interrogatories. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions to avoid payment of separate filing fees deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect.

 

            The Court therefore conditions its ruling on this motion on the payment of an additional $180 in filing fees within 10 days of this order.

 

Interrogatories and Requests for Production

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel responses to form and special interrogatories and requests for production propounded to Defendant.

 

When a party to whom an inspection demand or an interrogatory is directed fails to respond, a party making the request may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(b) [interrogatories]; 2031.300(b) [requests for production].) A party who fails to provide timely responses waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(a); 2031.300(a).) For a motion to compel initial responses, no meet and confer is required. All that must be shown is that a set of requests for production was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response has been served. (Leach v. Sup. Ct. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-06.)

 

            Plaintiff served the interrogatories and requests for production on Defendant via email on September 7, 2021. (Declaration of Kyle Lee ISO Mot. ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) As of the date this motion was filed, no responses had been received. (Id. ¶ y.) Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order compelling responses to the interrogatories and requests for production without objections.

 

//

 

Requests for Admission

 

            Plaintiff also moves for an order compelling responses to requests for admission. No such motion is contemplated by the Code of Civil Procedure, which only authorizes a motion to deem the truth of matters stated in the requests in the event no response is received. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280.) Plaintiff’s request is therefore improper.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Admission is DENIED.

 

            This ruling is conditioned on Plaintiff paying $180 in filing fees within 10 days of this order.
