[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: 

 

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

           

Plaintiff Circa 1200, LLC (“Plaintiff”) requests that the Court enter default judgment against Defendant Marquita Leonard. Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $75,345.45, comprising $65,878.99 demanded in the Complaint, $8,266.46 in interest, $683.00 in costs, and $517.00 in attorney fees.

The Court notes a few defects with the submitted default judgment package.

First, Plaintiff’s counsel has not submitted any declaration attesting to the amount of attorney’s fees requested.

Second, Plaintiff cites to paragraph 32 of the subject lease agreement to support Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees. (Garner Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. A.) However, it appears that portions of paragraph 32 (and other sections of the lease agreement) are missing. (Ibid.) It appears that the bottom of the pages of the lease agreement are cut-off and/or obscured. (Ibid.) 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies the request for default judgment without prejudice. The Court will discuss with counsel a schedule for submitting a new default judgment package.

 

DATED:  October 10, 2024                           ________________________________

Hon. Teresa A. Beaudet

Judge, Los Angeles Superior Court

DEFENDANT DUANE MORRIS LLP’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES FROM PLAINTIFF TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 47, OF ITS FOURTH SET OF REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTIONS

 

           

            Background

Plaintiff Intelligent SCM, LLC, dba American Worldwide Agencies filed this action on February 13, 2015 against Defendants Russell W. Roten (“Roten”) and Duane Morris.  

Intelligent SCM, LLC, dba American Worldwide Agencies filed the operative Fourth Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on July 9, 2019 against Roten, Duane Morris, and Andrew P. Scott. The FAC alleges causes of action for (1) constructive fraud and breach of fiduciary duty, (2) legal malpractice, (3) negligence (4) fraud, (5) tort of another, (6) intentional interference with contractual relationship, (7) negligent interference with contractual relationship,

(8) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, (9) negligent interference with prospective economic advantage, (10) negligent supervision, (11) conversion, and (12) aiding and abetting.   

On April 27, 2017, Andrew Scott (“Scott”) filed a Cross-Complaint against a number of Cross-Defendants, alleging five causes of action. On May 3, 2017, Peter Lamy and Graham Burford filed a Cross-Complaint against Scott. The Cross-Complaint alleges two causes of action.

On August 25, 2023, Duane Morris LLP (“Duane Morris”) served its Fourth Set of Requests for Production of Documents on Intelligent SCM, LLC (“ISCM”). (Scott Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2.) The Fourth Set of Requests for Production of Documents include, inter alia, Request for Production No. 47. (Ibid.) On October 5, 2023, ISCM served responses to Duane Morris’s Fourth Set of Requests for Production of Documents. (Scott Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. 3.)

Counsel for Duane Morris met and conferred with ISCM’s counsel concerning asserted deficiencies in ISCM’s responses to the Fourth Set of Requests for Production of Documents. (Scott Decl., ¶¶ 6-8, Exs. 4-5.)  

On April 24, 2024, the parties participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”). The Court’s April 24, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Informal Discovery Conference is held. The parties fulfilled the IDC requirement for the issues raised in the IDC Statements for today’s IDC.”[1]

Duane Morris now moves for an order “pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.310 compelling Plaintiff, Intelligent SCM, LLC dba American Worldwide Agencies…to provide a verified response to Request for Production 47 in which it commits to producing all responsive documents, and to produce all such responsive documents within fourteen days of the Order, as well as for monetary sanctions…” ISCM opposes.

            Legal Standard

            Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a) provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1)¿A statement¿of compliance with the demand is incomplete. (2)¿A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3)¿An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” A motion under subdivision (a) shall “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand,” and “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b).)

Discussion

            As set forth above, Duane Morris moves for an order “compelling [ISCM]…to provide a verified response to Request for Production 47 in which it commits to producing all responsive documents…” (Notice of Mot. at p. 2:6-9.)

            Duane Morris’s Request for Production No. 47 seeks “All INVOICES and BILLS RELATED TO any attorney services provided to YOU in connection with the MALPRACTICE CASE.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2.)[2] ISCM’s response to Request for Production No. 47 sets forth objections, and provides, inter alia, that “[s]ubject to and without waiving the foregoing objections, Plaintiff responds as follows: Information contained in the invoices and bills for attorney services provided to Plaintiff in this Malpractice Case includes vast amounts of information that is absolutely privileged work product, and protected attorney-client communication.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. 3.)

            In the motion, Duane Morris asserts that “ISCM seeks to recover as damages the attorneys’ fees it purportedly has incurred in litigating this action.” (Mot. at p. 2:13-14.) Duane Morris points to pages 51 and 52 of ISCM’s “Third Amended, Further, and Supplemental Responses” to Duane Morris’s “Special Interrogatories to Putative Plaintiff Intelligent SCM, LLC, Set No. Three.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. 1.) ISCM’s “Second Amended Supplemental Response To Interrogatory No. 9” lists a number of “fees” and “costs” next to the categories “Speed Case,” “Embezzlement Case,” “Malpractice Case,” and “Crease Case.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. 1, pp. 51-52.)[3]

            Duane Morris cites to Byers v. Superior Court (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 1003, 1006, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n this writ proceeding, George and Sheila Byers challenge the trial court to vacate its order granting USAA General Indemnity Company’s (USAA) motion to compel further responses to document requests seeking attorney fees documents. The Byerses further request that we order the trial court to delay production of their attorney fees documents until after the jury makes a determination of liability. We conclude that the trial court did not err and deny the petition.” The Byers Court found that “[w]hen a party seeks attorney fees as damages caused by an insurer’s breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing under Brandt v. Superior Court (1985) 37 Cal.3d 813, 819 [210 Cal. Rptr. 211, 693 P.2d 796] (Brandt), it impliedly waives the attorney-client privilege with respect to its attorney fees agreements, invoices, fees statements, billing records, receipts, and proof of payments (collectively, attorney fees documents) that evidence the amount of its damages.” (Ibid.)

            The Court of Appeal in Byers found that “the Byerses’ admission that they are seeking Brandt fees as an element of their damages is an implied waiver of the attorney-client privilege at least as to the attorney fees documents that the Byerses plan to rely upon to seek to prove the amount of fees they reasonably incurred to establish their right to benefits under USAA’s insurance policy. The parties have not cited, nor are we aware of, any controlling authority specifically holding that a party claiming Brandt fees impliedly waives the attorney-client privilege as to documentation supporting the fees, including fees agreements and invoices. However, it is well established in other contexts that [w]here privileged information goes to the heart of the claim, fundamental fairness requires that it be disclosed for the litigation to proceed.” (Byers v. Superior Court, supra, 101 Cal.App.5th at p. 1011 [internal quotations and citation omitted].) The Court of Appeal noted that “[w]e agree with the trial court that USAA has a right to learn during discovery of the attorney fees aspect of the Byerses’ alleged damages and that by seeking such damages the Byerses have impliedly waived the attorney-client privilege. The Byerses have put at issue the attorney fees they incurred in an effort to seek coverage under their insurance policy, and disclosure of documents supporting their claim for such fees is necessary to fairly adjudicate the issue of damages.” (Id. at p. 1012.)

            Duane Morris asserts that accordingly, “[h]aving made a claim to recover its attorneys’ fees as damages, ISCM must produce the documents relevant to that claim…” (Mot. at p. 2:1-3.)

            In the opposition, ISCM does not appear to dispute that it is seeking attorney’s fees in this case. However, ISCM asserts that it “cannot be compelled to produce unredacted invoices.” (Opp’n at p. 10:14.) ISCM notes that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.030, subdivision (a), “[a] writing that reflects an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories is not discoverable under any circumstances.” ISCM submits the declaration of its counsel, who states that “[t]he ISCM invoices sought by RFP No. 47 are replete with its attorneys’ mental impressions, conclusions, opinions, legal research subjects and theories.” (Zelener Decl., ¶ 9.)[4]

            ISCM also asserts that “Byers – the case upon which Duane Morris principally relies – expressly contemplates redaction of work product.” (Opp’n at p. 11:17-18.) In Byers, the Court of Appeal noted as follows:

 

“The Byerses claim that the trial court abused its discretion by ordering an in camera review of attorney invoices. Their argument overstates what the trial court actually ordered and is premature given the current stage of the proceedings.

 

The relevant portion of the order states: ‘Byers’ counsel may redact the documents to remove references that counsel believes reflect attorney work product, i.e., information that may give an indication of counsel’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories, or as to consultants or expert witnesses for which the attorney work product doctrine has not been waived by Byers’ counsel. [¶] … [¶] USAA GIC may challenge the redactions made by counsel for Byers, and such redactions will be evaluated by the Court in an in camera hearing.’

 

The Byerses have not yet produced any responsive documents, and it is unknown the extent to which they may redact any responsive documents and whether USAA will seek to challenge the redactions. Thus, the trial court’s order regarding in camera review is conditioned upon redactions and subsequent challenges to the redactions. Given the undeveloped record, the issue framed by the Byerses is speculative and premature. Further, the issue of the propriety of conducting an in camera review of potentially redacted documents and the proper scope of such a review was not presented to the trial court. When the trial court made its ruling at the hearing on USAA’s motion to compel, it mentioned the possibility of conducting an in camera review at some point in the future and neither party raised any objections to such a proceeding. If the Byerses produce redacted documents in response to USAA’s document requests and if USAA challenges any redactions, the parties may then present their positions to the trial court regarding whether, and to what extent, it may review the redacted material in camera.” (Byers v. Superior Court, supra, 101 Cal.App.5th at p. 1014.)

ISCM argues that “[a]s Duane Morris correctly states, Byers holds that, where attorney’s fees are sought as an element of damages, defendant is entitled to take discovery into the attorney’s fees sought. However, there is absolutely nothing in Byers that stands for the proposition that defendant is entitled to the wholesale production of unredacted invoices.” (Opp’n at p. 11:19-22.) This does not appear to be disputed by Duane Morris.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Duane Morris has demonstrated good cause for an order compelling a further response to its Request for Production No. 47. However, the Court also finds that there is merit to ISCM’s objections that Request for Production No. 47 seeks privileged work product and protected attorney-client communications. Accordingly, as discussed in further detail below, the Court is inclined to grant the motion, subject to the limitations discussed below in the “Conclusion” section of this order.

            Lastly, Duane Morris seeks monetary sanctions against ISCM. Duane Morris does not appear to cite any legal authority in support of its request for sanctions. To the extent Duane Morris seeks monetary sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, this provision provides that  “the court shall impose a monetary sanction…against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).) The Court finds that ISCM acted with substantial justification in presenting its position in the opposition. Thus, the Court declines to award monetary sanctions to Duane Morris.

            ISCM also seeks monetary sanctions against Duane Morris. The Court finds that Duane Morris acted with substantial justification in presenting its position and declines to award monetary sanctions to ISCM.  

Conclusion

            Based on the foregoing, Duane Morris’s motion is granted in part.

The Court orders ISCM to provide a further verified response to Duane Morris’s Request for Production No. 47, within 30 days of the date of this Order.¿In addition, within 30 days of the date of this Order, ISCM is ordered to produce documents responsive to Duane Morris’s Request for Production No. 47, but ISCM may redact the documents to remove references that ISCM’s counsel believes reflects “an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030, subd. (a).) If Duane Morris disputes the redactions made by counsel for ISCM, the parties must meet and confer in person or via telephone or video conference in a good faith effort to resolve the dispute; if the meet and confer is unsuccessful, the parties must participate in an informal discovery conference (i.e., a further meet and confer with the Court as a participant).

            Duane Morris’s request for monetary sanction is denied. ISCM’s request for monetary sanctions is also denied.

Duane Morris is ordered to provide notice of this order.
