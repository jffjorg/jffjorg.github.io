DEPARTMENT 78 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV39133    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: 78

 

Superior Court of California¿ 

County of Los Angeles¿ 

Department 78¿ 

¿ 

LL JOHN DOE MB, 

Plaintiff(s), 

vs. 

DEFENDANT DOE SCHOOL DISTRICT, et al., 

Defendant(s). 

Case No.:¿ 

22STCV39133 

Hearing Date:¿ 

August 2, 2024 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO STAY PROCEEDINGS 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

On December 16, 2022, plaintiff LL John Doe MB (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Doe School District, et. al. for allegations arising from childhood sexual abuse occurring in or around 1995-1996. The complaint sets forth seven causes of action for 1) Claim for Childhood Sexual Assault in Violation of Cal. Code of Civil Procedure 340.1; (2) Negligence; (3) Negligent Supervision/Failure To Warn; (4) Negligent Retention/Hiring; (5) Negligent Failure To Warn, Train Or Educate; (6) Intentional Infliction Of Emotional Distress; and (7) Sexual Harassment. 

On July 9, 2024, defendant Doe School District (“the District”) filed the instant motion to stay proceedings, in reliance upon California Rules of Court Rule 3.515 and Caiafa Prof. Law Corp. v. State Farm Fire and Casualty Co. (1993) 15 Cal. App. 4th 800, 803-04, inter alia. Plaintiff opposes the motion, and District filed a reply. 

 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE 

The District’s request for judicial notice is granted. (Cal. Evid. Code § 452(d).) 

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted. (Cal. Evid. Code § 452(b).) 

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD 

CCP Section 128(a)(8) provides that courts have the inherent power to conform their processes and order to conform to justice. (CCP § 128(a)(8).) CCP Section 597 provides that “where the defense of another action pending … is sustained (and no other special defense is sustained) an interlocutory judgment shall be entered in favor of the defendant pleading the same to the effect that no trial of other issues shall be had until the final determination of that other action.” (CCP § 597.)  

Whether or not to grant a stay “where the issues in [the] actions are substantially identical is a matter addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Thompson v. Continental Ins. Co. (1967) 66 Cal.2d 738, 746, citations omitted.) Duplicative lawsuits are disfavored. (Bistawros v. Greenberg (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 189, 191.) The definition of a related case includes cases that: “[i]nvolve the same parties and are based on the same or similar claims.” (CRC 3.300(a)(1).) Per relevant case law, in a PAGA suit, “The government entity on whose behalf the plaintiff files suit is always the real party in interest.” (Tanguilig v. Bloomingdale’s, Inc. (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 665, 671.)  

Additionally, the doctrine of exclusive concurrent jurisdiction provides that when two California superior courts have simultaneous jurisdiction over the subject matter and parties in a case, “the first to assume jurisdiction has exclusive and continuing jurisdiction over the subject matter and all parties involved until such time as all necessarily related matters have been resolved.” (People ex rel. Garamendi (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 760, 769-70, citations omitted.) 

 

IV. DISCUSSION 

In sum, the District moves to stay the trial because (1) there are two pending writs before two different appellate courts to decide the constitutionality of California Assembly Bill 218 (“AB 218”) and (2) the District avers Plaintiff has a likelihood of prevailing on the merits, and the District fears any settlement that its relevant Board will make will violate the law to authorize payments for any settlement agreement. AB 218 authorized a three-year window beginning in January 1, 2020 that allowed claims of childhood sexual assault to be brought regardless of how long the abuse allegedly occurred.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1, subd. (q).) AB 218 amended Government Code § 905 to retroactively strip statutory governmental immunities for public entities which violates Article XVI section 6 of the Constitution and its prohibition of gifts of public funds. 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that AB 218 has been subject to constitutional challenges in trial courts, and that oral argument on the West Contra Costa case took place on July 18, 2024, and that the July 3, 2024 tentative opinion was in favor of affirming the lower court’s opinion that AB 218 does not violate the gifts clause. Plaintiff argues the relevant factors favor not staying the proceedings, because the decisions made at the appellate level will be appealed, and the ultimate decision may potentially take years. Plaintiff argues a stay would be prejudicial to Plaintiff due to the possible death of witnesses due to age, and protracted litigation for a “wait and see” approach on decisions that may ultimately have no bearing on this case matter.  

In reply, the District reasserts the same arguments. 

 Here, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that staying the matter would be prejudicial. “In exercising its discretion [regarding stay] . . . the court should consider the importance of discouraging multiple litigation designed solely to harass an adverse party, and of avoiding unseemly conflicts with the courts of other jurisdictions . . . [and] whether the rights of the parties can best be determined by the court of the other jurisdiction because of the nature of the subject matter, the availability of witnesses, or the stage to which the proceedings in the other court have already advanced.” (Farmland Irr. Co. v. Dopplmaier (1957) 48 Cal.2d 208, 215.) The District concedes that there is no multiple litigation by the same parties, and that Plaintiff has not brought this lawsuit with the intent to harass. The Court finds that there is no guarantee that the decisions made by the Court of Appeals in the pending writs would create a definitive ruling on the issue, as it could always be further appealed to the Supreme Court. Considering the amount of contention surrounding AB 218’s constitutionality, the Court finds it more than likely the appellate decisions will be appealed. The Court is unwilling to indefinitely suspend proceedings in this action. 

 

V. CONCLUSION 

The District’s motion to stay proceedings is DENIED. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiffs Blair Cobb and Adam Eatroff (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a First Amended Complaint against defendants Elsie I. Wynn (“Wynn”), 3557 Stoner, LLC, Thomas James Capital, Inc., Thomas Beadel, and Does 1 to 25 for damages arising from construction and destruction of a stone retaining wall, resulting in loss of use and loss of value of property. The complaint sets forth two causes of action for negligence and nuisance. 

On April 12, 2024, defendants Wynn, Thomas James Capital, Inc. and Thomas Beadel (collectively, “Defendants”) filed a motion for a protective order that Wynn’s deposition not be taken at all on the grounds that she is bedridden and suffers from dementia. 

On July 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition to the motion. 

On July 26, 2024, Defendants filed their reply. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD 

Any party may obtain discovery, subject to restrictions, by taking the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)¿ A properly served deposition notice is effective to require a party or party-affiliated deponent to attend and to testify, as well as to produce documents for inspection and copying.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a).)¿ 

The party served with a deposition notice waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)¿ In addition to serving this written objection, a party may also move for an order staying the taking of the deposition and quashing the deposition notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (c).)¿ 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party . . . without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving notice may move for an order compelling deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production . . . of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)¿ 

As for protective orders, the court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may direct that the deposition not be taken at all.¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.420 (b)(1).)¿ 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

Defendants argue that Wynn is a 97-year-old woman in poor health, and that she lacks the mental and physical capacity to attend an oral deposition. Wynn’s son, Ron Wynn, attests that Wynn is bedridden, has dementia, cannot walk or speak, and has two 24-hour nurses providing her care. (Mot. Ron Wynn Decl. ¶¶2-4.) 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants (1) did not object to the deposition notice, (2) Defendants failed to provide competent medical evidence that Wynn is too infirm to be deposed, and (3) Wynn is the only person who can testify as to who built the wall at issue. Plaintiffs assert that they are aware of Wynn’s age, and that they will take care in seeking her testimony and have offered to take her deposition at her home. 

In reply, Defendants reasserts the same contentions, and provides a letter by Barry D. Mann, M.D. (“Dr. Mann”), dated July 6, 2024. This informal letter declares that Dr. Mann is a retired general surgeon and acquaintance of the Wynn family. (Reply. Exh. H.) Dr. Mann states he visited Defendant in May 2024, and observed the following: Defendant is unable to leave her bed, and “While I find her to be coherent, she has great difficulty focusing on a single subject for more than a few minutes. Her memory for recent and distant events is quite cloudy.” (Ibid.)  

Here, Defendants objected to Wynn’s deposition on the grounds that Wynn could not appear due to her age, health, and lack of mental and physical capacity. (Mot. Exh. B.) The parties agree that, at the previous IDC on this issue presided over by the Hon. Judge Jill Feeney, the issue of Wynn’s incompetency would need to be established with medical evidence. Here, the Court finds that the letter from Dr. Mann does not affirmatively establish the impossibility of Wynn’s deposition. Aside from this Court’s skepticism as to whether Dr. Mann has sufficient foundation to render an opinion, Dr. Mann’s informal letter suggests Wynn has limited capabilities, but that she is otherwise competent and coherent. In terms of her inability to leave bed, Plaintiffs have already provided they are willing to depose Wynn at her home due to her physical restriction. Although the amount of discovery to be obtained from Wynn may be limited due to her alleged “cloudy” memory and difficulty in focusing, Defendants have not established that Wynn’s testimony would be an exercise in futility, nor have Defendants established with any medical evidence that Wynn has been formally diagnosed with dementia or is otherwise deemed incompetent as a matter of law, such that any testimony obtained would be inadmissible. Further, the Court notes that Dr. Mann’s letter notably contradicts Wynn’s son’s attestation that Wynn could not speak. 

There is simply no evidence that Wynn is incompetent for this Court to find good cause to grant a protective order. 

 

IV. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the motion for protective order is DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR ACCOUNTING 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Jason Sydney (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Jack Joseph Puig (“Defendant”) for breach of contract and fraud, wherein the parties formed an agreement of providing a business plan for a company and to serve as its Chief Operating Officer in exchange for an equity interest in the company and a three-year employment agreement. (Compl. ¶¶7-8.) After providing the services, Defendant did not honor the agreements. (Id. ¶ 9.) 

On July 26, 2016, default was entered against Defendant.  

On November 22, 2016, default judgment was entered against Defendant in the amount of $319,373.90. 

On June 13, 2019, the Hon. Robert S. Draper (“Judge Draper”) granted Plaintiff’s motion for turnover or assignment, in which Defendant was ordered to assign all rights and title held in Rose 99 Entertainment, Inc. (“Rose 99”) and Matador Rose, Inc. (“Matador”) to Plaintiff. Defendant earns annual royalties from Waves Audio, which Defendant deposits into Rose 99 and Matador. Judge Draper found that Defendant never made or offered to make any truly substantial payment to satisfy the judgment out of the presumably substantial royalty funds received, necessitating a turnover order. As such, it was found appropriate to assign all rights and title that Defendant held in Rose 99 and Matador to Plaintiff in order to satisfy the judgment. (Final Ruling, June 13, 2019.) 

On June 3, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion requesting an order for (1) accounting, (2) turnover of Matador and Rose 99, (3) payment of all funds necessary to bring Matador and Rose 99 into compliance, and (4) indemnification of Defendant by Plaintiff against any and all tax liabilities. Defendant argues that after the court ordered the entities and accounts to be turned over to Plaintiff, instead of assigning only Defendant’s interest in the royalty payments, Defendant is now in the dark as to whether Plaintiff’s judgment has been fully satisfied, at which point the entities would need to be turned back over to Defendant. Defendant also argues that the entities are now in a suspended status, that there has been no accounting as to the amount of money Plaintiff has collected from Matador and Rose 99, and that Plaintiff failed to use a sheriff collections account. 

On July 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition, arguing that Defendant is attempting to appeal the turnover order, that there are no grounds for a turnover, and that there is no basis for an accounting. 

On July 29, 2024, Defendant filed a reply reasserting the same arguments. 

 

II. DISCUSSION 

The Court has reviewed the briefs. The Court disagrees with Plaintiff’s contention that this is an improper attempt to appeal an order. Further, there is no basis for Defendant’s demand that Plaintiff bring Rose 99 and Matador into tax compliance, when the evidence demonstrates that these entities were already suspended years prior to the action. The only relevant issue is the request for accounting, and whether there has been a material change to merit a modification or setting aside the assignment. 

CCP § 708.560 states, “Either the judgment creditor or the judgment debtor may apply to the court on noticed motion for an order to modify or set aside the assignment order.” (CCP § 708.560(a).) Additionally, “The court shall make an order modifying or setting aside the assignment order upon a showing that there has been a material change in circumstances since the time of the previous hearing on the assignment order. The court may order a reassignment of the right to payments as necessary. The order shall state whether and to what extent it applies to payments already made.” (CCP § 708.560(b).) As provided by the Law Revision Commission, section 708.560 gives the court broad discretion to modify or set aside an assignment order depending upon the circumstances of the case. The statute and the commentary provide that this section is broadly encompassing, and there is no apparent restriction on its applicability as argued by Plaintiff. 

The purpose of any lawsuit or judgment, generally, is to make the plaintiff whole. This Court did not preside over the matter at the time the assignment order was given. However, upon review of the order, the purpose of the turnover was to assist Plaintiff in satisfying the judgment with Defendant’s royalties. Once Plaintiff’s judgment has been satisfied in full, this would effectively be a material change in circumstances to order a reassignment. There is no equitable basis for Plaintiff to continue to hold onto Rose 99 and Matador to collect funds in perpetuity, above and beyond its judgment against Defendant. However, it is impossible to know whether there has been a material change for a reassignment under CCP § 708.560, unless there is an accounting demonstrating the amounts collected, and the outstanding balance on the judgment. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that the current outstanding balance against Defendant is $173,880.50. However, Plaintiff’s counsel has not established any foundation or a basis for this attestation with any evidence or other information.  

 

III. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the Court sets an OSC: Re Accounting To Determine Reassignment Pursuant to CCP § 708.560 for ________________________. Plaintiff is ordered to file a declaration, with supporting evidence, concerning the transactions and the judgment calculation to be filed no later than 5 court days prior to the hearing.
