DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV38950    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER

20STCV38950

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Olan Law Corporation and David Olan

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiff Frank Alonso

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Frank Alonso (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Olan Law and David Raphael Olan (“Olan,” collectively, “Defendants”), alleging claims for elder abuse and conversion.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants represented Plaintiff’s aunt, Dolores Camou (“Camou”), in a personal injury action, Camou v. Toll Brothers Inc., et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2016, No. BC621015 (“the BC621015 case”)).  Camou died during the litigation, and Plaintiff thereafter represented Camou’s interests.  Plaintiff further alleges Defendants failed to inform Camou of settlement funds Defendants received on Camou’s behalf.  Defendants move for summary judgment o, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s operative first amended complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.    

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)   

DISCUSSION

First Cause of Action: Elder Abuse

“[Welfare and Institutions Code s]ection 15610.30, subdivision (a), provides that financial abuse of an elder or dependent adult occurs where a person takes or assists in taking real or personal property of such an adult to a wrongful use or with an intent to defraud, or both.”  (Wood v. Santa Monica Escrow Co. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 1186, 1189.)  In the first amended complaint, Plaintiff alleges that on April 8, 2016, Defendants received a check for $17,841.04 in partial settlement of the claims Camou asserted in the BC621015 case, and Defendants did not inform Camou of this settlement (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 8, 10); and Plaintiff was not aware of the partial settlement until Plaintiff, as Camou’s successor-in-interest, reached a settlement in the BC621015 case after Camou’s death  (id., ¶¶ 12-13).

In August 2017, Defendants contend they informed Plaintiff they settled Camou’s claim for a gross amount of $40,000; as Defendants had initiated a lawsuit on Camou’s behalf, pursuant to the agreement between Camou and Defendants, Defendants took 40% of the gross settlement as fees, or $16,000; and Defendants then deducted $13,330, purportedly to satisfy an outstanding medical lien, and $270 in costs, leaving $10,400 for Plaintiff.  (Declaration of David Olan, Exhibit 2.)  Plaintiff questioned the amount that was to be disbursed to him and having been informed of medical liens and the impact they would have on the settlement.  (Id., Exhibit 3.)  Defendants were unaware at that time a medical payment of $17,841.04, which Defendants had received in April, 2016, had been omitted from the statement provided to Plaintiff in August 2017.  (Id., ¶ 11.) In September 2018, Plaintiff communicated with Defendants and insisted the medical lien be removed from the settlement funds and he was entitled to $23,730. (Id., Exhibit 5.)  After meeting with Plaintiff’s counsel in February 2019, Olan reviewed the case file and discovered discrepancies in the accounting, related this to Plaintiff’s counsel by identifying the medical payment, and stated Plaintiff was due $21,797.54.  (Id., Exhibit 6.)  Plaintiff refused to accept what Defendants stated was the correct distribution and filed the instant action.  (Id., ¶ 17.) 

Olan’s declaration does not meet Defendants’ burden of showing Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for financial elder abuse.  For example, Plaintiff contends Defendants wrongfully retained from Camou the initial $17,841.04 medical payment.  Despite Defendants’ explanation concerning the accounting mistake, Defendants present no evidence that actually refutes this claim.  This creates a genuine issue of material fact that precludes this Court from granting summary judgment in Defendants’ favor.

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s claims are time-barred.  The statute of limitations on a claim for financial elder abuse is four years.  (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657.7.)  However, Defendants argue the one-year statute of limitations for legal malpractice should apply.  The statute of limitations for legal malpractice is one year after Plaintiff discovered the facts supporting the claim for legal malpractice, or four years from the date of the wrongful act or omission, whichever is earlier.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.6, subd. (a).)

In determining which statute of limitations applies, the Court considers the primary interest upon which a defendant’s wrongful conduct infringes.  (See Barton v. New United Motor Mfg., Inc. (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1200, 1207 [“The statute of limitations to be applied in a particular case is determined by the nature of the right sued upon or the principal purpose of the action, not by the form of the action or the relief requested”].)  A professional malpractice action is based on “a failure to meet a standard of reasonable care which results in the tortious invasion of a property right.”  (Ventura County Nat. Bank v. Macker (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1528, 1531.)  But Plaintiff does not allege Defendants breached the standard of reasonable care.  As such, Plaintiff’s “elder abuse allegations alter[] the gravamen of what would otherwise have been professional negligence causes of action.”  (Smith v. Ben Bennett, Inc. (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1507, 1525.)  Accordingly, the pertinent statute of limitations is that which applies to an elder abuse claim.  Thus, Defendants have also failed to meet their burden of showing the one-year statute of limitations for professional responsibility applies to Plaintiff’s first cause of action.

            However, Plaintiff’s first cause of action against Olan is untimely regardless.  Plaintiff did not add Olan to this action until Plaintiff filed the first amended complaint on May 4, 2023.  Although Plaintiff had included “DOES” as defendants, Plaintiff did not substitute Olan as a DOE in the action.  Rather, Plaintiff amended the complaint and identified Olan as a named defendant.  Under these circumstances, Plaintiff’s addition of Olan to the action did not relate back to the filing date of the original complaint, as there is no indication Plaintiff was ignorant of Olan’s identity when Plaintiff filed the original complaint.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 474 [“when the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint  . . . and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly”].)  Thus, Plaintiff’s first cause of action against Olan individually fails. 

Second Cause of Action: Conversion

            The one-year statute of limitations does, however, apply to Plaintiff’s claim for conversion.  “[Code of Civil Procedure] section 340.6(a)'s time bar applies to claims whose merits necessarily depend on proof that an attorney violated a professional obligation while providing professional services. In this context, a ‘professional obligation’ is an obligation that an attorney has by virtue of being an attorney, such as fiduciary obligations, the obligation to perform competently, the obligation to perform the services contemplated in a legal services contract into which an attorney has entered, and the obligations embodied in the Rules of Professional Conduct.”  (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1236-1237.)

            In the cause of action for conversion, Plaintiff alleges Defendants “failed to timely disclose and tender” settlement money.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 32.)  The Rules of Professional Conduct govern the obligations of Defendants with respect to settlement funds.   (See, e.g., Cal. Rules Prof. Conduct, rule 1.15, subd. (d)(1).)  As such, the statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 340.6 applies to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for conversion. 

Defendants advance evidence Plaintiff was aware of his claim at least as of March 7, 2019, when Defendants informed Plaintiff of the additional $17,841.04medical payment.  (Olan Decl., Exhibit 6.)  Plaintiff thus had until March 9, 2020 to assert his cause of action for conversion.  Plaintiff did not file his initial complaint in this matter until October 9, 2020 and did not add the conversion claim until he amended the complaint on May 4, 2023.  As such, Plaintiff’s claim for conversion is untimely.

Plaintiff requests a continuance. If the party opposing a summary judgment submits a declaration that demonstrates evidence “may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, then be presented, the court shall deny the motion . . . ,” continue it for a reasonable period, or “make any other order as may be just.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (h).)  The opposing party's declaration must include facts to support the argument controverting evidence may exist and why the information sought is essential to opposing the motion; the specific reasons why the party cannot present the evidence before the current deadline to file opposition papers; an estimate of the time necessary to obtain such evidence; and the specific procedures the opposing party intends to use to seek the evidence. (Granadino v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 411, 420.)  The grant of a continuance is “virtually mandated” if the opposing party makes the required showing. (Dee v. Vintage Petroleum, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 34, internal quotations omitted.)  

Plaintiff’s counsel, Michael Labrum (“Counsel”), has submitted a declaration in which he  states he served a deposition notice on Olan, but the deposition has not yet occurred.  Counsel does not state, however, what facts Counsel hopes to elicit at deposition to show Plaintiff’s claims against Olan are timely.  As such, Plaintiff fails to show a continuance is warranted.

Punitive Damages

            Defendants fail to advance evidence pertinent to the issue of whether punitive damages are warranted in this case.  As such, Defendants do not meet their burden to show Plaintiff cannot recover punitive damages.     

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The motion for summary judgment as to Olan individually is granted.  The motion for summary adjudication of the second cause of action for conversion is granted. The motion is otherwise denied.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPARTMENT

HEARING DATE

CASE NUMBER

23STCV02273A

MOTION

Motions to Quash Subpoenas

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Jane Doe

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.

 

MOTION

              Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves for protective orders to quash the subpoenas Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) served on Deponents Bradley Coddington and Catherine Coddington (“Deponents”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires deposition testimony or the production of documents, the Court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the Court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

In the instant case, Plaintiff contends, while she worked for Defendant, her co-worker Eric Pagel (“Pagel”) sexually harassed and assaulted her.  Defendant served a subpoena on Deponents, in which Defendant seeks to depose them and asks them to produce documents.  Plaintiff has a right to privacy in “various private or sensitive information regarding [her] personal life . . . .”  (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1003-1004.)  This includes Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponents.  Accordingly, the Court cannot order disclosure of information regarding Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponents unless such information is directly relevant to this litigation.  (See Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  

However, Deponents are percipient witnesses in this action in that Plaintiff has indicated she socialized with them the evening after Pagel’s alleged assault.  Plaintiff also testified she texted Pagel photographs of Plaintiff with Deponents.  Defendant is entitled to discovery concerning Deponents’ observations of Plaintiff in this time frame immediately after the assault, as Plaintiff testified she was distraught.  Plaintiff also testified she told Catherine Coddington about Pagel’s assault.  Defendant is entitled to learn what Plaintiff told Catherine Coddington.  (See Opposition, pp. 6-8.)  Accordingly, Defendant is entitled to depose Deponents, and the motion to quash is denied.  However, the Court concludes Defendant’s subpoena to Deponents is overbroad.  The entirety of the communication history between Plaintiff and Deponents and Deponents and Plaintiff’s husband is not pertinent to this action.  Accordingly, the Court limits the subpoena for production of documents to communications between Deponents and Plaintiff and Deponents and Plaintiff’s husband from: (1) January 14 to April 30, 2020; (2) May 22 to June 30, 2020; (3) August 6, 2020 to December 31, 2021; (4) January 1 to July 5, 2023; (5) October 1 to November 1, 2023; and (6) January 1, 2024 to the present.  Defendant indicates it is amenable to these limitations.  (Opposition, p. 9.)

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

Motion to Quash Subpoena

MOTION

              Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves for a protective order to quash the subpoena Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) served on Deponent John Aiello (“Deponent”).  Defendant opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires deposition testimony or the production of documents, the Court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the Court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

In the instant case, Plaintiff contends, while she worked for Defendant, her co-worker Eric Pagel (“Pagel”) sexually harassed and assaulted her.  Defendant served a subpoena on Deponent, in which Defendant seeks to depose Deponent and asks him to produce documents.  Plaintiff has a right to privacy in “various private or sensitive information regarding [her] personal life . . . .”  (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1003-1004.)  This includes Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponent.  Accordingly, the Court cannot order disclosure of information regarding Plaintiff’s friendship with Deponent unless such information is directly relevant to this litigation.  (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  

However, Deponent is a percipient witness in this action.  According to Plaintiff, she had dinner with Deponent the night after Pagel’s alleged assault.  Plaintiff also testified she texted Pagel photographs of Plaintiff and Deponent.  Defendant is entitled to discovery concerning Deponent’s observations of Plaintiff in this time frame immediately after the assault, as Plaintiff testified she was distraught.  Plaintiff also testified she told her husband about Pagel’s assault, and that Plaintiff’s husband told Deponent.  Defendant is entitled to learn what was told to Deponent.  Plaintiff also testified she told her husband about Pagel’s alleged assault at a dinner party at Deponent’s home.  (See Opposition, pp. 4-6.)  Accordingly, Defendant is entitled to depose Deponent, and the motion to quash is denied. 

However, the Court concludes Defendant’s subpoena to Deponent is overbroad.  The entirety of the communication history between Plaintiff and Deponent and Deponent and Plaintiff’s husband is not pertinent to this action.  Accordingly, the Court limits the subpoena for production of documents to communications between Deponent and Plaintiff and Deponent and Plaintiff’s husband from: (1) January 14 to April 30, 2020; (2) May 22 to June 30, 2020; (3) August 6, 2020 to December 31, 2021; (4) January 1 to July 5, 2023; (5) October 1 to November 1, 2023; and (6) January 1, 2024 to the present.  Defendant indicates it is amenable to these limitations.  (Opposition, pp. 8-9.)

Case Number: 23STCV20534    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV20534

MOTION: 

Demurrer to Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Mayer Moizel

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiffs Marlena Schwenck and Patrick Salway

 

MOTION

            Plaintiffs Marlena Schwenck and Patrick Salway (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendant Mayer Moizel (“Defendant”) for habitability violations.  Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part of the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

In his demurrer, Defendant argues Plaintiffs improperly allege facts on information and belief.  Defendant cannot demurrer to allegations.  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1167 (disapproved of on other grounds by Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 948.)  This argument fails.

Likewise, Defendant’s argument Plaintiffs’ allegations for the premises liability, nuisance, habitability, negligence causes of action are duplicative is unavailing.[1]  First, Defendant fails to explain how those claims are duplicative.  The Court notes, for example, Plaintiffs allege a different basis for the premises liability and nuisance claims and seek different damages.  Second, and in any event, Plaintiffs are entitled to plead in the alternative.  (See, e.g., Mendoza v. Continental Sales Co. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1402 [“When a pleader is in doubt about what actually occurred or what can be established by the evidence, the modern practice allows that party to plead in the alternative and make inconsistent allegations”].)[2] 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

Defendant further contends Plaintiffs fail to allege facts to support their claim for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.  To state such a claim, Plaintiffs must allege a material, defective condition existed in their rental unit, which impacted its habitability, and Defendant failed to restore the rental unit to habitability. (Knight v. Hallsthammar (1981) 29 Cal.3d 46, 55.)  In support of this cause of action, Plaintiffs allege Defendant “failed to inspect and remedy the Plaintiffs’ complaints of water intrusion, dampness and on information and belief, toxic mold exposure.”  (Complaint, ¶ 76.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice.  Defendant may obtain further information regarding Plaintiffs’ claims in discovery.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Therefore, the demurrer to the cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability is overruled. 

Fourth Cause of Action: Private Nuisance

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ claim for private nuisance.  To state a claim for private nuisance, Plaintiffs must allege Defendant substantially interfered with Plaintiffs’ use and enjoyment of the rental property; this caused actual damage; and the interference was unreasonable in terms of its nature, duration, or amount.  (San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938.)  In this regard, Plaintiffs allege Defendant, “by acting or alternatively failing to act, . . . created a condition or permitted a condition to exist that was: harmful to Plaintiffs' health; was indecent or offensive to the senses; was an obstruction to the free use of the property; and created a dangerous condition to Plaintiffs’ property; so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property.”  (Complaint, ¶ 88.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice. 

Sixth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. The elements for this claim are “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community. The defendant must have engaged in conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1001, internal quotations and citations omitted.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants failed to address dangerous conditions in Plaintiffs’ apartments despite knowing of the conditions.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 125-127.)  Plaintiff’s allegations suffice for pleading purposes as they constitute extreme and outrageous conduct that could support a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. Accordingly, the Court overrules the demurrer to Plaintiffs’ cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike the prayer for punitive damages.  In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendant failed to maintain the apartment Plaintiffs leased from Defendant in a habitable condition and to remedy serious issues with the apartment even though Plaintiffs informed Defendant of those issues.  (See, e.g., Complaint, ¶¶ 108, 132.)  However, Plaintiffs do not allege any specific facts to show Defendant acted with malice, rather than acted negligently.  As such, Plaintiffs have not alleged a claim for punitive damages against Defendant.  The Court grants the motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages but gives Plaintiffs the opportunity to amend the prayer.

Defendant moves to strike allegations on information and belief.  Plaintiffs may allege facts not within Plaintiffs’ knowledge on information and belief (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550), but Plaintiffs cannot allege facts they know directly on information and belief (see Thompson v. Sutton (1942) 50 Cal.App.2d 272, 279 [when “facts peculiarly within the knowledge of the party” is alleged on information and belief, they are insufficient and should be stricken]).  Plaintiffs’ allegations in paragraphs 16, 17, 20, 23, 35, and 36 are necessarily within Plaintiffs’ knowledge.  As such, the Court grants the motion to strike these allegations with leave to amend.  Plaintiffs’ allegations in paragraph 92 concern Defendant’s state of mind, of which Plaintiffs lack direct knowledge.  The motion to strike paragraph 92 is, therefore, denied.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ complaint is overruled.

Defendant’s motion to strike paragraphs 16, 17, 20, 23, 35, and 36 is granted with leave to amend.  Defendant’s motion to strike the punitive damages allegations is granted with leave to amend.  Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within 10 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
