DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV01709    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: W

HOVSEP GASPARYAN, et al. vs VOLKSWAGEN GROUP OF AMERICA, inc.

 

plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses

 

Date of Hearing:        October 22, 2024                                           Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        24VECV01709

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Hovsep Gasparyan and Akop Gasparyan

Responding Party:     Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.    

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Thomas decl. ¶¶6-9.)[1]

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On April 15, 2024, Plaintiffs Hovsep Gasparyan and Akop Gasparyan filed a complaint against Defendants Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. and Sherman Oaks-A, Inc. dba Audi Van Nuys for Violation of Song-Beverly Act and Negligent Repair.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Form Interrogatories to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is MOOT.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Admissions to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Request for Production of Documents to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is GRANTED, in part.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiffs Hovsep Gasparyan and Akop Gasparyan move the court for an order compelling further responses from Defendant Volkswagen Group of America to Plaintiff’s First Set of Special Interrogatories, Set One, First Set of Form Interrogatories, First Set of Requests for Admissions, and First Set of Requests for Production of Documents. Plaintiffs also seek monetary sanctions against Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. and its attorney of record, Squire Patton Boggs (US), LLP.

 

Interrogatories

 

Plaintiffs seek further responses to Plaintiffs’ Form Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 15.1 and 17.1. The court notes Plaintiffs have withdrawn their motion to compel further Special Interrogatories due to inadvertently filing and serving the wrong document.

 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ (CCP § 2030.300(a).)¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ (CCP § 2030.300(b).)¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ (California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).)

 

Plaintiffs argue Form Interrogatory 15.1 seeks the factual basis for each and every denial of a material allegation, special defense, and affirmative defense asserted by VWGoA, including identification of and contact information for all percipient witnesses, as well as documents or things which support said denials and defenses. The host of objections Defendant asserts makes it clear that Defendant fundamentally—and perhaps deliberately—misunderstands the nature of Form Interrogatories. Form Interrogatory 17.1 seeks the factual basis for every response to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Admission that is not an unqualified admission, along with the identities of persons and documents that would support these facts. Defendant’s response lumps together RFAs 1-6, 8, 10, 11, 14, 17, 19, 20-23 and 30-34 and provides a single catch-all response.

 

In reply, Plaintiffs assert on October 8, 2024, Defendant served Code-compliant supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories 15.1 and 17.1. Plaintiffs therefore no longer seek further responses to these Form Interrogatories.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Form Interrogatories to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is MOOT.

 

The court notes Plaintiffs also request sanctions in the amount of $1,305.00 (reduced amount requested in Reply). The court further notes Plaintiffs have requested additional sanctions in the amount of $3,615.00 for reviewing oppositions, reviewing tentative rulings and fees incurred in filing the three motions. The court DENIES the request for sanctions as defendants’ position was substantially justified and all of this expense could have been avoided through a more vigorous and meaningful meet and confer effort by plaintiff.

 

Request for Admissions

 

Plaintiffs seek further responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 12, 13 and 18.

 

A motion to compel further responses may be brought based on responses to requests for admission (“RFA”) that: (1) provide evasive or incomplete answers; or (2) make unmeritorious or overly-generalized objections.  (CCP § 2033.290(a).) 

 

Plaintiffs argue Defendant’s objections to RFA Nos. 12, 13, and 18 are without merit and further responses must be ordered. Defendant objected to each request on the grounds they are vague and ambiguous, vague as to time and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. In bringing this motion, Plaintiffs assert they will limit the applicability of RFAs 12 and 13 to the period the Subject Vehicle has been in existence and RFA 13 to California. Plaintiffs argue RFAs 12 and 13 are relevant because they address Defendant’s policies, procedures and practices for collecting information and providing such information to the individuals who evaluate repurchase/replacement requests and the relevance of RFA 18 cannot be seriously questioned.

 

In opposition, Defendant VWGoA argues Requests for Admission Nos. 12, 13, and 18 all seek information regarding VWGoA’s Policies and Procedures. VWGoA produced all the Policy and Procedure Documents in their Confidential Document Production to Plaintiffs on August 8, 2024.

 

The court finds further responses warranted. Whether or not VWGoA produced relevant documents does not mean they then do not have to answer requests for admissions. With the limitations to the period the Subject Vehicle has been in existence and to California, the court finds the requests for admissions relevant and answerable

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Requests for Admissions to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is GRANTED.

 

The court notes Plaintiffs also request sanctions in the amount of $1,305.00 (reduced amount requested in Reply). The court DENIES the request for sanctions as both parties’ positions were substantially justified.

 

Requests for Production of Documents

 

Plaintiffs seek further responses to RFPs 1-15, 17, 18, 19-32, 37, 40-41 and 45-46 on the grounds Defendant’s boilerplate objections are without merit, are not code-compliant, and Defendant improperly rewrites the requests and answers its own questions instead.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, parties may move for a further response to requests for production of documents where a statement of compliance is incomplete or where a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.  A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).)

 

The court notes in Reply, Plaintiffs contend Defendant served supplemental responses to RFPs 16, 17 and 33-36 that identify by bates number ranges responsive documents it has produced. Plaintiffs therefore do not seek further production of documents in response to these RFPs. As such, the remaining discovery in dispute are RFPs 3, 4, 6-15, 20-32 and 38-39.

 

Plaintiffs argue RFP 18 (request for the operative Franchise Agreement) is relevant and discoverable for three primary reasons: (1) showing the extent of the agency relationship between Defendant and the dealership; (2) it is relevant to any motion to compel arbitration; and (3) it will show the reciprocal obligations of Defendant and the dealership to provide information to each other regarding diagnosis and repair of Defendant’s vehicles.

 

In regards to RFPs 3, 4, 6-15, 20-32 and 38-39, Plaintiffs argue Defendant states in part “interpreting the request as excluding privileged communications between VWGoA and its attorneys,” but this is not something Defendant has the right to do. Moreover, in its responses to RFPs 1-2, 4-15, 19-32 and 38-39, Defendant states it is producing all responsive documents “to which no objection has been made,” but provides none of the information required by Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.240 regarding documents to which objections had been made.

 

The court finds further responses warranted. Defendant cannot alter the requests. They can appropriately produce non-privileged documents and provide a privilege log. Moreover, the responses do not with particularity specify the documents to which an objection is being made, and it does not provide sufficient factual information for Plaintiffs to evaluate the merits of Defendant’s privilege claim.

 

The court Plaintiffs have not demonstrated good cause for the franchise agreement (RFP 18). The court notes Plaintiffs’ contention that Defendant has produced documents responsive to RFP 18 in four cases. However, Plaintiffs have not established good cause in this action, except for a potential, yet unfiled motion to compel arbitration.  Plaintiffs have stated that they will withdraw this request if defendants stipulate they will not move to compel arbitration.  The court agrees that defendants cannot have it both way.  If they intend to move to compel arbitration, the agreement must be produced.  If they do not, the court will deem the request withdrawn. 

 

With regard to RFPs 37 and 40-41, Plaintiffs argue these RFPs seek documents that would explain the various codes used as a shorthand in the repair orders and other internal documents produced by Defendant.

 

The opposition states Defendant has already produced documents in response to these requests. It is unclear from the Reply whether all responsive documents were actually produced or not. At the hearing, the parties must explain whether all responsive documents have been produced. If not, Defendant is ordered to do so.

 

With regard to RFPs 45-46, Plaintiffs will limit the RFPs to California. It is unclear whether all responsive documents have now been produced. At the hearing, the parties must explain whether all responsive documents have been produced. If not, Defendant is ordered to do so.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiffs’ First Set of Request for Production of Documents to Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. is GRANTED, in part.

 

The court notes Plaintiffs also request sanctions in the amount of $2,385.00 (reduced amount requested in Reply). The court DENIES the request for sanctions.

Case Number: 24VECV02100    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: W

NAZELI DOBADZHYAN vs VENUS BANQUET, INC., et al.

 

demurrer to the first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 22, 2024                              Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02100

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Vagik Sarkisyan and Artour Ambartsoumyan

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Nazeli Dobadzhyan

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Fagerholm Decl. ¶¶2-4.)  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an employment action. Plaintiff alleges she was employed by Defendants as an exempt employee at their business. However, Plaintiff alleges she was misclassified as an Independent Contractor and during the last year of Plaintiff’s employment with Defendants, Plaintiff was not compensated at all, under the false promise that Plaintiff would become a “partner” in the business owned and operated by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges she was never paid her wages due and owed to her during her employment or upon termination.

 

On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff Dobadzhyan filed a First Amended Complaint against Defendants Venus Banquet, Inc., Vagik Sarkisyan, Artour Ambartsoumyan, and Edvin Baghumian for (1) Failure to Pay Wages; (2) Waiting Time Penalties (Labor Code §§ 201-203); (3) Failure to Provide Accurate Itemized Wage Statements (Labor Code § 226); (4) Failure to Timely Pay Wages Upon Termination (Labor Code § 201, 202, and 203); and (5) Unfair Competition (Bus & Prof Code § 17200 et seq.).

 

Request for entry of default was entered against Venus Banquet, Inc. and Edvin Baghumian on June 27, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants Vagik Sarkisyan and Artour Ambartsoumyan’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Vagik Sarkisyan and Artour Ambartsoumyan demur to the First Amended Complaint on the grounds all causes of action against Defendants are unintelligible and fails to state facts sufficient to support a cause of action against Defendants.

 

In¿a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co.¿(2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc.¿v.¿Accountants, Inc. Servs.¿(2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

 

Defendants demur to the amended complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs’ broad and overreaching allegations are not sufficient to state a claim under Labor Code section 558.1. Labor Code section 558.1 provides as follows: “Any employer or other person acting on behalf of an employer, who violates, or causes to be violated, any provision regulating minimum wages or hours and days of work in any order of the Industrial Welfare Commission, or violates, or causes to be violated, Sections 203, 226, 226.7, 1193.6, 1194, or 2802, may be held liable as the employer for such violation.” (Lab. Code § 558.1(a).) Defendants contend simply relying on Defendants Sarkisyan and Ambartsoumyan’s respective status as corporate officers is insufficient to state a claim under section 558.1. (See Espinoza v. Hepta Run, Inc. (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 44.) Moreover, Plaintiff alleges no facts bearing on Sarkisyan and Ambartsoumyan’s involvement in any violation of sections 201 and 203 and does not sufficiently allege a section 226 violation. Defendants also argue even if the violations are deemed to be adequately alleged by this court, Plaintiff’s description of Sarkisyan and Ambartsoumyan’s involvement therein is a mere conclusion of law.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues there are two theories of liability alleged against Defendants. Firstly, that Defendants are liable pursuant to Labor Code section 558.1 and secondly, that Defendants are the alter ego of Venus Banquet, Inc. Plaintiffs contend under Labor Code section 558.1, the FAC explicitly alleges that Defendants Sarkisyan, Ambartsoumyan, and Baghumian were all involved in the management and control of Venus Banquet, Inc., including decisions related to employee wages and misclassification. By approving of these wage practices, Plaintiffs contend they are liable under Section 558.1 for their direct actions, not merely due to their titles. These allegations, according to Plaintiff, allege way more than conclusions of law including specific allegations that Defendants failed to pay final wages upon termination, including earned commissions, and violated Sections 201 and 203 by willfully refusing to pay Plaintiff’s wages and failing to list total hours worked and proper pay rates in violation of Section 226. As for the alter ego allegations, Plaintiff argues the FAC sufficiently alleges facts supporting alter ego liability, demonstrating a unity of interest between the individual Defendants and Venus Banquet, Inc. (See FAC ¶¶10-14, 16-19.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts to support their claims for violation of labor laws against Defendants Vagik Sarkisyan and Artour Ambartsoumyan. As noted by Plaintiff, Defendants did not request judicial notice and is instead, challenging factual claims. Based on the amended complaint, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged Defendants could be liable for the violations of Labor Code Sections 201, 203 and 226. As noted by both parties, “in order to ‘cause’ a violation of the Labor Code, an individual must have engaged in some affirmative action beyond his or her status as an owner, officer or director of the corporation. However, that does not necessarily mean the individual must have had involvement in the day-to-day operations of the company, nor is it required the individual authored the challenged employment policies or specifically approved their implementation. But to be held personally liable he or she must have had some oversight of the company's operations or some influence on corporate policy that resulted in Labor Code violations.” (Espinoza, supra, 74 Cal.App.5th at p. 59.)

 

In the instant matter, Plaintiff has alleged more than just liability against Defendants as their position as owners, shareholders, officers, directors, and/or managing agents of Defendant Venus Banquet, Inc. Plaintiff has alleged Defendants aware that Plaintiff was improperly paid, not paid, and misclassified yet continued to approve the wage and hour violations concerning Plaintiff and collectively made decisions related to the employee management and payment of wages. This is sufficient of the purposes of a demurrer.  Moreover, as noted by Plaintiff, determining whether an owner violated, or caused to be violated, certain wage and hour laws within the meaning of Section 558.1 "cannot be determined by any bright line rule, as this inquiry requires an examination of the particular facts in light of the conduct, or lack thereof[.]” (Usher v. White (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 883, 897.) The court further notes the alter ego allegations sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer. (See FAC ¶¶10-14, 16-19.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants Vagik Sarkisyan and Artour Ambartsoumyan’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

 

 


Case Number: 24VECV03198    Hearing Date: October 22, 2024    Dept: W

YARIV SHOCHAT vs PORSCHE CARS NORTH AMERICA, INC., et al.

 

defendant ed carroll motor company, inc.’s motion to compel arbitration and stay further proceeding pending arbitration

 

Date of Hearing:        October 22, 2024                              Trial Date:       None

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV03198

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Ed Carroll Motor Company, Inc.  

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Yariv Shochat

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff Yariv Shochat filed a complaint against Defendants Porsche Cars North America, Inc., WHN, Inc. dba Porsche Woodland Hills, and Ed Carroll Motor Co., Inc. for violation of the Song-Beverly Act, negligent repair, violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act, violation of the Colorado Consumer Protection Act, fraudulent inducement – concealment and negligent misrepresentation.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Ed Carroll Motor Company, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Further Proceedings Pending Arbitration is DENIED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits objections to the declaration of Scott Allen. Objections 1 – 3 are OVERRULED.        

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Ed Carroll Motor Company, Inc. moves for an order compelling Plaintiff to submit his individual claims alleged in his First Amended Complaint against Defendant Ed Carroll to arbitration and stay the proceeding pending competition of arbitration. Defendants Porsche Cars North America, Inc. (“PCNA”) and WHN, Inc. dba Porsche Woodland Hills (“PWH”) joins in Defendant Ed Carroll’s motion to compel arbitration and stay action.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy . . . the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists,” unless the court finds that the right to compel arbitration has been “waived by the petitioner,” or that “grounds exist for recission” of the arbitration agreement. (CCP § 1281.2(a), (b).) 

 

A written arbitration agreement is “valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” (CCP § 1281.) “The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.) “The general rule is that only a party to an arbitration agreement may enforce it.” (Ronay Family Limited Partnership v. Tweed (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 830, 837.) 

 

Defendant Ed Carroll argues Plaintiff entered into an enforceable agreement to arbitrate all disputes arising from his purchase of the 2019 Porsche 911 GT3 RS, which he purchased from Defendant Ed Carroll. According to Defendant, the Agreement stated that all claims arising out of the Subject Vehicle’s sale must be sent to binding arbitration by a neutral arbitrator upon request by either party to the Agreement. Because Plaintiff’s claims arise out of defects to the Subject Vehicle, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s claims fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration provision of the Agreement signed, acknowledged, and understood by Plaintiff.

 

There is no dispute over the existence of the arbitration agreement or that Plaintiff signed the arbitration agreement. Rather, Plaintiff opposes the arbitration agreement on the grounds Defendant Ed Carroll fraudulently induced Plaintiff to enter into a purchase agreement with an arbitration clause and there is no arbitration agreement that covers Plaintiff's claims against Defendant Porsche NA and Defendant Porsche WHN, which as a result, could lead to a serious possibility of conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact.

 

Plaintiff first argues not only was Plaintiff fraudulently induced to enter into an arbitration agreement because Defendants engaged in fraudulent conduct that led Plaintiff to agree to the arbitration clause, but there was fraud at the inception and execution of making of the purchase agreement containing the arbitration agreement. Plaintiff contends Defendants’ fraudulent conduct regarding the condition and certification of the Subject Vehicle affected the foundation of Plaintiff’s agreement to purchase the vehicle and, by extension, the arbitration agreement that was part of the purchase. Specifically, Defendant assured that the vehicle was a Porsche NA Certified Pre-Owned vehicle meeting the high standards set by Defendant Porsche NA, which was a significant factor in Plaintiff’s decision to purchase the vehicle. Moreover, Defendant failed to disclose crucial information about the vehicle’s actual condition including the vehicle had been tracked for over 1,500 miles and that the vehicle had severe rock chip damage from track use.

 

The court finds the claims still arbitrable even if fraudulently induced in entering the arbitration agreement. Plaintiff has not alleged facts showing there was fraud in the inception or execution. However, Plaintiff has shown fraud in the inducement. "California law distinguishes between fraud in the “execution” or “inception” of a contract and fraud in the “inducement” of a contract. In brief, in the former case “ ‘the fraud goes to the inception or execution of the agreement, so that the promisor is deceived as to the nature of his act, and actually does not know what he is signing, or does not intend to enter into a contract at all, mutual assent is lacking, and [the contract] is void. In such a case it may be disregarded without the necessity of rescission.’ ” [Citation.] Fraud in the inducement, by contrast, occurs when “ ‘the promisor knows what he is signing but his consent is induced by fraud, mutual assent is present and a contract is formed, which, by reason of the fraud, is voidable. In order to escape from its obligations the aggrieved party must rescind....’ ” [Citation.]” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 415.)

 

Here, as stated by the court in Rosenthal, “[b]y entering into the arbitration agreement, the parties established their intent that disputes coming within the agreement's scope be determined by an arbitrator rather than a court; this contractual intent must be respected even with regard to claims of fraud in the inducement of the contract generally.” (Rosenthal, supra, 14 Cal.4th at p. 416.) Here, Plaintiff does not deny he entered the arbitration agreement with the intent that disputes arising out of the agreement would be arbitrated. Plaintiff merely contends he would not have entered the agreement had he known the issues with the Subject Vehicle.

 

Next, Plaintiff argues the motion to compel arbitration should be denied because there is no arbitration agreement governing the claims against Defendants Porsche NA and Porsche WHN and these Defendants cannot use estoppel or third-party beneficiary arguments to enforce arbitration. As such, if the court were to compel arbitration, there could be a possibility of inconsistent rulings. The court agrees. Under the Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, the Second District found that equitable estoppel nor third party beneficiary applied to manufacturers who were not parties to the sales contract. Similarly here, Plaintiff’s claims against Defendants Porsche NA and Porsche WHN do not rely or depend on the existence or terms of the Purchase Agreement and the agreement does not show consent to arbitrate claims with third party nonsignatories.

 

As a result, there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on between the claims. Given the possibility of conflicting rulings, the court finds that no claims should be sent to arbitration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2(c).

 

Accordingly, Defendant Ed Carroll Motor Company, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Further Proceedings Pending Arbitration is DENIED.
