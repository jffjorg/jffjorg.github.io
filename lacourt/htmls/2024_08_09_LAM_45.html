Case Number: 23STCV00314    Hearing Date: August 9, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

CAHUENGA PROFESSIONAL BUILDING, LLC, a California limited liability company; and NAREK KAZARIAN, an individual;,

 

                             Plaintiff,

EMIL AKOPIAN, an individual; DAVID ISSAIANS, an individual; EMIL AKOPIAN AND DAVID RAPHAEL ISSAIANS DBA ALLIANCE PARTNERS; FIRST CORE GROUP INC., a California corporation DBA KELLER WILLIAM REAL ESTATE SERVICES; 3623 CAHUENGA LLC, a California limited liability company; MING SZE FONG, an individual; STUDIO CITY CAREGIVERS, INC., a California corporation; MING SZE FONG DBA STUDIO CITY CAREGIVERS COOPERATIVE, INC., and

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed: 01/06/2023

[1st Amended Complaint Filed: 02/07/2024]

Trial Date: 06/23/2025

 

Hearing Date:            August 9, 2024

Moving Party:           Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc.

Responding Party:    Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional                                              Building, LLC

           

Motions:

 

1.      Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Narek Kazarian, Set 1

2.      Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC Set 1

 

Tentative Ruling: The Court considered the moving papers, oppositions, and replies. The Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Narek Kazarian, Set 1 filed by Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc. and the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC Set 1 are GRANTED. Additionally, the Court will impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC each, and award them to Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc. in the amount of $6,538.73 on Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and $6,538.73 Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC each. Code compliant responses and monetary sanctions are due within 15 days of this order.  

 

 

Background

            On January 6, 2023, Plaintiffs Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC and Narek Kazarian (“Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Emil Akopian; David Issaians; Emil Akopian and David Raphael Issaians dba Alliance Partners; First Core Group Inc. dba Keller William Real Estate Services; 3623 Cahuenga LLC; Ming Sze Fong; Studio Caregivers, Inc.; Ming Sze Fong dba Studio City Caregivers Cooperative, Inc. (“Defendants”); and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive.

            The motions now before the Court are (1) the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Narek Kazarian, Set 1 and (2) the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC Set 1 (the Motions). The Motions were filed by Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc. (Studio City). Plaintiff Narek Kazarian (Kazarian) opposes the Motion filed against them, and Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC (Cahuenga) opposes the Motion file separately against them (hereinafter, Kazarian and Cahuenga will be referred to as “Responding Parties”). Studio City files a reply to both opposition. Because the parties’ contentions surround the same issues in both Motions, both will be addressed here.

 

Discussion

Legal Standard

            The propounding party may bring motions to compel further responses to interrogatories or requests for production if it believes (1) the responses received are evasive, or (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a).) A respondent has the burden to justify objections in response to a motion filed to compel further responses. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  

            The court shall impose a monetary sanction…against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(d).)

 

Analysis

            The Parties Arguments

            In their moving papers, Studio City puts forth the Request for Production (RFPs) at issue are RFPs Nos. 1-16. On February 23, 2024, Studio City served Responding Parties with RFPs, and on April 5, 2024, Responding Parties served responses. (Declaration of Giandominic Vitiello, hereinafter Vitiello Decl., ¶¶3-5.) In those responses Responding Parties provided no documents, but solely objections. Studio City contends that these objections are unjustified, generalized, and boilerplate.

            Upon opposition, Responding Parties contend the Motion is moot because Responding Parties produced responsive documents on July 18, 2024. (Opposition Papers, 3:8-9.) Additionally, Responding Parties argue that although the objections based on attorney-work product and attorney-client privilege were asserted in each objection, the assertions are limited to communications between attorneys and their clients and not information that is responsive. (Opposition Papers, 3:17-19.) Finally, Responding Parties claim the objections based on documents being equally available and the requests being too broad are valid because there are certain communications that were exchanged between defendants that Responding Parties may not have and Responding Parties state that demands asking for “all” documents is not a demand that can be complied with because “Plaintiff cannot know all responsive documents until he is able to full conduct discovery in this matter.” (Opposition Papers, 4:4-5.)      

            Upon reply, Studio City argues the responses served on July 18, 2024 were unverified and contained many of the same meritless objections. As explained further below, the Court agrees, grants the instant Motion, and awards sanctions to Studio City.

            Responding Parties February 23, 2024 Responses

            As an initial matter, “Unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.” (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) Therefore, the July 18, 2024 responses are invalid and will not be addressed further.

            The primary inquiry therefore is whether the initial February 23, 2024 responses were code compliant, valid responses. The Court answers this inquiry in the negative. As argued by Studio City and as shown in Studio City’s Separate Statement supporting this Motion, the response to each and all of the 16 RFPs was the nearly identical, boilerplate objection. The objections are not specific nor detailed, but simply designed to encompass any and all objections that could be possible.

            For example, the objection based on an RFP being overbroad is made to each RFP, and no explanation for why the RFP is overbroad is provided. Indeed, many of the RFPs are specific for example, RFP No. 3 requests all documents regarding Responding Parties’ respective offers to purchase real property located at 3623, 3625, 3629, 3631 Cahuenga Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90068 (the Subject Property). Responding Parties fail to provide any justification for an objection based on overbreadth for this RFP. The same pattern persists through the majority of the RFPs, including RFP Nos. 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, & 9.    

            A second example is Responding Parties failing to provide explanation for how the RFPs invade attorney-client privilege. For example, RFP No. 4 requests documents between Responding Parties and Minge Fong, Studio City, and Studio City Caregivers Cooperative, Inc. However, attorney-client privilege is one of the basis for objection to RFP No. 4. “The attorney-client privilege, set forth at Evidence Code section 954, confers a privilege on the client ‘to refuse to disclose, and to prevent another from disclosing, a confidential communication between client and lawyer....’” (Costco Wholesale Corp. v. Superior Court, (2009) 47 Cal.4th 725, 740.) However, RFP No. 4 is asking for communications between Responding Parties and certain defendants. Responding Parties provide no explanation for how the attorney-client privilege applies here, and the Court sees none.

            One final example is Responding Parties’ arguments that the documents requested are equally as available to Studio City as they are to Responding Parties. RFP Nos. 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, and 9 all ask for documents regarding communications between Kazarian and other defendants that are not Studio City. Again, Responding Parties provide no explanation for how this objection is relevant.

Sanctions

            Defense counsel provides the following calculations inclusive of both Motions:

·         Counsel’s hourly rate is $550.00

·         Counsel’s office support staff rate is $185.00

·         Counsel states that their office expended 19.83 hours of attorney time and 2.0 hours of support staff time

·         Counsel incurred a $60.00 filing fee for each Motion

·         Counsel incurred a cost of $30.96 in mailing meet and confer correspondence

·         Counsel totaled the incurred fees and costs at $11,248.54[1]. Counsel additionally requests $2751.46 across both Motions for reviewing opposition papers, submitting reply papers, and attending the hearing. However, it is unclear how Counsel calculated the additional $2,751.46.

·         This amounts to a total of $14,000 in sanctions for both Motions, which equates to $7,000 for each Motion.

 

            Noting arithmetical error in the above calculations, the Court will calculate the sanctions as follows:

·         $550.00 attorney billing rate x 19.83 hours = $10,906.5

·         $185 support staff billing rate x 2.0 hours = $370.00

·         $60.00 filing fee x 2 Motions = $120.00

·         Add $30.96 in mailing costs for meet and confer correspondence = $30.96

·         1 hour reviewing opposition papers, plus 1 hour drafting reply papers, plus 1 hour for attending the hearing, all at Counsel’s $550.00 billing rate = $1,650

·         Therefore, the total amount of sanctions across both Motions amounts to $13,077.46. Therefore, the amount of monetary sanctions imposed on Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC each is $6,538.73.  

Conclusion

            The Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Narek Kazarian, Set 1 filed by Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc. and the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents to Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC Set 1 are GRANTED. Additionally, the Court will impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC, and award them to Defendant Studio City Caregivers, Inc. in the amount of $6,538.73 on Plaintiff Narek Kazarian and $6,538.73 Plaintiff Cahuenga Professional Building, LLC each. Code compliant responses and monetary sanctions are due within 15 days of this order.  

 

            It is so ordered.

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed: 08/02/2023

[1st Amended Complaint Filed: 11/08/2023]

Trial Date: 06/30/2025    

 

Hearing Date:            August 9, 2024

Moving Party:           Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Hassan Mohammadkhanighiy Asvand

Responding Party:    Defendant/Cross-Complainant Alen Manookian

           

Motions: Cross-Defendant Hassan Mohammadkhanighy Asvand’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint

Tentative Ruling: The Court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Cross-Defendant Hassan Mohammadkhanighy Asvand’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is OVERRULED.

Background

Factual and Procedural Background

            On August 2, 2023, Ravand Construction, Inc. (Construction) and Hassan Mohammadkhanighiy Asvand aka Ramin Ghiyasvand (RG) filed a Complaint against Alen Air Systems (AAS) and Alen Manookian (Manookian). The Complaint alleged six causes of action based on the alleged failure to repay three separate loans made by Construction and RG as creditors to Alen Air Systems and Manookian as debtors. On November 8, 2023, a Cross-Complaint (XC) was filed by AAS and Manookian against RG alleging three causes of action: (1) assault, (2) battery, and (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress. The XC alleges that on May 31, 2023, RG attacked Manookian with a box cutter while the two were in RG’s vehicle. (XC, ¶¶10-15.) Manookian suffered a slashing injury and a broken nose. (Id. at ¶15.) The XC additionally alleges that RG was criminally charged and has a criminal case currently pending. (Id. at ¶16.)

            The motion before the Court is RG’s demurrer to the XC. RG demurs solely to the first cause of action for assault. Manookian opposes the demurrer, RG files a reply.  

Meet and Confer

            “Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a).) There is no indication that any meet and confer efforts took place, therefore the requirements under Code Civ. Proc. §430.14(a) remain unsatisfied. However, per Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a)(4), “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” Therefore, the Court will turn its attention to the demurrer.    

 

Discussion

Legal Standard

            “[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Analysis

            “The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed [he or] she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.)

            Upon demurrer, RG argues that at no time could Manookian “have reasonably [believed] he was about to be touched since the subsequent alleged touching came suddenly and without warning.” (Opposition Papers, 6:5-9.) The Court is unpersuaded. Manookian has properly pled the elements for assault sufficient to survive demurrer.

            Manookian alleges that RG was traveling on the freeway “at a high rate of speed of about 80 to 90 MPH” (XC, ¶10) all the while yelling at Manookian that he intended to kill them both. (Id.) The XC continues stating that RG “threatened to take Manookian to a place in the City of Lancaster, where he intended to kill Manookian.” (XC, ¶12.) Moreover, in addition to threatening to kill Manookian’s parents (XC, ¶13) RG was allegedly holding a box cutter with the blade out in his right hand. (XC, ¶14.) RG then allegedly struck Manookian causing a slashing injury and a broken nose. (XC, ¶15.)

            The XC alleges the first element of intent by alleging the threats and intention to kill Manookian. The second element is properly alleged in that Manookian, believing he was in danger, repeatedly requested that RG stop the vehicle and allow Manookian to leave. Third it is clear by the XC, that Manookian in no way consented to being touched, however, Manookian was then struck with the box cutter, thereby fulfilling the fourth element. Finally, the XC makes clear that RG’s actions were a substantial factor in causing the harm. Therefore, the demurrer to the first cause of action for assault is overruled.

 

Conclusion

            Accordingly, Cross-Defendant Hassan Mohammadkhanighy Asvand’s Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

            It is so ordered.

                             Plaintiff,

VERONIKA MIKHAYLOVA,

Action Filed: 08/29/2023

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Thuong Luong

Responding Party:      N/A - Unopposed

Motion:                      Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendant’s Answer

Tentative Ruling: The Court considered the moving papers. Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendant’s Answer is GRANTED.

 

Background

Factual and Procedural Background

            On August 29, 2023, Thuong Luong (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Veronika Mikhaylova (Defendant) alleging a single cause of action for breach of written contract. After the deadline for an answer had passed, Plaintiff filed a Request for an Entry of Default, which was entered on April 15, 2024. On May 21, 2024, Defendant filed an Answer.

            The motion now before the Court is Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendant’s Answer (the Motion). No opposition was filed.   

Meet and Confer

            “Before filing a motion to strike…the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc. §435.5.) Plaintiff presents no evidence that any meet and confer efforts took place. However, per Code Civ. Proc. §435.5(a)(4), “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to grant or deny the motion to strike.” Therefore, the Court will now turn its attention to the Motion.  

 

Request for Judicial Notice

            Accompanying the Motion, Plaintiff files a Request for Judicial Notice (RJN) and asks the following to be judicially noticed:

1.      The Clerk’s Notice of Voiding of Filing of Defendant Veronika Mikhaylova’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons filed on November 1, 2023.

2.      The substitution of attorney by Defendant’s attorney on March 21, 2024.

3.      Plaintiff’s Request for Entry of Default/Judgment of Defendant filed on April 15, 2024.

4.      The Court’s Minute Order for the hearing on Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons filed on April 16, 2024.

5.      Plaintiff’s Second Request for Entry of Default/Judgment of Defendant filed on May 16, 2024.

6.      The Declaration of Thuong Luong Pursuant to C.C.P. § 585 in Support of Entry of Default/Judgment of Defendant filed on May 16, 2024.

7.      Defendant’s Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint filed on May 21, 2024.

 

            The RJN is denied as to items two and six. Although permitted by Evid. Code § 452(d), “There is, however, a precondition to the taking of judicial notice in either its mandatory or permissive form—any matter to be judicially noticed must be relevant to a material issue.”  (People ex rel. Lockyer v. Shamrock Foods Co. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 415, 422 fn. 2.) However, items one, three, four, five, and seven are judicially noticed.

 

Discussion

Legal Standard

            The court may, upon motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.)

 

Analysis

            Plaintiff contends the Motion should be granted because Defendant’s Answer was filed after the entry of default on April 15, 2024. The entry of the default cuts off the

opportunity to plead, unless default is vacated. (6 Witkin, Cal. Proc. 6th PWT § 215 (2024). Also see Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 882.) As of this date, the Court does not possess a motion to vacate a default entry in its record filed by Defendant. Therefore, the filing of Defendant’s Answer is inappropriate, subject to a motion to strike, and shall be struck.

            The Court notes Plaintiff additionally requests that a default judgment be entered. A Motion to Strike is not the proper vehicle to request a default judgment. A default judgment packet has been submitted, and the Court will evaluate the submission to determine whether a default judgment is appropriate.

 

Conclusion

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike Defendant’s Answer is GRANTED.

 

            It is so ordered.
