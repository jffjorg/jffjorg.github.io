DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV08660    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing Date: October 21, 2024

Re: 21STCV08660 Kahlee Van Kirk, an Individual, vs Joaquin Reidiess, an Individual, et al.

 

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

Defendant Secure Property Management, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel nonparty Munish Lal, M.D. (“Dr. Lal”) to comply with a deposition subpoena for the production of business records. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records.  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2020.010.)  A deposition subpoena may command either: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent, (2) only the production of business records for copying, or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things.  (CCP § 2020.020.)   

 

A service of a deposition subpoena shall be affected a sufficient time in advance of the deposition to provide the deponent a reasonable opportunity to locate and produce any designated documents and, where personal attendance is commanded, a reasonable time to travel to the place of deposition.  (CCP § 2020.220(a).)  Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies. (CCP § 2020.220(c).)  A deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena may be punished for contempt without the necessity of a prior order of the court directing compliance by the witness. (CCP § 2020.240.) A motion to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena must be made within 60 days after completion of the deposition record, the date objections are served, or the date specified for production, and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP §2025.480, subd., (b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.) 

 

CCP § 1987.1(a) states: “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” 

  

CCP §1987.2(a) states, in relevant part, “. . . in making an order pursuant to motion made . . . under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification . . .” 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

Defendant served the subpoenas upon Dr. Lal on May 13, 2024. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 9; Exh. B.) On May 21 and May 29, 2024, Defendant followed up on their business records request via email. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 10; Exh. C.) Defendant again followed up on June 21, 2024. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 11; Exh. D.) After no response, Defendant sent Dr. Lal a meet and confer letter on June 25, 2024. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 12; Exh. E [meet and confer letter].) Dr. Lal did not respond. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 15.) Accordingly, the meet and confer requirement is met.

 

DISCUSSION

 

This case involves an alleged slip and fall on a staircase owned by Defendant. Plaintiff Kahlee Van Kirk (“Plaintiff”) alleges serious injuries as a result. On May 13, 2024, Defendant issued a deposition subpoena for the production of business records on Dr. Lal, who performed a MRI on Plaintiff in connection with their sustained injuries. (Ruiz Decl. ¶¶ 5 and 9.) The subpoena’s date of production was June 25, 2024.

 

Though Defendant contends they issued a valid subpoena, their only proof of service of is the business record of ABI Document Support Services (“ABI”), who was retained by Defendant to execute the subpoenas. (Ruiz Decl. ¶ 7; Exh. B.) This proof is insufficient. Defendant provides no other proof of personal service upon Dr. Lal.

 

Therefore, the motion is denied as procedurally defective.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Order Compelling Munish Lal, M.D. to Comply with Subpoena.

 

Defendant shall give notice of the Court’s order.

Re:                         21STCV11343 Carlos Burgos vs Arthur M. Danielian

 

 

Plaintiff Carlos Burgos’s Request for Default Judgment

 

Defendant: Arthur M. Danielian

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff Carlos Burgos’s request for default judgment is DENIED.

           

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a June 15, 2020, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiff Carlos Burgos (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendants Arthur M. Daniellian (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”), negligently operating their motor vehicle, struck Plaintiff, who was a pedestrian, and caused Plaintiff to sustain serious injuries.

 

On March 24, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging two causes of action for: (1) motor vehicle negligence; and (2) general negligence.

 

On October 27, 2023, default was entered against Defendant.

 

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendant.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff’s default judgment packet contains the following deficiencies:

 

1.      Plaintiff failed to serve Statement of Damages before default was entered. (CCP § 425.11(c) and (d).) In fact, Plaintiff did not file a Statement of Damages at all.

2.      Plaintiff failed to file Form CIV-100. (CRC Rule 3.1800(a).)

3.       Plaintiff fails to provide documentary evidence to support amount of damages sought.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Alexander M. Danielian is DENIED. Plaintiff was put on notice at the last OSC on 8/30/24 that a third defective filing risks dismissal for failure to prosecute under CCP § 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h). As such, Defendant Arthur M. Danielian is dismissed without prejudice.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

 


Case Number: 21STCV28439    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: B

 

Hearing:             10/21/2024

Re:                         21STCV28439 Emma Leyson, a Minor by and Through Her Guardian Ad Litem Eduardo Leyson, vs Soulcycle, Inc., et al.

 

Plaintiff Emma Leyson’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury

 

Opposition: NONE

 

TENTATIVE RULING

           

Plaintiff’s Motion for Relief from Potential Waiver of Trial by Jury is GRANTED.

           

BACKGROUND

           

On August 2, 2021, Plaintiff Emma Leyson (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Soulcycle, Inc. (“Soulcycle”), Rebecca Ryan (“Ryan”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) general negligence; and (2) negligent hiring, retention, and supervision. Plaintiff alleges that, on September 15, 2019, Defendants negligently yanked Plaintiff’s foot from between the pedal and handlebar of Defendants’ stationary bicycle during an exercise class, causing Plaintiff to sustain severe injuries.

 

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for order granting relief from potential waiver of trial by jury.

 

On September 18, 2024, Soulcycle filed a joinder and notice of non-opposition to Plaintiff’s motion.

 

A jury trial is set for November 25, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The right to a trial by jury as declared by Section 16 of Article I of the California Constitution shall be preserved to the parties inviolate.”  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc., § 631(a).)  “In civil cases, a jury may only be waived pursuant to subdivision (f).”  (Ibid.)  CCP section 631(f) provides that a party waives trial by jury by (1) failing to appear at the trial; (2) written consent filed with the clerk or judge; (3) oral consent, in open court, entered in the minutes; (4) failing to announce that a jury is required, at the time the cause is first set for trial, if it is set upon notice of stipulation, or within five days after notice of setting if it is set without notice or stipulation; (5) failing to timely pay the fee described in subdivision (b), unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee; or (6) failing to deposit with the clerk or judge, at the beginning of the second and each succeeding day’s session, the sum provided in subdivision (e).  (Ibid., § 631(f).) “The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.”  (Ibid., § 631(g).)  “The trial court should grant a motion for relief of a jury waiver ‘unless, and except, where granting such a motion would work serious hardship to the objecting party.’”  (Mackovska v. Viewcrest Road Properties LLC (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1, 10 [quoting Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 806, 809].)  “When there is doubt about whether to grant relief from a jury trial waiver, the court must resolve that doubt in favor of the party seeking a jury trial.”  (Ibid.)  

 

“In a motion for relief from waiver of a jury trial, the crucial question is whether the party opposing relief will suffer any prejudice if the court grants relief.”  (Mackovska, supra, 40 Cal.App.5th at 10.)  “‘The prejudice which must be shown from granting relief from the waiver is prejudice from the granting of relief and not prejudice from the jury trial.’” (Ibid. [quoting Massie v. AAR Western Skyways, Inc. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 405, 411].) “‘The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.’” (Ibid. [quoting Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811].) “Denying relief where the party opposing the motion for relief has not shown prejudice is an abuse of discretion.” (Ibid.)  

 

ANALYSIS

 

This action was commenced on August 2, 2021. Therefore, jury fees were required to be posted no later than August 2, 2022. (CCP § 631(c)(2)[requiring that, if no initial case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, fees are due no later than 365 days after filing the initial complaint].) Here, Plaintiff posted jury fees on April 25, 2023, about eight months past the statutory deadline.

 

Plaintiff does not proffer a reason for the late posting of jury fees. However, while delayed posting of jury fees constitutes waiver of trial by jury, it is “not a sufficient reason for denying relief from waiver.” (TriCoast Builders, Inc. v. Fonnegra (2024) 15 Cal.App.5th 766, 782.) Therefore, the Court accepts Plaintiff’s late posting for the purposes of CCP § 631(f).

 

Plaintiff argues that granting this motion will not pose undue hardship on Defendants. Plaintiff claims that all parties have been preparing for a jury trial. (Molchan Decl. ¶ 3.) Both Defendants and Plaintiff have made jury trial demands and posted fees. Furthermore, Plaintiff notes that trial in this matter is months away. The Court also notes that Soulcycle joined in this motion.

 

Given the Court’s liberal policy of granting relief from waiver of trial by jury, as well as Defendants’ lack of opposition, Plaintiff’s motion for relief is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff’s motion for relief from potential waiver of trial by jury is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On June 7, 2022, Plaintiff Tracy Lynn Kamar (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Parking Concepts, Inc. (“Parking Concepts”), and Does 1 to 25 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging two causes of action: (1) general negligence; and (2) premises liability. The complaint arises from a February 25, 2022, incident where Plaintiff allegedly sustained serious injuries after tripping over Defendant’s negligently placed parking block. 

On April 21, 2023, Parking Concepts filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint. 

On June 5, 2024, the matter was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024. 

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

On October 14, 2024, Parking Concepts filed their reply. 

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            Parking Concepts moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the following grounds: (1) the alleged dangerous condition was so open, obvious, and avoidable that Parking Concepts had no duty to warn or remedy the condition; and (2) Parking Concepts had no notice of a dangerous condition. Therefore, Parking Concepts argues that they do not owe Plaintiff a duty as a matter of law. (Notice of Motion, p. 2: 6-10.) 

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.  (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)  “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) “The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections 

 

Parking Concepts objects to the ASTM 1637-19 Standard, Exhibits C through H of Philip Rosescu’s Declaration, the National Safety Council Data Sheet #597, and the Institute of Transportation Engineers – Traffic Engineering Handbook. These objections are not properly raised in a separate evidentiary objections memorandum and are therefore overruled. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1354(b).) 

Defendant’s Burden 

Parking Concepts meets their initial burden. 

Parking Concepts moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the following grounds: (1) the alleged dangerous condition was so open, obvious, and avoidable that Parking Concepts had no duty to warn or remedy the condition; and (2) Parking Concepts had no notice of a dangerous condition. Therefore, Parking Concepts argues, they did not owe Plaintiff a duty as a matter of law. (Notice of Motion, p. 2: 6-10.) 

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence:¿duty, breach, causation, and damages.¿ (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.)¿Those who own, possess, or control property generally have a duty to exercise ordinary care in managing the property to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.¿(Annocki v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)¿If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition reasonably safe for [customers’] use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.”¿(Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)¿The existence and scope of a property owner’s duty are legal questions for the court.¿(Annocki, supra, 232 Cal.App.4th¿ at p. 36.)¿ 

Because the owner is not the insurer of the visitor’s personal safety . . . the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to establishing its liability.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.) “[W]here the plaintiff relies on the failure to correct a dangerous condition to prove the owner’s negligence, the plaintiff has the burden of showing that the owner had notice of the defect in sufficient time to correct it.” (Ibid.) The party asserting the cause of action has the burden to prove that the owner had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to correct it.¿ (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th at 1203, 1206.) To establish liability for negligence, “[t]here must be some evidence . . . to support the conclusion that the condition had existed long enough for the proprietor, in the exercise of reasonable care, to have discovered and remedied it.”¿(Girvetz v. Boys’ Market (1949) 91 Cal.App.2d 827, 829; Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1206.) 

A property owner owes no further duty to make a condition reasonably safe or give an adequate warning of a condition when the danger of the condition is so obvious that the condition itself serves as a warning.¿(Kinsman v. Unocal Corp (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 673.) “[I]f the danger is so obvious that a person could reasonably be expected to see it, the condition itself serves as a warning, and the landowner is under no further duty unless harm was foreseeable despite the obvious nature of the danger.”¿(Danieley v. Goldmine Ski Associates, Inc. (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 111, 122.) Whether a condition is obvious and whether the obviousness negates a property owner’s duty of care are legal questions for the court.¿(See Jacobs v. Coldwell Banker Brokerage Co. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 438, 446-450 [affirming a trial court’s granting of summary judgment based on a finding that a dangerous condition was obvious].)¿ 

The obvious character of the condition does not negate a property owner’s duty of care to remedy a conspicuous danger when it is foreseeable that a person will encounter the danger despite the danger’s obviousness. (Martinez v. Chippeqa Enterprises, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1179, 1185 [indicating a dangerous condition encountered on a sole access way from the street to the defendant’s building would not negate the defendant’s duty of care owed to the plaintiff]; see also Osborn v. Mission Ready Mix (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 104, 123 [finding an employee did not assume the risk of an obvious danger when the employee’s employment required him to encounter a dangerous condition].)  

Plaintiff alleges that she tripped over a parking block in a garage owned by Parking Concepts. (Parking Concepts Separate Statement of Facts “PC-SSF” No. 1; Whitfield Decl. ¶ 4 Exh. 7 [Plaintiff’s Complaint].) The subject parking block was in a disabled-accessible parking stall. (Ibid. at No. 2; Exh. 2 [photograph of subject parking block].) At the time of the underlying incident, there was no car in the subject stall. (PC-SSF No. 3; Exh. 6 p. 5:26-28, p. 6:1-2.) The subject stall is outlined with 4-inch wide blue stripes and contains the painted disabled logo as required by California Building Code, Title 24, Chapter 11B. (PC-SSF No. 5; Exh. 1 [photo of parking stall].) The subject parking block is painted blue and set 26 inches from the right front inside stripe of the stall. (PC-SSF No. 8; Exhs. 1 and 2.) Between the front of the subject stall and the parking garage wall is a 6-foot wide, blue-striped access route to the garage’s exit. (PC-SSF No. 9; Exh. 1.) 

Parking Concepts alleges that these facts show that the subject parking block was “open and obvious.” Further, Plaintiff’s fall was captured on security video, which shows Plaintiff had a plain view of the parking block. (PC-SSF No. 12; Exh. 3 [notice of lodging of video]; Espana Decl. ¶ 2 [laying foundation for security video].) The footage shows Plaintiff walking directly towards the parking block. (PC-SSF No. 15; Exh. 3; Espana Decl. ¶ 2; Wolfe Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff turns around to look behind her, and when she turns back around, she proceeds to trip over the parking block. (PC-SSF No. 17; Exh. 3; Espana Decl. ¶ 2; Wolfe Decl. ¶ 6.) No obstacles prevented Plaintiff from walking around the parking block. (PC-SSF No. 20; Exh. 3; Espana Decl. ¶ 2; Wolfe Decl. ¶ 6.) Furthermore, Plaintiff was familiar with the parking garage as her parking pass data shows she had used her pass on at least 65 separate days prior to her fall. (PC-SSF No. 21; Exh. 4 [entry log]; Espana Decl. ¶ 3.) Prior to Plaintiff’s injury, Parking Concepts had no reports of any incidents involving the subject parking block. (PC-SSF No. 22; Espana Decl. ¶ 5.) 

                Parking Concepts has set forth sufficient evidence showing that the subject parking block was “so obvious that the [block] itself serve[d] as a warning.” Kinsman v. Unocal Corp (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 673.) A reasonable person could have been expected to see the parking block. Additionally, Parking Concepts provided evidence showing that it had no notice or prior reports of the parking block’s dangerous condition. 

                Accordingly, Parking Concepts meets its initial burden. The burden now shifts to Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff’s Burden 

Plaintiff argues that (1) the parking block’s dangerous condition was not open and obvious and (2) Parking Concepts had notice of the parking block’s dangerous condition. 

First, Plaintiff argues that the parking block’s dangerous condition was not open and obvious. Plaintiff argues that, at most, only three seconds elapsed between when she looked back and when she tripped over the parking block. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts “P-SSF” No. 35; Exh. 3 [security video].) She argues that, up to the eight second mark, she did not see anything on the ground in front of her because the blue parking block blended in with the blue driver side limit line and access aisle marking. (P-SSF No. 37; Ehx. 4 [Kamar Decl. ¶ 10].) As a result, Plaintiff argues the wheel stop was not open and obvious. However, upon review of the evidence, the Court finds that the brightly colored wheel stop stands out from the grey cement of the parking stall. Plaintiff has failed to meet her burden on this ground. 

Next, Plaintiff argues that Parking Concepts had notice of the parking block’s dangerous condition. Parking Concepts conducted monthly inspections or walkthroughs of the premises. (P-SSF No. 40; Exh. 2 [Alvarez Depo. 17:10-21, 48:6-19].) The subject parking block had been in its location since at least 2018. (P-SSF No. 41; Exh. 2 [Alvarez Depo. 43:5-21].) All the parking blocks at the subject parking garage are off-center. (P-SSF No. 43; Exh. 2 [Alvarez Depo. 44:17-24].) Philip Rosescu (“Rosescu”), Plaintiff’s retained safety and liability expert, is a licensed professional civil engineer. (P-SSF No. 44; Exh. 3 [Rosescu Decl. ¶ 2].) Currently Rosescu is a forensic engineer employed by Aperture, a forensic engineering firm. (Rosescu Decl. ¶ 3.) In the past 15 years, Rosescu has analyzed over 15,000 accidents. (Ibid.) Rosescu provides that industry standards discourage the use of parking blocks as they cause pedestrians to fall. (Rosescu Decl. ¶ 10; Exh. 3E [National Safety Data Council Sheet #597].) Parking blocks should be inset from the limit lines of the parking stall to assure that there is a free pedestrian walkway between cars. (Exh. 3H [Charles E. Turnbow, Slip and Fall Practice].) If a parking block extends past the vehicle, pedestrians are likely to trip on them. (Ibid.) Further, it is industry standard to place parking blocks within the center of parking stalls. (Exh. 3C [ASTM 1637-19, Section 9.4].) Here, it is undisputed that the left side of the parking block abuts the driver side limit of the parking stall. (P-SSF Exh. 2 [Alvarez Depo. 42:21-44].) Therefore, upon Parking Concepts’s monthly inspections of the premises, and in light of industry standards, Parking Concepts knew or should have known of the subject parking block’s dangerous positioning. 

On summary judgment, the opposing party’s affidavits or declarations are liberally construed. (Corwin v. Los Angeles Newspaper Service Bureau, Inc. (1971) 4 Cal.3d 842, 851-852.) Therefore, Plaintiff meets her burden with evidence showing a triable issue of material fact as to whether Parking Concepts knew or should have known of the subject block’s dangerous condition. 

 

V.       CONCLUSION

             A triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Parking Concepts knew or should have known of the subject parking block’s dangerous condition. Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED. 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.
