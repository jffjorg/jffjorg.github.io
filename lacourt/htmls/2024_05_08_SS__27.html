DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV41114    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Hearing Date: 5/8/24

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV41114 FELIPE LARA PENA vs ALVIN BRUCE FERGUSON, INC.

Moving Party: Defendant ALVIN BRUCE FERGUSON

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED

 

Background

 

From June 2021 to October 2021, Plaintiff, through Michael Milburn (“Millburn”), a claims negotiator for Plaintiff’s attorney, Joan M. Lauricella (“Lauricella”), and Bruce Anderson (“Anderson”), Defendant's insurance adjuster, engaged in communications relating to the settlement of the present case. The parties exchanged several communications, and on October 17, 2021, Milburn and Anderson held a phone conversation during which they allegedly settled the case for $125,000. The following day, Milburn sent an email confirming the settlement. However, Plaintiff allegedly disagreed with the net amount of the settlement and refused to sign the release.

 

On October 27, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present case, and Defendant filed an answer alleging a defense that the suit is barred because the case had been settled. Defendant now moves for summary judgment to enforce the settlement allegedly reached on October 17, 2021.

 

Legal Standard 

Summary judgment is an available remedy for enforcing settlement agreements and establishing a bar to claims. (See Kilpatrick v. Beebe (1990) 219 Cal.App.3d 1527.) Stewart v. Preston Pipeline Inc. (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1565, 1570 [A motion for summary judgment based on the affirmative defense that a settlement agreement was entered into is one method to enforce a settlement agreement other than a motion to enforce settlement agreement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6]

Defendant’s answer filed on August 24, 2022, included a twenty-first affirmative defense stating, "That Plaintiff’s lawsuit against this responding party is barred because Plaintiff completely and unequivocally resolved, compromised, or otherwise settled all such claims set forth in the Complaint in their entirety against this answering Defendant." Given that Defendant has raised the affirmative defense of a settlement having been entered into, moving for summary judgment is appropriate.

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). "When the motion for summary judgment is supported by affidavits sufficient to sustain the motion,…the burden of showing that triable issues exist shifts to the party opposing the motion. [Citation.] A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.) 

As for summary judgment to enforce a settlement agreement, defendants had the burden of showing that a cause of action had no merit by demonstrating that there was a complete defense to that cause of action…. A settlement agreement is a contract, and the legal principles that apply to contracts generally apply to settlement contracts….Its validity is thus judged by the same legal principles applicable to contracts generally….Defendants therefore had the burden of establishing each contractual element—[1] parties who are capable of entering into contract, [2] their mutual consent, [3] a lawful object, and [4] sufficient cause or consideration. (Stewart v. Preston Pipeline Inc., (2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1565, 1586.)

 

Analysis

 

1.  Defendant’s Initial Burden

 

Defendant has met its initial burden of demonstrating that a settlement agreement was reached between the parties. Milburn, negotiator for Lauricella, who was and currently is Plaintiff’s attorney, engaged in negotiation talks with Anderson, claims adjuster for Defendant’s insured, to settle the present case for Plaintiff. On August 31, 2021, Milburn communicated that Plaintiff had granted new settlement authority of $150,000.00 in exchange for a duly executed Release of any and all medical and liability claims against Defendant. On October 1, 2021, Milburn responded to Defendant’s offer of $120,000, stating, “We will get back to you as soon as we hear back from our client.” (Ex. H to Anderson Decl.[1]). On October 8, 2021, Milburn indicated that the client was not satisfied with your offer of $120,000, continuing with, “we do know he will accept $135,000, that we do know!” (Ex. I). On October 27, 2021, Anderson and Milburn spoke on the phone, during which conversation the parties agreed to settle the claim for $125,000, as detailed in Anderson’s declaration (Anderson Dec., Paragraph 23). On October 28, 2021, at 11:30 a.m., Milburn sent an email to Anderson confirming the settlement by stating, “I'm delighted to confirm by way of this email that we have reached an agreement to settle Mr. Lara's personal injury claim for a total sum of $125,000.00” (Ex. J).

 

Through the course of these negotiations and the email confirming the settlement at $125,000, Defendant has met its initial burden that a settlement agreement, involving a payment of $125,000 in total gross settlement in exchange for a full release, was entered into between the parties.

 

2.  Actual and Apparent Authority

 

The crux of Plaintiff’s argument is that Milburn did not have apparent or actual authority to settle the case on his behalf. “[T]he law is well settled that an attorney must be specifically authorized to settle and compromise a claim, that merely on the basis of his employment he has no implied or ostensible authority to bind his client to a compromise settlement of pending litigation.” (Blanton v. Womancare, Inc., supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 404, quoting Whittier Union High Sch. Dist. v. Superior Court (1977) 66 Cal. App. 3d 504, 508.)  “The client as principal is bound by the acts of the attorney-agent within the scope of his actual authority (express or implied) or his apparent or ostensible authority; or by unauthorized acts ratified by the client.” (Blanton v. Womancare, Inc. (1985) 38 Cal. 3d 396, 403.)

 

Actual authority stems from the conduct of the principal which causes the agent reasonably to believe that the principal has consented to the agents act. Ostensible authority stems from the conduct of the principal which leads the third party reasonably to believe that the agent is authorized to bind the principal. (Civ.Code, 2300, 2315, 2316, 2317; Tomerlin v. Canadian Indem. Co., 61 Cal.2d 638, 643; South Sacramento Drayage Co. v. Campbell Soup Co., 220 Cal.App.2d 851, 856.)

 

Apparent authority is not applicable in this situation because Defendant produced no evidence of any conduct from the principal (Plaintiff) that led the claims adjuster to reasonably believe that the agent (Milburn) was authorized to bind the principal. The only conduct or communication indicating potential authority came from Milburn himself and not from Plaintiff.

 

In Gaine v. Austin (1943) 58 Cal.App.2d 250, 261, the court found that an agent had apparent authority to sell a principal’s property because the principal had, on several occasions, instructed third parties to communicate and negotiate with the agent concerning the sale. In contrast, in the present case, Anderson, Defendant’s claims adjuster, did not have any direct communication or contact with the Plaintiff that could have led him to believe that Milburn had the apparent authority to settle this case.

 

However, the court finds that Defendant did establish its initial burden in demonstrating that Milburn had actual authority to settle the case for $125,000. Actual authority arises from the conduct of the principal which leads the attorney or agent reasonably to believe that the principal has consented to the agent's act. First, Plaintiff testified that he authorized his attorneys to engage in settlement negotiations on his behalf. (Plf's Depo at p.10). Second, Milburn's communications with Anderson relating to the offer and acceptance of the settlement was very explicit about the nature and extent of authorization from Plaintiff. For example, Milburn responded to Defendant's offer of $120,000, stating “we will get back to you as soon as we hear back from our client” (Ex. H) but “we do know [Plaintiff] will accept $135,000, that we do know!” (Ex. I.) Although the settlement of $125,000 was reached over the phone, the subsequent email confirmation from Milburn did not include any qualifying language suggesting that the settlement was tentative or subject to the client’s approval. Instead, the language of the email conveyed a sense of finality, stating “I'm delighted to confirm by way of this email that we have reached an agreement to settle Mr. Lara's personal injury claim for a total sum of $125,000.00 ... It has been a distinct honor working with you on this claim and bringing about a mutually fair and agreeable outcome,” which typically indicates that the settlement has been finalized and approved by the client. Furthermore, a subsequent email suggests that the reason why Plaintiff wanted to back out of the settlement is because he was unsatisfied with the net amount he would receive after deductions for costs and attorney's fees. (Ex. L.) The Court finds that Defendant has met its initial burden in demonstrating that Milburn had actual authority to enter into the agreement for $125,000.

 

1.  Triable Issue of Fact

 Defendant failed to raise a triable issue of fact that Milburn did not possess actual authority to settle this case for $125,000. Plaintiff’s evidence consists of two declarations, one from Milburn and another from Plaintiff’s attorney, Lauricella. Notably, no declaration was submitted by Plaintiff himself. When questioned whether he gave Milburn authority to settle the case for $125,000, Plaintiff testified that he does not recall. 

 

Milburn declared that he, along with a Spanish-speaking staff member, had a conversation with Plaintiff where he allegedly received authority to settle the case for $135,000 (Milburn Decl. ¶ 3). Milburn recalls instructing the Spanish-speaking staff member to convey the offer of $125,000 to Plaintiff. However, Milburn did not specify what Plaintiff’s response was to the conveyance of an offer for $125,000. (Milburn Decl. ¶ 4.)

 

Milburn then states that the settlement for $125,000 was tentative, a statement contradicted by his emails. Milburn’s email prior to October 17, 2021, included language reflecting that any settlement was tentative (or, more accurately, conditional): "we will get back to you as soon as we hear back from our client"; and "if we can obtain client’s approval" (Ex. H). In contrast, Milburn’s confirmation email relating to the $125,000 settlement states, "I'm delighted to confirm by way of this email that we have reached an agreement to settle Mr. Lara's personal injury claim for a total sum of $125,000.00… Lastly, it has been a distinct honor working with you on this claim and bringing about a mutually fair and agreeable outcome." Nothing in the email suggests that the settlement was tentative or conditioned upon Plaintiff’s approval as Milburn had explicitly stated in his previous emails. Milburn further declared that he discussed the settlement offer again with Mr. Pena, at which time Mr. Pena did not agree to accept the $125,000.00. What is missing from Milburn’s declaration is a statement that Plaintiff did not authorize him to enter into the settlement when he informed Plaintiff of the $125,000 settlement the first time, prior to October 17, 2021. Further, Plaintiff did not submit a declaration from the Spanish translator who was present when Milburn initially communicated the $125,000 settlement to Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff’s other piece of evidence is the declaration of Lauricella, who states that " Based on review of the file notes, my office only had authority from Mr. Pena to settle his claim for $135,000; Prior to the emails between Mr. Milburn and Mr. Anderson, I personally did not communicate a $125,000 settlement offer to Mr. Pena; and Mr. Pena never gave me authority to settle his claim for $125,000; Additionally, there are no notes, emails, or documents evidencing that my employee, Mr. Milburn, had authority to settle Mr. Pena's claim for $125,000, or that any other staff member had communicated the $125,000 offer to Mr. Pena.” (Lauricella Decl. ¶4-6.) Lauricella’s declaration essentially states that she could not find any documentation that Milburn had authority from Plaintiff to settle the case for $125,000. However, to raise a triable issue of fact, Plaintiff must provide more than just a statement that there are no documents supporting Defendant's position. Plaintiff must introduce affirmative evidence. While the opposing party may attack the moving party's evidence, such as on the basis of credibility or the sufficiency of the evidence, more is required to defeat the motion or raise a triable issue of fact. CCP § 437c(e). Moreover, the lack of documentation is understandable if the offer was conveyed to Plaintiff during a meeting or through a phone call. Lauricella’s statement that “Mr. Milburn mistakenly believed he had Mr. Pena’s authority to settle the action for $125,000” is speculative unless she lays a proper foundation.

 

The essential problem with Plaintiff's evidence is twofold. First, there is no documentation indicating that Milburn did or did not have actual authority to accept the $125,000 settlement prior to October 17, 2021. Second, knowledge of the communication where the $125,000 settlement was conveyed to Plaintiff prior to October 17, 2021 is limited to only three individuals: Plaintiff, Milburn and a Spanish translator. Plaintiff himself testified that he does not recall if he gave Milner authorization. There is no declaration or testimony from the Spanish-speaking staff member. And, while Milburn confirms that he communicated the $125,000 offer to Plaintiff, he did not specify whether Plaintiff accepted or rejected the offer. As a result, Plaintiff failed to introduce evidence that raised a triable issue of fact for the jury to weigh on.

 

Thus, summary judgment is GRANTED.
