DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV39676    Hearing Date: January 23, 2025    Dept: 73

01/23/25

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. v. Senait Estifanos (21STCV39676)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:                Collection at Law, Inc.   

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:             N/A

 

Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment (filed 11/25/24)

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion to vacate dismissal and enter judgment pursuant to stipulation is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This is a collections case. On October 28, 2021, Plaintiff Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action by filing its Complaint against Defendant Senait Estifanos (“Defendant”) for credit card debt in the amount of $27,895.85.

 

On December 15, 2021, the Court approved the Stipulation for Entry of Judgment and Settlement and Release & Dismissal of Action With Consent to Court Retaining Jurisdiction Pursuant to C.C.P § 664.6 6 (“Stipulation”) submitted by Plaintiff and signed by the parties. The Court ordered Plaintiff’s Complaint dismissed without prejudice and stated that it retains jurisdiction to make orders to enforce any and all terms of settlement, including judgment, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6.

 

On November 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to vacate dismissal under C.C.P. § 664.6 6 & enter judgment pursuant to stipulation, arguing:

·                Defendant is in default and has failed to cure such default.

·                The Court should therefore vacate the dismissal and enter judgment pursuant to the Stipulation.

Defendant has failed to oppose the motion.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Vacate Dismissal and Enter Judgment 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides in part:

 

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

 

(b) For purposes of this section, a writing is signed by a party if it is signed by any of the following:

 

(1) The party.

 

(2) An attorney who represents the party. “(3) If the party is an insurer, an agent who is authorized in writing by the insurer to sign on the insurer’s behalf

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subds. (a), (b).)

 

B.      Dicussion

 

Plaintiff and Defendant stipulated that the Court would dismiss the action without prejudice. (Declaration of Ashley Mulhorn, ¶ 3; Ex. 1.) In return, Defendant agreed to make specified monthly payments to Plaintiff until Defendant paid the amount it owed Plaintiff ($27,895.85). (Id. at ¶ 4.)

 

Defendant made monthly payments totaling $11,160.00 but then defaulted on its payment obligation. (Id. at ¶ 5.) Plaintiff’s counsel sent a default payment letter to Defendant on May 30, 2024 advising Defendant that it was in default. (Id. at ¶ 6; Ex. 2.) Defendant has failed to bring its payments current. (Id. at ¶ 7.)

 

Because Defendant did not cure the default, Plaintiff asks the Court to enter judgment in Plaintiff’s favor for $16,735.85, plus $510.00 in costs, for a total judgment amount of $17,245.85. The Court grants the motion.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s motion to vacate dismissal and enter judgment. The Court vacates the December 15, 2021 dismissal and awards Plaintiff $16,735.85 against Defendant Senait Estifanos.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

II.            ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion for Terminating Sanctions

 

A misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (g).) A misuse of the discovery process may warrant the imposition of terminating sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) Terminating sanctions may be imposed by one of the following orders: (1) an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process; (2) an order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) an order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; and (4) an ordering rendering a judgment by default against that party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1)-(4).)  

 

Further, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. The court may also impose this sanction on one unsuccessfully asserting that another has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process, or on any attorney who advised that assertion, or on both. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).)  

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “[C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  (Ibid.) Where discovery violations are “willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid.)  A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) Where discovery sanctions are requested against a party, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Ibid.)   

 

“[A] sanction cannot go further than is necessary to accomplish the purpose of discovery.” (Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613.) “[S]anctions may not be imposed solely to punish the offending party.” (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1193.) “Furthermore, the sanction chosen should not provide a windfall for the other party, by putting the prevailing party in a better position than if he or she had obtained the discovery and it had been favorable.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff ignored multiple ACH or wire instructions, which resulted in a delay of the payment process. (Ginder Decl., ¶ 8.) Counsel states that the motion for terminating sanctions is moot. (Ginder Decl., ¶ 10.) Counsel states that Plaintiff has suffered no prejudice. (Ginder Decl., ¶ 15.)

 

D.     Appropriateness of Sanctions

 

The Court has reviewed Plaintiff’s discovery motions filed as to Defendant, and the Court agrees with Mr. Ginder that he was not served with any of those motions. The proofs of service as to Plaintiff’s discovery motions as to Defendant only show that Mr. Agrella was served with the motions on Defendant’s behalf. While Mr. Ginder was not served with the initial motions, the Court finds his co-counsel, Mr. Agrella, was served with the motions. Thus, Mr. Agrella should have been on notice as to Defendant’s discovery obligations.

 

However, Plaintiff did serve the Court’s notice of ruling as the Court’s September 12, September 13, and October 1, 2024 orders on both Mr. Agrella and Mr. Ginder. Irrespective of any lack of service of the motions on Mr. Ginder, the Court finds that both of Defendant’s attorneys were served with the notice of ruling as to the Court granting the motions to compel responses to Plaintiff’s special interrogatories and requests for production, as well as the court order deeming the RFAs as admitted. Thus, Defendant was aware of this Court’s orders as to such discovery and therefore was aware of its discovery obligations and obligation to pay monetary sanctions. As to the Court’s September 17, 2024 order concerning the form interrogatories, Mr. Agrella appeared at the hearing and notice was waived. (See 09/17/24 Minute Order at p. 4.) Thus, Defendant was also aware of its obligations to provide responses to Plaintiff’s form interrogatories and the payment of monetary sanctions pursuant to Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses thereto.

 

Given that counsel for Defendant had notice of the Court’s September 12, September 13, September 17, and October 1, 2024 orders, the Court finds that Defendant failed to comply with such orders. As to the RFA motion, while Defendant provided responses on September 9, 2024, which was prior to the hearing on such motion, Defendant did not pay monetary sanctions pursuant to any of the Court’s orders until January 10, 2025. Further, Defendant failed to comply with this Court’s September 13, September 17, and October 1, 2024 orders by failing to provide verified responses within 30 days of the date of each respective order. 

 

The Court also takes issue with the statement in the declaration of Mr. Ginder that the payment of sanctions was late due to the financial hardship of Mr. Agrella. (Ginder Decl., ¶ 7.) The Court questions why the financial hardship of Mr. Agrella is relevant given that none of the relevant court orders ordered Mr. Agrella to pay sanctions. In fact, the September 12, September 13, September 17, and October 1, 2024 orders only imposed sanctions against Defendant.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant’s failure to comply with this Court’s discovery orders issued on September 12, September 13, September 17, and October 1, 2024 was willful. It is apparent that Defendant provided untimely discovery responses despite court orders ordering Defendant to provide said responses within 30 days. Moreover, Defendant’s untimely payment of monetary sanctions also failed to comply with this Court’s orders.

 

However, the Court declines to impose terminating sanctions. Given that Defendant has, although belatedly, provided discovery to Plaintiff and has paid the outstanding monetary sanctions, the Court finds that imposing terminating sanctions against Defendant would amount to punishment and would provide a windfall to Plaintiff. Moreover, the Court must take an incremental approach to sanctions. While evidentiary or issue sanctions might have been appropriate, Plaintiff did not request such sanctions.

 

Critically, the Court notes that the notice of motion does not indicate which type of terminating sanction is sought. While Plaintiff indicates in the memorandum of points and authorities that she seeks an order striking Defendant’s answer (Mem. of Ps and As at p. 6:2-3), such request should have been identified in the notice of motion. “A request for sanction shall, in the notice of motion . . . specify the type of sanction sought.” (Sole Energy Co. v. Hodges (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 199, 207.) Given that terminating sanctions are not uniform in their application, Plaintiff should have set forth which type of terminating sanction she sought in the notice of motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1)-(4).)  

 

Plaintiff’s request for terminating sanctions is therefore DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

The Court, however, does find that monetary sanctions are appropriate. Defendant did not provide the requisite discovery and pay monetary sanctions in a timely manner despite court orders. The Court will only impose monetary sanctions against Defendant as the notice of motion does not identify which counsel of Defendant monetary sanctions are sought against. (Not. of Mot. at p. 2:1-2.) The Court is unsure whether Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Mr. Agrella, Mr. Ginder, or both.

 

Here, Defendant disobeyed multiple court orders pertaining to discovery. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,515.00 (which represents three hours of work on the motion and the $60 filing fee). The Court reduces sanctions given the straightforward nature of the motion. Monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,515.00 are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff by Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. within 30 days of the date of this order.

 

III.            DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions Against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. and Request for Monetary Sanctions.   

 

Plaintiff’s request for terminating sanctions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant Only Escrow, Inc. in the reduced amount of $1,515.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.

II.            ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion to Tax Costs

 

“Except as otherwise provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) A prevailing party is “the party with a net monetary recovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (a)(4).)

 

“The right to recover . . . costs is determined entirely by statute.” (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 71.) “In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court’s first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face.” (Ibid.) “If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show the costs to be unnecessary or unreasonable.” (Ibid., citation omitted.) Items not expressly allowed or prohibited by CCP § 1033.5 “may be allowed or denied in the court’s discretion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(4).) An award of costs is subject to the limitation that “[a]llowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).) Moreover, costs which are allowed “shall be reasonable in amount.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(3).)

 

“[I]tems on a verified cost bill are prima facie evidence the costs, expenses and services listed were necessarily incurred, and when they are properly challenged the burden of proof shifts to the party claiming them as costs.” (Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “[T]he burden is upon the moving party to establish the illegality of the challenged items; otherwise the amount demanded in the verified cost bill is controlling.” (Wilson v. Nichols (1942) 55 Cal.App.2d 678, 682-83.) “If the items appearing on a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.) “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ibid.) “[T]he mere filing of a motion to tax costs may be a proper objection to an item, the necessity of which appears doubtful, or which does not appear to be proper on its face.” (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 131, internal quotations omitted.)

 

B.      The Effect of the Pending Appeal

 

Initially, the Court will address Defendant HPC’s argument that the Court lacks jurisdiction to award costs given the pending appeal of the judgment. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 8:8-25.) Defendant HPC argues that the “proceeding seeking costs should be stayed pending the outcome of the forthcoming appeal.” (Memo. of Ps. and As at p. 4:24-25.)

 

“[T]he perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 189.) “Unless an undertaking is given, the perfecting of an appeal shall not stay enforcement of the judgment or order in the trial court if the judgment or order is for . . . (1) [m]oney or the payment of money, whether consisting of a special fund or not, and whether payable by the appellant or another party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 917.1, subd. (a)(1).)

 

“Under section 916, the trial court is divested of subject matter jurisdiction over any matter embraced in or affected by the appeal during the pendency of that appeal.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino, supra, 35 Cal.4th 180, 196-97.) “Thus, [the trial court] is without power to proceed further as to any matter embraced therein until the appeal is determined.” (Ibid.) Any proceedings “taken after [a] notice of appeal [is] filed are a nullity.” (Davis v. Thayer (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 892, 912.) “The purpose of the automatic stay under section 916 is to preserve the status quo until the appeal is decided . . . by maintaining the rights of the parties in the same condition they were before the order was made.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino, supra, 35 Cal.4th 180, 198.) “Otherwise, the trial court could render the appeal futile by altering the appealed judgment . . . by conducting other proceedings that may affect it.” (Ibid.) “The primary example of collateral matters not affected by a notice of appeal are awards of attorney fees or sanctions.” (United Grand Corp. v. Malibu Hillbillies, LLC (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 142, 161.)

 

Initially, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs that Defendant HPC’s request for a stay is procedurally deficient as it was not articulated in the notice of motion. “A notice of motion must state in the opening paragraph the nature of the order being sought and the grounds for issuance of the order.” (Cal. R. Ct., rule 3.1110(a).) The Court therefore DENIES Defendant HPC’s procedurally improper request for a stay.

 

Irrespective of the procedural deficiencies of Defendant HPC’s request for a stay, the Court is aware that both Defendants HPC and Maddux are appealing this Court’s entry of judgment. The Court, however, finds that Defendant HPC’s argument that the Court lacks jurisdiction to award costs at this juncture is not persuasive. The Court informs Defendant HPC that “filing a notice of appeal does not stay any proceedings to determine the matter of costs . . . .” (Korchemny v. Piterman (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1052.) Here, the judgment indicates that Plaintiffs shall recover their costs and disbursements. (11/07/24 Judgment at ¶ 4.) The Court finds that awarding costs is a collateral matter and does not deprive this Court of subject matter jurisdiction from determining Plaintiffs’ entitlement to certain costs. Defendant HPC has presented no applicable legal authority which stands for the proposition that a pending appeal prevents the trial court from awarding costs.

 

C.      The Cost Items at Issue

 

Filing and Motion Fees

 

Defendant HPC takes issue with the $1,268.72 of the $3,123.68 in filing and motion fees that is claimed in Plaintiffs’ memorandum of costs.

 

Initially, the Court notes that there is a three (3) cent discrepancy as the March 7, 2023 ex parte application for extension of time to serve was not $74.95 as claimed on the memorandum of costs but was rather $74.92 as set forth in the invoice attached to counsel’s declaration in opposition to the motion. (See Memorandum of Costs Worksheet at p.4; see also McCormick Decl., ¶ 11(a), Exh. A at p. 1.)

 

Filing and motion fees are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).) Here, Plaintiffs submitted a memorandum of costs which is prima facie evidence that the costs and expenses were necessarily incurred. Additionally, Plaintiffs have presented evidence of the costs incurred, and their counsel has attested to the necessity of the filing and motion fees. (McCormick Decl., ¶¶ 3, 4, 5, 13, 14; Exh. A.) Defendant HPC has not met its burden in challenging the reasonableness of such costs. Defendant HPC merely argues that certain cost items concerning filing and motion fees were not reasonably incurred but does not present any evidence on such point. In fact, the declaration of Brian M. Ledger in support of the motion is void of any statement that the costs incurred by Plaintiffs were unreasonable or were unnecessary to this litigation.  Defendant HPC has therefore not met its burden “of showing that an item is not properly chargeable or is unreasonable . . . .” (Nelson v. Anderson, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 132.) “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

Given the discrepancy in costs as to the March 7, 2023 ex parte application, the Court will tax three (3) cents from Plaintiffs’ costs for filing and motion fees. Thus, Defendant HPC’s request to tax filing and motion fees is GRANTED IN PART. Plaintiffs are awarded filing and motion fees in the sum of $3,123.65 instead of the claimed $3,123.68.

 

Deposition Costs

 

Defendant HPC contends that the Court should strike all $18,588.60 of Plaintiffs’ costs for depositions as such depositions were not reasonably necessary for litigation. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 6:18-19.)

 

“Taking, video recording, and transcribing necessary depositions, including an original and one copy of those taken by the claimant and one copy of depositions taken by the party against whom costs are allowed” are allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(3)(A).) “The burden of proof that the deposition was unnecessary or that the costs of taking the deposition were unreasonable is on the party seeking to have that item taxed or reduced.” (County of Kern v. Ginn (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 1107, 1113.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ counsel has attested to the necessity and reasonableness of the challenged deposition costs. (McCormick Decl., ¶¶ 6-10, 14.) Defendant HPC has not met its burden to challenge the reasonableness of such costs under County of Kern v. Ginn, supra, 146 Cal.App.3d 1107, 1113. Again, Defendant HPC merely argues, without setting forth any facts in the declaration of its counsel, as to the unreasonableness of such costs.

 

The Court therefore DENIES Defendant HPC’s request to strike and/or tax Plaintiffs’ deposition costs.

 

Service of Process Fees

 

Defendant HPC seeks to strike $5,690.95 in costs related to service of parties unrelated to Defendant HPC in this matter. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 7:14-15.)

 

Costs for service of process are recoverable. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(4).) “Ordinary witness fees pursuant to Section 68093 of the Government Code” are also recoverable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(7).)

 

The Court notes that the challenged service of process costs concern persons or entities that are not Defendant HPC. (Ledger Decl., Exh. B.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states that the subpoenas served on Hazard Management Consulting, South Coast Air Quality Management District, and the Workers Compensation Insurance Bureau were reasonably necessary as the documents received from such entities were reasonably necessary to the prosecution of Plaintiffs’ claims. (McCormick Decl., ¶ 12.)

 

Plaintiffs’ citation to Acosta v. SI Corp. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 1370 is inapposite as such case did not address the issue of imposing service costs on now-dismissed defendants onto the remaining defendants. The Court informs Plaintiffs that “[a] case is not authority for a proposition not considered therein or an issue not presented by its own particular facts.” (McConnell v. Advantest America, Inc. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 596, 611.) Thus, the Court fails to see how it is appropriate to impose service costs on Defendants HPC and Maddux for service on now-dismissed defendants and third party entities.

 

On the other hand, Defendant HPC cites to no legal authority standing for the proposition that “[i]t is unreasonable for Defendant to pay for the service of other persons or parties in this matter as those parties should be responsible for costs related to their own service of process fees.” (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 7:15-18.) The Court informs Defendant HPC that “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel has stated that the service of subpoenas on third-party entities and all costs for service of process were all reasonably necessary to the prosecution of Plaintiffs’ claims. (McCormick Decl., ¶¶ 12, 14; Exh. C.) Plaintiffs have also provided invoices to substantiate such costs. (Id.) Given that the memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence that the cost items were necessary incurred, and Plaintiffs’ counsel has attested that the costs for service of process were all reasonably necessary, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have substantiated such costs. Defendant HPC, however, offers no evidence that the costs for service of process are unreasonable or were unnecessary. As stated above, the declaration of Mr. Legder is severely deficient and states no facts attesting to unreasonableness or the lack of necessity of the challenged cost items.

 

The Court therefore DENIES Defendant HPC’s request to tax $5,690.95 in costs related to service of process.

 

Court Reporter Fees

 

Defendant HPC argues that the $1,500.00 in claimed court reporter fees should be stricken.

 

Court reporter fees as established by statute or allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(11).) Transcripts of court proceedings not ordered by the court are not allowable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(5).) Charges for court reporter fees, which are an entirely different expense than transcripts of court proceedings, are allowable. (Chaaban v. Wet Seal, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 49, 58-59.) 

 

The opposition brief does not specifically address the appropriateness of the costs claimed for court reporter fees. The Court takes such lack of response as a waiver that such costs are inappropriate and were not ordered by the Court under Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo, supra, 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215. Moreover, Plaintiffs have failed to provide any invoices to substantiate court reporter fees.

 

The Court therefore GRANTS Defendant HPC’s request to strike Item 12 from Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs for court reporter fees in the amount of $1,500.00.

 

III.            DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS IN PART Defendant HPC’s Motion to Tax Plaintiffs’ Costs. Plaintiffs are awarded costs in the sum of $29,265.45.

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc. filed the instant action against Defendant XMJ Fashion Inc. aka XMJ Fashion Inc alleging a cause of action for breach of promissory note.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about January 4, 2024, for a valuable consideration, Defendant made, executed and delivered to Plaintiff their promissory note in writing whereby Defendant agreed to pay to Plaintiff the sum of $919,783.60 under the terms and conditions and in the manner and installments provided therein. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant made payments in the total sum of $5,000.00, but failed to make any additional payments when due and owing pursuant to the terms of said note, leaving a balance due on said note of $914,783.60, the entire balance of which is now due, owing and unpaid, pursuant to the terms of said note with interest at the rate of ten percent (10 %) per annum from January 27, 2024. (Complaint, ¶¶ 9-10.)

 

On August 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Request for Entry of Default Judgment, seeking judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $979,761.98.

 

On October 11, 2024, the Court denied without prejudice Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment filed on August 30, 2024. Regarding Plaintiff’s request for $11,037.83 in attorneys fees, the Court stated it is unclear if Plaintiff is requesting attorney fees pursuant to Local Rule 3.214 or California Code Section 1717. The Court ordered Plaintiff to submit a new default judgment packet.

 

On October 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a new default judgement packet, seeking judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $995,827.33.

 

II.ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.)
