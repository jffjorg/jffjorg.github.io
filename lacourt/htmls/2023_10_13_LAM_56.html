DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 19STCP00482    Hearing Date: October 13, 2023    Dept: 56

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

CITIZENS FOR AMENDING PROPOSITION L, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

CITY OF POMONA, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 

      CASE NO.: 19STCP00482

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date:  October 13, 2023

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

Jury Trial: May 6, 2024

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:  Defendant/Cross-Complainant City of Pomona (the “City” or “Pomona”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

This action arises out of a dispute over the City’s regulation of billboards and was initiated by Plaintiffs on February 14, 2019.  The currently operative sixth amended complaint (the “6AC”) alleges: (1) declaratory relief, injunctive relief, and damages for deprivation of civil rights (First Amendment).

 

In relevant part, the 6AC alleges: In 1993, the City passed Proposition L, which imposed restrictions on the erection of new billboards in the City.  (See 6AC ¶ 18.)  Stephens, who owns and operates billboards, has been denied permits to construct billboards in the City.  (6AC ¶ 84.)  Despite Proposition L and the City’s refusal to allow Stephens to operate billboards, the City has allowed Real-Party-In-Interest Regency (“Regency”) to operate ten billboards (the “Regency Billboards”).  (6AC ¶ 85.) 

 

On May 5, 2023, the City filed a motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”) to the 6AC on the grounds that there are no triable issues of material fact regarding the first cause of action and the City is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  Specifically, the Motion argues that: (1) Plaintiffs’ claims are time-barred; and (2) the evidence demonstrates that Plaintiffs’ facial and as-applied constitutional challenges to Proposition L are meritless. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            The City’s Request for Judicial Notice is GRANTED as to the existence of the documents and their legal effect, but not to the truth of the matters stated therein.  (See Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400.)

 

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The City’s objections to the Declaration of J. Keith Stephens (“Stephens Decl.”) numbers 1, 3-7, and 9 are SUSTAINED.  The City’s objections to the Stephens Declaration numbers 2 and 8 are OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 437c, subdivision (c) requires the judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication meets the burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if the plaintiff has proved each element of the cause of action entitling them to judgment on that cause of action.  (CCP § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)  Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Courts liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of the opposing party.  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  The Court cannot weigh the credibility of witnesses or weigh other evidence, and where there is a conflict in declarations presented by the parties on material issues of fact or a conflict in the inferences to be drawn from that evidence, the Court must deny the motion.  (Binder v. Aetna Life. Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.)

 

The City’s Evidence

In support of the Motion, the City provides evidence that on or about June 24, 1993, the City and Regency entered into a development agreement (the “Development Agreement”) for Regency to operate the Regency Billboards.  (Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) 1.)  The Development Agreement governed the Regency Billboards’ operation for ten years with an option for an automatic extension for a second 10-year term.  (UMF 2.)  On November 2, 1993, Pomona voters adopted Proposition L, which provides that “[n]o new or structurally altered offsite billboards shall be permitted within the City of Pomona.”  (UMF 3.) 

 

The second term of the Development Agreement began in 2004 and had an expiration date of June 24, 2014.  (UMF 5.)  On July 7, 2014, the City adopted Ordinance No. 4190 to extend the Development Agreement for a third term.  (UMF 6.)  In 2018, an appellate court struck down Ordinance No. 4190 on the grounds that it violated Proposition L.  (UMF 7.)[1]  The City made Regency aware of its intention to remove the Regency Billboards beginning in 2019.  (UMF 8.)  Stephens and his affiliated entities have not submitted applications for permits to erect billboards since 2015.  (See UMF 11-14.) 

 

First Amendment Challenges

A challenge to the constitutional validity of a statute may be of two types: a facial challenge and an as-applied challenge.  (San Francisco Unified School Dist. v. City and County of San Francisco (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1070, 1079.)  A facial challenge to the constitutional validity of a statute or ordinance considers only the text of the measure itself, not its application to the particular circumstances of an individual.  (Id.)  To support a determination of facial unconstitutionality, petitioners must demonstrate that the act's provisions inevitably pose a present total and fatal conflict with applicable constitutional prohibitions.  (Id.)  In contrast, an as-applied challenge seeks relief from a specific application of a facially valid statute or ordinance to an individual or class of individuals who are under allegedly impermissible present restraint or disability as a result of the manner or circumstances in which the statute or ordinance has been applied.  (Id.) 

 

An as-applied challenge may seek: (1) relief from a specific application of a facially valid statute or ordinance to an individual or class of individuals who are under allegedly impermissible present restraint or disability as a result of the manner or circumstances in which the statute or ordinance has been applied; or (2) an injunction against future application of the statute or ordinance in the allegedly impermissible manner it is shown to have been applied in the past.  (Ribakoff v. City of Long Beach (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 150, 167.)  Such a challenge contemplates analysis of the facts of a particular case or cases to determine the circumstances in which the statute or ordinance has been applied and to consider whether in those particular circumstances the application deprived the individual to whom it was applied of a protected right.  (Id.)  To prevail on such a contention, the party asserting an as-applied challenge must establish a pattern of impermissible application of the statute, rule or policy.  (Id.)

 

Facial challenges to a legislative body's decision to adopt a zoning ordinance must be brought within 90 days of the date the ordinance becomes effective.  (County of Sonoma v. Superior Court (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 1312, 1323-24.)  As-applied constitutional challenges have a statute of limitations of two years.  (See Action Apartment Ass'n, Inc. v. Santa Monica Rent Control Bd. (9th Cir. 2007) 509 F.3d 1020, 1026-27.)

 

Although the 6AC alleges that Proposition L is unconstitutional both on its face and as applied to Plaintiffs, the 6AC does not specifically allege why the text of the law violates constitutional free speech protections.  Rather, the facial challenge is alleged as a legal conclusion and the gravamen of Plaintiffs’ claim is that the City’s enforcement of Proposition L is unconstitutional based on the contrast between the City’s treatment of Regency and the Regency Billboards and Stephens’s attempts to acquire a permit to operate billboards.  (See 6AC ¶¶ 84-91.)

 

            The City has met its burden to establish that the 6AC’s claims are outside the statute of limitations for an as-applied challenge based the deposition testimony given by Stephens on April 7, 2022 stating that he had not applied for a permit since 2015.  The opposition (the “Opposition”) presents evidence that Stephens applied for a permit on July 20, 2022.  (See Stephens Decl. ¶ 19.)  The Court notes that the Opposition’s evidence of the July 20, 2022 attempt to acquire a permit is outside the scope of the 6AC, which was filed on February 18, 2022.  The Opposition provides no other evidence of attempts to apply for a permit within the two-year limitations period. 

 

Regardless of the evidence of Stephens’s 2022 permit application, Plaintiffs present no evidence of differential treatment within the two-year limitations period.  The 6AC’s challenge to Proposition L is premised on Stephens being forbidden from operating billboards while Regency was contemporaneously allowed to maintain the Regency Billboards.  Before 2014, the Regency Billboards were operated pursuant to the Development Agreement -- a contract that fell outside the parameters of Proposition L because it was executed before that law’s enactment.  After the City passed Ordinance No. 4190 in an attempt to further extend the Development Agreement, the legality of the Regency Billboards has been an ongoing subject of litigation.[2]  There is no pattern of the City’s impermissible application of Proposition L—the Regency Billboards were previously not encompassed by the law’s restrictions, and the City is currently litigating their removal. 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that there are no factual issues regarding Plaintiffs’ claims.  The Court therefore GRANTS the Motion.
