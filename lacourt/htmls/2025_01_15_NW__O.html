     CASE NO.: 23VECV00503

 

ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR TERMINATING AND/OR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Level 8 Management, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) brings a breach of contract action against Defendants Nicola Di Lello a/k/a Nick Di Lello (“Di Lello”) and Ital Concepts, LLC d/b/a Bottega Pizzeria Ristorante (“Bottega”) (collectively, “Defendants”) arising out of Plaintiff’s alleged performance of professional accounting services to assist Defendants apply for and obtain Employee Retention Tax Credits.

On September 20, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s eight separate motions to compel Defendants’ further discovery.

Plaintiff now moves for terminating sanctions and/or monetary sanctions.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed its Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Common Counts.

On April 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed its First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging (1) Breach of Contract; and (2) Common Counts.

On August 7, 2023, the court overruled Defendants’ Demurrer and denied Defendants’ Motion to Strike.

On September 6, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer.

On September 20, 2024, the Court denied Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings and granted in part Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Defendants’ Further Discovery Responses.

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Terminating and/or Monetary Sanctions (“Motion”).

On October 23, 2024, the Court continued the non-jury trial.

On January 2, 2025, Defendants filed their Opposition.

On January 6, 2025, Plaintiff filed its Reply.

On January 13, 2025, Defendants filed an Objection to Plaintiff’s Reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Terminating, Issue, Evidentiary, and/or Monetary Sanctions

On motion, if a party “fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction” as provided in Section 2030.010, et seq. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(h).)[1] The “court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after an opportunity for hearing, may impose” monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions “against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process[.]” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a)-(d).) Failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are misuses of the discovery process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)   

IV.          ANALYSIS

Plaintiff requests this Court strike Defendants’ Answer and enter default against them and/or issue monetary sanctions in the amount of $4,800.00. (See Motion, pg. 3.) Plaintiff argues such sanctions are necessary because Defendants did not serve supplemental discovery responses by October 11, 2024, as required by the Court’s September 20, 2024, Order (“Order”). (See id.)

In opposition, Defendants argue (1) “Level 8 Management, LLC” is “an entity that does not exist” and “any purported assignment to Plaintiff was ineffectual” and “no contractual or other rights can be enforced in this case[]”; (2) Defendants did provide supplemental responses; and (3) Plaintiff did not meet and confer before filing the instant Motion as required by the Court’s Order. (Opposition, pp. 2-8.) Defendants also request sanctions against Plaintiff in the amount of $2,000.00. (See Declaration of Alexander Kasendorf filed January 2, 2024 (“Kasendorf Decl.”), ¶ 13.)[2]

In reply, Plaintiff argues Defendants’ supplemental responses are riddled with objections and the Court’s Order specifically requests Defendants serve supplemental responses without objections. (See Reply, pg. 2.) Plaintiff also argues there is no meet and confer requirement for a terminating sanctions motion. (See id.)

Plaintiff’s Motion is denied in its entirety for failure to meet and confer. The Court’s Order stated “[t]hough not attached to any subsequent filings, Defendants appear to have served supplemental responses to all eight of Plaintiff’s discovery requests on September 5, 2024. (See, e.g., Di Lello RFPs Opposition.) The Court will analyze each of Plaintiff’s eight motions as of the date of filing (July 9, 2024) because the Court does not consider motions to compel further responses moot when responses are provided after such motions are filed unless the moving party withdraws them. Whether these supplemental responses comply with this Court’s order here will be up to counsel to ultimately determine.” (September 20, 2024, Order, pg. 12, fn. 3.) Plaintiff should have analyzed whether Defendants’ September 5, 2024, supplemental responses complied with the Court’s Order regardless of Defendants’ objections. If not, as appears to be Plaintiff’s position, Plaintiff should have met and conferred with Defendants about their defective supplemental responses before filing the instant Motion. Plaintiff did not do so here.[3]

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion is DENIED as the Court has warned counsel for failing to meet and confer prior to filing previous motions.

The Court thus finds it appropriate to award Defendants’ counsel sanctions for their efforts in opposing the Motion. Defendants request $2,000.00 in sanctions “which amounts to the fees incurred in drafting this Opposition, reviewing Plaintiff’s reply and attending the hearing on this Motion.” (See Kasendorf Decl., ¶ 13.) Defendants do not, however, list their billing rate or hours expended to support their sanctions request. (See id.) The Court therefore finds it appropriate to reduce Defendants’ requested sanctions by $1,000.00, for a total award of $1,000.00.

Plaintiff’s counsel shall pay Defendants’ counsel $1,000.00 in discovery sanctions on or before February 28, 2025.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating and/or Monetary Sanctions.

Plaintiff’s counsel shall pay Defendants’ counsel $1,000.00 in discovery sanctions on or before February 28, 2025.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a Song-Beverly Act case. Plaintiff Ellen Sarkisyan (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (“Defendant”) violated the Song Beverly Act’s Breach of Express and Implied Warranty.

Defendant now seeks to compel Plaintiff’s response to its request for production of documents (“RFPs”), its form interrogatories (“FROGS”), and its special interrogatories (“SROGS”). Defendant also moves to compel Plaintiff’s deposition and requests an order deeming the truth of the matters admitted in its requests for admissions (“RFAs”).

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On April 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Express Warranty Obligations; and (2) Breach of Implied Warranty Obligations.

On July 27, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On October 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff, Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted (“Motion RFAs”), Motion to Compel Responses to SROGS (“Motion SROGS”), Motion to Compel Responses to FROGS (“Motion FROGS”), and Motion to Compel Responses to RFPs (“Motion RFPs”).

Plaintiff did not oppose.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Request for Production

 A party upon whom a demand for the production of documents has been made has 30 days after service of the demand to respond. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)[1] The propounding and responding parties may agree to extend the time for response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.270(a).) When a party fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a motion to compel response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b).) A responding party that does not provide responses waives the right to object to the demand, including objections based on privilege or work product. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).)

B.             Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)    

C.             Depositions

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a) provides that, “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action… without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it…, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410(a), provides that, “[a]ny party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with [the notice requirements in Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2025.210 through 2025.290] waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.”

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.290, which provides for a limit of seven hours of total testimony of a witness, does not apply to the deposition of a person designated as the person most qualified to be deposed. (See Code. Civ. Proc. § 2025.290(b)(5).)

D.            Requests for Admission

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared…” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).)

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)    

IV.          ANALYSIS

Five motions are presently before the Court. The ruling will address Defendant’s Motion RFPs, then its Motion FROGS and SROGS, then its Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff, and, finally, its Motion RFAs.

A.             The Court Grants Defendant’s Motion RFPs.

Defendant’s counsel declares Plaintiff was served with written discovery on May 16, 2024. (See Declaration of Sabrina Narain filed October 24, 2024 (“Narain Decl.”), ¶ 5, Ex. A.) Defendant’s counsel further declares Plaintiff has not served a response or requested an extension. (See id. at ¶ 7.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion RFPs and finds Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff’s response to its RFPs. Plaintiff is ordered to provide code-compliant responses without objection on or before January 31, 2025.

B.             The Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion FROGS and Motion SROGs.

Defendant’s counsel declares Plaintiff was served with written discovery on May 16, 2024. (See Narain Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. A.) Defendant’s counsel further declares Plaintiff has not served a response or requested an extension. (See id. at ¶ 7.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion FROGS and Motion SROGS and finds Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff’s response to its FROGS and SROGS. Plaintiff is ordered to provide code-compliant responses without objection on or before January 31, 2025.

C.             The Court Grants Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition.

Defendant’s counsel declares Defendant served Plaintiff a Notice of Deposition on May 16, 2024, for a deposition date of July 10, 2024. (See Narain Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. B.) Defendant’s counsel declares Plaintiff was sent a Zoom link for the remote deposition on July 9, 2024, but that Plaintiff did not show for the deposition on July 10, 2024. (See id. at ¶¶ 8-9, Exs. C-D.) Defendant further declares Plaintiff did not reply to Defendant’s follow-up email until July 11, 2024, and did not provide alternate dates for Plaintiff’s deposition. (See id. at ¶ 11, Exs. F, G.)

A motion set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration or, when the deponent does not attend the deposition, a “declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).) Defendant has done so here. (See Narain Decl., ¶¶ 6, 8-9, Exs. B-D.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff’s deposition. Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff. Plaintiff shall appear for deposition on or before February 14, 2025.

D.            The Court Grants Defendant’s Motion RFAs.

Defendant’s counsel declares Plaintiff was served with written discovery on May 16, 2024. (See Narain Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. A.) Defendant’s counsel further declares Plaintiff has not served a response or requested an extension. (See id. at ¶ 7.)

Plaintiff did not oppose.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion RFAs and finds Defendant is entitled to an order deeming the truth of the matters admitted in its RFAs.  

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Compel Responses to RFPs, Motion to Compel Responses to FROGS, Motion to Compel Responses to SROGS, Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff, and Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted.

Plaintiff is ordered to serve Defendant with code-compliant responses to Defendant’s RFPs, FROGS, and SROGS without objection on or before January 31, 2025.

Plaintiff shall appear for deposition on or before February 14, 2025.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
