DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21TRCV00673    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: P

BACKGROUND

On September 20, 2021, plaintiffs Bui Tran and Danh Do sued RSVP Group, LLC; Garry Mear; Christopher Michael Mear; and DOES 1 to 25 for the following causes of action: (1) breach of fiduciary duty; (2) breach of contract; (3) securities fraud; and (4) securities fraud.

On January 25, 2022, plaintiffs filed proofs of service showing that the complaint was served via personal service on RSVP Group, LLC, and Garry Mear on November 4, 2021, at 301 Calle Miramar #1, Redondo Beach, California 90277. The clerk entered default against the defendants on January 25, 2022. Plaintiffs dismissed Christopher Michael Mear on August 29, 2022. Plaintiffs dismissed Doe Defendants on November 27, 2023. This is Plaintiffs’ first request for default judgment.

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure Section 585 permits entry of a judgment after a defendant’s default has been entered. A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and the following: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP Section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. CRC 3.1800.

JC Form CIV-100 must be used to request court judgment. Item number 2 must be completed. Item number 8 must be completed if the Defendant is an individual. Plaintiffs must submit a proposed form of judgment.

Plaintiffs used JC Form CIV-100 and completed items number 2 and 8. Plaintiffs have submitted a proposed form of judgment.

Plaintiffs request a total judgment of $552,523.72, consisting of the demand of the complaint for $414,000.00, interest in the amount of $137,918.72, and $605.00 in costs.

By defaulting, a defendant is deemed to admit all material allegations of the complaint that are well pleaded. Molen v. Friedman (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1156. The complaint need only allege facts sufficient to apprise a defendant of the nature of the plaintiff’s demand, and it is immaterial that the complaint might have been subject to a demurrer for failure to make an allegation necessary to state a cause of action. Id. at 1157. See also CCP § 431.20; Bristol Convalescent Hospital v. Stone (1968) 258 Cal.App.2d 848, 859 (stating that defaulting defendant admits absolute verity of all allegations of complaint giving rise to liability).

The plaintiff must prove the amount of damages before an actual entry of default judgment. See Ostling v. Loring (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1731, 1745. In determining whether the plaintiff is entitled to an award of damages after the defendant has defaulted, the plaintiff is only required to present evidence establishing a prima facie case for damages. Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361-362. The trial court must hear the evidence offered by the plaintiff and render judgment “‘in [its] favor for such sum, not exceeding the amount stated in the complaint, or for such relief, not exceeding that demanded in the complaint, as appears from the evidence to be just. [Citations.]’” Id. at 362.

A. General Damages

Plaintiffs request $414,000.00. Request for Entry of Default Judgment, ¶ 2. Plaintiffs have submitted the declaration of Danh Do. Do states that he and his wife invested $350,000 for the non-voting membership shares in RSVP Group, LLC. Decl. Do, ¶ 5. He also states that they have not received any K-1s, annual statements, profit and loss statements, and/or books and records. Decl. Do, ¶ 6. He further states that the business is no longer active, and he does not know why. Decl. Do, ¶ 6. In addition, he states that they loaned an additional $64,000.00. Decl. Do, ¶ 16. However, Plaintiffs do not support these statements with evidence of payment. There are no cancelled checks or bank statements, e.g. They refer to the $350,000 payment as Exhibit 4, but they have not submitted an Exhibit 4.

Thus, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently proven their damages of $414,000.00.

B. Interest

According to form CIV-100, Plaintiffs request interest in the amount of $137,918.72. Plaintiffs calculate interest based on 10 percent of $414,000 from July 12, 2019, to November 10, 2022: $41,400 per year divided by 365, equally $113.42 per diem, which adds up to 1,216 days totaling interest of $137,918.72. (Decl. Do, ¶ 24.)

Accordingly, Plaintiffs would be entitled to interest in the amount of $18,802.00.

C. Costs

Plaintiffs request recovery of costs in the amount of $605.00. The memorandum of costs states that the request is based on $435.00 in clerk’s filing fees and $170.00 in process server fees. CIV-100, ¶ 7.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs would be entitled to costs in the amount of $605.00.

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiffs Buy Tran and Danh Doh’s request for entry of default judgment in their favor and against Defendants RSVP Group, LLC and Garry Mear is DENIED.

Case Number: 23TRCV00457    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request for Stay

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers

RULING

The motion is GRANTED. The action is stayed pending resolution of the arbitration proceedings.

BACKGROUND

On February 16, 2023, plaintiff Blanca Cadenas De Garcia filed a complaint against defendants Hudson Insurance Company (“Hudson”) and Redondo Automotive, LLC, dba Redondo Mitsubishi (“Redondo Automotive”) arising from her purchase of a used 2009 Cadillac CTS on October 25, 2022. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) intentional misrepresentation; (2) negligent misrepresentation, (3) violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200; (4) bond claim; and (50 violation of consumer legal remedies act.

On March 20, 2023, defendant Hudson filed its answer to the complaint.

On March 21, 2023, Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed defendant Redondo Automotive from the complaint. On the same day, Plaintiff filed an Amendment to Complaint, substituting in Malty Automotive, Inc. for Doe 1.

On October 13, 2023, defendant Malty Automotive, Inc., dba Redondo Mitsubishi (“Malty Automotive”) filed the instant motion to compel arbitration and request for stay. On November 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed a limited opposition. On November 20, 2023, defendant Malty Automotive filed its reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

When seeking to compel arbitration of a plaintiff’s claims, the defendant must allege the existence of an agreement to arbitrate. (Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219.) The burden then shifts to the plaintiff to prove the falsity of the agreement. (Ibid.) After the Court determines that an agreement to arbitrate exists, it then considers objections to its enforceability. (Ibid.) The Court must grant a petition to compel arbitration unless the defendant has waived the right to compel arbitration or if there are grounds to revoke the arbitration agreement. (Ibid.; Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.)

DISCUSSION

Defendant Malty Automotive requests an order compelling binding arbitration and to stay the proceedings.

A. Existence of an Agreement to Arbitrate

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) “If the party opposing the petition raises a defense to enforcement—either fraud in the execution voiding the agreement, or a statutory defense of waiver or revocation [citation]—that party bears the burden of producing evidence of, and proving by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to the defense.” (Ibid.)

In support of its burden, defendant Malty Automotive submits evidence of the Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) between it and Plaintiff in connection with her purchase of the subject vehicle on October 25, 2022, which contains an arbitration provision. (Motion at pg. 3; Malty Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1 at pg. 5.) The arbitration provision states in pertinent part:

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, any allegation of waiver of rights under this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action…The arbitrator may not preside over a consolidated, representative, class, collective, injunctive, or private attorney general action. (Emphasis added.)

The arbitration provision includes both substantive and procedural protections for both sides and was signed by both parties to the contract.

Based on this evidence, the Court finds that defendant Malty Automotive has met its burden in showing that an arbitration agreement exists between it and Plaintiff. Furthermore, as indicated in the 2019 agreement, the FAA is the governing law. (Ibid.; Rodriguez v. American Technologies, Inc., (2006) 136 Cal. App. 4th 110, 1117.)

Plaintiff does not dispute that an arbitration agreement exists for the sale at issue in this litigation; accordingly, the court finds an agreement to arbitration exists between the parties.

B. Enforceability of Arbitration Agreement

Plaintiff argues that the arbitration agreement is unenforceable under McGill v. Citi bank, NA. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 945 because Plaintiff seeks public injunctive relief. (Opposition at pg. 1.) Defendant asserts that, even if McGill limits the enforceability of the arbitration agreement, the court should excise the claim which is not subject to arbitration and order the parties to arbitration for the balance of the claims.

In her Fifth Cause of Action, plaintiff seeks “to obtain an injunction against [Defendant] which enjoins [Defendant] from continuing to make false and misleading statements to consumers regarding the sale of motor vehicles.” (Compl. ¶82.)1 Within the arbitration

agreement itself, the parties agreed not to have the arbitrator make decisions when an injunction is being sought. (Malty Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1 at pg. 5.) “Arbitration under the FAA is a matter of consent, not coercion, and parties may limit by contract the issues that they will arbitrate.” (McGill v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 2 Cal.5th at 966.) “[C]ase law establishes that a stay of proceedings as to any inarbitrable claims is appropriate until arbitration of any arbitrable claims is concluded.” (Id.) Here, the agreement between the parties clearly provides that “[t]he arbitrator may not preside over a[n]…injunctive…action.” (Malty Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1 at pg. 5.) However, injunctive relief is sought only as to one of the five causes of action alleged in plaintiff’s complaint. Therefore, the court finds that the parties agreed to arbitrate the each of plaintiff’s cause of action, but for the request for injunctive relief.

Based on the foregoing, the motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. The action is stayed until such time as all claims for money damages have been resolved by arbitration.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
