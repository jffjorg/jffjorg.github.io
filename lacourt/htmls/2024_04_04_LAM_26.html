DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV35574    Hearing Date: April 4, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

AVO ABOULIAN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

EDVIN O. ovasapyan

                        Defendant.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV35574   

 

  Hearing Date:  April 4, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s DEMURRER and motion to STRIKE the FIRST amended complaint

 

Procedural Background

            On November 9, 2022, Plaintiff Avo Aboulian (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action arising out of Plaintiff’s purchase of a luxury watch from Defendant Avo Ovasapyan (“Defendant”). Plaintiff alleged three causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200, and (3) Fraud – Civil Code § 1710(4).

            On September 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendant alleging an additional fourth cause of action for (4) Violation of Penal Code § 350 Selling Counterfeit Goods.

            On October 23, 2023, Defendant filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike the FAC.  On March 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike. On March 27, 2024, Defendant filed replies for both the demurrer and motion to strike. 

Allegations of the Operative Complaint

The FAC alleges as follows:

On December 6, 2021, Plaintiff agreed to purchase a green dial Rolex 18k yellow gold Daytona watch (the “Rolex”) from Defendant for $90,000.00. (FAC ¶¶ 4, 11.) Plaintiff completed payment on December 13, 2021. (FAC ¶ 5.) Fifteen minutes before delivering the Rolex, Defendant informed Plaintiff that the watch in his possession in fact had a white dial, not a green dial, which significantly diminishes the watch’s value. (FAC ¶ 6.) Instead of refunding Plaintiff’s money, Defendant proposed that the Rolex be replaced with a stainless steel Patek Philippe Nautilus (the “Patek”). (Ibid.) Plaintiff took possession of the Patek and brought it to the Patek Philippe store in Beverly Hills to be authenticated. (Ibid.) Plaintiff was informed by the store that the watch was counterfeit. (Ibid.) 

Legal Standard

Demurrer Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). (Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.)

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)  

Motion to Strike Standard

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer (i.e., words, phrases, prayer for damages, etc.). (See CCP §§ 435-437.)  A party may file a motion to strike in whole or in part within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, however, if a party serves and files a motion to strike without demurring to the complaint, the time to answer is extended.  (CCP §§ 435(b)(1), 435(c).)

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  (CCP § 436.)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleadings or by way of judicial notice.  (CCP § 437.) 

Meet and Confer Requirement

Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41, subdivision (a) requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer¿in person or by telephone¿with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due and if they are unable to meet the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension.  (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).)  The demurring party must also file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts.  (Id.¿at (a)(3).)¿ If an amended pleading is filed, the parties must meet and confer again before a demurrer may be filed to the amended pleading.  (Id.¿at (a).)  There is a similar meet and confer requirement for motions to strike.  (CCP § 435.5.)

Defendant’s counsel sent a 6-page meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel on October 5, 2023. (Mitilian Decl., ¶ 2.) A response was only received on October 23, 2023. (Id.) The FAC was filed on September 29, 2023 and the Court had ordered Defendant to file a responsive pleading within 30 days. (9/28/23 Minute Order.) It is not clear to the Court whether the parties adequately met and conferred as required; nevertheless, the Court will consider the demurrer on the merits. The Court admonishes the parties to strictly comply with all statutory requirements in the future. 

Discussion – Demurrer

First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

Defendant contends that the first cause of action for breach of contract fails because Plaintiff does not state whether the alleged contract is written, oral or implied by conduct. CCP § 430.10(g) provides that a defendant can object by demurrer to a complaint if “in an action founded upon a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct.” (CCP § 430.10, subd. (g).)

Plaintiff did not specify anywhere in the FAC whether the alleged contract is written, oral or implied by conduct. In opposition, Plaintiff contends, somewhat confusingly, that the contract in question was “partially written and partially oral” (Opp’n, 4:14) but that the contract’s nature (whether in writing or oral) “cannot be alleged without further evidence.” (Opp’n 4:6-7.) If the contract was partially written, Plaintiff should provide more information on the written portions. If the contract was entirely oral, Plaintiff should plead as so.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend. 

Fourth Cause of Action: Violation of Penal Code § 350 Selling Counterfeit Goods

            Defendant contends that Plaintiff lacks capacity to prosecute this cause of action, which also fails because it does not state sufficient facts.

Penal Code Section 350 prohibits a person from willfully manufacturing, intentionally selling, or knowingly possessing counterfeit marks for sale. As Defendant correctly points out, this section is premised on a defendant first being convicted of a crime. (See Penal Code § 350 subd. (a) [“Any person . . . shall, upon conviction, be punishable as follows . . .”]; subd. (b) [“Any person who has been convicted of a violation . . . shall, upon a subsequent conviction . . . be punished by . . .”]; subd. (c) [“Any person who has been convicted of a violation of subdivision (a) . . . shall . . . be punished . . .”]; subd. (d) [“in any action brought under this section resulting in a conviction, the court shall . . .”]; and subd. (i) [“when a person is convicted of an offense under this section, the court shall . . .”].)

            Plaintiff’s arguments and case law raised in opposition are inadequate and, fatally, fails to address the fact that the remedies provided by Section 350 are only available after a criminal conviction for selling counterfeit products, which Plaintiff never alleged either in the FAC or in his opposition.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. 

Discussion – Motion to Strike

            Defendant moves to strike numerous paragraphs of the FAC.  Specifically, Defendant moves to strike the entire fourth cause of action and related prayer, punitive damages, allegations concerning an unrelated criminal proceeding and civil action involving Defendant, and compensatory damages for the second cause of action for violation of Business and Professions Code sec. 17200.

            The Fourth Cause of Action

            As the Court sustained its demurrer to the fourth cause of action without leave to amend, Defendant’s motion to strike the entire fourth cause of action and related prayer from the FAC is GRANTED. 

            Punitive Damages

            Punitive damages are not recoverable in a breach of contract dispute. Civil Code § 3294(a), which Plaintiff himself cites in his opposition, clearly states that exemplary damages provided by that section are available only in “an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a) [emphasis added].) Neither of the two cases Plaintiff cites to support its request for punitive damages (Hartzell v. Myall (1953) 115 Cal.App.2d 670 and Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1) involved a contract.

For this reason alone, Defendant’s motion to strike all mention of punitive damages in the FAC is GRANTED. 

            Unrelated civil and criminal proceedings involving Defendant

            Defendant argues that mentions of other civil and criminal proceedings that Defendant is involved in, which have nothing to do with Plaintiff’s claims in this action, are immaterial and irrelevant, and therefore should be stricken. Plaintiff argues in opposition that pattern and practice evidence is permitted to demonstrate unfair business practices and fraud (Allied Grape Growers v. Bronco Wine Co. (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 432, 453) and the allegations of Defendant’s convictions and civil judgments for fraud are evidence of such a pattern and practice. Defendant fails to cite any authority to the contrary in reply.

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike from the FAC allegations of other civil and criminal proceedings involving Defendant is DENIED. 

            Compensatory damages for the second cause of action

            Plaintiff’s second cause of action is for violation of California’s Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”), codified by Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq. Defendant contends that compensatory damages are not recoverable for violation of the UCL. Rather, recovery is limited to injunctive relief and restitution, which does not include attorney fees, interest, costs or other damages. Plaintiff argues in opposition that prejudgment interest is a recoverable component of restitution under § 17200, citing Ballard v. Equifax Check Services, Inc. (ED CA 2001) 158 F.Supp.2d 1163, 1176-1177 and Irwin v. Mascott (ND CA 2000) 112 F.Supp.2d 937, 956. Although these decisions are not binding on this Court, they are persuasive as they also interpret California’s UCL. Defendant does not address Plaintiff’s argument in reply.

            Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike the following sections from the FAC is DENIED:

·       Paragraphs 2-4 of the Prayer for the Second Cause of Action, page 8, lines 17 through 21: “2. For all costs and expenses; For interest on sum certain; and 4. For any other

·       damages the Court deems are just and proper”.

·       Paragraph 17 of the FAC, page 5, lines 10 through 11: “plus sum certain interest under California Civil Code § 3287 at 10% from Dec. 15, 2021, plus all court costs and any other damages the Court deems just.”

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Edvin Ovasapyan’s demurrer to the first amended complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Plaintiff is granted leave to amend the first cause of action only and is granted 20 days to amend.

Defendant’s motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part. Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to (1) punitive damages and (2) the fourth cause of action and its related prayer. Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED as to (1) mentions of other civil and criminal proceedings involving Defendant and (2) relief requested in connection with the second cause of action.

            Defendant is to give notice to all parties.

Procedural Background

            On February 19, 2016, Plaintiff Robert Scott Shtofman ("Plaintiff") filed this case. On June 24, 2021, Plaintiff entered into a Settlement Agreement with Defendant Arch Insurance Group, Inc. The Settlement Agreement provided that Plaintiff was to and did receive a gross amount of $103,000 from Defendant Arch Insurance Group, Inc. On October 19, 2022, trial began with the remaining Defendants. On November 4, 2022, the jury in the above-entitled matter rendered their respective verdicts. Defendant Gloria Lopez prevailed in full as against the Plaintiff. The jury rendered a damages verdict as against Defendant Julie C. Lim ("Lim") in the amount of more than $200,000 plus punitive damages.

On November 21, 2022, Defendant Julie C. Lim moved the court for an offset credit of the $103,000 settlement amount, received by Plaintiff.

On August 25, 2023, Plaintiff Shtofman filed a proposed Judgment. On the same day Plaintiff Shtofman filed his Memorandum of Costs.

            On September 11, 2023, Defendants Gloria Lopez and Julie C. Lim filed the instant motion to tax costs.  On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.  As of April 4, 2024, Defendants have not filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

“A prevailing party is entitled ‘as a matter of right’ to recover costs in any action or proceeding unless a statute expressly provides otherwise.”  (Segal v. ASICS America Corp. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 651, 658.)  “Section 1033.5 sets forth the types of expenses that are and are not allowable as costs under section 1032. Specifically, subdivision (a) of section 1033.5 describes items that are ‘allowable as costs,’ subdivision (b) describes items ‘not allowable as costs, except when expressly authorized by law,’ and section 1033.5(c)(4) provides that ‘[i]tems not mentioned in this section and items assessed upon application may be allowed or denied in the court's discretion.’”  (Ibid.)

To recover a cost, it must be reasonably necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount. (Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises (l992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 244.) If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. (Ladas v. California State Automotive Assoc. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 773-74.)  On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.) 

California Rule of Court, Rule 3.1700 requires that “[a]ny notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended” by up to 20 days. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(1).) Additionally, “[T]he court may extend the times for serving and filing the cost memorandum or the notice of motion or tax costs for a period not to exceed 30 days.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(b)(3).)  

Discussion

            Defendants Gloria Lopez and Julie C. Lim argue that good cause exists for making the instant motion because no judgment has been entered, the motion is not ripe, and the memorandum of costs seeks costs which were incurred as to third parties, who mutually waived their costs during settlement. Defendants also argue that Plaintiff's Memorandum of Costs "cannot be reconciled", given the settlements with Arch Insurance Company and Alliance United Insurance Company.

“It is not necessary that the [moving party], …, accompany the motion to tax cost with any affidavit.”  (State of California v. Meyer (1985) 174 Cal.App.3d 1061, 1075.)  Rather, “[i]f items on a memorandum of costs appear to be proper charges on their face, those items are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services are proper and necessarily incurred. [Citation.] The burden then shifts to the objecting party to show them to be unnecessary or unreasonable.”  (Doe v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Children & Family Services (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 675, 693.)  “[T]he objecting party has the burden to show that a cost item is unrecoverable because it was not necessary to the litigation.”  (Hooked Media Group, Inc. v. Apple Inc. (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 323, 338.)  “However, more than a bare objection is needed to rebut the presumption that fees were reasonable and necessarily incurred.”  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ arguments are conclusory and not supported by law. Moreover, Plaintiff argues he was the prevailing party in his complaint against Defendant Lim even though he lost against Defendant Lopez. (11/4/22 Minute Order; Amended Judgment filed 12/19/23.) Plaintiff further argues that he was a prevailing party in the cross-complaint that was filed and prosecuted by Defendants Lim and Lopez against him.

On February 27, 2023, the Court ordered the following “The jury verdict of $110,000 for quantum meruit shall be offset by the $103,000.00 that Plaintiff Robert Shtofman has received from settlements with Alliance United Insurance Company and Arch Insurance Group. Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve a proposed judgment in accordance with the jury’s November 4, 2022 verdicts and in accordance with the instant order.” (02/27/23 Minute Order.) 

Filing Fees

Filing fees are expressly allowable as costs (CCP §1033.5(a)(1)), thus, the burden is on Defendants to show that the $8,691.35 in filing costs was not reasonably necessary for the litigation.  Defendants fail to meet this burden as they merely claim that each of the filing fees sought to be taxed are vague, misleading and improper because Plaintiff is attempting to seek the collective sum for filing fees of $7,710.00 for which he was not the prevailing party in violation of CRC 8.278(1) and (2).  However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.)

Thus, while all the filing fees incurred may not have been reasonably necessary, Defendants fail to meet their burden in showing which fees should not be recovered and why. 

Deposition Costs

            Defendants seeks to tax $844.85 for Gloria Lopez’s deposition costs, $621.00 for the video taping of the deposition, and $545.00 for a Spanish language translator for a total amount of $2,010.85.  The costs for taking, video recording, and transcribing necessary depositions are expressly permitted as costs.  (CCP § 1033.5(a)(3)(A).)  Therefore, the burden is on Defendants to show that the costs are unnecessary or unreasonable.  Defendants fail to do so. 

            Defendants also assert that “ Plaintiff did not prevail as against Defendant Gloria Lopez and cannot collect that cost.” (Mot. to Tax p.7.)  However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.)

            At most, Defendants merely provide general objections to these costs in their memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs.  This is insufficient to tax a permitted cost.  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.) Further,  the depositions taken in this case, including the deposition of Gloria Lopez, were "reasonably necessary" for Plaintiff to prevail in this case, both in pre-trial motions and in trial. For instance, the testimony in those depositions was used to defeat summary judgments brought by Lim and Lopez. The depositions were also necessary to prevail at trial against Lim.

 

Service of Process Costs

Defendants seeks to tax an unknown amount for the service of process for parties other than Defendant Julie C. Lim, with whom he either settled his case or dismissed them as a Defendant. Defendants provide an example of $300.00 to be taxed however, it is unclear if this is the only amount sought to be taxed. As stated, the verified Memorandum of Costs serves as prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary  Burden of Proof., 7 Witkin, Cal. Proc. 6th Judgments § 149 (2023). Therefore, the burden is on Defendants to show that the costs are unnecessary or unreasonable.  Defendants fail to do so. Defendants merely state that section 877 bars nonsettling defendants from seeking contribution from settling defendants and that because Plaintiff settled with Arch and Alliance, Plaintiff released Arch and Alliance from further liability for service of process costs. (Mot. to Tax p.7.) However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.) At most, Defendants merely provide general objections to these costs in their memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs. This is insufficient to tax an otherwise permitted cost.  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

 

Witness Fees

            Defendants seek to tax $3,020.00 in witness fees. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5(a)(7) and 1033.5(c), Plaintiff as the prevailing party in tort litigation is expressly permitted and may recover as cost "ordinary witness fees" as defined in Government Code section 68093 and Code of Civil Procedure section 1986.5.  Defendants note that the witnesses were never called to testify. Defendants did not submit any evidence to support this claim. However, Plaintiff conceded that the witnesses were not called to testify. Thus, the Court strikes this cost item in its totality.

 

Court Ordered Transcripts

            Defendants contend that the Court ordered transcripts in the sum of $8,828.43 were waived because Plaintiff entered into a settlement agreement. Court ordered transcripts are expressly permitted and recoverable under section 1033.5(a)(9) and 1033.S(c) as reasonably necessary expenses to conduct litigation. Plaintiff was required by the court to pay for these costs. Defendants did not submit any evidence to support this claim. Therefore, they did not meet their burden. Thus, these were reasonably necessary costs.

 

Court Reporter Fees

            Defendant contends that the Court reporter fees in the sum of $10,997.30 were waived because Plaintiff entered into a settlement agreement.   In general, Court reporter fees are only permitted in the amount established by statute.  (CCP § 1033.5(a)(11).)  Moreover, such costs must have been reasonably incurred.  (CCP § 1033.5(c)(2).)  Defendants fail to meet this burden.  Defendants merely claim that the “court reporter fees are not applicable as to Gloria Lopez whatsoever (as the prevailing party), and should not apply as to Defendant Julie C. Lim. Further, the court reporter fees allegedly incurred on March 24, 2016, was related to Arch Insurance Group, Inc., of $345.00 (which Plaintiff waived in his Settlement Agreement with Arch), plaintiff cannot recover the cost as against Defendant Gloria Lopez. As to Defendant Julie C. Lim, Plaintiff makes no break-out why such costs were incurred.” (Mot. to Tax p. 8.) However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.) Thus, while such costs may not have been reasonably necessary, Defendants fail to show as such.

 

Models, Enlargements, and Photocopies of Exhibits

Defendants seek to tax $ 5,124.05 because “Plaintiff has made no break-out as to which Defendant necessitated the alleged costs. Therefore, Defendant has no way of determining whether the alleged copy charges were reasonable and necessary." (Mot. to Tax p.8.) Models. Enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits are expressly recoverable under sections 1033.5(a)(l3) and 1033.S(c) as reasonably necessary expenses to conduct litigation. The verified Memorandum of Costs serves as prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were no reasonable or necessary. Burden of Proof., 7 Witkin, Cal. Proc. 6th Judgm § 149 (2023). However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.) At most, Defendants merely provide general objections to these costs in their memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs.  This is insufficient to tax a permitted cost.  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

 

Fees for Electronic Filing or Service

Defendants seek to tax $1,804.70 because Defendants have no way of determining whether the alleged filing fees were reasonable and necessary. These costs are expressly permitted and recoverable under sections 1033.5(a)(l4) and 3 1033.5(c). However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.) At most, Defendants merely provide general objections to these costs in their memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs.  This is insufficient to tax a permitted cost.  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

 

Other Fees

Defendants seek to tax $1,302.67 because these include Plaintiff’s appellate filings in which the Defendants were the prevailing parties. The Memorandum of Costs requests $1,302.67 in other fees including 16 remote court appearances ($15.00 each), courtesy copies, and binding costs for petitions. However, no evidence is submitted to support this claim as a Defendants’ memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs is not evidence and “the court must disregard ‘facts’ contained in an unverified statement.”  (Smith, Smith & Kring v. Superior Court (Oliver) (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 573, 578.)  "The only evidence the trial court should have considered and which we may consider here is that contained in the declarations filed in support of and in opposition to the motion. The matters set forth in the unverified “Statement of Facts” and in memoranda of points and authorities are not evidence and cannot provide the basis for the granting of the motion.” (Id.) At most, Defendants merely provide general objections to these costs in their memorandum in support of their motion to tax costs.  This is insufficient to tax a permitted cost.  (State of California, supra, 174 Cal.App.3d at p.1075.)

 

Conclusion and ORDER

Based on the foregoing, Defendants motion to tax costs is GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART. 

Plaintiff’s costs are granted in a reduced total amount of $45,001.84. The Court strikes the $3,020.00 in witness fees.
