DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV00517    Hearing Date: October 18, 2023    Dept: 32

 

HERMENGILDO AHUMADA-BURGARA,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

M.S. AEROSPACE INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  22STCV00517

  Hearing Date:  October 18, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant’s motion to compel deposition

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 6, 2022, Plaintiff Hermenegildo Ahumada-Burgara filed this employment discrimination action against Defendants M.S. Aerospace Inc. and Harry Smith. Plaintiff alleges that he is a disabled Latino male over 40 years old who was terminated while on medical leave and replaced by a younger, less qualified employee. Defendant M.S. Aerospace was allegedly Plaintiff’s employer, and Defendant Smith is allegedly a manager of M.S. Aerospace.

            On October 31, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration. Arbitration was thereafter initiated, and an arbitrator was appointed. However, on May 17, 2023, JAMS issued an invoice for $86,000 that was due upon receipt. Defendant did not pay the invoice on the due date or within thirty days. On July 31, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to withdraw from arbitration.

            On August 10, 2023, Defendant M.S. Aerospace served a notice of deposition upon Plaintiff, for a deposition at defense counsel’s offices on September 5, 2023. (Giamela Decl., Ex. A.) Defendant also offered alternative dates of September 6 or 8. (Id., Ex. B.) On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff served his objections to the notice of deposition, asserting, inter alia, that the parties had previously agreed to conduct the deposition remotely. (Id., Ex. C.) Plaintiff did not appear on September 5, and a certificate of non-appearance was taken. (Id., Ex. F.) The parties were thereafter unable to agree on whether the deposition should take place in-person or remotely, and no alternative date has been agreed upon.

            On September 21, 2023, Defendant M.S. Aerospace filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition. Plaintiff filed his opposition on October 5, 2023. Defendant filed its reply on October 11, 2023.

LEGAL STANDARD

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document … described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document … described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

MEET AND CONFER

            A motion to compel deposition must be accompanied by a declaration demonstrating meet and confer or, when the deponent fails to appear or produce documents, “by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(b)(2).) The Court finds that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Giamela Decl. ¶¶ 8-11, Ex. D-G.)  

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

I. Deposition

            It is undisputed that Defendant is entitled to take Plaintiff’s deposition. The parties primarily dispute whether the deposition should take place in-person or remotely. Defendant argues that an in-person deposition is necessary due to the volume of exhibits involved. Plaintiff

argues that the parties had previously agreed to a remote deposition.

The agreement that Plaintiff refers to is a Joint Discovery Plan from the arbitration, which states that “[t]he Parties intend on taking the depositions remotely.” (Rand-Lewis Decl., Ex. A.) However, the case has since been withdrawn from arbitration, and Plaintiff does not explain how an arbitration discovery plan is binding on the instant action. Plaintiff also does not dispute that the volume of exhibits warrants an in-person deposition. Defendant has demonstrated good cause for an in-person deposition, and Plaintiff has not rebutted that showing. Therefore, the deposition shall take place in-person.  

II. Documents

            The deposition notice also requested Plaintiff to bring documents related to Plaintiff’s claims and damages. (Giamela Decl., Ex. A.) Plaintiff objected to the document requests and then responded that he “has already produced responsive documents.” (Ibid.)

            Defendant’s motion seeks a supplemental response to the document requests. However, Defendant did not propound RFPs under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.010 et seq. Rather, this was a request for documents accompanying a deposition notice. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.220(a)(4).) Plaintiff responded to the deposition notice. Defendant provides no authority for compelling a further response to a deposition notice. The Court will simply order the deposition to proceed. Defendant also cites no authority requiring a response to a deposition notice to comply with Section 2031.230 for written discovery.  

            Section 2025.450 authorizes a motion to compel the production of documents described in a deposition notice if the motion demonstrates good cause for the production. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(a), (b).) However, Defendant’s motion does not discuss good cause or request an order compelling the production of documents.

CONCLUSION

            Defendant M.S. Aerospace Inc.’s motion to compel deposition is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall appear for deposition in-person. Sanctions are denied as the parties acted with substantial justification.

Case Number: 23STCV09309    Hearing Date: October 18, 2023    Dept: 32

 

AMERICAN BUILDERS & CONTRACTORS SUPPLY CO., INC.,

                       

                         Plaintiff,

            v.

 

ENERGY ROOFING, INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV09309

  Hearing Date:  October 18, 2023

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ motion to quash service of summons

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On April 26, 2023, Plaintiff American Builders & Contractors Supply Co., Inc. filed this action against Defendants Energy Roofing, Inc., Nadav Marciano, and Arturo Suarez. The complaint asserts causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) open book account, and (3) account stated.

            On July 27, 2023, Defendants Energy Roofing and Nadav Marciano filed the instant motion to quash the service of summons on the grounds that it was served to the incorrect address. Plaintiff filed its opposition on September 15, 2023.

LEGAL STANDARD

“[T]he court in which an action is pending has jurisdiction over a party from the time summons is served on him as provided by Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 413.10).” (Code Civ. Proc, § 410.50(a).) “[A] court acquires jurisdiction over a party by proper service of process or by that party's general appearance.” (In re Jennifer O. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 539, 547.) Actual notice of a lawsuit is not a substitute for proper service of process. (Abers v. Rohrs (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1199, 1206.) A defendant may serve and file a notice of motion to quash service of summons on the ground that the court lacks jurisdiction. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a).)

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            Defendants’ objections to the Anderson declaration are overruled.

DISCUSSION

“If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served . . . a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person’s dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address . . . in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge . . . at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20(b).)

Defendant Marciano is the CEO of Defendant Energy Roofing. (Marciano Decl. ¶ 2.) Plaintiff initially attempted service at 24007 Ventura Blvd., Ste. 240, Calabasas, CA 91302 (Ventura Blvd. address), the address listed on Energy Roofing’s website and other materials. (Anderson Decl. ¶ 12.) The process server informed Plaintiff that Energy Roofing was not located at the Ventura Blvd. address. (Id., ¶ 13, Ex. 5.) Plaintiff next attempted service at 21926 Martinez St., Woodland Hills, CA 91364 (Martinez St. address), Energy Roofing’s current address for service of process according to the Secretary of State. (Id., ¶ 14.) The process server attempted service at the Martinez St. address on eleven separate days in May and June 2023. (Id., ¶ 15, Ex. 6.) Plaintiff then discovered an alternate address, a property owned by Marciano located at 6540 Franrivers Ave., Woodland Hills, CA 91307 (Franrivers address). (Id., ¶ 16.) Plaintiff directed a process server to serve both Defendants at the Franrivers address. (Id., ¶ 17.) The proofs of service indicate that a registered process server effectuated substitute service at the Franrivers address. (Id., Ex. 8.)

Marciano denies that the Franrivers address is his residence, business, or mailing address. (Marciano Decl. ¶ 4.) However, Marciano confirms that the correct address is registered with the Secretary of State. (Id., ¶ 5.) This means that the Martinez St. address where Plaintiff previously attempted service is the correct address. (See Anderson Decl. ¶¶ 14-15.) Indeed, Plaintiff personally served Energy Roofing’s agent for service of process at the Martinez St. address on September 18, 2023. (October 5, 2023 Proof of Service.)

Plaintiff has no evidence to contradict Marciano’s testimony that the Franrivers address is not his “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 415.20(b).) Mere ownership is insufficient. Marciano has confirmed that the Martinez St. address is correct, and Plaintiff has demonstrated that service is possible at that address. Therefore, the service at Franrivers is ineffective and insufficient to confer jurisdiction.

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ motion does not comply with the notice and timing requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 418.10(b). However, Plaintiff had notice of the motion sufficient to file a substantive response. A procedurally defective motion does not change the fact that the Court lacks personal jurisdiction due to ineffective service at the wrong address. Defendants have not generally appeared and therefore have not waived the jurisdictional challenge. The Court also has discretion to extend the time for filing a motion to quash. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10(a).) Therefore, the timing requirements in Section 418.10(b) do not dispose of the matter.  

CONCLUSION

            Defendants’ motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED.
