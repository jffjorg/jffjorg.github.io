DEPARTMENT 54 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV05367    Hearing Date: September 27, 2023    Dept: 54

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

Renee Erickson, 

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

19STCV05367

 

vs.

 

 

Tentative Ruling

 

 

ADP, LLC,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date: September 27, 2023

Department 54, Judge Maurice A. Leiter

Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Motion for Summary Adjudication

Moving Party: Defendant ADP, LLC

Responding Party: Plaintiff Renee Erickson

 

T/R:   DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION IS DENIED.

 

DEFENDANT TO NOTICE.

 

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative, please email the courtroom at SMCdept54@lacourt.org with notice to opposing counsel (or self-represented party) before 8:00 am on the day of the hearing.

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

               

On February 15, 2019, Plaintiff Renee Erickson sued Defendant ADP, LLC, asserting causes of action for (1) fraud; (2) negligent misrepresentation; (3) breach of oral contract; (4) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (5) violation of The Independent Wholesale Sales Representative Act; (6) quasi-contract; and (7) aiding and abetting. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant falsely promised Plaintiff would receive full commissions on sales following a merger of Plaintiff’s former employer and Defendant. Defendant allegedly then capped commissions and gave Defendant’s other employees full compensation and full credit on Plaintiff’s sales.

 

EVIDENCE OBJECTIONS

“In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court need rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion.” (CCP § 437c(q).) Defendant’s objections to the declaration of Plaintiff are OVERRULED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties' pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Trial judges are required “to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) Once the defendant has met that burden, “the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Id.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.” (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

A. First and Second Causes of Action for Fraud and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claims for fraud fail because Defendant did not make any misrepresentations and Plaintiff did not justifiably rely on any misrepresentations. Defendant presents evidence purporting to show that Plaintiff was repeatedly informed her position as an independent Regional Sales Manager was governed by the “Rules of Engagement,” which contained the commission calculations for Plaintiff’s position. Defendant asserts that the commission calculations that Plaintiff alleges she was promised were for Defendant’s in-house sales position, which Plaintiff chose not to take.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff presents evidence purporting to show her supervisor promised her multiple times that the Rules of Engagement would not apply to certain clients Plaintiff obtained. Plaintiff asserts that she relied on these representations by continuing to work for Defendant. This is sufficient to create a triable issue of fact as to misrepresentation.

 

In reply, Defendant asserts Plaintiff’s declaration is a “sham” and contradicts Plaintiff’s testimony. Defendant states that Plaintiff “admitted” that her supervisor provided her with the commission calculations for the inside sales position at a November 2017 meeting. Defendant argues that this establishes her supervisor never promised her these commissions for her position as an outside salesperson. As stated, Plaintiff represents that her supervisor promised on multiple different occasions that she would be entitled to certain commission calculations. That Plaintiff attended a meeting in which her supervisor described the commission calculations of an inside salesperson does not “contradict” her declaration.

 

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED. Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the first and second causes of action is DENIED.

 

B. Third, Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action for Breach of Oral Contract, Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and Quasi-Contract.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

Defendant argues that the claims for breach of oral contract, bad faith, and quasi-contract fail because Defendant did not promise Plaintiff she would be exempt from the Rules of Engagement. Defendant asserts that any promises for a different commission calculation were for the in-house sales position that Plaintiff declined to take. As discussed, Plaintiff presents evidence to the contrary, creating triable issues of fact.

 

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the third, fourth and sixth causes of action is DENIED.

 

C. Fifth Cause of Action for Violation of the Independent Wholesale Sales Representative Act

 

The Independent Wholesale Sales Representatives Act requires manufacturers and distributors to enter written contracts with persons acting as independent wholesale sales representatives and to provide certain information to the sales representatives with payments of commissions. (Civil Code § 1738.13.) Under Civil Code § 1738.15, a manufacturer who willfully fails to enter into a written contract as required by the Act “shall be liable to the sales representative in a civil action for treble damages proved at trial.”

 

Defendant asserts it did not willfully fail to enter into a written contract. As discussed, Plaintiff asserts Defendant orally promised certain commission calculations, but failed to reduce the promise to writing. This is sufficient to create a triable issue of fact.

 

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the fifth cause of action is DENIED.

 

D. Seventh Cause of Action for Aiding and Abetting

 

“‘Liability may . . . be imposed on one who aids and abets the commission of an intentional tort if the person (a) knows the other’s conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act or (b) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person’s own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.’”  (Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1318, 1325-26 (quoting Saunders v. Superior Court (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 832, 846; see also Schulz v. Neovi Data Corp. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 86, 93.)

 

Defendant asserts the claim for aiding and abetting fails because they did not aid and abet anyone in the commission of a tort, and there is only one Defendant named in this action. In opposition, Plaintiff asserts Defendant aided and abetted her supervisor’s torts. This is sufficient to create a triable issue of fact.

 

E. Punitive Damages

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff is not entitled to punitive damages because there is no evidence of fraud or malice, and Plaintiff’s supervisor does not have discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determined ADP’s corporate policy. In opposition, Plaintiff represents that Defendant held her supervisor out to have such authority. This is sufficient to create a triable issue of fact.

 

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the claim for punitive damages is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On July 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Kimberly Di Bonaventura and Boomin’ Real Estate, LLC sued Defendants Jonathan Lachman and Omerta Farms, LLC for breach of settlement agreement.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A. Demurrer to Complaint

 

A demurrer to a complaint may be taken to the whole complaint or to any of the causes of action in it.  (CCP § 430.50(a).)  A demurrer challenges only the legal sufficiency of the complaint, not the truth of its factual allegations or the plaintiff's ability to prove those allegations.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High Sch. Dist. (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 726, 732.)  The court must treat as true the complaint's material factual allegations, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  (Id. at 732-33.)  The complaint is to be construed liberally to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Id. at 733.)

 

Defendants demur to the complaint on the grounds that it is barred by res judicata, and that Plaintiffs failed to attach or recite the contract terms verbatim.

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

Plaintiffs allege they and Defendants entered into a business relationship to start a cannabis dispensary. Defendants failed to pay back Plaintiffs’ investment in the business; Plaintiffs filed an earlier action to recover their funds. The parties eventually entered into the settlement agreement at issue here. Plaintiffs now allege that Defendants breached the settlement in these ways: (1) Defendants failed to pay utilities totaling approximately $320,000 prior to October 31, 2022; (2) Defendants breached their contractual representation that they were not in default under the terms of the sublease; and (3) Defendants failure to return the Mercedes Sprinter Van for which Plaintiffs paid approximately $65,000. This is sufficient to allege a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

The decision in a prior proceeding is res judicata when it is final and on the merits; the successor action is based on the same cause of action as the former one; and the parties to the former proceeding are the parties in the successor action or are in privity with them. (See Zevnik v. Superior Court (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 76, 82-83; Levy v. Cohen (1977) 19 Cal.3d 165, 171.) Defendants assert certain alleged breaches of the settlement agreement that were addressed in the underlying action. But the Court cannot sustain a demurrer as to piecemeal allegations in the complaint. Nor can the Court consider evidence or facts outside of the four corners of the complaint.

 

Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

B. Motion to Strike

“Any party, within the time allowed to response to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part" of that pleading. (CCP § 435(b)(1).) “The Court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false or improper matter asserted in any pleading; (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the Court." (CCP § 436.)

Defendants move to strike Plaintiffs’ prayer for punitive damages on the ground that this is a standard breach of contract action with no allegations of malice, fraud, or oppression. The Court agrees. There are no factual allegations supporting punitive damages. Plaintiffs do not request leave to amend.

Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED.
