                        Plaintiffs,

ORDER RE:

 

HYUNG M. DO’S MOTION FOR ORDER COMPELLING GENOVEVA RINCON’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE) AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS IN THE AMOUNT OF $3,599.77

 

Plaintiff Hyung M. Do (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order compelling Defendant Genoveva Rincon (“Rincon”) to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories (Set One) and for monetary sanctions against Rincon in the amount of $3,599.77.

Plaintiff’s motion will be continued to a new date as set forth below. NO HEARING WILL TAKE PLACE ON OCTOBER 1, 2024.   

Pursuant to the Court’s power to “amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(8)), the Court orders the parties in this case to participate in a meet and confer with the Court (“Informal Discovery Conference” or “IDC”). (Note:¿The Court’s policy regarding IDCs appears in the Courtroom Information available in Dept. 50 and on the Court’s website.)[1]¿Lead or other designated counsel for the parties with full authority and any self-represented party are ordered to participate in person in an IDC. After consulting with opposing counsel and/or any self-represented party regarding available dates, Plaintiff must make a prompt reservation for the IDC using the Court’s online reservation system. Plaintiff must file Dept. 50’s one-page IDC form in the department seven days prior to the IDC, and the responding parties may file the same form in the department setting forth a response three days prior to the IDC.  

Once Plaintiff has confirmed an IDC date, Plaintiff must use the Court’s online reservation system to continue the motion to a post-IDC discovery hearing date. The parties are ordered to have with them whatever materials are needed to make the IDC session productive and successful. Prior to the IDC date, lead or other designated counsel for the parties with full authority and any self-represented party are to meet and confer, in person or via telephone in a further attempt to resolve as many of the issues as possible before the IDC. (See CRC Rule 3.670, subd. (f)(2).) If the parties resolve their discovery disputes before the IDC date, Plaintiff is ordered to take both the IDC and the motion off calendar as soon as possible. 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this Order. 

 

DATED:  October 1, 2024                             ________________________________

Hon. Teresa A. Beaudet

Judge, Los Angeles Superior Court


[1]The Court notes that Plaintiff filed an IDC Statement on April 9, 2024 regarding, inter alia, Form Interrogatories Plaintiff served on Rincon. Plaintiff’s April 9, 2024 IDC Statement references an April 16, 2024 IDC hearing date. However, the docket for this case does not appear to show that an IDC took place on April 16, 2024 or on any other date. It appears the April 16, 2024 IDC was taken off calendar.

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTIONS

 

 

 

Background

On April 22, 2022, Plaintiff KCS West, Inc. (“KCS”) filed this action against Defendants 5959 LLC (“5959”) and Koar Airport Associates, LLC. The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) foreclosure of mechanic’s lien, (2) breach of contract, (3) common count – quantum meruit, and (4) violation of prompt payment statutes.  

On July 15, 2022, a Cross-Complaint was filed in this action by 5959 against KCS and Kajima U.S.A. Inc. (“Kajima”). 5959 filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint in this action on May 26, 2023 against KCS, Kajima, and Kohler Co. (“Kohler”) alleging causes of action for

(1) breach of contract, (2) express indemnity, (3) statutory indemnity, (4) recovery on guarantee agreement, (5) strict products liability, (6) breach of implied warranty of merchantability, and

(7) breach of implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose.   

            In its First Amended Cross-Complaint, 5959 alleges, inter alia, that it “entered into a prime construction contract dated March 29, 2018 (‘Contract’) with cross-defendants KCSW and Roes 1 through 10 (collectively referred to in the singular as ‘KCSW’) under which KCSW agreed to serve as the general contractor for the construction of extensive renovations to an existing commercial office building located at 5959 West Century Boulevard, Los Angeles, California, and conversation [sic] of the building to a Hyatt Hotel facility (‘Project’).” (FACC, ¶ 7.) 5959 alleges that “Kohler and ROES 21-30 (collectively referred to in the singular as ‘Kohler’) designed, distributed, produced, manufactured, marketed, or sold the Kohler Bellwether Shower Base (‘Shower Pans’) incorporated into the Project.” (FACC, ¶ 29.) 5959 alleges that “[t]he Shower Pans contained a manufacturing or product defect when they left the possession of Kohler,” and that “[t]he Shower Pans and/or their component parts are defective and fail to perform their intended use.” (FACC, ¶¶ 30-31.) 5959 further alleges that “KCSW has breached the Contract by failing to perform all of its obligations under the Contract including, but not limited to, failing to provide and install non-defective shower pans in the guest room bathrooms at the Project, and failing to defend and protect [5959] from and against mechanic’s liens and mechanic’s lien foreclosure actions by various subcontractors of KCSW on the Project.” (FACC, ¶ 9.)

On August 30, 2022, KCS filed a Cross-Complaint against 5959 and Johnson Diversified, Inc., dba Plumbing Solutions (“Plumbing Solutions”). KCS filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint on September 16, 2022 against 5959 and Plumbing Solutions, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) negligence, (3) breach of contract third party beneficiary,

(4) express indemnification, (5) implied indemnification, (6) declaratory relief, (7) strict product liability, (8) breach of express/implied warranty of merchantability, (9) breach of express/implied warranty of fitness for a particular purpose, (10) strict product liability - failure to warn, and (11) negligence - duty to warn. 

On July 5, 2023, Kohler filed a Cross-Complaint in this action against KCS and Plumbing Solutions, alleging causes of action for (1) indemnification, (2) apportionment of fault, (3) declaratory relief, and (4) express indemnity.  

On October 3, 2023, KCS and Kajima filed a Cross-Complaint against Kohler. The Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) equitable indemnity, (2) contribution, and (3) declaratory relief. The Cross-Complaint alleges, inter alia, that “[i]n equity and in good conscience, if 5959 recovers against Cross-Complainants, then Cross-Complainants are entitled to equitable indemnity, apportionment of liability, contribution among, and from, Cross-Defendants, and each of them, according to their respective fault, for the injuries and damages allegedly sustained by 5959, if any, by way of sums paid by settlement, or in the alternative, any judgements rendered against Cross-Complainants in the action herein based upon 5959’s FACC.” (Cross-Compl., ¶ 10.)[1]

On March 6, 2023, the Court issued a minute order in this action providing, inter alia, that “[t]he Court orders the following cases, 22STCV13462, 22TRCV00350, and…22TRCV00639, consolidated and assigned to Department 50 in Stanley Mosk Courthouse for all purposes. The Court designates 22STCV13462 as the lead case.”  

KCS now moves “to amend its Cross-Complaint as against Kohler Co., only.” Kohler opposes.

Discussion

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” Amendment may be allowed at any time before or after commencement of trial. ((Id., § 576.) “[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings. The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428 [internal citations omitted].) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend…” ((Morgan v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments. (¿¿Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)¿¿ The motion must also state what allegations are proposed to be deleted or added, by page, paragraph, and line number.  (¿Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd¿. (a).) Finally, “¿[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1)¿The effect of the amendment; (2)¿Why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3)¿When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4)¿The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)

Exhibit N to KCS’s counsel’s declaration is a copy of KCS and Kajima’s proposed “First Amended Cross-Complaint as to Kohler Co.” (the “FACC”). (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(a), Ex. N.) KCS also provides a “copy of a redline of the proposed amended Cross-Complaint against the original Cross-Complaint.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(a), Ex. O.) The proposed FACC against Kohler adds new causes of action for “rescission – Civil Code Section 1689,” negligent misrepresentation, and fraud/intentional misrepresentation. (Ibid.)

In his supporting declaration, KCS’s counsel states that “[t]he KCS West team appears to have first noticed issues with the Shower Pans at the Project in or about December 2019 or January 2020.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 3(a).) KCS’s counsel further states that “emails and letters from Kohler in 2020 and early 2021 show that representatives of Kohler repeatedly sought to reassure KCS West that the defects in the Shower Pans was merely ‘cosmetic.’” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 3.) “In March 2021, KCS West and Kohler entered into a Settlement Agreement.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 4.)

KCS’s counsel states that “[o]n February 24, 2023, before Kohler was a party to this dispute, KCS West issued a subpoena to Kohler with various requests for documents.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 6.) KCS’s counsel asserts that “[o]n May 19, 2023, Kohler responded to KCS West’s subpoena with boilerplate objections and a production of some 52 pages of documents,” and that “Kohler failed to produce all documents in response to the subpoena, including those relating to a defect in the manufacturing process and reports of damage to the Shower Pans on other Projects.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶¶ 7-8.)

KCS’s counsel states that “[a]fter Kohler was joined in the action, KCS West served Kohler with party discovery. Among these were KCS West’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, which were served on Kohler on December 14, 2023. After extensions, Kohler responded to KCS West’s discovery on February 15, 2024.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 9.)

On June 14, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held. (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 11.) The Court’s June 14, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he parties attended the Informal Discovery Conference. The parties agreed and the Court ordered as follows: On or before July 10, 2024, Cross-Defendant Kohler Co., will serve further verified code compliant responses to the written discovery described in the June 12, 2024 email from Plaintiff and Cross-Complainant KCS West to Ms. Stone on behalf of Kohler, except no further response is required as to request for production numbers 12 and 22 propounded to Kohler by KCS West and special interrogatory numbers 25 and 27 propounded to Kohler by KCS West.”

KCS’s counsel states that “[o]n July 10, 2024, Kohler served further responses and produced further documents,” and asserts that “the new production contained many highly probative documents regarding the same defects showing up on Kohler shower pans at different locations throughout North America.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 12.) KCS’s counsel further states that “over the past several months, I learned that KCS West Consultants Gerald Zamiski and Anthony B. Lum held the opinion that the substantial cracking in the porcelain was due to constant residual tensile stress in the Shower Pan enamel created during the cooling process, and was a defect, not a ‘cosmetic’ condition.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 14.)

KCS’s counsel indicates that “[t]he proposed amendment to KCS West’s Cross-Complaint against Kohler would add three new causes of action to KCS West’s Cross-Complaint based on these new discoveries: (1) Rescission of the parties’ Settlement Agreement; (2) Negligent Misrepresentation; and (3) Fraud/Intentional Misrepresentation.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(a).) KCS’s counsel asserts that “[t]he proposed amendment is necessary and proper because KCS West only recently received the above-described highly probative evidence from Kohler indicating that Kohler knew that the Shower Pans suffered from a serious manufacturing defect and therefore intentionally or recklessly misrepresented the defect in the Shower Pans as ‘cosmetic’ in the lead-up to the execution of the Settlement Agreement.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(b).) KCS’s counsel also asserts that “KCS West has suffered monetary damage in an amount in excess of $100,000 in having to defend the cross-complaint by 5959 to have all the Shower Pans at the Property replaced, which is estimated to cost more than $2 million. KCS West has also incurred consultants’ fees and costs to investigate and test Kohler’s Shower Pans to determine the cause of the deterioration of their ceramic coating. Absent amendment to KCS West’s Cross-Complaint, KCS West would be deprived of meritorious grounds to recover for these damages.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(b).)

KCS’s counsel states that “[t]he facts giving rise to the amended allegations in KCS West’s Proposed Amended Cross-Complaint were discovered on or about July 2024, after KCS West reviewed Kohler’s newly produced documents and discovered ample evidence of Kohler’s knowledge of a serious manufacturing defect.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(c).) KCS’s counsel further states that “KCS West did not request amendment earlier because it was not aware of the above-described grounds to pursue the misrepresentation and rescission causes of action until Kohler produced its new documents in July 2024, which it had failed to produce for some eighteen (17) months since KCS West’s original February 2023 subpoena requesting said documents.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(d).)

In the opposition, Kohler asserts that “leave to amend should be denied due to KCS West’s unwarranted delay.” (Opp’n at p. 8:16-17.) Kohler asserts that “KCS West claims that it just found out about other hotels experiencing the same issues with the shower pans when Kohler produced documents in July 2024, but this is certainly not new information…This information was known to KCS West at least 4 years ago.” (Opp’n at pp. 8:26-9:2.) In support of this assertion, Kohler appears to rely on a September 28, 2020 email authored by Kohler representative Amanda Hildebrand. (Cotter Decl., ¶¶ 4, 10, Ex. H.) The September 28, 2020 email indicates inter alia, that “[w]e have a call into the GM from a hotel near Riverside who recently had corner crazes repaired on some of their cast iron receptors. Once we receive approval to share their contact information, we will do so.” (Cotter Decl., ¶ 10, Ex. H.)

            Kohler also asserts that “as outlined from the emails and letter authored by Jon Staabs…KCS West knew about the ‘defective’ condition of Kohler shower pans…as early as 2020 – long before KCS West ever entered into a settlement agreement with Kohler, long before KCS West filed the instant lawsuit against 5959, long before KCS West filed its Cross-Complaint against Kohler, and certainly long before the present motion for leave to amend.” (Opp’n at p. 9:3-9.) Kohler provides a September 9, 2020 email from Jon Staabs providing, inter alia, that “I do have information from Thursday and Friday inspection that I wanted to share. 90 of 207 were found to have corner craze. 18 pans were found to have drop damage found at the feet. This is a crack in the enamel down to the cast iron caused by dropping the shower pan. I have attached the Kohler drop test for your reference. This is not a manufacturing defect and can be repaired.” (Cotter Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. C.)

Kohler also indicates that on July 29, 2020, Jon Staabs sent an email stating, inter alia, “[t]he corner craze was not on all receptors. Different machines run the receptor and at different times. Either a receptor had it or it didn’t. It will not develop over time. It will not get worse over time. They are not being resurfaced. The corner craze are thin surface cracks in the glass like enamel finish on top of the cast iron. Many don’t go all the way through and are not visible without a couple inch away inspection with the eye. They will be taped off, repaired and warrantied.” (Cotter Decl., Ex. J.)[2]  

Kohler also argues in the opposition that “while KCS West’s counsel claims in his declaration that he first learned about Mr. Zamiski and Mr. Lum’s opinions ‘over the past several months’…we know that this is untrue. The opinions of KCS West’s experts have been known to Kohler for more than a year. They were certainly known to KCS West and their attorneys long before that.” (Opp’n at p. 9:16-21, emphasis omitted.) Kohler submits the Declaration of Chad Jorgensen, who has “been employed by Kohler Co since August 2006...” (Jorgensen Decl., ¶ 1.) Mr. Jorgensen states that “I personally participated in several joint laboratory examinations and testing of two shower pans which were removed from the LAX Hyatt hotel. They took place at Vollmer-Gray Engineering Laboratories and both Mr. Zamiski and Mr. Lum were present. The first of these occurred on September 14, 2022, and additional inspections took place on January 4th, 5th, 17th, and 18th of 2023.” (Jorgensen Decl., ¶ 4.) Mr. Jorgensen further states that “[o]n September 12, 2023, I attended a joint expert roundtable discussion with counsel and experts for all parties. The main purpose of the meeting was for Mr. Zamiski and Mr. Lum to present their findings and opinions following the several rounds of destructive testing that occurred in January 2023. There were several other representatives present on behalf of KCS West, Inc. including one expert, Shant Markarian, and two attorneys, Lisa Dal Gallo and Edgar Navarrete.” (Jorgensen Decl., ¶ 5.) Mr. Jorgensen states that “[d]uring this call, Mr. Zamiski and Mr. Lum opined that the shower pans contained manufacturing defects due to tensile residual stress in the enamel, carbon flecks, and glass layer bubbles.” (Jorgensen Decl., ¶ 6.)

In the reply, KCS counters that it “promptly sought to assert its proposed cross-claims after receiving a long-delayed production of documents revealing Kohler’s knowledge, an essential element of misrepresentation.” (Reply at p. 3:17-19.) As discussed, KCS’s counsel asserts that the documents served on July 10, 2024 “contained many highly probative documents regarding the same defects showing up on Kohler shower pans at different locations throughout North America.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 12.) KCS points to a September 22, 2020 email from a Kohler representative with the subject line “Doubletree Westside LA Kohler Bellwether Receptor,” providing, inter alia, that “[c]ertainly it is your choice to allow for the repairs or not, but we do want to make sure that everyone is clear that we cannot guarantee that the cracking found in the corners of the bases will not get worse over time. It is possible they could become too damaged to repair and would no longer be able to be handled under our warranty. We want to make sure all involved are satisfied with the bases today and over time…” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 12, Ex. M.)

KCS asserts that it “did not know that Kohler told at least one other hotel that the ‘cracking’ in the corners might actually get worse when Kohler had informed KCS West of exactly the opposite just months before.” (Reply at p. 4:2-4, emphasis omitted.) KCS points to the July 29, 2020 email from Jon Staabs providing, inter alia, “[t]he corner craze was not on all receptors. Different machines run the receptor and at different times. Either a receptor had it or it didn’t. It will not develop over time. It will not get worse over time. They are not being resurfaced. The corner craze are thin surface cracks in the glass like enamel finish on top of the cast iron. Many don’t go all the way through and are not visible without a couple inch away inspection with the eye. They will be taped off, repaired and warrantied.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 3(b), Ex. B.) KCS also provides a May 13, 2020 email from Jon Staabs providing, inter alia, that “[w]hile on site visit, he found some receptors had craze in the corner. This is a cosmetic issue on the surface of the receptor and will not impact the form or function of the receptor itself.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 3(a), Ex. A.) As discussed, KCS argues that Kohler “misrepresented the defect in the Shower Pans as ‘cosmetic’ in the lead-up to the execution of the Settlement Agreement.” (Baghdassarian Decl., ¶ 15(b).)

Based on a consideration of the foregoing, the Court finds that KCS has sufficiently shown that there was not unwarranted delay in bringing the instant motion.

Kohler’s counsel also states in his supporting declaration that “[t]he three new causes of action proposed by KCS West are for (1) rescission of the settlement agreement, (2) intentional misrepresentation / fraud, and (3) negligent misrepresentation. There has been zero discovery into these issues in this litigation to date.” (Cotter Decl., ¶ 14.) Kohler’s counsel states that “Kohler would be required to propound written discovery and to take depositions into the issues of who, when, how, and to whom each alleged misrepresentation was made, into issues regarding contract formation of the subject settlement agreement, and into issues of KCS West’s alleged damages.” (Cotter Decl., ¶ 16.) In addition, Kohler’s counsel asserts that “Kohler would also be greatly prejudiced if it is not afforded an opportunity to file motions for summary judgment or adjudication into the intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, and rescission causes of action…These causes of action involve highly prejudicial claims, such as claims for punitive damages, and the rescission cause of action in particular involves questions of law for the Court to decide. As such, Kohler would be greatly prejudiced if such claims were allowed to go to a jury trial…on short notice.” (Cotter Decl., ¶¶ 17-18.)

In the reply, KCS asserts that “there is no undue prejudice to Kohler in granting the motion.” (Reply at p. 8:5.) KCS asserts that “[t]he parties have not conducted any depositions thus far and KCS West has agreed in a meeting with counsel to produce KCS West’s key decision maker at the time of these misrepresentations for deposition.” (Reply at p. 8:17-19.) KCS also argues that “KCS West has been diligently pursuing discovery on defects in Shower Pans at other locations since February 2023. Kohler waited until less than 5 months before trial to release highly probative documents regarding the pervasiveness and severity of manufacturing defects in the Shower Pans, and only after being ordered by the Court after the IDC hearing. It is because of Kohler that KCS West is forced to file this Motion at this time.” (Reply at p. 8:6-11.)

In the opposition, Kohler also asserts that “opinions or predictions about future performance of the shower pans cannot form the basis of a misrepresentation claim.” (Opp’n at p. 11:1-3.) In addition, Kohler argues that “public policy dictates that the settlement agreement should not be undone.” (Opp’n at p. 12:4-5.) Further, Kohler argues that “neither KCS West’s motion for leave to amend, nor the proposed First Amended Cross-Complaint, provide any competent or admissible evidence of the exact alleged misrepresentation made by Kohler, to whom the alleged misrepresentation was made, or how the unnamed representative of KCS West specifically relied upon such alleged misrepresentation in being induced into entering the settlement agreement.” (Opp’n at p. 13:9-13.) However, in Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048, cited by KCS in the motion, the Court of Appeal noted that “the preferable practice would be to permit the amendment and allow the parties to test its legal sufficiency by demurrer, motion for judgment on the pleadings or other appropriate proceedings.” Thus, the Court does not find that the asserted legal deficiency of the proposed FACC warrants denial of leave to amend.           

Based on a consideration of the foregoing, the Court finds that KCS has demonstrated good cause to file the proposed FACC.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, KCS’s motion for leave to file a first amended cross-complaint is granted. The Court orders KCS to file and serve the first amended cross-complaint within 3 days of the date of this Order.¿¿

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTION FOR ORDER SUBSTITUTING WENDY ROSEN AS SUCCESSOR IN INTEREST TO DECEDENT, PLAINTIFF CINDY ROSEN

 

 

Background

On February 22, 2023, Plaintiff Cindy Rosen (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Jay Bloom, Murder Inc., LLC, The Mafia Collection, LLC, Julien Entertainment.Com, Inc. and Julien’s Auction House, LLC.

On August 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint, alleging causes of action for (1) conversion, (2) unjust enrichment, (3) intentional misrepresentation,

(4) financial elder abuse, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (6) unfair competition, and (7) negligence.   

            Wendy Rosen now moves “for an order permitting her, as successor in interest to decedent Plaintiff CINDY ROSEN, to proceed in this action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §377.30, et seq.” The motion is unopposed.

Discussion

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 377.30, “[a] cause of action that survives the death of the person entitled to commence an action or proceeding passes to the decedent’s successor in interest, subject to Chapter 1 (commencing with Section 7000) of Part 1 of Division 7 of the Probate Code, and an action may be commenced by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 377.31, “[o]n motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” Code of Civil Procedure section 377.11 provides that “[f]or the purposes of this chapter, ‘decedent’s successor in interest’ means the beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of a cause of action.”

Wendy Rosen cites to Code of Civil Procedure section 377.32, which provides that “[t]he person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest under this article, shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following:

(1) The decedent’s name.

(2) The date and place of the decedent’s death.

(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate.”

(4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest.

(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof:

(A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.”

(B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.”

(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.”

(7) “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (a).)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 377.32, subdivision (c), “[a] certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration.”

On June 18, 2024, a notice of errata was filed attaching the Declaration of Wendy Rosen. In her supporting declaration, Wendy Rosen states that “Decedent is CINDY ROSEN, who died on November 11, 2023, at Henderson, Nevada. She was m

.+y natural sister. Attached hereto as Exhibit ‘A’ is a true and correct copy of the Certificate of Death of Cindy Beth Rosen.” (Wendy Rosen Decl., ¶ 2.) Wendy Rosen states that “[t]here is no proceeding pending now in California for administration of the CINDY ROSEN’s estate.” (Wendy Rosen Decl., ¶ 3.)

Wendy Rosen further states that “[o]n February 1, 2024, I was issued Letters of Administration by the District Court in Clark County, Nevada, Case No. P-24-119222-E, wherein I was appointed Special Administrator of the Estate of Cindy Beth Rosen, deceased, (‘Estate’), providing me with sole authority to act on behalf of the Estate for purposes of maintaining the within action.” (Wendy Rosen Decl., ¶ 4.) Exhibit “B” to Wendy Rosen’s declaration is “Letters of Special Administration” filed in Case No. P-24-119222-E, In the Matter of the Estate of Cindy Beth Rosen, in the District Court of Clark County, Nevada. (Wendy Rosen Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. B.) The Letters of Special Administration provide, inter alia, that “Special Administrator, Wendy Rosen may act and have sole authority to act on behalf of the Estate for purposes of maintaining the pending case of Cindy Rosen v. Jay Bloom, an individual; Murder Inc., LLC, a Nevada limited liability company; Julien Entertainment.com, Inc., a California corporation; and Julien’s Auction House, LLC, a California limited liability company, which is Case No. 23STCV03826, in the Superior Court of the State of California in Los Angeles County.” (Wendy Rosen Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. B, underline in original.)

However, Wendy Rosen’s declaration does not appear to state either of the following, “with facts in support thereof: (A) ‘The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.’ (B) ‘The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.’” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (a)(5).)

In addition, Wendy Rosen’s declaration does not appear to state that “[n]o other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (a)(6).)

In light of the foregoing, the Court does not find that Wendy Rosen’s supporting declaration provides all of the necessary information under Code of Civil Procedure section 377.32, subdivision (a).

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Wendy Rosen’s motion is denied without prejudice.

Wendy Rosen is ordered to give notice of this Order.¿

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANTS [sic] 1346 STANLEY, LLC MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT UNDER MANDATORY PROVISION OF C.C.P. § 473(b)

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Nawal Bengholam (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on October 27, 2023, against Defendants 1346 Stanley, LLC (“1346 Stanley”), Stanley 1, LLC, and Charles & Cynthia Eberly Inc. The Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) breach of contract/covenant of quiet enjoyment/warranty of habitability, (2) tortious breach of the implied warranty of habitability, (3) negligence, (4) violation of unfair business practices, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (6) wrongful eviction.

            On January 18, 2024, default was entered against 1346 Stanley.

            On February 23, 2024, 1346 Stanley filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff. The Cross-Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) implied indemnity, (2) express indemnity,

(3) declaratory relief, (4) contribution, (5) negligence, and (6) breach of contract.

1346 Stanley now moves for an order setting aside the default entered against it. Plaintiff opposes.

Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) provides in pertinent part:

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”

 “[B]ecause the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.” ((Elston v. City of Turlock (1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 233 [negative treatment on other grounds].) Where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and no prejudice to the opposing party will result from setting aside the default, “very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.” (Ibid.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) also contains a mandatory provision: “Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”

In support of the motion, 1346 Stanley submits the declaration of its counsel, who states that “[o]n May 7, 2024, counsel for Defendants [sic][1] became aware that they were in default, and that their declaration had not extended the deadline by 30 days as anticipated…” (Derflinger Decl., ¶ 4.) The Court notes that 1346 Stanley’s counsel does not appear to specify what “declaration” he is referring to. 1346 Stanley’s counsel further states that “[o]n that same day, counsel for Defendants [sic] requested counsel for Plaintiffs [sic] agree to stipulate to set aside the default. Counsel for Plaintiff did not respond.” (Derflinger Decl., ¶ 4.)

1346 Stanley’s counsel states that “[d]efault was entered against Defendants [sic] as a result of their attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, and/or neglect – i.e., my mistake, inadvertence and neglect. The entry of default against Defendants [sic] was not due to any conduct or neglect of Defendants [sic] themselves. The error was solely attributable to Defendants’ [sic] counsel.” (Derflinger Decl., ¶ 5.)

1346 Stanley indicates that it is “moving for mandatory relief from entry of default under C.C.P. § 473(b) on the grounds of their [sic] attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Mot. at p. 2:8-9, emphasis omitted.)

In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that 1346 Stanley’s motion is untimely, as it was not filed within six months of the entry of default. However, as discussed, 1346 Stanley seeks relief under the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), which provides that “[n]otwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Emphasis added.) No judgment has yet been entered in this matter. Accordingly, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has shown that the instant motion is untimely.

Plaintiff also argues that “STANLEY’s counsel contends that they filed a declaration regarding an extension of time to meet and confer on the pleadings which is the reason they were late in the filing of their responsive pleading. No such declaration was attached, filed with the court, or sent to Plaintiff’s counsel likely because it does not exist. Absent this, they have provided no excuse for not timely responding to the complaint or not timely filing this motion.” (Opp’n at p. 3:4-8.) However, 1346 Stanley’s counsel states in his declaration under penalty of perjury that “[d]efault was entered against Defendants [sic] as a result of their [sic] attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, and/or neglect – i.e., my mistake, inadvertence and neglect.” (Derflinger Decl., ¶ 5.) The Court notes that under the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), the attorney’s neglect does not need to be excusable. ((Henderson v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 215, 225.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants 1346 Stanley’s motion to set aside the default under the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).

Lastly, the Court notes that under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), “[t]he court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.” Here, however, Plaintiff has not provided evidence of any legal fees or costs.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, 1346 Stanley’s motion is granted. The default entered against 1346 Stanley on January 18, 2024 is ordered set aside.

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

           

Plaintiff Circa 1200, LLC (“Plaintiff”) requests that the Court enter default judgment against Defendant Darius Kennedy (“Kennedy”). Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $111,265.90, comprising $99,712.22 demanded in the Complaint, $10,353.68 in interest, $1,006.00 in costs, and $194.00 in attorney fees.

The Court notes a few defects with the submitted default judgment package.

First, Plaintiff filed a proof of service on April 12, 2024 indicating that the summons and Complaint were served on Kennedy by mail and acknowledgment of receipt of service, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40. However, the proof of service does not indicate the date on which the documents were served. (See April 12, 2024 proof of service, Item 5(c)(1).) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40, “[a] summons may be served on a person outside this state in any manner provided by this article or by sending a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served by first-class mail, postage prepaid, requiring a return receipt. Service of a summons by this form of mail is deemed complete on the 10th day after such mailing.”

Second, Plaintiff’s counsel has not submitted any declaration attesting to the amount of attorney’s fees requested. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies the request for default judgment without prejudice. The Court will discuss with counsel a schedule for submitting a new default judgment package.
