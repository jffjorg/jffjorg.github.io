DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01458    Hearing Date: April 3, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY DAVIS, et al. v. PAIGE HEMMIS, et al.

 

PLAINTIFFS KIMBERLY DAVIS AND JONATHAN DAVIS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF PERSON MOST QUALIFIED OF GOOGLE, LLC

 

Date of Hearing:        April 3, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV01458

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis

Responding Party:     Non-Party Google, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a third amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125).

 

On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution. Cross-Complainants dismissed their cause of action for express indemnification.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis’ Motion to Compel Deposition of Person Most Qualified of Google, LLC is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of several court documents (RJN Exhs. 1-7.) The court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis move the court for an order compelling Google, LLC to provide the person(s) most qualified for Google to be deposed. Plaintiffs make the motion on the grounds that Google, LLC has failed to provide a person most knowledgeable from Google for deposition, which seek basic information that is highly material to the adjudication of this matter. Plaintiffs also request this court award Plaintiffs an award of $11,890 against Google, LLC as sanctions for having to file this motion.

 

Any party may obtain discovery by taking in California the oral deposition of any person.¿ (CCP § 2025.010.)¿ A deposition subpoena may command the attendance and the testimony of a nonparty deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things.¿(CCP § 2020.020.)  If a nonparty deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.¿ (CCP §§ 2024.480, 2025.480.)¿¿¿ 

 

Plaintiffs argue in this case, Google, LLC has entirely refused to produce a “person most qualified” witness even though the identity of the person who created and utilized the kadavislaw1967@gmail.com email address is one of the biggest issues in this case. Plaintiffs contend this entire case stems from the fact that someone electronically forged the Plaintiffs' name on a multi-year lease with an option to buy in an effort to steal the Plaintiffs' home. Plaintiffs further argue Google, LLC’s objections are meritless.

 

In opposition, Google argues it has already authenticated those records by a certificate of authenticity, as permitted by California law, and the records produced speak for themselves. Nothing more is required from Google. Google further argues it is not clear that it could provide the kind of testimony that Plaintiffs require. Google contends it can only provide Plaintiffs with the data it has and Plaintiffs must make their own conclusions from the data, either by expert witness or otherwise. Google cannot establish who was actually sitting at a computer at the time the account was registered or used.

The court finds the Certificate of Authenticity does not obviate the need to depose Google, LLC’s PMK. It is true business records may be authenticated by a certificate of authenticity. (Evid. Code §§1561, 1562.) However, the law does not permit a witness to submit a certificate of authenticity in lieu of live testimony. Plaintiffs have demonstrated the answers sought are subject to discovery as many of the records are not self-explanatory and could readily be clarified in a short deposition. If Google is unable to give a truthful and responsive answer to the questions asked, Google may state so. Moreover, it does not appear to this court Plaintiff is seeking to elicit expert testimony from Google but only basic factual information and the meaning of the terms by Google.  

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests $11,890 in monetary sanctions to be awarded against Google for Google’s refusal to sit for a deposition.

 

“If a deponent on whom a deposition subpoena has been served fails to attend a deposition or refuses to be sworn as a witness, the court may impose on the deponent the sanctions described in¿Section 2020.240.” (CCP § 2025.440(b).)  “If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with¿Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2025.450(g)(1).)

 

Google argues Google fully complied with its discovery obligations under the Subpoena, raised meritorious objections, informed Plaintiffs that it would not appear for deposition, engaged in meet and confer efforts with Plaintiff, and offered to amend its certificate of authenticity as needed. As such, sanctions aren’t warranted.

 

The court finds that Google acted with substantial justification.  The request is DENIED. 


Case Number: 23VECV03956    Hearing Date: April 3, 2024    Dept: W

RAUL ULISES SEGOVIA v. VOLKSWAGEN GROUP OF AMERICA, INC.

 

PlaintiffS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE

 

Date of Hearing:        April 3, 2024                                      Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03956

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Raul Ulises Segovia

Responding Party:     Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises in the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. On September 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant for 1. Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Express Warranty; 2. Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Implied Warranty; and 3. Violation of The Song- Beverly Act Section 1793.2.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED, in part.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff Raul Ulises Segovia moves for an order to strike Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.’s objections and compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Request Nos. 1 – 31. Plaintiff brings the motion on the grounds that Defendant´s objections are not code-compliant and that Defendant failed to provide adequate responses to Plaintiff’s RFPs, which seek documents relevant to the asserted Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act causes of action.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections. (CCP § 2031.310(c).) “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2031.310] shall comply with both of the following: (1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand. (2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿(CCP § 2031.310(b).) In addition, a separate statement is required.¿(CRC 3.1345(a)(3).)

 

Plaintiff argues Requests 1 through 14 seek documents relating to Plaintiff’s own vehicle. To prevail on a claim under Song-Beverly, Plaintiff must prove a nonconformity with a vehicle that substantially impaired its use, value, or safety that Defendant, and/or its authorized representatives, failed to repair after a reasonable number of attempts. Requests 15 through 29 seek documents reflecting Defendant’s written warranties and the policies and procedures, which would support Plaintiff’s claim for civil penalties in that it will show Defendant’s awareness of a prevalent defect which neither Defendant, nor its authorized repair facilities, could repair. Lastly, Requests 30 and 31 seek documents related to Defendant’s knowledge of the same or similar defects in other vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle. According to Plaintiff, this would show whether the manufacturer knew of the alleged defects, whether this knowledge be from evidence that the Subject Vehicle was not repaired after a reasonable number of attempts, or by virtue of internal investigations and warranty records of other related vehicles showing that other consumers were similarly affected.

 

Defendant first opposes the motion on the grounds Plaintiff failed to meet and confer in good faith prior to bringing the motion. Defendant contends their counsel offered his availability to telephonically meet and confer yet Plaintiff did not once bother to contact Volkswagen’s counsel telephonically to meet and confer. Rather, Plaintiff served two generic and inaccurate letters, then filed this Motion. Plaintiff disputes the meet and confer was insufficient. Regardless, as the correspondence makes clear, the parties have reached an impasse as to this issue and the court will address the motion on its merits.  (See Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 332.) 

 

Defendant next opposes the motion on the grounds it has not waived objections regarding ESI. A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §2031.300(a).) Section 2031.210(d) further provides that a party that objects to the discovery of electronically stored information “on the grounds that it is from a source that is not reasonably accessible because of undue burden or expense and that the responding party will not search the source in the absence of an agreement with the demanding party or court order” must specifically identify the types or categories of sources of electronically stored information that it asserts are not readily accessible.  (CCP § 2031.210(d).)  

 

First, Plaintiff has not demonstrated the responses were untimely. Next, Defendant objected to the requests on grounds including that the requests were burdensome and overbroad. Although the court grants Plaintiffs’ motion, the court finds that Defendant was substantially justified in initially raising its objections and that it did not waive its right to object to the scope of ESI.  

 

Defendant next argues this court should deny the motion as to RFP Nos. 1-14 because Defendant has already provided substantive responses and produced responsive documents. Specifically, the documents produced in response to RFP Nos. 1-11 and 13-14 were organized as Exhibits A through I. As for No. 12, Defendant provided that “… [a] diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with the demand; however, the particular item or category has never existed.” (Petrosyan Decl. ¶, Exh.E.) If Defendant has not produced all substantive documents in their possession, they are ordered to do so. The court finds the objections are without merit. Defendant fails to articulate what expert information is necessary, as this is a request for production of documents that already exist.

 

As for RFP Nos. 15-29, Defendant argues the documents at issue are confidential internal documents regarding Defendant’s policies and procedures of Defendant’s warranty, repurchase, and customer service programs and related training information. Despite this, Defendant has already produced 485 pages of confidential documents regarding its policies/procedures and training materials pertaining to the pre-litigation handling of repurchase/replacement requests pursuant to the Stipulation. Defendant notes RFP No. 22 is not in their possession and as for Nos. 23-29, Defendant has already produced its policies and procedures provided to authorized repair facilities.

 

The court agrees the requests are overbroad and moreover, the production must be upon execution of the standard Stipulation and Protective Order. For example, No. 21 asks “All DOCUMENTS evidencing and/or describing YOUR training materials related to YOUR policy regarding how to calculate a repurchase.” However, this request is overbroad as it is without limitation as to whether the training materials at issue would apply to Plaintiff’s vehicle, during the time Plaintiff owned the vehicle, or to California consumers. As such, all requests should be limited to such and produced as such pursuant to the executed Protective Order.

 

Lastly, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s request for further response to RFP Nos. 30 and 31 should be denied because Plaintiff fails to establish good cause and moreover, the objections by Defendant are proper as the requests are vague, overbroad and unduly burdensome. The court disagrees. Plaintiff states Defendant’s knowledge of the same or similar defects in other vehicles of the same year, make, and model would establish Defendant’s knowledge and inability to repair the defects, as well as its failure to adequately compensate Plaintiff with respect to the Subject Vehicle despite Defendant’s inability to repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of repair opportunities. Next, the requests are not so vague. No. 30 defines “Substantially Similar” as “similar customer complaint that would be the same nature of the reported system, malfunction, trouble code, Technical Service Bulletin Recommendation, dashboard indicator light, or other manifestation of a repair problem, as description listed in any warranty summary or repair order for the SUBJECT VEHICLE.” (RFP No. 30.)  It also includes clarification that the request is not seeking routine or scheduled maintenance items. (Id.) Moreover, Defendant has failed to explain why it stands to be unduly burdened if it is required to produce such information.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED, in part.
