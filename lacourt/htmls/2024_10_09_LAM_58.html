DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV07478    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             October 9, 2024  

Case Name:                 Christopher Y. Chong v. Mardirossian Akaragian LLP, et al.

Case No.:                    23STCV07478

Motion:                       Motion to Tax Costs

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Christopher Y. Chong

Responding Party:      Defendant Mardirossian Akaragian LLP

 

Tentative Ruling:      Grant in part, striking $1,997.70 for a total costs award of $1,686.68..

 

 

Background

 

Plaintiff Christopher Y. Chong, by power of attorney and through his attorney-in-fact, step-father Harry Sloan (“Plaintiff”) sued defendants Mardirossian Akaragian LLP, Kroll Law Corporation, Blue Shield of America, and American International Group, Inc. on April 4, 2023, asserting a single claim for declaratory relief. Plaintiff alleged Mardirossian Akaragian represented him on a contingency basis in a personal injury suit where they recovered an approximate $6 million settlement. A dispute arose about how the proceeds should be allocated, so Plaintiff sought a declaration settling the dispute.

 

On July 31, 2024, the Court entered judgment for Mardirossian Akaragian (“Defendant”) in the amount of approximately $3.3 million, with Plaintiff to recover nothing.

 

Defendant filed a timely memorandum of costs on August 15, 2024, its bill amounting to $3,684.38.

 

Plaintiff filed a timely motion to tax Defendant’s costs bill on August 28, 2024. Defendant filed its opposition on September 25, 2024. Plaintiff replied on September 30, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032(b).)

 

A party claiming costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a).) The opposing party may then contest the costs by filing a motion to tax costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1034; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b).) “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774.)

 

/ / /

 

Costs and Objections

 

Defendant claims $1,255.00 in filing and motion fees, $194.23 in electronic filing and service fees, and $2,235.15 in “other” fees, which Defendant characterizes as costs for (1) photocopies, (2) attorney service, (3) hearing transcripts, (4) research, and (5) mileage.

 

Plaintiff moves to strike all costs in Defendant’s $2,235.15 “Other” category.

 

                        1. Photocopies

 

As to Defendant’s first subcategory, “photocopies”: the Code expressly enumerates “photocopying charges” as not allowable, “except for exhibits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(b)(3).) Because the charges appear improper on their face, it is Defendant’s burden to justify them upon Plaintiff’s objection.

 

Defendant argues that because it specifies the photocopies were of “court documents” and “subpoenaed records”, they were necessary and should be allowed. The Court has no discretion to make such an award; the discretion to award necessary costs only extends to costs “not mentioned [elsewhere] in [section 1033.5] ... .” (Id., subd. (c)(4); see also id., subd. (b) [enumerated costs not allowable “except when expressly authorized by law”.)

 

The Court may not award photocopying costs. The Court strikes $170.70 in photocopying costs from Defendant’s memorandum.

 

            2. Attorney Service

 

            Attorney service charges are not expressly enumerated in section 1033.5. The Court may award them at its discretion if they are reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(c)(3).) Here, the Court finds they are. The Court’s Department Guidelines instruct parties to submit courtesy copies. That is sufficient for the costs to be reasonably necessary. And $237.45 in attorney service costs is not unreasonable over the course of an entire litigation.

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to tax is denied as to attorney service charges. These costs are allowed.

 

                        3. Hearing Transcripts

 

            As with photocopies, section 1033.5(b) expressly disallows costs for “[t]ranscripts of court proceedings not ordered by the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(b)(5).) A claim for hearing transcripts is not proper on its face, so it is Defendant’s burden to justify it. In order to do so, they must show the transcripts were ordered by the Court. They did not.

 

            The Court strikes $158.75 from Defendant’s memorandum.

 

/ / /

 

                        4. Research

 

“Research” costs, which Defendant identifies as “charges for legal research on Westlaw” (Feit Decl., ¶ 16), are not expressly enumerated in section 1033.5. Because the costs do not appear proper on their face, Defendant bears the burden to justify them. Counsel’s testimony that legal research “[was] necessary to address the substantive legal issues raised in this action” does not establish why these particular research charges were necessary – or even related – to this action.

 

The Court strikes $1,498.25 from Defendant’s memorandum.

 

            5. Mileage for Court Appearances

 

            Mileage for court appearances is not expressly awardable under section 1033.5(b). Because they are not proper on their face, Defendant bears the burden to justify them as reasonably necessary and reasonable in amount. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5(c)(2)-(3).) Defendant does not show the costs are necessary; it accuses Plaintiff of failing to cite authority – which he does elsewhere in his moving papers – and of bringing the litigation wrongfully in the first place. The justice or injustice of the litigation itself does not bear on the reasonability of costs.

 

            The Court strikes $170.00 from Defendant’s memorandum.

 

Conclusion

 

            The Court grants Plaintiff’s motion in part, striking $1,997.70 from Defendant’s bill for a total costs award of $1,686.68.

Judge Bruce Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             October 9, 2024

Case Name:                 Corbel Architects, Inc., et al. v. Hot Hand Corporation, et al.

Case No.:                    23STCV21008

Motion:                       Request for Default Judgment

Moving Party:             Plaintiffs Corbel Architects, Inc. and David Seong Bae Kim

Responding Party:      N/A (Defaulted Defendants Hot Hand Corporation and Hyo Suk Kim)

 

Tentative Ruling:      Hearing on OSC continued for supplemental filings.

 

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs Corbel Architects, Inc. and David Seong Bae Kim sued defendants Hot Hand Corporation and Hyo Suk Kim on August 31, 2023 for breach of contract and related claims.

 

Plaintiffs now request entry of default judgment in against both defendants.

 

Applicable Law

 

Judgment may be entered against a defendant by default where “the defendant has been served, other than by publication, and no answer, demurrer, [or other responsive paper] has been filed with the clerk of the court within the time specified in the summons ... .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 585(b).) In such cases, the clerk shall enter default upon plaintiff’s written application and the plaintiff “thereafter may apply to the court for the relief demanded in the complaint.” (Ibid.)

 

A party seeking a default judgment on declarations must use the mandatory form CIV-100 and also file with the clerk (1) a brief summary of the case, (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support, (3) interest computations as necessary, (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements, (5) a declaration of non-military status, (6) a proposed form of judgment, (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought, (8) exhibits as necessary, and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees, if they are sought. (Cal. R. Ct., Rule 3.1800(a).)

 

Service and Default

 

Plaintiffs filed proofs of service indicated they served Defendants via mail with acknowledgment of receipt. The proofs of service were accompanied by Notices of Acknowledgment signed by defendant Hyo Suk Kim individually and as chief executive for Hot Hand Corporation. Plaintiffs’ counsel attested to mailing the necessary documents on March 7, 2024, setting defendants’ date to file a responsive pleading on April 16, 2024. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 415.40 [adding ten days to service on persons outside of state].)

 

Neither defendant filed a responsive pleading. The clerk entered Defendants’ defaults on September 16, 2024. Plaintiffs’ CIV-100 Request for Default contains a declaration of mailing and of nonmilitary status. Plaintiffs dismissed all fictitiously-named defendants on September 19, 2024.

 

Judgment Form, Notice of Damages, and Proof of Facts

 

Plaintiffs’ CIV-100 contains a statement of the amount of the judgment and a memorandum of costs, as well its own declarations of mailing and nonmilitary service. However, the statement is unsigned. A signed form must be submitted before the Court may enter judgment.

 

Plaintiffs request no more than what they expressly pray for in their complaint. Defendants had notice of their potential liability when they defaulted.

 

Plaintiffs’ complaint and the declaration of David Kim in support of judgment lay out the parties’ contract verbatim and attest that Corbel Architects performed all services required, but Defendants failed to pay $721,916.43 owed to Plaintiffs in return. They have proven Hot Hand’s liability to Corbel Architects.

 

However, the parties’ contract (Kim Decl., Exh. A) reveals that the contract was between Hot Hand Corporation and Corbel Architects only. Hyo Suk Kim is listed on the contract as CEO, and David Kim as Principal for Corbel Architects. Neither signed in their individual capacity. Kim’s declaration also explains “Hot Hand agreed to pay Corbel”, that Kim and his “team ... at Corbel Architects ... have performed all obligations”, and “Defendants have failed to pay the remaining balance ... to Corbel Architects, Inc.” (Kim Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4-5.) In fact, the declaration attests “Defendants are indebted to Corbel Architects”, not to Kim. (Id., ¶ 6.) The allegations in Plaintiffs’ complaint similarly establish Kim is not a party.

 

Once counsel cures the unsigned CIV-100, the Court may enter judgment against Hot Hand Corporation in Corbel Architects, Inc.’s favor. But Hyo Suk Kim must first be dismissed from the complaint, and David Kim may not recover from either defendant on the current record.

 

Prejudgment Interest

 

Plaintiffs are entitled to $197.79 in per diem damages beginning at least from the date their complaint was filed. That calculation, accounting for 405 days until the date of this hearing, results in an amount higher than the claim in Plaintiffs’ JUD-100. They are entitled to recover all the prejudgment interest claimed.

 

Fees

 

Fees upon default are awarded according to the schedule in Local Rule 3.214. For a principal judgment of $721,916.43, Plaintiffs are entitled to $2,890 plus 1% of the excess over $100,000.00. Plaintiffs request approximately $2,500.00 less than the amount in the schedule. The Court may award what is claimed.

 

Costs

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel attests to reasonable filing and service fees totaling $1,076.75.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court continues the hearing on Plaintiffs’ Request for Default to a date to be determined.

 

Prior to the continued hearing, Counsel is instructed to:

            (1) submit a signed CIV-100 Request for Entry of Judgment,

(2) prove up the individual plaintiff and individual defendant or dismiss the individual defendant from the complaint.
