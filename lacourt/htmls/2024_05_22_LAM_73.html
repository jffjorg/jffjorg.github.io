DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV08196    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: 73

5/22/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

LAURA PADILLA v. YOUTH POLICY INSTITUTE, INC., et al. (19STCV08196) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Laura Padilla (Pro Per) 

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Lyne Richardson (Olgetree, Deakins, Nash, Smoak & Stewart, P.C.) 

 

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL (filed 8/16/2023) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 11, 2019, Plaintiff Lauren Padilla (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Youth Policy Institute, Inc. (“Youth Policy”) and Enrique Rodriguez (“Rodriguez”).

 

Plaintiff is a former employee of Defendant Youth Policy.  Youth Policy is in the business of transforming Los Angeles neighborhoods to reduce poverty, including the Youth Policy on Western Avenue, where all events alleged in the complaint occurred.  In June 2016, Defendant Youth Policy hired Plaintiff as a receptionist.  Plaintiff was later promoted to Program Assistant/Promotor.  Plaintiff alleges that, in March 2017, she was diagnosed with chronic migraines.  Because of her disability, Plaintiff was given special accommodations (i.e., more time to complete paperwork, more rest breaks, and not forced to do any physical activities).  In July 2017, Defendant Rodriguez began working for Youth Policy.  Rodriguez allegedly began a campaign of harassment against Plaintiff by: (1) taking away Plaintiff’s accommodations, (2) singling Plaintiff out to do tasks that she could not do in attempt to humiliate and degrade her (e.g., moving heavy boxes and throwing out heavy bags of garbage); and (3) yelling at, berating, and embarrassing Plaintiff by making certain derogative comments as well as pushing her (see Compl., ¶¶ 19-25).  On February 28, 2019, Plaintiff’s employment was terminated.   

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for:  

 

C/A 1: Against Youth Policy for Disability Discrimination 

C/A 2: Against Youth Policy for Wrongful Termination  

C/A 3: Against Youth Policy for Retaliation (GC § 12940(h)) 

C/A 4: Against Defendants for IIED 

C/A 5: Against Defendants for Harassment 

 

On August 16, 2023, counsel for Defendants filed a Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Youth Policy and Rodriguez each. They also filed supporting declarations and proposed orders.  

 

No opposition or other response has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

California Rules¿of Court,¿rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-053)).¿ 

 

The¿court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted¿provided that¿there is no prejudice to the client, and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.¿¿(See¿Ramirez v.¿Sturdevant¿(1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)¿ 

 

B.     Discussion

Here, Defendants’ counsel, Lyne Richardson and Brittney Turner, seek relief under Code of Civil Procedure, Section 284(2). Counsel correctly completed and filed the notice (Form MC-051) and declaration (MC-052). (Code Civ. Proc., § 284(2); Cal. Rules Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a) & (c).) In the declaration, counsel sufficiently explains that this motion is being brought under Section 284(2) instead of 284(1) because Defendants Rodriguez and Youth Policy have breached a material term of their Attorney Retainer Agreement concerning fees. Defendants were provided with reasonable warning after the breach that undersigned counsel would withdraw unless Defendants performed their obligations. However, Defendants have not cured the breach.

Counsel served the notice (MC-051), the declaration (MC-052), and the proposed order (MC-053) on Defendants and filed a Proof of Service with the Court.

The Court finds all the requirements for relief have been completed. Additionally, trial has not been scheduled in this case and Defendants have a reasonable amount of time to obtain new counsel, such that they should not be prejudiced by the withdrawal.

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is granted.

AARONS v. ALLIANCE PORTFOLIO PRIVATE EQUITY FINANCE, INC. et al. (19STCV42747)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Pro Per Plaintiff Ashley Aarons

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Nigel Burns (The Law Offices of Nigel Burns)

 

MOTION TO DISMISS (filed by Specially Appearing Defendant Maria Cecelia S. Doratan on 11/16/23)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court dismisses the case as to Defendant Maria Cecelia S. Doratan pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 583.410(a) and 583.420(a)(2).

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 2, 2019, Plaintiff Ashley Aarons, individually and as trustee of the Ashley Aarons 2015 Trust dated May 15, 2015, (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants: Alliance Portfolio Private Equity Finance, Inc.; George Wong, Trustee of the George Wong Retirement Plan; Robert N. Cleaves and Emmy S. Cleaves, Trustees of the Cleaves Family Trust; Hal Dryan, Trustee of the Geoffrey Michael Dryan 2009 Irrevocable Trust Dated June 9, 2009 (“Dryan”); and Ray Gokaldas, Trustee of the Ray Gokaldas MD A Professional Corp Profit Sharing Plan Trust Dated January 1, 2013.

 

On December 20, 2021, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) negligence, (4) fraud, (5) cancellation of instruments, (6) quiet title, (7) declaratory relief, (8) unfair business practices, (9) accounting, and (10) wrongful foreclosure.

 

On November 16 2023, Specially Appearing Defendant Maria Cecelia S. Doratan filed a Motion to Dismiss, arguing:

·       Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.250(a)(2), the Court must dismiss for failure to serve defendant within 3 years or return proof of service within 60 days after the three-year period. Plaintiff has failed to comply with both of these requirements

·       Plaintiff filed her complaint on December 2, 2019, more than 4 1/2 years ago

·       Further, Section 583.420 allows dismissal because service of the summons and complaint have not been made within two years after the action was commenced nor has trial commenced within the three-year period

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that:

·       Plaintiff named the Defendant Doratan in her First Amended Complaint filed on December 20, 2021. When Plaintiff amended her complaint, the “gravamen” of the Complaint changed because she added a new cause of action based upon different facts. Thus, the period for service of summons runs from the date of the amended complaint. Plaintiff served Defendant on June 21, 2023.

·       In March 2020, during the Covid outbreak, the courts stopped holding hearings until July 1, 2020. Plaintiff argues this state response was out of her control and delayed her case.

·       Plaintiff’s bankruptcy matters have resulted in stays in this matter

o   Plaintiff argues there is a tolling of time while the bankruptcy trustee has control over the assets of the debtor. Here, Chapter 7 bankruptcy trustee tolled time for 13 months.

In reply, Defendant argues:

·       Plaintiff never served Defendant with the opposition

o   Plaintiff’s opposition must not be considered by the Court

o   The “DECLARATION OF SERVICE BY EMAIL” submitted by the Plaintiff in support of her opposition lists no email address of any kind showing to whom service of her “opposition” was made at the Law Offices of Nigel Burns. The attorneys of record at the Law Offices of Nigel Burns never received a service of copy of Plaintiff’s opposition paperwork as required under California Code of Civil Procedure § 1005(b).

·       Plaintiff’s attached proof of service show Defendant was never served with summons and complaint in this matter

o   Here, for proof of “substituted service” Plaintiff’s submitted paperwork states that when service was attempted, Plaintiff’s process server was informed that “[Defendant Doratan] no longer lives at this residence address. Per John Doe [Defendant Doratan] is unknown at address [Defendant Doratan] does not live here or receive mail.”

o   Therefore Plaintiff, by her own admission, never served Defendant at the Defendant’s “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge.” California Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Motion to Dismiss

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 583.410, the court may dismiss an action for delay in prosecution if the plaintiff does not serve the defendant within two years, or bring the action to trial within three years. ¿(Code Civ. Proc., §§ 583.410, 583.420.)  Dismissal is warranted where the plaintiff has failed to prosecute the action with “reasonable diligence.”  (Dunsmuir Masonic Temple v. Superior Court (1970) 12 Cal.App.3d 17, 23.)   The plaintiff has the burden of showing excusable delay once a complaint is not served within the two-year period. (Ladd v. Dart Equipment Corp. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 1088, 1099-110.) The court must consider all relevant matters, including the court’s file in the case and declarations and supporting date submitted by the parties, the diligence in seeking to effect service of process, whether the interests of justice are best served by dismissal or trial of the case, and any other fact or circumstance relevant to a fair determination of the issue. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1342(e).) 

B.     Failure to Serve Opposition

 

California Code of Civil Procedure § 1005(b) states that “all opposition papers must be filed and served at least 9 court days before the hearing.”

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff failed to serve Defendant with her opposition. The “DECLARATION OF SERVICE BY EMAIL” submitted by the Plaintiff in support of her opposition fails to list an email address that the opposition was served at. Defendant’s counsel state in their declaration that they were not served with an opposition. (See Burns Decl., Brown Decl.) Further, they state there has been no stipulation in the case to allow email service of pleadings as required under 1010.6 (4) (c)(i-ii). Given that Defendant addressed the opposition on their merits and thus are not prejudiced, the Court will consider Plaintiff’s opposition.

 

C.     Discussion

 

Defendant argues mandatory dismissal of the action is warranted under Code of Civil Procedure section 583.210 for failure to serve the Defendant within 3 years.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.210 states as follows:

 

(a) The summons and complaint shall be served upon a defendant within three years after the action is commenced against the defendant. For the purpose of this subdivision, an action is commenced at the time the complaint is filed.

(b) Proof of service of the summons shall be filed within 60 days after the time the summons and complaint must be served upon a defendant.

Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 583.250 states:

(a) If service is not made in an action within the time prescribed in this article:

(1) The action shall not be further prosecuted and no further proceedings shall be held in the action.

(2) The action shall be dismissed by the court on its own motion or on motion of any person interested in the action, whether named as a party or not, after notice to the parties.

(b) The requirements of this article are mandatory and are not subject to extension, excuse, or exception except as expressly provided by statute.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.240 provides exceptions to this statute. In computing the time within which service must be made pursuant to this article, there shall be excluded the time during which any of the following conditions existed:

 

(a) The defendant was not amenable to the process of the court.

(b) The prosecution of the action or proceedings in the action was stayed and the stay affected service.

(c) The validity of service was the subject of litigation by the parties.

(d) Service, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile due to causes beyond the plaintiff's control. Failure to discover relevant facts or evidence is not a cause beyond the plaintiff's control for the purpose of this subdivision.

(Code of Civil Procedure section 583.240)

 

Here, the Complaint in this action was filed on December 2, 2019. Therefore, service of summons and complaint should have been effected within three years on or before Monday, December 5, 2022.

Defendant argues that Defendant was never served with summons and complaint in this matter. In support, Defendant cites to Plaintiff’s June 23, 2023 “Proof of Service of Summons” and February 22, 2024 “Amended Proof of Service.” (Reply, Exs. A, B.) The proofs of service state that Plaintiff’s process server was informed that “[Defendant Doratan] no longer lives at this residence address. Per John Doe [Defendant Doratan] is unknown at address [Defendant Doratan] does not live here or receive mail.” (Ibid.) California Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20 requires service at Defendant’s “dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge.” Thus, the proofs of service do not indicate they were ever served upon Defendant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20.

Accordingly, the Court finds that since Defendant Maria Cecelia S. Doratan was not served within three years of the action being filed, the Court is required to dismiss the action as to this Defendant pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 583.210 and 583.250

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.  The Court dismisses the case as to Defendant Maria Cecelia S. Doratan pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 583.410(a) and 583.420(a)(2).

KAM’S AUTOMOTIVE, INC. v. ANGELES RUIZ (23STCP04300) 

 

Counsel for Petitioner/moving party:  Paul Bigley (Freeman Mathis & Gary, LLP)

 

PETITION FOR AN ORDER AUTHORIZING PRESERVATION OF EVIDENCE PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE § 2035.010 (filed 11/27/2023) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Petition for an Order Authorizing Preservation of Evidence is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 27, 2023, Petitioner Kam’s Automotive, Inc. (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition for an Order Authorizing Preservation of Evidence.

 

Petitioner contends that it expects to be defendant in an action cognizable in a court in the State of California, namely an action related to an automobile accident that occurred on August 2, 2022 in Los Angeles, California. Petitioner’s expectation is based on a letter of representation dated August 23, 2022, which included a demand to disclose policy limits, sent by Wilshire Law Firm to the claims department for New York Marine and General Liability Insurance.

 

Petitioner seeks to employ the following discovery methods to preserve evidence in anticipation of Respondent filing a lawsuit: physical examinations of Respondent and inspections of documents, things, and places, including document production demands propounded onto Respondent and subpoenas to Respondent’s medical providers as they become known.

 

No opposition or other response has been filed.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

"One who expects to be a party or expects a successor in interest to be a party to an action that may be cognizable in a court of the state, whether as a plaintiff, or as a defendant . . . may obtain discovery . . . for the purpose of perpetuating that person's own testimony or that of another natural person or organization, or of preserving evidence for use in the event an action is subsequently filed.” (CCP §¿2035.010(a).) “One shall not employ the procedures of this chapter for¿purposes of¿either ascertaining the possible existence of a cause of action or a defense to it, or of identifying those who might be made parties to an action not yet filed.” (Id. at (b).) 

 

“One who desires to perpetuate testimony or preserve evidence for the purposes set forth in Section 2035.010 shall file a verified petition in the superior court of the county of the residence of at least one expected adverse party, or, if no expected adverse party is a resident of the State of California, in the superior court of a county where the action or proceeding may be filed.” (CCP §¿2035.030(a).) The petition must be titled in the name of the one who desires the perpetuation of testimony or the preservation of evidence, and must set forth all of the following: 

 

(1) The expectation that the petitioner or the petitioner's successor in interest will be a party to an action cognizable in a court of the State of California. 

(2) The present inability of the petitioner and, if applicable, the petitioner's successor in interest either to bring that action or to cause it to be brought. 

(3) The subject matter of the expected action and the petitioner's involvement. A copy of any written instrument the validity or construction of which may be called into question, or which is connected with the subject matter of the proposed discovery, shall be attached to the petition. 

(4) The particular discovery methods described in Section 2035.020 that the petitioner desires to employ. 

(5) The facts that the petitioner desires to establish by the proposed discovery. 

(6) The reasons for desiring to perpetuate or preserve these facts before an action has been filed. 

(7) The name or a description of those whom the petitioner expects to be adverse parties so far as known. 

(8) The name and address of those from whom the discovery is to be sought. 

(9) The substance of the information expected to be elicited from each of those from whom discovery is being sought. 

 

(CCP § 2035.030(b)(1)-(9).) “The petition shall request the court to enter an order authorizing the petitioner to engage in discovery by the described methods for the purpose of perpetuating the described testimony or preserving the described evidence.” (CCP § 2035.030(c).) 

 

“When a minor, a person who lacks legal capacity to make decisions, or a person for whom a conservator has been appointed is a party, that person shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

The Petition satisfies the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure, section 2035.030.  

 

Petitioner sets forth grounds that it expects to be party to an action in the State of California. According to the Petition, Petitioner is the owner of the vehicle allegedly involved in the August 2, 2022 accident. (Cerezo Decl., ¶ 3.) Petitioner is also the employer of driver James Thomas. (Cerezo Decl., ¶ 3.) Based on information and belief, the subject accident occurred in the County of Los Angeles and all involved parties are believed to reside within the County of Los Angeles. (Cerezo Decl., ¶ 3, 4.) Petitioner has been issued a letter of representation and Designee Authorization on behalf of Respondent. (Cerezo Dec., Ex. A.) Respondent’s counsel also issued a demand to disclose insurance policy limits. (Ibid.) Petitioner provides that no litigation has yet been filed against it and Petitioner cannot cause the action to be brought. (C.C.P. § 2035.030(b)(2).)

 

Petitioner seeks the following discovery methods to preserve evidence in anticipation of Respondent filing a lawsuit: physical examinations of Respondent, inspections of documents, things, and places, including medical, billing, or radiology records related to Respondent’s injury claims, subpoenas to Respondent’s medical providers as they become known and Respondent’s employers to discover employment records, paystubs, and related information related to Respondent’s anticipated and potential claim for loss of earnings. These methods are available for discovery under this provision. (CCP §§¿2035.035(b)(4),¿2035.020(b) (authorizing discovery by “[i]nspections of documents, things, and places” and “physical examinations”).) Petitioner contends it desires to preserve the evidence requested because it is unclear who Respondent’s employers and medical providers are or if they have policies that would result in the destruction of records after a given time. Further, Petitioner requests the physical examinations to be taken closer in time to the alleged incident to “assess the Respondent’s injuries and potential damages in real-time.” (Petition, p. 9.) Petitioner’s above-described discussion establishes the substance of the information expected to be elicited from each of those from whom discovery is being sought

 

Petitioner has also identified expected adverse parties, Angeles Cristina Ruiz, and provides the name and address of those from whom the discovery is to be sought who have been ascertained at this time. (CCP §¿2035.035(b)(7).) The Court finds Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure, section 2035.030.  

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Petition for an Order Authorizing Preservation of Evidence.

 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Petition for an Order Authorizing Preservation of Evidence is GRANTED.

SHERRY TAHERI v. JOHN TIUNG HAN LI and JULIE MICHELLE YABU (23STCV27706)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Amy Mousavi (Mousavi Law, PC)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party: Jack M. LaPedis (The Law Offices of Jack M. LaPedis)

 

MOTION FOR PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION (filed 01/17/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

GRANTED

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff Sherry Taheri (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants John Tiung Han Li and Julie Michelle Yabu (collectively “Defendants”).

 

On December 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Trespass to Land (Continuing Trespass), (2) Financial Abuse of Elder or Dependent Adult, (3) Quiet Title, and (4) Injunctive Relief.

 

The parties are adjoining neighbors. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ trees and planter at the property boundary are causing damage to Plaintiff’s property. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants are causing damage by overwatering the trees and planter. Plaintiff seeks damages and an injunction.

 

On January 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Preliminary Injunction. On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Motion for Preliminary Injunction.

·        Plaintiff requests an order of the Court, ordering Defendants 1) to immediately stop watering the trees bordering and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property; and 2) removing and/or relocating the trees bordering, and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property

·        The Balancing Test Imposing a Preliminary Injunction Weighs In Plaintiff’s Favor

o   The intrusion of water and tree roots into Plaintiff’s Property constitutes continuing trespass

o   Absent an injunction – Plaintiff will be forced to file successive actions periodically to address the growth of the roots, water damage and damages she will incur in replacing, reinforcing or reconstructing the retaining wall at issue.

·        Plaintiff is Entitled to the Injunction Give Her Likelihood of Success on the Merits

·        Plaintiff Stands to Lose More if the Preliminary Injunction is Denied

 

In opposition, Defendants argue:

·       Plaintiff Does Not Have a Reasonable Probability of Success on Her Claims Since Plaintiff’s Claims are Based Almost Entirely on False Factual Allegations. Plaintiff will not suffer irreparable harm.

o   Defendants assert that Defendants are not watering tress in the planter

o   There is no “sprinkler” system on Defendants property to be turned off.

o   Any order for Defendants to not water in the area would be moot and Plaintiff will not suffer irreparable harm, as outlined in CCP § 526(a)(2), if the motion is denied.

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues:

·       Plaintiff maintains she is entitled to a preliminary injunction.

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

The function of a preliminary injunction is the preservation of the status quo until a final determination of the merits.” (Beehan v. Lido Isle Cmty. Ass’n (1977) 70 Cal.App.3d 858, 866.) “Trial courts traditionally consider and weigh two factors in determining whether to issue a preliminary injunction. They are (1) how likely it is that the moving party will prevail on the merits, and (2) the relative harm the parties will suffer in the interim due to the issuance or nonissuance of the injunction.” (Dodge, Warren & Peters Ins. Servs., Inc. v. Riley (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1414, 1420.) “The greater the showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.” (Ibid., quoting Butt v. State of California (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 678, brackets and ellipses omitted.) The burden of proof is on the plaintiff as the moving party “to show all elements necessary to support issuance of a preliminary injunction.” (O’Connell v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Preliminary injunctive relief requires the use of competent evidence to create a sufficient factual showing on the grounds for relief. (See, e.g., Ancora-Citronelle Corp. v. Green (1974) 41 Cal.App.3d 146, 150 [injunction erroneously granted without verified complaint, affidavits, or declarations to support injunctive relief].) Injunctive relief may be granted based upon a verified complaint only if it contains sufficient evidentiary, as opposed to ultimate, facts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 527, subd. (a).) A plaintiff seeking injunctive relief must also show the absence of an adequate damages remedy at law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 526, subd. (a)(4).)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A preliminary injunction may be classified as either a prohibitory injunction, which requires parties “to refrain from a particular act,” or a mandatory injunction, which requires parties to perform “an affirmative act that changes the position of the parties.” (Davenport v. Blue Cross of California (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 435, 446.) Mandatory preliminary injunctions are rarely granted. (Teachers Insurance & Annuity Association v. Furlotti (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1493.) More specifically, “[t]he granting of a mandatory injunction pending trial is not permitted except in extreme cases where the right thereto is clearly established.” (Ibid.)¿¿ 

 

A preliminary injunction may be granted upon affidavits where the complaint or affidavits establish sufficient grounds. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿527(a).) The burden is on the moving party to show all elements necessary to support issuance of a preliminary injunction.¿ (O'Connell v. Superior Court¿(2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1481.)¿ 

¿ 

“In deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction, a trial court weighs two interrelated factors: the likelihood the moving party ultimately will prevail on the merits, and the relative interim harm to the parties from the issuance or nonissuance of the injunction.” (Hunt v. Superior Court¿(1999) 21 Cal.4th 984, 999.) “The trial court's determination must be guided by a ‘mix’ of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff's showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.” (Butt v. State of California¿(1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 678.) 

 

B.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of the Minute Order dated April 7, 2023 in Sherry Taheri v. John Tiung Hanli, et al. Case No. 23SMSC00107.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ 

 

The court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

C.     Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court rules on Plaintiff’s objections to the Declaration of Julie Yabu as follows:

·       ¶¶ 6, 7: Overruled.

·       ¶ 9: Sustained (Lacks foundation)

·       Yabu Decl., Exhibit B: Sustained (Lacks foundation; Hearsay)

·       ¶ 20: Overruled

 

D.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff requests an order of the Court, ordering Defendants 1) to immediately stop watering the trees bordering and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property; and 2) removing and/or relocating the trees bordering, and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property.

 

a.     Likelihood of Prevailing on the Merits

 

                                                    i.     First Cause of Action: Continuing Trespass; Fourth Cause of Action: Injunctive Relief

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ trespass of trees and water constitutes a continuous trespass. Trespass is an unlawful interference with possession of property. (Staples v. Hoefke (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1397, 1406.) “[T]he two primary characteristics of a continuing nuisance or trespass are: (1) the nuisance/trespass is abatable, and/or (2) the damages from the nuisance/trespass may vary over time. [If] an encroachment ... is abatable, the law does not presume that such an encroachment will be permanently maintained. The maintenance of such an encroachment is a continuing trespass or nuisance.” (Spar v. Pac. Bell (1991) 235 Cal. App. 3d 1480, 1485.) “In these instances, persons harmed by [the continuing nuisance] may bring successive actions for damages until the nuisance is abated. [Citation.] Recovery is limited, however, to actual injury suffered prior to commencement of each action. Prospective damages are unavailable.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges that Defendants’ actions of planting trees on Plaintiff’s property and watering the trees constitutes a trespass onto Plaintiff’s property. (FAC, ¶¶ 47-52.) The FAC alleges Defendants continue to water the encroaching trees which continues to cause damage to Plaintiff’s property. (Ibid.) It further alleges that Defendants have failed to monitor or cut back the roots of the encroaching trees. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges the growing roots of the encroaching trees continues to cause foundational damage to Plaintiff’s property and the new retaining wall. (Ibid.)

In opposition, Defendants merely argue that Defendants do not water near the property line between their property and Plaintiff’s property. Defendants attach photographs showing that when they bought their property, the trees in the planter area near Plaintiff’s property were already present. (Yabu Decl., Ex. A.)

However, Plaintiff alleges that she is experiencing continuing damage and cites to a letter from her contractor in support. Plaintiff alleges that she wanted to erect an ADU on her property. (FAC, ¶ 26.) However, on June 10, 2023, Plaintiff’s contractor was forced to halt the project due to damage caused by Defendants trespassing. (FAC, ¶ 28.) Plaintiff’s contractor sent her a letter stating:

“To summarize, upon commencing the project, we encountered issues with the north wall, specifically concerning its foundation and water damage. This damage was caused by irrigation seeping through the neighboring planter box located at the neighbor property with the address of 10764 Rochester Ave. Additionally, the roots of the existing trees within that planter box had adversely affected the wall's foundation…Regrettably, despite the implementation of the new wall footing, we are still experiencing water seepage through the wall…Due to this persistent issue, we are unable to proceed with the construction work on the northern side of the building.” (FAC, Ex. 4.)

Since Plaintiff’s FAC is based on Defendants’ alleged continuous trespass, the remedy for a continuous trespass is either a suit for injunctive relief or successive actions for damages as new injuries occur. (Kornoff v. Kingsburg Cotton Oil Co. (1955) 45 Cal.2d 265, 269.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of success on the merits.

 

                                                   ii.     Second Cause of Action: Financial Abuse of Elder or Dependent Adult

 

“[F]inancial abuse of an elder . . . occurs when a person or entity does the following:”

 

(1) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(2) Assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult for a wrongful use or with intent to defraud, or both.

 

(3) Takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains, or assists in taking, secreting, appropriating, obtaining, or retaining, real or personal property of an elder or dependent adult by undue influence, as defined in Section 15610.70.

 

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).)

 

The FAC alleges Plaintiff has been a senior citizen since February 2022. (FAC, ¶¶ 64-65.) Due to her physical disabilities, Plaintiff alleges she is a dependent adult. (FAC, ¶¶ 64-65.) The FAC alleges that Defendants continuing trespass has deprived Plaintiff of her real property rights and full use of her home by refusing to allow Plaintiff to waterproof her retaining wall, or remove/address encroaching trees and roots. (FAC, ¶¶ 61-70.) Based on the same evidence discussed above attesting to damage to Plaintiff’s property, the Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of success on the merits.

 

                                                 iii.     Third Cause of Action: Quiet Title

 

CCP section 761.020 provides that a complaint for quiet title shall be verified and include: (1) a description of the property that is the subject of the action, including both the legal description and its street address or common designation if the subject property is real property; (2) the plaintiff’s title as to which determination is sought and basis of the title; (3) the adverse claims to the plaintiff’s title against which determination is sought; (4) the date of which the determination is sought; and (5) a prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.)  

 

The FAC describes the Wilkins Property to be “10761 Wilkins Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90024, Assessor’s Identification Number 4325-014-014” and “Lot 14 of Block 29 of Tract No. 7803, in the City of Los Angeles, County of Los Angeles, State of California, as per Map recorded in Book 88, Pages 73 to 75 inclusive of Maps, in the Office of the County Recorded of said County.” (FAC, ¶¶ 10, 75.) Plaintiff also describes the method of vesting of title to the Plaintiff by fee simple title via grant deed dated February 13, 2006. (FAC, ¶ 11.) Plaintiff states the adverse claims to Plaintiff’s title on the basis that Defendants claim an easement of the Wilkins Property where the trees and tree planters lie on the northern side of the retaining wall. (FAC, ¶¶ 76-78.)

 

Upon review of the FAC, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the description of the subject property (FAC, ¶¶ 10, 75), that Plaintiff obtained title to the subject property (FAC, ¶ 11), and Defendants’ adverse claims to Plaintiff’s title (FAC ¶¶ 76-78). Based on the foregoing, the Court finds Plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of success on the merits.

 

b.     Balance of Harms

 

The second factor which a trial court examines is the interim harm that plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants a preliminary injunction. (Donahue Schriber Realty Group, Inc. v. Nu Creation Outreach, (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1177.)  This factor involves consideration of the inadequacy of other remedies, the degree of irreparable harm, and the necessity of preserving the status quo.  (Ibid.) 

 

Plaintiff argues that she will suffer irreparable harm. She asserts the constant watering and growing roots will lead to further damage to the Plaintiff’s property and compromise her home’s foundation. Plaintiff is also forced to put off erecting an ADU on her property. (Taheri Decl., ¶¶ 26-28.)

 

In opposition, Defendants do not argue they will suffer any harm. Instead, Defendants dispute that any water damage is caused by Defendants. The Court finds the harm Plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied outweighs any harm that the Defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants the preliminary injunction.

 

Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently established a reasonable probability of success on her claims and that she will suffer irreparable harm if the motion is denied. Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion ordering Defendants 1) to immediately stop watering the trees bordering and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property; and 2) removing and/or relocating any trees bordering, and/or encroaching on the Plaintiff’s Property.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction is GRANTED.
