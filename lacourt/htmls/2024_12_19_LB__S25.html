DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Imed Hsini v. Mohsen Ghariani, et al.
LASC Case No. 24LBCV00194
Hearing Date: September 11, 2024
Request for Default Judgment by Court
 
 
In preparation for the September 11, 2024 default judgment hearing, the court has identified several concerns with Plaintiff’s request: 
 

  1. Request for Entry of Default was entered on May 9, 2024 which is less than 30 days after service of the summons and complaint to Defendant Danielle Canissa and CDW Logistics, LLC. (C.C.P. §587);
 
  1. Plaintiff checked the wrong box and should have checked “Court Judgment” in the “Request For” section on the 1st page. (See 7/29/2024 Request for Entry of Default/Judgment);
 
  1. No DOEs dismissal filed;
 
  1. No attached/adequate and/or authenticated exhibits provided with Plaintiff’s Declaration. Exhibits should demonstrate, e.g. costs for title and insurance for two vehicles, power of attorney, agreement entered into with Defendants to purchase trucks and provide logistic and shipping services to clients;
 
  1. No contractual or statutory basis provided by Plaintiff as to recovery of attorney fees. Also, attorney fees requested is more than what is allowed in a contract for recovery of reasonable attorney fees under Local Rule of the Court, rule 3.214(a);  
 
  1. Prayer for complaint does not specify damages claimed;
 
  1. No notice of punitive damages as required under Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subd. (c).  Appears Plaintiff may be waiving punitive damages based upon proposed judgment;
 
  1. Proposed Judgment (UD-100) incorrect calculations as to attorney fees and prejudgment interest calculations.


Case Number: 23LBCV01169    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: S25

Motion:  Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. and Portier, LLC’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint

Procedural Background

On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc., Portier, LLC, Joseph Gallegos and Does 1 to 50. Plaintiff alleges that the action arises from an automobile collision that occurred on or about May 14, 2023, on Pacific Coast Highway, at or near Redondo Avenue, in the City of Long Beach (“Incident”). (Compl., ¶ 1.) On October 12, 2023, Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. and Portier, LLC (“Uber Defendants” or “Defendants”) filed an answer. On April 22, 2024, the Court granted the parties stipulation, continuing the final status conference to October 4, 2024, the trial date to October 14, 2024, and all discovery and other pre-trial deadlines to be based from the new trial date. (April 22, 2024 Order.) On May 23, 2024, Defendant Joseph Gallegos (“Defendant Gallegos”) filed an answer. On August 29, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a first amended complaint (“FAC”), with the FAC deemed filed as of August 29, 2024. (August 29, 2024 Minute Order.)

On September 30, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion to strike. On December 3, 2024, the Court continued Defendants’ motion to continue trial hearing to December 19, 2024. (December 3, 2024 Minute Order.) On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On December 12, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

Legal Standard

A party may move to strike matters from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false or improper. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, subd. (a).) Motion to strike may also target all or any part of pleadings not filed or drawn in conformity with the applicable laws, rules or orders. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) A Motion to Strike is the proper procedure for testing the adequacy of a punitive damages allegation because punitive damages are not a cause of action. (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.)

To obtain punitive damages in an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, a plaintiff must demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that the defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) The clear and convincing evidence standard “requires a finding of high probability . . . ‘so clear as to leave no substantial doubt’; sufficiently strong to command the unhesitating assent of every reasonable mind.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1211.)

“Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) "[D]espicable” connotes conduct that is ". . . so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” (Lackner, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at p. 1210.) “Fraud” is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).) “Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Not only must there by circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166.)

Meet and Confer

The requirements are satisfied. (Loayza Decl., ¶ 8, Ex. C; Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(2) & (3).)

Parties’ Arguments

Defendants move to strike the following portion of the FAC: Page 8, Prayer for Damages.

First, Defendants argue Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages does not contain specific allegations supporting a conclusion that Defendants acted with oppression, fraud, malice. Defendants point to Plaintiff’s conclusory allegation that the “Uber Defendants knew or should have known, of Defendant GALLEGOS’ unfitness to drive” and that no other specific facts are offered by Plaintiff. Also, Defendants distinguish the present matter from Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890 in that here, the FAC lacks facts to support a finding of malicious or oppressive conduct against Defendants, while in Taylor, supra, 24 Cal.3d at pp. 892, 895-896, the Supreme Court found that plaintiff’s factual assertions that the defendant knew he was an alcoholic for a substantial period of time as well his dangerous tendency to drive while intoxicated supported an imposition of punitive damages.

Next, Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s punitive damage allegation should be stricken because Plaintiff does not allege involvement of any officer, director, or managing agent of Defendants. (See Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b); Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 167 [“the punitive damage statute requires proof of malice among corporate leaders: the ‘officers, directors, or managing agents”].)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants are purposefully withholding evidence by refusing to respond to certain discovery requests issued by Plaintiff. In the event the Court grants the instant motion, Plaintiff requests the Court to permit Plaintiff to further amend her operative complaint and permit Plaintiff time to amend the complaint to 30 days after Defendants produce the discovery currently in dispute.

In reply, Defendants reassert their earlier arguments. Defendants analogize the instant matter to Herrick v. Superior Court (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 787, 788-789 wherein the Court granted defendant’s motion to strike plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages as the defendant driver failed to stop for a posted stop sign while driving under the influence. Defendants add that Plaintiff’s social media posts two months after the incident show her enjoying life and in no pain or discomfort as she attends Taylor Swift concerts internationally. (Loayza Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendants also counter that Plaintiff’s argument that the parties are engaged in a discovery dispute does not remedy the deficiencies in the FAC, further stating that the discovery Plaintiff seeks are confidential documents that do not support a claim for punitive damages. Defendants further state that they will suffer undue prejudice if Plaintiff is granted to leave to amend her complaint at this stage with the trial date currently set for January 28, 2025.

Tentative Ruling

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to warrant punitive damages. Plaintiff has failed to allege facts that rise to the level of malice, fraud, or oppression. Plaintiff fails to allege any specific facts supporting Defendant's advance knowledge of the Defendant Gallegos’ unfitness. Likewise, Plaintiff does not allege that an officer, director, or managing agent of Defendants had such advance knowledge. Accordingly, the instant motion to strike is GRANTED and Page 8, Prayer for Damages: 3. For punitive damages” is stricken.

If information is subsequently obtained in discovery that may support Defendant's advance knowledge of the Defendant Gallegos’ unfitness, Plaintiff can seek leave to amend.


Motion: Defendant Joseph Gallegos’ Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint

Procedural Background

On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc., Portier, LLC, Joseph Gallegos and Does 1 to 50. Plaintiff alleges that the action arises from an automobile collision that occurred on or about May 14, 2023, on Pacific Coast Highway, at or near Redondo Avenue, in the City of Long Beach (“Incident”). (Compl., ¶ 1.) On October 12, 2023, Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. and Portier, LLC (“Uber Defendants” or “Defendants”) filed an answer. On April 22, 2024, the Court granted the parties stipulation, continuing the final status conference to October 4, 2024, the trial date to October 14, 2024, and all discovery and other pre-trial deadlines to be based from the new trial date. (April 22, 2024 Order.) On May 23, 2024, Defendant Joseph Gallegos (“Defendant Gallegos”) filed an answer. On August 29, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a first amended complaint (“FAC”), with the FAC deemed filed as of August 29, 2024. (August 29, 2024 Minute Order.)

On September 30, 2024, Defendant Gallegos filed the instant motion to strike. On December 3, 2024, the Court continued Defendants’ motion to continue trial hearing to December 19, 2024. (December 3, 2024 Minute Order.) On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On December 12, 2024, Defendant Gallegos filed a reply.

Legal Standard

A party may move to strike matters from an opposing party’s pleading if it is irrelevant, false or improper. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, subd. (a).) Motion to strike may also target all or any part of pleadings not filed or drawn in conformity with the applicable laws, rules or orders. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) A Motion to Strike is the proper procedure for testing the adequacy of a punitive damages allegation because punitive damages are not a cause of action. (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.)

To obtain punitive damages in an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, a plaintiff must demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that the defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) The clear and convincing evidence standard “requires a finding of high probability . . . ‘so clear as to leave no substantial doubt’; sufficiently strong to command the unhesitating assent of every reasonable mind.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1211.)

“Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) "[D]espicable” connotes conduct that is ". . . so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” (Lackner, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th at p. 1210.) “Fraud” is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(3).) “Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Not only must there by circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d at p. 166.)

Meet and Confer

The requirements are satisfied. (Jamshidi Decl., ¶ 17, Ex. A; Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(2) & (3).)

Parties’ Arguments

Defendant Gallegos moves to strike the following portion of the FAC: Page 8, Prayer for Damages.

First, Defendant Gallegos argues Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages does not contain specific allegations supporting a conclusion that Defendant Gallegos acted with oppression, fraud, malice. Defendant Gallegos distinguishes the present matter from Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890 in that here, the FAC has not added any new allegations regarding Defendant Gallegos and makes the same assertions, while in Taylor, supra, 24 Cal.3d at pp. 892, 895-896, the Supreme Court found that plaintiff’s factual assertions that the defendant knew he was an alcoholic for a substantial period of time as well his dangerous tendency to drive while intoxicated supported an imposition of punitive damages. (See FAC ¶¶ 10-19.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant Gallegos and Defendants are purposefully withholding evidence by refusing to respond to certain discovery requests issued by Plaintiff. In the event the Court grants the instant motion, Plaintiff requests the Court to permit Plaintiff to further amend her operative complaint and permit Plaintiff time to amend the complaint to 30 days after Defendants produce the discovery currently in dispute.

In reply, Defendant Gallegos reasserts his earlier arguments. Defendant Gallegos also counters that Plaintiff’s argument that the parties are engaged in a discovery dispute does not remedy the deficiencies in the FAC, further stating that the discovery Plaintiff seeks are confidential documents from Defendants that do not support a claim for punitive damages.

Tentative Ruling

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to warrant punitive damages. Plaintiff has failed to allege facts that rise to the level of malice, fraud, or oppression. Furthermore, Plaintiff has not added new allegations to the FAC that sufficiently demonstrate Defendant Gallegos’ conduct was malicious, fraudulent or oppressive. Accordingly, the instant motion to strike is GRANTED and Page 8, Prayer for Damages: 3. For punitive damages” is stricken.

If information is subsequently obtained in discovery that may support that Defendant Gallegos acted with oppression, fraud, malice, Plaintiff can seek leave to amend.

Case Number: 24LBCV01406    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On July 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants America Honda Motor, Co., Inc. and Does 1 to 50, alleging five causes of action: (1) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(d); (2) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(a)(3); (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; (5) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment. The complaint asserts that on September 11, 2022, Plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with Defendant, regarding a 2021 Honda Passport vehicle identification number 5FNYF7H56MB000408 (“Subject Vehicle”) and that defects and/or nonconformities to the warranty manifested themselves in the vehicle, but Defendant failed to fix them. (Compl., ¶¶ 6-12, 14, 15.)

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One. On December 6, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On December 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply. Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Nos. 1-3, 7, 9, 14-20, 29, 30, 39, 40, 43, 44, 47, 48, 53, 54, 55, 56, 69-74, 83-86, 89, 92, 93, 104, 107, 108, 110, 114, 115, 120, 121, 126, 127, 135, 136, 139, 140, and 142-145 (the “Requests” or “RFPs”).

Meet and Confer

As evidenced in counsel’s attached declarations, these law firms are engaged in multiple discovery exchanges involving various other plaintiffs. Based upon the declaration as well as the number of requests for further production in an arguably straight forward Song -Beverly matter, the Court believes the parties have not sufficiently had a meaningfully met and confer conversation. In fact, it is unclear if counsel have ever directly spoken to each other or if the “meet and confer” effort is just an exchange of emails.

On the Court’s own motion, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses is continued to January 29, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. for counsel to engage in a more meaningful and productive meet and confer process. For guidance, below are some basic parameters for what the court typically orders with respect to these types of discovery disputes. Typically, the court orders and limits production of documents including ESI in a Song-Beverly single-vehicle case as follows:

1. Purchase and/or lease contract concerning the subject vehicle must be produced.

2. Repair orders and invoices concerning the subject vehicle must be produced, including the “accounting” copies showing the hours and dates of activity by dealer personnel.

3. Communications between the plaintiff and the warrantor/manufacturer and/or its servicing dealers, and communications between Plaintiff and the manufacturer’s factory representative and/or call center concerning the subject vehicle.

4. Warranty claims submitted to and/or approved by Defendant concerning the subject vehicle.

5. The applicable Warranty Policy and Procedure Manual published by defendant and provided to its authorized repair facilities, within the State of California, for the year the lawsuit was filed. If a separate written policy, procedure, or manual exists regarding repurchases or buy backs applicable to vehicles sold or leased in California, that would also be required to be produced for the year the lawsuit was filed and/or the year Plaintiff contends the vehicle became qualified for repurchase (such as upon satisfaction of the Presentation Element of a Song-Beverly cause of action under Section 1793.2(d)(2) or 1793.22). Such documents would be produced subject to a protective order.

6. Any internal analysis and/or investigation regarding the primary or other recurring alleged defects claimed by plaintiff in her/his vehicle, applicable to the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle. The Court tends to focus on the claimed symptom experienced by the plaintiff as described in the dealers’ repair records, rather than a broad and vague characterization of the claimed defect as described by counsel in the litigation. For example, if the customer experienced a black Infotainment screen when shifting in reverse, or a harsh engagement or clunk of the transmission when accelerating from 2nd to 3rd gear, those symptoms rather than any other nature of malfunction of the Infotainment system or transmission would be the narrowing of scope of such internal analysis or investigation to be produced. In this case, there appears to be no allegations of defects related to the “Sensing” system of the subject vehicle. However, delay in subject vehicle may fall under a narrow definition if “Engine Concern.”

7. Other customers’ complaints similar to the alleged defects (e.g. static when on Bluetooth, intermittent delay at start up and car not always starting/delay in starting) claimed by plaintiff, limited to vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle. The other customer complaints again would be limited in scope to the description of the symptom as shown on the dealer repair records. The Court typically discusses with counsel the type of documents that may be required and the format for a production of documents such as in a sortable Excel spreadsheet that lists a compilation or summary of voluminous documents.

8. Technical Service Bulletins and/or Recall Notices for vehicles purchased in California for the same year, make and model of the subject vehicle, whether mentioned in the repair history of the subject vehicle or not.

9. Requests for documents and deposition testimony in other lawsuits is not relevant and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. Further, such evidence may be subject to a protective order.

Using these guidelines, the parties are ordered to discuss these discovery issues with a meaningfully “meet and confer” by January 3, 2025. A joint status report setting forth any remaining discovery disputes should be submitted by the parties by January 17, 2025. On the Court’s own motion, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses is continued to January 29, 2025 at 8:30 a.m.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
