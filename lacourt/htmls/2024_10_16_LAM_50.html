Case Number: 23STCV22254    Hearing Date: October 16, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

MARTIN DORSLA,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

SALLY FOSTER JONES, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

  23STCV22254

Hearing Date:

October 16, 2024

Hearing Time:    10:00 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANTS DONALD H. JONES; SALLY FORSTER JONES; DSJ THAYER EXCHANGES, LLC; SFJ THAYER LLC; DAJO THAYER EXCHANGES, LLC; AND CAPRI PROPERTIES’ MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF’S SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT;

 

JOINDER TO DEFENDANTS DONALD H. JONES; SALLY FORSTER JONES; DSJ THAYER EXCHANGES, LLC; SFJ THAYER LLC; DAJO THAYER EXCHANGES, LLC; AND CAPRI PROPERTIES’ MOTION TO STRIKE PLAINTIFF’S SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

            Background

Plaintiff Martin Dorsla (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on September 15, 2023 against Defendants Sally Foster Jones, Donald H. Jones, DSJ Thayer Exchanges, LLC, SFJ Thayer LLC, DAJO Thayer Exchanges, LLC, Capri Properties, LLP, Enrique Camacho, Housed, Inc., Joe J Huezo, and Huezo Construction.  

Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on October 31, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of warranty of habitability, (2) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, (3) premises liability, (4) private nuisance, (5) negligence and negligence per se, (6) negligent infliction of emotional distress, (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (8) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.

Defendants Joe Huezo and Huezo Construction (jointly, the “Huezo Defendants”) demurred to each of the causes of action of the FAC and moved to strike portions of the FAC. On April 26, 2024, the Court issued an Order sustaining the demurrer to the first, second, third, fifth, and eighth causes of action of the FAC, with leave to amend. (April 26, 2024 Order at p. 15.) The Court sustained the demurrer to the sixth cause of action of the FAC without leave to amend. (Ibid.) The demurrer to the fourth and seventh causes of action of the FAC was overruled, and the motion to strike was granted in part, with leave to amend, and denied in part as moot. (Ibid.) The Court’s April 26, 2024 Order provides, inter alia, that “Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days, the Court orders [Huezo Defendants] to file and serve their answer to the FAC within 30 days of the date of this order.” (April 26, 2024 Order at p. 15:20-22.)

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (the “SAC”).  The SAC alleges ten causes of action.

Donald H. Jones, Sally Forster Jones (erroneously sued as Sally Foster Jones), DSJ Thayer Exchanges, LLC, SFJ Thayer LLC, DAJO Thayer Exchanges, LLC, and Capri Properties (erroneously sued as Capri Properties, LLP) (collectively, the “Moving Defendants”) now move for an order striking the SAC. The Moving Defendants also move for an order striking the first and ninth causes of action of the SAC. The motion is unopposed.

 The Huezo Defendants join in the Moving Defendants’ motion.

A court may strike any “¿irrelevant, false, or improper matter¿inserted in any pleading¿” or any part of a pleading “¿not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿” (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 436¿¿.) “¿The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 437¿.)¿¿ 

The Moving Defendants’ Motion

The Moving Defendants move for an order striking the SAC. As discussed above, the Court’s April 26, 2024 Order on the Huezo Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC and motion to strike portions of the FAC provides, inter alia, that “Plaintiff is ordered to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days, the Court orders [the Huezo Defendants] to file and serve their answer to the FAC within 30 days of the date of this order.” (April 26, 2024 Order at p. 15:20-22.)

Twenty days after April 26, 2024 is May 16, 2024. Plaintiff’s SAC was filed on May 29, 2024, well after the May 16, 2024 deadline. Before the SAC was filed, the Huezo Defendants filed an answer to the FAC on May 23, 2024. In addition, on May 24, 2024, the Moving Parties filed an answer to Plaintiff’s “operative Complaint.” 

The Moving Defendants assert that “[p]ursuant to Court Order, Plaintiff had 20 days to amend his first amended complaint following demurrer. Plaintiff failed to amend with the time provided by the Court…” (Mot. at p. 2:12-13.) As noted by the Moving Defendants, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “[t]he court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:…(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b), emphasis added.) Plaintiff does not oppose the motion and thus does not dispute that the SAC was not timely filed by the deadline specified in the Court’s April 26, 2024 Order.

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the Moving Defendants’ motion for an order striking the SAC.

The Moving Defendants also move to strike the first and ninth causes of action of the SAC. As the Court grants the Moving Defendants’ motion for an order striking the SAC, the Court finds that the Moving Defendants’ motion to strike the first and ninth causes of action of the SAC is moot.

The Huezo Defendants’ Joinder

In their joinder in the instant motion, the Huezo Defendants assert that “[t]he language of the Court’s minute order is clear, Plaintiff was ordered to file the proposed Second-Amended Complaint within 20 days, limited to amending only those causes of action for which leave had been granted. However, Plaintiff failed make the amendments within the 20-day timeframe specified by the Court, prompting Defendants to undertake the time and expense of preparing and filing responsive pleadings based on the allegations of the First Amended Complaint.” (Joinder at p. 5:5-9.) The Huezo Defendants assert that “Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint was in violation of the Court’s order issued on April 26, 2024. As such, the Court should strike the Second Amended Complaint, in its entirety.” (Joinder at p. 5:12-14.) The Court grants the Huezo Defendants’ joinder in the Moving Defendants’ motion to strike the SAC.

The Huezo Defendants also argue that “absent striking the entire Complaint, the Court should strike the third and eighth causes of action.” (Joinder at p. 6:6-7.) As set forth above, the Court grants the Huezo Defendants’ joinder in the Moving Defendants’ motion to strike the SAC. Thus, the Huezo Defendants’ request to strike the third and eighth causes of action is moot.[1]

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Moving Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s SAC is granted. The Moving Defendants’ motion to strike is otherwise denied as moot.

The Huezo Defendants’ joinder in the Moving Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s SAC is granted. The Huezo Defendants’ joinder is otherwise denied.

The Moving Defendants are ordered to give notice of this Order.

DEFENDANTS RUSSELL W. ROTEN AND DUANE MORRIS LLP’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF ROBERT ARONSON AND A FURTHER DEPOSITION OF HOWARD ZELENER

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTIONS

 

           

            Background

Plaintiff Intelligent SCM, LLC, dba American Worldwide Agencies filed this action on February 13, 2015 against Defendants Russell W. Roten (“Roten”) and Duane Morris.  

Intelligent SCM, LLC, dba American Worldwide Agencies filed the operative Fourth Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on July 9, 2019 against Roten, Duane Morris, and Andrew P. Scott. The FAC alleges causes of action for (1) constructive fraud and breach of fiduciary duty, (2) legal malpractice, (3) negligence (4) fraud, (5) tort of another, (6) intentional interference with contractual relationship, (7) negligent interference with contractual relationship,

(8) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage, (9) negligent interference with prospective economic advantage, (10) negligent supervision, (11) conversion, and (12) aiding and abetting.   

On April 27, 2017, Andrew Scott (“Scott”) filed a Cross-Complaint against a number of Cross-Defendants, alleging five causes of action. On May 3, 2017, Peter Lamy and Graham Burford filed a Cross-Complaint against Scott. The Cross-Complaint alleges two causes of action.

Roten and Duane Morris LLP (“Duane Morris”) (jointly, the “Moving Parties”) indicate that Intelligent SCM, LLC (“ISCM”) is represented in this action by attorneys Howard Zelener and Robert Aronson. (Scott Decl., ¶ 10.) On March 29, 2024, the Moving Parties served a Deposition Subpoena for Personal Appearance on Howard M. Zelener. (Scott Decl., ¶ 12, Ex. 9.) On March 29, 2024, the Moving Parties also served a Deposition Subpoena for Personal Appearance on Robert A. Aronson. (Scott Decl., ¶ 13, Ex. 10.)

The Moving Parties’ counsel states that “Counsel for Messrs. Zelener and Aronson, Mr. Rosen, responded to the Zelener Subpoena and the Aronson Subpoena (collectively the ‘Subpoenas’) stating that the Subpoenas were premature.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 15.) The Moving Parties’ counsel states that “[t]he parties conducted an informal discovery conference on, among other discovery issues, the topic of Messrs. Zelener and Aronson’s depositions, on April 24, 2024.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 16.) The Court’s April 24, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Informal Discovery Conference is held. The parties fulfilled the IDC requirement for the issues raised in the IDC Statements for today’s IDC.”[1]

The Moving Parties now move “for an Order compelling the deposition of Robert Aronson and a further deposition of Howard Zelener.” ISCM and third-parties Howard Zelener and Robert Aronson (collectively, the “Opposing Parties”) appear to oppose the motion.[2]

            Discussion  

            ¿¿¿Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a)¿¿ provides as follows:

 

“¿If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

“¿If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.¿” (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (a)¿.) Pursuant to ¿Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.610, subdivision (b)¿, “¿for good cause shown, the court may grant leave to take a subsequent deposition, and the parties, with the consent of any deponent who is not a party, may stipulate that a subsequent deposition be taken.¿”   

In the motion, the Moving Parties argue that “California law supports Duane Morris taking the depositions of Aronson and Zelener.” (Mot. at p. 7:10-11.) The Moving Parties cite to

Carehouse Convalescent Hospital v. Superior Court (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1558, 1562, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[d]epositions of opposing counsel are presumptively improper, severely restricted, and require extremely good cause—a high standard. There are strong policy considerations against deposing an opposing counsel. The practice runs counter to the adversarial process and to the state’s public policy to [p]revent attorneys from taking undue advantage of their adversary’s industry and efforts. Discovery was hardly intended to enable a learned profession to perform its functions…on wits borrowed from the adversary.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.) “Attorney depositions chill the attorney-client relationship, impede civility and easily lend themselves to gamesmanship and abuse.” ((Id. at p. 1563.)

The Carehouse Court noted that “California applies a three-prong test in considering the propriety of attorney depositions. First, does the proponent have other practicable means to obtain the information? Second, is the information crucial to the preparation of the case? Third, is the information subject to a privilege? Each of these prongs poses an independent hurdle to deposing an adversary’s counsel; any one of them may be sufficient to defeat the attempted attorney deposition.” ((Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)

The Carehouse Court further noted that “[w]ithout question, the proponent has the burden of proof to establish the predicate circumstances for the first two prongs. But California has not directly addressed the issue of which party has the burden of proof to establish the third prong, that the deposition would (or would not) impinge upon privileged information. One federal court, in implementing the three-prong test, has placed the burden of establishing the applicability of the attorney-client privilege and the work product doctrine on the party opposing discovery. ‘[T]he one asserting the work-product and attorney-client privileges has the burden of demonstrating the applicability of those privileges to the specific items which he claims are not subject to discovery.’ (First Sec. Sav. v. Kansas Bankers Sur. Co. (D.Neb. 1987) 115 F.R.D. 181, 182.) We agree. Parties claiming the benefit of the work product rule have the burden to show preliminary facts to support its applicability.” ((Ibid.)  

First, the Moving Parties contend that the Carehouse test “has no application to the testimony Duane Morris seeks here, which would relate to Messrs. Zelener’s and Aronson’s work on other cases and on issues in this case that are no longer in play in this litigation.” (Mot. at p. 7:13-16.) But as noted by the Opposing Parties, Carehouse does not appear to draw any “distinction as to whether the deposition concerns the present action or other related actions.” (Opp’n at p. 10:19-20.) The Moving Parties also argue that “because testimony regarding Messrs. Zelener’s and Aronson’s work on the Underlying Cases would not be in their capacity as counsel here, but rather as percipient witnesses who performed work for which ISCM is seeking compensation, the principles animating the Carehouse test have no application whatsoever to that portion of the requested testimony.” (Mot. at p. 8:8-11.) But the Moving Parties cite to a nonbinding federal case to support this proposition. (Mot. at p. 8:12-14.) Moreover, the Moving Parties seek to depose Mr. Zelener and Mr. Aronson, counsel for ISCM in this case. As discussed, Carehouse provides that “California applies a three-prong test in considering the propriety of attorney depositions.” ((Ibid.) The Court does not see how such test is somehow inapplicable here, when the Moving Parties seek to depose ISCM’s counsel in this action. (Scott Decl., ¶ 10.)
            The Moving Parties also argue that “even if the Carehouse test applies here, it is fully satisfied.” (Mot. at p. 9:16.) As discussed, the first prong of the test is “does the proponent have other practicable means to obtain the information?” ((Ibid.) The Moving Parties assert that here, “Mr. Zelener and Mr. Aronson are the only people who can provide the requested testimony…” (Mot. at p. 9:22-23.)

The Moving Parties point to pages 51 and 52 of ISCM’s “Third Amended, Further, and Supplemental Responses” to Duane Morris’s “Special Interrogatories to Putative Plaintiff Intelligent SCM, LLC, Set No. Three.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 5.) ISCM’s “Second Amended Supplemental Response To Interrogatory No. 9” lists a number of “fees” and “costs” next to the categories “Speed Case,” “Embezzlement Case,” “Malpractice Case,” and “Crease Case.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 5, pp. 51-52.)[3] The “Second Amended Supplemental Response To Interrogatory No. 9” also references, inter alia, a “Zelener Total” of $1,633,165.40 in fees, and an “Aronson Total” of $1,150,268.50 in fees. (Scott Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 5, p. 52.)

The Moving Parties assert that “while ISCM has provided Mr. Zelener’s invoices, those invoices do not contain detailed time entries after March of 2014.” (Mot. at p. 10:13-14.) The Moving Parties point to a “copy of the invoices representing Mr. Zelener’s invoices, previously produced in this action…on August 1, 2023…” (Scott Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. 8.) The Moving Parties argue that “even a surface level analysis of these invoices reveal that ISCM’s attempt to allocate $1.25 million in Mr. Zelener’s fees to the Embezzlement Case strains credulity.” (Mot. at p. 11:1-2.) The Moving Parties assert that “[t]he numbers simply do not add up, and Duane Morris needs Mr. Zelener’s testimony both to understand them and to be able to meaningfully challenge them.” (Mot. at p. 11:12-13.)

The Moving Parties also argue that “as with Mr. Zelener, Duane Morris must be able to cross examine [Mr. Aronson] about his background and qualifications as part of its challenge to the reasonableness of his fees.” (Mot. at p. 12:25-26.) The Moving Parties’ counsel states that “[t]o date, ISCM has not produced Mr. Aronson’s invoices in this matter…” (Scott Decl., ¶ 46.)

The Moving Parties argue that “Duane Morris must be permitted to test ISCM’s theory of damages and causation by questioning ISCM’s counsel about the work they actually performed in this litigation and the Underlying Cases and what connection, if any, that work has to anything that Duane Morris did or did not do.” (Mot. at p. 12:15-18.)

In the opposition, the Opposing Parties argue that “Duane Morris’ motion waxes on about all the information it would like to learn about ISCM’s damages claims (much of it clearly not discoverable, as discussed below). But Duane Morris wholly fails to prove that the information it is entitled to is only available from Zelener and Aronson.” (Opp’n at p. 13:21-24, emphasis omitted.) In the motion, the Moving Parties assert that “Messrs. Zelener and Aronson must sit for deposition so that Duane Morris can first understand, and then challenge, ISCM’s claimed damages.” (Mot. at p. 1:8-9.) The Opposing Parties counter that “there is nothing that precludes Duane Morris from serving proper follow-up written discovery to the responses that ISCM has already provided in this case including the time spent, the amounts billed, and the facts that support ISCM’s position that these fees were incurred as a result of the actions taken by Defendants.” (Opp’n at pp. 12:27-13:2, emphasis omitted.) Indeed, interrogatories appear to be a practicable means of obtaining the information sought.  

The Opposing Parties cite to Estate of Ruchti (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1593, 1596, where “Respondents’ trial attorney, Peter M. Williams (Williams), was handed a subpoena for his own deposition on December 13, 1989, by appellant, Richard A. DeSantis (DeSantis), attorney for Robert R. Ruchti II (Ruchti).” Williams filed an application for a protective order, and “DeSantis opposed the protective order application and lost.” (Id. at p. 1597.) The Court of Appeal found that “Appellant has made various assignments of error regarding the upholding of the protective order and the awarding of sanctions. These assignments of error go to,” inter alia, “the law regarding the taking of trial counsel’s deposition…There is no merit in any of appellant’s contentions.” (Id. at pp. 1599-1600.)

The Court of Appeal in Estate of Ruchti noted that “[t]he circumstances under which opposing counsel may be deposed are limited to those where (1) no other means exist to obtain the information than to depose opposing counsel; (2) the information sought is relevant and not privileged; (3) the information is crucial to the preparation of the case.” (Estate of Ruchti, supra, 12 Cal.App.4th at p. 1600, internal quotations omitted, citing Spectra-Physics, Inc. v. Superior Court (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 1487, 1496.) The Court of Appeal found that “Williams acted properly when he invited appellant to propound interrogatories. Properly drafted interrogatories could elicit the facts upon which the co-executors based their contentions and would require the identification of witnesses and of documents. Other common discovery devices could also have been used. It is clear that the three-part Spectra-Physics test was not met by appellant. Rather than utilize readily available discovery techniques, DeSantis apparently decided to rely on other litigation tactics by serving the deposing attorney with a deposition subpoena during the deposition of the client of DeSantis.” (Id. at p. 1601.)

Here too, the Court does not see why other readily available discovery techniques, such as interrogatories, would not be a “practicable means to obtain the information.” ((Ibid.) The Court does not find that the Moving Parties have shown that they lack other practicable means to obtain information regarding ISCM’s claimed attorney’s fees in this action. As set forth above, each of the prongs discussed in Carehouse “poses an independent hurdle to deposing an adversary’s counsel; any one of them may be sufficient to defeat the attempted attorney deposition.” (Carehouse Convalescent Hospital v. Superior Court, supra, 143 Cal.App.4th at p. 1563.)

The second prong of the Carehouse test provides, “is the information crucial to the preparation of the case?” (Carehouse Convalescent Hospital v. Superior Court, supra, 143 Cal.App.4th at p. 1563.) The Moving Parties assert that the requested testimony is “critical to Duane Morris’s defense.”[4] (Mot. at p. 9:23.) The Moving Parties’ counsel asserts that “Duane Morris’s preparation for trial and its trial presentation would be severely impaired if it is unable to depose the attorneys whose fees are the centerpiece of ISCM’s damages claims.” (Scott Decl., ¶ 51.) The Opposing Parties argue that “information about the attorney’s fees ISCM seeks as damages in this case is easily available from other sources. Therefore, it necessarily is not crucial that this information comes from Zelener and Aronson.” (Opp’n at p. 14:1-3.) But as discussed, the second prong concerns whether the information is crucial to the preparation of the case. The Court agrees with the Moving Parties that information concerning “ISCM’s claimed damages” (Mot. at p. 1:9) is crucial to Duane Morris’s preparation of the case.

The third prong of the Carehouse test provides, “is the information subject to a privilege?” (Carehouse Convalescent Hospital v. Superior Court, supra, 143 Cal.App.4th at p. 1563.) The Moving Parties assert that the information sought is not subject to any privilege.

The Moving Parties cite to Byers v. Superior Court (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 1003, 1006, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n this writ proceeding, George and Sheila Byers challenge the trial court to vacate its order granting USAA General Indemnity Company’s (USAA) motion to compel further responses to document requests seeking attorney fees documents. The Byerses further request that we order the trial court to delay production of their attorney fees documents until after the jury makes a determination of liability. We conclude that the trial court did not err and deny the petition.” The Court of Appeal found that “[w]e agree with the trial court that USAA has a right to learn during discovery of the attorney fees aspect of the Byerses’ alleged damages and that by seeking such damages the Byerses have impliedly waived the attorney-client privilege. The Byerses have put at issue the attorney fees they incurred in an effort to seek coverage under their insurance policy, and disclosure of documents supporting their claim for such fees is necessary to fairly adjudicate the issue of damages.” ((Id. at p. 1012.) The Moving Parties argue that here too, “ISCM has placed the legal work of its counsel squarely at issue and so has waived any right to claim privilege.” (Mot. at p. 14:12-13.)

In the opposition, the Opposing Parties assert that their burden on the third prong of the Carehouse test is “easily met because the vast majority of the testimony Duane Morris seeks is work product.” (Opp’n at p. 14:20-21.)

As noted by the Opposing Parties, the motion provides that Duane Morris seek, inter alia, to “understand, and to cross-examine, these attorneys regarding what they did, whether it was necessary and reasonable, and whether it bears any causal link to any of ISCM’s claims of malpractice.” (Mot. at pp. 1:28-2:2.) The Moving Parties also contend that Duane Morris “must be permitted to test ISCM’s theory of damages and causation by questioning ISCM’s counsel about the work they actually performed in this litigation and the Underlying Cases…” (Mot. at p. 12:15-17.) The Opposing Parties note that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.030, subdivision (a), “[a] writing that reflects an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories is not discoverable under any circumstances.”[5] The Court agrees with the Opposing Parties that the Moving Parties appear to seek testimony regarding information protected under Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.030, subdivision (a).

The Opposing Parties also assert that “there is absolutely nothing in Byers that stands for the proposition that defendant is entitled to discovery into protected work product.” (Opp’n at p. 15:18-20.) In Byers, the Court of Appeal noted as follows:

 

“The Byerses claim that the trial court abused its discretion by ordering an in camera review of attorney invoices. Their argument overstates what the trial court actually ordered and is premature given the current stage of the proceedings.

 

The relevant portion of the order states: ‘Byers’ counsel may redact the documents to remove references that counsel believes reflect attorney work product, i.e., information that may give an indication of counsel’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories, or as to consultants or expert witnesses for which the attorney work product doctrine has not been waived by Byers’ counsel. [¶] … [¶] USAA GIC may challenge the redactions made by counsel for Byers, and such redactions will be evaluated by the Court in an in camera hearing.’

 

The Byerses have not yet produced any responsive documents, and it is unknown the extent to which they may redact any responsive documents and whether USAA will seek to challenge the redactions. Thus, the trial court’s order regarding in camera review is conditioned upon redactions and subsequent challenges to the redactions. Given the undeveloped record, the issue framed by the Byerses is speculative and premature. Further, the issue of the propriety of conducting an in camera review of potentially redacted documents and the proper scope of such a review was not presented to the trial court. When the trial court made its ruling at the hearing on USAA’s motion to compel, it mentioned the possibility of conducting an in camera review at some point in the future and neither party raised any objections to such a proceeding. If the Byerses produce redacted documents in response to USAA’s document requests and if USAA challenges any redactions, the parties may then present their positions to the trial court regarding whether, and to what extent, it may review the redacted material in camera.” (Byers v. Superior Court, supra, 101 Cal.App.5th at p. 1014.)

            The Moving Parties appear to acknowledge that the work product doctrine may apply to certain testimony sought here. In the reply, the Moving Parties state that “[i]n fact, to the extent any question calls for mental impressions, the attorneys could assert work product and Duane Morris would not be entitled to the information.” (Reply at p. 3:10-12.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the Opposing Parties have met their burden on the third prong by demonstrating that the depositions of Mr. Zelener and Mr. Aronson appear to seek information covered by the work product doctrine.

In light of the foregoing, the Court denies the Moving Parties’ motion for an order compelling the deposition of Robert Aronson and a further deposition of Howard Zelener.[6]

Lastly, the Opposing Parties seek an award of monetary sanctions. In support of such request, the Opposing Parties cite to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, subdivision (c), which provides that “[e]xcept as provided in subdivision (d), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” But the instant motion is not a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection. Thus, the Court does not see how Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, subdivision (c) is applicable here. The Court does not find that the Opposing Parties have demonstrated that the Court should award them sanctions here. Thus, the Court denies such request.

Conclusion

            Based on the foregoing, the Moving Parties’ motion is denied. The Opposing Parties’ request for sanctions is denied.

The Opposing Parties are ordered to provide notice of this order.
