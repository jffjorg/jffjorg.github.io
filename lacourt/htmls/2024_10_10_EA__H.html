DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV00436    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: H

De Valdivia v. Apex Pallets, Inc., Case No. 23PSCV00436

ORDER ON MOTION TO QUASH SUMMONS 

Specially Appearing Defendant Alex Loyola’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is GRANTED.

Background

Plaintiff Jesus De Valdivia (“Plaintiff”) alleges he sustained injuries after being attacked and bit by a dog. On February 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting negligence and strict liability causes of action against Apex Pallets, Inc. (“Apex”), and Does 1-20.

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” wherein Alex Loyola (“Loyola”) was named in lieu of Doe 1.

Pending before the Court is Special Appearing Defendant Loyola’s motion to quash service of summons.

Legal Standard

“When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint, . . . and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by any name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 474).

“[W]hen a complaint sets forth a cause of action against a defendant designated by a fictitious name because the plaintiff is genuinely ignorant of his name or identity, and his true name thereafter is discovered and substituted by amendment, he is considered a party to the action from its commencement so that the statute of limitations stops running as of the date the original complaint was filed.” (General Motors Corp. v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 580, 589). “A plaintiff is ‘ignorant of the name’ if he knows the identity of the person but is ignorant of the facts giving him a cause of action against such person.” (Wallis v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1976) 61 Cal.App.3d 782, 786.)

“Improper service of a defendant under section 474 may be attacked by a motion to quash.” (McClatchy v. Coblentz, Patch, Duffy & Bass, LLP (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 368, 375.)

“A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion. . . (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10.)

Discussion

Loyola and Apex move for an order quashing service of summons as to Loyola.

At the outset, the Court construes the motion as having been brought by Loyola only, inasmuch as Apex would not appear to have standing to seek any relief on behalf of Loyola.

Loyola contends that he is not a true “Doe” defendant because Plaintiff was aware of his identity and of facts giving rise to liability prior to the filing of this action. The Court agrees.

This action arises from a March 31, 2021 dog bit incident. (Bell Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) On or about April 5, 2022, Apex’s (and now Loyola’s) counsel Randy Bell (“Bell”) was served a demand letter from Plaintiff’s counsel Tigran Martinian, which attached a copy of the animal control report from the County of San Bernardino. (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. B.) The report identifies Plaintiff as the “Person Bitten,” lists an incident date of March 31, 2021 and the reporting agency as “Pomona Police Department,” identifies Loyola as the “Owner” under the section listed as “Animal” and sets forth Loyola’s address as “225 E Caldwell” in Pomona, in addition to his phone number. (Id.)

Plaintiff advised in discovery responses that he received the above report in October 2021. (Id., ¶¶ 6 and 8, Exhs. D, p. 7 and E, p. 30.) The complaint was filed February 14, 2023. (Id.) Again, Loyola was named in lieu of Doe 1 via an “Amendment to Complaint” filed January 16, 2024. (Id., ¶ 9, Exh. F.)

Plaintiff asserts that section 474 applies because the dog bite occurred just outside of Apex’s business premises on East End Street in Pomona and “[a]ll available evidence at the time,” including the fact that the dog was walking alongside an employee of Apex, led him to believe that the dog was associated with Apex and not Loyola personally. (Borisov Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Plaintiff concedes that, while he knew of Loyola’s identity, he only first learned of the facts giving him a cause of action against Loyola through Apex’s December 29, 2023 discovery responses, when it became known to him that Loyola, and not Apex, was the personal owner of the dog. (Id., ¶ 10.) This position ignores the fact that Loyola was identified on the report as the dog’s “Owner” and that Loyola’s contact information, including a physical address different from Apex’s location, was listed; had the owner been Apex, the report would have listed Apex’s contact information.

Plaintiff also argues that the motion should be denied because Loyola has not articulated any prejudice. “However, where the requirements of section 474 are not met, lack of prejudice alone does not allow the addition of a new defendant by amendment after the statute of limitations has run.” (Hazel v. Hewlett (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 1458, 1466.)

The motion is granted.  

Case Number: 23PSCV02530    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: H

Chavez v. Red Robin International, Inc., et al., Case No. 23PSCV02530

Defendant Red Robin International, Inc.’s, Red Robin Gourmet Burgers, Inc.’s and Alfred Esparza’s Motion for an Order Approving Pro Hac Vice Admission of Thomas Richard Leemon Pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40 is GRANTED. 


Background

Plaintiff Carolina E. Chavez (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she sustained injuries in an October 8, 2021 slip and fall while at a restaurant.

On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Red Robin International, Inc., Red Robin Gourmet Burgers, Inc., and Does 1-100 for (1) general negligence, and (2) premises liability.

On October 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” wherein Alfred Esparza (“Esparza”) was named in lieu of Doe 81. 

Pending before the Court is the defendants’ motion allowing the pro hac vice admission of Thomas Richard Leemon. 

 

Legal Standard 

“A person who is not a licensee of the State Bar of California but who is an attorney in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States, and who has been retained to appear in a particular cause pending in a court of this state, may in the discretion of such court be permitted upon written application to appear as counsel pro hac vice, provided that an active licensee of the State Bar of California is associated as attorney of record. No person is eligible to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule if the person is:

(1) A resident of the State of California;

(2) Regularly employed in the State of California; or

(3) Regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(a).) 

“A person desiring to appear as counsel pro hac vice in a superior court must file with the court a verified application together with proof of service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1013a of a copy of the application and of the notice of hearing of the application on all parties who have appeared in the cause and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office. The notice of hearing must be given at the time prescribed in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 unless the court has prescribed a shorter period.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(c)(1).) 


“The application must state:

(1) The applicant’s residence and office address;

(2) The courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission;

(3) That the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts;

(4) That the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court;

(5) The title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and

(6) The name, address, and telephone number of the active licensee of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(d).)

“The State Bar of California may set an appropriate fee to be paid by counsel pro hac vice.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(e)[FN1].)

 

Discussion 

Red Robin International, Inc., Red Robin Gourmet Burgers, Inc., and Esparza (collectively, “Defendants”) move, pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40, for an order allowing the pro hac vice admission of Thomas Richard Leemon. 

The application complies with the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 9.40; accordingly, the application is granted. 


[FN1] The fee is currently $500.00. (See https://www.calbar.ca.gov/Portals.0/documents/rules/Rules_Appendix_ASched-Chgs-Deadlines.pdf.) 

Case Number: 23PSCV02921    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: H

Ramirez v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., Case No. 23PSCV02921

The hearing on Plaintiff Victor Paredes Ramirez’s Motion to Compel Deposition Attendance of a Person Most Qualified and Custodian of Records of Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. and Request for Documents is CONTINUED to December 12, 2024, at 9:30 a.m. 

Background

 

Plaintiff Victor Paredes Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

 

On November 2, 2021, Plaintiff entered into an express written contract with American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) regarding a new 2022 Honda CR-V, VIN # 7FARW1H75NE003437. The subject vehicle is equipped with a computerized driver-assisting safety system known as “Honda Sensing,” which includes adaptive cruise control, lane departure warnings, and autonomous braking known as the Collision Mitigation Braking System. The Honda Sensing system suffers from a defect that causes the various subsystems within it to malfunction dangerously while the vehicles are driven (the “sensing defect”). Defendant knew or should have known about the sensing defect before the subject vehicle was sold and failed to disclose same.

 

On September 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendant and Does 1-10 for (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act—Breach of Express Warranty and (2) Fraudulent Inducement—Concealment.

 

An Order to Show Cause Re: Why Sanctions Should Not Be Imposed Against Defense Counsel for Failure to Appear at the Informal Discovery Conference on June 11, 2024 is set for October 10, 2024.

 

The Final Status Conference is set for February 4, 2025. Trial is set for February 18, 2025.

 

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition attendance of a person most qualified and custodian of records and request for documents.

 

Legal Standard

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230).

“If, after service of a deposition notice, . . . a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection. . ., fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)

“A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. . ., or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contracted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b).)

“If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction. . ., unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)

Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request must be accompanied by a separate statement providing all information necessary to understand each discovery request and all the responses at issue. (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a),(c).)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff moves the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, for an order compelling Defendant to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian of Records to be deposed. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record in the amount of $2,509.00.

Plaintiff’s counsel Timothy Lupinek (“Lupinek”) represents, and/or the accompanying exhibits reflect, as follows:

On May 1, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with a “Notice of Deposition of Defendant, American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian(s) of Record” (“Notice”), setting the deposition for June 24, 2024; said Notice was comprised of 54 categories of matters for examination and 53 document production requests. (Lupinek Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A.) On June 18, 2024, Defendant served its “Objection to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition of the Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian(s) of Record and Request for Production of Documents” (“Objection”); said Objection provided objections to each of the matters for examination and document production requests and advised that the deposition would not be proceeding on the date and time scheduled. (Id., ¶ 8, Exh. C.) On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter, accompanied by an email requesting an alternative deposition date. (Id., ¶¶ 6 and 9, Exhs. B and D). On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office sent Defendant’s counsel a video link to enable Defendant’s witness to participate in the deposition. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. B.)

On June 24, 2024, at 1:00 p.m., Plaintiff’s counsel logged onto the video link for the deposition and stayed on for 30 minutes, but no one appeared. (Id., ¶¶ 6 and 11, Exh. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel then emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant’s counsel an additional 3 days to provide alternate dates for the deposition. (Id.) Defendant’s counsel advised that same day that he would “look into this” and “work on providing . . . dates shortly.” (Id.) On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel followed up with Defendant’s counsel regarding the requested dates; that same day, Defendant’s counsel advised, “[w]e are working on obtaining dates for AHM’s PMQ and I will provide them once I am able.” (Id.) The instant motion followed on August 20, 2024.

The Court is inclined to continue the hearing based on a lack reasonable and good faith meet and confer. Plaintiff’s 7-page meet and confer dated June 20, 2024 is devoid of specific references to any of the 53 matters for examination and 54 document production requests (i.e., aside from Matter No. 28 and Category No. 8).

Accordingly, the Court will continue the hearing, to December 12, 2024, at 9:30 a.m. The Court instructs the parties to engage in a reasonable and good faith meet and confer in the interim.

Counsel is instructed to file and serve a Joint Status Report, outlining the parties’ meet and confer efforts and identifying what specific categories of requested testimony and document requests remain in dispute, no later nine court days prior to the continued hearing date.  

Case Number: 24PSCV01311    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: H

Wang v. Liu, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01311 

The hearing on Proposed Intervenor United Financial Casualty Company’s Motion for Leave to Intervene is CONTINUED to November 5, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. On or before October 30, 2024, Proposed Intervenor is directed to file a supplemental declaration supporting its claim that DLJMK is a defunct corporation that was terminated.


Background

Plaintiff Min Wang (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries and damages in a May 2, 2022 motor vehicle accident.

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting a negligence cause of action against Bin Liu, DLJMK, Inc. (“DLJMK”), Zhi Qin Cai Zhang, and Does 1-25.

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed three “Amendment[s] to Complaint,” wherein MJKC, Inc., JKM Warriors, Inc. and Katerine Lineth Cai Lane were substituted in lieu of Does 1-3, respectively.

Pending before the Court is United Financial Casualty Company’s motion to intervene.


Legal Standard

 

“A nonparty shall petition the court for leave to intervene by noticed motion or ex parte application. The petition shall include a copy of the proposed complaint in intervention or answer in intervention and set forth the grounds upon which intervention rests.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (c).) 

 

“The court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if either of the following conditions is satisfied:

(A) A provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene.

(B) The person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(1).)

 

“The court may, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(2).)

 

Discussion 

Proposed Intervenor United Financial Casualty Company (“Proposed Intervenor”) moves for an order to intervene on behalf of its insured, DLJMK, to file an Answer-In-Intervention and to stay discovery requests against DLJMK. 

“Intervention may . . . be allowed in the insurance context, where third party claimants are involved, when the insurer is allowed to take over in litigation if its insured is not defending an action, to avoid harm to the insurer.” (Royal Indemnity Co. v. United Enterprises, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 194, 206.) The only manner in which an insurer may defend or exercise the powers of its insured suspended corporation is by intervening in the action under Code of Civil Procedure section 387 and asserting any defenses on behalf of its insured.  (Kaufman & Broad Communities, Inc. v. Performance Plastering, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 212, 220.)

 

“An insurer’s right to intervene in an action against the insured, for personal injury or property damage, arises as a result of Insurance Code section 11580[FN1].” (Reliance Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 383, 386.)

Proposed Intervenor asserts that DKJMK is a “defunct corporation that was terminated.” (Motion, 3:28; see also, 7:1 and 8:4-5.) Proposed Intervenor, however, fails to provide the court with any evidence in support of this statement; accordingly, the court will continue the hearing on the motion, to November 5, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

Proposed Intervenor’s counsel is instructed to file and serve a supplemental declaration in this regard by no later than October 30, 2024.

    

[FN1] Insurance Code § 11580 reads as follows:

“A policy insuring against losses set forth in subdivision (a) shall not be issued or delivered to any person in this state unless it contains the provisions set forth in subdivision (b). Such policy, whether or not actually containing such provisions, shall be construed as if such provisions were embodied therein.

(a) Unless it contains such provisions, the following policies of insurance shall not be thus issued or delivered:

(1) Against loss or damage resulting from liability for injury suffered by another person other than (i) a policy of workers' compensation insurance, or (ii) a policy issued by a nonadmitted Mexican insurer solely for use in the Republic of Mexico.

(2) Against loss of or damage to property caused by draught animals or any vehicle, and for which the insured is liable, other than a policy which provides insurance in the Republic of Mexico, issued or delivered in this state by a nonadmitted Mexican insurer.

(b) Such policy shall not be thus issued or delivered to any person in this state unless it contains all the following provisions:

(1) A provision that the insolvency or bankruptcy of the insured will not release the insurer from the payment of damages for injury sustained or loss occasioned during the life of such policy.

(2) A provision that whenever judgment is secured against the insured or the executor or administrator of a deceased insured in an action based upon bodily injury, death, or property damage, then an action may be brought against the insurer on the policy and subject to its terms and limitations, by such judgment creditor to recover on the judgment.”
