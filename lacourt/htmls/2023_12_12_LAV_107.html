DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01970    Hearing Date: December 12, 2023    Dept: 107

BACKGROUND

 

This is a premises liability case. On November 10, 2022, Plaintiff Mary Howard (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants RCLP Property (erroneously sued as El Camino Shopping Center, LLC) (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10, asserting one cause of action for premises liability.

 

The Complaint alleges the following: on December 2, 2020, Plaintiff was near her handicap parking spot at El Camino Shopping Center when she fell in a recessed paint and hidden parking lot area. As a result of the fall, she suffered a chest aneurysm and other injuries. She would not have sustained those injuries if not for El Camino Shopping Center’s negligence in maintaining its parking lots’ safety.

 

On November 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant motions, seeking an order deeming her Request for Admissions, Set One, admitted and compelling Defendant to provide initial responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record for necessitating the motions.

 

As of December 7, 2023, no opposition to the motions has been filed.

 

[Tentative] Rulings

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Mary Howard’s (1) Motion for an Order to Have Plaintiff’s Request For Admissions, Set One Deemed Admitted and (2) Motion for an Order Compelling Defendant to Provide Responses to Plaintiff’s Request For Production of Documents, Set One as follows.

 

The truth of the matters specified in the plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

Defendant El Camino Shopping Center, LLC is ordered to provide code-compliant responses, without objections, to the plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One, within 30 days of this ruling.

 

The Court further orders the defendant and its counsel of record, Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith, to pay the plaintiff sanctions of $2,920, jointly and severally. This amount represents the total sanctions for both motions.  

THE REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION MOTION

 

“Within 30 days after service” of a demand for inspection, the responding party shall serve responses to the discovery “unless on motion” the court has shortened or extended the time for response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.260, subd. (a).) The propounding and responding parties can also agree to extend the time for response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270(a).)

 

If a party to whom a demand for inspection fails to serve a timely response to it, (1) the responding waives all objections to the demand (unless the court on motion relieves the party from the waiver) and (2) the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

Here, Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s initial responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Set One (“RPD”).

 

Plaintiff’s counsel attests to the following facts in support of the RPD motion. On or about June 5, 2023, counsel’s office served Plaintiff’s RPDs on Defendant via email and resent them again on or about July 18, 2023, after defense counsel’s firm stated that it had not received the discovery. (RPD Motion, declaration of Jackie Rose Kruger (“Kruger Decl.”), ¶¶ 2-5.) On or about July 18, 2023, defense counsel responded to the email thanking Plaintiff’s counsel for sending the discovery and stating that the firm “will provide a response as soon as possible.” (Kruger Decl., ¶ 6.) On or about August 14, 2023, defense counsel’s law firm requested an extension to serve the responses, but Plaintiff only agreed to grant an extension for Defendant to serve responses to Plaintiff’s form interrogatories. (Kruger Decl., ¶¶ 7-12.) As of the date counsel signed her declaration on November 15, 2023, no responses to the RPDs had been served. (Kruger Decl., ¶ 13.)

 

In light of Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration and Defendant’s failure to oppose the motion, the Court finds it proper to grant the request to compel Defendant’s responses to the RPDs.

 

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

 

Here, there are no circumstances that would make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions of $4,185 for the RPD motion. The requested sanctions consist of 0.5 hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent reviewing the file and on communications, 2 hours she spent preparing the moving papers, 1 hour she anticipated spending reviewing an opposition, 1 hour she expected to spend on a reply to the opposition, and 1 hour she anticipates spending at the hearing, a total of 5.5 hours at counsel’s billing rate of $750 per hour (equals $4,125), plus a $60 filing fee.

 

The Court finds the requested sanctions and counsel’s billing rate excessive, given that the RPD motion is not complex and no opposition or reply was filed.

 

Therefore, for the purposes of the instant motions only, the Court finds a billing rate of $400 reasonable.

 

Accordingly, for the RPD motion, the Court will only award sanctions of $1,460, consisting of 3.5 hours of counsel’s time at the hourly rate of $400 (equals $1,400), plus a $60 filing fee.

 

THE REQUEST FOR ADMISSION MOTION

 

If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, a “party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with section 2023.010).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)

 

Here, Plaintiff moves for an order deeming the truth of the matters specified in her Request for Admissions, Set One (“RFA”) admitted.

 

Similar to the RPD motion, Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that her office initially propounded the RFAs on or about June 5, 2023, but when defense counsel’s law firm stated they had not received the RFAs, emailed the RFAs to Defendant again on or about July 18, 2023. (RFA Motion, Kruger Decl., ¶¶ 2-5.) Thereafter, the parties did not agree on an extension to serve the response. (Kruger Decl., ¶¶ 6-12.) As of the date counsel signed her declaration in support of the RFA motion on November 15, 2023, Plaintiff had not yet received Defendant’s responses to the RFAs. (Kruger Decl., ¶ 15.)

 

The RFAs at issue asked the defendant to admit the following:

  1. Admit that You owned the PREMISES on December 2, 2020, the date of the SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  2. Admit that YOU were responsible for operating the PREMISES on December 2, 2020, the date of the SUBJECT INCIDENT.

  4. Admit that YOU were negligent in your maintenance or use of the PREMISES.

  6. Admit that YOU failed to make regular inspections of the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT prior to the INCIDENT.
  7. Admit that there was a DANGEROUS CONDITION at the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT.
  8. Admit that YOU were aware that there was a DANGEROUS CONDITION at the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT prior to the date of the INCIDENT.
  9. Admit that the DANGEROUS CONDITION at the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT existed for such a period of time before the INCIDENT that YOU should have discovered the condition.
  10. Admit that YOU did nothing to repair the DANGEROUS CONDITION at the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT prior to the date of INCIDENT.
  11. Admit that PLAINTIFF was on the PREMISES on December 2, 2020, the date of the PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS TO DEFENDANT, SET ONE SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  12. Admit that PLAINTIFF was injured on December 2, 2020, the date of the SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  13. Admit that PLAINTIFF was caused to suffer various injuries as a result of the SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  14. Admit that the Plaintiff was injured when she fell due to the DANGEROUS CONDITION at the LOCATION OF THE INCIDENT at the time of the INCIDENT.
  15. Admit that YOUR negligence was a substantial factor in causing the SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  16. Admit that YOU do not dispute that YOU were at fault with respect to this INCIDENT.
  17. Admit that there is no evidence that PLAINTIFF committed any negligent acts that contributed to causing the SUBJECT INCIDENT.
  18. Admit that YOU do not dispute liability for Plaintiff’s injuries resulting from this INCIDENT.

(RFA Motion, Kruger Decl., ¶ 2; Exhibit A – a copy of the RFAs.)

 

“The court shall make [an] order [admitting the truth of any matters specified in the RFAs], unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

Here, there is no evidence that Defendant has served, before the hearing on the instant motion, a proposed response to the RFAs. In light of the evidence before the Court and Defendant’s failure to oppose the RFA motion, the request to deem the truth of the matters specified in the RFAs admitted is granted.

 

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with a motion to deem matters specified in a request for admissions as true against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes the motions unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Here, like for the RPD motion, Plaintiff seeks sanctions of $4,185, consisting of 0.5 hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent reviewing the file and on communications, 2 hours she spent preparing the moving papers, 1 hour she anticipated spending reviewing an opposition, 1 hour she anticipated spending on a reply to the opposition, and 1 hour she anticipates spending at the hearing, a total of 5.5 hours at counsel’s billing rate of $750 per hour (equals $4,125), plus a $60 filing fee.

 

The Court will only impose sanctions of $1,460 for the RFA motion for the same reasons it has awarded that amount in connection with the RPD motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiff Mary Howard’s motions are GRANTED.

The Court further orders the defendant and its counsel of record, Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith, to pay the plaintiff sanctions of $2,920, jointly and severally. This amount represents the total sanctions for both motions.

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO DISCOVERY – REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Maria Camila Hernandez

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

  1. An order compelling Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One Request Nos. 1 through 37.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Motion to Compel Further Responses as to Request for Production of Documents, Set One Request Nos. 1 through 37 is DENIED.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On May 8, 2023, Plaintiff Maria Camila Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. (“Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.”), Volkswagen Santa Monica, LLC, D/B/A Volkswagen Santa Monica (“Volkswagen Santa Monica”) and DOES 1 through 10 inclusive (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

The complaint alleged four causes of action for violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty, violation of the Song-Beverly Act–Breach of Implied Warranty, violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Section 1793.2, and negligent repair. Plaintiff alleges that the Defendants delivered a 2021 Volkswagen Jetta, having VIN No.: 3VWC57BU3MM060863 ("the Subject Vehicle”) to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty.

 

On June 8, 2023, Defendants filed an answer.

 

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request For Production of Documents, Set One.  

 

On November 29, 2023, Defendant filed an opposition, declarations in support of its opposition, and separate statement.

 

On December 7, 2023, Plaintiff replied.  

 

PLAINTIFF’S EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

1. Declaration of Erin Tallent: OVERRULED

2. Declaration of Chris Lewis: OVERRULED

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The propounding party may bring a motion to compel further responses to a demand for production if the propounding party deems that production is deficient, incomplete, or contains meritless objections. (CCP § 2031.310(a)).  The legal burden to justify refusing or failing to provide discovery lies with the objecting party. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220). A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) 

 

The motion must be accompanied by a good-faith meet-and-confer declaration. (CCP § 2031.310(b)). “A determination of whether an attempt at informal resolution is adequate . . . involves the exercise of discretion.” (Stewart v. Colonial W. Agency (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016).  “The history of the litigation, the nature of the interaction between counsel, the nature of the issues, the type and scope of discovery requested, the prospects for success and other similar factors can be relevant.  Judges have broad powers and responsibility to determine what measure and procedures are appropriate in varying circumstances.” (Id.) 

 

Meet and Confer:

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted discovery on propounded Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on Defendant Volkswagen World Group of America Inc., seeking documents relating to: (1) Plaintiff’s own vehicle; (2) Defendant’s warranty and repurchase policies, procedures, and practices; and (3) Defendant’s knowledge of the same or similar defects in other vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle on June 27, 2023. (Dec. Sogoyan, ¶19). Defendant served its responses on July 31, 2023. (Id. at ¶20.)

 

Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter on August 7, 2023, with supplemental responses due on August 14, 2023. (Id. at ¶22; Exhibit 5.) On August 14, 2023, Plaintiff’s Counsel followed up on the meet and confer letter and requested availability for a telephonic meet and confer. On August 15, 2023, Defendant’s Counsel replied to Plaintiff’s email to schedule a telephonic meet and confer. (Dec. Tallent ¶10,12,14; Exhibit C.) Later that day, Plaintiff sent another meet and confer letter after Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. failed to provide supplemental responses. (Id. at ¶27; Exhibit 6.) The meet and confer letter indicated that the responses were due on August 22, 2023. (Id. at ¶28; Exhibit 6.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s Counsel satisfied the CCP § 2031.310(b) meet and confer requirements because counsel sent two meet and confer letters and tried to set up a telephonic meeting.

 

Separate Statement

 

Under Rule 3.1345, a Separate Statement must include the request, the response, and reason that a further response is needed. Here, the Plaintiff’s Separate Statement has the required information. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement p. 3-205.)

 

Timeliness

 

Under CCP § 2030.300, a party has 45 days after receipt of the verified responses to file a motion to compel further. Here, the verified responses were provided on July 31, 2023. (Dec. Sogoyan ¶20). Forty-five business days after July 31, 2023 is October 3, 2023. Thus, the motion is timely.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Plaintiff filed this Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One on the grounds that Defendant waived its objections and failed to provide adequate responses. Defendant argues that the (1) Plaintiff failed to meet and confer in good faith, (2) VWGoA has served appropriate objections/responses and produced all documents that Plaintiff needs to determine issues of liability under the Song-Beverly Act, including relevant repair orders, technical communications, the technical service bulletin performed on the vehicle, pre-litigation contact logs, and VWGoA’s policy and procedure materials, and (3) Plaintiff fails to establish good cause for any additional responses or documents.

 

The Code of Civil Procedure contemplates three forms of proper responses to a request for production: a statement of compliance in full or in part (CCP § 2031.220); a statement of inability to comply (CCP § 2031.230); and a partial objection coupled with a statement of compliance or representation of inability to comply (CCP § 2031.240). 

 

Here, Defendant objected to Nos. 1-37. (Defendant Volkswagen Group of America Inc.’s Responses). For instance, Defendant responded with a statement of inability to comply to Nos. 1-5, 7-23, 25-28, but still produced responsive documents. (Defendant’s Separate Statement Nos.1-5, 7-23, 25-28; Dec. Lewis ¶7). With respect to Nos. 6, 24, and 29, Defendant responded that no documents exist in its custody/possession/control which is also a statement of inability to comply. (Defendant’s Separate Statement Nos. 6,24,29). Moreover, Defendant also properly objected to Nos. 30-37 because it said that the demand was overbroad and burdensome. (Defendant’s Separate Statement Nos. 30-37; Dec. Lewis ¶8-15). Plaintiff replies that the Defendant’s argument fails because it is unfounded and fails to conform to the Code of Civil Procedure. Plaintiff’s reliance on Coriell v. Superior Court is instructive. In Coriell, the court reasoned that “although asserting that answers to some of the designated interrogatories will be oppressive because of the expenditure of time and money to compile data from records, [defendant] omits any showing of why those interrogatories cannot be answered as permitted in Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030 (c).” However, here, Defendants did provide additional information, such as Plaintiff’s failure to “reasonably particularize each category of item” requested. (Coriell v. Superior Court (1974) 39 Cal.App.3d 487, 493 [114 Cal.Rptr. 310].)

 

Therefore, Requests for Further Responses as to Production of Documents Nos. 1-37 is DENIED.
