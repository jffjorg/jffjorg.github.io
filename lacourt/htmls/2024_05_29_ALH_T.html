DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV10542    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: T

Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment

 

Moving Party: Defendant Postmates, LLC f/k/a Postmates, Inc. (“Defendant”)

Responding Party:    Plaintiff Rafael Quijano (“Plaintiff”)

Tentative Rulings: Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  Defendant’s application to            seal is GRANTED.

 Background

 This is an action for negligence arising from a vehicle collision which took place in March 2019. Plaintiff filed his form Complaint against Defendant and Mario Hernandez on March 18, 2021. Plaintiff filed his operative form FAC on March 19, 2021. The FAC’s sole cause of action for general negligence alleges:

Defendants MARIO HERNANDEZ, POSTMATES, INC., POSTMATES, LLC, and DOES 1 to 50, inclusive and each of them . . .  were the agents and employees of the other defendants and acted within the course and scope; Defendants owed a duty and breached that duty by, among other things, failing to adequately and properly control, delegate, hire, entrust, train, protect, and supervise.

 Defendant filed its answer on October 7, 2021.

 Defendant now moves for summary judgment on the grounds that (1) no act or omission on Defendant’s part contributed to any harm suffered by Plaintiff who was struck by a negligent third party driver; and (2) Defendant owed no duty to Plaintiff who admittedly and intentionally misrepresented his identity by using the credentials of Mario Hernandez (“Hernandez”) to create an account on Defendant’s platform.

 On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition.

 On May 23, 2024, Defendant filed its reply and an application to seal documents submitted by Plaintiff.

 Application to Seal

 The Court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it finds that (1) there is an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced absent sealing, (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exists to achieve the overriding interest.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  A motion seeking an order sealing records must be accompanied by a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).) 

 Discussion

 Plaintiff lodged four documents with its opposition to Defendant’s motion for summary judgment that were previously designated as "Confidential" and subject to the protective order entered by this Court on December 13, 2022. Specifically, Plaintiff lodged Defendant’s Insurance Policies and attached the following three exhibits to the Deposition of Clyde Collins: (1) Exhibit 10, Checkr Report; (2) Exhibit 13, Account History; and (3) Exhibit 14, Account History.

 Defendant seeks to seal all four documents and submits the Declaration of L. Kuck in support. The Court finds that said declaration provides sufficient facts to justify the sealing. (Declaration of Danielle L. Kuck ¶ 2-3.) The application is therefore GRANTED.

 Motion for Summary Judgment

 A party seeking summary judgment has the burden of producing evidentiary facts sufficient to entitle them to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c).) The moving party must make an affirmative showing that they are entitled to judgment irrespective of whether or not the opposing party files an opposition. (Villa v. McFerren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 742- 743.) Thus, “the initial burden is always on the moving party to make prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519 (citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)

 The moving party’s “affidavits must cite evidentiary facts, not legal conclusions, or ‘ultimate’ facts” and the courts must construe the evidence in support of the opposing party, resolving any doubts in favor of the opposing party. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639; Scalf, 128 Cal.App.4th at 1519; Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 The opposing party on a motion for summary judgment is under no evidentiary burden to produce rebuttal evidence until the moving party meets its initial burden. (Binder v. Aetna Life Insurance Company (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 840.) Once the initial movant’s burden is met, then the burden shifts to the opposing party to show, with admissible evidence, that there is a triable issue requiring the weighing procedures of trial. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p).)

 Summary judgment must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 Discussion

 Defendant moves for an order granting summary judgment in its favor on the sole cause of action for negligence in Plaintiff’s FAC.  As the moving party, Defendant must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)

 Request for Judicial Notice

 Judicial notice may be taken of “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States of any state of the United States.” (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (d).)

 Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of the operative First Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff on March 19, 2021, in LASC Case No. 21STCV10542 and the operative First Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff on April 18, 2019, in LASC Case No. 19STCV13497. The requests for judicial notice filed by Defendant are GRANTED as to the existence of the documents but not to the truth of the matters stated therein. (Scott v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 752-54.)

 Evidentiary Objections

 The Court may decline to rule on objections that are not material to its disposition of the motion. (Code of Civ. Proc. 437c(q).)

 Plaintiff submits evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Ned Collins and the Declaration of Mario Hernandez. The Court OVERRULES objections 3-8. Plaintiff also submits evidentiary objection to the reply evidence submitted by Defendant. The Court OVERRULES objections 1-2.

 Defendants submits evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Todd Roescher, the Declaration of Adam Feit, the exhibits attached to Plaintiff’s compendium of exhibits, the Declaration of Plaintiff, and the deposition testimony of Plaintiff.  The objections are OVERRULED. 

 First Cause of Action for Negligence

The elements for negligence are: (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)  

             Duty

 “The first element, duty, may be imposed by law, be assumed by the defendant, or exist by virtue of a special relationship.” (Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1128, internal quotations omitted.)

 California Civil Code section 1714, subdivision (a) states, in part: “[e]veryone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care . . . .”  “As a general rule, each person has a duty to use ordinary care and is liable for injuries caused by his failure to exercise reasonable care in the circumstances.”  (Romero v. Superior Court (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1080, citation and quotations omitted.) 

 “The issue of whether a duty exists is a question of law to be determined by the court . . . .”  (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1620.)  A party who moves for summary judgment because it did not owe a duty of care must affirmatively negate the existence of duty. (Eriksson v. Nunnink (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 826, 848-850.) 

 Here, Defendant contends that it is entitled to summary judgment because it did not owe any duty to Plaintiff. Specifically, Defendant argues it had no duty to “control, delegate, hire, entrust, train, protect, and supervise” Plaintiff or anybody else to prevent the subject accident. Defendant’s evidence in support establishes that Plaintiff was neither an employee nor an independent contractor, and that Plaintiff only gained access to Defendant’s platform by using Hernandez’s California driver’s license and social security number to create a delivery account in Hernandez’s name. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 9-18.) Further, Defendant’s evidence establishes that Defendant had no knowledge of Plaintiff since Defendant did not know at the time of the subject accident that Plaintiff had posed as Hernandez to create an account. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 19.) The Court finds the foregoing evidence is sufficient to satisfy Defendant’s initial burden of negating the existence of the element of duty.  The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant owed a duty to Plaintiff.

 Plaintiff attempts to raise a triable issue of material fact by arguing that a special relationship existed between Plaintiff and Postmates, thus triggering a duty. Plaintiff argues there is substantial evidence that establishes that Defendant exercises control over its “couriers,” including dictating which customers to deliver to and the locations deliveries were to be made.  (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos. 57, 59, 63.)

 As a preliminary matter, the Court finds that Plaintiff does not satisfy his responsive burden because Plaintiff does not address the fact that he was not an authorized user of Defendant’s platform. Thus, even if Defendant owed a duty of ordinary care to “couriers” based on a special relationship, Plaintiff’s special relationship argument fails as Plaintiff was not a driver for Postmates.

 Plaintiff then argues that Postmates allows couriers to establish subcontractors, and his account was created with Hernandez’s authorization and consent. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF No. 11.)  The Court finds, however, that Plaintiff misstates the evidence.  The evidence establishes that subcontracting is permitted only between authorized users of the platform and only to persons who meet and accept all the requirements of the Fleet Agreement and complete "the process to separately receive Delivery Opportunities on the Postmates Platform." (Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, UMF No. 9.) The evidence shows that all of the qualifications were not met here. (Defendant’s Separate Statement, UMF Nos.  9-10, 13, 19.)  It is undisputed that Plaintiff was not an authorized user of Defendant’s platform, that Plaintiff did not meet the qualifications for subcontracting, and that at the time of the subject incident Defendant was unaware of Plaintiff’s unauthorized use of Defendant’s platform. 

 Even assuming that Plaintiff was a Postmates courier, Plaintiff fails to set forth any competent evidence that Postmates would then have a duty to encourage Defendant to wear reflective clothing while on deliveries.  Rather, the undisputed evidence is that Postmates couriers are independent contractors and that Postmates does not exercise control over how couriers complete their work.  Instead, couriers are defined in the Postmates Agreement as “independent providers of delivery services” and couriers expressly agree they are not employees of Postmates and that they have “the sole right to control the manner in which Deliveries are performed and the means by which those deliveries are completed.”  (Appx. Of Evidence in Support of Defendant’s Reply, Ex. F, p. 1) 

 While Plaintiff goes through great lengths to detail precisely how Postmates connects couriers with customers, how deliveries are assigned, how finances are handled, and ensuring that minors don’t access alcohol and tobacco through the service, Plaintiff makes no reference whatsoever to Postmates exercising control over how couriers are to actually complete their deliveries.  (Ex. F, p. 2)  The Agreement goes on to further define the relationship with couriers: 

  Postmates shall have no right to, and shall not, supervise, direct or control Contractor, or control the manner or prescribe the method Contractor uses to perform Deliveries, and the general public and all governmental agencies regulating the activities contemplated by this Agreement shall be so informed. Contractor shall be solely responsible for determining the most effective, efficient, satisfactory, and safe manner to perform Deliveries, including determining the manner of pickup, delivery, and route selection.  (Ex. F, Para. 2B)

 The Agreement goes on to disclaim any authority to control the means of a delivery:

  Contractor authorizes Postmates, during the course of a Delivery, to communicate with Contractor, customer, and/or Merchant to assist Contractor, to the extent permitted by Contractor, in facilitating a Delivery. However, under no circumstances shall Postmates be authorized to control the manner or means by which Contractor performs a Delivery.  (Ex. F, Para. 3E)

 Based on these undisputed facts, Plaintiff failed to provide evidence that would support finding that a duty arose under a special relationship under the facts set forth in Biakanja v. Irving (1958) 49 Cal.2d 647 or Brown v. USC Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204.  These cases and their progeny do not stand for the proposition of exercising control over independent contractors for the safety of the independent contractor. 

 There is a long line of cases in the construction context defining the duties of a principal to subcontractors flowing from Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689 which held that an entity that hires an independent contractor to do work generally is not liable for on-the-job injuries to the independent contractor's workers.  The Court is satisfied that it is more appropriate to analyze this case under the rules set forth under the body of law flowing from Privette as it pertains to the relationship between principals and independent contractors.

 “When a person or organization hires an independent contractor, the hirer presumptively delegates to the contractor the responsibility to do the work safely. [Citations.] This presumption is grounded in two major principles: first, that independent contractors by definition ordinarily control the manner of their own work; and second, that hirers typically hire independent contractors precisely for their greater ability to perform the contracted work safely and successfully.” Sandoval v. Qualcomm Incorporated (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256, 269. 

 There are two exceptions to the Privette doctrine:  retained control and concealed hazard.  Retained control applies when: (1) the hirer retains control over the manner in which the contractor performs the work; (2) the hirer actually exercises its retained control by involving itself in the work such that the contractor is not entirely free to do the work in its own manner; and (3) the hirer's exercise of retained control affirmatively contributes to the worker's injury. Sandoval at 276-277.  Here, retained control does not apply because the undisputed evidence is that Postmates completely, expressly and unambiguously disclaimed any right to control or actual control over how deliveries were to be accomplished.  While Plaintiff contends that Postmates dictated the order in which deliveries should be made, this contention is not supported by the evidence.  Rather, Postmates only “suggested” delivering to the closest address first.  (Plaintiff’s Ex. 11:  Collins Depo., 69:1 – 69:10.)

 The concealed hazard exception is limited to situations where the hirer is also the owner or occupier of land. Kinsman v. Unocal Corp. (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 675.  Here, Postmates was not the owner or occupier of the accident site.  Even if public policy compelled extending the concealed hazard exception beyond land owners/occupiers, it would still not apply here for “a ‘concealed’ hazard means something specific: a hazard that the hirer either knows or reasonably should know exists, and that the contractor does not know exists and could not reasonably discover without the hirer's disclosure.”  Sandoval, supra, at 272.  Here, the undisputed facts are that the California Motorcycle Handbook recommends that motorcycle riders wear protective clothing with reflective materials.  (Plaintiff’s Additional Undisputed Material Facts 83 – 89.)  It is further undisputed that Plaintiff took the written test based on the California Motorcycle Handbook to obtain his permit to drive a motorcycle.  (Defendant’s Undisputed Material Fact 23.)  It is also undisputed that Defendant rode a motorcycle every day for approximately 5 – 6 years.  (Defendant’s Undisputed Material Fact 27.)  Based on this record and the reasonable inferences that can be drawn from it, there is not a triable issue of fact over whether Defendant had knowledge of a concealed fact that Plaintiff could not have reasonably discovered without Defendant’s disclosure. 

 Because of the findings above, the Court will not engage in an analysis of the Rowland factors.  (Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108.)  As Plaintiff concedes, the first step of the Rowland analysis is whether a special relationship or “some other set of circumstances” give rise to an affirmative duty to protect.  (Brown, supra, 11 Cal5th at 209.)  Here, there is no special relationship nor is there a set of circumstances giving rise to an affirmative duty.  As such, there is no need to conduct a Roland analysis. 

 Because the Court finds that Defendant met its initial burden by negating the existence of duty and Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of material fact, Defendant is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

 Accordingly, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.

 Moving party is ordered to give notice.

 

Case Number: 21STCV27987    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: T

Motion To Compel Plaintiff’s Second Independent Medical Examination

Moving Party:                    Defendant Costco Wholesale Corporation

Responding Party:          Plaintiff Cynthia Hopes

Tentative Ruling:             Denied

BACKGROUND

On January 30, 2024, the Court granted Costco’s motion to reopen discovery based on the July 23, 2024 trial date.

On February 23, 2024, Costco initially filed its motion to compel Plaintiff’s second medical examination, but it was later taken off-calendar on March 21, 2024 by the Court, with the Honorable Judge Lisa Jaskol presiding. 

On May 6, 2024, Costco re-noticed its motion to compel Plaintiff’s second medical examination. On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed her opposition. On May 22, 2024, Costco filed its reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

In any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff where: (1) the examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive; and (2) the examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220 (a).) A defendant may make a demand for physical examination without leave of the court after that defendant has been served or has appeared (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220 (b)), and the physical examination demanded shall be scheduled for a date at least 30 days after service (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220 (d)). 

"If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2032.310(a).) A motion for an examination shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310(b).) The Court shall grant the motion only for good cause shown.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310(a).)

DISCUSSION

Costco has failed to meet and confer in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure §§ 20160.040 and 2032.310(b). While Costco’s counsel sent an email requesting a second medical examination on December 13, 2023 (Breuer Decl. ¶ 19, Exh. I), there is no indication that the parties actually met and conferred in good faith. There is no indication that Costco’s counsel attempted to discuss with Plaintiff’s counsel following receipt of Plaintiff’s objection. The mere sending of an email or letter does not establish a good faith attempt to meet and confer. While there may be good cause in compelling a second medical examination, these procedural defects cannot be overlooked.

Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES Costco’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s second independent medical examination without prejudice. 

Plaintiff to give notice of this ruling.


Case Number: 21STCV43665    Hearing Date: May 29, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to compel further responses

Moving Party:           Defendant/Cross-Complainant GGP Northridge Fashion Center, LC (erroneously named and served as Brookfield Properties Retail Group) (“GGP Northridge”)

Responding Party:    None

Tentative Ruling:       Granted

Background

On November 30, 2021, Nahid Yazdanparast (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Ashley and GGP Northridge for negligence arising from a slip and fall incident in the parking lot of the Ashley Homestore located at 9301 Tampa Ave., Northridge, California.  

On May 18, 2023, Ashley filed its answer. On May 30, 20223, GGP Northridge filed its answer. 

On September 12, 2023, GGP Northridge filed its Cross-Complaint against Ashley for equitable indemnity, contribution, express indemnity, breach of contract, and declaratory relief.

On November 13, 2023, Ashley filed its answer to the Cross-Complaint.

On January 8, 2024, GGP Northridge filed the instant motion to compel Ashely’s further discovery responses to requests for admission without objection. GGP Northridge requests sanctions in connection with the motion.

No opposition has been filed as of May 25, 2024.

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Admission  

Where responses to requests for admissions have been timely served but are deemed deficient by the requesting party, the requesting party may move for an order compelling the responding party’s further response.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (a).)  For the Court to have jurisdiction to hear a motion for orders compelling an opponent’s further responses to discovery, the moving party must have served the motion on the responding party within 45 days of the moving party’s receipt of the initial or supplemental responses for which the motion is being brought.  (Id. at § 2033.289, subd. (c).) 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response … unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (d).) 

Discussion

Defendant/Cross-Complainant GGP Northridge moves to compel Defendant/Cross-Defendant Ashely’s further discovery responses, without objection, to request for admission numbers 9 and 25. GGP Northridge also request monetary sanctions against Ashley and its attorneys in the sum of $1,811.65.

1.      Timeliness and Meet and Confer

For the Court to have jurisdiction to hear GGP Northridge’s motion, GGP Northridge must have filed it without 45 days of receiving responses from Ashley.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.289, subd. (c).)  The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration in compliance with CCP § 2016.040. (Code Civ. Pro., § 2033.290, subd. (b).)

Here, Ashely served unverified responses on November 13, 2023 and later served a signed verification on December 1, 2023. (Declaration of Michael F. Avila ¶ 11; Ex. “B.”) The parties agreed to extend the deadline to file motions to compel to January 8, 2023. (Id. at ¶ 14; Ex. “E.”) GGP Northridge filed this motion on January 8, 2023.  The Court finds the motion is timely and the declaration of counsel satisfies the meet and confer requirements. 

2.      Requests for Admission

 RFA No. 9: “Admit that on the date of the INCIDENT, 12/22/19,Defendant/ Cross-Complainant, GGP NORTHRIDGE FASHION CENTER, LP qualified as one of the “Indemnified Parties” under Section 12.2 of the lease attached hereto as Exhibit “1 (See pages BATES Numbered GGP-020- GGP-021).”

 RFA No. 25: “Admit that Defendant/ Cross-Complainant, GGP NORTHRIDGE FASHION CENTER, LP qualifies as an additional insured under a commercial general lability policy issued to Defendant/ Cross-Defendant, ASHLEY FURNITURE INDUSTRIES, LLC and/or its related entities.”

 Ashley provides the same response to each request. Specifically, Ashley objects claiming the requests are vague, ambiguous, and asks the responding party to speculate as to the meaning of the term “qualified.” Further, the requests seek a legal conclusion.

 First, the Court finds that GGP Northridge has shown good cause that the RFAs are relevant to this action because, per the lease agreement, GGP Northridge claims that Ashley is required to defend and indemnify GGP Northridge from Plaintiff’s claim and that Ashley was required to obtain general liability insurance with limits of $5 million and to name GGP Northridge as an additional insured so that GGP Northridge would be protected by said insured under claims such as Plaintiff’s claims in this instant lawsuit. (Avila Decl. ¶¶ 5, 8 12.)

 Second, the Court finds Ashley’s objections are meritless. The Court finds that the requests are neither vague nor ambiguous. As written, the requests are intelligible. Further, the Court finds that Ashley is not required the speculate as to the meaning of the term “qualified” because the term is used in it ordinary and plain meaning. Lastly, “a request [for admission] may ask a party for a legal conclusion.” (Grace v. Mansourian (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 523, 529.)  The Court therefore grants the motion and orders Ashley to provide further responses to these RFAs without objection.

 3.      Sanctions

 As concerns motions to compel, the law only requires sanctions in the event that a party unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (d).) As such, any monetary sanctions granted are within the discretionary power of the Court as per section 2033.290. As per section 2023.010, subdivision (d), failure to respond constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. Additionally, it is customary to grant sanctions where a party has filed a motion to compel, and the other party fails to file an opposition. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)   

 Here, Ashley has provided no opposition to the instant motion. GGP Northridge requests sanctions in the sum of $1,811.65. (Avila Decl. ¶ 15.) The Court awards sanctions in the amount of $936.65. This amount reflects five hours of attorney work (preparing this motion and appearing at the hearing) at the reasonable rate of $175.00 per hour, plus the $61.65 filing fee. This amount does not include the time spent preparing a reply, as no opposition was filed. The Court finds this amount of sanctions appropriate to compensate for the cost of the instant motion.    

 Conclusion

 Defendant/Cross-Complainant GGP Northridge Fashion Center, LC’s motion to compel further responses is GRANTED.

 Defendant/Cross-Defendant Ashley Furniture Industries, LLC is ORDERED to provide further responses to Request for Admission numbers 9 and 25 without objection within 20 days of this order.

 Defendant/Cross-Defendant Ashley Furniture Industries, LLC and its attorney are ORDERED to pay sanctions to Defendant/Cross-Complainant GGP Northridge Fashion Center, LC, by and through counsel of record, in the total amount of $936.65, within 20 days of this order.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
