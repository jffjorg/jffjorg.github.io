Case Number: 22STCV33658    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date:10-9-24 c/f 7-22-24

Case #: 22STCV33658

Trial Date: 1-9-25 c/f 6-24-24

 

FURTHER DOCUMENTS

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, Samuel Welch

RESPONDING PARTY: Unopposed/Plaintiff, Shenikwa Malone

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiffs were tenants of a single family welling at 32270 Saticoy Street, West Hills, and allege unsanitary and/or unsafe conditions on the premises as a result of improper maintenance and upkeep.

 

On October 17, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their complaint for Failure to Provide Habitable Dwellings, Breach of Covenant of Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property, Nuisance, and Negligence. Defendants answered the complaint on February 23, 2023.

 

On June 10, 2024, Derrick Robinson filed a Request for Dismissal from the complaint.

 

RULING: Granted.

Defendant, Samuel Welch moves to compel further responses to request for production of documents, numbers 4, 9 and 12, from Plaintiff Shenikwa Malone. The responses consist of references to other items.

 

Such responses constitute an improper, factually incomplete answer. Responding Party may not refer to prior discovery. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783–784 [“Answers must be complete and responsive. Thus, it is not proper to answer by stating, ‘See my deposition’, ’See my pleading’, or ‘See the financial statement’”].)

 

The unopposed motion is therefore granted. Responding is ordered to serve responses in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.210-2031.240 within 30 days of this order. No sanctions requested.

 

The court does not conduct any informal discovery conferences. While the court understands the prior use and perhaps reliance on the IDC system, the court finds the number of items on the court calendar through the next three months presents a potentially inordinate burden. The court reserves the right to set an OSC re: Discovery Referee in lieu of a hearing on any given motion, and may take off any and all motions in lieu of the OSC hearing. The court invites the parties to continue meeting and conferring, including the provision of supplemental responses, when possible.

 

Trial remains set for January 21, 2025.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 


Case Number: 23STCV21144    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 10-9-24 a/f 3-5-25 (9-18-24 minute order)

Case 23STCV21144

Trial Date: Not Set

 

FURTHER DOCUMENTS

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants, Rio Hondo Community College District, et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Pinner Construction Co., Inc.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (set two)

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On November 15, 2016, Plaintiff Pioneer Construction Co., Inc. entered into a contract with defendant Rio Hondo Community College District (Rio Hondo) for the construction of Rio Hondo College “L” Tower Project. Rio Hondo also hired defendant Del Terra Real Estate Services, Inc. dba Del Terra Group as construction manager for the project. The president of Del Terra is defendant Luis Rojas.

 

Plaintiff alleges the Del Terra defendants intentionally sought to delay construction in order to yield additional fees and/or cut compensation to Plaintiff. After a purported 925 day delay, Del Terra “forced” Plaintiff and the subcontractors to “accept far less than what was owed” under the contract or file for bankruptcy. Plaintiff settled for $5,243.525, thereby foregoing an additional $2,691.484 sought. Plaintiff alleges the Del Terra parties are under criminal investigation and were able to engineer the delays due to “control” of at least three seats on the Rio Hondo Community College District Board of Trustees. Defendants Marilyn Flores, Kristal Orozco, Anais Medina Diaz, Oscar Valladares, Rosaelva Lomeli, and Vicky Santana, are identified as members of said board of trustees.

RULING: Denied

Defendants Rio Hondo Community College District, Marilyn Flores, Kristal Orozco, Anais Medina Diaz, Oscar Valladares, Rosaelva Lomeli, and Vicky Santana move to compel further responses to request for production of documents (set two), numbers 19-37, 39-42, and 45-51. Defendants contend Plaintiff promised to produce all non-privileged documents, but no such documents were produced, thereby necessitating the subject motion. Plaintiff acknowledges the promise to produce but contends additional time was required due to the volume of requests. Plaintiff represents that over 3,700 pages of documents have already been produced with more to follow (on an unspecified time frame). Plaintiff requests the court refrain from imposing sanctions. Defendants in reply reiterates the invalid objections to production of non-privileged information, and the continued delays in production. Defendant maintains non-compliance with full production of all non-privileged documents, and the demand for sanctions.

 

Notwithstanding the reserved motion to compel further responses, Defendants correctly cite to the section regarding an unfulfilled promise to produce. “If a party filing a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling under Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280 thereafter fails to permit the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling in accordance with that party's statement of compliance, the demanding party may move for an order compelling compliance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.320, subd. (a).)

 

Notwithstanding the Plaintiff conceding to the promise, Plaintiff counters that the burdensome objection justified some delay. “The documents shall be produced on the date specified in the demand pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (c) of Section 2031.030, unless an objection has been made to that date. If the date for inspection has been extended pursuant to Section 2031.270, the documents shall be produced on the date agreed to pursuant to that section.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.280, subd. (a).) Plaintiff concludes that the objection in and of itself provided additional, unspecified times, since Defendants refused to cooperate in establishing a new deadline.

 

"Whether a particular response does resolve satisfactorily the issues raised by a motion is a matter best determined by the trial court in the exercise of its discretion, based on the circumstances of the case. In many cases involving untimely responses, the propounding party will take the motion off calendar or narrow its scope to the issue of sanctions. If the propounding party proceeds with the motion, however, the trial court has the discretion to rule on the motion. The trial court might compel responses without objection if it finds no legally valid responses have been provided to one or more interrogatories; it might deny the motion to compel responses as essentially unnecessary, in whole or in part, and just impose sanctions; it might treat the motion as one under section 2030.300 and either determine that further answers are required, or order the propounding party to “meet and confer” (§ 2030.300, subd. (b)) and file a separate statement (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1020(a)(2), (c)); or it might take the motion off calendar, thereby requiring the propounding party to file a motion under section 2030.300.”

 

(Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 408–409.)

 

It remains unclear when the represented 3,700 pages documents were produced. Defendants represent an e-mail link to documents provided on August 22, 2204, and the motion was filed on August 30, 2024. [Declarations of Gregory Sonnenberg and Joelle Leib.] Plaintiff also sent a second e-mail with a 193 page “chart” identifying the responsive documents on September 18, 2024. [Leib Decl., Ex. B-C.]

 

It also remains unknown whether further documents remain potentially outstanding by the time of the hearing. The court finds no valid basis of extension simply based on the presentation of  objections without the support of an actual written agreement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270.) Defendants in fact specifically rejected the objections, due to prior refusal to produce documents all together, only to see Plaintiff later agree to production anyway.

 

Barring a showing at the hearing of remaining outstanding production, the court finds no basis to compel compliance with the promise for further production under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.320 assuming all promised production occurred. The court therefore DENIES the motion without prejudice. If responses remain outstanding, the court will order further production.

 

Assuming full compliance at the time of the hearing, the court also concludes that the motion, at a minimum, prompted further production after filing, and was therefore rightfully filed. The court adheres to a policy that whenever a motion prompts a response demonstrating validity to the motion, the court will impose sanctions in favor of the moving party. Given both the filing of the action by Plaintiff and withholding of documents, the court finds no justification for the delays. The non-existent agreement for further extension based on unilateral objections lacks support. The court therefore imposes the statutory minimum of $1,000 in sanctions joint and several against Plaintiff and counsel of record. Payable within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.050, subd. (a), 2031.280, subd. (a).)

 

Discovery disputes leading to voluminous requests from the court may lead to the appointment of a discovery referee. The court does not conduct independent discovery conferences.

 

Motion to compel further responses to production of documents (set one) on calendar for October 14, 2024. Case Management conference set for November 15, 2024.

 

Defendants to give notice to all parties.
